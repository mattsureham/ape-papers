## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether lowering the default urban speed limit from 30mph to 20mph actually reduces road crashes when done at national scale, not just in selected neighborhoods. Using Wales’s abrupt 2023 nationwide reform and England as a comparison, the paper argues that collisions on affected roads fell meaningfully, with killed-or-seriously-injured casualties also declining.

A busy economist should care because this is not really a paper about traffic engineering; it is a paper about whether broad default regulation changes behavior at scale in a politically salient domain with large externalities. The setting is attractive because the policy was sweeping, salient, and controversial, and because the welfare tradeoff—safety versus travel time and inconvenience—is exactly the kind of policy question economists should want credible evidence on.

### Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Reasonably well, but not optimally. The current opening leans heavily on general road-death statistics and the petition controversy. That makes the paper sound like a road-safety evaluation rather than a broader economics paper about the effectiveness of default rules and large-scale safety regulation. The second paragraph is cleaner, but the paper still undersells why this belongs in a general-interest economics journal.

### The pitch the paper should have

A stronger first two paragraphs would say something like:

> Governments often rely on default rules to change behavior at scale, yet we know surprisingly little about whether default-based regulation works when applied broadly rather than targeted to selected places. Urban speed limits are a leading example: lower limits are widely advocated on safety grounds, but existing evidence comes mostly from city-level or zone-level interventions where road redesign, enforcement, and policy selection are bundled together.  
>  
> This paper studies a rare nationwide change in the default urban speed limit: in September 2023, Wales lowered the default limit on restricted roads from 30mph to 20mph overnight, while England did not. Using police-reported collision data, I estimate that the reform reduced collisions on affected roads by about 15 percent and lowered serious casualties as well. The results suggest that changing a legal default can materially alter risky behavior even without redesigning the underlying infrastructure.

That is the AER version of the paper: not “here is a DiD on Welsh roads,” but “here is evidence on whether default regulation changes behavior at scale.”

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Urban speed is a major source of accident risk, but evidence on the benefits of lower limits comes mostly from localized interventions where speed limits, road redesign, enforcement, and local selection are tangled together.

### Tension

We do not know whether simply changing the default legal speed limit at scale—without redesigning roads everywhere—actually changes driver behavior enough to improve safety. Skeptics can easily believe that drivers ignore posted limits or that any effect in selected zones will not scale to a whole country.

### Resolution

Wales’s overnight nationwide shift from 30mph to 20mph provides evidence that such a default change did reduce collisions on affected roads, with some evidence of reductions in serious casualties too.

### Implications

Default-based regulation can matter even in a domain where compliance is politically contested and enforcement is imperfect. More broadly, legal defaults may be a scalable policy tool for addressing externalities, though the welfare case depends on the size of travel-time or convenience costs relative to safety gains.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the ingredients, but the arc is not fully disciplined. It currently reads as:

1. big road-safety problem,
2. Wales reform,
3. reduced-form estimates,
4. placebo,
5. severity-share wrinkle,
6. partial cost-benefit discussion.

That is serviceable, but it still feels somewhat like a collection of sensible empirical sections rather than a sharpened story. In particular, the “severity composition effect” is treated almost like a coequal contribution, when in fact it is a secondary interpretive result. It distracts from the stronger arc.

### What story should it be telling?

The paper should tell a simpler, stronger story:

- **Setup:** lower speed limits are popular but existing evidence is mostly local and bundled;
- **Tension:** economists do not know whether a nationwide default-rule change works on its own;
- **Resolution:** Wales provides rare evidence that it does, with sizable collision declines;
- **Implication:** legal defaults can shift risky behavior at scale, making them a serious policy tool, but the welfare case hinges on the cost side.

That story is much more coherent and much more AER-relevant.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?

“I’ve got a paper claiming that when Wales lowered the default urban speed limit from 30 to 20 nationwide, collisions on affected roads fell by about 15 percent.”

That is a decent lead fact. People will not reach for their phones immediately.

### Would people lean in?

Yes, initially. The policy is salient, controversial, and easy to understand. The setting is unusually clean at first pass. It has the virtue of being both concrete and generalizable.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Almost immediately: **“Okay, but how much did actual speeds and travel times change?”**

That is the central follow-up because economists want the tradeoff, not just one side of it. The second question would be: **“Is this really about lower speeds, or about something else that changed in Wales around the same time?”** Referees can handle the latter. Strategically, the first is the more important missing piece for journal fit.

### If the findings are modest: is that okay?

Yes, the findings are not too small to matter. A 15 percent collision decline is a real fact. The issue is not that the estimates are modest; it is that the paper has not fully converted them into an economically resonant claim. The current writeup is too eager to present significance details and too reluctant to own the broader policy question.

The null on fatalities is not a problem. Readers will accept that fatalities are too rare in this setting. The serious issue is whether the paper can say something disciplined about **the magnitude of benefits relative to costs**.
