**Idea Fidelity**

The paper largely pursues the core original idea from the manifest: it asks whether congressional oversight hearings are causally affected by competing news events using a large panel of hearings and pre-determined mega-events as instruments. It departs from the manifest’s specific implementation (the manifest envisioned closed-caption TV coverage counts for scandals, while the paper operationalizes salience via Google Trends and reduces the empirical strategy to a reduced-form/IV setup anchored on mega-event months). The divergence is acceptable as long as the causal channel stays focused on the competing-news mechanism, but the paper would benefit from explicitly acknowledging and justifying this shift from the initially proposed data sources (Internet Archive TV transcripts) to the substitutes used here.

**Summary**

The paper studies whether pre-determined mega-events (Olympics, impeachments, World Cups, royal events) affect the count of congressional oversight hearings attached to federal agencies from 2009–2024. It exploits this exogenous variation to test an Eisensee-Strömberg competing-news mechanism, finding no average effect but a striking asymmetry: mega-events increase hearings under divided government and decrease them under unified government. The interpretation is that the opposition uses media distraction to push hearings forward, while the majority takes advantage of distraction to scale back oversight.

**Essential Points**

1. **Threat to the identification strategy:** The reduced-form comparison relies on the assumption that mega-events only affect hearings through media displacement. Yet mega-events also co-occur with predictable political periods (Olympics tend to fall in August, which is already a low-hearing month, and impeachments by definition absorb congressional attention). The paper attempts to absorb seasonality with month fixed effects, but the heterogeneous bottom-line flips by government configuration (divided vs. unified) suggests that mega-event timing is correlated with political control in ways that violate the exclusion restriction. For example, the 2016 Rio Olympics and 2021 Tokyo Olympics overlap with different partisan regimes; splitting the sample by government structure risks conditioning on a collider (since mega-event timing and political control are both functions of the calendar). Without an event-study around each mega-event or some plausibly exogenous variation in mega-event timing uncorrelated with political control, the reduced-form asymmetry may reflect omitted political-cycle dynamics rather than media distraction.

2. **Weak link in the causal channel:** The IV specification that instruments “scandal interest” with competing-event intensity lacks strength and clarity. The binary mega-event instrument produces a wildly imprecise second stage (column 2 of Table 4) and the continuous version—while statistically significant—yields an implausibly large coefficient relative to the OLS estimate. This should raise concern that the first stage is weak and that the instrument may not isolate media displacement of scandals but other contemporaneous forces (e.g., shifts in legislative priorities during mega-events). The paper needs to report and discuss first-stage diagnostics more thoroughly, and to demonstrate that mega-events indeed suppress media coverage of agency scandals (perhaps using the proposed Google Trends data or alternative media measures).

3. **Mechanism and measurement gaps:** The paper claims that mega-events reduce scandal coverage and thus hearings, but the empirical evidence for the mechanism is thin. The Google Trends “agency scandal” index is only indirectly linked to coverage, and the paper never shows that mega-event months systematically depress these Google Trends indices. Without this confirmatory step, it is difficult to interpret the asymmetry—especially since divided government (tweet-line) may have intrinsically different hearing dynamics regardless of media coverage. Moreover, the matching of hearings to agencies via keyword overlap is not validated; if this matching performs differently across agencies or over time, the resulting noise could wash out effects. The paper should provide validation exercises (hand-coding samples, comparing to Congressional hearing calendars) and possibly use additional oversight measures (GAO reports or committee reports) to triangulate the findings.

**Suggestions**

1. **Strengthen the instrument and mechanism evidence.** Provide concrete evidence that mega-event months suppress actual media coverage of agency scandals. For instance, show that the Google Trends “agency scandal” index falls in Olympic months relative to nearby months; use TV transcript counts if available (per the original idea). This would document the first stage more transparently and tie the mechanism back to media displacement. If the first-stage remains weak, consider constructing a continuous measure of meg-event coverage intensity (minutes of Olympic coverage, impeachment airtime) that can be better linked to the dependent variables.

2. **Implement event-study designs around mega-events.** Instead of treating mega-events as simple binary indicators, estimate dynamic effects in the weeks/months before and after each event to test whether hearings are delayed rather than simply suppressed. This would also allow a better check on exclusion (if hearings dip only during the event month but rebound immediately afterwards, the mechanism becomes clearer). Stack the events in time and visualize the hearing response, ideally separately for divided vs. unified government, to see whether the asymmetry is not just an artifact of aggregating different calendar spans.

3. **Address potential confounding by political control.** Since the key heterogeneity hinges on divided/unified government, it would help to control directly for other time-varying partisan factors (e.g., majority party in each chamber, presence of an impeachment inquiry) and to interact those with the mega-event indicator. Use regression discontinuity methods around transitions (e.g., after midterms when control switches) if feasible. Alternatively, consider an instrumental variable for mega-event timing that is orthogonal to political control (e.g., Olympic host year on the calendars of subunits) to bypass the danger of colliding with partisan cycles.

4. **Validate and expand outcome measurement.** Show that the keyword matching algorithm reliably maps hearings to agencies—perhaps by comparing to official committee schedules or by hand-labeling a subset of hearings. Additionally, consider alternative oversight outcomes: the number of hearings where a given agency is the primary subject (not just mentioned), issuance of hearings transcripts, committee reports, or GAO investigations tied to each agency. This would help demonstrate that the effects are not driven by noisy counts or by a few high-profile agencies.

5. **Clarify the theoretical story and compare to alternate hypotheses.** The current interpretation is that the opposition exploits distraction under divided government, while the majority shirks under unified government. However, this could also be consistent with the opposition actively launching hearings during mega-event months because the majority lacks bandwidth; the paper should clarify what empirical signatures distinguish these stories. For example, check whether the committees controlling the hearings change (e.g., more minority-led committees, more oversight hearings announced by specific subcommittees) or whether the contents of hearings differ (e.g., focus on executive agencies vs. standard legislative topics). Incorporating textual analysis of hearing titles could help.

6. **Discuss implications for the broader accountability literature more explicitly.** The conclusion frames the finding as complicating the Eisensee-Strömberg framework, but the policy relevance would be clearer if the paper could say how much typical misconduct might escape oversight due to timing. Consider quantifying the aggregate discretion lost under unified government mega-event months and linking it to real policy costs (e.g., improper payment estimates per Ban & Hill). This would help readers assess the magnitude of the “asymmetric scandal lottery.”

By addressing these points, the paper can shore up the causal identification, strengthen its mechanism claims, and better connect the empirical results to the broader theory of media-induced accountability.
