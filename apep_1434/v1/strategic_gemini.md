## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
This paper examines how the "news cycle" dictates the intensity of Congressional oversight. By using pre-determined global mega-events (like the Olympics) as shocks to media attention, the author shows that federal agencies are not held accountable based on their failures alone, but on whether those failures happen to compete with a gold medal match for airtime—a phenomenon the author calls the "scandal timing lottery."

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this well, but it buries its most provocative finding: the "Asymmetric" part. The current intro spends too much time on the "lottery" (the null pooled effect) and not enough on the "asymmetry."

**Revised Pitch:** 
*Does the Olympics determine which federal agencies get investigated? While media distraction generally suppresses accountability, this paper finds a striking partisan asymmetry: under unified government, politicians use the cover of the news cycle to bury investigations into their own agencies, but under divided government, the opposition party strategically ramps up oversight during mega-events to bypass the majority’s gatekeeping.*

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**Contribution:** The paper demonstrates that the "competing news" effect on political accountability is not a universal law of media distraction but is strategically mediated by the partisan structure of government.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It is clearly differentiated from Eisensee & Strömberg (2007). That paper is about *passive* response (voters don't care, so aid drops); this is about *active* strategic behavior (the opposition party uses the distraction as a tactical window).
*   **The World vs. The Literature:** It frames itself as answering a question about the world (how agencies are held accountable). This is high-stakes and strong.
*   **What would make it bigger?** The contribution would be massive if it linked these "distracted" months to downstream outcomes like agency budget changes or rule-making activity. Currently, it stops at the hearing stage. Showing that "Olympic-month scandals" result in lower long-term budget cuts than "slow-news-month scandals" would turn this from a paper about *hearings* into a paper about the *entire state apparatus*.

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
The paper sits at the intersection of Political Economy (Oversight) and Media Economics.

*   **Neighbors:** Eisensee & Strömberg (2007); Snyder & Strömberg (2010) on media/accountability; McCubbins & Schwartz (1984) on "Police Patrols vs. Fire Alarms."
*   **Positioning:** It should "build and pivot" from Eisensee & Strömberg. The "scandal timing lottery" is the bridge, but the "partisan asymmetry" is the new house the author is building.
*   **Missing Conversations:** The paper needs to speak more to the "Strategic Communication" or "News Management" literature in Political Science (e.g., Groeling). There is a missed opportunity to connect to the "Friday News Dump" literature—this is essentially an *exogenous* Friday news dump.

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC
*   **Setup:** Oversight is the primary check on the "deep state."
*   **Tension:** Oversight requires media oxygen, but that oxygen is limited by a fixed news-carrying capacity.
*   **Resolution:** The "lottery" is real but partisan. The majority party hides behind the Olympics; the minority party uses the Olympics as a shield to launch attacks.
*   **Implications:** Institutional designs that rely on "fire alarm" oversight (triggered by media) are structurally biased and fragile during global events.

**Evaluation:** The narrative is excellent. It turns a "null result" (pooled) into a "feature, not a bug" (asymmetry).

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
*   **The Lead Fact:** "The Olympics actually *increase* oversight when Congress is divided, because the minority party can finally get a hearing scheduled when the majority thinks no one is watching."
*   **Reaction:** People will lean in. It’s counterintuitive. The "distraction = less oversight" story is the boring version. The "distraction = tactical window for the opposition" is the AER version.

---

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
*   **Front-loading:** Move the divided/unified government results (Table 4) much earlier. Table 2 (the null) is a "referee's table"—it proves the author did the work, but it’s not the story.
*   **Appendix:** The "Royal Wedding" and "Queen’s Death" events feel a bit thin. I’d suggest moving the sensitivity of the instrument (which events drive the result) to an appendix to keep the main text focused on the Olympics/World Cup (the truly exogenous ones).

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The gap is **Ambitious Scope.** 

The paper is technically competent and the story is "cute." But to move it from a "good JAERE/JPE" paper to an AER paper, the author needs to prove that this "lottery" has **teeth**. 

**Single biggest piece of advice:** Link the "distracted" hearings to a real-world agency outcome (e.g., subsequent-year budget appropriations or a reduction in "improper payments" following the Ban et al. 2023 style). If an agency escapes a hearing because of the Olympics, does it actually stay "corrupt" or "inefficient" longer? Prove that the news cycle changes the *trajectory of the bureaucracy*, not just the *calendar of the committee*.
