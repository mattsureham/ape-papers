### 1. Idea Fidelity
The paper deviates substantially from the original idea manifest. The manifest promised a causal test of TV news coverage of agency scandals (using Internet Archive's 3.97M TV News Corpus closed captions for agency x scandal keyword co-occurrences) on oversight, instrumented by competing mega-events (Olympics, impeachment), with event studies around specific scandals (e.g., VA 2014, FAA 2019), heterogeneity by majority/minority control, agency profile, and Fox vs. CNN composition, plus GAO reports and budgets. Instead, the paper uses Google Trends search interest ("[agency] scandal") as the endogenous salience measure, GovInfo hearings only (keyword-matched titles), a broader set of mega-events including royal weddings, no TV data, no event studies, no GAO/budgets, and heterogeneity only by divided/unified government and agency profile (no partisan media split). This shifts from media-driven causality to a reduced-form mega-event effect, missing the core novelty of TV transcript analysis and promised causal chain.

### 2. Summary
This paper examines whether pre-determined mega-events (e.g., Olympics) affect congressional oversight hearings of 19 federal agencies via displacing scandal coverage, using a competing-news reduced-form design in a 2009–2024 agency-month panel (N=3,648). The pooled effect on hearings is null, but mega-event months increase hearings by 2.6 under divided government while decreasing them by 2.8 under unified government. This asymmetry suggests opposition parties exploit distractions for oversight while majorities reduce it, complicating uniform media-displacement predictions.

### 3. Essential Points
**(1) Failure to demonstrate media displacement channel.** The paper claims an Eisensee-Strömberg design but never shows that mega-events reduce *agency-specific* media/scandal coverage using the promised TV corpus; instead, Google Trends "[agency] scandal" searches are instrumented with time-varying *competing-event* Trends (F-stats 4–10, weak instrument territory), yielding imprecise, sign-flipping IV estimates (e.g., Table 2). Reduced-form mega-effects are the focus, but without first-stage evidence of salience reduction *per agency*, the economic story (scandal lottery) lacks causal support—public searches may respond to hearings, not vice versa. Must validate channel with TV data or drop IV claims.

**(2) Implausible hearing-agency matching and outcome noise.** Keyword matching (e.g., "veterans affairs" → VA) on titles is error-prone (e.g., multi-agency hearings, generic terms like "EPA" in non-EPA contexts); mean=17 hearings/agency-month with SD=16 suggests massive noise or misclassification, as GovInfo's 46k hearings thinly spread over 19 agencies x 192 months imply ~1–2 true matches/month. No validation (e.g., manual audit, machine learning classifier) or alternative outcomes (GAO reports, as promised). Magnitudes (±2.6–2.8 hearings) are ~15% of mean but lack credibility without cleaning; any-hearing/IHS specs hint at extensive-margin effects, but counts dominate.

**(3) Instrument validity threats.** Mega-events mix pure shifters (Olympics, World Cup; fixed 7+ years ahead) with endogenous ones (impeachments, which correlate with partisan oversight incentives); robustness to Olympics-only shows negative effect (-1.7, Table 4), but pooled null relies on all events. Exclusion fails if impeachments proxy unified/divided shifts (e.g., Trump impeachments during divided periods). With only 19 agencies and quarter/year FEs, serial correlation in ε_at (e.g., recess overlap) may inflate SEs despite agency clustering; permutation p=0.70 confirms null but not mechanism.

### 4. Suggestions
**Data and measurement improvements (priority).** Implement the manifest's TV News Corpus: scrape Internet Archive/GDELT for agency-scandal mentions (e.g., "VA" + "scandal/wait times"), compute coverage intensity as shares or counts per agency-month, and directly regress on that (IV: mega-event displacement of total news slots). Validate hearing matches via (i) fuzzy string matching with agency lists/LLMs, (ii) manual sample of 500 titles, (iii) cross-validation with Congress.gov reports/GAO (add as outcomes). Use GDELT TV API for robustness (mentioned in manifest smoke test). For salience, average multiple queries (e.g., "[agency] failure/misconduct/scandal") and geo-restrict properly.

**Empirical design enhancements.** Add event studies around 5–10 named scandals (VA 2014, etc., as promised): plot hearings ±6 months vs. synthetic controls or mega-event timing. Test mechanism with (i) first-stage plots (agency-scandal coverage residuals vs. mega), (ii) heterogeneity by agency scandal-proneness (e.g., FEMA vs. NASA). Interact mega with lagged scandals (Google Trends spike >90th percentile). For asymmetry, define divided/unified precisely (e.g., table by Congress: 111th–118th) and test minority-led committees (e.g., chamber-specific hearings). Include Fox/CNN splits via GDELT channel metadata—opposition media may amplify under divided govt.

**Robustness and specification tweaks.** (i) Agency×year FEs to absorb trends (current year FEs insufficient for 19 agencies); (ii) two-way clustering (agency×month, as in Table 4 col 2, but extend to year-quarter); (iii) wild cluster bootstrap for small N_agencies=19; (iv) dynamic effects (leads/lags of mega); (v) placebo mega-events (shifted Olympics). Subperiods by media era (pre/post-2016). Control for recess dummies explicitly. For IV, use continuous mega-intensity (e.g., % news slots via GDELT) and weak-IV diagnostics (Anderson-Rubin CI).

**Magnitudes and interpretation.** Effects are plausible (±15% of mean hearings) and economically meaningful (SDEs ~0.13 moderate, Appendix Table 5), but clarify: 2.6 extra hearings/agency-month under divided govt = ~50 hearings/Congress/agency, potentially shifting ~1–2% of agency budgets if Ban-Hill (2023) causal effects hold. Add back-of-envelope: simulate scandal timing lottery under asymmetry (e.g., 15% mega-months → net +0.2 hearings/year). Discuss policy: strengthens case for non-media triggers (e.g., IG auto-referrals).

**Presentation and extensions.** Add figures: (i) timeline of mega-events vs. hearings (pooled/divided); (ii) binned scatter of residuals (salience vs. competing interest); (iii) parallel trends for heterogeneity. Shorten background; expand conclusion with institutional implications (e.g., minority rights reforms). Replicate Ban-Hill improper payments as long-run outcome.
