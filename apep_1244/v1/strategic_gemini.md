### 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether the introduction of the first US social safety net—Workers’ Compensation—distorted the labor market by inducing workers to take riskier jobs (a classic moral hazard story). Using newly available linked census data for 6.3 million men, the author finds a "precisely estimated null": workers did not flock to dangerous industries once insured. Instead, the safety net actually facilitated *exit* from hazardous work, suggesting that structural economic shifts, not marginal insurance subsidies, dictate career trajectories.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this clearly. The first two paragraphs successfully bridge the gap between a historical institutional change and a fundamental economic concept (moral hazard). It correctly identifies the "ladder" vs. "license" tension. No rewrite is needed; the pitch is AER-ready.

---

### 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution:** The paper provides the first individual-level, large-scale empirical rejection of the extensive-margin moral hazard hypothesis in the context of the Progressive Era's most significant social legislation.

- **Differentiation:** It is well-differentiated from Fishback & Kantor (aggregate data) and Gruber (employer-side). By using individual-level transitions, it solves the "ecological fallacy" that plagued earlier work.
- **Framing:** It is framed as a question about the **WORLD** (does social insurance change what jobs we choose?) rather than just a gap-filler. 
- **The "DiD" Trap:** A smart economist would not say "just another DiD." They would say "the paper that uses big data to debunk the idea that WC caused risky sorting."
- **Bigger Contribution:** To make it even larger, the author needs to lean harder into the "Exit" result. The finding that WC increased exit from mining/manufacturing is more interesting than the null on entry. It suggests insurance provides the "liquidity" or security needed to transition *out* of bad matches.

---

### 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

- **Closest Neighbors:** Fishback & Kantor (2000), Gruber (1994), Autor & Duggan (2003) on disability, and the "job-lock" literature (e.g., Bailey 2015).
- **Positioning:** It builds on Fishback/Kantor by refining their results but "attacks" the implicit assumption that worker-side sorting drove the aggregate injury spikes they found. 
- **Unexpected Conversation:** The paper should speak more to the **"Job Quality"** and **"Structural Transformation"** literatures. If insurance didn't drive sorting, but industrialization did, then the paper is really a commentary on the limits of policy relative to secular trends.

---

### 4. NARRATIVE ARC

- **Setup:** The rapid, staggered adoption of no-fault insurance in the 1910s.
- **Tension:** Theory predicts a "race to danger" (moral hazard), but aggregate data is ambiguous about whether workers or firms are responsible for rising injuries.
- **Resolution:** A precise null on entry. Workers weren't chasing insurance into the mines.
- **Implications:** Social insurance design should worry less about occupational "distortions" and more about firm-level safety incentives.

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong. It’s a "detective story" where the prime suspect (worker moral hazard) is proven to have an alibi (structural transformation).

---

### 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

- **Lead Fact:** "When the US introduced Workers' Comp, not a single extra person moved into a dangerous job because of the insurance." 
- **Reaction:** Lean in. It counter-intuitively suggests that for major life decisions (like career choice), the "safety net" is too small a signal to override the "structural noise."
- **Follow-up:** "Then why did injury rates go up?" (The paper answers: firm behavior or effort on the job).

---

### 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

- **Front-loading:** The paper is well-structured. However, the **Exit Probability** result (page 7) is buried. This should be a main-table headline result, not a secondary "check."
- **Appendix:** Some of the heterogeneity (race/literacy) could move to an appendix to keep the focus on the "Structural vs. Insurance" narrative.

---

### 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The gap between this and a "Safe" Econ History paper is **ambition in the mechanism.**

To excite the top 10 people in the field, the author needs to prove the **"Why."** Is it because the wage premia for risk were already high? Or because workers were "locked" into industries by geography? 

**Single most impactful advice:** Elevate the "Exit" result to a primary finding and use it to argue that social insurance acts as a "buffer" that allows workers to seek *better* matches, rather than a "magnet" for *risky* ones. This flips the paper from a "Null result on moral hazard" to a "Positive result on labor market fluidity."
