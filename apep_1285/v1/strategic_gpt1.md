## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple, policy-relevant question: when banking secrecy ends in the world’s largest offshore financial center, does the returning wealth spill into domestic housing markets and bid up real estate prices? Using regional variation within Switzerland around the 2017 AEOI reform, the paper argues that the answer is no: wealth-management hubs such as Zurich and Geneva did not experience a relative housing-price boom.

A busy economist should care because this is a clean test of a widely invoked but rarely tested mechanism linking tax enforcement, offshore wealth, capital reallocation, and asset prices. If true, it would matter for how we think about the domestic consequences of anti-evasion policy and the broader incidence of financial transparency reforms.

Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs? **Almost, but not quite.** The current opening is competent, but it is too inward-looking and too quick to slide into design. It opens with Switzerland and AEOI, but it does not sufficiently foreground the broader economic question: **what happens to hidden wealth when secrecy ends, and does it fuel local asset markets?** The introduction should lead with the world question and only then bring in Switzerland as the unusually sharp laboratory.

### The pitch the paper should have

> Governments around the world are dismantling offshore secrecy, but we know little about where the affected wealth goes once it is exposed. A common concern is that repatriated wealth will simply be redirected into domestic real estate, raising housing prices even as tax evasion falls.  
>   
> This paper studies that hypothesis in the strongest possible setting: Switzerland, historically the world’s largest offshore financial center, after the 2017 Automatic Exchange of Information reform. Comparing Swiss regions with different exposure to the wealth-management industry, I find no evidence that banking centers such as Zurich and Geneva experienced a relative real estate boom. The broader implication is that financial transparency reforms need not mechanically translate into domestic housing inflation.

That is the AER pitch. Right now the paper is close, but it still reads a bit like “here is a null DiD on Swiss house prices” rather than “here is a test of a first-order mechanism in the political economy of offshore wealth.”

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper’s contribution is to show that the end of Swiss banking secrecy did **not** generate a relative increase in housing prices in Swiss wealth-management centers, challenging the common presumption that offshore wealth repatriation flows mechanically into domestic real estate.

### Is the contribution clearly differentiated from the closest papers?

**Partially, but not sharply enough.** The paper names a few literatures, but the differentiation is still somewhat generic. It says, in effect:

- papers on tax transparency show offshore deposits fall;
- papers on capital inflows show house prices rise;
- this paper studies whether repatriation raises house prices.

That is directionally right, but the introduction needs a crisper statement of novelty relative to specific neighbors:

1. **Tax transparency / offshore wealth papers** document effects on declarations, deposits, and compliance.
2. **Foreign capital and housing papers** document that cross-border inflows can raise real estate prices.
3. **This paper** tests whether those two facts imply a domestic housing transmission channel from anti-secrecy reforms.

That logical bridge is the novelty. Right now it is there, but it is not presented as a sharp unresolved question.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a gap in a literature?

At present it is **mixed**, with too much “this contributes to three literatures.” That is usually a warning sign. The stronger version is clearly a **world question**:

- When hidden offshore wealth is forced into the open, does it show up in domestic asset markets?
- More specifically: does transparency reform reallocate capital into housing?

That is stronger than “the domestic consequences of wealth repatriation remain understudied.”

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the intro?

Right now, they might say: **“It’s a DiD on Swiss regional house prices around AEOI, and it finds no effect.”** That is not enough. You want them to say:

**“It tests a widely assumed transmission mechanism from anti-tax-evasion policy to housing markets, in the most relevant country, and finds the mechanism basically absent.”**

That is much better.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Several possibilities:

1. **Broader asset-market comparison.**  
   The biggest limitation of the current framing is that the paper only shows where the money did **not** go. The discussion says it likely stayed in financial assets, but that is speculative. The contribution would be materially bigger if the paper could compare real estate to some proximate financial-market outcome, even descriptively. The obvious next question is: if not housing, then what?

2. **Sharper mechanism or margin.**  
   The most persuasive version would distinguish between:
   - owner-occupied housing,
   - luxury/high-end property,
   - investment property,
   - local banking-center property vs amenity destinations.
   
   If the theory is really about wealthy households reallocating portfolios, average regional apartment indices may be too blunt an object. A more targeted outcome—luxury transactions, high-end urban property, second-home markets—would make the contribution more ambitious.

3. **Stronger external framing.**  
   The paper should be less about Swiss banking geography per se and more about a general prediction from models of tax enforcement and portfolio substitution. Switzerland is the setting; the real question is about the incidence of transparency reforms.

4. **A more explicit asymmetry claim.**  
   The interesting conceptual claim is not just “null result,” but perhaps **foreign capital inflows into housing are not the mirror image of domestic wealth repatriation**. That asymmetry is potentially important and publishable if argued carefully.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Before this paper, we know that transparency reforms reduce the attractiveness of offshore secrecy and can induce disclosure or outflows from offshore centers. We also know that capital inflows can inflate real estate prices. Policymakers therefore worry that anti-secrecy policy may have an unintended side effect: pushing wealth into domestic property.

### Tension

The tension is straightforward and good: **we observe the policy shock and the offshore response, but we do not know the domestic destination of the wealth.** Does it end up in housing, or not? Switzerland is the ideal test case because if the mechanism exists anywhere, it ought to show up there.

### Resolution

The paper’s answer is: **no sustained real estate boom in Swiss banking hubs after AEOI.** The feared housing channel does not appear in the aggregate regional data.

### Implications

The implications should be:
- the domestic incidence of financial transparency reform may differ from popular fears;
- repatriated wealth is not simply the mirror image of foreign speculative inflows into property;
- policymakers should rethink where the wealth actually goes.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

**Serviceable, but not fully earned.** The raw ingredients are there, but the paper still feels somewhat like a collection of estimates organized around a null. The story is not yet as tight as it should be.

The key missing piece is that the paper needs to lean harder into the central puzzle:

> If secrecy reform caused large balance-sheet adjustment, why don’t we see it in the most obvious domestic asset market?

That puzzle gives the null bite.

Right now, too much of the narrative energy is spent convincing the reader that the coefficient is null, and not enough is spent elevating why this null changes how we think about offshore wealth, asset demand, and transparency policy.

### What story should it be telling?

Not “I estimated a regional DiD and found no effect.”

Instead:

**“A common political-economy fear about anti-evasion policy is wrong, at least in the canonical case. Even when secrecy collapses in the global epicenter of offshore wealth, the money does not visibly flood domestic housing markets.”**

That is the story.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party?

I would say:

**“When Switzerland effectively ended banking secrecy and tens of billions left Swiss banks, Zurich and Geneva did not experience a relative housing-price boom.”**

That is the best fact in the paper.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

**Some would lean in, but many would only do so if the framing is sharper.** In its current form, the paper sounds like a careful null result in a small-sample regional design. In a seminar room that may hold attention; at an AER editorial level it needs to sound like a broader lesson about where hidden wealth goes.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Immediately:

**“If not housing, where did the money go?”**

That is the paper’s most important downstream question, and currently it can only speculate. That is a strategic weakness because the null invites exactly that question.

### Is the null itself interesting?

**Yes, but only if the paper sells the prior correctly.** A null result is interesting when:
1. the prior belief was strong,
2. the setting is highly powered conceptually,
3. the null meaningfully updates beliefs.

This paper has (1) and (2) in principle. It needs to do more to establish them in the introduction. The reader must come away believing that Switzerland is the **most likely place** to observe the feared mechanism. Then the absence of a boom becomes informative rather than anticlimactic.

At present the paper is reasonably good at saying “there was concern,” but not yet strong enough at showing that economists should have genuinely expected an effect.
