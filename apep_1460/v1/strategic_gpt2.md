## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper studies a rare policy event: Poland first raised, then reversed, its statutory retirement age, creating an unusual opportunity to compare labor supply responses to increases versus decreases in retirement eligibility. The headline claim is that lowering the retirement age caused a much larger employment response than the original increase, suggesting that retirement ages function as powerful social reference points rather than symmetric financial incentives.

Why should a busy economist care? Because nearly every aging society is grappling with pension-age reform, and this paper asks a first-order question about policy reversibility: if governments raise retirement ages and later backtrack, do workers simply retrace their prior behavior, or do reversals trigger disproportionately large exits?

### Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Mostly yes, but not optimally. The introduction gets to the core question quickly, which is good. But it leans too fast into the “retirement ratchet” label and lifecycle-model symmetry claim before fully establishing the broader economic stakes. The paper should spend less time announcing the catchy concept and more time making the reader feel the importance of the world question: whether pension reforms are behaviorally reversible.

### The pitch the paper should have

“Governments across Europe are raising retirement ages to cope with population aging, but we know almost nothing about what happens if those reforms are later undone. Poland offers a uniquely informative case: after gradually increasing the retirement age, it abruptly restored earlier eligibility, allowing us to compare the labor supply effects of raising versus lowering the retirement threshold. We show that the reversal caused much larger employment exits than the original increase generated, implying that retirement-age policy is not behaviorally symmetric and may be far less reversible than standard models suggest.”

That is the AER version of this paper. The current opening is close, but not yet disciplined enough around that one big idea.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper claims to provide the first evidence that lowering a statutory retirement age can reduce employment more strongly than a prior increase raised it, implying asymmetric labor supply responses to pension-age policy.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?

Only partly. The paper names a lot of papers, but the differentiation is still too generic: most of the current positioning is “previous work studies retirement-age increases; I study a reversal.” That is a factual distinction, but not yet a fully intellectual one. The real differentiator is not just “a reversal exists,” but “this setting lets us ask whether retirement-age reforms are behaviorally reversible and whether statutory ages act as asymmetric reference points.”

Right now the paper risks sounding like:
- another pension age paper,
- in another country,
- with another quasi-experimental design.

That is not enough for AER.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a gap in a literature?

It is framed partly as a world question, which is good, but it slips too often into literature-gap language: “no modern economy has reversed…” “first evidence on lowering…” etc. The stronger framing is world-facing:

- Are retirement-age reforms reversible?
- Do workers respond more strongly to restored eligibility than to withdrawn eligibility?
- Are statutory retirement ages social focal points rather than just incentive schedules?

That is much stronger than “the literature has not studied reversals.”

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?

They could probably say: “It’s about Poland reversing a retirement-age increase, and the reversal reduced women’s employment a lot.” But I am less confident they would say: “This paper shows pension-age reforms are asymmetric and behaviorally hard to reverse.” In other words, the empirical event is clearer than the conceptual contribution.

At present, some readers will summarize it as “a DiD paper on a pension reform reversal in Poland,” which is not where you want to be.

### What would make the contribution bigger?

Most importantly: sharpen the comparison from “effect of reversal” to “reversibility of pension reform.” That is the big question.

Specific ways to make the contribution feel larger:
1. **Center the asymmetry comparison much more forcefully.**  
   The paper’s main intellectual asset is not the treatment effect alone; it is the comparison between the raise and the reversal. Right now that comparison arrives as a later table, and even there the interpretation is muddied by the awkward sign discussion. The whole paper should be built around that asymmetry.

2. **Develop the mechanism as a contest between interpretations.**  
   “Reference point” is plausible, but currently too asserted. The paper would feel bigger if it explicitly framed the result as discriminating between:
   - symmetric incentive models,
   - friction/inertia models,
   - reference-point/social-norm models.  
   Even without new identification, the framing can be more theory-facing.

3. **Add implications beyond employment rates.**  
   Not in a referee sense, but strategically: a top-field audience will ask whether the paper speaks to fiscal costs, claiming behavior, retirement timing, or gender inequality. Even a simple back-of-the-envelope on aggregate employment loss or pension-system burden could make the stakes feel more macro/public-finance relevant.

4. **Lean into the gender angle only if it is elevated to a broader question.**

At the moment, oddly, both.

It may also underplay:

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Many countries are raising retirement ages in response to aging populations. Existing evidence shows that increasing eligibility ages delays retirement and raises employment among older workers.

### Tension

But we do not know whether these reforms are behaviorally reversible. If governments later lower the retirement age, do workers simply retrace the earlier path symmetrically, or do restored entitlements trigger a much stronger response? This matters for both theory and policy design.

### Resolution

Poland’s abrupt 2017 reversal provides a rare natural experiment. The paper finds that the drop in women’s employment after the reversal was substantially larger than the employment increase associated with the original reform.

### Implications

Statutory retirement ages appear to work as focal points/reference points, not merely prices; reform reversals may therefore undo labor supply gains rapidly and disproportionately. Pension reform may be much less reversible in behavioral terms than in legislative terms.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the ingredients, but the arc is not fully disciplined. There is a strong story available here, but the paper keeps drifting between three stories:

1. a Poland reform paper,
2. a reference-points paper,
3. a pension-policy warning paper.

It needs to choose one main story and subordinate the others. The best choice is:

**Main story:** pension reform is behaviorally asymmetric and hard to reverse.  
**Secondary interpretation:** this pattern is consistent with retirement-age thresholds serving as reference points.  
**Institutional vehicle:** Poland is the rare setting that lets us observe this.

Right now the “retirement ratchet” phrase is doing too much conceptual work too early. The paper should earn that label through the narrative, not announce it as a brand before the reader is convinced.

### What fact would I lead with at dinner?

That is a good lead fact.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

Moderate lean-in. Among labor/public economists, yes, because reversals are genuinely rare and the asymmetry question is interesting. Among general-interest economists, the current presentation may still feel narrower than it should. The event is intriguing, but the paper needs to make clearer why this changes how we think about policy design generally.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Almost certainly:  
“Is this really asymmetry in behavior, or just asymmetry in implementation? The increase was gradual; the decrease was immediate.”

That is the obvious question, and strategically the paper must confront it more directly in the framing. It currently acknowledges treatment-intensity differences, but the reader is still left thinking that the “ratchet” may simply be a gradual-versus-sharp policy contrast rather than a deeper behavioral asymmetry. Even if the estimates survive normalization, the paper needs a crisper conceptual response.

A second likely question:
“Is this about reference points, or just about access to a pension check?”

That too must be handled as a framing issue. You do not need referee-level mechanism proof in the memo, but as an editor I would say the paper should sound more modest and precise: the evidence shows asymmetry in reduced-form responses; reference points are an interpretation, not yet the sole explanation.

### If findings are modest or null?

Not applicable—the main effect is not null. The issue is not whether the result is interesting; it is whether the paper is extracting the maximum intellectual value from the result.
