### 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper exploits a unique "natural experiment" in Poland—the only modern EU country to significantly increase and then immediately reverse its retirement age—to test whether labor supply responses to pension eligibility are symmetric. It finds a "retirement ratchet": the employment decline from lowering the retirement age is nearly twice as large as the gain from raising it, suggesting that statutory ages act as powerful, asymmetric reference points rather than simple financial thresholds.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this very clearly. The first two paragraphs of the introduction do an excellent job of moving from the specific Polish event to the universal economic question (symmetry in lifecycle labor supply). No rewrite is needed; the author has successfully signaled that this is a "big idea" paper using a specific case study.

---

### 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:** The paper provides the first causal evidence of an asymmetric labor supply response to retirement age changes, demonstrating that "releasing" workers from a pension constraint triggers a much larger behavioral shift than "imposing" a higher age.

- **Differentiation:** It is well-differentiated. While Seibold (2021) and others establish that retirement ages are reference points, they look at one-way movements (increases). This paper is the first to test the *reversibility* of those reference points.
- **World vs. Literature:** It frames the question around a world problem (aging societies, policy reversibility) which makes it high-stakes.
- **AER standard:** A smart economist would say: "It’s a paper showing that once you move the retirement goalposts, moving them back causes a much bigger exodus than the original move prevented." That is a "sticky" finding.
- **Bigger Contribution:** The paper currently uses aggregate (cell-level) Eurostat data. The contribution would be exponentially bigger—and a "shoo-in" for the AER—if it used individual-level administrative data (ZUS) to distinguish between voluntary retirement and employer-side "push-outs."

---

### 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

- **Closest Neighbors:** Seibold (2021, *AER*), Gruber & Wise (1999), Staubli & Zweimüller (2013), and Manoli & Weber (2016).
- **Strategy:** The paper builds on the "Reference Point" literature. It should position itself as the "stress test" for the reference point theory: if these ages are truly focal points, the "ratchet" effect is the smoking gun.
- **Missing Conversations:** The paper could engage more with the **Hysteresis** literature. Is this a permanent shift in norms? It also touches on the **Gender Gap** in pensions but could more forcefully link to the literature on how pension design exacerbates old-age poverty for women.

---

### 4. NARRATIVE ARC

- **Setup:** Global trend of raising retirement ages to combat aging demographics.
- **Tension:** Standard models assume symmetry (if +1 year delays work by X, -1 year should accelerate it by X). But if behavior is reference-dependent or labor markets are frictional, this breaks.
- **Resolution:** Poland reversed its reform, providing the perfect "undo" button. The result is a massive, asymmetric employment collapse.
- **Implications:** Pension reforms are "one-way doors." Undoing them is disproportionately "expensive" in terms of lost human capital.

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong. It feels like a complete story.

---

### 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

- **Lead fact:** "Poland reversed its retirement age increase, and the employment rate for women fell twice as fast as it had risen when the age was first raised."
- **Reaction:** People would lean in. It is counter-intuitive to the "rational agent" model and has massive fiscal implications.
- **Follow-up:** "Was it the workers' choice, or did firms use the lower age to fire older women?" This is the question the author needs to answer (or acknowledge more deeply) to survive the referees.

---

### 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The science is there, and the setting is "gold-standard" for a natural experiment. The gap is **mechanism**. Using aggregate Eurostat data is "clean" but "thin." 

To satisfy the AER’s appetite for deep causal mechanisms, the author needs to prove *why* the ratchet exists. Is it **Search Frictions** (once you're out, you can't get back in)? Or **Social Norms** (60 is "the age," and any year after feels like a "loss")? 

**Single biggest improvement:** Incorporate (or at least proxy for) micro-level evidence to rule out employer-side "forced" retirement as the driver of the asymmetry. If the author can show this is a *supply-side* preference shift, it’s a landmark behavioral labor paper.
