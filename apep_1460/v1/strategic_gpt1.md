## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper studies a rare policy event: Poland first raised the statutory retirement age and then reversed that reform, suddenly making many older women newly eligible for pensions. The paper’s central claim is that labor supply responds asymmetrically to these changes: lowering the retirement age induces exits much more strongly than raising it delays them, which matters for how economists think about retirement behavior and for how governments should evaluate the reversibility of pension reform.

The paper **does articulate something close to this pitch early**, but not as cleanly or credibly as it should. The first two paragraphs get to the right question quickly, but the paper then overreaches into a strong theoretical claim (“standard lifecycle model predicts symmetry”) and a branded concept (“retirement ratchet”) before the reader is convinced the empirical comparison is conceptually apples-to-apples. The introduction would be stronger if it led with the unusual world fact and the policy stakes, then framed the asymmetry question more cautiously.

### The pitch the paper should have

“Retirement reforms are usually studied when governments raise pension ages. But an equally important question is what happens when those reforms are undone. Poland offers a uniquely informative case: after gradually increasing the retirement age, it abruptly reversed course in 2017, generating a large and immediate wave of pension claiming among older women.

This paper asks whether labor supply responses to retirement-age changes are symmetric. I show that the employment decline following Poland’s sudden eligibility expansion was much larger than the employment increase associated with the earlier, gradual tightening. The broader implication is that statutory retirement ages may operate less like smooth financial incentives and more like salient retirement thresholds, making pension reform easier to dismantle than to build.”

That is the AER-facing story. It is about the world: **when governments relax retirement eligibility, people exit fast; when they tighten it, adjustment is weaker and slower.**

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper’s contribution is to use Poland’s unusual raise-then-reverse pension reform sequence to argue that employment responses to statutory retirement ages are asymmetric, with lowering the age producing much larger labor-supply effects than raising it.

### Is this clearly differentiated from the closest papers?

Only partially. The paper distinguishes itself mechanically by saying “no modern economy has reversed a retirement age increase,” which is useful and likely true enough to be memorable. But the substantive differentiation from the nearest literature is still muddy.

Right now, the paper sits too close to several existing literatures without clearly staking out where it departs:

- from the retirement-age reforms literature, it looks like another reduced-form paper showing employment moves around eligibility thresholds;
- from the reference-point literature, it looks like an application rather than a conceptual advance;
- from the Poland-specific policy literature, it risks reading as a country case study.

The asymmetry angle is the real contribution. But the current draft does not yet make the reader feel that the asymmetry comparison is the paper, rather than one result among several.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or filling a gap in a LITERATURE?

It is mixed, but too often framed as a literature gap: “no one has studied reversals,” “first evidence on lowering retirement age,” etc. That is fine as supporting language, but the stronger framing is about the world:

- Are retirement-age reforms reversible in behavioral terms?
- Do statutory ages work as focal points rather than smooth prices?
- Does restoring eligibility unleash pent-up exits in a way tightening does not mirror?

Those are first-order world questions. The paper should lean harder into them.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?

At present, they could say something like: “It’s a DiD on Poland’s retirement age reversal showing older women exited employment when eligibility was restored.” That is decent, but not yet enough.

You want them instead to say: “It uses the only clean modern reversal of a retirement-age increase to show that eligibility expansions can unwind labor supply much faster than eligibility contractions build it.”

That is a much sharper and more memorable novelty claim.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Most importantly: **make the asymmetry comparison more conceptually central and more persuasive.** Right now, the paper compares a gradual reform to an abrupt reversal and interprets the difference as behavioral asymmetry. That is plausible, but the story currently bundles together at least three things:

1. direction of change,
2. speed of implementation,
3. breadth/intensity of exposure.

To make the contribution bigger, the paper should sharpen which of these it is about.

Specific ways to do that:

- **Frame the paper as about reversals of social-insurance reforms**, not just retirement age thresholds. That broadens the audience from pension economists to political economy and public finance.
- **Show dynamics more prominently.** If the central fact is “rapid exit upon restored eligibility,” then an event-study style figure of the reversal period is more important than multiple tables. Even without deeper causal discussion, the narrative benefit is huge.
- **Expand outcomes conceptually.** Employment is fine, but the story would feel larger if the paper foregrounded transitions into pension claiming/inactivity as the central phenomenon and employment as the labor-market manifestation. Right now the ZUS claim spike is the most vivid fact in the paper, but it is treated almost as scene-setting rather than core evidence.
- **Tone down the pure reference-point claim unless mechanism evidence is added.** The paper becomes bigger if it is framed as a broad fact about salience/eligibility/focal points, rather than a narrower and harder-to-prove claim about reference dependence per se.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

### Closest neighbors

The closest neighbors appear to be:

- **Mastrobuoni (2009)** on labor supply responses to retirement age reforms.
- **Manoli and Weber (2016/2017-type retirement claiming papers)** on sharp responses around pension eligibility.
- **Cribb, Emmerson, and Tetlow (2016)** on state pension age increases in the UK.
- **Staubli and Zweimüller (2013)** on raising early retirement age in Austria.
- **Seibold (2021)** on retirement as a reference point / bunching around statutory ages.
- Also relevant: **Atalay and Barrett / Atalay et al.** on Australia, **Vestad** on Norway, **Hanel and Riphahn** on Switzerland, **Börsch-Supan and related reform work** in Germany/Europe.

### How should it position itself relative to them?

Mostly **build on them**, not attack them.

The paper should say:

- prior work convincingly shows that retirement behavior responds to statutory ages and pension eligibility;
- prior work mostly studies **tightening** reforms;
- this paper adds a missing margin: **what happens when the government gives eligibility back**;
- the reversal setting lets us ask whether the response is symmetric.

That is a natural extension. There is no need for adversarial positioning.

### Is the paper positioned too narrowly or too broadly?

Oddly, both.

- **Too narrowly** in that it sometimes reads like a Poland pension reform case study for labor/public-finance specialists.
- **Too broadly** when it leaps from one country episode to sweeping claims about lifecycle models, reference dependence, and reform irreversibility.

The right middle ground is: this is a highly unusual quasi-experimental policy episode that reveals a broader behavioral fact about retirement eligibility.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?

Two areas feel underdeveloped:

1. **Political economy of policy reversals / reform durability.**  
   The paper’s most interesting policy implication is not just “retirement ages matter,” but “reversals may undo labor supply gains quickly.” That connects to literature on reform persistence, credibility, and dynamic political incentives. The current draft barely exploits that.

2. **Social norms / focal points / institutional salience beyond reference dependence.**  
   The reference-point literature is one hook, but the paper might fit even better in work on institutional thresholds as coordinating devices. A broader behavioral-public-finance framing may travel better than a tight commitment to one mechanism.

Possibly also:

- female labor supply and pension design,
- gendered retirement rules,
- reform announcements versus implementation,
- policy credibility and anticipation.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?

Not quite. It is having a respectable conversation with the pension-reform literature, but the most impactful version would connect **three conversations**:

1. retirement and pension eligibility,
2. behavioral responses to salient policy thresholds,
3. durability and reversibility of social-insurance reform.

That third conversation is where the paper could become more than a competent reform evaluation.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Governments across Europe are raising retirement ages in response to aging populations. The empirical literature generally finds that higher eligibility ages keep people in work longer, and policy discussions often treat these effects as roughly reversible.

### Tension

But almost no one observes what happens when such reforms are undone. If retirement ages are just prices/incentives, upward and downward changes should have roughly symmetric effects. If they are salient social thresholds or focal points, reversals may trigger much faster exits than reforms induce delayed retirement.

### Resolution

Poland’s abrupt 2017 reversal generated a large drop in employment among women newly restored to pension eligibility, and that drop appears larger than the employment response associated with the earlier gradual increase.

### Implications

Pension reforms may be behaviorally fragile: governments can lose labor supply quickly by restoring eligibility, even if earlier tightening took years to build employment gains. More broadly, statutory ages may operate as powerful retirement norms rather than merely budget constraints.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the ingredients of a strong arc, but not yet the discipline. At times it feels like:

- an interesting descriptive fact (357,000 claims),
- plus a treatment-effect paper,
- plus a reference-point paper,
- plus a policy-warning paper,

without fully deciding which one it is.

The story it **should** be telling is:

> “This is the first clean evidence on the behavioral consequences of undoing a retirement-age reform. The striking fact is not merely that employment fell after eligibility was restored; it is that the unwind was faster and larger than the original build-up. That reveals something important about how retirement institutions shape behavior.”

That is the narrative spine. Everything else should serve it.

One caution: the paper’s current asymmetry section is narratively vulnerable because the earlier reform estimate comes in with a sign that requires substantial explanation. Even if the explanation is ultimately satisfactory, it weakens the elegance of the arc. A strong narrative cannot rest on a comparison the reader experiences as interpretively messy. That is a framing problem even before it is an econometrics problem.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?

“Poland lowered the retirement age in 2017 and within two quarters more than 350,000 new pension claims were filed; employment among women 60–64 dropped sharply, and the decline appears larger than the employment gains from the earlier increase in the retirement age.”

That is vivid. People will listen.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

They would **lean in initially**, because the policy event is genuinely unusual and concrete. But they may drift if the presentation quickly turns into “cross-country DD, cross-country DDD, within-Poland DD...” The identification menu is not the selling point. The selling point is the reversal and the asymmetry.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Probably one of these:

- “Is this really asymmetry, or just the difference between an abrupt reform and a gradual one?”
- “Does this show reference dependence, or simply immediate access to pension income?”
- “How much of the response is a one-time stock adjustment versus a permanent flow effect?”
- “Why should I think Poland generalizes?”

Those are exactly the questions the paper needs to anticipate rhetorically, even if some are ultimately for referees.

### If the findings are modest or null

They are not null. The problem is not modesty; the problem is interpretation. The effect is large enough to be interesting. The challenge is making the reader believe the paper has learned a broad fact rather than documented one dramatic but idiosyncratic policy unwind.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The biggest gap is **not raw topic quality**. The topic is promising. The gap is that the paper currently feels like a sharp, unusual policy episode in search of a sufficiently big economic lesson.

### What is the main problem?

Mostly a **framing problem**, with some **ambition problem**.

- **Framing problem:** The paper has not fully decided whether it is about Poland, about retirement-age reversals, about asymmetric labor supply, or about reference points. It wants all four, but top papers usually have one dominant idea.
- **Ambition problem:** The evidence is used to support a fairly strong behavioral claim, but the paper does not yet broaden the conceptual payoff enough. “Asymmetric responses to a reversal” is interesting; “policy reversals can unwind labor supply faster than reforms build it” is bigger.
- **Novelty problem:** There is novelty in the event, but the empirical machinery feels familiar. That is fine if the world fact is striking enough. The paper needs to convince the reader that it is.
- **Scope problem:** Somewhat. The paper would feel more consequential if it developed the stock-versus-flow interpretation, the gender angle, or the reform-durability implication more explicitly.

### What is the gap between current form and an AER paper?

An AER paper here would make readers feel they learned a new general principle:

> Restoring access to social-insurance benefits can have disproportionately large behavioral effects because statutory thresholds serve as coordinating institutions, not just prices.

This draft is not yet there. It is still closer to:

> Here is an interesting case where employment fell a lot after a retirement age reversal, and maybe more than it rose before.

That is publishable somewhere, but not yet AER-level in strategic positioning.

### Single most impactful piece of advice

**Reframe the paper around reform reversibility rather than around a branded “retirement ratchet”: make the central claim that undoing pension-age reforms can unwind labor supply faster than tightening created it, and treat reference dependence as one possible interpretation rather than the headline theorem.**

That one change would do several things at once:

- enlarge the audience,
- make the paper more world-facing,
- reduce overclaiming on mechanism,
- and give the Poland episode broader policy relevance.
