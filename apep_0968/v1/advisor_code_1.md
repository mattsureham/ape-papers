**Idea Fidelity**

The paper faithfully pursues the idea laid out in the manifest. It leverages the USDA SNAP Policy Database to construct continuous measures of recertification intensity, identifies the 26 integrated eligibility system (IES) states via the KFF classification, and links these to state-month Medicaid enrollment volatility from CMS. The core identification strategy—a DiD-like interaction between SNAP recertification intensity and IES status, with state and month fixed effects—is present, as are robustness exercises (non-IES placebo, pre-COVID subsample, randomization inference). The proposed mechanism—administrative resource competition within IES—receives empirical attention through application counts and expansion-status heterogeneity. **Summary**

This paper documents an unintended spillover of SNAP administrative intensity onto Medicaid stability in states operating integrated eligibility systems. Exploiting state-level variation in SNAP recertification intensity (share of households on ≤6-month recertification) and interacting it with IES status in a state-month panel, the authors find that short-cycle recertification increases Medicaid enrollment volatility in integrated states by about 0.24 percentage points per 10 pp of short-cycle share while reducing volatility in non-integrated states. Robustness checks (placebo, pre-COVID, permutation inference) and mechanism probes (applications, expansion heterogeneity) support the interpretation that shared administrative infrastructure transmits SNAP shocks into Medicaid churn.

**Essential Points**

1. **Causal Interpretation of the Interaction**: The paper treats the interaction coefficient as identifying the “administrative contagion” effect, but the DiD analogy is only valid if recertification intensity is exogenous and the differential response between IES and non-IES states is driven by shared infrastructure rather than other state-specific shocks correlated with both SNAP policy and Medicaid volatility. The paper does not convincingly show that recertification intensity changes are uncorrelated with other time-varying administrative decisions or unobserved shocks that might affect IES and non-IES states differently. Essential additional evidence is needed (e.g., event-study showing parallel trends in the absence of recertification intensity variation, or falsification tests using outcomes that should not be affected by shared infrastructure). Without such evidence, the credibility of the interaction as causal is limited.

2. **Measurement of the Outcome and Mechanism**: The main dependent variable is the absolute month-over-month percent change in total Medicaid enrollment. This metric conflates administrative churn with genuine eligibility-driven fluctuations. The mechanism (resource competition creating delays) would instead predict lapses or gaps rather than absolute changes that include reversals. Without distinguishing between enrollment drops due to administrative delay and natural adjustments, the interpretation that the interaction reflects “contagion” is ambiguous. Consider using Medicaid enrollment gaps (e.g., drops followed by recoveries), claims submission lags from T-MSIS, or measures of re-enrollment delays to better capture administrative processing disruptions.

3. **Role of COVID Waivers and Time Variation**: The paper leans on the pre-COVID subsample and the March 2020 blanket waivers to argue against pandemic confounding, but it still pools the entire 2018–2020 period. SNAP recertification intensity is likely sticky, yet the waiver period (March 2020 onward) dramatically altered administrative behavior simultaneously across IES and non-IES states. The interaction could therefore capture differential compliance or reporting changes rather than the proposed mechanism. The paper should either restrict the main analysis to the pre-waiver period or provide a more thorough decomposition showing that the results are not driven by the waiver period (e.g., by excluding March–December 2020, modeling structural breaks, or explicitly estimating the effect before and after waivers). Without this, it is hard to rule out COVID-era confounding.

**Suggestions**

1. **Strengthen Parallel Trends / Pre-Trend Arguments**: Provide event-study graphs (e.g., recertification shocks over time) or placebo interactions (e.g., lagged recertification intensity × IES should not predict current volatility) to show that prior to increases in SNAP recertification intensity, Medicaid volatility trends did not diverge between IES and non-IES states. If recertification intensity is slowly varying, exploiting leads and lags can help demonstrate that the interaction coincides temporally with volatility changes rather than reflecting pre-existing differences.

2. **Use Alternative Outcomes That Better Capture Administrative Frictions**: The administrative contagion story implies delays or gaps in Medicaid coverage. Consider constructing outcomes such as (i) the share of beneficiaires who exit Medicaid and then re-enter within 1–2 months, (ii) claims submission lag from T-MSIS (if available), (iii) rolling coefficients of variation (already mentioned in appendix but not central). Claim-based metrics (e.g., T-MSIS claims volume decrease/stops) might better reflect processing backlogs than absolute enrollment changes. These could show that the volatility captures undesirable churn rather than legitimate eligibility adjustments.

3. **Detail Treatment Variation and Timing**: Clarify how much within-state variation in recertification intensity exists over time. If the short-cycle share moves slowly, the estimates may rely heavily on cross-sectional differences, undermining the DiD interpretation. Provide descriptive figures showing recertification intensity trajectories for several states (IES vs. non-IES). If the variation is largely cross-sectional, consider alternative identification strategies such as instrumental variables (e.g., use policy changes or federal guidance shifts that differentially affected states) or focus on states with explicit recertification policy changes.

4. **Address Potential Reverse Causality / Simultaneity**: It is plausible that spikes in Medicaid volatility prompt states to adjust SNAP recertification policy (e.g., to stabilize their administrative workload), especially in IES states where administrators see integrated data. Discuss and, if possible, test for reverse causality—e.g., by checking whether future Medicaid volatility predicts current recertification intensity changes or by instrumenting for recertification moves using policy catalysts (federal funding changes, legislative sessions).

5. **Clarify the Mechanism with Administrative Load Indicators**: The paper refers to shared caseworkers and IT queues but does not directly measure caseworker workloads or processing times. If data are available, include proxies such as average application processing time, call center wait times, or staffing levels (possibly from state administrative reports). If not, explain more concretely how the log applications result supports the mechanism: why would more applications be processed if the system is overloaded? The interpretation that more recorded applications imply more backlog requires elaboration—perhaps it reflects a queue buildup that takes longer to process, causing volatility.

6. **Explore the Reminder vs. Burden Channels Further**: The counterfactual in non-IES states—that recertification reduces volatility—is interesting. Expand on that by exploring whether the reminder effect is stronger for certain populations (e.g., low-income households) or whether SNAP policy changes that increase contact frequency (e.g., mandatory interviews) also reduce Medicaid volatility only where systems are separate. This would bolster the claim that integration flips the sign by shifting the dominant channel.

7. **Address Potential Confounds from Medicaid Expansion Status**: The heterogeneity by expansion status is informative, but expansion itself is endogenous and correlated with administrative capacity and SNAP policy. Consider controlling for expansion status in the main regression (e.g., interacting expansion with recertification intensity) or including time-varying indicators of coverage expansions. Alternatively, weight the sample to account for differences in population size, since Medicaid enrollment volatility is measured in percentage terms but the welfare implications differ by state size.

8. **Supplement Randomization Inference with a Placebo Interaction**: The randomization inference permutes IES status, which is helpful, but a placebo interaction (e.g., using a policy that should not affect Medicaid) would further strengthen the argument. For example, interact recertification intensity with a “fake” integrated system variable (e.g., number of letters in state name) or with a program that isn’t processed jointly with Medicaid to show the effect is specific to shared infrastructure.

9. **Ground the Welfare Interpretation with Back-of-the-Envelope Calculations**: The welfare paragraph is compelling but could be quantified more cautiously. Provide assumptions (e.g., what share of volatility corresponds to coverage gaps vs. legitimate turnover) and, if possible, use linked data (claims gaps, reinstatement rates) to estimate the actual number of affected beneficiaries. Making clear that the 3,100 figure is illustrative, not precise, will keep the policy message credible.

10. **Discuss External Validity and Future Research Paths**: The SNAP Policy DB ends in 2020; since many states updated systems and policies thereafter, clarify how the results might generalize to later periods or other integrated platforms (e.g., SSA benefit integration). Suggest how future data (e.g., the renewed SNAP Data Hub or new T-MSIS releases) could extend this research and test whether administrative contagion persists when systems scale up.

Overall, the paper tackles an important question and presents suggestive evidence of cross-program spillovers. Addressing these points will sharpen the identification, clarify the mechanism, and deepen the policy relevance.
