**Idea Fidelity**

The paper strays materially from the manifest idea. The original pre-registration proposed to exploit the textual “grandfathering” premium shock under FEMA’s Risk Rating 2.0 to identify the price elasticity of flood insurance demand, with an implicit expectation that grandfathered policies would face large premium increases relative to non-grandfathered policies. By contrast, the paper emphasizes a “cap trap” mechanism showing that grandfathered policies actually experienced relative premium declines (owing to the 18% cap) while non-grandfathered policies bore the burden of repricing, and it interprets this as the main story. Furthermore, the original manifest discussed a nationally representative sample, heterogeneity by flood map revisions and coverage adequacy, a focus on lapse/renewal behavior, and an auxiliary RDD along SFHA boundaries. The paper, however, limits itself to five states, omits the map-revision intensity arguments, does not implement the SFHA boundary RDD, and reframes the research question from estimating elasticity to documenting a transition-induced incidence reversal. These divergences make it unclear whether the paper is answering the manifest’s core question or a different one. At a minimum, the authors should clarify why the manifest’s identification plan (especially the expectation of grandfathered premium hikes) failed and why the alternative “cap trap” mechanism supplants it. If the goal shifted intentionally, the manuscript should explicitly report that shift and revisit the promised analyses (e.g., coverage adequacy, heterogeneity by map revision, dose-response design keyed to grandfathering-induced discounts, or the SFHA boundary RDD) or explain why they were infeasible.

**Summary**

The authors analyze FEMA OpenFEMA NFIP policy data over 2019–2024 for five high-exposure states to study Risk Rating 2.0’s elimination of grandfathered rates. Using a difference-in-differences design comparing grandfathered to non-grandfathered policies, they document an unexpected “cap trap”: because grandfathered policies were subject to an 18% annual rate cap while non-grandfathered properties faced immediate actuarial repricing, grandfathered policies saw relative premium decreases, lower lapse rates, and higher coverage ratios post-RR2.0. The paper argues this reversal redirected the reform’s intended burden, with stronger effects among mandatory purchasers and investment properties.

**Essential Points**

1. **Identification and Evolution of the Research Question.** The manifest and the main text are misaligned. The manifest envisioned exploiting a grandfathering-induced price shock to estimate price elasticity, with heterogeneous intensity based on flood map revisions and an additional RDD at SFHA boundaries. The paper now focuses on the asymmetric effect of the 18% cap, showing that grandfathered policies fared better than non-grandfathered ones. The authors need to make explicit whether the manifest’s proposed inference is abandoned or reframed. If the goal shifted, they must justify why (e.g., data revealed the unexpected cap mechanism), and they must either still examine the originally promised comparisons (map revision intensity, coverage adequacy, dose-response, SFHA boundary RDD) or explain why those are no longer feasible.

2. **Credibility of the DiD Comparison.** The treatment and control groups differ substantially: grandfathered policies are concentrated in higher-risk zones, older properties, and perhaps among more committed owners (permanent coverage). The DiD relies on parallel trends, but the paper’s evidence is mainly inference that event-study pre-trends are flat. Given the large pre-existing level differences and potential time-varying selection (e.g., differential hurricane exposure, policyholder composition, or enforcement changes), the authors should more thoroughly test whether unobserved shocks could confound the treatment. For example, non-grandfathered policies already had lower lapse rates pre-treatment, and some of the observed post-treatment divergence could reflect state-level reforms or unmeasured hurricane risk correlated with grandfathering status. The authors should therefore: (i) estimate specifications with county-by-quarter fixed effects (or even finer geography) to soak up differential shocks; (ii) present falsification tests with “synthetic control” groups that match on pre-trends or on detailed observables; and (iii) show whether parallel trends hold within zones/flood-risk strata or other strata where grandfathering status varies idiosyncratically (e.g., across different map revision vintage cohorts).

3. **Interpretation of Treatment Intensity and the Role of the 18% Cap.** The narrative hinges on the cap binding for grandfathered policies while non-grandfathered ones face uncapped increases. But the authors do not show direct evidence that grandfathered premiums indeed increased by less than non-grandfathered premiums at the policy level (e.g., by calculating the capped increase from the prior premium) or that the cap was binding across the relevant sample. Put differently, the chief evidence is a negative DiD coefficient on log premium and higher coverage/lower lapses. Yet this could equally arise if grandfathered policies were already on a downward “selection” trend (e.g., high-commitment owners who never lapse). The authors should document the actual magnitude of capped increases, for example by comparing the counterfactual premium trajectory if the cap had not applied, and distinguishing cases where grandfathered policies hit the 18% limit from those where they would have increased less even absent the cap. This would make the “cap trap” story more concrete and allow the reader to verify that the cap, rather than other factors, generated the observed asymmetry.

**Suggestions**

1. **Clarify treatment definitions and sample selection.** The manuscript states that it restricts to grandfatheringTypeCode ∈ {1,3} and five states, but does not specify how it handles repeated policies (the dataset contains 72 million records). Provide a clear description of the sampling (e.g., one observation per policy-year, how renewals are identified, how duplicates and missing data are handled). Also specify how grandfathering status evolves over time—do policies switch codes when they lose their grandfathered status? Given that RR2.0 changed the inference, it would be useful to show how many grandfathered policies transitioned out and how that affects the composition.

2. **Strengthen evidence on the cap mechanism.** The “cap trap” is central. Provide direct evidence that the cap was binding for grandfathered policies, such as plotting the distribution of annual premium changes before and after RR2.0 for grandfathered versus non-grandfathered policies. If the cap was binding, the annual premium change for grandfathered policies should be truncated at 18%, whereas non-grandfathered policies could exceed that. Alternatively, compute the theoretical capped premium (previous premium × 1.18^t) and compare it to the observed premium to show that the cap constrained the increase. Connect this to the dose-response figure: the premium change variable used there should illustrate how the cap limited increases for grandfathered policies, while non-grandfathered ones experienced larger dots.

3. **Address possible selection/anticipation within the treatment group.** Even though the authors argue that grandfathering status was determined historically, there could still be selection via lapses or refunds in the pre-period: e.g., the policyholders most likely to maintain continuous coverage (and thus be grandfathered) may differ in unobserved dimensions from others. To mitigate this, consider (i) implementing a matched DiD where grandfathered policies are matched to similar non-grandfathered policies on observables and pre-trends, or (ii) using an event-study that conditions on the time since last renewal or policy duration. In addition, the paper mentions the possibility of anticipatory effects but does not show the relevant event-study coefficients for 2019–2021 for outcomes like coverage ratio. Including those plots (in the appendix) would reassure readers that the treatment effect is not emerging earlier.

4. **Expand robustness checks and heterogeneity.** The results are interesting, but the conclusions would be more compelling with additional checks:

   - **Placebo outcomes:** Examine variables that should be unaffected by the cap (e.g., non-premium-related policy characteristics such as deductible choice or coverage type) to ensure that the DiD is not capturing a broader administrative change.

   - **Alternative control groups:** Instead of comparing grandfathered policies strictly to non-grandfathered ones, consider comparing to policies that could not have been grandfathered (e.g., new construction after map revision) or policies in states with postponed rollout to demonstrate that the effect is specific to RR2.0.

   - **Dynamic treatment timing:** Some policies renew after October 2021, so their effective treatment may occur in 2022. Estimate specifications using policy-specific treatment dates and show that the results are robust to this heterogeneity.

   - **Dose-response instrument validity:** In the appendix, the authors mention an IV approach using cumulative premium changes instrumented by treatment. Provide the first-stage statistics and report whether the instrument is strong.

5. **Deepen discussion of welfare and policy implications.** The discussion section introduces welfare and distributional consequences but remains qualitative. Quantify the implications where possible. For instance, compute the implied difference in premium volume (per policy) lost from lapsing non-grandfathered policies versus the increase retained by grandfathered ones. Similarly, if coverage ratios increased by 8.9 percentage points, what is the implied change in expected claim payouts or insured values? Even a back-of-the-envelope calculation would help anchor the policy argument.

6. **Address representativeness and general equilibrium concerns.** The sample is restricted to five states, with Florida and Texas driving many of the results. Provide more detail on how representative these states are relative to national NFIP policy distributions. Also, discuss whether the cap trap could have spillover effects, such as altering private insurance entry or affecting community ratings, and whether the short window (up to 2024) limits those concerns.

7. **Reconcile the manifest’s dose-response and SFHA boundary ideas.** The manifest mentioned using map revision intensity to determine treatment dose and an RDD at SFHA boundaries. Even if these ideas were not feasible due to data limitations (e.g., because SRL codes changed), it would be helpful to discuss this in the paper. If possible, implement at least one such extension: for example, exploit variation in how long a policy had been grandfathered (duration since the relevant map revision), which determines the size of the implied subsidy and the cap’s effect. Alternatively, examine policies just inside versus just outside SFHAs in the gg data to see if the cap trap manifests differently for mandatory versus voluntary zones.

8. **Clarify the contribution relative to prior literature.** The paper positions itself as a new take on NFIP reform, but the mechanism is similar to threads in prior work (Blickle/Santos, Wang, etc.). Strengthen the literature review by spelling out how the cap trap narrative differs materially from earlier expectations, and perhaps cite recent articles specifically on RR2.0’s rollout (if available) to contextualize the novelty. For example, if there is literature showing large premium increases for non-grandfathered policies under RR2.0, reference it and explain how the current paper adds behavioral incidence evidence.

By addressing these points, the paper would better align its empirical strategy with the research question, reinforce the credibility of the identification, and sharpen the policy implications of the “cap trap” finding.
