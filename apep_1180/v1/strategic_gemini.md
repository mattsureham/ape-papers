**SUBJECT:** Strategic Positioning of "The Language Barrier in Capital Markets: Evidence from Korea’s Mandatory English Disclosure"

---

### 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper exploits a 2024 natural experiment in South Korea to test whether mandating English-language financial disclosures reduces information frictions for international investors. By comparing large firms forced to adopt English filings against a control group of smaller firms, the paper asks if "speaking the language of global finance" can solve the persistent valuation discount and liquidity issues seen in emerging-ish markets. It matters because it isolates a specific, actionable policy lever to reduce the "home bias" and information costs that fragment global capital.

**Evaluation:** The paper does a decent job in paragraph 1, but it gets bogged down in the "Korea Discount" institutional details too quickly.
**The Pitch the Paper Should Have:** "Does the language of a financial filing act as a non-tariff barrier to capital? Using a first-of-its-kind mandate in South Korea, I show that English-language disclosure significantly reduces illiquidity for financial firms—where balance sheet opacity is highest—but has a negligible effect on non-financial firms where analyst coverage substitutes for filings. This suggests that 'language frictions' are real but highly sector-specific, providing a roadmap for regulators seeking to attract global institutional capital."

### 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution:** The paper provides the first quasi-experimental evidence on the effect of *mandatory* (rather than voluntary) English disclosure on market liquidity.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It successfully moves beyond the voluntary adoption literature (Jeanjean et al. 2015) which is plagued by the "internationally-oriented firms choose to speak English" selection bias.
*   **Framing:** Currently, it's framed slightly too much as "solving the Korea Discount" (a local problem). It should be framed as "Information Frictions in Global Capital Allocation."
*   **The "Smart Economist" Test:** A reader would likely say, "It's a DiD on a Korean reform that shows English matters for banks but maybe not for Samsung." That’s a bit too narrow for the AER.
*   **Making it Bigger:** To make this an AER contribution, the author needs to move from "does it work" to "how does it work." They need **investor-level data**. If the author can show that foreign institutional ownership specifically increased in the treated financial firms, the contribution shifts from a "policy evaluation" to a "fundamental discovery about information costs."

### 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

*   **Closest Neighbors:** Jeanjean et al. (2015) on voluntary language; Daske et al. (2008) on mandatory IFRS; and the classic home bias literature (Ahearne, Griever, and Warnock, 2004).
*   **Positioning:** It should **synthesize** the disclosure and home bias literatures. It bridges the gap between "what we disclose" (IFRS/accounting standards) and "how we communicate it" (language).
*   **Unexpected Conversation:** The paper could speak to the **Market Microstructure** literature (Kyle 1985; Glosten and Milgrom 1985). The finding that volatility increased (Table 2, Col 3) is actually the most "AER-style" result—it suggests more informed trading, not just "more trading."

### 4. NARRATIVE ARC

*   **Setup:** Global capital is stuck at home; emerging markets trade at discounts.
*   **Tension:** Is this discount due to bad governance (chaebols) or simply a "lost in translation" information cost?
*   **Resolution:** Language is a binding constraint for opaque sectors (Finance) but not for "visible" sectors.
*   **Implications:** Mandatory disclosure rules are only as good as the accessibility of the medium.

**Evaluation:** The arc is "Serviceable." The tension is clear, but the resolution is weak because the main effect is insignificant. The author is currently apologizing for the null result. They should instead lean into the **heterogeneity** as the primary narrative: "Language doesn't matter for everyone; it only matters when other information intermediaries (analysts) are absent."

### 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

*   **The Lead Fact:** "When Korea forced its banks to file in English, their illiquidity dropped by nearly 40%, but for tech companies, it did almost nothing."
*   **The Reaction:** People lean in for the 40% drop, but then check their phones when they hear the triple-interaction is insignificant (p=0.58).
*   **The Follow-up:** "How many of those banks were actually being looked at by foreigners before the change?"

### 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

*   **Front-load the Volatility:** The volatility result in Table 2 is buried. In finance, price informativeness is often more prestigious than simple liquidity. If English makes prices "jump" more on news because foreigners can finally read the news, that's a high-quality AER story.
*   **Appendix:** The RDD section is a distraction. With 90 observations, it’s underpowered and adds noise. Move it entirely to a footnote or deep appendix.
*   **Conclusion:** It’s currently a "limitations" list. It needs to pivot to the future of "Global Disclosure Standards."

### 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

In its current form, this is a solid *Journal of Corporate Finance* or *Journal of Accounting Research* paper. To reach the AER, it needs to overcome the "suggestive" nature of the results.

**The Single Most Impactful Piece of Advice:**
The author must obtain **firm-level foreign ownership data** (TIC data or similar) to prove the mechanism. If you can show that the liquidity in banks improved *because* US/EU pension funds entered those specific stocks after the English filings hit the DART system, the insignificant aggregate result becomes a secondary detail to a powerful story about international market integration.
