**4. Suggestions**

**Economic Magnitudes and Interpretation**
The headline heterogeneity result—an increase of 10 recipients per 1,000 population in low-baseline states—is the paper's strongest contribution. However, the economic interpretation needs sharpening. The mean SNAP rate is 106.8 per 1,000. A 10-point increase represents roughly a 9-10% rise in participation for those specific states. To make this more concrete for an *AER: Insights* audience, consider converting this into dollar terms. If the average benefit is roughly \$200/month, 10 additional recipients per 1,000 population in a state like Mississippi (population ~3 million) implies roughly 30,000 new recipients statewide. Multiplying this by average benefits provides an estimate of the transfer magnitude. Comparing this to the estimated IT implementation costs (if available from state budgets or GAO reports) would allow for a rudimentary cost-benefit analysis, strengthening the policy implication that "digital doors" are cost-effective in low-takeup environments.

**Refining the Econometric Strategy**
While the CS estimator is appropriate, the "not-yet-treated" control group relies on the assumption that late adopters are valid counterfactuals for early adopters. Given the long window (2002–2019), macroeconomic conditions changed drastically (e.g., the Great Recession).
*   **Cohort-Specific Trends:** Consider allowing for cohort-specific linear trends. States adopting in 2011 (the largest cohort) did so during the aftermath of the financial crisis. If low-participation states adopted later during high-unemployment periods, the "effect" might partly capture cyclical recovery. Adding state-specific linear trends (while being mindful of the "bad control" problem in DiD) or interacting year fixed effects with baseline characteristics could help isolate the policy effect from macro trends.
*   **Placebo Tests:** To bolster confidence in the identification, run a placebo test using a demographic group unaffected by SNAP eligibility (e.g., population above 200% of the poverty line, if available, or a different program's caseload). Alternatively, shuffle the treatment dates across states and re-estimate the CS ATT to generate a distribution of null effects. Showing your true estimate lies in the tail of this distribution would reinforce the validity of the 10-point finding.

**Data and Measurement Nuances**
*   **Participation Rate vs. Caseload:** You use recipients per 1,000 *total* population. While standard, this conflates eligibility changes with takeup changes. If a state's poverty rate drops, the denominator (population) stays stable, but the numerator (eligible people) shrinks, potentially biasing the rate downward even if takeup *among the eligible* rises. If state-level eligibility estimates (e.g., from CBPP or USDA eligibility tables) are available for a subset of years, using a "takeup rate" (participants / eligible) would be the gold standard. If not, explicitly acknowledge this limitation and control for state unemployment rates more aggressively to proxy for eligibility shocks.
*   **The "NA" in Table 2:** Regarding the failed control specification: If the 49 policy controls create perfect multicollinearity with year fixed effects (likely, as many policies are federal mandates adopted by many states simultaneously), switch to a time-varying index of "policy restrictiveness" (e.g., the first principal component of the policy variables) or select the 3-4 most variable policies (BBCE, fingerprinting, interview waivers).
