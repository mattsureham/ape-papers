## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
The paper evaluates whether digitizing the "front door" of the social safety net—specifically the move to online SNAP applications—actually increases program participation. While the average effect is a statistically insignificant "null," the paper argues that this masks a major success: in states where participation was historically lowest, online applications increased enrollment by roughly 15%.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this reasonably well, but the second paragraph currently focuses too much on a technical critique of a specific NBER working paper (Jones et al., 2021). To meet the AER standard, the pitch needs to move away from "I'm fixing a DiD bias" toward "I'm uncovering a fundamental truth about how administrative burdens interact with baseline state capacity."

**The Pitch the Paper Should Have:**
"Administrative burdens are often cited as the primary reason for the 'takeup gap' in social programs. We show that while digitizing applications is not a silver bullet for the average state, it acts as a critical equalizer. By reducing the fixed costs of entry, online applications effectively 'unlock' the safety net in low-performing jurisdictions, closing nearly one-sixth of their enrollment gap."

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**Contribution:** The paper demonstrates that the efficacy of administrative simplification is conditional on the baseline level of program penetration, with digitization acting as a targeted remedy for the most "shut" systems.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It differentiates itself from Jones et al. (2021) by using administrative data and modern staggered DiD tools. However, being "the better version of Jones et al." is a *Journal of Public Economics* contribution, not an AER one.
*   **World vs. Literature:** It is currently 50/50. It spends a lot of time on the "forbidden comparison" problem. To be AER-level, the "World" side (the mechanism of the marginal applicant) needs to be the star.
*   **The "Smart Economist" Test:** A smart economist would currently say, "It’s a clean re-evaluation of SNAP digitization using Callaway-Sant’Anna that finds the effect is actually there if you look at the right states."
*   **Bigger Contribution:** To make this bigger, the author needs to bridge the gap between "participation rates" and "welfare." Who are these 10 per 1,000 people? Are they the poorest? The working poor? Connecting the heterogeneity not just to *state* baselines but to *household* types (even via proxy) would elevate the paper.

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
**Closest Neighbors:** 
1.  **Currie (2006)** on the theory of takeup.
2.  **Bhargava & Manoli (2015)** and **Finkelstein & Notowidigdo (2019)** on frictions/nudges.
3.  **Jones, Marinescu, & Mas (2021)** – the direct "competitor."

**Positioning:** The paper should spend less energy "attacking" Jones et al. and more energy "synthesizing" the friction literature. The current narrative is "their econometrics were wrong." The AER narrative should be "the previous literature's focus on *average* effects led to a premature dismissal of digitization as a tool for equity."

**Unexpected Conversation:** The paper should speak to the **"State Capacity"** literature. This isn't just about SNAP; it's about how digital infrastructure allows "low-capacity" states to leapfrog traditional bureaucratic hurdles.

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC
*   **Setup:** The US safety net has a massive "leaky bucket" problem (82% takeup). 
*   **Tension:** We've spent 20 years and millions of dollars moving apps online, but the best recent evidence says it did... nothing.
*   **Resolution:** It did nothing for states that were already "open," but it was a massive success for states that were "closed." 
*   **Implications:** Bureaucratic "front-end" fixes only matter when the front end is the primary bottleneck.

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong. The "Door Ajar" metaphor works well. The "resolution" feels earned because the heterogeneity (Table 4) is so stark.

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
**The Party Fact:** "Moving SNAP applications online did absolutely nothing for states like Oregon, but it increased food stamp participation by 15% in states like Georgia or Mississippi."

**Lean in or Phone?** Lean in. Most economists care about the "Digital Divide" and the "Safety Net." The idea that technology benefits the "worst" systems the most is a counter-intuitive and optimistic finding.

**Follow-up:** "Does it actually help the people who need it most, or just the people with iPhones?" This is the question the paper needs to be ready to answer.

---

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
*   **Methodology Heavy:** Section 4.2 and 4.3 are a bit "econometrics textbook." Move the detailed explanation of the "forbidden comparison" to an appendix. Every AER reader knows Goodman-Bacon by now. Use that space for more "Institutional Background" on *why* some states were so low to begin with.
*   **Front-loading:** The log specification (Column 5, Table 2) is actually more interesting than the level specification because it addresses the power issue. It should be given more prominence.
*   **Conclusion:** It’s a bit repetitive. It should pivot to a broader discussion of "Digital Government" as a field.

---

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The paper is currently a "very high-quality redo" of a known question. To make it a "Top 5" paper, it needs to be **Ambitious about the Mechanism.** 

**The Single Most Impactful Advice:** 
Stop framing this as a "correction" of the Jones et al. paper and start framing it as a "Theory of Targeted Simplification." Use the heterogeneity not just as a "result," but as a way to test a model of marginal costs. If the author can show *which* types of households (rural vs. urban, elderly vs. young) drove the 10-point increase in low-baseline states, it moves from a measurement paper to a behavior paper.
