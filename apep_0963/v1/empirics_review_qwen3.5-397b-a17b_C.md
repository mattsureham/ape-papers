1. **Idea Fidelity**
The paper largely adheres to the Original Idea Manifest, utilizing the specified CPS Food Security Supplement microdata (2018–2023), ACS state-level SNAP participation rates, and a continuous difference-in-differences framework. The core research question—evaluating the causal effect of the 2021 Thrifty Food Plan (TFP) revision on food insecurity—remains intact. However, there is a notable deviation in the identification strategy. The manifest promised a triple-difference approach leveraging the staggered termination of Emergency Allotments (EA) across ~18 states to isolate the TFP effect. In the paper, this triple-difference is relegated to the Appendix and admitted to offer "no additional identifying power" (coefficients are imprecise and insignificant). Consequently, the paper relies primarily on the continuous DiD, which was intended to be the baseline, not the sole identifier. This shift weakens the causal claim regarding the TFP specifically, as the EA confound remains unresolved empirically, though it is addressed narratively.

2. **Summary**
This paper evaluates the impact of the October 2021 Thrifty Food Plan revision, which permanently increased SNAP benefits by 21%, on household food security. Using state-level SNAP participation rates as a dosage measure in a difference-in-differences framework, the author finds no statistically significant evidence that the revision reduced food insecurity. The author argues that the permanent benefit increase was quantitatively overwhelmed by the simultaneous expiration of larger, temporary COVID-era Emergency Allotments, creating an "adequacy illusion" where structural improvements were masked by transitional cliffs.

3. **Essential Points**
1.  **Endogeneity of State SNAP Rates:** The identification relies on the assumption that states with higher SNAP participation rates would have had parallel food insecurity trends to low-SNAP states absent the TFP revision. This is a strong assumption. High-SNAP states systematically differ from low-SNAP states in terms of poverty traps, labor market structures, and political economy (e.g., state-level safety net generosity). While state fixed effects control for time-invariant heterogeneity, they do not control for differential recovery trends post-2020. High-SNAP states may have experienced slower economic recoveries or higher inflation exposure, driving food insecurity independently of the TFP.
2.  **Conflation of TFP and Emergency Allotments:** The paper's central narrative—that the EA cliff overwhelmed the TFP gain—is plausible but empirically unverified. The failed triple-difference suggests the data cannot distinguish between the TFP effect and the EA expiration effect. Since EA expiration was also staggered and correlated with state politics (which correlates with SNAP rates), the "Post" period captures a net shock of TFP + EA expiration. Attributing the null result specifically to the EA cliff rather than TFP ineffectiveness is an interpretation, not an empirical finding of this design.
3.  **Inference and Power:** With only 51 clusters (states), standard asymptotic inference is risky. While the author correctly employs wild cluster bootstrap, the effective sample size for identification is small. The standard error (0.088) implies a Minimum Detectable Effect (MDE) that may be larger than plausible policy effects. Claiming the result is "well-powered" requires a formal power calculation showing that the study could have detected an effect size consistent with prior literature (e.g., Bitler et al.). If the MDE is larger than the expected effect, the null is uninformative.

4. **Suggestions**
    **Refining the Identification Strategy**
    The current design treats state SNAP participation as an exogenous dose, but it is fundamentally endogenous to state economic health. To strengthen the causal claim, consider the following adjustments:
    *   **Net Benefit Shock Measure:** Instead of using pre-period SNAP rates as a proxy for exposure, construct a state-year specific measure of the *net benefit change*. Calculate the average TFP increase per household minus the average EA loss per household for each state and year. This captures the actual fiscal shock hitting the state rather than just the potential exposure. Use this continuous measure interacted with Post in the DiD. This moves the identification closer to the actual mechanism (money in pocket) rather than the proxy (participation rate).
    *   **Synthetic Control Methods:** Given the small number of clusters (states), a Synthetic Control Method (SCM) approach might be more robust than fixed effects. Construct a synthetic "high-SNAP state" using a weighted combination of low-SNAP states to match pre-trends in food insecurity and economic indicators. This would provide a clearer visual and statistical counterfactual for what would have happened in high-SNAP states without the TFP/EA dynamics.
    *   **Control for Differential Trends:** Explicitly interact the state SNAP rate with linear time trends or include state-specific economic controls (e.g., change in unemployment rate, change in median income) interacted with Post. This helps mitigate the concern that high-SNAP states were on different economic trajectories during the pandemic recovery.

    **Improving Measurement and Heterogeneity**
    The aggregate null result masks potential heterogeneity that is theoretically crucial.
    *   **Focus on Recipients:** The CPS FSS allows you to identify SNAP recipients directly (variable `HESP1`). The current analysis mixes recipients and non-recipients within states. A more direct test is to restrict the sample to SNAP-eligible or SNAP-receiving households only. The treatment intensity would then be the benefit level change itself, not the state participation rate. If the TFP worked, it should show up strongest among actual recipients. The current heterogeneity table hints at this (low-income coeff is negative), but the sample selection should be driven by receipt status, not just income.
    *   **Benefit Adequacy Threshold:** The paper argues the old benefit level was below the adequacy threshold. To test this, you could classify households by their pre-TFP benefit adequacy ratio (benefits vs. poverty line or food costs). If the "adequacy illusion" holds, the effect should be largest for those who were furthest below the adequacy threshold prior to 2021.

    **Clarifying Inference and Magnitudes**
    The interpretation of the null result needs to be precise to avoid policy misinterpretation.
    *   **Equivalence Testing:** Instead of simply failing to reject the null, perform an equivalence test. Define a smallest effect size of interest (SESO) based on prior literature (e.g., a 1 percentage point reduction in food insecurity). Test whether you can reject effects larger than this threshold. This allows you to claim "no *meaningful* effect" rather than just "no *statistically significant* effect."
    *   **Power Calculations:** Include a formal power calculation in the Appendix. Given 51 clusters and the observed variance in food insecurity, what is the probability of detecting a 1 ppt or 2 ppt reduction? If power is low (<80% for plausible effects), the null result must be framed as "inconclusive" rather than "evidence of no effect."
    *   **Standardized Effect Sizes:** The paper reports a Standardized Effect Size (SDE) of 0.011. Contextualize this. In social safety net literature, what constitutes a meaningful SDE? Comparing this to the SDE of the original SNAP program introduction or the EA expansion would help readers gauge whether 0.
