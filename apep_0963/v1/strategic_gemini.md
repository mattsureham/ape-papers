## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
The paper evaluates the 2021 Thrifty Food Plan (TFP) revision—the largest permanent increase in SNAP history—to see if it reduced food insecurity. Using a continuous difference-in-differences design based on state-level SNAP exposure, the author finds a null effect: the permanent $36/month increase was quantitatively overwhelmed by the simultaneous $300+/month "benefit cliff" from expiring pandemic Emergency Allotments. Economists should care because it demonstrates that even massive structural reforms can be rendered invisible when implemented during periods of high-magnitude benefit volatility.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this very clearly. The "adequacy illusion" framing is high-quality. No rewrite of the first two paragraphs is necessary; they successfully bridge the gap between policy history and empirical finding.

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**The Contribution:** This paper provides the first quasi-experimental evaluation of the 2021 TFP revision’s impact on food insecurity, identifying a "null" result driven by the scale mismatch between permanent policy gains and temporary benefit losses.

*   **Differentiation:** It differentiates itself from Bitler & Seifoddini (2023) by moving from cyclical changes (Great Recession) to a permanent structural recalibration. 
*   **Framing:** It is framed as a question about the **WORLD**—specifically, why a historic expansion of the safety net failed to move the needle on hunger.
*   **AER Potential:** It risks being seen as "another DiD paper about SNAP," but the "benefit cliff" narrative elevates it. To make the contribution bigger, the author needs to lean harder into the **political economy of policy timing.** Why did the USDA/Congress implement a permanent fix exactly when a temporary one was expiring? Is this a generalizable failure of "benefit stacking"?

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
The paper sits at the intersection of public economics and the economics of poverty.

*   **Closest Neighbors:** Hoynes & Schanzenbach (2016) on SNAP history; Bitler & Seifoddini (2023) on benefit levels; Ziliak (2016) on TFP adequacy; and the recent "benefit cliff" literature (e.g., Ganong & Noel).
*   **Positioning:** It should **synthesize** the advocacy for benefit adequacy with the practical realities of the COVID-era safety net. It currently builds on these neighbors but needs to "attack" the implicit assumption in the TFP advocacy literature that any increase—no matter how small relative to the total budget—will show up in aggregate insecurity data.
*   **Missing Links:** The paper is somewhat quiet on the **inflation** literature. During 2021–2023, food-at-home inflation was record-breaking. Is this a null result because of the EA cliff, or because the $36 was immediately eaten by 12% inflation?

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC
*   **Setup:** For 40 years, SNAP was underfunded due to an archaic food plan.
*   **Tension:** In 2021, the "holy grail" of SNAP reform happens ($36B/year), yet food insecurity *increases* shortly after.
*   **Resolution:** The permanent increase was a "cushion" too small for the "cliff" created by ending pandemic supplements.
*   **Implications:** Benefit "adequacy" is relative, not absolute. Policy success depends on the net trajectory of the household budget, not just the legislative win.

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong. It tells a coherent story of a "failed success."

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
*   **The Lead Fact:** "The government just gave SNAP its biggest permanent raise ever, and it was so small compared to what was being taken away that we can't even find it in the data."
*   **Reaction:** Lean in. It challenges the "more is always better" policy mantra by highlighting the importance of the net change.
*   **The Follow-up:** "Does this mean the TFP revision was a waste of money, or would things have been 10x worse without it?" The author’s heterogeneity analysis (Table 4) begins to answer this but needs to be more central.

---

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
*   **Front-loading:** The "back-of-the-envelope" calculation on page 11 (the $36 vs $300 comparison) is the most important part of the paper. It should be moved to the **Introduction** or the **Institutional Background**. It explains the null result before the reader even sees a regression table.
*   **Appendix:** The Triple-Difference results (Section C) are currently a bit weak. If they don't provide identifying power, they belong in the appendix (as they are), but the author should explain *why* it fails—likely because EA timing was highly correlated with political leaning and other state-level shocks.

---

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The gap is **Ambition/Scope**. A "null result" on a single policy is a tough sell for the AER unless it fundamentally changes how we think about the safety net. 

**The single most impactful piece of advice:**
The author should pivot from "I found a null for the TFP" to **"A Theory of the Benefit Cliff."** They need to use the TFP revision as a case study to build a broader argument about why marginal structural reforms are doomed to fail when they are poorly synchronized with the withdrawal of emergency fiscal support.
