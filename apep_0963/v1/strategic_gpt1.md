## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper studies the 2021 revision of the Thrifty Food Plan, which permanently raised SNAP benefits by 21 percent, and asks whether that historically large benefit increase reduced household food insecurity. Its central claim is no: at the aggregate state level, the increase appears to have had little detectable effect because it coincided with the expiration of much larger pandemic-era Emergency Allotments, so a permanent expansion was swamped by a larger temporary withdrawal.

A busy economist should care because the paper is potentially not just about SNAP. It is about a broader question in public economics: when does a nominal expansion in social insurance fail to improve outcomes because policy changes are embedded in a larger transition path of gains and losses?

Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs? **Mostly, but not optimally.** The current introduction has a nice journalistic hook and gets to the result quickly. But it still reads too much like “a null result on a specific nutrition-policy reform” and not enough like “a general lesson about social insurance design, policy stacking, and benefit cliffs.” The paper’s best idea is the interaction between a permanent reform and the unwinding of emergency aid. That should be the front door.

### The pitch the paper should have

“In October 2021, the United States permanently increased SNAP benefits by 21 percent through the first major revision of the Thrifty Food Plan in decades. Yet this historically large reform arrived just as pandemic-era Emergency Allotments were being withdrawn, raising a broader question for social insurance policy: can a permanent increase improve material hardship when households simultaneously face a much larger benefit cliff? Using state variation in SNAP exposure, I show that the TFP revision produced no detectable aggregate reduction in food insecurity, suggesting that the effects of benefit reforms depend not just on their level but on the transition path of overlapping programs.”

That framing makes the paper about the world, not just about one USDA rule change.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### Contribution in one sentence

The paper’s contribution is to argue that the 2021 permanent increase in SNAP generosity did not measurably reduce aggregate food insecurity because it was quantitatively overwhelmed by the simultaneous expiration of much larger temporary pandemic benefits.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?

**Not yet clearly enough.** The paper distinguishes itself from papers on SNAP participation effects and from work on earlier benefit increases, but the differentiation is still mostly mechanical: extensive margin vs. intensive margin, permanent reform vs. cyclical expansion. That is true, but not sufficient. A top journal reader will ask: why is this more than another reduced-form SNAP paper with a null estimate generated by overlapping policies?

What needs sharpening is that the paper is **not primarily estimating the elasticity of food insecurity with respect to SNAP benefits**. It is documenting something more conceptually interesting: **the political economy and welfare consequences of layered safety-net changes**. If that is the contribution, the closest comparisons are not only SNAP papers but also work on temporary transfers, benefit cliffs, and the unwinding of pandemic supports.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a gap in a literature?

Right now it is **half-and-half**, with too much tilt toward the literature-gap style. The stronger framing is the world question:

- When do permanent benefit reforms fail to move hardship?
- How should we think about adequacy when households experience large offsetting changes from overlapping programs?
- Are headline expansions misleading if they are implemented during retrenchment elsewhere?

That is much stronger than “the literature has studied the extensive margin more than the intensive margin.”

### Could a smart economist explain what is new after reading the introduction?

A smart economist could say: “It’s a paper about the 2021 SNAP increase and it finds basically no aggregate effect on food insecurity.” That is clear enough.

But they might also say: “Sounds like another DiD on a policy change with a null result.” That is the danger. The current draft does not yet make the reader say: “Ah, the real point is that benefit adequacy cannot be understood apart from offsetting policy withdrawals.”

### What would make the contribution bigger?

Be specific:

1. **Reframe the estimand around net benefit changes, not just the TFP revision.**  
   The paper’s most compelling fact is the mismatch between the TFP gain and the EA loss. If it can organize the paper around “net transfer exposure” or “policy offset” rather than only TFP dosage, the contribution becomes broader and more original.

2. **Show a sharper implication for actual recipients or likely recipients.**  
   The current low-income heterogeneity result is suggestive but underdeveloped. Strategically, the paper would benefit from centering the question on households plausibly exposed to SNAP, rather than leading with all households in the CPS. That would make the story feel less diffuse.

3. **Connect food insecurity to the broader concept of consumption smoothing / social insurance transitions.**  
   If the paper can speak to how households respond when emergency support expires, it belongs to a bigger literature than food policy alone.

4. **Elevate the “benefit cliff” mechanism from a discussion point to the central contribution.**  
   Right now “adequacy illusion” is catchy, but slightly slogan-like. The bigger contribution is not the label; it is the claim that policy evaluations based on statutory generosity can mislead when benefit schedules are changing along multiple margins.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

SNAP had long been tied to an outdated Thrifty Food Plan, and the 2021 revision was billed as a major permanent correction to benefit inadequacy.

### Tension

Despite being the largest permanent SNAP expansion in history, the reform happened during an unusual policy environment in which pandemic-era Emergency Allotments were being phased out. So the puzzle is: should we expect a structural benefit increase to reduce hardship, or was it too small relative to what households were simultaneously losing?

### Resolution

The paper finds no detectable aggregate reduction in food insecurity in higher-exposure states, and interprets this as evidence that the permanent increase was overwhelmed by the emergency-benefit cliff.

### Implications

The implication is that policy adequacy cannot be assessed from statutory benefit levels alone; transition paths and interactions with other programs may matter more than the headline expansion.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

**Yes, but only in embryo.** The raw materials are there. The introduction is actually better than many submissions: it has a setup, a puzzle, and a takeaway. But the body of the paper still feels somewhat like a conventional empirical paper organized around tables rather than a paper relentlessly advancing one claim.

The story it should be telling is:

- Policymakers thought they solved a long-standing adequacy problem.
- In practice, households experienced reform through the lens of a much larger withdrawal.
- Therefore, the relevant object for welfare and behavior is net support over time, not the statutory reform in isolation.

That is a coherent arc. Right now the paper periodically loses that thread and reverts to “here is a null estimate, here are some heterogeneity tables, here are some robustness checks.”

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?

“I’d open with: the largest permanent increase in SNAP benefits in modern history appears to have produced no detectable aggregate reduction in food insecurity, because it arrived alongside the expiration of even larger emergency benefits.”

That is a good sentence. People will understand it immediately.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

**Lean in at first.** The topic is salient, and the paradox is real.

But the next 30 seconds matter. If the pitch becomes “we run a continuous DiD using state SNAP rates and get a null,” attention drops. If instead it becomes “this tells us that safety-net expansions can look ineffective when implemented during retrenchment,” attention holds.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Probably one of these:

- “So is the lesson that SNAP benefit increases don’t matter, or just that the EA unwind dominated everything?”
- “Who actually got hurt or helped—actual recipients, low-income households, or high-SNAP states?”
- “Is this a food-policy story or a general lesson about policy overlap and unwinding?”

Those are telling. The paper needs crisp answers to them.

### If the findings are null or modest, is the null itself interesting?

**Potentially yes, but only if the paper makes the right case.** A null result can be interesting here because the policy was large, salient, and permanent. But the null is not interesting simply as “we found nothing.” It is interesting as evidence that **headline policy generosity can be economically irrelevant if embedded in an offsetting sequence of changes**.

At present, the paper partly makes that case, but not forcefully enough. The null currently still risks reading like a failed attempt to isolate the reform. The paper must instead insist that the inability of the reform to move aggregate hardship **is itself the substantive fact**.
