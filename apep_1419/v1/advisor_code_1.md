**Idea Fidelity**

The submitted paper diverges in important respects from the original idea manifest. The manifest proposed exploiting the employer-size staggered auto-enrollment roll-out and the April 2019 contribution step-up, using employer-level (ASHE) and DWP statistics to compare firms that had less time to absorb increases. The paper, however, works at the local-authority level and exploits cross-area differences in the share of employment at small firms as a continuous treatment intensity. It therefore abstracts from the promised employer-size × staging-date variation and never uses the employer-level contribution data (ASHE pension rates or DWP opt-out statistics) that were central to the manifest. While the research question remains in the same family—whether the April 2019 step-up compressed wages—the identification strategy is different, and the paper should acknowledge that it departs from the staggered cohort DiD and employer contribution heterogeneity laid out in the manifest.

**Summary**

The paper examines whether the April 2019 increase in the UK auto-enrollment employer contribution minimum led to wage compression. Using a local-authority panel (2015–2023) and exploiting pre-treatment variation in the share of employment at small firms—who were most likely to bind at the floor—the author estimates a difference-in-differences model. The point estimate is positive: richer small-firm areas saw faster wage growth post-2019, running counter to Summers-style full incidence predictions. The paper backs this with various robustness checks (placebos, exclusions, alternative treatment measures) and interprets the result through behavioral/default and tight labor-market lenses.

**Essential Points**

1. **Credibility of the Continuous Treatment Design:** By construction, the identification hinges on the assumption that areas with a higher pre-2019 small-firm share experienced a larger marginal cost shock in April 2019. But the paper never directly links small-firm share to actual bindingness of the contribution minimum; the Business Counts data provide counts of firms by size, not contributions. The mechanism relies on small firms contributing at the floor, which might be correlated with small-firm prevalence but is untested. The author should provide empirical evidence (e.g., from ASHE Table 4.6a or DWP contribution distributions) showing that local authorities with higher small-firm share indeed had a larger share of workers at binding minimums. Without this, the treatment variable is plausibly capturing other structural differences entirely, threatening the identification.

2. **Pre-trend Concerns in Event Study:** The event-study results show statistically significant negative coefficients for 2015–2017 and even 2019–2021, undermining the parallel-trends assumption required for the DiD. The paper concedes this but downplays it by citing an insignificant 2017 placebo and calling the main estimate an “upper bound.” The paper as currently written cannot interpret the post-2019 positive effect causally if pre-trends are so clearly non-zero. The authors must address why these pre-trend coefficients arise (e.g., differential business cycles, measurement error, or composition changes) and either adjust the specification (e.g., include leads of the treatment, pre-trend controls, flexible trends) or provide stronger diagnostics that the observed pre-trends do not bias the post-2019 estimate. As it stands, the positive coefficient may simply reflect spurious trajectory differences.

3. **Alternative Explanations for Positive Coefficient:** The paper interprets the positive effect as evidence against wage offsets, yet several plausible alternative mechanisms remain untested. For example, if small-firm-heavy areas experienced stronger local economic growth, wage gains would be mechanically associated with small-firm share. The paper relies on LA fixed effects and a single placebo, but this does not rule out differential trends—for instance, small firms may be clustered in regions with a manufacturing boom or housing-market-led wage growth. To claim a causal effect of the pension step-up, the authors need to control for time-varying local shocks (e.g., employment growth, industry composition, housing prices) or include local linear trends interacted with small-firm share. Without doing so, the positive coefficient could simply reflect omitted time-varying heterogeneity.

If additional critical issues arise beyond these three, they should motivate rejection, but the above already raise serious doubts about causal interpretation.

**Suggestions**

1. **Directly Tie Treatment to Bindingness Using Pension Contribution Data:** One of the paper’s ambitions is to test the Summers (1989) mandate-tax prediction via the binding contribution minimum. To make that connection explicit, add descriptive analysis linking small-firm share to the share of employees at the minimum contribution rate, leveraging publicly available ASHE pension contribution tables or DWP contribution distributions. For example, regressing local authority-level small-firm share on the share of employees reporting employer contributions equal to the minimum could validate the treatment. If that relationship is weak, consider redefining the treatment intensity using contribution-level data (e.g., share at 2% employer only) rather than firm size proxies.

2. **Revisit the Event Study and Pre-Trends:** The presence of significant pre-treatment coefficients undermines the DiD’s parallel trends assumption. Use two approaches: first, plot the event study with confidence intervals and consider adding a figure showing smoothed trends; second, re-estimate the specification with local authority-specific linear or quadratic time trends interacted with small-firm share (or with time-varying controls such as local GDP growth). Alternatively, implement a “stacked” DiD or synthetic control approach that selects more comparable comparison groups. If the positive effect persists under these more flexible specifications, the paper’s causal claims become more convincing.

3. **Address Potential Confounders Directly:** The paper should control for time-varying economic conditions correlated with small-firm prevalence. Possible controls include local employment change, sectoral mix (e.g., share of hospitality or retail employment), housing price growth, or business formation rates (available from NOMIS). Including these variables, possibly interacted with year dummies, would help ensure that the estimated coefficient is not simply capturing a boom in small-firm-dense areas. Presenting a table showing that the treatment variable is orthogonal to these controls in pre-treatment years would further strengthen the identification.

4. **Consider Alternative Functional Forms/Exposure Windows:** The current specification uses small-firm share × post-2019, which assumes the effect is immediate and permanent post-April 2019. You could explore a more nuanced dynamic specification (e.g., interactions with year indicators from 2019 onwards) to verify when the positive effect emerges. Additionally, since the step-up affected employers gradually (with April 2018 already raising employer contributions to 2%), consider testing whether the small-firm share predicts wage changes already in 2018—a pre-treatment placebo. If the effect only appears after 2019 and not in 2018, this would bolster the causal narrative.

5. **Clarify the Role of COVID-19:** The pandemic overlapped with the post-2019 period and could differentially affect small-firm areas. While the robustness check dropping 2020–2021 is useful, more discussion is warranted. For instance, examine whether pandemic-related industry shocks (hospitality vs. finance) correlate with small-firm share and whether the results remain using employment data that exclude pandemic years altogether. Alternatively, re-estimate the model treating 2020–2021 as separate periods with their own interactions to see whether the positive effect is driven by post-pandemic recovery rather than the pension mandate.

6. **Discuss Mechanisms in Greater Detail:** The paper offers two potential mechanisms (default anchoring, tight labor markets) but leaves them at the conceptual level. Where possible, provide empirical support: e.g., show that small-firm areas also had lower pre-2019 opt-out rates (consistent with anchors), or that employment growth was faster (supporting tight labor markets). If such auxiliary outcomes are unavailable, explicitly acknowledge the limitation and outline how future work could use richer datasets (RTI microdata) to test these mechanisms.

7. **Expand on Policy Interpretation with Quantitative Back-of-the-Envelope:** The conclusion claims that raising the auto-enrollment minimum would increase total compensation without wage compression. To make this claim more compelling, provide a simple calculation: using the estimated elasticity, what is the implied change in total compensation (wages + pension) per worker after the 1 percentage point employer increase? This would ground the policy implication in the estimated magnitude rather than an abstract assertion.

Following these suggestions would substantially strengthen the causal story, clarify the connection between small-firm exposure and the binding contribution minimum, and improve the paper’s usefulness for policy debates on mandate design.
