## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
This paper examines the demographic and health profile of the 32,000 "additional" babies born following the *Dobbs* decision. While previous research confirmed that abortion bans increased birth rates, this paper asks if those marginal births are systematically different (e.g., more likely to be born to teen, unmarried, or low-health mothers); surprisingly, it finds no detectable change in birth composition at the state level.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this well in the second paragraph, but the first paragraph is a bit slow. It should lead with the tension immediately. 

**The pitch the paper should have:**
"Does the *Dobbs* decision primarily increase births among the most vulnerable populations, as selection theory predicts, or has a modern infrastructure of travel and contraception blunted this effect? While post-abortion-ban states saw a significant 1.5% increase in total births, we find that the composition of these births—measured by maternal age, marital status, and infant health—remained unchanged. This 'quantity without quality' shift suggests that the fiscal and social impacts of *Dobbs* may scale linearly with the number of births rather than being compounded by increased per-child disadvantage."

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**Contribution:** The paper provides the first comprehensive evidence that the post-*Dobbs* fertility increase did not significantly alter the aggregate socioeconomic or health composition of newborns at the state level.

- **Differentiation:** It is clearly differentiated from Myers et al. (2024), which established the *quantity* effect. This paper moves the conversation to the *intensive* margin.
- **Framing:** It is currently framed as a "puzzle" (filling a gap in literature) but should be framed more as a "Modern Challenge to Selection Theory."
- **Clarity:** A smart economist would understand it as: "Quantity went up, but the 'mix' of people stayed the same."
- **Bigger Contribution:** The contribution would be much larger if the authors could get closer to *mechanisms*. A null result at the state level is interesting, but is it a null because of "offsetting behaviors" (contraception) or "geographic leakage" (travel)? 

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
- **Closest Neighbors:** Myers et al. (2024) on *Dobbs* fertility; Gruber et al. (1999) on *Roe* selection; Pop-Eleches (2006) on Romanian bans; Akerlof et al. (1996) on the economics of out-of-wedlock births.
- **Positioning:** It should more aggressively **attack** the external validity of historical studies (Gruber, Pop-Eleches). The paper implies that 1970s/1960s selection theory is "dead" in an era of telehealth and TikTok-coordinated travel.
- **Missing Literature:** It needs to speak more to the **public finance** literature. If composition is unchanged, the "Medicaid bill" for states is predictable and linear. This moves the paper from "health econ" to "general interest."

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC
- **Setup:** *Dobbs* happened; birth rates went up.
- **Tension:** Standard economic theory (Akerlof, Gruber) predicts these marginal births should be the most disadvantaged.
- **Resolution:** They aren't. The "marginal" baby looks just like the "average" baby.
- **Implications:** Behavioral/technological substitutes for abortion (travel, pills, LARC) are now so efficient that they effectively "smooth" the shock across demographic groups.

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong. It’s a "Dogs that didn't bark" story.

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
At a dinner party, you lead with: **"Even though abortion was banned in 21 states, the babies being born there aren't any more likely to be born to teen moms or have health issues than before."**

Economists would lean in and ask: **"Is that because the bans didn't work, or because the rich and poor found ways to bypass them equally?"** That follow-up is where the AER-level "juice" is.

---

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
- **Front-load the "Why the null matters":** The discussion of travel vs. contraception (Section 6) is the most intellectually interesting part. Move some of that speculation/preliminary evidence into the intro.
- **Appendix the TWFE:** Given the *Dobbs* timing, Callaway-Sant’Anna is the only credible lead. No need to give TWFE equal billing in the main text.
- **Visuals:** The paper needs a map or a bin-scatter showing birth composition vs. distance to the nearest legal state to address the "travel" mechanism more directly.

---

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The current version is a very high-quality *Journal of Human Resources* or *Journal of Health Economics* paper. To make it **AER**, it needs to be an "End of an Era" paper. 

The authors need to prove *why* the Akerlof/Gruber selection effects disappeared. Is it because the "marginal" woman is now middle-class? Or because the "disadvantaged" woman is the one most likely to use mail-order pills?

**Single most impactful advice:** Use the 2023 individual-level data (if available) or county-level distance-to-border variation to test if the "null" is driven by travel. If the null holds even in the middle of Texas (far from a border), you have a massive story about the effectiveness of medication abortion/contraception that overrides 50 years of economic theory.
