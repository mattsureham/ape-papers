### 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper investigates whether state-level film production tax credits—a popular but controversial $10 billion industrial policy—actually create jobs and, more importantly, who gets them. Leveraging new demographic data, it finds that while these subsidies successfully boost overall industry employment, the gains are captured almost exclusively by White and Hispanic workers, leaving Black employment unchanged even in states with high Black populations like Georgia. This suggests that place-based subsidies can fail to reduce racial inequality if hiring occurs through exclusive occupational networks or "below-the-line" sectors that bypass certain local groups.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this well, but the first two paragraphs spend a bit too much time on the "Georgia boom" anecdote and the DiD econometrics. 
**The Revised Pitch:** 
*“Over the last two decades, US states have spent $10 billion on film production tax credits to lure high-wage creative jobs. While the aggregate employment effects of these place-based subsidies are debated, their impact on racial equity—a central goal of modern industrial policy—remains unknown. This paper shows that while these subsidies successfully scale the motion picture industry, they do so by reinforcing existing 'casting gaps': Hispanic workers see a 36% employment boost, while Black employment remains stagnant, suggesting that the 'local' hiring stimulated by these credits flows through racially segmented labor networks.”*

---

### 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:** The paper identifies a racial "mismatch" in the efficacy of place-based subsidies, proving that industry-specific tax credits can simultaneously succeed in job creation while failing in inclusive growth.

- **Differentiation:** It directly challenges Button (2019) by using more recent data and robust estimators (CS-DiD) to find a positive employment effect that was previously missed. It then adds a layer of demographic analysis (QWI) that prior work lacked.
- **World vs. Literature:** It is currently framed as 50/50. To be an AER paper, it needs to be 80% about the *World* (the failure of subsidies to reach Black workers).
- **The "So What?"** It is more than "another DiD paper." It is a paper about the *mechanics of exclusion* in a high-glamour industry.
- **Bigger Contribution:** To maximize impact, the author needs to dig deeper into *why* the Hispanic/Black divergence exists. Is it because Hispanic workers are over-represented in the specific "below-the-line" trades (construction, catering) that film crews hire? Linking the QWI data to occupational requirements would make this a "Must Cite" paper.

---

### 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

- **Closest Neighbors:** Button (2019) on film credits; Slattery & Zidar (2020) on business incentives; Lang & Lehmann (2012) on racial gaps.
- **Positioning:** It builds on the "place-based policies" literature but acts as a bridge to the "labor market discrimination" literature.
- **Missing Literature:** It should engage more with the **Sociology of Hollywood** (occupational silos) and **Social Network** literature in labor economics. The "casting gap" isn't just about firms; it's about who knows the person hiring the construction crew.

---

### 4. NARRATIVE ARC

- **Setup:** States spend billions to create a "Hollywood" at home.
- **Tension:** We know these jobs are high-paying, but the industry is notoriously non-diverse. Can a check from the taxpayer fix the "Whiteness" of film?
- **Resolution:** Only partially. It works for Hispanic workers (who might already be in the relevant supply chains) but fails for Black workers.
- **Implications:** Policy designers cannot assume "local hiring" leads to "diverse hiring." Credits need social equity requirements to close the gap.

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong. It moves from a macro policy question to a micro distributional puzzle.

### 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

**The Dinner Party Fact:** "States gave $10 billion to film studios to create jobs, and in Georgia, film employment tripled, but Black workers got virtually zero of those new jobs." 
**Reaction:** People lean in. It's a provocative finding that contradicts the "rising tide lifts all boats" narrative of local development.

### 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

- **Front-load the Mechanism:** The "Discussion" (Section 6) contains the most interesting theory (above-the-line vs. below-the-line). Move a summary of this into the Introduction.
- **The NC Repeal:** This is a "cool" result (Panel B, Table 4). It should be more prominent. It provides the "symmetry" that proves the original effect wasn't a fluke.
- **Data Appendix:** The cell suppression issue (Section 5) is a bit worrying. I'd like to see a more robust handling of this in the main text to ensure the "zero" for Black workers isn't just a "suppressed small number."

### 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The gap is **Ambition.** Right now, it’s a very clean, very competent empirical exercise. To get into the AER, the author needs to move beyond "X happened" to "X happened because of Y." 

**The Single Biggest Improvement:** The author should merge their QWI data with state-level data on the racial composition of the *underlying* industries (Construction, Transportation, Catering). If they can show that film credits only help groups that are already "over-represented" in the local support industries, they turn a "film paper" into a "general theory of industrial policy incidence."
