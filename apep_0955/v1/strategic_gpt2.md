## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether one of the New Deal’s most racially harmful agricultural policies had an unintended effect on Black children’s human capital. It argues that the AAA cotton acreage reduction program, by collapsing demand for child field labor and displacing Black sharecropping families, increased schooling for children who were at school-attendance ages when the policy hit, with later occupational gains.

A busy economist should care because this is a potentially striking inversion of a canonical historical narrative: a policy remembered as destructive may also have shifted children from labor into school, with consequences for racial inequality, structural transformation, and the economics of child labor.

**Does the paper itself articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Mostly, but not optimally. The current introduction gets to the point quickly, which is good, but it leads with the historical injustice and then pivots to “what happened to the children.” That is interesting, but the real hook is sharper than that: **can a labor-demand collapse increase human capital among children in poor agricultural households, even when the shock itself is harmful?** The current opening also mixes the descriptive historical claim, the empirical design, and the contribution too early. It needs a cleaner conceptual setup.

**What the first two paragraphs should say instead:**

> The Agricultural Adjustment Act’s cotton program is typically remembered as a racial injustice: federal payments to reduce acreage led Southern landlords to evict large numbers of Black sharecroppers. But for children in those households, the program may also have changed a central economic tradeoff. In the cotton South, school attendance competed directly with child field labor. When the AAA reduced cotton acreage, it may have lowered the opportunity cost of schooling precisely for children at school-attendance ages.
>
> This paper studies that question using linked census records that follow Black farm children from 1930 to 1950. I show that within displaced families, children who were school-aged when the AAA hit attained more education than their younger siblings in the same household, with later occupational gains. The broader point is that destructive economic shocks can have heterogeneous effects within families: even as the AAA harmed Black sharecropping households overall, it may have shifted some children from labor into human capital investment.

That is the pitch. It is about a world question, not a data exercise.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:**  
The paper shows that the AAA cotton program, despite harming Black sharecropping households, appears to have increased schooling and later occupational attainment for Black children who were at school-attendance ages when cotton labor demand collapsed.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?

- a historical linked-census paper,
- with sibling fixed effects,
- on a famous New Deal episode.

That is method-plus-setting. AER papers need the substantive novelty to dominate the econometric packaging.

The differentiation should be against at least three neighboring conversations:

1. **AAA/New Deal/racial inequality history** — papers documenting that the AAA harmed Black tenants and sharecroppers.
2. **Historical human capital formation of Black Southerners** — papers on Rosenwald schools, school quality, migration, etc.
3. **Child labor/opportunity cost of schooling** — theory and modern evidence.
4. **Structural transformation/agricultural decline and education** — mechanization or labor-demand shifts affecting schooling.

The paper currently nods to all four but does not sharply say: “those papers show X; I show Y.” It needs a simple contrast table in prose.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a gap in a literature?
It starts with the world, which is good, but it drifts into literature-gap language (“what has never been measured,” “first individual-level evidence,” “within-family design”). That weakens the pitch. The stronger framing is:

- In poor agricultural economies, when child labor demand falls, do children actually gain schooling?
- Did this mechanism matter in one of the most consequential racialized labor shocks in U.S. history?

That is a world question.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?
Not quite cleanly. They could probably say:  
“It's a sibling fixed-effects paper on the AAA showing Black kids got more education when cotton labor demand fell.”

That is decent, but still too close to “another DiD paper about X.” The paper needs to make the conceptual novelty stick: **the unintended human-capital consequences of a racially discriminatory labor-demand shock**.

### What would make this contribution bigger?
Several possibilities:

1. **Make the paper more explicitly about child labor rather than about the AAA per se.**  
   The AAA is the historical event; the general result is about the opportunity cost of schooling. The latter is bigger.

2. **Clarify whether the paper is about relative within-family reallocation or absolute gains in human capital.**  
   The Discussion admits the key limitation: the paper identifies relative sibling effects, not average effects on all children. That caveat is important and currently undercuts the headline. To make the contribution bigger, the paper needs to lean into the more precise claim rather than oversell “the AAA increased human capital” in a broad sense.

3. **Bring mechanism evidence to center stage.**  
   The occupation timing pattern is suggestive, but if there are any direct measures of school attendance, weeks worked, child labor, school term, or seasonal absence, those would make the story much larger. Right now the mechanism is plausible but somewhat inferred.

4. **Tie the result to long-run racial inequality and structural change more directly.**  
   The paper hints at Great Migration and rising returns to schooling. If it can show that the schooling gains mattered for urban transition or occupational sorting beyond one occupational index, the stakes rise materially.

5. **Reframe away from “accidentally invested” unless the evidence can support that rhetoric.**  
   That title is vivid but currently somewhat too sweeping relative to what is identified. The bigger contribution is not that the state “invested,” but that a coercive labor-market shock changed children’s time allocation in ways that raised education at the margin.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Before this paper, the world looks like this: the AAA cotton program is understood as a large-scale racial injustice that displaced Black sharecroppers and worsened household welfare. At the same time, in the cotton South, child labor and schooling were direct substitutes.

### Tension
The motivating tension is strong: if the policy harmed Black families, should we expect children to do worse because household resources fell, or better because the main alternative to school—cotton labor—collapsed? That tension is genuinely interesting.

### Resolution
The paper’s resolution is that school-age children in more exposed places appear to gain education relative to their siblings, and those gains later show up in occupational attainment. The interpretation is that the opportunity-cost channel dominated for children at the schooling margin.

### Implications
The implication is not that the AAA was good. It is that harmful structural shocks can have offsetting developmental effects for children when they reduce child labor demand. More broadly, the paper suggests that the incidence of policy shocks differs within families and across children’s ages.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
It has the ingredients, but the arc is not fully disciplined. Right now it is somewhere between a story and a result bundle.

What weakens the arc:

- The paper opens with the moral-historical narrative, then jumps quickly to design.
- The main result is strongest for 1940 education, but the text sometimes talks as if it has a fully developed long-run human capital story.
- There is some inconsistency across tables and prose about which education outcome is primary.
- The caveat in the Discussion is actually central to the story, but it appears late.

A cleaner story is:

3. **That creates an ambiguous prediction for schooling.**

5. **This suggests a broader lesson: labor-demand shocks can raise human capital at the margin even when they lower family welfare overall.**

That is a real narrative. Right now the paper sometimes slips into “surprising finding + design + contribution list” rather than sustaining the tension.

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?
I would say:

> “The AAA cotton program probably harmed Black sharecropping households overall, but within those families, kids who were school-age when cotton acreage was cut ended up with more schooling and somewhat better occupations later on.”

That is the fact.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
They would **lean in initially**, because the result cuts against a familiar narrative and raises a nontrivial conceptual question. The historical setting is big enough and the reversal is surprising enough.

But the next 30 seconds matter. If the presenter immediately slips into “I use sibling fixed effects and a county proxy...,” attention will drop. If instead the presenter says, “the mechanism is that removing child labor opportunities can increase schooling even during a harmful shock,” attention will stay.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Almost certainly:

- “Is this an absolute gain or just a relative gain across siblings within harmed families?”
- “How do you know it’s about child labor rather than migration or schooling supply?”
- “How big is this in substantive terms?”
- “Does this matter for long-run racial inequality, or is it a modest within-household reshuffling?”

Those are exactly the questions the framing should anticipate.

### If the findings are modest, is that a problem?
The effect size is not enormous, but it is not trivial either. A gain of roughly 0.38 years of schooling in this setting is potentially meaningful. The problem is not effect size; it is **interpretation scope**.

The paper does make a reasonable case that the result is interesting even if modest, because it overturns what many would have predicted for children. But it must be more careful to present the result as a **mitigating channel** rather than as a redemptive coda to the AAA. “Displacement dividend” is memorable, but it risks sounding glib relative to the underlying historical violence and the paper’s own admitted limitation.
