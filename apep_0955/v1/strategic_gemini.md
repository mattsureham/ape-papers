### 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
The paper examines the 1933 Agricultural Adjustment Act (AAA), which paid Southern landowners to idle cotton acreage, leading to the mass displacement of Black sharecroppers. It asks how this shock affected the long-run human capital of the children in those families. Surprisingly, it finds that school-aged children in high-exposure counties gained ~0.4 years of education and higher adult occupational status compared to their siblings, because the policy destroyed the child labor market that previously competed with schooling.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this pitch remarkably well. The first two paragraphs clearly state the historical context, the "surprising" central finding, and the mechanism. It immediately signals that this is not just a "damage" paper, but a "mechanism" paper.

---

### 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
The paper identifies an "unintended dividend" of a discriminatory policy: by destroying demand for child agricultural labor, the AAA reduced the opportunity cost of schooling for Black children at the margin.

- **Differentiation:** It differentiates itself from the "New Deal as harm" literature (Alston & Ferrie) by finding a silver lining in developmental outcomes, and from the "Great Migration" literature (Boustan) by using a within-family design to isolate the labor-demand shock.
- **Framing:** It is framed as a question about the **WORLD** (how do shocks to child labor demand affect long-run human capital?) rather than just a gap in New Deal history.
- **Clarity:** A smart economist would immediately understand the "opportunity cost" story. It is not "another DiD paper"; it is a "counter-intuitive mechanism" paper.
- **Bigger Contribution:** To make it even bigger, the author could more explicitly link this to the "Quantity-Quality" trade-off literature or provide more evidence on whether this educational gain facilitated the Great Migration (it currently suggests children "stayed in place" in the short run).

---

### 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
The paper sits at the intersection of Economic History, Development (Child Labor), and Labor (Human Capital).

- **Neighbors:** Aaronson & Mazumder (2011) on Rosenwald schools; Hornbeck (2012) on environmental shocks; Basu & Van (1998) on child labor theory.
- **Strategy:** It **synthesizes** these. It takes a development theory (Basu & Van) and tests it using a historical U.S. "natural experiment." 
- **Conversation:** It is having the right conversation, but it could speak more to the **Development** community. This is a paper about "Structural Transformation and Human Capital" that happens to be set in 1930s Alabama.

---

### 4. NARRATIVE ARC
- **Setup:** The cotton South relied on child labor; schooling was an afterthought.
- **Tension:** A federal policy (AAA) displaced families and was intended to help landowners, seemingly harming Black families across the board.
- **Resolution:** Because the policy killed the cotton labor market, children had nothing to do *but* go to school. This "accidental investment" paid off in better jobs by 1950.
- **Implications:** Reducing the demand for child labor is a powerful (if accidental) tool for human capital accumulation.

**Evaluation:** The narrative arc is very strong. It follows a "Paradox" structure: *Why did a harmful policy lead to better long-run outcomes for children?*

---

### 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
- **The Lead Fact:** "The New Deal's mass eviction of Black sharecroppers actually boosted the education of their children by nearly half a year."
- **Reaction:** People will lean in. It challenges the "unambiguously harmful" consensus.
- **Follow-up:** "Did they end up moving to the North because of that education?" (The paper currently says they stayed in the short run, which is a bit of a tension).

---

### 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
- **Front-loading:** The paper is well front-loaded. Table 2 delivers the goods.
- **Mechanism Section:** The "Gender Heterogeneity" and "Occupational Status Flip" (lower status in 1940, higher in 1950) are the strongest parts of the story and should be emphasized more.
- **Appendix:** The "Standardized Effect Sizes" (Table 6) is very helpful for the "So What?" test and should stay prominent.

---

### 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The paper is already highly ambitious and technically competent. The "AER gap" is primarily about **Generalizability**. 

**The Single Most Impactful Advice:** Explicitly connect the findings to the "Structural Transformation" of the South. Did this educational gain provide the "fuel" for the later stages of the Great Migration or the Civil Rights movement? If the author can show that this 0.4-year gain was the catalyst for leaving the South entirely by 1960 or 1970, it becomes a "Big History" paper.
