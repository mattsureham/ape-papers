## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether one of the most infamous racially discriminatory New Deal policies—the AAA cotton acreage reduction program—had an unintended effect on Black children’s human capital. The core claim is that by collapsing demand for child labor in cotton, the policy pushed school-age Black children in displaced sharecropping families to acquire more education and later sort into better occupations.

A busy economist should care because this is, potentially, a striking and uncomfortable result: a policy remembered primarily for harming Black families may also have altered the transition from child labor to schooling in the rural South. That speaks not just to New Deal history, but to the broader economics of child labor, structural change, and human capital formation under coercive or discriminatory institutions.

**Does the paper articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Mostly, but not optimally. The current opening has a strong historical fact and a strong result, but it slips too quickly into “what has never been measured” and then into design. The paper’s real hook is not “we measure children” or “we use siblings”; it is the world-level question: **when a labor-demand shock destroys children’s outside option, does schooling rise even in a deeply discriminatory setting?**

**What the first two paragraphs should say instead:**

> The Agricultural Adjustment Act’s cotton program is remembered as a major racial injustice: landlords were paid to idle land, and hundreds of thousands of Black sharecroppers were displaced. But the same shock also reduced the demand for children’s labor in cotton fields. Did that make Black children worse off along every margin, or did it unintentionally increase schooling for those at the age when farm work most directly competed with education?
>
> This paper shows that school-age Black children in more cotton-dependent counties gained education relative to their siblings who were younger or older when the AAA hit. Using linked 1930–1940–1950 census records and within-family comparisons, I find that children at the school-attendance margin acquired more schooling and later entered higher-status occupations. The paper’s main point is not that displacement was benign; it is that labor-demand shocks can increase human capital even when they arise from discriminatory and harmful institutions.

That is the pitch the paper should own.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:**  
The paper shows that the AAA’s racially unequal disruption of Southern cotton agriculture reduced the opportunity cost of schooling for Black children at the school-attendance margin, generating relative gains in education and later occupational status within affected families.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?
Not yet clearly enough. The paper says “first individual-level evidence” and “within-family design,” but those are not, by themselves, contributions that matter to AER readers. The contribution needs to be differentiated substantively, not just methodologically.

Right now, the paper risks sounding like:
- another historical linked-census paper,
- another sibling FE paper,
- another paper on an unintended consequence of the New Deal.

What is actually new is the **combination**:
1. a well-known episode of racialized economic displacement,
2. a child-labor-to-schooling mechanism,
3. long-run human capital consequences,
4. an ironic sign reversal relative to the canonical historical narrative.

That combination should be emphasized more sharply.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or as filling a gap in a LITERATURE?
It starts with the world, which is good, but then it quickly reverts to literature-gap framing (“what has never been measured,” “contributes to three literatures,” “innovation here is the within-family design”). That is weaker.

The stronger framing is:
- **World question:** When agricultural child labor collapses, do children move into school?
- **Historical stakes:** Did one of the New Deal’s most exclusionary policies also accelerate human capital accumulation for some children?
- **General lesson:** Labor-demand shocks can matter for education through opportunity costs, not just incomes or school supply.

### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what's new?
At present, maybe, but not cleanly. They might say: “It’s a historical DiD/sibling paper showing the AAA increased Black children’s schooling.” That is decent, but still sounds like “another DiD paper about X.”

You want them to say:
> “It argues that the AAA, despite displacing Black sharecroppers, shut down child labor in cotton and thereby increased schooling for Black children who were school-age at the time. It’s a paper about how a racist labor-market shock changed human capital investment.”

That is sharper and more memorable.

### What would make this contribution bigger?
Several possibilities, in descending order of importance:

1. **Make the object of interest larger than ‘the AAA’.**  
   Frame it as a paper about **child labor, structural change, and racial inequality**, with the AAA as the shock. Right now the policy episode is bigger than the economic question; in an AER paper, it should be the reverse.

2. **Strengthen the life-cycle payoff margin.**  
   The 1950 occupational result helps, but “higher occupational score by 1950” still feels modest. If the data permit, a stronger adult-outcome margin—earnings, urban residence, migration destination quality, literacy, home ownership, marriage to more educated spouses, or next-generation outcomes—would enlarge the contribution materially.

3. **Make the mechanism more central and visible.**  
   The paper says opportunity cost of schooling is the mechanism, but the current draft still reads as if mechanism is inferred rather than dramatized. A stronger age-by-seasonality / crop dependence / child-labor intensity comparison would make the story feel bigger and more convincing conceptually.

4. **Clarify whether this is a reallocation story or a protection story.**  
   Is the paper about reduced child labor, delayed labor-market entry, altered migration timing, or changed family adaptation under displacement? The paper gestures at all four. Picking one dominant mechanism would make the contribution more legible.

- The child labor literature established that reducing labor demand can increase schooling.
- The historical human capital literature showed that early-life shocks have long-run consequences.
- **This paper connects those literatures in a setting where the shock is racially targeted and morally fraught.**

That is a synthesis paper with a striking substantive result. That is stronger than picking fights.

Oddly, both.

The paper could speak more directly to:

It also undersells connections to the literature on how reductions in child labor arise from demand-side changes rather than school-side interventions. That is likely the most interesting general-economic conversation here.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?
Not fully. Right now it is having a somewhat safe conversation: “historical linked data + New Deal racial policy + education.” The more impactful conversation is:

> What happens to children when an economic system stops valuing their labor?

That is a broader and more resonant economic question. The AAA is then the historically specific shock that lets the paper answer it.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
The world before the paper: in the cotton South, Black children’s time was split between school and field labor; the AAA disrupted cotton production and displaced Black sharecroppers; historians have treated this as a case of severe racial harm.

### Tension
The unresolved question is whether the destruction of child labor opportunities made children unequivocally worse off, or whether it shifted some of them from fields into school. That is a real tension because income loss and displacement point one way, while lower opportunity costs of schooling point the other.

### Resolution
The paper finds that Black children who were school-age when the AAA struck acquired more schooling relative to siblings and later moved into higher-status occupations. The interpretation is that the shock reduced the pull of child labor at a pivotal age.

### Implications
Economists should update on two fronts:
1. harmful and discriminatory policies can generate offsetting developmental effects through labor-demand channels;
2. human capital accumulation can respond strongly to reductions in child labor demand even when there is no direct schooling intervention.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
It has the ingredients, but the arc is not fully disciplined. The paper currently reads as:
- dramatic historical opening,
- design paragraph,
- mechanism paragraph,
- three-literature contribution paragraph,
- then tables.

That is competent, but a bit assembled. The strongest story is:

1. The AAA displaced Black sharecroppers.
2. That harmed families, but also destroyed child labor demand.
3. For children near the school-attendance margin, those two forces pointed in opposite directions.
4. The paper shows the opportunity-cost channel dominated on relative human capital outcomes.
5. This changes how we think about labor shocks, child labor, and the micro-incidence of discriminatory policy.

That should be the spine. Right now the method intrudes too early and weakens the story.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?
I would say:

> “The AAA is remembered for displacing Black sharecroppers. This paper claims that for school-age Black children, that same shock increased schooling because it wiped out child labor in cotton.”

That is a genuinely provocative fact.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
They would lean in—at least initially. The result is surprising, morally complicated, and connected to a famous policy episode. That is good AER material in principle.

The danger is the second sentence. If the explanation becomes “using sibling FE with county-level Black farm share as a proxy...,” people will start reaching for their phones. So the paper needs to keep the headline economically intuitive and historically important.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Probably one of these:
- “Is this just a relative sibling effect, or did these children actually end up better off in absolute terms?”
- “How much of this is reduced child labor versus migration or family breakup?”
- “Does the schooling gain matter enough to offset any of the economic harm?”
- “Why should I interpret this as a general lesson rather than an oddity of one historical setting?”

The current paper partially anticipates the first question in the discussion, which is smart. But that issue needs to be brought forward earlier because it is the central interpretive challenge.

### If the findings are modest: is that okay?
Yes, because the sign is what is interesting. The estimated schooling effect is not enormous, but it is meaningful. More importantly, the paper is not “policy X changed education by 0.1σ.” It is “a notorious racist displacement shock accidentally increased schooling.” That is inherently interesting.

But the paper needs to make the case that the null/weak 1950 education outcome and modest occupational effects still matter because the key contribution is about **mechanism and sign**, not intervention magnitude.
