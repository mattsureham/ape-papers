**Idea Fidelity**  
The paper substantially diverges from the original idea manifest. Rather than testing whether CRD IV/Basel III–induced bank branch closures generated regional populist vote swings, it shifts focus to whether these regulatory changes produced regional economic “deserts” (employment, unemployment, GDP). The manifest promised a NUTS2 panel linking branch declines to populist voting, exploiting IBank branch loss allocation via pre-shock financial employment shares, an SSM treatment variation, IV leveraging cooperative bank shares, and CS-DiD for staggered transposition. None of these elements appear: the paper neither studies political outcomes nor implements the proposed IV/CS-DiD strategy. Instead, it reinterprets the Bartik design purely at the economic outcome level and concludes that the supposed negative effect is a “composition illusion.” As an AER: Insights submission, fidelity to the submitted idea is low.

**Summary**  
The paper retools the proposed project into an empirical note that examines whether regions with high pre-shock financial employment shares suffered economically after CRD IV/Basel III. Using a simple Bartik-style interaction between the 2008 NACE K share and post-2014 years, the results show a strong positive association with total employment, driven by faster non-financial employment growth in high-share (mainly financial center) regions. Event-study evidence documents pre-trends, and additional diagnostics argue the result reflects a “composition illusion” rather than causal exposure to retail banking contraction.

**Essential Points**  
1. **Identification is invalidated by the very pattern the paper uncovers.** The Bartik estimator assumes that pre-shock financial employment shares proxy exogenous exposure to regulatory tightening. The event study shows large, monotonic pre-trends beginning in 2009, and the pre-treatment linear trend regression rejects parallel trends. In the absence of a credible instrument or control for these differential trends, the core coefficient cannot be interpreted causally. At minimum, the authors need to either (a) re-establish identification with stronger exogenous variation (e.g., IV based on heterogeneous cooperative bank exposure as originally proposed) or (b) abandon causal claims and reframe the paper as describing correlations and the dangers of composition bias. Making a causal claim with glaring pre-trend violations renders the main result unreliable.

2. **The empirical strategy does not match the paper’s stated research question.** The abstract and title promise a test of whether branch closures created regional “economic deserts,” implying a focus on causation from regulatory shock to outcomes in branch-dependent regions. Yet the estimation uses total NACE K employment share, a broadly defined indicator that conflates multiple activities, without any credible source of exogenous variation in branch closure intensity (e.g., actual branch counts, cooperative bank concentration, or regional exposure to regulatory compliance costs). As such, the empirical approach cannot speak to the stated question. The authors need to either: (i) construct and leverage more precise measures of branch closures and regulatory burden (e.g., actual branch microdata, cooperative bank reliance, SSM vs. non-SSM timing) or (ii) explicitly frame the exercise as diagnostic/cautionary regarding shift-share designs rather than as an evaluation of CRD IV’s regional economic impact.

3. **Key pieces of the original identification strategy are absent.** The manifest proposed allocating national branch losses to regions using pre-shock NACE K employment shares, exploiting SSM introduction for Eurozone vs. non-Eurozone variation, and using IV based on cooperative bank market shares. None of these are implemented. Without the promised cross-sectional or instrumented variation, the results lack the variation necessary to isolate the regulatory shock’s impact. The authors should either implement the promised methodology or justify why it was unfeasible, providing the alternative identification path and describing its limitations in the paper.

**Suggestions**  
- **Reframe the paper’s contribution.** If the authors stick with the observed “positive” association and emphasize the composition illusion, the paper should be presented as a methodological cautionary tale about shift-share designs in financial geography, rather than as evidence that CRD IV caused no harm. Explicitly state in the introduction and conclusion that the exercise estimates a correlation driven by pre-existing differences, and that causal inference fails without additional sources of exogenous variation.

- **Leverage richer data on branch closures.** The manifest identifies ECB SSI data, GERDA, and EBF reports as sources documenting the 110K closures. Incorporating this branch-level data (even in aggregate) would allow constructing a more direct treatment variable: regional branch change rates, number of small bank closures, or exposure to cooperative bank consolidation. This would enable a more credible test of the regulation → branch closure → regional economy channel. At minimum, provide evidence on the relationship between actual branch contractions and the financial employment share to justify the latter as a proxy.

- **Implement the promised identification enhancements.** The original manifest proposed: (a) translating national branch loss into regional exposure using pre-shock NACE K employment shares (a Bartik decomposition, but applied to actual branch losses rather than overall employment); (b) exploiting SSM’s staggered introduction and CRD IV transposition dates (2013–2016) for variation; (c) using IV based on pre-2008 cooperative bank market share interacted with compliance costs. These elements could salvage causal inference. For example, interact regional cooperative bank employment shares with CRD IV transpostion timing, or use differential timing of national compliance (some countries delayed implementation until after 2014). If these variations are still feasible, implementing them would align the paper with the intended research question.

- **Control for differential trends using pre-treatment information.** Even if additional instruments are unavailable, the authors can attempt to control for the observed pre-trends by including region-specific linear (or higher-order) time trends or by using synthetic control/in-time placebo approaches. While not fully solving the identification issue, such robustness checks would clarify how much of the post-2014 association is driven by the pre-existing trajectory.

- **Explore heterogeneous effects by rural vs. urban or branch-density regions.** The composition illusion hinges on financial centers dominating the high-share regions. Showing how the treatment correlates with urbanization, branch density, or rural shares would help readers understand why the initial design fails. This also paves the way for future work: if the authors can identify truly branch-dependent regions (e.g., rural cooperative bank areas), they could test whether these specific areas experienced negative shocks even if the overall NACE K share does not capture it.

- **Align title and abstract with the actual findings.** The current title (“The Composition Illusion: Why European Bank Branch Closures Did Not Create Regional Economic Deserts”) promises a causal refutation that the paper cannot deliver. Either soften the claim (“…appears not to have created…”), or reframe the title to emphasize the methodological lesson (e.g., “The Composition Illusion: Cautionary Evidence on Shift-Share Identification with Financial Employment Shares”). Similarly, the abstract should clarify that the Bartik estimate captures selection rather than treatment, so the paper documents the pitfalls rather than the policy impact.

- **Include more detailed data construction and measurement discussion.** The appendix should expand on how the treatment, outcomes, and controls are constructed. For instance, how are employment changes measured (“percentage change relative to 2008” is mentioned, but is this log-difference or simple percentage point change)? Are any regions or years dropped due to missingness? How sensitive are the results to alternative definitions (e.g., raw employment levels, log employment)? Providing these details will aid reproducibility.

- **Consider alternative outcomes or mechanisms.** Since the main economic outcomes do not move in the hypothesized direction, the authors might explore other margins where branch closures could plausibly have effects (e.g., small business formation, credit volumes, consumer spending in rural areas). Even without causal identification, documenting where the closures left traces would add value.

In summary, to make this paper suitable for publication the authors must realign the empirical strategy with the posed question, either by implementing a more credible identification approach and using the promised data sources or by reframing the paper around the methodological caution offered by the composition illusion.
