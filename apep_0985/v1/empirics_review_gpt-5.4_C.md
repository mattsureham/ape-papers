1. **Idea Fidelity**

The paper pursues the broad research question in the manifest—whether catalytic-converter anti-theft laws reduced theft, and whether falling palladium prices rather than deterrence explain the observed decline—but it departs from the original design in consequential ways.

Most importantly, the paper abandons the proposed crime data. The manifest’s core empirical design relied on NIBRS state-month theft counts (“Theft of Motor Vehicle Parts or Accessories”), potentially validated with NICB insurance claims. Instead, the paper uses Google Trends search intensity for “catalytic converter theft” as the primary outcome. That is not a minor substitution. It changes the estimand from theft incidence to a noisy composite of victimization, media coverage, public salience, and policy attention. Given that the paper’s main challenge is endogenous legislative timing during a nationally salient crime wave, this choice weakens the design substantially.

Second, the intended decomposition was to use staggered DiD plus commodity-price interactions to separately identify price effects, law effects, and possibly their interaction. The paper does estimate a treatment-by-price interaction, but it does not really “decompose” theft declines into price and law components in a convincing way because palladium price varies only nationally over time and is fully collinear with month fixed effects except through the interaction. Thus the only identified object is whether treated states evolve differentially from controls when national prices are high versus low. That is a useful heterogeneity exercise, but it is not the manifest’s more ambitious decomposition.

Third, the paper raises but does not exploit policy heterogeneity meaningfully. The manifest emphasized differences across felony penalties, scrap dealer regulations, and marking requirements. Here the laws are largely collapsed into a single treatment indicator, despite obvious differences in likely mechanisms. That misses one of the more promising dimensions of the original idea.

2. **Summary**

This paper studies staggered adoption of state catalytic-converter anti-theft laws during the 2021–2023 crime wave and asks whether these laws reduced theft, net of the simultaneous collapse in palladium prices. Using state-month Google Trends search interest as the outcome, the paper finds no average treatment effect, but reports that laws appear more effective when palladium prices are low and ineffective when prices are high, which the author interprets as a Becker-style “deterrence discount.”

The topic is timely and potentially important. But in its current form, the paper does not yet deliver a persuasive, economically meaningful causal result because the outcome measure is too weak, policy timing is clearly endogenous, and the central interaction estimates are difficult to interpret as deterrence rather than residual policy endogeneity.

3. **Essential Points**

**1. The outcome variable is not adequate for the claims being made.**  
Google Trends for “catalytic converter theft” is too indirect to support a paper about theft incidence and deterrence. Search behavior is likely driven by media coverage, insurer campaigns, dealership warnings, and the passage of the laws themselves. Indeed, laws may increase searches mechanically by raising awareness, which makes the positive coefficients at high prices especially hard to interpret. For an AER: Insights paper, this is the biggest problem. You need to move the main analysis to actual crime or claims data—NIBRS thefts of motor vehicle parts/accessories, NICB insurance claims, or both—and use Google Trends at most as a supplementary validation exercise.

**2. The identifying assumptions are currently not credible, and the paper’s own placebo test shows why.**  
A placebo treatment 12 months before enactment yields a large, significant positive effect. That is not a benign footnote; it is evidence that adoption is strongly related to prior outcome dynamics. The paper then asserts that this “does not invalidate” the price interaction because it compares treated states across price regimes. That is too quick. Since price is common nationally and adoption is concentrated in 2021–2023, the interaction is picking up whether states that adopted at different moments of the national wave had different post-treatment paths. Without a stronger design, that can easily reflect endogenous timing and differential mean reversion rather than deterrence varying with criminal returns. You need event-study evidence stratified by early versus late adopters, tighter controls for pre-trends, and ideally a design anchored on predetermined institutional timing (e.g., legislative session constraints) or policy content.

**3. The magnitudes and statistical presentation need much more discipline.**  
The coefficient estimates are unstable across specifications: +0.356 in TWFE, essentially 0 in CS, −0.426 with state trends, and then a huge level shift of −10.081 plus +1.445 × log price in the interaction model. Those are not easy to reconcile, especially with an outcome whose support is IHS of a 0–100 index and whose overall SD is about 1.5. The implied net effects depend heavily on extrapolation to an uninterpretable baseline of log price = 0. Standard econometric practice here is to center log palladium price at a meaningful value (say the sample mean), so the main effect is interpretable. More broadly, the paper leans heavily on 10 percent significance and multiple close-to-threshold p-values. The standard errors clustered at the state level may be acceptable with roughly 47 clusters, but because the key source of heterogeneity comes from a common national time series, inference for the interaction terms deserves more care—at minimum wild cluster bootstrap for the interaction specifications and a discussion of the limited effective time-series variation.

4. **Suggestions**

The paper has a good question and a potentially publishable setting, but it needs a substantial redesign around stronger data and cleaner interpretation. The first-order improvement is obvious: use actual theft outcomes. NIBRS is not perfect, but “motor vehicle parts or accessories” thefts are much closer to the object of interest than search intensity. If state-month NIBRS coverage is uneven, show coverage rates, restrict to consistently reporting agencies/states, and aggregate carefully. NICB claims are especially attractive because catalytic converter theft was a prominent insured event; even if monthly state claims are not readily public, annual or semiannual state panels could still be informative. A paper that triangulates NIBRS and NICB would be far stronger than one built on Google Trends.

Relatedly, validate the outcome carefully. If you insist on keeping Google Trends in the paper, demonstrate that it tracks actual theft measures in overlapping data. A simple plot or panel regression of state-month Trends on NIBRS/NICB outcomes would go a long way. Right now the paper cites general Google Trends papers, but that is not enough. This is a highly specific search term in a high-media-attention environment.

On the econometric side, I would simplify and sharpen the design. Start with a clean event-study using actual theft data, estimated with Sun-Abraham or Callaway-Sant’Anna, and present cohort-specific dynamics. The central question is whether theft falls after laws take effect relative to plausible controls. Only once that baseline is credible should you layer on price interactions. And when you do, it would be better to estimate heterogeneity in a framework that respects staggered adoption rather than relying mainly on TWFE interactions. There are now several approaches for interacted staggered-treatment designs; at a minimum, you could estimate ATT separately for periods/cohorts defined by high- versus low-price environments or by early versus late adopters.

The treatment variable also deserves refinement. These laws are not homogeneous. Dealer regulations and delayed-payment rules plausibly affect fencing opportunities; felony upgrades may affect punishment severity but not disposal margins; VIN marking is different again. If the paper’s substantive conclusion is that “penalties fail when returns are high,” then it is important to separate laws that raise expected punishment from laws that directly reduce resale value. It may well turn out that dealer regulations work better than penalty enhancements, which would be both more credible and more policy relevant than the current pooled result.

I would also encourage you to think harder about policy timing. The paper currently states that timing reflects legislative calendars and salience, but that is asserted rather than used. Legislative session timing, partisan control, preexisting scrap-metal regulation, or neighboring-state policy diffusion may help explain adoption in ways that can be exploited descriptively or even quasi-experimentally. Even if you do not have a full instrument, showing that adoption aligns with institutional calendars rather than just theft spikes would improve confidence. Conversely, if timing is mostly reactive to rising theft, you should be frank that average treatment effects are difficult to identify and reframe the paper more modestly as documenting heterogeneous correlations rather than deterrence.

The interpretation of magnitudes should be made more transparent. IHS units of a Google Trends index are not intuitive. Translate coefficients into percentage changes at representative baseline values, or better, into changes in actual theft counts once you move to better data. The current claim that a low-price effect of −0.299 is “one-fifth of a pre-treatment standard deviation” is not especially informative for policy. A policymaker wants to know whether a law prevented 5 percent, 20 percent, or essentially none of thefts.

There are also some internal consistency issues to clean up. The paper alternates between 34 and 35 treated states, and between 12 and 13 never-treated states. The quartile cutoffs differ between text and table. The “deterrence discount” table appears malformed and does not clearly correspond to a conventional regression output. These are fixable, but they matter because the paper is already asking readers to trust a somewhat unusual design.

I would substantially temper the rhetoric. “Becker was right” is too strong for the evidence currently shown. At most, the paper suggests that any deterrent effect of these laws is weaker in high-price periods. But because the data and identification are not yet tight, the contribution should be framed more cautiously: this setting appears consistent with the idea that punishment-based interventions may be less effective when criminal returns are elevated, and stronger evidence can be obtained with better outcome data.

Finally, I think the paper should reconsider what its cleanest contribution is. The most convincing version may not be “decomposing theft declines into price and deterrence” but rather: (i) commodity prices strongly predict catalytic converter theft, (ii) states enacted laws in response to the wave, and (iii) once one accounts for commodity-price conditions and endogenous timing, there is little evidence of large average deterrence from these laws, with suggestive heterogeneity by policy type and price regime. That is already an interesting result. But to support it in AER: Insights format, the paper needs direct theft data, a more credible identification strategy, and tighter interpretation.
