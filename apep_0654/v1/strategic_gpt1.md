## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple policy question with broader labor-market implications: when states make occupational licenses portable across borders, do firms hire more workers, or do incumbent workers simply gain bargaining power? Using the staggered adoption of Universal License Recognition laws, the paper argues that easing relicensing barriers raises wages and lowers turnover in licensed sectors, with little net job creation — suggesting that portability works less as a vacancy-filling reform than as a reduction in employer monopsony over licensed workers.

A busy economist should care because this turns a familiar deregulatory policy into a different kind of economic object. Instead of being mainly about allocative efficiency and interstate mobility, the paper says license portability changes the distribution of surplus inside firms.

**Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Pretty well, actually — better than many submissions. The introduction gets to the policy, the expected channel, and the reversal of the conventional prediction quickly. But the pitch is still too policy-memo-ish and not yet sharp enough for AER. The paper currently sounds like “we evaluate ULR and find a surprising pattern.” The stronger version is “we use ULR to answer a bigger question about how removing mobility frictions affects labor-market power.”

**What the first two paragraphs should say instead:**  
Start with the broader economic question, then introduce ULR as the clean setting.

**Suggested pitch the paper should have:**

> Labor markets are shaped not just by wages and vacancies, but by frictions that limit workers’ outside options. Occupational licensing creates one such friction: even qualified workers often cannot move across states without costly relicensing, giving employers local market power over licensed labor.  
>   
> This paper studies what happens when that friction is removed. Between 2019 and 2023, 26 states adopted Universal License Recognition laws that allow out-of-state licensees to work immediately. The striking result is that license portability does not produce a hiring boom. Instead, it raises earnings and reduces separations in licensed sectors, suggesting that reducing mobility barriers primarily shifts bargaining power toward incumbent workers.

That is the AER version of the story: labor-market power, outside options, mobility frictions, then policy.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution in one sentence:**  
The paper’s contribution is to show that reducing interstate licensing frictions changes labor-market outcomes primarily through worker bargaining power and retention rather than through increased hiring or employment expansion.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?
Only partially. The paper distinguishes itself from licensing papers using CPS-style aggregate outcomes and says it is the “first establishment-side evidence on ULR effects,” but that is not yet enough. “First using QWI” is a data contribution; AER needs a conceptual contribution. The real distinction should be from papers that treat licensing reform as a labor-supply shock or mobility reform. The paper should more explicitly say: existing work asks whether portability raises mobility/employment; this paper asks whether portability alters firm-side wage-setting and turnover.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or as filling a gap in a literature?
It is mixed, but currently too much of the framing slips into literature-gap language (“first establishment-side evidence,” “contributes to three literatures”). The stronger parts of the introduction are world-facing: policymakers expected vacancies to be filled; instead wages rise and churn falls. The paper should lean harder into that.

### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what’s new?
Yes, but only because the “retention dividend” label is memorable. Without that phrase, the risk is that the paper sounds like “another staggered-adoption paper on occupational licensing.” The novelty is not the design; it is the pattern of outcomes and its interpretation. That needs to be made unmistakable.

### What would make this contribution bigger?
Several possibilities:

1. **Make the outside-option angle more direct.**  
   Right now the paper infers bargaining power from wages + lower separations + lower hiring. That is a sensible pattern, but strategically the paper would feel much bigger if it directly connected treatment effects to where interstate outside options are plausibly strongest:
   - occupations/sectors with more preexisting interstate mobility,
   - states bordering many nonadopting/adopting states,
   - occupations with weaker preexisting reciprocity compacts,
   - sectors with harder-to-replace incumbents.

2. **Broaden the implications beyond ULR.**  
   Frame the paper as evidence on how reducing worker mobility frictions affects monopsony. If this becomes a paper about the economics of outside options rather than a paper about one state reform, it gets much larger.

3. **Clarify who gains and who does not.**  
   The most provocative margin may be distributional: incumbents gain; entrants may not. If the paper can foreground that distinction, it becomes more than a policy evaluation.

4. **Connect to shortages and vacancy-filling rhetoric more tightly.**  
   If the selling point is “this widely touted shortage-fix does not expand employment,” then the comparison to the policy’s stated objective should be front and center.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Licensed workers face state-specific barriers to moving across jurisdictions. Reformers argue that removing these barriers should improve matching, fill shortages, and increase employment.

### Tension
If licensing barriers really constrain labor supply, portability should produce hiring and job creation. But if those barriers mostly suppress workers’ outside options, then removing them may instead raise wages and retention.

### Resolution
The paper finds the second pattern: higher earnings, lower separations, lower hiring, and little net job creation.

### Implications
License portability may be less a shortage-filling reform than a redistribution of bargaining power toward incumbent licensed workers. That matters for how economists think about labor-market frictions, monopsony, and the welfare logic of licensing reform.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
**Yes, more than most submissions.** The “retention dividend” gives the paper a decent spine. But the narrative is still not fully disciplined. There are moments where it becomes a collection of outcomes — wages, hires, separations, job creation, heterogeneity by industry, education compression — rather than one integrated story.

The biggest narrative weakness is that the paper tries to tell **two stories**:
1. ULR does not create a hiring boom.
2. ULR increases bargaining power and retention.

Those are related, but the second is the stronger AER story. The first is a policy surprise; the second is an economics insight.

**What story should it be telling?**  
It should be telling the story of **outside options**. ULR is valuable because it alters the fallback position of workers in licensed labor markets. Once that is the story, the outcomes line up naturally:
- wages up,
- separations down,
- hiring down because retention rises,
- no net employment surge.

The education-compression result currently feels tacked on. Unless it directly sharpens the main mechanism, it is more clutter than payoff.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?
“States made occupational licenses portable, and instead of getting a hiring boom, firms paid incumbent licensed workers more and turnover fell.”

That is a good line. People would understand it immediately.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
They would **lean in**, at least initially. The reversal of the expected mechanism is genuinely interesting. The phrase “retention dividend” also helps.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Almost certainly:

- “So did workers actually move more, or is this entirely an in-state bargaining effect?”
- Then: “Why didn’t employment rise if the barrier was removed?”
- And: “Is this about monopsony, or just composition?”

Those are exactly the strategic questions the paper needs to anticipate in framing, even if referees handle identification details.

### If the findings are modest, is that okay?
The findings are not null, but they are **modest in scope** relative to the paper’s claims. The issue is not effect size; it is whether the pattern changes what we believe. It can — if framed as evidence that mobility reforms may primarily operate through bargaining rather than quantities.

The paper mostly makes that case, but it needs to do so more confidently and more centrally. Otherwise it can read like an interesting failed shortage-filling reform, which is publishable somewhere, but not necessarily AER-level.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

This is **not primarily a methods problem**. It is mainly a **framing and ambition problem**, with some scope issues.

### What is the gap?
The paper in current form reads like a strong field-journal paper on occupational licensing reform. To become an AER paper, it needs to convince readers that ULR is not just the object of study but a setting for learning something bigger about labor markets.

The risks are:

- **Framing problem:**  
  The science is presented as a policy evaluation of ULR, when the bigger story is about mobility frictions and labor-market power.

- **Scope problem:**  
  The evidence is concentrated in a fairly aggregate state-industry design. That can still work, but then the conceptual framing has to do more heavy lifting.

- **Novelty problem:**  
  “Another paper on occupational licensing” is a real danger unless the introduction relentlessly emphasizes the unexpected mechanism.

- **Ambition problem:**  
  The paper is careful and competent, but still somewhat safe. The AER version would make a sharper claim: many reforms intended to expand labor supply may instead mostly reprice incumbent labor by changing outside options.

### Be honest: how far is it?
At present, **medium to far** from AER. Not because the core result is uninteresting — it is interesting — but because the paper has not yet fully transformed that result into a broad economics contribution.

### The single most impactful piece of advice
**Rewrite the paper around one big idea: Universal License Recognition is a natural experiment in workers’ outside options, and the key contribution is showing that reducing mobility frictions can raise wages through retention rather than expand employment through supply.**

If they only change one thing, it should be the framing. Everything else follows from that.
