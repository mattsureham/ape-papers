**Summary**

This paper studies the labor-market effects of Universal License Recognition (ULR) laws across 26 adopting states between 2019 and 2023 using Census QWI flow data. Employing a state-industry-quarter triple-difference design (licensed vs. unlicensed sectors, treated vs. untreated states, pre vs. post), it finds that ULR raises new-hire and average earnings while reducing hiring and separation rates in licensed sectors—interpretation: a “retention dividend” driven by strengthened incumbent bargaining power rather than an inflow of new workers. The paper bolsters identification with a Callaway–Sant’Anna event study, placebo checks, and an education-level heterogeneity analysis.

**Essential Points**

1. **Aggregation Level and Interpretability of Flows.** The manifest and motivation emphasize micro-level establishment dynamics (county-industry-quarter, education-by-sex decomposition). The paper aggregates to the state-industry-quarter level without explaining whether this is driven by data suppression, computational feasibility, or other constraints. Aggregation could mask important within-state variation and complicate the interpretation of flows as firm-level adjustments. Please clarify why the analysis cannot be conducted at the county-industry level or with finer education breakdowns, and assess whether the aggregation affects the parallel-trends assumption (e.g., is within-state heterogeneity driving the treatment variation?). If the aggregation is necessary, consider providing robustness checks that more directly measure firm-level dynamics (e.g., variation within large states) or justify why state-level estimates still capture the establishment-level mechanisms highlighted in the pitch.

2. **Triple-Difference Identification and Timing Variation.** The focus on licensed versus unlicensed sectors within state-by-quarter fixed effects implicitly assumes that systemic trends in the licensed/unlicensed gap do not differ between adopting and non-adopting states. Given that ULR adoption is correlated with regulatory philosophies (early adopters are mostly Republican-leaning, later ones bipartisan), these states may simultaneously pursue other labor-market reforms (e.g., scope-of-practice expansions, Medicaid changes) that affect licensed sectors differently from unlicensed ones. The paper provides only cursory discussion of such confounders. Please strengthen the identification discussion by (a) presenting differential pre-trends separately for licensed and unlicensed sectors across the two groups; (b) controlling for other state policy changes or economic shocks around the same periods (e.g., Medicaid expansion waves, pandemic aid); and (c) considering alternative controls such as state-specific linear trends or interacting time fixed effects with observable covariates. Without these additions it remains unclear whether the “retention dividend” is ULR-specific or driven by broader policy shifts that coincide with adoption.

3. **Mechanism evidence is suggestive but incomplete.** The interpretation hinges on a bargaining-power story: ULR strengthens outside options, so firms boost incumbent earnings and cut hiring/separations. While the observed patterns are consistent with this, the paper lacks direct evidence linking ULR to increased worker mobility or bargaining leverage. For instance, the education heterogeneity result—high-education workers experiencing relative declines—does not obviously map to the proposed mechanism (if anything, portable higher-credential workers should benefit most). Please either (a) present more direct proxies for bargaining power (e.g., data on quit rates, interstate inflows if available, or wage dispersion within occupations over time) or (b) refine the narrative to acknowledge that alternative mechanisms (such as post-ULR shifts in the labor demand composition or selection into licensed workers) could explain the pattern. Strengthening the mechanism section is crucial for interpreting the policy takeaway.

If these issues cannot be resolved satisfactorily, particularly the identification concerns, the paper risks overstating causal claims and should not proceed in its current form.

**Suggestions**

1. **Clarify and expand the DDD specification.** Equation (1) lumps the entire triple-difference into a single interaction. Consider reparameterizing to estimate (i) the coefficient on Post (licensed & unlicensed) to measure state-level treatment effects, (ii) the Licensed indicator interacted with state-by-quarter trends to show how the licensed vs. unlicensed gap evolves over time, and (iii) the Post × Licensed interaction as currently defined. This would make it easier for readers to see whether effects are driven by changes in licensed sectors or by unusual dynamics in unlicensed ones. Additionally, the DDD model would benefit from a robustness check where the treatment is defined separately for each licensed industry (e.g., health vs. construction) to assess whether the results are driven by one dominant sector.

2. **Provide richer event-study diagnostics.** The paper mentions the CS-DiD event study for earnings and explains that the hire-rate pre-trends are small, but no figure is shown. Include a plotted event study for both new-hire earnings and the hire/separation rates (ideally with 95% CIs) to demonstrate visually the parallel trends and dynamic responses. If the adoption timing is clustered (e.g., many states adopted in 2020), the event study may be pooling states with limited post-treatment horizons; consider reporting the number of treated states contributing to each relative-period coefficient to ensure readers understand the power in later leads/lags.

3. **Expand on heterogeneity by education and sector.** The paper currently reports that high-education workers see relative wage declines, but there is minimal unpacking of why. Delve deeper: are these patterns driven by specific sectors (e.g., healthcare vs. professional services)? Is it possible that high-education workers are a mix of licensed and non-licensed occupations? Provide more granular tables or figures showing the interaction between ULR and education within each sector. If available, consider using the sex-by-education panel to show whether the wage impacts vary by female/male workers, as licensing occupations often have gendered compositions (e.g., nurses vs. engineers). Such heterogeneity checks can make the retention-dividend story more credible or highlight its limits.

4. **Address the COVID-19 overlay more systematically.** The treatment period overlaps with the pandemic, which severely affected employment flows and could differentially influence licensed sectors (healthcare surges, construction slowdowns). The robustness check excluding 2020Q2–2021Q2 is a good start, but consider (a) interacting a COVID dummy with the treatment to see if effects differ in pandemic vs. non-pandemic quarters, (b) controlling for pandemic-related federal transfers (where data permit), and (c) discussing whether ULR adoption timing correlates with pandemic severity across states. These steps will reassure readers that the retention effect is not confounded by pandemic shocks.

5. **Elaborate on policy relevance and welfare nuances.** The conclusion touches on welfare implications, suggesting ULR benefits incumbents but not new entrants. This is an important insight—expand it by sketching a simple numerical example of the incumbent vs. entrant trade-off, or by discussing how the retention effect might interact with shortages (e.g., if hiring declines, does that exacerbate vacancy crises despite higher retention?). Drawing on existing monopsony or match-creation models (e.g., Manning (2003), Krueger-Pischke) could help anchor the narrative. If feasible, mention potential downstream effects, such as whether lower separations reduce churn costs for employers or whether higher retention might slow productivity gains from new hires.

6. **Transparency on data and estimation.** Provide more detail in the appendix about how the QWI data were aggregated (e.g., were suppressed cells imputed/swapped? Did you winsorize outliers?). Specify how the weights (hires/employment) were constructed for each specification. Finally, mention whether the standard errors account for the relatively small number of treated states (26) and whether alternative inference procedures (e.g., wild cluster bootstrap) yield similar confidence intervals; the manifest’s “randomization inference” point could be an opportunity to present such results if feasible.

Addressing these points will strengthen the paper’s credibility and clarity, making the novel contribution—establishment-level flow evidence on ULR—more convincing for policymakers and researchers alike.
