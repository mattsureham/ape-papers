## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a policy-relevant question: do states with older dam stocks face more downstream flooding because those dams were designed for outdated hydrologic conditions? Using state-year variation in dam vintage, flood disaster declarations, insurance claims, and precipitation, the paper says no: older dams are not associated with more aggregate flood disasters, suggesting that “aging infrastructure” is a weaker guide to flood risk than current policy rhetoric implies.

Does the paper itself articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs? Only partly. The current introduction gets quickly into institutional detail about TP-40 and the IIJA, but the core economic question is not stated sharply enough at the outset. The first two paragraphs should lead with the broader claim under dispute — that age-based infrastructure policy assumes old capital becomes riskier as climate changes — and then say this paper provides a direct empirical test in the dam context.

**The pitch the paper should have:**

> Policymakers increasingly argue that America’s aging infrastructure is inherently becoming more dangerous as climate changes outpace the design standards under which older assets were built. Dams are a vivid case: many were designed using mid-century precipitation assumptions, and recent federal spending has partly been justified by the idea that these “obsolete by design” structures now magnify downstream flood risk.
>
> This paper provides a direct test of that claim. Combining nationwide data on dam vintage, flood disaster declarations, flood insurance claims, and precipitation, I ask whether places with older dam stocks actually experience more flooding. At the aggregate state-year level, they do not. The result suggests that infrastructure age alone is a poor proxy for realized climate risk and that policy should target exposure and vulnerability rather than vintage per se.

That is the AER-relevant pitch: not “a dam paper,” but a paper about whether climate adaptation policy is misallocating attention by equating old infrastructure with high risk.

But these are not all equally close. In truth, the nearest intellectual neighbors are not classic dam papers; they are papers on **adaptation and the mapping from physical climate exposure to economic damages**.

It should **build on and refine** the adaptation literature, not mainly attack infrastructure policy rhetoric. The combative posture toward the IIJA is too journalistic and politically contingent. The more durable positioning is:

- climate change can alter physical hazard,
- but physical hazard does not mechanically map into realized damage,
- because institutions, maintenance, adaptation, and exposure management intervene,
- and infrastructure age by itself may be a poor sufficient statistic for risk.

That is a serious economics conversation.

### Too narrow or too broad?
Currently both, oddly enough.
- **Too narrow** in empirical execution: it is a state-year paper about dams and FEMA declarations.
- **Too broad** in rhetorical claims: it acts as if one null aggregate association can rebut an expansive narrative about aging infrastructure.

That mismatch is dangerous editorially. The paper needs a more disciplined scope: “In this setting, aggregate age-based targeting does not line up with realized disaster incidence.”

### What literature is it missing?
A few conversations seem underdeveloped:
- **Capital obsolescence and adaptation:** economics of durable capital under changing environments.
- **Public capital maintenance and depreciation:** not just infrastructure investment, but maintenance as endogenous adaptation.
- **Risk targeting / misallocation in public investment:** if vintage is a poor proxy, what proxies are better?
- **Spatial exposure / hazard mapping:** even if not causal, the paper should speak to how aggregate proxies fail when risk is highly localized.

The paper also leans heavily on disaster and climate citations but less on the economics of infrastructure asset management and maintenance. That is a missed opportunity.

### Is it having the right conversation?
Not quite. Right now the paper is having a conversation with a straw version of policy advocacy: “people say old dams are dangerous; I don’t find that.” The better conversation is with economists studying adaptation and targeting: **what observable characteristics of legacy capital actually predict climate damages?**

That repositioning would increase its relevance substantially.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
The world before this paper: policymakers and commentators believe aging infrastructure is increasingly risky because it was designed for historical climate conditions that no longer hold.

### Tension
That belief is plausible physically, but it is not obvious economically. Realized flood damage depends not just on original design standards but also on maintenance, regulation, exposure, insurance, adaptation, and geography. So does infrastructure vintage actually predict realized flood risk?

### Resolution
At the aggregate state-year level, the paper finds that older dam stocks do not predict more flood declarations or flood insurance claims. A coarse age-based “obsolescence” narrative does not show up in realized outcomes.

### Implications
Age alone may be a poor targeting criterion for adaptation investment; policymakers should focus more on specific risk determinants like exposure, storage, hazard class, and perhaps specific older cohorts rather than broad “pre-1970” categories.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
Serviceable, but uneven. It has the ingredients, but the current draft sometimes reads like a collection of regressions plus policy rhetoric. The main narrative is repeatedly interrupted by:
- too much institutional detail,
- too many precise coefficient values in the introduction,
- too many speculative mechanisms,
- and a somewhat random 1930s result that is not well integrated into the main story.

### What story should it be telling?
The story should be:

1. **Simple policy intuition:** old infrastructure designed for old climates should be riskier now.  
2. **Economic complication:** realized losses depend on adaptation and targeting, not just engineering vintage.  
3. **Empirical test:** in dams, age-based risk does not show up in aggregate outcomes.  
4. **Nuance:** what matters is not oldness per se but asset-specific exposure/risk heterogeneity; the 1930s result hints at that.  
5. **Broader lesson:** adaptation policy should move away from crude vintage proxies.

That is a coherent narrative. The current draft is close, but not disciplined enough.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?
“I tested one of the most common claims in climate infrastructure policy — that older dams create more flood risk because they were designed for outdated rainfall standards — and, at least in aggregate U.S. data, I don’t find it.”

That is a decent dinner-party line.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
Some would lean in, but only if presented as a challenge to a broad policy presumption. If presented as “state-year regressions on old dams and FEMA declarations,” they will absolutely reach for their phones.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Immediately: **“Why not?”**  
And then: **“Is that because age really doesn’t matter, or because your measure is too aggregated?”**

That second question is existential for the paper’s positioning. Since the paper itself emphasizes aggregation bias as a live possibility, it cannot simultaneously oversell the result as dispositive. The right move is not to hide this, but to use it productively: the paper should present itself as a test of whether age is a useful aggregate targeting heuristic, not a final word on dam-level risk.

### If findings are null or modest, is the null interesting?
Potentially yes. The null is interesting because it challenges a salient and intuitive policy claim, not because nulls are inherently interesting. But the paper needs to make that case more elegantly.

Right now the null sometimes feels like a failed attempt to find an effect, dressed up as “well-powered nulls are informative.” That line rarely persuades on its own. What rescues the paper is the importance of the claim being tested. The authors should frame the null as:
- a rejection of a specific targeting heuristic,
- not a rejection of dam risk,
- and not a rejection of climate adaptation spending generally.

That distinction is crucial.
