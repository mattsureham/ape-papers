## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple and potentially interesting question: when states legalize a much cheaper funeral technology—alkaline hydrolysis—does entry finally bring competition to a market widely thought to be insulated from normal price pressure? The paper’s central claim is that the answer is no: rather than disciplining incumbents, legalization appears to expand the funeral-services market, suggesting that in some emotionally charged, information-friction-heavy markets, cheaper entry creates a new segment instead of eroding incumbent rents.

That is a pitch a busy economist could care about. It speaks to competition, deregulation, and the limits of textbook entry logic in unusual markets.

But the paper itself does not articulate this pitch as clearly or as sharply as it should in the first two paragraphs. The current introduction spends too much time on colorful description (“grief premium”) before locking in the broader economic question. The opening should do less scene-setting and more strategic framing: this is not mainly a paper about funerals; it is a paper about when deregulation fails to produce competitive discipline.

### The pitch the paper should have in the first two paragraphs

> Standard models predict that legalizing a cheaper substitute should intensify competition, reduce incumbent rents, and shift activity toward the low-cost technology. But that logic may fail in markets where demand is unavoidable, choices are made under distress, and consumers face severe informational and behavioral frictions. This paper studies that possibility in the U.S. funeral industry, where states have gradually legalized alkaline hydrolysis, a much cheaper alternative to burial and flame cremation.
>
> Using staggered state legalization, I ask whether removing this legal barrier made the funeral market more competitive—or simply larger. I find evidence for the latter: legalization increases funeral-industry establishments and employment, with little sign of contraction elsewhere in death care. The broader implication is that in markets shaped by behavioral frictions and social norms, deregulation may expand product variety and market size without meaningfully disciplining incumbent firms.

That is the version that belongs in AER territory: a general question, a sharp test, then a surprising answer.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:**  
The paper claims that legalizing a cheaper disposition technology in funeral services expands industry activity rather than intensifying competition, highlighting a boundary condition on the usual prediction that deregulation reduces incumbent rents.

That is a real contribution in principle. The problem is that the paper’s differentiation from neighboring work is only partially clear.

### Is it clearly differentiated from the closest papers?
Not yet convincingly. The introduction says prior work studies funeral regulation and casket deregulation, while this paper studies AH legalization. That is fine at the domain level, but it is not enough for AER-level positioning. The novelty cannot just be “different regulation, same setting.” It has to be:

- prior papers ask whether regulation affects prices or cremation rates;
- this paper asks whether deregulation changes **market structure and equilibrium margins**;
- and more broadly, whether cheaper entry disciplines incumbents or expands variety.

Right now the paper is too content with “first causal evidence in funeral services.” That is a field-gap contribution, not a world-question contribution.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world or filling a literature gap?
Mixed, but too often the latter. The strongest version is a world question:

- When does legalizing a cheaper alternative fail to produce incumbent displacement?
- What kinds of markets are insulated from standard competitive discipline?

That is much stronger than “the funeral industry has received little attention.”

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the intro?
At present, maybe—but not cleanly. They might say: “It’s a staggered DiD on funeral-sector outcomes after alkaline hydrolysis legalization, and the sector grows instead of shrinks.” That is okay, but still sounds like “another DiD paper about a quirky industry.”

The intro needs to make the novelty legible as:
1. a surprising comparative-statics result,
2. in a market with unusually strong behavioral frictions,
3. with implications for how we think about deregulation more generally.

### What would make the contribution bigger?
Several concrete possibilities:

1. **Shift the outcome hierarchy from industry size to competitive discipline.**  
   Employment and establishment growth are interesting, but they are indirect proxies for competition. The paper needs a clearer statement of what it means for competition to “fail.” If there is any way to say more about prices, markups, service bundles, or the composition of funeral offerings, the paper gets much bigger.

2. **Make product differentiation the centerpiece, not an after-the-fact interpretation.**  
   Right now “market expansion” is inferred from no contraction in adjacent sectors plus growth in funeral services. That is suggestive but thin. If the paper could show that entry occurs disproportionately in environmentally oriented or high-income-progressive markets, or where cremation demand was already rising, the segmentation story would become more compelling and general.

3. **Reframe around a broader class of markets.**  
   Funeral services alone is niche. Funeral services as a laboratory for “deregulation under distress, salience, and norm-driven demand” is much bigger.

4. **Clarify whether this is about deregulation, innovation, or nonprice competition.**  
   As written, the paper wants to be all three. It should pick one primary frame and subordinate the others.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
The standard view is that legal barriers protect incumbents; remove those barriers and cheaper entrants should reduce rents and reallocate demand.

### Tension
The funeral market may violate the assumptions behind that intuition: demand is unavoidable, consumers buy under distress, price shopping is limited, and social norms matter. So deregulation may not generate standard competitive displacement.

### Resolution
Legalizing alkaline hydrolysis appears to increase employment and establishments in funeral services, with little sign of offsetting contraction elsewhere, consistent with market expansion rather than incumbent displacement.

### Implications
The broader implication is that deregulation does not mechanically produce lower incumbent rents; in some markets it mostly increases variety, segmentation, or total demand served.

There is a real narrative arc here. That is the good news.

The bad news is that the paper does not fully commit to it. Parts of the paper still read like a collection of reduced-form results attached to an attractive intuition. The story is there, but the evidence presented in the main text is only loosely organized around that story.

In particular, the paper says:
- deregulation should reduce rents,
- it instead increases employment and establishments,
- therefore the market expanded.

That is a plausible story, but the bridge from “these outcomes rose” to “incumbent rents stayed intact and a new segment was created” is conceptually longer than the paper admits. Strategically, this matters. For AER, the story needs to be disciplined:

**The paper should be telling the story of why lower-cost entry need not be rent-dissipating when adoption is segmented by norms and low search.** Everything should serve that story. Right now there is still a little too much “and here is another suggestive piece of evidence.”

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?
“I would lead with: A much cheaper funeral technology became legal, and instead of shrinking incumbent activity, the funeral industry got bigger.”

That is a good dinner-party fact. Economists will lean in, at least initially, because it cuts against standard intuition.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
Lean in—for about two minutes. The hook is strong. The risk comes immediately after, when the audience asks: “Bigger in what sense?” and “Does this really mean competition failed?” If the answer is only employment and establishment counts, interest may fade unless the framing is very sharp.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Almost certainly:
- “So did prices fall or not?”
- Or: “Is AH really a substitute, or just a differentiated premium/green product?”
- Or: “Are incumbents adopting it themselves?”

Those are exactly the questions the paper should anticipate and strategically organize around, even if it cannot fully answer them.

### If the findings are modest, is the modesty itself interesting?
Yes, but the paper needs to make that case more self-consciously. A non-result on displacement is interesting **if** the paper frames it as evidence against a widely presumed mechanism. The current language sometimes overshoots (“grief premium remains undissolved”) relative to what the data shown can support. Better to say:

- legalization did not generate the expected contraction in incumbent-side activity;
- instead, the observable equilibrium response is expansion;
- this suggests a segmented-demand interpretation.

That is interesting. But the paper must avoid sounding like a failed attempt to find price competition.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

At the moment, this feels **farther from AER than the writing initially suggests**. The paper has a strong hook and a cute setting, but top-journal placement requires more than an unusual market plus a sign-reversed result.

### What is the main gap?
Primarily a **framing problem**, secondarily an **ambition/scope problem**.

- **Framing problem:** The paper has not yet convincingly established that this is a paper about the economics of deregulation under behavioral frictions, rather than a funeral-market DiD with an interesting sign.
- **Scope problem:** The outcomes shown are a bit too indirect to support the strongest interpretation.
- **Ambition problem:** The paper is competent but still somewhat safe. It stops at documenting industry expansion rather than pushing harder on what equilibrium concept this speaks to.

### Is it a novelty problem?
Partly. The underlying question is novel enough if framed correctly, but the empirical move—state staggered adoption and industry outcomes—is familiar. So novelty has to come from the conceptual claim, not the design.

### What is the single most impactful advice?
**Reframe the paper around a general boundary condition on deregulation—when cheaper entry expands variety without disciplining incumbents—and make every section serve that claim, rather than presenting this as a funeral-industry study with broader implications tacked on.**

If the author can only change one thing, it should be the **intellectual center of gravity** of the paper. Right now the paper starts from the setting and reaches for the theory. For AER, it needs to start from the theory and use the setting as a clean test.
