## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
The paper examines whether legalizing a significantly cheaper, greener alternative technology (alkaline hydrolysis) disciplines a notoriously uncompetitive market: the U.S. funeral industry. Exploiting staggered state-level legalization, the author finds that instead of forcing incumbents to lower prices, the new technology expands the total market—increasing employment and establishment counts without cannibalizing existing services.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this well in the abstract and intro. However, it could be sharper.
**The pitch it should have:** "In markets where 'grief premiums' and behavioral frictions are high, does price competition actually work? I show that legalizing a low-cost alternative to traditional burial does not discipline incumbents; instead, it expands the market frontier, suggesting that behavioral barriers, not just legal ones, protect supranormal markups."

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**One-sentence contribution:** The paper demonstrates that in high-friction markets with inelastic demand, removing legal barriers to entry via new technology leads to market expansion (Schumpeterian "creative creation") rather than competitive price discipline.

*   **Differentiated?** Yes. It moves beyond the "does licensing reduce entry?" question (Kleiner) to "does entry discipline incumbents?" in a behavioral setting.
*   **World vs. Literature:** It frames itself as answering a question about the world (how we deal with death care costs), which is a major strength.
*   **Explaining to a colleague:** A smart economist would say: "It’s a paper showing that when you introduce a cheap competitor into a market where people are too sad or pressured to shop around, the incumbents don't lose; the market just gets bigger."
*   **Making it bigger:** To reach the AER, the contribution needs more than just employment numbers. It needs **price data**. Without observing prices, we don't know if the "grief premium" is actually "undissolved" or if incumbents responded in ways that employment doesn't capture.

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
*   **Neighbors:** Chevalier and Morton (2020), Harrington and Krynski (2002), Kleiner and Krueger (2013).
*   **Positioning:** It builds on the "funeral industry" niche but attempts to speak to the broader IO/Labor literature on entry and licensing.
*   **Missing Conversations:** The paper needs to speak more to **Behavioral Industrial Organization**. This isn't just a licensing story; it's a "why does competition fail?" story. It should cite Heidhues and Kőszegi or similar work on "shrouded attributes" or "exploiting naive consumers."
*   **Right Conversation?** Currently, it’s a bit too much of a "policy evaluation" of a specific law. To be AER, it needs to be an IO paper *using* this law to test fundamental theories of competition.

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC
*   **Setup:** The funeral industry is a $20B "monopoly" of norms and high prices.
*   **Tension:** Alkaline hydrolysis (AH) is 1/3 the cost. Textbook theory says it should crush incumbent margins.
*   **Resolution:** It doesn't. Employment and establishments *grow* across the board.
*   **Implications:** Deregulation in "high-friction" markets doesn't work like a textbook; it just creates a new niche for the "green/cheap" consumer while the majority keep overpaying for tradition.

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong. It’s a "surprising result" paper, which the AER loves.

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
*   **The Lead Fact:** "Legalizing a way to dissolve bodies for $2,000 actually made the $8,000 funeral homes *busier*."
*   **The Reaction:** People will lean in. It's a "freakonomics-style" topic with serious IO implications.
*   **Follow-up:** "Wait, so are the old funeral homes just doing the dissolving themselves, or is it new firms?" (The paper tries to answer this but the QCEW data is too coarse).

---

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
*   **Shorten:** The "Institutional Background" is a bit long on the chemistry of AH and short on the economics of the *firms*.
*   **Move:** The TWFE vs. CS-DiD discussion is standard now; move most of the "threats to validity" regarding estimator choice to the appendix to keep the narrative moving.
*   **Front-load:** The finding that *cemeteries* don't shrink is a key "no-substitution" result. This should be highlighted earlier as it's the smoking gun for the "market expansion" story.

---

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The gap is **Data Granularity**.
Using QCEW (6-digit NAICS) is "adequate" for a good field journal, but the AER wants to see the "guts" of the market.
*   **The Problem:** You can't see prices. You can't see if the *same* firm is offering both.
*   **The Fix:** If the author could scrape price data (from the "Funeral Rule" disclosures) or use consumer-level survey data on *what* they bought, it becomes an AER paper.
*   **Single biggest advice:** Prove the "Grief Premium" didn't move. You claim it's "undissolved," but you only measure employment. If employment went up but prices fell 20%, the story changes. **Find price data.**

### STRATEGIC ASSESSMENT

*   **Current framing quality:** Compelling (The "grief premium" is a great hook).
*   **Contribution clarity:** Somewhat fuzzy (Market expansion is shown, but the *mechanism* of why incumbents are unharmed is speculative).
*   **Literature positioning:** Well-positioned but needs more Behavioral IO.
*   **Narrative arc:** Strong.
*   **AER distance:** Medium.
*   **Single biggest improvement:** Incorporate price data or firm-level service menus to prove that incumbent pricing/offerings remained stagnant despite the entry of a low-cost alternative.

**Decision:** Do not reject. The "market expansion" finding in a supposedly zero-sum market (everyone dies once) is a classic AER-style puzzle. However, the author needs to be pushed on the "So What" of the welfare/price implications.
