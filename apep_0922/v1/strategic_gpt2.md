## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a clean and potentially interesting question: when states legalize a much cheaper funeral technology, does competition finally bite in a market famous for high prices and vulnerable consumers? The headline claim is counterintuitive: instead of squeezing incumbents, legalization appears to expand the funeral-services market, suggesting that in markets shaped by social norms, distress, and low search, cheaper entry can create a new segment rather than erode incumbent rents.

Does the paper itself articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs? Not quite. The opening is vivid, but it oversells some claims (“perfectly inelastic,” “one-third the price,” “grief premium”) and spends too much time on scene-setting before landing the actual question. The first two paragraphs should more quickly establish the broader economic problem—when does deregulation increase competition versus expand variety?—and use funeral services as the sharp setting, rather than leading with industry color.

### The pitch the paper should have

“Economists often assume that legalizing a cheaper substitute intensifies competition and reduces incumbent rents. But in markets where consumers face emotional distress, weak price search, and strong social norms, a new low-cost option may instead expand product variety without disciplining existing suppliers. I study this question in the U.S. funeral industry, where states have staggeredly legalized alkaline hydrolysis, a cheaper alternative to burial and flame cremation. I find that legalization increases funeral-industry establishments and employment, with little evidence of contraction elsewhere in death care, consistent with market expansion rather than incumbent displacement.”

That is the AER version of the paper. Right now the paper is framed as “funeral markets are weird, here is a reform,” when it should be framed as “here is a sharp test of when deregulation fails to generate textbook competition.”

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:** The paper argues that legalizing a cheaper funeral-disposition technology expanded the funeral-services market rather than intensifying competition, highlighting a boundary condition for standard deregulation logic in markets with strong behavioral frictions.

This is a real contribution, but it is not yet crisply differentiated from neighboring literatures.

### Is it clearly differentiated from the closest papers?
Only partially. The paper names a few funeral-specific papers and some broad licensing/entry references, but the differentiation is still too shallow. The novelty is not “first paper on alkaline hydrolysis” or “first causal funeral paper”; that is too small for AER. The novelty has to be: **a direct empirical case where removing a legal barrier to a cheaper substitute does not reduce incumbent activity, because the market margin is segmentation, not substitution.**

The closest neighbors are not just funeral papers. They are papers on:
- deregulation and entry,
- licensing and barriers to practice,
- behavioral frictions in consumer markets,
- product differentiation and market expansion from entry.

If readers come away saying “it’s another DiD on a niche regulation,” that is a framing failure. Right now they might.

### World question or literature gap?
The paper does better than average here, but it still slips into “contributes to three literatures” mode. The stronger version is a question about the world:

> When policymakers legalize a cheaper alternative, under what conditions does that increase competition versus simply enlarge the menu of options and total market activity?

That is much stronger than “there is little evidence on funeral services.”

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new?
At present: maybe, but not confidently. A smart reader would probably say: “It’s a DiD showing water cremation legalization increased funeral employment.” That is not enough.

They should instead say: “It shows that deregulation can expand a market without harming incumbents when search and social norms prevent the cheaper entrant from being a close substitute.”

### What would make the contribution bigger?
Most importantly, the paper needs evidence that speaks more directly to the economic object of interest: **substitution versus market expansion**.

Specific ways to make it bigger:
1. **Outcome variables closer to competition and substitution**
   - Cremation rates, burial rates, funeral spending, disposition mix, pre-need contracts, funeral package composition.
   - Even imperfect proxies would help. Employment and establishments are too upstream and indirect for a top-field contribution.

2. **Mechanism evidence**
   - Is AH adopted by incumbents or by entrants?
   - Does it grow in greener, more secular, more educated, or higher-income places?
   - Does it affect direct cremation or burial margins differently?
   - Is the effect concentrated where social acceptance of cremation was already high?

3. **Reframing the comparison**
   - Compare to settings where deregulation *did* reduce incumbents’ rents.
   - Or compare AH to flame cremation diffusion historically.
   - Or benchmark against other death-care reforms like casket-sale deregulation.

4. **More discipline around the main claim**
   - “Incumbent grief premium remains intact” is too strong given the outcomes shown.
   - A bigger paper would either measure prices/rents more directly or narrow the claim to market scale and composition.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

### Closest neighbors
The paper’s nearest neighbors likely include:
- **Harrington and Krynski (2002)** on funeral regulation and cremation.
- **Chevalier and coauthors** on funeral/casket deregulation and pricing.
- **Kleiner and Krueger / Kleiner (2006, 2013)** on occupational licensing.
- **Bresnahan and Reiss (1991)** on entry and competition in concentrated markets.
- Potentially broader work on **consumer search and shrouded pricing**, e.g. **Gabaix and Laibson (2006)**, **Ellison and Ellison**, and work on markets with vulnerable consumers.

The paper also ought to be aware of literature on:
- **behavioral IO / search frictions**
- **healthcare-like markets** where consumers are distressed and bad at shopping
- **product differentiation and market expansion**
- **technology adoption under regulation**
- possibly **moral markets / taboo consumption** if it wants to lean into norms.

### How should it position itself?
Mostly **build on and synthesize**, not attack.

The right positioning is:
- Build on licensing/deregulation papers by showing a case where lifting a barrier does not produce standard competitive discipline.
- Build on IO entry papers by emphasizing that entry can expand category demand when products are weak substitutes.
- Build on search/behavioral papers by showing that consumer frictions can mute pass-through from legal reform to competitive outcomes.

It should not pick a fight with “textbook economics” in a cartoonish way. Phrases like “opposite of the textbook prediction” are rhetorically punchy but too crude. Textbook differentiated-products models absolutely allow market expansion. The stronger claim is that **the usual policy intuition around deregulating a cheaper substitute predicts stronger incumbent displacement than what is observed here.**

### Too narrow or too broad?
Currently, oddly both.
- **Too narrow** in the institutional positioning: lots of funeral-industry detail.
- **Too broad** in the theoretical claims: sweeping statements about deregulation, inelastic demand, healthcare, education, religion.

The paper should narrow its conceptual claim to something like:
> deregulation of cheaper alternatives may fail to reduce incumbent activity when the entrant relaxes a participation margin rather than competing on an intensive margin.

That is broad enough to matter and narrow enough to be defensible.

### What literature does it seem unaware of?
Most notably:
- behavioral IO / consumer search / add-on pricing / vulnerable consumers,
- markets under distress (healthcare is the obvious analog),
- technology diffusion and regulation,
- category expansion / non-substitution in differentiated markets.

The occupational licensing literature is not quite the right lead literature. AH legalization is not mainly a licensing paper; it is a **technology-permission / market-design / entry-and-substitution** paper. “Licensing” can be secondary.

### Is it having the right conversation?
Not yet. It is currently trying to talk to funeral-regulation, licensing, and generic market-structure literatures. The better conversation is:

**What happens when policymakers legalize a cheaper substitute in a market where consumers do not behave like standard searchers?**

That is a much more interesting conversation, and one that could reach IO, public economics, law and economics, and behavioral economics readers.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
A high-cost, emotionally charged market in which consumers rarely shop around, and policymakers/legal reformers hope that legalizing a cheaper alternative will introduce competition.

### Tension
Standard policy logic says cheaper legal entry should discipline incumbents. But in funeral markets, social norms, distress, and weak search may make the new technology a poor substitute for incumbent offerings.

### Resolution
Legalization appears to increase establishments and employment rather than shrinking the incumbent sector, with little evidence of offsetting contraction nearby.

### Implications
Deregulation does not mechanically translate into stronger competition. In some markets, the relevant margin is category expansion and segmentation, not rent dissipation.

This is a potentially strong narrative arc. But the current draft is still somewhat a **collection of results looking for a story**. The story is present, but the paper keeps slipping between three different papers:

1. a funeral-industry paper,
2. a deregulation/licensing paper,
3. a behavioral-frictions paper.

It needs to choose one primary story. The right one is #2 filtered through #3.

The paper should be telling this story:

> We often think of deregulation as moving surplus from incumbents to consumers via competition. This paper studies a setting where that logic may fail because the newly legal product is not a close substitute for incumbent products. Funeral services offer a clean test because AH is cheaper, newly legal, and plausibly salient. The evidence points to market expansion, implying that deregulation can work by broadening participation or variety rather than by compressing incumbent rents.

That is a coherent AER-style narrative. Right now the prose is good, but the narrative discipline is not.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?
“I expected legalizing a funeral option that is much cheaper than burial or cremation to squeeze the incumbents. Instead, funeral-sector employment rises.”

That is a decent dinner-party fact.

### Would people lean in?
Yes, initially. The setting is unusual, memorable, and the result is contrary to naive intuition.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Immediately: **“So does this mean the cheaper option is mostly serving new consumers rather than replacing old services? How do you know?”**

That is the key vulnerability in the paper’s current positioning. The paper has an interesting headline, but the very next question is the one it only partially answers. “No contraction in adjacent crematories” helps, but not enough. Economists will want a more direct read on substitution.

A second follow-up would be:
**“Is this actually about funeral services, or is it a general lesson about deregulation under search frictions?”**

The paper wants the latter, but the evidence currently supports mainly the former plus a suggestive broader interpretation.

### If findings are modest
They are not null, but they are somewhat modest and indirect. Employment and establishment changes are publishable facts; they are not by themselves field-defining facts. The paper needs to make the case that the surprising direction of the effect is itself important because it challenges a common policy heuristic.

Right now the paper does this reasonably well, but it still sounds a bit like a clever niche application rather than a result that changes how economists think about deregulation.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

At the moment, the paper is not there yet.

The biggest gap is a mix of **framing problem** and **scope problem**.

### Framing problem
The science may be competent, but the story is not yet pitched at the level of an AER conversation. The paper is still selling:
- funeral industry novelty,
- first evidence on AH,
- a broad anti-textbook punchline.

That will not do it.

It needs to sell:
- a clean empirical test of when cheaper legal entry fails to produce competitive displacement,
- with implications for how economists think about deregulation under consumer frictions and social norms.

### Scope problem
The current outcomes are too far from the core claim. Establishments, employment, and wages are suggestive but indirect. To excite the top 10 people in this field, the paper needs stronger evidence on one of:
- substitution patterns,
- prices/spending,
- incumbent versus entrant response,
- market composition,
- heterogeneous effects that isolate the mechanism.

### Novelty problem
The setting is novel; the broader idea is less so. Economists already know that entry can expand markets and that behavioral frictions mute competition. What would make this paper new is a sharp, persuasive empirical demonstration in a clean policy setting. Right now the evidence gets halfway there.

### Ambition problem
The paper is competent but a bit safe. It has settled for outcomes available in QCEW and built a story around them. An AER paper would likely push harder for data that get closer to the mechanism or use the existing data to ask a more ambitious comparative question.

### Single most impactful advice
**Reframe the paper around the general question—when does legalizing a cheaper substitute increase competition versus expand the market—and then bring every piece of evidence to bear on distinguishing substitution from market expansion.**

If the author can only change one thing, it should be that. Not more econometrics. Not more funeral background. A sharper conceptual frame tied to more direct evidence on substitution.
