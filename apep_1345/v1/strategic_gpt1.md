## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether public school quality labels move housing markets, and whether they do so differently across neighborhoods. Using the timing of Ofsted inspection publications in England, it argues that the average effect of a bad school rating on nearby house prices is near zero, but that this average masks a sharp distributional pattern: bad ratings lower house prices in deprived areas and do not do so in affluent areas, implying that accountability labels may exacerbate spatial inequality.

A busy economist should care because this is potentially not just another school-quality capitalization paper. If true, the interesting claim is that **the same public information shock has different asset-price consequences depending on households’ outside options**, linking school accountability, housing markets, and inequality.

Does the paper articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs? Not quite. The opening is readable, but the introduction initially sounds like a standard “school quality capitalizes into house prices” paper, and only later reveals the real hook: **heterogeneous capitalization by deprivation**. The current first paragraphs undersell the distributional idea and overinvest in the generic capitalization literature.

### What the first two paragraphs should say instead

The paper should open with the world question, not the institutional detail:

> School quality ratings do more than inform parents: they may change local asset values. But whether those labels matter equally across neighborhoods is an open question. If affluent families can substitute into private schools, tutoring, or nearby catchments while poorer families cannot, then the same negative school label may have very different consequences across places—potentially making accountability systems a driver of spatial inequality rather than merely a source of information.

Then the paper should immediately state the core empirical fact:

> This paper studies the publication of Ofsted school ratings in England and shows that the average house-price response to a bad rating is close to zero, but that this average hides strong heterogeneity. In deprived neighborhoods, a bad rating is associated with falling house prices; in more affluent neighborhoods, prices do not fall and may even rise. The central contribution is thus not that school quality labels matter on average, but that they are capitalized asymmetrically across places, with the strongest penalties where households have the fewest alternatives.

That is the pitch. Right now the paper gets there, but too slowly and too defensively.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:**  
The paper’s contribution is to show that public school accountability labels are capitalized into housing markets in a sharply heterogeneous way, with negative ratings reducing property values in deprived neighborhoods but not in affluent ones.

### Is this clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?
Only partly. The paper does distinguish itself from classic school-quality capitalization papers, but the distinction is not yet as crisp as it needs to be. The closest literature already studies housing responses to school quality, school report cards, and Ofsted ratings; so the paper cannot merely say “I study school labels and house prices.” It has to say:

1. prior work mostly estimates average capitalization of school quality or report-card information;
2. prior work does not center the **distributional heterogeneity** of capitalization by neighborhood constraint set;
3. the paper’s object is not “does information matter?” but “**for whom and where does information matter?**”

That is a stronger and more original claim.

### World question or literature gap?
The paper is mixed, but tilted in the right direction. Its strongest framing is as a question about the world:

- Do accountability labels redistribute housing wealth?
- Do public information systems have regressive incidence?

Its weakest framing is as a literature-gap paper:

- “Most studies compare persistent quality differences; I study label changes.”

That latter point is fine but not enough for AER. The former is much stronger.

### Could a smart economist explain what is new after reading the intro?
At present: maybe, but not confidently. A smart economist might say, “It’s a DiD paper on Ofsted ratings and house prices, with heterogeneity by deprivation.” That is not yet memorable enough.

You want them to say:

> “It shows school accountability labels have unequal incidence in housing markets: bad labels hurt poor places, not rich ones.”

That is a much better sentence.

### What would make the contribution bigger?
Several options:

- **Sharper mechanism/outcome framing:** Move beyond average house prices to who buys, transaction volume, property composition, days on market, or entry/exit of families with children. If the big idea is constrained choice sets, the paper would be stronger if it showed adjustment margins differ across neighborhoods, not just prices.
- **Exploit rating changes rather than levels:** The most natural substantive comparison is downgrade versus upgrade, or unexpected bad news relative to prior rating. This would align better with the “label shock” framing.
- **Connect more directly to household wealth inequality:** If a 1% house-price decline in deprived areas is the key fact, then frame it as a distributional asset-market effect of public policy, not as an education paper with a housing footnote.
- **Use a more choice-relevant spatial unit:** Strategically, the current postcode-district geography makes the result feel coarse. Even if referees assess feasibility, editorially this matters because the paper’s substantive claim is local capitalization; the spatial mismatch weakens the story’s ambition.
- **Push the “outside options” mechanism more concretely:** Private-school prevalence, nearby school density, catchment substitutability, or mobility frictions would make the paper seem less like a descriptive heterogeneity exercise and more like a general result about how markets process public information under constraint.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
School quality is capitalized into housing prices, and Ofsted ratings are salient public signals in England.

### Tension
It is unclear whether these ratings add information beyond what buyers already know, and—more importantly—whether the same rating has the same consequences across neighborhoods with different outside options.

### Resolution
Average capitalization is weak or nonexistent, but deprived neighborhoods experience price declines after bad ratings while affluent neighborhoods do not.

### Implications
Accountability systems may not simply inform households; they may unevenly penalize already disadvantaged places, amplifying spatial inequality through housing wealth.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
It has the ingredients, but the arc is not yet disciplined. Right now the paper spends too much time on a result it itself regards as fragile—the pooled effect—and then pivots to the heterogeneity result, which is plainly the real paper. This creates the sense of a paper discovering its story midway through.

In other words: **this currently reads like a collection of results looking for a story.** The author has found the story, but the draft has not yet reorganized around it.

### What story should it be telling?
The paper should tell this story:

1. Everyone thinks school labels matter for house prices.
2. On average, they barely do.
3. But averages are misleading because labels matter when households are constrained.
4. The housing-market incidence of school accountability is therefore unequal.
5. That changes how we should think about school ratings: not just as information, but as place-shaping policy.

That is clean, surprising, and has implications beyond England.

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

### What fact would I lead with at dinner?
“Bad school ratings don’t lower nearby house prices on average—but they do in poor neighborhoods, and not in rich ones.”

That is the interesting fact.

### Would people lean in?
Yes, briefly. The asymmetry is intuitively provocative. It invites a real conversation about why information matters only when households have limited alternatives.

If instead you lead with “I estimate the effect of Ofsted ratings on house prices using 9,780 inspections,” phones come out.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Almost certainly:

- “Why would prices rise in affluent areas?”
- Then: “How do you know this is about labels rather than underlying school decline?”
- And then: “Is this specific to Ofsted, or a general feature of performance labels?”

Those questions are actually a good sign. They mean the paper has a provocative empirical pattern. But the introduction should anticipate that the affluent-area positive effect is not itself the main message. Right now the draft indulges a bit too much speculative mechanism (“bargain hunters”) that feels post hoc. The cleaner message is asymmetry, not rich-area appreciation per se.

### If findings are null or modest, is that interesting?
The average null is mildly interesting, but not enough for AER on its own. “School ratings don’t matter much on average” feels like either a failed replication of a salience story or a context-specific null.

The heterogeneous result is what rescues the paper. The paper needs to make that explicit: the average null is informative because it reveals that **distributional incidence washes out in aggregate**. That is a much better use of the pooled null than treating it as a preliminary disappointment.

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

### What would make it read better?

#### 1. Reorder the introduction around the heterogeneous effect
The paper should not spend so much early real estate on the pooled effect. The central result should appear in the first page, ideally first three paragraphs. Current structure makes the reader wait too long to learn what is actually interesting.

#### 2. Shrink generic institutional detail
The institutional background is competent but overlong for the paper’s needs. Ofsted’s rating scale and publication timing can be explained more succinctly. Unless there is a strategic reason, this section can be shortened substantially.

#### 3. Compress identification prose in the introduction
The introduction currently spends too much time telling the reader exactly what the design is doing, including caveats. Some honesty is admirable, but strategically the intro should first establish the question and answer. The design details can come after the headline contribution.

#### 4. Bring the core figure/result forward
If there is an event-study graph by deprivation group, that should be the main visual centerpiece very early. If such a figure does not exist, the paper needs it. Editorially, the whole story is in the contrast, not in the average coefficient table.

#### 5. Move some robustness detail out of the main text
Randomization inference and window sensitivity for the pooled estimate may matter to referees, but they are not the narrative center. They currently occupy more conceptual space than they deserve. The main text should focus on the deprivation contrast.

#### 6. Rework the conclusion
The conclusion mostly restates the main result. It should instead do one of two things:
- either generalize: what does this imply for performance labeling systems more broadly?
- or discipline the policy message: when should regulators worry that transparency tools have regressive local incidence?

Right now it summarizes more than it synthesizes.

### Is the paper front-loaded with the good stuff?
Not enough. The good stuff is buried after the pooled estimate and some caveats.

### Are there buried results that belong in the main text?
Yes: any evidence that the deprivation interaction is stable across windows, phases, or property types is more central than the fragile pooled effect. If the heterogeneity is the paper, supporting evidence for that heterogeneity belongs front and center.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The gap is mainly a **framing-plus-ambition** problem, with some scope issues.

### Framing problem
The science may be adequate for a solid field journal, but the paper is still written as if the main contribution is “Ofsted ratings and house prices.” That is too small. The real paper is about **unequal incidence of public information through housing markets**.

### Scope problem
The current evidence supports an interesting heterogeneity fact, but the paper’s broader claims about “spatial inequality” and “feedback loops” are more asserted than demonstrated. To reach AER level, it would ideally show more of the mechanism or incidence:
- constrained choice sets,
- differential substitution margins,
- broader distributional consequences,
- or stronger links to household sorting and wealth.

### Novelty problem
Average school-quality capitalization is old news. Even school labels and housing is not new enough by itself. The novelty resides in the **heterogeneous capitalization** and the implication that labels are regressive. That needs to be unmistakable.

### Ambition problem
The paper is competent but a bit safe. It takes a familiar setting and finds an interaction. Top-field attention will require making the interaction feel like a new conceptual object, not a subgroup result.

### Single most impactful advice
**Rewrite the paper around one claim: accountability labels have unequal housing-market incidence because households’ outside options differ across places; everything else should support that claim.**

That means:
- demote the average effect,
- elevate the heterogeneity,
- connect it to a broader economics question about information, constraints, and asset prices,
- and stop sounding like a standard school-quality DiD.
