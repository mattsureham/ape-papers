**Idea Fidelity**

The paper departs substantially from the idea manifest. The original proposal was to exploit quasi-random variation in Ofsted inspector leniency—using Inspector Lottery IV à la Kling (2006)—to isolate the causal impact of published ratings on downstream outcomes such as house prices and student achievement. Instead, the submitted paper runs a difference-in-differences (DiD) around inspection publication, comparing pre/post price changes for schools receiving “bad” versus “good” ratings and seeking heterogeneous effects by deprivation. None of the inspector-leniency IV strategy, the leave-one-out instrument construction, or the focus on identifying the label effect conditional on inspector assignment is implemented. Consequently, the paper does not pursue the manifest’s core identification and provides only correlational evidence about events surrounding inspection publication. This is a critical divergence from the proposed research design, and it undermines the argument that the results recover the causal effect of arbitrary label assignment.

**Summary**

The paper studies the effect of negative Ofsted inspection ratings on local house prices, using a DiD setup that compares price trajectories before and after publication across schools rated 3–4 (Requires Improvement/Inadequate) versus 1–2 (Good/Outstanding). The pooled effect is near zero, but a large heterogeneity emerges: in deprived areas a negative rating reduces prices by about 1.1%, whereas in affluent areas the effect is a 1.0% increase. This repressed/booster gap is interpreted as evidence that Ofsted’s labels exacerbate spatial inequality.

---

**Essential Points**

1. **Identification is not credible without the intended inspector-leniency IV.** The differential pricing of post-inspection neighborhoods is likely confounded by time-varying school and neighborhood shocks that simultaneously influence inspections and housing markets. Schools receiving bad ratings tend to be in declining areas, and the design does not isolate exogenous variation. The manifest proposed exploiting random inspector assignment as an instrument; abandoning this strategy without replacing it with another credible source of exogenous variation means the estimated DiD coefficients may simply reflect omitted trends rather than the “label” effect. The authors must either implement the promised inspector-IV strategy or provide compelling evidence that inspection timing and outcomes are exogenous conditional on their controls.

2. **Parallel trends and post-treatment dynamics are insufficiently demonstrated.** The main pooled coefficient flips sign, is fragile across event windows, and fails permutation inference. The pre-trend test (linear interaction) yields a p-value of 0.138, which is weak support. There is no graphical event study showing dynamic coefficients before and after publication to inspect whether pricing trends diverged prior to treatment or reacted plausibly afterward. Without strong pretreatment balance, especially given the observed level differences in Table 1, the DiD estimates cannot be trusted.

3. **Interpretation of the deprivation heterogeneity is unsupported by causal evidence.** The core empirical contribution is a 2.1 percentage-point gap between deprived and non-deprived neighborhoods. However, as the authors note, they cannot separate the label effect from genuine quality changes. Since deprived neighborhoods are more likely to receive Negative ratings because of real declines, the heterogeneity could reflect differential underlying trends (e.g., market weakness, policy shocks) rather than differential responses to labels. The paper needs a more credible research design—ideally the inspector-leniency IV—to isolate the label effect and then test whether its impact varies by deprivation. As it stands, the heterogeneity may simply mirror preexisting socioeconomic divergences.

Given these foundational concerns, I cannot recommend the paper for publication in its current form. The paper’s main identification strategy is weak, and without implementing the novel inspector-leniency IV, the key conclusions about the causal “label” effect and its distributive consequences remain speculative.

**Suggestions**

1. **Implement the inspector-leniency IV as originally proposed.** The manifest lays out a feasible set-up: construct a leave-one-out leniency measure for each inspector within region and year, and use it as an instrument for the observed rating. This approach isolates variation attributable to inspector assignment rather than school performance. Data sources (Ofsted reports with inspector names, Management Information, and Land Registry prices) are available, and the paper already documents sufficient variation across inspectors. Estimating the effect of the rating instrumented by inspectors’ average behavior would directly address the question of the “label” effect and align the paper with its stated innovation. Even if the main outcome remains housing prices, instrumenting the rating should yield credible causal estimates that can then be interacted with deprivation indices.

2. **Augment the DiD design with richer diagnostics and robustness checks.** If the DiD design is retained as a complementary approach, the paper should present full event-study graphs with confidence bands to assess pre-trends and dynamics. Include school-by-linear-time trends or even flexible local trends to absorb differential year-on-year shocks. Use alternative control groups (e.g., schools inspected in the same month but receiving good ratings) and placebo tests (e.g., “false” treatment dates, neighboring postcode districts without affected schools) to demonstrate that the post-publication spike is not driven by contemporaneous shocks. Apply synthetic control matching on pretreatment price trajectories to ensure that treated and control areas were following similar paths prior to the inspection.

3. **Clarify and expand the interpretation of the heterogeneity.** The deprivation interaction is intriguing but needs a firmer grounding. Present descriptive evidence that bad-rated schools in deprived areas are not experiencing contemporaneous shocks (enrollment declines, local employment shocks) that could directly affect prices. If inspectors’ leniency can be exploited, show whether the instrumented effect differs by deprivation to see if the “label” effect itself varies. Alternatively, examine other outcomes, such as search intensity on property portals or school application rates, to provide indirect evidence of demand-side responses that are likely to react to labels rather than to unobserved quality changes. Discuss why affluent neighborhoods would react positively to bad ratings—can it be due to speculative demand or investor activity? Providing a mechanism (e.g., affordability-driven demand for homes near under-rated schools) would make the interpretation more convincing.

4. **Refine spatial measurement and data linkage.** The paper currently aggregates to postcode districts, which vary in size and often contain multiple schools. Consider using finer geography (postcode sector, LSOA) if transaction volume allows, or instrumenting district-level averages with school-level variation. Alternatively, focus on single-school postcode districts to isolate the local school’s market. Also, account for spatial spillovers—price changes may be smeared across multiple nearby districts, causing attenuation. Mapping the treated school’s catchment area (e.g., through school boundary data or drive-time buffers) and aggregating transactions within that “market” could sharpen the estimate of the label’s effect.

5. **Address potential information diffusion timing.** The identification hinges on the publication date as the information shock. But consumers may have prior knowledge (leaks, inspections are scheduled in advance) or delayed responses (media coverage). Investigate whether there are anticipatory effects in the months before publication or delayed reactions by extending the event window and checking for gradual responses. You can also examine sharp timing by using the publication week rather than month if the data permits. If publication timing is clustered (e.g., most reports released on certain days), adjust standard errors or clustering accordingly.

6. **Assess general equilibrium and policy implications carefully.** The stated policy message is that Ofsted labels penalize deprived areas. Before making strong normative claims, simulate the implied wealth effects (e.g., aggregate housing wealth changes) and consider countervailing mechanisms (e.g., pressure on low-performing schools to improve). Discuss whether the distributional effect is robust when accounting for different property types (flats vs. houses) and whether the interaction persists when controlling for local supply constraints or planning restrictions that could drive price sensitivity to school quality.

7. **Improve transparency about sample selection and missing data.** Table 1 shows 9,780 graded schools but only 1,257 in the bad rating category. Provide a flowchart showing how many schools are excluded due to missing house price data, insufficient pre/post observations, or co-located schools in the same district. Explain whether inspection timing correlates with other policy changes (e.g., academization waves) that could confound the estimates, and include appropriate controls or fixed effects if so.

8. **Consider additional outcomes to triangulate the effect.** School quality labels may also influence rental markets, mobility, or application rates. If data allow, test whether rental prices respond similarly, or whether there is an effect on the share of first-time buyers. Adding student achievement outcomes (from DfE performance tables) could link the label effect to education outcomes and help distinguish between label-driven and substantive quality changes.

9. **Document external validity and limitations candidly.** The paper is clear about the coarseness of postcode districts and lack of full inspection history. Extend this discussion to acknowledge that the DiD estimates pertain only to schools inspected in 2019–2024, potentially omitting those with older ratings, and that housing market reactions may differ in periods of macroeconomic instability. This will help readers understand the scope of the conclusions.

Overall, the paper addresses an important question with rich data but needs a much stronger identification strategy—ideally the inspector-leniency IV proposed in the manifest—to support its causal claims. Enhancing diagnostic checks, spatial precision, and interpretation of heterogeneity will significantly improve the paper’s contribution.
