### 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
This paper examines how public school quality "labels" (Ofsted ratings) are capitalized into house prices in England. It asks whether these government-assigned ratings provide new information to the market and, crucially, whether the market's response depends on neighborhood affluence. The paper finds that while the aggregate effect of a bad rating is zero, this masks a sharp distributional divide: bad ratings depress property values in poor areas by 1% but correlate with price *increases* in wealthy areas, suggesting that accountability systems may inadvertently exacerbate spatial inequality.

**Evaluation:** The paper does a decent job in the first two paragraphs, but it leans too heavily on the London estate agent anecdote. It needs to pivot faster to the "why care" for a global audience.
**The Pitch the Paper Should Have:** "Do government school-quality labels improve market efficiency or merely create arbitrary winners and losers? Using 9,780 information shocks in the UK, I show that these labels are fundamentally regressive. While affluent markets are resilient to 'bad' ratings due to outside options like private schooling, deprived markets experience immediate wealth destruction, suggesting that transparency without choice creates a 'quality trap' for the poor."

---

### 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**Contribution:** The paper identifies that the capitalization of school quality information is highly heterogeneous, acting as a regressive tax that penalizes property values in deprived areas while leaving affluent areas unscathed.

- **Differentiation:** It differentiates itself from **Black (1999)** and **Gibbons (2013)** by shifting focus from *underlying quality* to the *labeling shock*. It builds on **Figlio and Lucas (2004)** by adding the deprivation interaction, which is the "AER-level" hook.
- **World vs. Literature:** It is currently framed as answering a question about the WORLD (inequality and accountability), which is a strength. 
- **The "So What?":** A smart economist would say: "It’s a paper showing that the same information signal has opposite wealth effects depending on the credit/mobility constraints of the local population."
- **How to make it bigger:** The paper needs to move beyond "house prices." To be a top-tier paper, it needs to explore the *feedback loop*. Does the price drop lead to lower tax takes (not applicable in the UK system, but relevant for US readers) or, more importantly, a change in the *composition* of students/teachers?

---

### 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
The paper sits at the intersection of Urban Econ (Capitalization), Education (Accountability), and Information Economics.

- **Neighbors:** Figlio & Lucas (2004) on report cards; Hastings et al. (2009) on school choice; Hussain (2016) on Ofsted.
- **Positioning:** It should **attack** the "pooled" approach of previous literature. It needs to argue that looking at the average effect of school ratings (as Hussain does) is a mistake that hides the most important economic story: the distributional consequence.
- **Missing Conversations:** It needs to speak more to the **Tiebout sorting** literature and **Search/Friction** models. Why do prices go *up* in affluent areas after a bad rating? The paper mentions "investors," but this needs a more rigorous theoretical grounding. Is it a "reversion to the mean" bet?

---

### 4. NARRATIVE ARC
- **Setup:** We value transparency and use ratings to solve principal-agent problems in education.
- **Tension:** If ratings are capitalized into home equity, a "bad" rating might hurt the very families it is intended to help by destroying their primary asset.
- **Resolution:** The "label" effect is only negative where families are trapped. In affluent areas, the label is ignored or treated as a "buy" signal.
- **Implications:** Transparency is not a "free lunch"—in the presence of credit and mobility constraints, information can be a regressive shock.

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong but the "Resolution" is currently a bit "leaky" because of the 0.36% pooled positive result. The author needs to lean harder into the "Aggregation Bias" narrative.

---

### 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
- **The Fact:** "In England, giving a school a 'Bad' rating actually *increases* house prices in rich neighborhoods, but wipes out 1% of home value in poor ones."
- **Reaction:** People will lean in. The counter-intuitive "price rise in rich areas" is the "cocktail party" hook that generates discussion.
- **Follow-up:** "Wait, why do rich people pay more for a bad school?" (The author needs a better answer for this than "investors.")

---

### 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
- **The Caveat:** Page 3 contains a massive "Important Caveat" about not being able to isolate the label effect from quality changes. **Move this.** Don't apologize in the intro. Put it in the Empirical Strategy section and use the deprivation heterogeneity as your defense (i.e., why would quality decline *only* hurt prices in poor areas?).
- **Coarseness:** The author admits the "Postcode District" is too coarse. This is a potential killer for the AER. They need to try to get LSOA-level data or explain why the district level is the relevant market for a parent.

---

### 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The paper is currently a "Solid Field Journal" paper (e.g., *Journal of Urban Economics*). To make it an **AER paper**, it needs to bridge the gap from "empirical finding" to "general economic principle."

**The Gap:** The "Ambition Problem." It currently feels like a UK-specific policy evaluation.
**Single Most Impactful Advice:** Develop a simple theoretical framework of "Capitalization under Constraints." Show that the difference in price elasticity to information is a formal function of the outside options (private schools/mobility). If the author can prove that the *reason* for the asymmetry is the lack of choice (using the "Secondary school" vs "Primary school" distance as a proxy for choice sets), the paper becomes a major statement on the limits of information in economics.
