**Idea Fidelity**

The paper does not fully pursue the original idea outlined in the manifest. The manifest proposed exploiting karst/non-karst geological boundaries to construct a spatial regression discontinuity (RD) that isolates variation in PFAS exposure arising from exogenous transport properties of karst aquifers. The submitted paper, however, instead compares public water systems across counties classified as “karst” versus “non-karst” using a simple OLS specification with state fixed effects and county-clustered standard errors. No geospatial RD is implemented, no distance-to-boundary running variable is used, and the DoD PFAS source network mentioned in the original idea is nowhere leveraged. As a result, the key novel identification strategy—combining geological discontinuities with PFAS point-source geography—has been abandoned, and the analysis now hinges on a cruder county-level comparison that may fail to capture the causal mechanism. Given the divergence from the manifest, it is important either to revive the intended RD design or to explain why the revised approach still addresses the research question credibly.

**Summary**

This short paper asks whether karst geology—characterized by rapid conduit flow and minimal soil filtration—predicts higher PFAS contamination in U.S. public water systems. Using EPA’s UCMR5 monitoring data merged to USGS county-level sinkhole susceptibility classifications, the author reports modest positive but statistically insignificant associations between county-level karst exposure and PFAS detection, concentration, and exceedance of the 4 ppt MCL. Robustness checks (groundwater subset, alternative transformations, karst fraction bins) leave the substantive conclusion unchanged: county-level geological variation does not generate large detectable contamination differences in this cross-section.

**Essential Points**

1. **Identification strategy is not credible.** The paper claims that karst geology provides exogenous variation in PFAS transport, but the estimating equation simply compares PFAS outcomes across counties with and without any karst area. This amounts to a coarse cross-sectional difference, not an RD exploiting the geological discontinuity described in the manifest. Without exploiting the boundary or distance to karst/non-karst transitions (and without matching to contamination sources), geological exposure is endogenous to many economic, demographic, and infrastructural factors. The current design cannot credibly isolate the causal effect of karst conduit flow on PFAS contamination; the null result may simply reflect biased estimates and attenuation from heterogeneous treatment intensity. The authors must either implement the promised spatial RD (e.g., compare systems near karst boundaries controlling for proximity to known PFAS point sources) or develop alternative identification that addresses omitted-variable bias.

2. **Measurement and aggregation weaken the treatment.** Defining a county as “karst” if it contains any area in SSI bins 3–5 collapses rich geological heterogeneity into a binary indicator, which induces substantial measurement error, especially since karst influence operates at the well-head scale rather than the county. This classical measurement error attenuates coefficients toward zero and undermines the ability to detect meaningful effects. Moreover, counties with small karst pockets (e.g., a few square miles) are treated the same as counties dominated by karst. The authors acknowledge this limitation in the discussion, but the analysis should quantify it—for instance, by reweighting systems by the precise karst fraction within their catchment or using spatially-resolved karst maps matched to the wells themselves.

3. **Confounders are insufficiently addressed.** Karst counties may systematically differ in PFAS source proximity (industrial facilities, military bases), socioeconomic characteristics, and water treatment infrastructure. The current specification controls only for state fixed effects, leaving these county-level confounders untouched. Without adjusting for PFAS source density (e.g., DoD installations, airports, manufacturing sites) or socioeconomic/demographic proxies (e.g., urbanization, income), the estimated association captures both the true geological mechanism and other correlated determinants of contamination. This concern is especially acute because the groundwater placebo yields similar point estimates, suggesting that the current coefficient may reflect residual confounding rather than karst transport. The authors need to incorporate source controls or exploit the geologic boundary directly to credibly isolate the mechanism.

**Suggestions**

- **Implement a spatial RD at karst boundaries.** To realize the original research idea, identify contiguous karst/non-karst boundaries and construct a running variable measuring distance from each public water system (or wellhead centroid) to the nearest boundary. Limiting the sample to systems within a narrow bandwidth around the boundary can better approximate the exogenous variation generated by geological transport. Include controls for proximity to known PFAS point sources (e.g., DoD bases, airports, industrial facilities) and other geographic covariates to improve precision. If information about subterranean aquifer assignments is available, consider matching each system to the underlying formation rather than using a county average. The RD would also allow graphical inspection (e.g., PFAS outcomes versus distance to boundary) to assess continuity and treatment variation.

- **Incorporate source proximity and treatment controls.** Since PFAS contamination is highly localized, augment the regression with variables capturing the number or distance of PFAS-emitting facilities within a certain radius, the presence of military bases, and possibly socioeconomic/demographic characteristics of the served population. If detailed treatment data are unavailable, use proxies such as system size or whether the system is part of a larger utility to proxy treatment capacity. These controls can help isolate the geological effect from correlated determinants and reduce omitted-variable bias.

- **Refine the geological treatment variable.** Instead of a binary “any karst,” use continuous measures that better capture treatment intensity. For example, calculate the share of the PWS service area (if available) lying within karst zones, or weigh county karst fraction by the area of karst within the ZIP codes relevant to each system. Alternatively, use distance-weighted karst density measures derived from high-resolution geological shapefiles. If the data permit, consider interacting karst measures with well depths or source type to capture the idea that karst effects primarily matter for groundwater systems. Explicitly report the distribution of these refined measures to demonstrate treatment variation.

- **Separate identification for groundwater versus surface water systems.** While the paper does subsample groundwater and surface water systems, the fact that the surface water placebo produces similar coefficients suggests the current design lacks specificity. To strengthen the groundwater channel argument, focus on systems classified unequivocally as groundwater sourced with well coordinates. If coordinates are not public, consider limiting to systems where the water source is known to be a well tapping a carbonate aquifer. This would reduce heterogeneity and provide a clearer test of the hypothesized mechanism.

- **Address statistical power and precision.** Given the noisy estimates and wide confidence intervals, consider calculating minimum detectable effects for each outcome given the sample size and variation. If the paper’s contribution is to “bound” the effect, formalizing the precision would help readers interpret how large karst effects could be and still be consistent with the data. Reporting confidence intervals (not just p-values) and discussing whether the null is “informative” (e.g., ruling out effects above policy-relevant thresholds) would strengthen the empirical narrative.

- **Explore heterogeneity and nonlinearities meaningfully.** The discussion briefly mentions a quadratic karst fraction effect that is significant, but this is not reported in the main tables. If a threshold mechanism exists (e.g., only counties with very high karst density show effects), this should be part of the main empirical section with appropriate robustness checks, rather than an offhand note. Presenting spline/glm plots or dose-response curves can help assess whether the effect is genuinely nonlinear or driven by outliers. Ensure multiple testing concerns are addressed when exploring such patterns.

- **Clarify implications and limitations.** Given the null findings, the paper can still contribute by clarifying the policy implications more precisely. For instance, if geology does not predict contamination at the county level, what does that imply for EPA monitoring strategies or for economists seeking instruments for PFAS exposure? Be careful not to overstate the generality of the conclusion, especially since the treatment variation is coarse. Additionally, if the intended RD could not be implemented due to data constraints, articulate those constraints explicitly and suggest how future work (e.g., obtaining well-level coordinates or detailed karst maps) might overcome them.

In sum, the paper addresses an important question and benefits from excellent data linkage, but it currently falls short of delivering a credible causal estimate of the karst mechanism. By reintroducing the spatial RD, refining the treatment measure, and controlling for PFAS sources, the paper can better match the research question to the empirical approach and make a more compelling contribution to the literature on PFAS exposure and environmental health economics.
