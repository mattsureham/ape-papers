## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
This paper uses the 2022 *Dobbs* decision as a natural experiment to test whether reproductive rights are valued as a residential amenity. By looking at the St. Louis metropolitan area—where Missouri’s instant abortion ban created a sharp discontinuity with protected access in Illinois—the paper asks if this loss of rights is capitalized into home values. Despite the "pure" Tiebout setting, the author finds a null result: the loss of reproductive rights did not move the needle on property values at the border over a 30-month horizon.

**Evaluation:** The paper does a decent job of setting the scene, but the first two paragraphs are a bit too "journalistic" about the timing of the decision and not "economic" enough about the stakes. 

**The pitch the paper should have:**
"Does the sudden removal of fundamental legal rights affect where people choose to live and, consequently, the value of land? Using the sharpest policy discontinuity in recent American history—the overnight activation of Missouri’s abortion ban—this paper tests for Tiebout capitalization of reproductive rights at the Missouri-Illinois border. Contrary to models of amenity valuation, I find no evidence that home values responded to the policy shock, suggesting that reproductive access may be an 'episodic' rather than 'daily-use' amenity that fails to trigger house-price sorting in dense metropolitan areas."

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**Contribution:** The paper provides a high-stakes null result on the Tiebout capitalization of reproductive rights using a geographic difference-in-discontinuities design.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It differentiates itself well from Dench et al. (2025) by moving from state-level synthetic control to a sharp border design. It is clearly distinct from the classic "good amenity" papers (Black, 1999) by focusing on a "rights-based" amenity.
*   **Question vs. Gap:** It frames itself as answering a question about the WORLD (how do rights affect markets?), which is a strength.
*   **Clarity:** A smart economist would say: "It's a clean null result showing that *Dobbs* didn't cause immediate residential sorting reflected in prices at state borders."
*   **Bigness:** To make this bigger, the paper needs to dive deeper into *heterogeneity*. Are there effects in ZIP codes with higher concentrations of women of reproductive age? Is there a volume effect (number of listings) even if prices didn't move?

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
The paper sits at the intersection of Urban/Labor (Tiebout sorting) and Health/Public Economics. 

*   **Closest neighbors:** Oates (1969) on capitalization; Black (1999) on school borders; Chay & Greenstone (2005) on air quality; and the nascent *Dobbs* literature (Myers, Dench).
*   **Positioning:** It should position itself as a **boundary test** for Tiebout. We know schools and crime capitalize. Why doesn't this? The paper correctly suggests the "episodic" nature of the service, but it could build more on the "Access Blunting" theory.
*   **Right conversation?** Yes, but it could speak more to the Political Economy literature. Is there a "political sorting" happening that is masked by the "healthcare sorting"?

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC
*   **Setup:** Tiebout predicts that local policies/amenities are priced into land. 
*   **Tension:** Reproductive rights are a massive policy shift, yet they differ from "daily-use" amenities like schools. Do they actually drive the most expensive decision a household makes (buying a home)?
*   **Resolution:** They don't—at least not at the border within 30 months.
*   **Implications:** Proximity to a "protection" state might "blunt" the impact of a ban, meaning residents in border cities are shielded from the market consequences of their state's laws.

**Evaluation:** The arc is clear. It’s not just a collection of results; it’s a focused test of a specific economic mechanism (capitalization).

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
*   **The Fact:** "In St. Louis, you can live 10 minutes from a clinic or 10 minutes from a prison sentence for providing care, and the market doesn't care."
*   **Response:** People will lean in because *Dobbs* is visceral.

*   **ZHVI Discussion:** The author admits Zillow data is "smoothed." In an AER-level paper, the referees will demand transaction-level data (e.g., CoreLogic) to ensure the null isn't just a result of model-based smoothing in a low-volume environment.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The gap is **ambition and mechanism**. A null result in the AER usually needs to be "the final word" or offer a deep explanation of *why* the theory failed.

*   **The Single Biggest Improvement:** The author must address the **"Interest Rate Lock-in"** confounder. The post-period (2022-2024) coincides exactly with the fastest rate hike in Fed history. If no one is moving because they are locked into 3% mortgages, the Tiebout mechanism is mechanically broken. The author needs to show that *other* amenities (e.g., changes in school ratings) *did* still capitalize during this period to prove the "market" was still functioning.
