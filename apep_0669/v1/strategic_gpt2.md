## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple, high-interest question: when Missouri lost abortion rights overnight after *Dobbs* while adjacent Illinois did not, did that new policy divide show up in housing prices at the St. Louis border? The answer is no detectable border capitalization over roughly 30 months, suggesting that even a sharp and salient rights shock may not be priced into local housing markets when cross-border access is easy.

A busy economist should care because this is really about a broader question than abortion policy: when do politically salient place-based policies become housing-market amenities? That is an AER-type question if framed correctly.

**Does the paper articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Not quite. The opening is vivid, but the paper spends too much time narrating the institutional event and too little time immediately elevating the question to one of broad economic interest. The current intro says, in effect, “here was a sharp policy discontinuity; I estimate its effect.” That is competent. It does not yet say, “this is a test of how rights, mobility, and market access interact in local equilibrium.”

**What the first two paragraphs should say instead:**

> A central question in urban and public economics is which policies households value enough to capitalize into local housing prices. Schools, crime, and environmental quality are routinely priced. But what about civil or reproductive rights—highly salient policies that matter deeply to some households, yet may be exercised infrequently and may be partially offset by access to nearby jurisdictions?
>
> The *Dobbs* decision created an unusually clean test. Missouri banned abortion almost immediately while neighboring Illinois maintained legal access, generating a sharp policy discontinuity within the integrated St. Louis housing market. If households treat reproductive rights as a local amenity, the Missouri side of the border should become less valuable relative to the Illinois side. I show that it does not: despite the salience and abruptness of the shock, there is no detectable housing-price discontinuity at the border over the following 30 months, consistent with cross-border access blunting local capitalization.

That is the pitch the paper should have.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:**  
The paper shows that an abrupt and highly salient loss of abortion rights in one state does not generate detectable housing-price capitalization at a dense metropolitan border, implying limits to Tiebout sorting for rights-based policies when nearby cross-border access remains available.

### Evaluation

**Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?**  
Only partly. The paper does distinguish itself from state-level post-*Dobbs* papers by saying it studies **border capitalization** rather than aggregate state effects. That is the right instinct. But the differentiation is still too design-centric and not conceptual enough.

Right now the paper’s novelty reads as:
- “I have a cleaner geographic discontinuity.”
  
It should read as:
- “I test a different economic margin: whether rights shocks are valued as locally priced amenities in equilibrium, as opposed to affecting aggregate migration, political behavior, fertility, or statewide demand.”

That distinction is much stronger.

**Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a gap in a literature?**  
Mixed, but too often the latter. The paper repeatedly says “this contributes to three literatures” and “fills this gap.” That is serviceable but second-tier framing. The stronger world question is: **Do rights shocks alter where people want to live enough to move housing prices, and under what conditions?**

**Could a smart economist explain what is new after reading the intro?**  
Not cleanly enough. Right now they might say: “It’s a border DiD on *Dobbs* and house prices with a null result.” That is not enough. They should instead be able to say: “It’s a paper about the conditions under which rights are or are not capitalized into land values.”

**What would make the contribution bigger?**  
Most importantly: **reframe the estimand as a test of a broader class of policies.** Several concrete ways to enlarge the contribution:

1. **Make cross-border access the organizing mechanism, not a post hoc discussion point.**  
   The paper’s most interesting idea is not the null per se; it is the hypothesis that rights may fail to capitalize when access is geographically substitutable. That could become the main contribution.

2. **Exploit heterogeneity in access intensity.**  
   Even without changing the design, the paper would be much bigger if it asked: are null effects concentrated where Illinois clinics are minutes away, and do effects grow with travel time to legal care? That would turn the paper from “no effect” into “capitalization depends on effective access.”

3. **Connect to broader “rights as amenities” questions.**  
   The contribution could be broadened by explicitly linking to whether households price legal environments involving reproductive rights, LGBTQ protections, marijuana legality, gun laws, etc. The paper need not estimate all of these, but the framing should make clear this is a general question about legal regime capitalization.

4. **Shift from “property values after Dobbs” to “limits of Tiebout for salient but episodic amenities.”**  
   That is a much more original and durable contribution.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Before this paper, economists know that many local amenities and policies are capitalized into housing prices, and that *Dobbs* created sharp policy differences across states. There is also emerging evidence that reproductive policy affects migration and perhaps broader economic outcomes.

### Tension
If abortion access matters to households, an overnight Missouri–Illinois split inside an integrated metro area should be a near-ideal setting for capitalization. Yet there is an offsetting reason it might not: when the border itself provides easy access to the protected regime, the effective amenity wedge may be small even if the legal wedge is large.

### Resolution
The paper finds no meaningful border discontinuity in home values after *Dobbs* in St. Louis, and the Kansas City comparison does not support a consistent rights-capitalization story either.

### Implications
The broader implication is not merely “no effect in St. Louis.” It is that **salient rights shocks are not automatically priced into land values**, especially when service access is spatially substitutable. That has implications for Tiebout models, border economics, and how economists should interpret the local incidence of rights-based policies.

### Evaluation
At present, the paper has a **partial** narrative arc but not a fully convincing one. The structure exists, but the tension and implications are underdeveloped. The paper often reads like a careful sequence of specifications supporting a null, rather than a sharp story with stakes.

The biggest weakness is that the **mechanism comes too late**. “Cross-border clinic access neutralizing the Tiebout mechanism” is currently presented as an interpretation after the results. But that is actually the most interesting story in the paper. It should appear in the setup and tension, not only in the discussion.

So yes: in its current form, it is somewhat a collection of results looking for a bigger story. The story it should tell is:

> *Dobbs* offers a clean test of whether rights are local amenities. The test fails—not because rights do not matter, but because legal access is not the same as geographic access, and border proximity can undo the local-equilibrium response.

That is a real narrative.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

**What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?**  
“Missouri lost abortion rights overnight, right across the river from Illinois, and home prices at the border didn’t move.”

That is a good opening fact.

**Would people lean in or reach for their phones?**  
They would lean in initially because the shock is salient and the setting is intuitive. But they will only stay engaged if the speaker quickly answers the follow-up: **why didn’t prices move?**

**What follow-up question would they ask?**
1. “Does that mean households don’t value reproductive rights?”
2. “Or does it just mean cross-border access makes the effective policy difference small?”
3. “Would the answer be different far from a legal-access border?”
4. “Are housing markets just too slow, or is this really about amenity salience?”

Those are strong follow-up questions. Good sign.

**Is the null result itself interesting?**  
Yes, potentially very much so—but only if the paper makes the case that this is a meaningful falsification of a natural prior. The paper partly does this by emphasizing the cleanliness of the shock. But it needs to push harder on why the null is informative:
- the treatment was sharp,
- the issue was salient,
- the market was integrated,
- the margin is one where Tiebout logic should bite if it is going to bite anywhere.

Then the null becomes valuable evidence about **limits of capitalization**, not a failed search for significance.

Right now it is close, but not fully there. It still risks feeling like “we looked and found nothing.” The paper needs to convert that into: “we learned something important about why some politically important policies do not become locally priced amenities.”

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

### What would make it read better?

1. **Shorten the institutional background.**  
   Much of Section 2 can be compressed substantially. The paper does not need a long retelling of *Dobbs*, Missouri’s trigger law, Illinois protections, Kansas referendum, and the Politico leak in separate mini-sections. A leaner institutional section would preserve pace.

2. **Bring the key result earlier.**  
   The paper does front-load the null result in the introduction, which is good. But it takes too long to get from “interesting shock” to “why the null matters.” The intro should state the interpretation earlier: this is evidence on the limits of capitalization when cross-border access is easy.

3. **Move some design jargon and auxiliary specifications out of the main text.**  
   The current introduction gets method-heavy quickly. The paper does not need to advertise every specification variant early on. Keep the core design, but save some of the “five specifications / rdrobust / placebo architecture” for later.

4. **Demote or remove the standardized effect-size appendix/table.**  
   The SDE table adds little and feels mechanical. It is not helping the story.

5. **Likewise, trim repetitive robustness narration.**  
   There is some redundancy across main text, robustness section, and appendix discussion. For a null paper especially, repetition can make the paper feel defensive. Better to be sharper and more interpretive.

6. **The discussion should do more than offer caveats.**  
   It currently reads like a standard “three possible mechanisms” section. It should instead crystallize the main conceptual takeaway:
   - legal rights as amenities,
   - geographic substitutability,
   - implications for border incidence and spatial equilibrium.

7. **Conclusion should be shorter and bolder.**  
   The current conclusion mostly restates. It should end with one strong sentence about what economists should update: not all salient policy rights are capitalized, especially where border access blunts local scarcity.

### Are there results buried in the robustness section that should be in the main results?

Yes: **the bandwidth pattern** is actually conceptually important, not just a robustness exercise. The fact that estimates are near zero or negative very close to the border and become positive farther away is central to the argument that the effect is spatial trend, not capitalization. That should be elevated as a key substantive pattern.

Also, if the paper can present any simple evidence on **distance to clinics / travel access**, that belongs in the main paper, not buried.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

At present, the main gap is **not primarily technical**. It is a combination of:

- **Framing problem**
- **Ambition problem**
- Some **scope problem**

Less of a novelty problem than it might seem, because the underlying question is interesting. But the current package is too close to: “a clean null on one border.” That is not enough for AER.

### The real gap
An AER paper here would not just document no effect in St. Louis. It would use this setting to answer a general question about **how rights, geography, and local equilibrium interact**. It would probably either:
1. show systematic heterogeneity by access/friction and thereby identify when capitalization appears, or
2. embed the null in a broader comparative framework across border types, distances, or policy environments.

Right now the paper is competent but safe. The empirical setting is sharp; the intellectual claim is not yet sharp enough.

### Single most impactful advice
**Rebuild the paper around the claim that rights are capitalized only when geographic access is not easily substitutable, and use the existing evidence to make cross-border access—not Dobbs alone—the paper’s central conceptual contribution.**

That is the one thing I would tell the author to change.

If they do only one thing, it should be this:  
Stop selling the paper as “a null effect of *Dobbs* on border home values” and start selling it as “evidence on the limits of Tiebout sorting for rights-based amenities when borders preserve access.”
