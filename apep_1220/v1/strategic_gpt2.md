## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper studies Denmark’s 2024 property tax reform, which sharply reduced municipal land tax rates while giving incumbent homeowners a tax discount that disappears when they sell. The paper asks whether this kind of reform changes housing-market liquidity, and its main short-run finding is that larger tax cuts reduced forced sales, suggesting that property taxes can matter as a liquidity constraint for distressed homeowners.

Why should a busy economist care? Because this is potentially a rare modern policy episode combining two big issues in public and urban economics: property-tax incidence/liquidity on the one hand, and tax-induced housing lock-in on the other. If framed well, it could speak to a broad question: when housing tax policy changes, does it mainly distort mobility, or does it also relax household financial distress in economically meaningful ways?

### Does the paper articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?
Not quite. The current opening oversells the “lock-in” angle, but the actual paper does not yet show lock-in effects on mobility or turnover. The paper’s credible empirical result is about **forced sales falling after larger tax cuts**. So the introduction currently promises one paper and delivers another.

### What the first two paragraphs should say instead
The paper should open with the world question it can actually answer:

> Property taxes are usually studied as a capitalization or incidence issue, but for many homeowners they are also a recurring liquidity obligation. When those obligations fall sharply, do financially constrained owners become less likely to lose their homes? Denmark’s 2024 property tax reform offers a useful test: it cut municipal land-tax rates nationwide, but by very different amounts across municipalities, creating large variation in the size of homeowners’ immediate tax relief.
>
> I use that variation to study the reform’s short-run effects on housing-market distress. The main finding is that municipalities receiving larger tax cuts saw larger declines in forced property sales, suggesting that property taxation can meaningfully affect homeowner liquidity and foreclosure-type outcomes. The reform also introduced an incumbent-specific tax discount that may create future mobility lock-in, but the contribution of this paper is to document the short-run liquidity channel rather than long-run turnover effects.

That is the pitch the paper actually has.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution
The paper provides early evidence from Denmark’s 2024 property tax reform that larger property-tax cuts reduced forced home sales, implying that property taxes can operate as an important liquidity burden for distressed homeowners.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest papers?
Only partially. Right now the paper tries to sit in three literatures at once:
1. Denmark’s reform,
2. property tax capitalization,
3. Prop-13-style lock-in.

But it is not actually making a major contribution to (2), and it is not yet empirically contributing much to (3), because it does not show an effect on turnover, mobility, listing behavior, or transaction volume. The distinctive contribution is narrower: **short-run distress relief from a large property tax cut**.

Closest-neighbor differentiation is fuzzy because a reader could reasonably summarize it as: “another reduced-form paper showing a tax cut affected an outcome after a reform.” The paper needs much sharper differentiation around **forced sales / financial distress** as the key outcome.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a literature gap?
Too much as a literature gap. “First causal evidence on Denmark’s 2024 reform” is not, by itself, an AER contribution. The stronger world question is:
- Are property taxes a meaningful driver of homeowner distress and involuntary exits?
That is broader and more important than “nobody has yet studied this reform.”

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?
Not cleanly. They would probably say: “It’s a DiD on a Danish property tax reform, with some evidence on forced sales, and maybe something about lock-in.” That is not yet a memorable contribution. The introduction muddies the message by foregrounding lock-in while the result is liquidity relief.

### What would make the contribution bigger?
Most importantly: **show actual market liquidity or mobility outcomes, not just distress outcomes**. For example:
- total sales/turnover,
- time on market,
- listings,
- owner moves,
- downsizing/upsizing,
- refinancing or mortgage distress,
- delinquency or arrears if available.

A second route would be to strengthen the distress story:
- heterogeneity by age, income, leverage, house-price growth, or baseline tax burden,
- stronger evidence that the effect is concentrated where tax bills were a larger share of household resources,
- evidence separating owner-occupiers from other property owners.

A third route is reframing:
- not “lock-in discount” as the central contribution,
- but “property taxation as a liquidity shock to homeowners.”

That would make the paper more coherent and potentially bigger.

That is a stronger and more novel conversation than the current one.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Denmark replaced an outdated property-tax system with a major reform that cut land-tax rates by very different amounts across municipalities and gave incumbents a discount that disappears upon sale.

### Tension
The reform combines two potentially opposing forces:
- immediate tax relief for current owners,
- a new tax wedge tied to moving.
So what dominates in the short run: distress relief or lock-in?

### Resolution
In the short run, municipalities with larger tax cuts saw fewer forced sales. The paper does not yet show turnover or mobility effects from the lock-in provision.

### Implications
Property taxes may materially affect housing distress, not just prices or mobility. But the lock-in implications remain speculative until longer-run turnover data are available.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
Only partially. At present it is a bit of a **results bundle looking for a story**. The title, abstract, and intro say “lock-in discount” and “housing market liquidity,” but the main evidence is “forced sales declined.” Those are related, but not identical. Forced sales are one margin of liquidity/distress; they are not the same as market liquidity broadly conceived.

### What story should it be telling?
The story should be:

1. Property taxes create recurring cash-flow obligations for homeowners.
2. Denmark unexpectedly changed those obligations at large scale and with large cross-place variation.
3. The immediate effect of bigger tax relief was fewer forced sales.
4. This shows a neglected channel: property-tax design affects homeowner distress.
5. The lock-in feature makes Denmark an important setting for future work on mobility, but that is not this paper’s main result.

That narrative is honest, coherent, and potentially interesting. The current “lock-in” story is aspirational, but not yet earned by the evidence.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?
“I’d lead with: a large property-tax cut in Denmark appears to have reduced forced home sales in the short run.”

That is the cleanest and most interesting fact.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
Some would lean in, especially urban/public/household finance economists. But many would immediately ask: “Interesting—but where is the lock-in? Did turnover fall?” If the answer is no, some of the excitement dissipates because the title and framing prime readers for a different result.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Almost certainly:
- “Did the reform affect mobility or transactions?”
Then:
- “Is this just a short-run foreclosure/distress story?”
And:
- “Who is affected—high-tax places, low-income owners, elderly owners, highly leveraged households?”

### If the findings are modest: is the modest result itself interesting?
Yes, potentially. A well-established null on turnover would be interesting, and a modest but credible forced-sales effect can also be interesting if the paper makes the case that this is the relevant welfare margin. But at present it risks feeling like a paper that wanted to study lock-in and instead found a reduction in forced sales. That can still be publishable in a good field journal, but for AER the author must persuade readers that **this margin is the main event**, not a consolation prize.

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

### What would make it read better?

#### 1. Retitle the paper
Current title: **The Lock-in Discount: Property Tax Reform and Housing Market Liquidity in Denmark**

That title oversells what is shown. A better title would foreground the actual result:
- **Property Tax Relief and Forced Home Sales: Evidence from Denmark’s 2024 Reform**
or
- **Property Taxes and Homeowner Distress: Evidence from Denmark**
or
- **Property Tax Reform, Liquidity Relief, and Forced Sales in Denmark**

#### 2. Rewrite the abstract
The abstract should not imply the paper is about lock-in if it does not estimate lock-in. The last sentence currently admits the key limitation, but too late. The abstract should say upfront that this is a short-run distress paper in a setting that may later be useful for studying lock-in.

#### 3. Shorten institutional detail in the intro
The first pages spend too much time on the reform’s moving pieces before giving the reader the punchline. Put the main finding earlier.

#### 4. Move descriptive tax-revenue material back
The tax-revenue and assessment-value results feel secondary and not especially exciting. They are also the least strategically useful if they have pre-trend issues. They should not share equal billing with the main result. AER readers will not care much that tax revenue moved in a mechanical way unless it helps sharpen the main economic mechanism.

#### 5. Bring the forced-sales result to the front
The best figure/table should appear very early:
- a simple graph showing municipalities with larger cuts had larger post-2024 declines in forced sales,
- or event-study figure for forced sales.
That should be the paper’s visual centerpiece.

#### 6. Cut robustness discussion from the main narrative
The current robustness section is too centered on tax revenue, which is not the flagship contribution. That is exactly backwards from a storytelling standpoint.

#### 7. Strengthen the conclusion
The conclusion currently mostly summarizes and points to future work. It should instead make a broader claim about what economists should learn:
- recurring housing taxes can affect household distress,
- reform design involves a tradeoff between immediate relief and future mobility distortion.

That would give the paper more policy bite.

#### Also a scope problem
Yes. For AER, one short-run reduced-form result on forced sales from one reform is probably too narrow unless it unlocks a much bigger conceptual point or is paired with richer outcomes/mechanisms.

Somewhat. There is a familiar feel to the design and result. “Tax cut reduced distress” is plausible and useful, but not obviously field-defining without broader evidence. The paper needs either:

- sharper mechanism,

**Version A: The bigger lock-in paper**
- show effects on turnover, listing, mobility, match quality, downsizing, or transaction prices;

Right now the paper sits between A and B and therefore does not fully satisfy either.

**Stop selling this as a lock-in paper unless you can show mobility effects; instead, fully commit to the stronger short-run contribution that property-tax relief reduces homeowner distress, and build the paper around that question.**
