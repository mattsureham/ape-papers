## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper studies Denmark’s 2024 property tax reform, which sharply reduced municipal land tax rates and, for incumbent homeowners, introduced a tax benefit that is lost upon sale. Using cross-municipality variation in the size of the tax cut, the paper argues that larger cuts led to fewer forced property sales in the short run, suggesting that property tax relief can ease homeowner liquidity distress.

Why should a busy economist care? Because property taxes are one of the largest recurring housing costs, and reforms to them may affect not just prices and local public finance, but household distress and housing market functioning. A nationwide reform with large cross-place heterogeneity is potentially a very useful setting.

### Does the paper articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Not really. The opening is vivid, but the paper immediately leads with the lock-in discount and mobility penalty, while the actual evidence is about forced sales, i.e. liquidity relief. That is a serious framing mismatch. The first two paragraphs currently promise one paper and deliver another.

### What the first two paragraphs should say instead

The paper should open with the concrete economic question it can actually answer:

> Property taxes are a large, recurring housing expense, but we know surprisingly little about whether cutting them changes homeowner financial distress in the short run. Denmark’s 2024 property tax reform provides a rare opportunity to study this question because it reduced municipal land tax rates nationwide, with the size of the cut varying sharply across municipalities.
>
> This paper asks whether larger property tax cuts improve housing market liquidity by reducing distress sales. I show that municipalities receiving larger tax cuts experienced larger declines in forced property sales immediately after the reform. The broader reform also created an incumbent-specific tax advantage that may eventually reduce mobility, but the short-run fact established here is simpler and important in its own right: lower recurring housing taxes can prevent distressed exits from homeownership.

That is the pitch the paper should have. If the author wants to keep the lock-in angle, it should be presented as institutional context and future relevance, not as the headline contribution.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper’s contribution is to provide early evidence from Denmark’s 2024 property tax reform that larger cuts in recurring property tax liabilities reduced forced property sales in the short run.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest papers?

Only partially. The paper mentions Prop 13 and capitalization papers, but it does not sharply distinguish itself from them. Right now the contribution reads as a blend of:
- another property tax paper,
- another quasi-experimental housing paper,
- another “lock-in” paper,

without making clear which lane it is actually in.

The cleanest differentiation is not “first paper on Denmark’s reform.” That is too local and too weak for AER. The differentiation should be: **most existing lock-in papers study turnover; this paper instead studies the short-run distress/liquidity consequences of changing recurring property tax burdens.** That is a more world-facing contribution.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a gap in a literature?

Too much the latter, and too much “first evidence on Denmark.” The stronger framing is about the world:

- Do recurring property taxes push cash-constrained homeowners into distress sales?
- When governments reduce housing tax burdens, do they stabilize homeownership among financially strained households?

That is stronger than “this contributes to three literatures.”

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?

At present, probably not cleanly. They might say: “It’s a DiD on a Danish property tax reform, and something happens to forced sales.” Worse, because of the title and opening, they may think it is a paper about mobility lock-in and then be confused when the main outcome is forced sales.

### What would make the contribution bigger?

Most importantly: **measure the outcome that matches the framing.** If the title is “The Lock-in Discount” and the conceptual hook is mobility penalties, the paper needs turnover, listings, transactions, time-on-market, moving, or new-buyer incidence. Without that, the current title and setup oversell.

If the author cannot yet observe those outcomes meaningfully post-reform, then the contribution can still be enlarged by reframing around:
- **household distress** rather than lock-in;
- **housing market stability** rather than mobility;
- possibly outcomes like mortgage arrears, foreclosure filings, tax delinquency, bankruptcy, or refinancing if available.

A bigger paper would connect the tax cut to a broader margin of household balance-sheet adjustment, not just one distress outcome.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Property taxes are a major housing cost. Some property tax systems also create incumbent advantages that can distort market behavior. Denmark’s 2024 reform dramatically changed recurring tax liabilities across municipalities.

### Tension

There are two possible effects moving in opposite directions:
- lower tax bills may ease household cash-flow pressure and reduce distress;
- incumbent-specific tax advantages may discourage mobility and reduce market liquidity.

That is actually a nice tension. But the paper only has evidence on the first margin.

### Resolution

The paper finds that municipalities with larger tax cuts saw larger declines in forced sales immediately after the reform.

### Implications

Short-run property tax relief may stabilize homeownership among distressed households. Longer-run mobility distortions may emerge later, but this paper cannot yet show them.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

Only partially. Right now it is a somewhat mismatched arc:

- setup: lock-in discount and mobility,
- results: forced sales,
- implications: maybe mobility later.

That is not fully satisfying. It feels like a paper with a good setting and one interesting result, still searching for the story that best fits that result.

### What story should it be telling?

A better story is:

1. Governments often debate property tax reform in terms of fairness and revenue.
2. But recurring housing taxes may also act as a cash-flow shock for homeowners.
3. Denmark’s reform created large variation in tax relief.
4. The immediate effect was fewer forced sales in places that received more relief.
5. Therefore, recurring property taxes matter for financial distress, not just capitalization or mobility.

That is a clean narrative arc. The lock-in feature can appear in the final section as “why this setting will remain valuable for future research.”

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?

“Denmark cut property taxes a lot in some municipalities and a little in others, and the bigger the cut, the bigger the immediate drop in forced home sales.”

That is the fact.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

Some would lean in, but not all. The strongest reaction would come if the result were framed as a broader statement about homeowner distress and recurring tax burdens. If framed as “a Danish lock-in paper with no mobility evidence yet,” many would tune out.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Almost certainly: **“Did it reduce turnover or lock people in?”**

And the answer is currently: not yet known. That is the paper’s biggest strategic problem, because the paper itself invites exactly that question.

A second follow-up would be: **“Is this really about tax cuts relieving distress, or something special about the reassessment/discount bundle?”** Again, that is a referee question in part, but strategically it underscores that the main result is not yet enough to support the paper’s current title and framing.

### If the findings are modest: is the modesty itself interesting?

Yes, but only if the author leans into what is actually learned. The paper should not apologize for being “early evidence.” It should say: **the immediate margin is itself economically and policy relevant.** Learning that recurring property tax relief prevents forced sales is not a failed first stage on the way to a future mobility paper. It is a result.

But the paper must stop presenting itself as if the real contribution is the lock-in mechanism that it cannot yet test.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

### Scope problem

The scope is currently narrow relative to the ambition of the framing. One short-run distress outcome is interesting, but to become an AER-level paper it likely needs either:
- the key market outcome promised by the framing: turnover/mobility/liquidity, or
- a broader set of distress and housing outcomes that establish a more general economic mechanism.

### Novelty problem

“First evidence on Denmark’s reform” is not enough. The question must be bigger than the setting. The author needs to persuade readers that the paper changes how we think about property tax design generally.

### Ambition problem

- a broader conceptual payoff,
- a more comprehensive empirical picture,
- or a finding that is genuinely surprising and belief-changing.

Right now it is not yet there.

### Single most impactful piece of advice

**Choose one paper: either make this a paper about homeowner distress from recurring property taxes, or add actual mobility/turnover evidence so the lock-in framing is real; do not keep the current mismatch.**
