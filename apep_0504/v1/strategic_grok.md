## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH (Most Important)

This paper examines whether mandatory display of food hygiene ratings—separating it cleanly from inspections—affects market structure by deterring low-quality entrants, using staggered UK devolution (Wales 2013, Northern Ireland 2016 vs. England's voluntary system). It finds that while treated areas saw broad business entry declines, food businesses were relatively insulated (DDD +1.4 entries per LA-quarter), rejecting deterrence and suggesting disclosure might attract quality-conscious entrepreneurs. Busy economists should care because it tests if cheap transparency can improve market quality without the feared market-shrinking side effects, with implications for disclosure regimes in health care, finance, and environment.

The paper itself does a solid job in the first two paragraphs, hooking with the sticker anecdote and asymmetric info theory, then stating the question clearly. But it undersells the punch: the positive DDD is buried, and the intro frames it as "first quasi-experimental evidence" rather than "evidence against deterrence." Revised first two paragraphs:

"A sticker on the door of your neighborhood restaurant seems trivial, but it tests a big idea: can mandatory quality disclosure reshape markets by weeding out lemons without shrinking supply? In food services, where consumers can't easily observe hygiene, theory predicts disclosure drives low-quality exit and deters marginal entry—yet evidence is scarce on whether mandates work beyond inspections. This paper exploits UK devolution—mandatory display in Wales (2013) and Northern Ireland (2016) vs. voluntary in England, with identical ratings—to show disclosure does not deter entry: food businesses were relatively insulated from jurisdiction-wide declines (DDD +1.4 entries/LA-quarter, p<0.001), suggesting transparency attracts quality investors."

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

The paper's contribution is the first causal evidence isolating mandatory display (vs. voluntary ratings) on firm entry, showing it rejects entry deterrence and relatively boosts food entry amid general declines.

- Differentiation from closest papers (Jin 2002/2005 on LA cards improving hygiene/health via consumer response; Dranove 2003 on hospital report cards; Ho 2012 on voluntary UK ratings shifting distributions) is decent but thin: it notes England's voluntary FHRS isolates display, but doesn't quantify how its market-structure focus (entry vs. grades/health) differs—e.g., Jin focused on outcomes, not dynamics.
- Framed mostly as filling a lit gap (disclosure causal evidence), but hints at world question (does transparency substitute regulation?). Push harder to world: "Does disclosure deter low-quality supply, as feared?"
- A smart economist could explain: "Clever UK DiD/DDD: mandatory stickers don't scare off food startups vs. other firms—surprising positive." Not "another DiD on policy."
- To make bigger: Switch outcome to consumer welfare (e.g., foodborne illness rates, via hospital data) or link entry to post-entry quality upgrading (track new firms' ratings over time); frame as "disclosure selects better entrants" with entrant quality survival as key mechanism.
