**Idea Fidelity**

The submission faithfully follows the manifest’s core idea. It leverages Bosättningslagen’s quasi-experimental refugee quotas, constructs own exposure from non-EU/EFTA foreign-born changes, and implements the SCI-weighted Bartik-style network exposure at the county level. The set of outcomes (2014–2018 long differences with 2010–2014 placebo and 2018–2022 persistence) and the robustness checks directly mirror those enumerated in the manifest, including the use of the SCB API for election data and the Kolada/Bosättningslagen context. The identification strategy—separating direct exposure from social-network-driven spillovers—comes through clearly, and the paper does not omit any of the key elements promised in the idea. 

**Summary**

The paper studies how Sweden’s 2016 Settlement Act’s refugee quotas affected Sweden Democrats support, showing that social-network exposure—as measured by SCI-weighted refugee placements in other counties—explains more of the 2014–2018 SD surge than local refugee exposure alone. A double-difference setup distinguishes own exposure from network exposure, with wild-cluster bootstrapped inference on 20 county clusters. A 2010–2014 placebo test is null, and the 2018–2022 follow-up shows the initial contagion reversing sign, suggesting transient backlash. The main contribution is introducing an SCI-based contagion channel into the Swedish context alongside the mandatory quota shock.

**Essential Points**

1. **Causal interpretation of network exposure requires stronger exclusion arguments.** The SCI-weighted average of other counties’ refugee inflows is treated as a quasi-exogenous “spillover,” but the key identifying assumption is that these network weights are independent of omitted determinants of the local change in SD support once own exposure and controls are included. SCI is highly correlated with geographic proximity, so counties that are social neighbors also tend to share unobserved media markets, labor-market shocks, or migration decisions. The placebo and county- fixed-effect checks are helpful, but they do not rule out that the cross-county network weights capture spatially clustered shocks contemporaneous with the Bosättningslagen allocations (e.g., regional media narratives, national campaign efforts, or labor-market demand for refugees). A more rigorous test or instrumental strategy is needed to establish that network exposure is truly exogenous rather than a proxy for omitted spatial patterns.

2. **Own exposure measurement may absorb part of the treatment and confound interpretation.** The regression uses the change in non-EU/EFTA foreign-born share (2014–2017) as own exposure, but this variable may itself be endogenous: municipalities that were politically primed for SD gains may have simultaneously attracted fewer refugees before the law hit, and their differential trends could be correlated with the dependent variable. The omission of municipality-level pre-trends in SD support (beyond the 2014 level) leaves open the possibility that the estimated own exposure and, by extension, the network exposure capture regression-to-the-mean or contemporaneous shocks rather than the quota shock from Bosättningslagen. Given the emphasis on a quasi-experimental mandate, the paper should exploit the actual quota coefficients or administrative allocation (rather than the realized foreign-born change) to sharpen causal claims.

3. **The interpretation of the reversal (2018–2022) needs more nuance.** The claim that network contagion “fades” rests on the negative coefficient when the outcome is the 2018–2022 change, but the underlying assumptions shift: the SCI-weighted treatment continues to be measured during 2014–2017 while outcomes now reflect 2018–2022 changes, so the structural meaning of the coefficient differs. Without a theoretical model or additional data on what happens in the later period (e.g., differential policy responses, refugee integration outcomes), it is premature to interpret the negative coefficient as the same “network contagion channel” reversing, rather than a different process dominating the later election. The authors should clarify why the SCI-weighted pre-treatment treatment should still capture causal variation in the 2018–2022 change and consider alternative explanations (e.g., spatial heterogeneity in anti-immigrant policy responses after 2018).

If additional essential issues are identified beyond these three, the paper should be rejected outright.

**Suggestions**

*Expand the treatment definition to administrative quotas.* The manifest emphasizes the formulaic allocation of quotas across municipalities. Replacing the realized change in non-EU foreign-born shares with the legally determined quotas (or predicted quotas based on the formula’s inputs) would better isolate the policy shock. Such an approach would reduce endogeneity concerns and align more closely with the “mandatory dispersal” narrative. If only the change in foreign-born shares is available, consider instrumenting it with the pre-2016 quota formula components (labor-market capacity, population share, refugee stock) to obtain a cleaner own-exposure effect. This would also allow the SCI network exposure to be constructed from the instrumented treatment rather than the realized outcome, mitigating simultaneity concerns.

*Deepen the exclusion argument for network exposure.* Beyond the placebo and county FE checks, consider a direct test that distinguishes social from geographical proximity. For example, include a set of geographic (contiguity/distance) weights in the regression alongside SCI-based weights. If network exposure remains significant while geographic proximity falls, this supports the unique role of social ties. Alternatively, split the SCI weights into “local” versus “non-local” connections (e.g., ties within 50 km versus beyond) and show that the latter drive the result, which is harder to attribute to geographic shocks. Another idea is to exploit variation in SCI that is orthogonal to geography, such as using only social connections mediated via particular demographic groups (e.g., youth-to-youth connections) if such disaggregation is available.

*Report first-stage variation and diagnostic statistics for the shift-share.* In the current setup, the “shift-share” instrumentation is implicit in the construction of NetworkExposure, but it would strengthen credibility to present descriptive statistics showing the variability in the SCI weight matrix, the correlation structure across counties, and the share of variation in the network exposure coming from shifts (treatment) versus shares (SCI weights). Bailey et al. (2018, 2020) emphasize that both components matter. A simple decomposition (e.g., Firth’s shift-share decomposition) can reassure readers that the variation is not driven by a few large SCI links or outliers.

*Address potential spillovers on the “own” channel.* The model assumes that own exposure only affects the municipality in question, but mandatory quotas might also influence nearby municipalities through housing markets, commuting patterns, or redistribution of service provision. The SCI network exposure might partly capture those spatial spillovers, not just social-information contagion. To tease this apart, consider adding spatial lags of the dependent variable or other observable spillover controls (e.g., neighboring municipality refugee inflows) to the horse-race specification. Showing that the SCI-based effect is robust to these controls would bolster the inference that the mechanism is social contagion rather than geographically proximate policy spillovers.

*Clarify the temporal alignment across variables.* The own exposure is measured as the 2014–2017 change but the outcome is 2014–2018, so the treatment window ends before the election. Explain why this is appropriate (e.g., refugees arrived mostly by 2017, with SD reacting in 2018) and verify that results are similar if the treatment window shifts slightly (e.g., 2015–2018 change or 2014–2016). This would assuage doubts about timing mismatches and strengthen the causal story.

*Enhance the narrative around the reversal.* The negative 2018–2022 coefficient is interesting but would benefit from supplementary evidence. Consider showing whether municipalities with high network exposure experienced different socio-economic developments (e.g., unemployment, social cohesion measures) between 2018 and 2022, which could explain a reversal via contact effects. Alternatively, examine heterogeneity by media markets or party campaign intensity to see if the reversal is concentrated where the initial contagion was strongest. This contextualizes the reversal within plausible mechanisms rather than presenting it as a stylized fact.

*Discuss general equilibrium and policy implications carefully.* The concluding section interprets the results as suggesting that the political costs of mandatory settlement are short-lived. This policy implication assumes that the SCI-linked backlash is the only source of political costs. It might help to temper this by noting that other constituencies (e.g., municipality administrations, service providers) may still bear persistent costs, and that social networks could also amplify positive reactions if narratives shift. A more balanced discussion would situate the findings within the broader set of political economy responses to refugee dispersal.

*Include robustness to weighting schemes.* Since municipalities vary wildly in population, and the network exposure is county-level averaged, results could be sensitive to whether the regression is population-weighted or not. Table 4 reports a population-weighted version, but it would be helpful to show whether the main inference changes when using county-level aggregates (to align with the SCI's granularity) or when weighting by the inverse of standard errors (to reflect measurement error). This would assure readers that the results are not driven by a few populous or tiny municipalities.

*Provide clearer exposition of the SCI construction.* The method section says the SCI is row-normalized excluding own county, but some readers may be unfamiliar with the index. A brief footnote or appendix describing how the SCI is measured, its stability over time, and why county-level aggregation is appropriate would aid transparency. Also, specify whether the SCI data is symmetric (i.e., SCI_{c,k} = SCI_{k,c}) and how missing connections are handled.

Overall, the paper tackles an important question with a promising design. Addressing the above concerns—especially around the exclusion restriction for network exposure and the operationalization of the treatment—would greatly strengthen its empirical credibility and contribution.
