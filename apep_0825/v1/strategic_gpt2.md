## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether anti-immigration voting spreads through social networks rather than arising only from local immigrant exposure. Using Sweden’s 2016 mandatory refugee allocation and Facebook’s Social Connectedness Index, it argues that municipalities became more supportive of the Sweden Democrats not just when they themselves received more refugees, but when socially connected places did—and that this network-driven backlash later faded.

A busy economist should care because the paper is trying to say something broader than Sweden: political reactions to immigration may diffuse through social ties, meaning the political effects of migration policy are socially multiplied rather than purely local.

**Does the paper articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Reasonably, but not sharply enough. The introduction opens with political context and a geographic puzzle, which is fine, but it takes too long to tell the reader the real stakes. The paper’s most distinctive claim is not “here is another paper on refugee exposure and far-right voting,” but “the politically relevant unit of exposure may be the social network, and that effect may be transient.” That should be the first thing the reader hears.

**What the first two paragraphs should say instead:**

> Immigration shocks may reshape politics far beyond the places where immigrants actually settle. If voters learn about immigration through friends, family, and social contacts in other places, then refugee settlement can generate a politically consequential social multiplier: backlash may spread through networks even where direct exposure is low. Whether this happens is a first-order question for the political economy of migration, because most existing evidence focuses on local exposure.
>
> This paper studies that question in Sweden after the 2016 Settlement Act, which forcibly dispersed refugees across all municipalities. I combine this policy-induced variation in local refugee exposure with Facebook’s Social Connectedness Index to measure each municipality’s exposure to refugee settlement in socially connected places. I find that socially transmitted exposure predicts the 2018 rise of the Sweden Democrats more strongly than own local exposure, but that this network effect reverses by 2022. The central message is that anti-immigration backlash can be socially contagious without being politically durable.

That is the pitch. It is world-facing, conceptually clean, and puts the transient nature of the effect in play from the start.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Before this paper, the standard framing is that immigration affects politics mostly through local exposure: places receiving more refugees may experience more backlash or more contact-induced moderation.

### Tension
But actual political backlash often appears in places with little direct exposure. That creates a puzzle: if not local contact alone, what transmits the shock politically? And if backlash spreads socially, does it harden into durable political realignment, or is it temporary?

### Resolution
The paper’s answer is that social ties matter a lot: municipalities connected to high-refugee-exposure places saw larger Sweden Democrat gains in 2018, even conditional on own exposure. But that network-propagated effect subsequently reverses by 2022.

### Implications
The implications are potentially important: immigration policy may generate political spillovers far beyond treated locations, but those spillovers need not be permanent. That matters for both theory and policy design.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
It has the ingredients, but the arc is not fully disciplined. Right now it feels like:

1. Sweden refugee policy setting  
2. Own exposure results  
3. SCI network results  
4. Robustness  
5. Surprise reversal  

That is a sequence of findings. The stronger story is:

1. **Puzzle:** Backlash appears where refugees are not.  
2. **Hypothesis:** Social ties transmit political response.  
3. **Test:** Use formula-driven refugee allocation + SCI to measure indirect exposure.  
4. **Main result:** Indirect exposure matters more than direct exposure.  
5. **Dynamic result:** The indirect component fades, suggesting contagion rather than durable transformation.  
6. **Big implication:** Political externalities of migration are socially multiplied but not necessarily persistent.

That is the story it should be telling.

The biggest narrative issue is that the paper treats the 2022 reversal as both central and fragile. Editorially, that creates instability. Decide: is “fades” the paper’s title-worthy insight, or is it a provocative extension? The current manuscript wants the title and abstract payoff from the reversal without fully committing the paper’s structure to it.
