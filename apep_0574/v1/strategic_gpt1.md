## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple, important question: when a major energy shock destroys domestic manufacturing capacity, does trade step in and replace the lost output? Using the 2022 Russian gas cutoff to Europe, the paper argues that although gas-dependent, energy-intensive manufacturing sectors contracted sharply, imports did not rise to fill the gap—suggesting that supply shocks can destroy downstream demand and supply chains rather than merely reallocate production across borders.

A busy economist should care because this is potentially a broad claim about the limits of trade as an adjustment mechanism. If true, it speaks not just to the European gas crisis, but to how we should think about resilience, deindustrialization, supply-chain fragility, and the economic incidence of energy transition policies.

### Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Pretty well, actually. The paper has a live question, concrete setting, and a clear surprise. It is stronger than many introductions because it starts with an intuitive prediction and then claims to overturn it.

But the current opening also overreaches a bit. It invokes “the core prediction of Ricardian and Heckscher-Ohlin models” and then suggests the paper overturns textbook trade logic. That is rhetorically attractive, but risky. Referees will immediately say: standard models do not necessarily imply one-for-one import replacement in the presence of input-output linkages, demand shifts, adjustment frictions, quality differences, intra-EU reallocation, and broad equilibrium contraction. So the paper is strongest not as “trade theory is wrong,” but as “a widely presumed adjustment margin was much weaker than policymakers and standard intuition expected in this episode.”

### The pitch the paper should have

When Europe lost access to cheap Russian gas in 2022, energy-intensive manufacturing output fell sharply in the most exposed countries. A natural benchmark is that imports should replace lost domestic output. This paper shows that they largely did not: sectors hit hardest on the production side did not see differential increases in extra-EU imports, suggesting that large supply shocks can propagate through supply chains by shrinking downstream activity and severing supplier relationships, not just by shifting sourcing abroad.

That is the pitch. More disciplined, less doctrinal, and still interesting.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### Contribution in one sentence

The paper’s contribution is to show that the 2022 European gas shock caused large production losses in exposed energy-intensive manufacturing without corresponding extra-EU import substitution, implying that trade adjustment to major supply-side industrial shocks can be much weaker than conventional policy intuition assumes.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?

Only partially.

The production-side finding is not the contribution; that literature already exists. The distinctive piece is the absence of import substitution. The paper knows this, but it has not yet sharply separated “what we already knew” from “what is genuinely new here.”

Right now the reader gets:
- existing papers show production/GDP effects of the gas shock,
- this paper extends to trade,
- finds no import offset.

That is serviceable, but not crisp enough for AER. The paper needs to say more explicitly:

1. **Existing gas-crisis papers ask how much output fell.**  
2. **This paper asks whether trade cushioned the fall.**  
3. **That margin is central because many policy discussions implicitly assume it.**  
4. **The answer appears to be no, at least for extra-EU imports in broad energy-intensive sectors.**

That is a clear wedge.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or filling a gap in a LITERATURE?

Mostly the world, which is good. The strongest version is: **Can open economies rely on imports to cushion the loss of domestic industrial capacity after a major energy shock?** That is a world question.

But the paper keeps slipping into literature-gap language (“this literature focuses on production; I extend it to the trade margin”). That is true but smaller. AER wants the world question first.

### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what’s new?

They could, but with caveats. They’d probably say:

> “It’s a paper on the European gas shock showing that production fell in exposed energy-intensive sectors and extra-EU imports didn’t rise enough to offset it.”

That is not bad. It is definitely better than “another DiD paper about X.” But it is still vulnerable to being summarized as “a triple-diff paper on EU trade after the gas crisis” unless the authors sharpen the broader conceptual claim.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Several possibilities:

1. **Bring the substitution margin closer to the economic question.**  
   The current outcome is extra-EU imports at very broad SITC categories. That is a long way from “did Europe replace what it stopped producing?” A much bigger paper would use more granular product-level trade and link it more tightly to exposed industries.

2. **Show where the missing adjustment went.**  
   The paper’s mechanism is demand destruction. That is plausible, but still inferred more than shown. A bigger contribution would document downstream contraction directly—e.g., in industries that use chemicals, metals, glass, or fertilizer intensively.

3. **Bring in intra-EU reallocation.**  
   This is the biggest scope issue. If the question is “did trade substitute for lost production?”, excluding intra-EU trade is a major self-inflicted narrowing. If the answer is “extra-EU imports did not rise,” the natural response is “maybe imports from within the EU did.” The paper admits this as a limitation, but strategically that limitation is too central to leave standing.

4. **Reframe from “trade failed” to “which margins adjusted?”**  
   An AER-level version might compare several margins: domestic production, extra-EU imports, intra-EU imports, prices, and downstream output. Then the paper could say something bigger about the anatomy of adjustment after a supply shock.

If they could enlarge only one margin, it should be **intra-EU trade plus downstream demand evidence**.

   - Bernard, Jensen, Redding, Schott
   - Eaton, Kortum, and Kramarz-type relationship and sourcing papers
   - Recent work on buyer-supplier matching and trade adjustment frictions

### How should the paper position itself relative to those neighbors?

Mostly **build on and connect**, not attack.

The paper currently wants to “challenge standard trade theory.” That is too combative relative to what the evidence actually shows. Better positioning:

- Build on the energy-crisis literature by adding the missing trade-adjustment margin.
- Build on trade-adjustment work by showing a case where domestic collapse does not symmetrically generate import replacement.
- Connect to production-network and sourcing-friction literatures to explain why.

That triangulation is much stronger than picking a fight with textbook models.

### Is the paper positioned too narrowly or too broadly?

At the moment, both.

- **Too narrowly** in data scope: extra-EU imports only, broad SITC bins, one episode.
- **Too broadly** in rhetoric: “fundamental limitation of standard trade models,” “challenge the view that energy shocks merely redirect production,” “re-industrialization is harder than standard models imply.”

The combination is awkward: narrow evidence, sweeping claims.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?

The big missing conversation is **production networks / input-output propagation / buyer-supplier relationships**. This is exactly the literature that would make the “demand destruction” mechanism feel like economics rather than a post hoc narrative.

Also underused:
- literature on **trade elasticities and adjustment frictions**
- literature on **firm-to-firm networks**
- literature on **intermediate-input trade and relationship-specific sourcing**
- literature on **shock propagation through supply chains during COVID**

This paper should be speaking not just to trade and energy economists, but also to macro/network economists.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?

Not quite. The current conversation is: “trade theory says imports should rise; I show they don’t.” That is catchy but a little crude.

The better conversation is:  
**What margins of adjustment operate after a large industrial supply shock in an open economy, and why might sourcing through trade be weaker than expected when production is embedded in local supply chains?**

That is the more intellectually serious framing, and also the more AER-like one.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Europe entered 2022 with large cross-country variation in dependence on Russian gas. When gas prices spiked and physical supply was curtailed, energy-intensive manufacturing in exposed countries was hit hard.

### Tension

The obvious benchmark is that open trade should cushion such a shock by shifting sourcing toward unaffected foreign producers. But it is not obvious whether that happened, especially in sectors organized around intermediate inputs and local supplier relationships.

### Resolution

Production in exposed energy-intensive sectors fell sharply, but extra-EU imports of the same broad categories did not differentially rise. The paper interprets this as evidence that the shock also reduced downstream demand and disrupted supply chains, limiting import substitution.

### Implications

Open economies may be less resilient to sectoral supply shocks than simple reallocation stories suggest. Industrial-energy shocks can generate net capacity loss rather than smooth trade-based substitution, with implications for energy security, industrial policy, and decarbonization.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

Yes, more than most empirical papers. There is a real setup–surprise–implication structure. That is a strength.

But the paper still feels somewhat like a collection of related results because the mechanism section outruns the evidence. The current story is:

1. production fell,
2. imports didn’t rise,
3. therefore demand was destroyed.

The first two are evidence; the third is interpretation. For a top journal, that gap has to be handled more carefully. If the mechanism cannot be directly shown, the narrative should be modest and structured as:
- result,
- plausible interpretation,
- alternative margins left for future work.

Right now the story overshoots by presenting demand destruction almost as established fact.

### What story should it be telling?

It should tell a story about **the missing adjustment margin**:

> In this episode, Europe lost domestic production in exposed sectors. The striking fact is not just the production decline, but the absence of compensating extra-EU imports. That absence suggests that import substitution after large industrial cost shocks is not automatic, especially when the affected goods are intermediate inputs embedded in production networks.

That story is strong and appropriately bounded.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?

I’d lead with:

> “When Europe’s gas-intensive factories shut down in 2022, imports did not surge in the exposed countries to replace what was lost.”

That is the dinner-party line.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

Economists would lean in—for about 30 seconds. The follow-up is immediate and predictable:

- “Do you mean extra-EU only or all imports?”
- “How broad are these product categories?”
- “Isn’t this just a demand decline rather than a failure of trade adjustment?”
- “Did production shift within Europe?”
- “How do you know imports of the specific missing goods didn’t rise?”

Those are not econometric nitpicks; they are strategic questions about the claim itself. The paper needs to preempt them if it wants to land as a big result.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Most likely: **“Did sourcing shift within the EU rather than outside it?”**

That is the central strategic vulnerability. If the answer is unknown, then the paper’s broad claims about “the limits of trade adjustment” are too ambitious. The paper may still support “the limits of extra-EU trade adjustment,” but that is a smaller claim.

### If findings are null or modest: is the null itself interesting?

Yes, in principle. This is one of those cases where a null can be quite interesting because the expected sign is positive and policy-relevant. The paper is not just saying “we didn’t find anything”; it is saying “the cushioning margin many people expected appears absent.”

But to make the null compelling, the paper has to persuade the reader that:
1. the expected substitution margin was economically meaningful,
2. the outcome measure is close enough to that margin,
3. the confidence interval rules out substantively important positive adjustment.

The current draft does some of this, but not enough. It needs more discipline around **what magnitude of import response would count as meaningful** and whether the estimates rule that out.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

### What is the gap between the current form and an AER paper?

Mostly a **scope and framing** problem, with some novelty risk.

- **Framing problem:** the paper overstates what its evidence proves. It should not frame itself as overturning trade theory; it should frame itself as documenting a striking failure of one adjustment margin in a consequential episode.
- **Scope problem:** the outcome is too narrow relative to the claim. Extra-EU imports are not the same as trade adjustment overall.
- **Novelty problem:** production effects from the gas shock are known; the paper’s novelty lives or dies on the trade margin and mechanism.
- **Ambition problem:** the paper has the seed of a strong idea, but the empirical implementation is somewhat safe and coarse relative to the breadth of the claims.

### Be honest: how far is it?

In current form, **medium-to-far** from AER. Not because it lacks an interesting fact, but because the paper’s evidence base is narrower than its conceptual ambition.

An AER-ready version would probably need one of two upgrades:

1. **Broaden the empirical scope** to cover the full adjustment system:
   - domestic production,
   - extra-EU and intra-EU imports,
   - possibly prices and downstream sectors.

or

2. **Narrow the claim and deepen the mechanism**:
   - keep extra-EU focus,
   - use finer product data,
   - show directly that downstream sectors contracted where upstream exposure was greatest.

Without one of those moves, the paper risks being seen as “interesting but incomplete.”

### Single most impactful piece of advice

**Either expand the outcome to total trade adjustment—including intra-EU reallocation—or sharply narrow the claim from “limits of trade adjustment” to “lack of extra-EU import substitution,” because the current claim-to-evidence ratio is the paper’s biggest vulnerability.**
