## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a clean and potentially important question: when governments put sharp increases in marginal vehicle tax rates at emissions thresholds, do car manufacturers manipulate certified CO\textsubscript{2} ratings to land just below those cutoffs? Using Dutch vehicle-tax kinks and German registrations as a placebo benchmark, the paper’s answer is no: unlike notches, kinks do not appear to induce visible bunching in manufacturers’ certified emissions.

A busy economist should care because the paper is really about a broader design question: which kinds of nonlinear policy schedules generate strategic distortion, and which do not? That is a first-order issue for tax design, regulation design, and the interpretation of bunching evidence more generally.

### Does the paper articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Reasonably well, but not optimally. The current introduction is competent and clear, yet it still reads a bit like “a bunching paper in vehicle taxation” rather than “a paper about when nonlinear schedules do and do not distort firm behavior.” The distinction matters a lot for AER positioning.

The first paragraph currently starts with a vivid example, which is good. But by the second paragraph the paper slips into literature taxonomy and method. For AER, the opening should more aggressively foreground the world question and the conceptual stake:

1. policymakers increasingly use threshold-based environmental taxes;
2. existing evidence has taught economists that thresholds induce gaming;
3. but that evidence comes mostly from notches or standards, not kinks;
4. this paper shows that the form of the schedule matters decisively.

### The pitch the paper should have

Governments around the world use emissions-based vehicle taxes with sharp threshold changes, and economists often worry that such thresholds invite strategic manipulation of certified emissions. But that concern is based largely on evidence from notches and discrete regulatory thresholds, where crossing the line creates a first-order penalty. This paper shows that for purchase-tax kinks—where the tax schedule is continuous and only the marginal rate jumps—manufacturers do not bunch below the threshold, implying that policy shape, not just policy stringency, governs whether firms game environmental rules.

That is the AER-version pitch. The current paper almost has it, but does not quite claim it confidently enough.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### Contribution in one sentence

The paper’s contribution is to show that large marginal tax-rate jumps in a continuous vehicle purchase-tax schedule do not generate the kind of manufacturer emissions bunching observed under notch-like taxes and regulatory thresholds, implying that kinks may be far less distortionary than notches in product markets.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest papers?

Partially, but not sharply enough.

The paper does distinguish itself from:
- **Sallee (2012)** on the U.S. gas guzzler tax,
- **Ito and Sallee (2018)** on Japanese fuel-economy regulation,
- the broader bunching literature around **Saez (2010)**, **Chetty et al. (2011)**, and **Kleven (2016)**.

But the differentiation is still too methodological and instrument-specific. It says, in effect, “those study notches/standards; I study kinks.” That is true, but it is not yet enough. The stronger differentiation is conceptual:

- prior work established that threshold policies can distort product design;
- this paper asks whether **all threshold policies** do so;
- the answer is no: **continuous schedules may be much less gameable than discontinuous ones**.

That is a much stronger and more general contribution.

### World question or literature gap?

Right now it is mixed, but too often framed as filling a literature gap. The stronger framing is about the world:

> When governments design nonlinear environmental taxes, does the shape of the schedule determine whether firms manipulate products?

That is a world question. It has policy relevance and transports beyond vehicles.

A weaker version is:

> There is little evidence on bunching at purchase-tax kinks.

That is a literature gap. It sounds niche.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?

Yes, but not in the strongest way. Right now they might say:
> “It’s a bunching paper on Dutch car taxes, and it finds no bunching at kinks.”

That is not enough for AER. You want them to say:
> “It shows that the distortionary effects economists document around threshold policies may depend fundamentally on whether the schedule has a notch or just a kink.”

That version travels.

### What would make the contribution bigger?

Most importantly: **reframe upward**, not sideways. This paper does not need ten more outcomes as much as it needs a more ambitious conceptual claim.

That said, several things could enlarge the contribution:

1. **Make the object of interest policy design, not Dutch BPM.**  
   The paper should be about the comparative economics of kinks versus notches in product markets.

2. **Lean harder into firm behavior rather than density tests.**  
   The contribution is about manufacturers not strategically re-optimizing product attributes under continuous schedules. Anything that makes that behavioral margin more concrete would help.

3. **Stronger cross-policy comparison.**  
   The paper repeatedly references notch settings in the literature, but mostly narratively. A more explicit comparison table or framework contrasting the incentives under notches, kinks, and fleet-average standards would make the paper feel bigger.

4. **Mechanism sharpening.**  
   The current mechanism is “engineering/calibration costs exceed the local marginal tax savings.” If the paper can elevate that into a simple conceptual model or organizing framework, the null becomes theory-backed rather than merely empirical.

5. **Connect to policy incidence/design.**  
   If this is really about when taxes distort product design versus just consumer choice, that is broader than cars.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Governments increasingly use nonlinear environmental taxes and standards. Prior evidence has shown that thresholds can induce bunching, gaming, and product redesign in vehicle markets and elsewhere. This has fostered a broad belief that emissions thresholds are inherently distortionary.

### Tension

But that belief may rest on evidence from a specific class of policies—discontinuous notches and hard regulatory cutoffs. Economic theory says kinks are different: they change marginal incentives but do not create a dominated region. So do firms still game them?

### Resolution

In the Dutch vehicle market, despite large marginal tax-rate jumps at several emissions thresholds, manufacturers do not appear to bunch just below the kinks. The one eye-catching cluster is explained by EU-wide fleet regulation and appears equally in Germany.

### Implications

The shape of a policy schedule matters. If governments want to avoid strategic product manipulation, continuous schedules may be safer than notch-like designs. More broadly, economists should be cautious about generalizing from evidence on notches to all threshold-based policies.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

Yes, but it is weaker than it should be because the resolution is currently delivered as a sequence of empirical non-findings rather than as the answer to a conceptual puzzle. The paper is not a random collection of results, but it still feels too much like “we checked four thresholds and found nothing.” For AER, the story needs to be tightened into one clean arc:

- economists learned that thresholds cause gaming;
- theory says the nature of the threshold matters;
- here is a clean real-world test of that distinction;
- the distinction matters a lot.

That is a stronger narrative than the current “multi-cutoff null with placebo.”

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

### What fact would I lead with?

I would lead with:

> Even when a vehicle tax makes one extra gram of certified CO\textsubscript{2} worth hundreds of euros to buyers, manufacturers do not bunch just below kink thresholds—suggesting that continuous tax schedules may avoid the product manipulation seen under notches.

That is the memorable fact.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

A mixed answer.

Top public finance / IO / environmental economists would lean in if the paper is framed as a statement about **policy shape and firm gaming**. If framed as a Dutch null bunching paper, many would reach for their phones.

That is the core editorial issue here: not “is the finding true?” but “is the finding presented as a generalizable fact economists care about?”

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Likely one of these:
- “So is the lesson that notches distort and kinks don’t?”
- “Is this specific to vehicle engineering costs, or a more general tax-design principle?”
- “If firms don’t manipulate, do consumers still sort?”
- “Does this mean policymakers can get environmental incentives without inviting gaming?”

Those are good questions. The paper should tee them up more directly.

### If the findings are null or modest, is the null itself interesting?

Yes, potentially very much so. But only if the authors sell it as **a theory-consistent, policy-relevant null**, not as “we didn’t find significance.”

The current paper does some of this well, especially around the kink-versus-notch distinction. But it still leans too heavily on the language of estimator instability and lack of detectable discontinuity. A more compelling null paper says:

- the domain is one where many readers would expect gaming;
- theory predicts the expectation may be wrong under continuous schedules;
- the data strongly align with that theory;
- therefore the null updates our beliefs about policy design.

That is the case to make.
