## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple and important policy-design question: when governments tax vehicle emissions with **kinks** in the tax schedule rather than **notches**, do manufacturers manipulate certified CO\(_2\) ratings to land just below the thresholds? Using Dutch vehicle purchase-tax kinks and German registrations as a placebo, the paper’s core claim is that they do not: the bunching patterns that loom large in other settings appear absent here, suggesting that **instrument design, not just tax level, governs strategic response**.

A busy economist should care because this is really a paper about the economics of nonlinear policy design. The substantive domain is cars and emissions, but the broader question is whether marginal incentives without discrete jumps are strong enough to induce gaming by firms.

### Does the paper itself articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Mostly yes, but not quite at the level needed for AER. The current introduction is competent and clear, but it still reads somewhat like a well-executed bunching application in a specific institutional setting. The paper’s strongest pitch is not “I test for bunching in Dutch car taxes”; it is: **a prominent concern in environmental taxation is strategic manipulation, but that concern may be overstated for kinked schedules because firms respond very differently to kinks than to notches**.

The first two paragraphs should say that more directly and more globally. They should start with the policy-design question, not with a Corolla and a euro amount. The Corolla example is fine as a hook, but it currently comes before the bigger intellectual stake.

### The pitch the paper should have

Governments increasingly use nonlinear taxes and standards to steer product characteristics, but whether firms strategically manipulate those characteristics depends critically on the shape of the policy schedule. Existing evidence of strong threshold responses in vehicles comes mostly from **notches** and discrete regulatory boundaries, leaving open whether the same gaming occurs under the far more common case of **kinks**, where only marginal incentives change. This paper studies four sharp kinks in the Dutch vehicle purchase tax and shows that, despite large jumps in marginal tax rates, manufacturers do not bunch certified CO\(_2\) ratings below the thresholds; the apparent exception is a plug-in hybrid cluster that is present in Germany too and appears driven by EU fleet rules rather than national taxation.  

That framing tells the reader immediately: this is a paper about the limits of strategic response to nonlinear policy, not just about Dutch tax administration.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### Contribution in one sentence

The paper’s contribution is to show that, in contrast to the bunching documented around vehicle-tax notches and regulatory thresholds, **CO\(_2\)-based purchase-tax kinks do not generate detectable manufacturer bunching**, implying that the distinction between kinks and notches is economically consequential in product-market regulation.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?

Partially, but not sharply enough. The paper names the right contrast class—Sallee on the gas guzzler tax, Ito on Japanese standards, Kleven on bunching theory—but the differentiation is still somewhat mechanical: “they study notches / thresholds, I study kinks.” That is true, but not yet enough. For a top-journal contribution, the paper needs to convert that distinction into a stronger claim about the world:

- The existing literature may have led readers to infer that vehicle regulations generally induce gaming at thresholds.
- This paper says that inference is too broad.
- Firms may game **discrete discontinuities**, but they may not materially game **continuous schedules with slope changes**.
- That matters for tax design, incidence, and regulatory architecture.

Right now, the paper is adjacent to that claim but not fully leaning into it.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or filling a gap in a LITERATURE?

It does both, but it still leans too much toward literature-gap language: “first multi-cutoff test,” “advances the bunching literature,” etc. The stronger frame is a world question:

- **Do firms manipulate product characteristics when policy creates only marginal, not discrete, incentives?**
- **Are policymakers wrong to fear gaming under kinked environmental taxes?**

That is much stronger than “there is no paper yet on Dutch BPM kinks.”

### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what’s new?

A smart economist could probably say: “It’s a bunching paper on Dutch vehicle-tax kinks, and it finds null effects.” That is not quite enough. The hoped-for reaction is: “Interesting—the paper argues that the strategic manipulation documented in cars is a notch phenomenon, not a general feature of emissions-based taxation.”

At present, it still risks being summarized as “another threshold-response paper, except with a null.”

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Most importantly, make the object of interest broader than density around thresholds. Specific ways:

1. **Push the design question harder.**  
   Reframe from “is there bunching at Dutch kinks?” to “when do nonlinear product taxes distort firm behavior?” The Dutch case is then evidence for a broader proposition.

2. **Show implications for policy architecture, not just one tax.**  
   A more systematic comparison of kinked versus notch-based regimes across countries—or even a structured conceptual comparison with known notch settings—would make the paper feel bigger.

3. **Bring in product-design margins beyond immediate bunching.**  
   If possible, the broader question is whether firms respond to kinked taxes by:
   - shifting model mix,
   - changing powertrain offerings,
   - altering prices or trim availability,
   - or re-allocating sales across countries.
   
   Even descriptive evidence on these margins could enlarge the paper. Right now it is narrowly about local density manipulation.

4. **Clarify mechanism in economic rather than econometric terms.**  
   The real mechanism claim is: discrete jumps create a dominated region; slope changes do not; engineering adjustment costs swamp small marginal gains. If that mechanism were foregrounded and maybe tied to a simple conceptual model, the contribution would feel less like a null result and more like a sharp economic lesson.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Governments use emissions-based taxes and standards to shift the vehicle market. Prior work has shown bunching and manipulation around regulatory thresholds, creating a concern that firms game these systems.

### Tension

But those prior examples mostly involve notches or discrete boundaries. It is not clear whether the same strategic response should appear under kinked schedules, where only marginal rates change and no dominated region exists.

### Resolution

In the Dutch BPM, even large jumps in marginal tax rates at four CO\(_2\) thresholds do not generate detectable bunching. The one visually suggestive cluster, around 79 g/km, is entirely explained by plug-in hybrids and appears in Germany as well, indicating an EU-wide compliance phenomenon rather than response to Dutch taxation.

### Implications

The choice between kinks and notches may be first-order for whether environmental taxes induce gaming. Policymakers may be able to use kinked schedules without triggering the same sort of manufacturer manipulation seen under notch-like policies.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has one, but it is somewhat buried under method and result-description. The paper is closer to “a collection of sensible empirical exercises supporting a null” than to “a sharp story with escalating stakes.”

The current story is:
- here is the tax schedule,
- here are tests,
- the tests are null,
- the polynomial estimates are unstable,
- the PHEV cluster is explained away.

A stronger story would be:
1. The literature has taught us to fear threshold gaming.
2. But theory says kinks are different.
3. Dutch data offer a clean test because there are four kinks with varying strength.
4. None produce bunching.
5. The only apparent threshold pattern is actually generated by a different policy regime.
6. Therefore the object that matters is not “thresholds” generically but the exact shape of nonlinear incentives.

That is a much more satisfying arc.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?

“I looked at a car tax schedule where crossing certain CO\(_2\) thresholds can save buyers hundreds of euros per gram, and manufacturers still do not bunch their certified emissions below those thresholds.”

That is the lead fact.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

Initially, they would lean in—because threshold manipulation in vehicles is a live and policy-relevant topic. But if the next sentence is just “McCrary tests are insignificant,” they will reach for their phones. If instead the next sentence is “the reason is that these are kinks, not notches, so the policy creates no dominated region,” they stay engaged.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Almost certainly:  
**“So does this mean kinks generally don’t induce gaming, or just that this particular tax was too small?”**

That is the central follow-up, and the paper needs to answer it more forcefully. Right now it gestures toward engineering costs and the absence of a dominated region, but it does not yet fully separate:
- a general design lesson about kinks versus notches,
from
- a local calibration result about Dutch tax magnitudes.

If the authors want the paper to travel, they need to address that distinction head-on.

### If the findings are null or modest: is the null itself interesting?

Yes, potentially quite interesting. But nulls are only interesting when they overturn a strong prior or discipline policy thinking. This one can do that because the prior in this area is “threshold-based environmental policy creates gaming.” The paper’s job is to sharpen that into: “that prior is too coarse; gaming depends on the geometry of incentives.”

Right now the null is interesting, but still somewhat presented as “we checked and found nothing.” It should instead be presented as “the absence of bunching is itself the economic result predicted by the logic of kinks.”

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

At present, the gap is mainly a mix of **framing problem** and **ambition problem**, with a secondary novelty issue.

### Framing problem

The paper has a potentially important idea but frames itself too much as an application of bunching methods to a Dutch tax schedule. AER wants the broader lesson: **which forms of nonlinear regulation trigger strategic firm response, and which do not?**

### Ambition problem

The paper is careful and sensible, but a bit safe. It demonstrates absence of bunching at four kinks. That is a solid field-journal contribution. For AER, it needs to make a larger claim about **policy design under strategic response**, ideally with broader comparative or conceptual reach.

### Novelty problem

The novelty is real but subtle. “Null bunching at kinks” is not automatically enough on its own. The paper must convince readers that prior work led the profession toward an overly general lesson about threshold gaming, and that this paper materially corrects that lesson.

### Scope problem

Moderate. The paper is very focused on one empirical manifestation—local bunching in certified emissions. That may be too narrow if the broader policy question is firm response to emissions-based taxes. If the authors can show that other margins also do not move, or at least discuss them clearly, the paper gets bigger.

### The single most impactful piece of advice

**Rewrite the paper around one claim: evidence of strategic threshold manipulation in vehicle markets is largely a notch phenomenon, not a general consequence of nonlinear emissions taxation.**

That one change would force improvements everywhere:
- a sharper introduction,
- better literature positioning,
- a stronger narrative,
- and a more AER-worthy “so what.”
