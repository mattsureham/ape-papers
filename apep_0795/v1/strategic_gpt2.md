## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether the Social Security Act’s exclusion of agricultural and domestic workers changed Black workers’ occupational choices in the late 1930s. Using linked census data, it argues that exclusion from old-age insurance pushed Black domestic workers disproportionately into covered jobs, suggesting that the racial design of the early welfare state shaped labor-market allocation, not just benefit receipt.

Why should a busy economist care? Because the paper tries to move the conversation from “the welfare state excluded Black workers” to “the welfare state changed where Black workers worked,” which is a materially bigger claim about how public policy can reorganize labor markets.

**Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Reasonably well, but not sharply enough. The current opening is historically vivid, but it still reads a bit like an economic history paper filling in a missing piece, rather than a paper answering a broad economics question. The big idea is not “did they escape?” The big idea is: **when social insurance is tied to occupation and exclusions are racially uneven, policy can induce occupational reallocation along racial lines.** That should be the first-order pitch.

### The pitch the paper should have

In 1935, Social Security did more than redistribute future benefits: by covering some occupations and excluding others, it changed the relative value of jobs. Because excluded occupations were disproportionately held by Black workers, the policy created a racially uneven incentive to leave agriculture and domestic service for covered employment.

This paper shows that this incentive mattered, but only where workers could act on it. Using linked 1920–1940 census records, I find that Black domestic workers—unlike agricultural workers—shifted into covered occupations substantially more than comparable white workers after the Act. The broader implication is that the racial architecture of the welfare state shaped labor-market sorting and mobility, not merely access to transfers.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:**  
The paper’s contribution is to show that Social Security’s occupational exclusions induced race-differentiated occupational sorting—especially among Black domestic workers—revealing a labor-market channel through which the early U.S. welfare state affected Black economic mobility.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest papers?
Partially, but not fully. The paper distinguishes itself from the political-history literature on Social Security exclusions and from the Great Migration literature, but the differentiation is still a little mechanical: “they studied politics; I study behavior.” That is true, but for AER the differentiation needs to be conceptual, not bibliographic.

The sharper differentiation is:

- Existing work shows **who was excluded** and **why**.
- This paper asks whether exclusion **changed behavior ex ante**, before benefits were ever paid.
- That turns Social Security from a passive distributive institution into an active determinant of occupational allocation.

That is a meaningful distinction, and the paper should hammer it.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or filling a gap in a LITERATURE?
At present, it toggles between both, but too often slips into “no one has measured this before.” That is a literature-gap framing. The stronger framing is world-facing:

- When benefits are occupation-linked, excluded workers may re-sort into covered jobs.
- When exclusion is racially uneven, that re-sorting may alter racial patterns of mobility.
- The 1935 SSA is a clean historical case of this general phenomenon.

That is much better than “the literature hasn’t measured behavioral response.”

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?
They could, but not crisply enough. Right now the risk is they say: “It’s a linked-census DiD showing Black domestic workers moved into covered jobs more after Social Security.” That is accurate but sounds narrower and more design-driven than it should.

You want them to say:  
**“It shows that racialized social insurance design changed occupational allocation, and that the effect depended on workers’ ability to access alternative labor markets.”**

That version sounds like a contribution to economic history, labor, public economics, and political economy all at once.

### What would make this contribution bigger?
Most importantly: **reframe the estimand as the effect of occupation-linked social insurance on labor-market allocation under unequal mobility constraints**, rather than as a one-off historical estimate.

More specifically, the contribution would feel bigger with one or more of the following:

1. **A stronger mechanism/outcome hierarchy.**  
   Manufacturing entry is potentially a more memorable and economically meaningful endpoint than generic “switching to covered occupation.” If the paper can say “exclusion pushed Black domestic workers into manufacturing,” that is a bigger sentence than “switching rates rose.”

2. **A clearer connection to mobility constraints.**  
   The domestic/agriculture split is the paper’s best idea. It should be elevated from heterogeneity to the central mechanism: policy incentives matter when access frictions are low.

3. **A stronger framing around labor allocation, not pension awareness.**  
   The current discussion sometimes leans on whether workers understood pension rules. That is not the strongest framing. The bigger point is revealed behavior in response to a policy-created covered/uncovered margin, regardless of whether workers computed actuarial values precisely.

4. **A more explicit bridge to modern public economics.**  
   The historical result matters because many modern programs are tied to employers, sectors, or formal employment. The paper should make clear that this is an early example of benefit-linked labor-market sorting under unequal access.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
The U.S. welfare state was built with occupational exclusions that disproportionately left Black workers uncovered. Most scholarship has treated this as a distributional fact and a political compromise.

### Tension
If benefits are tied to occupation, exclusion may do more than deny transfers: it may change where people work. But whether that happens depends on whether excluded workers can actually move into covered jobs. So there is a tension between incentive and constraint.

### Resolution
The paper finds evidence of differential movement into covered occupations after the SSA, concentrated among Black domestic workers and absent among agricultural workers.

### Implications
The implication is that the racial design of social insurance affected labor-market sorting and socioeconomic mobility, but only where access frictions were low. More broadly, policy design and labor-market constraints jointly determine who can respond to incentives.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
Yes, but it is not yet fully disciplined. The paper has the ingredients of a strong narrative, but occasionally drifts into “collection of plausible results” mode:

- main DiD
- occupation heterogeneity
- SEI
- manufacturing
- placebo
- migration
- region split

The **real story** is not “many related outcomes point in the same direction.” The story is:

1. Social Security created a covered/uncovered occupational margin.
2. That margin was racially uneven.
3. Workers responded where switching costs were low.
4. Therefore policy-induced occupational sorting is the key channel.

That means the domestic/agriculture contrast is not a side result. It is the narrative core. The paper should be structured around **incentives x constraints**, not around a generic main table followed by heterogeneity.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?
I would lead with:  
**After Social Security excluded domestic and agricultural workers, Black domestic workers moved into covered occupations much more than white domestic workers did, while Black agricultural workers showed no comparable response.**

That is the memorable fact because it contains the mechanism in the result.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
Economists would lean in—if the paper is presented as a broad claim about policy design and labor allocation. If presented as “a historical DiD about 1930s occupations,” some will drift. The underlying finding is strong enough; the current packaging undersells it.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Probably one of these:

- “Why domestic workers and not agricultural workers?”
- “Is this really Social Security, or just Depression-era labor-market change?”
- “Did they move into manufacturing?”
- “What does this imply for modern occupation- or sector-linked benefit systems?”

The good news is the paper already has material for most of these. The bad news is it does not always foreground the most interesting follow-up.

### If findings are modest or null
The aggregate effect is modest, and the paper is smart not to oversell it. The domestic-worker effect is the true result. The paper should stop pretending the overall excluded-worker estimate is the centerpiece; it is not. The null for agricultural workers is genuinely interesting because it sharpens the mechanism: incentives alone do not move workers when access barriers are high.

This is not a failed experiment. It becomes one only if the paper insists that the average effect across all excluded workers is the headline.
