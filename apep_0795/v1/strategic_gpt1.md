## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether the Social Security Act’s exclusion of agricultural and domestic workers changed Black workers’ occupational choices in the late 1930s. Using linked census records, it argues that exclusion from old-age insurance pushed Black domestic workers—but not Black agricultural workers—into covered occupations, suggesting that the racial design of the early welfare state shaped labor-market mobility, not just benefit receipt.

Why should a busy economist care? Because the paper tries to move the Social Security exclusion story from “who was denied benefits?” to “how did policy design change behavior and economic trajectories?” That is potentially a much bigger and more AER-relevant question.

### Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Mostly, but not optimally. The current opening is good historical scene-setting, but it undersells the broader economics question. It starts as a paper about a historical exclusion and only gradually reveals that the real contribution is about policy-induced occupational sorting under unequal constraints. The introduction is strongest when it sounds like “welfare-state design changes labor allocation”; it is weaker when it sounds like “no one has measured this particular response before.”

### What the first two paragraphs should say instead

Something like:

> The Social Security Act created one of the largest new social insurance programs in U.S. history, but access depended on occupation. Because agricultural and domestic workers were excluded—and because Black workers were disproportionately concentrated in those jobs—the Act changed the returns to occupational mobility in sharply unequal ways across race.
>
> This paper asks a simple question with broader implications for labor economics and political economy: when social insurance is tied to occupation, do excluded workers re-sort into covered jobs, and are those responses shaped by mobility constraints? Using linked census data across 1920, 1930, and 1940, I show that Black domestic workers moved into covered occupations substantially more than comparable white workers after the Act, while agricultural workers did not. The result suggests that the racial architecture of the early welfare state affected not only who received benefits, but also the allocation of labor across occupations.

That version makes the world-question and the economics stakes much clearer.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### Contribution in one sentence

The paper’s contribution is to show that the occupational exclusions built into the 1935 Social Security Act appear to have induced differential occupational sorting—especially among Black domestic workers—revealing a behavioral margin through which discriminatory social insurance design affected economic mobility.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?

Only partially. The paper distinguishes itself from the political-history literature by emphasizing behavioral consequences rather than legislative origins, and from Black mobility history by pointing to a specific institutional mechanism. That is promising. But the introduction still reads a bit like “existing literature studies politics; I study behavior,” which is a thin differentiation unless the paper is explicit about what economists should update.

It needs sharper contrasts such as:
- prior work shows exclusion in program design;
- this paper shows exclusion changed labor allocation;
- prior work treats exclusion as a static distributional fact;
- this paper argues it also had dynamic equilibrium consequences, but only where switching costs were low.

That last clause—heterogeneous response by mobility frictions—is the part that elevates the paper above a straightforward historical DiD.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or filling a gap in a LITERATURE?

It oscillates between the two. The stronger framing is world-facing: **how occupation-linked social insurance changes workers’ sorting behavior under racial barriers**. The weaker framing is literature-facing: **no study has yet measured this behavioral response**. AER wants the former.

### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain what’s new?

Yes, but not cleanly enough. Right now they might say: “It’s a historical DiD on Social Security exclusion and Black occupational mobility.” That is not enough. You want them to say: “It shows that discriminatory welfare-state design altered occupational sorting, and that the response depended on switching costs—domestic workers moved, agricultural workers didn’t.”

That is a much more memorable sentence.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Most importantly: **lean harder into the general lesson about occupation-linked benefits and constrained mobility.** Specifically:

1. **Reframe the heterogeneity as the core result, not a side result.**  
   The aggregate effect is modest; the domestic/agricultural contrast is the paper’s real intellectual payload.

2. **Push the destination side more.**  
   “Moved to covered jobs” is somewhat abstract. If the paper can more vividly show what jobs they moved into—manufacturing, clerical, transport, retail—and connect those to long-run mobility, the contribution gets larger.

3. **Tie more explicitly to the economics of tied benefits.**  
   The health-insurance/job-lock analogy is good but currently underdeveloped. The paper should make clear that this is a historical case of benefit-induced labor reallocation under unequal access.

4. **If possible, foreground implications for racial inequality dynamics rather than only occupational switching.**  
   Occupational upgrading is a start. The bigger contribution is if the paper can persuade readers this changed the path of Black economic advancement, not merely occupational labels.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

The U.S. created a major social insurance program in 1935, but eligibility depended on occupation. Because Black workers were concentrated in excluded jobs, the program created racially unequal incentives.

### Tension

Exclusion is usually studied as a static denial of benefits or as political compromise. But if benefits are occupation-linked, exclusion may also induce workers to move—except that some workers face much higher switching costs than others. So did the policy actually reallocate labor, and for whom?

### Resolution

Using linked census data, the paper finds a sizable differential shift into covered occupations among Black domestic workers after the Act, little similar response among agricultural workers, and some evidence of occupational upgrading and entry into manufacturing.

### Implications

Discriminatory welfare-state design can shape labor-market behavior, not just transfer incidence. More generally, policy-linked benefits affect occupational sorting only where workers have meaningful access to alternatives.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the ingredients, but the arc is not yet fully disciplined. The paper currently has one very strong story hidden inside a broader and weaker one.

- **Weak story:** Social Security exclusion increased Black switching from excluded to covered occupations.
- **Strong story:** Social insurance tied to occupation induces sorting, but only when workers can realistically move; thus discriminatory policy interacts with mobility constraints to shape who can escape exclusion.

The latter is far better. The current draft sometimes treats the domestic/agriculture split as “heterogeneity” or “mechanism,” but in fact it is the central narrative resolution. The aggregate result is mild and fragile in the paper’s own presentation. The reader should be led quickly to understand that the main point is not an average effect across all excluded jobs.

So yes, the paper has a serviceable arc, but it needs to be rewritten around the domestic-worker contrast as the main story.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?

I would lead with this:

> When Social Security excluded domestic and agricultural workers, Black domestic workers were much more likely than white domestic workers to move into covered occupations—but Black agricultural workers were not.

That is the most interesting fact because it contains both the policy effect and the mobility-frictions insight.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

Some would lean in—especially economic historians, labor economists, and political economists—because the Social Security exclusion is well known, but the idea that it changed occupational sorting is not obvious. But many will reach for their phones if the pitch is merely “another historical difference-in-differences on Black-white occupational mobility.”

The dinner-party success depends almost entirely on framing. “The racial welfare state changed labor allocation” is interesting. “I estimate a switching-rate DiD using linked censuses” is not.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Probably: **Why domestic workers and not farm workers?**  
And then: **Is this really about access to outside options rather than just general racial labor-market trends in the 1930s?**

That is good news strategically, because the domestic/agriculture contrast is exactly where the paper has its strongest narrative leverage.

### If the findings are modest: is the modestness itself interesting?

The aggregate result is modest and not especially stirring on its own. The paper is wise not to build everything around it. The null for agricultural workers is genuinely interesting, but only because it clarifies the mechanism: policy incentives mattered only where escape was feasible.

So the paper is not a “failed experiment,” but it must resist overselling the pooled effect. The meaningful contribution lies in the patterned heterogeneity.
