## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple, potentially important question: did the arrival of the first generation of cellular service in the United States spur local economic development? Using the FCC’s late-1980s cellular license rollout across rural markets, the paper argues that early cellular service had essentially no measurable effect on county employment, establishments, or payroll, suggesting that voice-only mobile telephony was not economically transformative in a country already well served by landlines.

Why should a busy economist care? Because the paper speaks to a larger question than “cell phones matter”: **when does communications infrastructure generate real economic spillovers, and when is it just a convenience good?** That is an AER-relevant question if framed correctly.

### Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Not quite. The current opening is competent, but it starts with institutional detail about the lottery before establishing the larger economic question. The paper’s true hook is not “the FCC used a lottery”; it is: **modern work finds large effects of digital connectivity, but the first mobile revolution may have had no aggregate economic effect at all.** That contrast should be front and center immediately.

Right now the introduction gets to that point, but too slowly. The first two paragraphs should more clearly say:

1. Communications technologies are often assumed to drive growth.
2. But not all communications technologies are equally consequential.
3. This paper studies the first US cellular rollout and finds that early mobile voice service did not move local economies.
4. Therefore, the economic payoff to telecom infrastructure depends on capability and baseline context, not mere deployment.

### The pitch the paper should have

Here is the pitch the paper should be giving:

> Economists now have strong evidence that broadband and digital connectivity can transform local economies. But did the first wave of mobile telephony do the same? This paper studies the introduction of analog cellular service in late-1980s rural America and finds essentially no effect on county employment, establishments, or payroll. The result suggests a broader lesson: communications infrastructure generates large economic spillovers only when it meaningfully expands what firms and workers can do, not when it merely makes existing communication more portable.

That is the AER version of the paper. The current version is closer to “interesting historical natural experiment with null effects.”

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper’s contribution is to show that the initial rollout of US analog cellular service appears to have had little to no aggregate local economic impact, implying that the economic returns to telecom infrastructure depend critically on technological capability and preexisting communications access.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3-4 papers?

Only partially. The paper differentiates itself from broadband and developing-country mobile papers, but the differentiation is still a bit mechanical: “they study broadband; I study early cellular.” That is not enough. The author needs to sharpen the conceptual distinction:

- **Broadband papers** study technologies that expand information processing, market access, and organizational capability.
- **This paper** studies a technology that improved convenience for a narrow set of users but may not have changed production possibilities at scale.

That is a genuine contrast. But the paper needs to say more explicitly: **this is not just an earlier version of broadband—it is a different kind of technology with different expected equilibrium effects.**

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or as filling a gap in a LITERATURE?

Mostly the world, which is good. The strongest version is: **When does communications infrastructure matter for economic development?** The weaker version is: “No one has evaluated the cellular lottery before.” The paper currently oscillates between them. It should lean much harder into the world question.

### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what’s new?

Right now, maybe, but not crisply. They would likely say: “It’s a DiD paper using the FCC cellular lottery to show no county-level effects.” That is not enough.

You want them to say:  
**“It shows that early mobile telephony, unlike later broadband, did not generate local economic growth—so the economic effects of telecom depend on whether the technology expands capabilities versus just portability.”**

That is much better.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Several possibilities:

1. **A stronger outcome framing.**  
   Aggregate county employment/payroll is sensible, but the conceptual prediction may be more about coordination, market integration, business travel, emergency response, or firm organization than broad county growth. If the paper can show “no aggregate effects, and no detectable effects even in outcomes where portable communication should matter most,” that becomes more persuasive and more interesting.

2. **A sharper mechanism comparison.**  
   The paper hints at a threshold model: telecom matters when it enables new activity, not when it substitutes for existing landlines. That mechanism could be the paper’s real contribution. To make it bigger, the paper should organize evidence around that theory rather than treating it as after-the-fact interpretation.

3. **More heterogeneity by baseline need or likely usage.**  
   Not a robustness exercise, but a conceptual one: where should early cellular have mattered most? Remote counties? Places with weak landline alternatives? Business-intensive or travel-intensive local economies? If the answer is still “nowhere obvious,” the null becomes much more informative.

4. **A broader comparative framing.**  
   The paper could explicitly position itself as explaining why some telecom revolutions matter economically and others do not. That is a larger contribution than a historical case study.

Mostly **build on and qualify**, not attack.

- Relative to telecom-growth cross-country work: “The sign is not enough; the effect depends on technological content and baseline conditions.”
- Relative to spectrum-allocation work: “I repurpose a familiar institutional episode to study a different downstream question.”

The paper should not pretend to overturn the broader literature. It should present itself as identifying an important boundary condition.

### Is the paper currently positioned too narrowly or too broadly?

A bit too narrowly in implementation, and slightly too broadly in aspiration.

Too narrow because much of the text reads like a historical quasi-experiment paper about one idiosyncratic FCC rollout. Too broad because the intro gestures at “telecommunications infrastructure and growth” without fully earning that generality. The right balance is: **a focused historical case that identifies a broader economic principle about when connectivity matters.**

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?

It could speak more to:

- The literature on **general-purpose technologies** and why some technologies diffuse with large productivity effects while others do not.
- The literature on **technology adoption under complementary investments**.
- Work on **infrastructure complementarities**: technologies matter when paired with human capital, organizational change, software/data capability, etc.
- Potentially the literature on **transport/communication substitutes and complements**—portable voice communication may primarily reduce transaction costs for a small subset of users, not create economy-wide scale effects.

Even just bringing in these ideas conceptually would help. The paper does not need a giant literature review, but it needs a bigger intellectual home.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?

Not fully yet. It is currently in conversation with “telecom infrastructure causes development” papers. That is fine, but the stronger conversation is:

**What separates transformative infrastructure from marginal infrastructure?**

That is the more interesting audience and the more AER-relevant framing.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Economists increasingly believe that communications infrastructure can generate large economic gains. Later broadband and mobile internet technologies appear to have done so in many settings.

### Tension

But the first mobile rollout in the US was very different: voice-only, expensive, low-penetration, and introduced in a country with widespread landlines. So should we expect major local economic gains from “cellular service” per se, or only from richer communications technologies in contexts of greater scarcity?

### Resolution

This paper finds no meaningful county-level effects of early cellular service on employment, establishments, or payroll.

### Implications

The economic impact of communications technology is conditional. Infrastructure is not valuable simply because it is new or mobile; it matters when it expands functional capability or relaxes a major existing constraint.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the ingredients, but the arc is underpowered. Right now the paper reads more like:

1. Here is a quirky institutional setting.
2. Here is a design.
3. Here are null results.
4. Here is some discussion about why they may be null.

That is serviceable, but not memorable.

The stronger story is:

1. A broad claim in economics is that connectivity boosts growth.
2. But this should not be true for all communication technologies.
3. Early cellular is the ideal test of that boundary: mobile but not digital, novel but elite, new but not transformative.
4. The paper finds no local growth effects.
5. Therefore, what matters is not “telecom access” in the abstract but the interaction of technology capability and baseline frictions.

That is a real story. The paper should tell that story more deliberately.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?

I would say:

**“The first rollout of cellular service in rural America appears to have had basically zero effect on local employment or business activity.”**

That is the lead.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

Some would lean in—especially if the next sentence comes quickly:

**“So the big economic gains from connectivity seem to come not from mobile voice service itself, but from later technologies that actually expand what firms and workers can do.”**

Without that second sentence, many would reach for their phones. A null result about 1980s licensing timing is not enough by itself. The paper needs the conceptual punchline immediately.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Probably one of these:

- “Why didn’t it matter—was take-up too low?”
- “So is the lesson about technology quality, diffusion, or baseline landline saturation?”
- “Did it matter in remote places or communication-intensive sectors?”
- “Is this a useful boundary case for the broadband literature?”

Those are good follow-up questions. The paper should be structured to answer them.

### Is the null result itself interesting?

Yes, but only if the paper insists on why it is informative rather than accidental.

The null is interesting because this is **not** “job training program #47 with no effect.” It is a first-order technology question: a major communications innovation was privately valuable and commercially important, yet apparently not locally transformative in aggregate. That is an interesting distinction.

But the paper has to make the case that this is learning, not just non-finding. The current draft comes close, but it still occasionally feels like “we ran the design and got zeros.” The paper should instead say: **the zeros are theoretically revealing because they identify the boundary between convenience-enhancing and production-transforming communication technologies.**

Primarily a **framing and ambition problem**, with some **scope** concerns.

### Be honest: how far is it from AER?

In current form, this feels more like a solid field-journal or strong second-tier general-interest paper than an AER paper. The reason is not that the question is uninteresting. Ideally both, but if only one is possible, I would prioritize the first.

**Reframe the paper around a big economic claim—“communications infrastructure matters only when it expands capabilities rather than merely portability”—and organize the introduction, results, and discussion around proving that claim, not around the FCC lottery itself.**
