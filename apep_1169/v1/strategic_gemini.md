**SUBJECT:** Strategic Assessment of "Click to Incorporate"

---

## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
This paper asks whether reducing the administrative "hassle" of starting a company—specifically through one-stop online portals—actually increases the number of new firms. Leveraging the staggered adoption of these portals across 11 US states, it finds a precise zero: making registration easier doesn't move the needle on entrepreneurship in an advanced economy like the US.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this reasonably well, but the first two paragraphs are a bit "textbook." They focus on the history of the "Doing Business" narrative. To be an AER paper, the pitch needs to lead with the **theoretical stakes.** 

*The pitch the paper should have:* 
"Does the 'administrative state' actually suppress the animal spirits of American entrepreneurs? While a massive literature suggests registration costs are a first-order barrier in developing nations, we show that in the US, the 'friction' of multi-agency filing is economically irrelevant. We rule out even modest 5% increases in firm formation, suggesting that the binding constraints on US entrepreneurship are fundamental—risk and capital—not clerical."

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**Contribution:** The paper provides the first precise, causal null result demonstrating that administrative "one-stop" simplification is not a binding constraint on firm formation in a modern, low-friction institutional environment.

- **Differentiation:** It is well-differentiated from the "positive" results in Mexico (Bruhn, 2011) and Portugal (Branstetter et al., 2014) by switching the context to the US "intensive margin."
- **World vs. Literature:** It currently leans toward "filling a gap" in the US context. To get into the AER, it needs to be framed as a question about **The Limits of Institutional Reform.** 
- **The "Smart Economist" Test:** A reader would likely say "it's a precise null on a popular policy."
- **Bigger Contribution:** To make this "big," the author needs to explore **heterogeneity by industry.** Do portals matter for "low-margin" service businesses where time costs are a higher % of capital, vs. high-growth tech? If it's a null everywhere, the story of "universal irrelevance" is actually stronger.

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
- **Closest Neighbors:** Djankov et al. (2002) on entry costs; Bruhn (2011) on Mexico; Hurst and Pugsley (2011) on small business motives.
- **Positioning:** It should position itself as the **"Skeptical Maturity"** phase of the entry-regulation literature. The 2000s were about "Look, these costs matter!" This paper should be: "We've reached the point of diminishing returns; further simplification is now just a transfer to people who would have started businesses anyway."
- **Unaware of:** The paper could benefit from connecting to the **"Sludge" literature** (Thaler/Sunstein) regarding administrative burdens. Is this a case where "removing sludge" fails because the sludge wasn't thick enough to be a barrier?

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC
- **Setup:** Governments are obsessed with "one-stop shops" to fix entrepreneurship.
- **Tension:** This policy is based on evidence from countries where it took 60 days to register; does it work when it only took 2 days?
- **Resolution:** No. The effect is a precise zero.
- **Implications:** Policy should stop chasing "efficiency" as a growth lever and return to "capital/risk" as the levers.

**Evaluation:** The narrative is clear, but the "Implications" section needs more teeth. It feels a bit like a policy brief. It needs to speak to the **misallocation of government effort.**

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
- **Lead Fact:** "US states spent millions on one-stop portals, and it didn't create a single extra business."
- **Dinner Party Reaction:** People will lean in because everyone hates bureaucracy, but they will be surprised that *removing* it doesn't help. 
- **Follow-up:** "Does it at least save money for the businesses?" (The author should try to quantify the *time saved* even if it didn't induce new firms).

---

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
- **Front-loading:** The paper is well-structured.
- **Robustness:** Move the Goodman-Bacon and Sun-Abraham discussion to the main text more aggressively. In a "null result" paper, the reader's first instinct is "the DiD is broken." Proving the DiD is robust *is* the paper.
- **Conclusion:** The conclusion is a bit repetitive. It should focus more on the "Friction Fallacy" mentioned on page 12.

---

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The gap is **Ambition.** Right now, it's a very clean, professional evaluation of a specific policy. For the AER, it needs to be an evaluation of an **Idea.**

**The single most impactful piece of advice:** 
Stop framing this as an evaluation of "Portals" and start framing it as a test of the **"Marginal Entrepreneur."** Who is the person who *almost* starts a business? This paper proves that the person who is "stopped by a few extra forms" doesn't exist in the US. If you can show that even in "high-friction" US sectors (like those requiring complex licensing) the portals still don't work, you've killed a major policy paradigm.
