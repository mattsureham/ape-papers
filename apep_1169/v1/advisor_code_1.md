It uses the staggered timing of 11 state-level one-stop business registration portals and the Census Business Formation Statistics via FRED, applies a Callaway–Sant’Anna estimator, and focuses on the causal impact on BA, HBA, and WBA. **Summary**  
This paper exploits the staggered rollout of integrated one-stop business registration portals across 11 US states to estimate the effect on new firm applications using the Business Formation Statistics panel (51 jurisdictions, 12,189 state-months). Employing a Callaway–Sant’Anna DiD design and augmenting it with robustness checks (TWFE, Sun–Abraham, leave-one-out, Goodman–Bacon), it finds a precise null—effects on total, wage-planned, high-propensity, and corporate applications are statistically indistinguishable from zero. The interpretation is that administrative friction was not binding in the U.S. context, even though similar reforms produced positive effects in higher-friction developing-country environments.

**Essential Points**  
1. **Credibility of the Common Trends Assumption given Partial Portal Definitions.** The paper argues that timing of portal adoption was driven by administrative modernization initiatives, yet the definition of “one-stop portal” is not standardized and is potentially noisy (Appendix A notes ambiguity between partial and full portals). If the treatment indicator opens earlier when a portal is “partial” or later when bureaucratic coordination is “complete,” the treatment variable may not capture the sharp administrative change, attenuating the estimated ATT. Please document more concretely how the portal launch dates correspond to substantial reductions in registration steps and provide sensitivity checks to alternative treatment timing (e.g., earliest mention of portal, full integration date, or policy milestones tied to measurable friction reductions). Without this, the credible null may be driven by mis-timing rather than a true lack of effect.

2. **Treatment Selection and Time-Varying Confounders.** Even if adoption is largely administrative, states may fast-track portals in response to surges or declines in entrepreneurial activity, post-recession e-government pushes, or broader fiscal capacity (budget surpluses versus deficits). The event-study pre-trends help, but the paper relies solely on never-treated states as controls in the CS estimator, implicitly assuming there are no staggered anticipatory effects or co-occurring state-level reforms (e.g., tax policy, economic development funds) that coincide with portal launches. A stronger case requires either richer covariate adjustment (state-by-month controls for economic activity) or placebo treatments (e.g., randomly assigned pseudo-launch dates) to confirm the null is not due to dynamic confounding. Please elaborate on any simultaneous policy changes in treated states and, if possible, control for them or demonstrate their absence.

3. **Heterogeneous Responses and Magnitude Interpretation.** The paper concludes that portals do not affect applications at the extensive margin, but entrepreneurs differ by sector, firm type, capitalization, and motivation. The analysis stops at the aggregated state-month level, leaving open whether a subset of firms (e.g., high-propensity employer firms, corporate filings, rural counties, counties far from the capital) responded positively while others did not. Since the manifest highlighted mechanisms like corporate versus sole proprietorship formation and distance-to-capital interactions, it is crucial to explore heterogeneity to ensure the null is not masking offsetting effects. Please provide heterogeneity checks (e.g., split sample by state size, rural share, or portal scope; county-level effects if feasible) or justify why the aggregate analysis suffices for the research question.

**Suggestions**  
1. **Treatment Definition and Institutional Detail:** To strengthen the credibility of the treatment variable, consider assembling a matrix that describes each portal’s scope (Secretary of State only, includes labor/Revenue, includes licensing) and whether the launch date represents a technical rollout or full functionality. This would allow for event studies that align with substantive reductions in friction. If some portals merely added a unified login without reducing agency counts, bracket the analysis to the subset of states where the service meets a predefined integration threshold. Alternatively, treat portal scope as a continuous variable (number of agencies integrated) to assess whether deeper integration has larger effects.

2. **County-Level and Firm-Type Mechanisms:** The manifest mentioned county-level BFS data and a mechanism comparing capital-intensive (corporate) versus queue-sensitive (sole proprietorship) filings. Use the available county BFS to estimate whether counties further from the state capital—or with lower office capacity—react differently. This will test the notion that portals reduce physical location costs. Similarly, examine the corporate (CBA) series more deeply—perhaps by interacting treatment with county industry composition or pre-treatment employer density—to see if formal employer entries react differently from other applications. Even if these checks also yield nulls, reporting them will substantiate the conclusion about administrative friction’s relevance.

3. **Placebo and Anticipation Tests:** Perform placebo tests by assigning “fake” treatment dates to never-treated states or to treated states in pre-treatment periods and re-estimate the CS ATT. This will confirm that the estimation strategy is not picking up spurious trends or regression-to-the-mean effects. Additionally, examine whether application counts begin to move in the months immediately preceding the announced launch (anticipatory behavior) or show delayed reactions (perhaps due to slow adoption). Event-study graphs with cohort-specific leads and lags would be helpful to rule out pre- or post-adoption dynamics that the aggregate ATT might obscure.

4. **Weighting and Economic Significance:** The paper emphasizes precise null effects but could better contextualize economic significance. For instance, translate the confidence intervals into absolute application counts for a median state or mention what a 5 percent increase implies in terms of new hires or payroll. The “standardized effect sizes” table could be moved into the main text and expanded to discuss whether the null is economically meaningful and what sizes of effects are ruled out (e.g., could portals still explain a 1 percent increase?). This would help policymakers interpret whether the null is truly decisive or simply a small imprecise effect.

5. **Discussion of Alternative Frictions:** The discussion suggests capital and demand constraints bind more than paperwork. To reinforce this claim, consider presenting auxiliary evidence from other data sources (e.g., SBA lending series, VC investment, unemployment benefits) showing that treatments coincide with non-registration-related trends. Alternatively, mention whether treated states implemented any complementary policies (e.g., entrepreneurial training) that might have confounded the treatment effect. If such policies are absent, that strengthens the argument that the portal was the primary administrative change.

6. **Robustness to Spillovers and Partial Adoption:** Since entrepreneurs can incorporate across state lines (e.g., Delaware incorporations), consider whether residents from zero-portal states might incorporate elsewhere due to cross-border opportunities, diluting the within-state effect. A robustness check could exclude states with high incorporation inflows (e.g., Delaware). Additionally, note whether treated states might still require in-person steps, so adoption may simply rebrand existing processes; discussing survey or administrative data on startup time before and after adoption would reinforce the argument that portals genuinely reduced friction.

Overall, the paper addresses an interesting policy question with solid data and the appropriate modern estimator. Additional attention to treatment definition, heterogeneity, and placebo robustness would make the null result more convincing and policy-relevant.
