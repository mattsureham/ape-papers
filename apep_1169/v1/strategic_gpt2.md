## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple policy question: when U.S. states make business registration much easier through one-stop online portals, do more people start businesses? Using staggered adoption across states, the paper finds essentially no effect on business applications, suggesting that in an advanced economy with already-moderate entry procedures, administrative registration friction is not a major constraint on entrepreneurship.

A busy economist should care because this speaks to a large and influential idea in development, public economics, and entrepreneurship: that reducing regulatory/administrative burdens is a first-order lever for stimulating entry. If the answer in the U.S. is “not really,” that is potentially important—but only if the paper makes clear that it is testing a broader claim about what actually constrains entrepreneurship, not just evaluating a piece of e-government plumbing.

### Does the paper articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Reasonably well, but not sharply enough. The introduction currently starts from “government modernization” and “one-stop portals,” then moves to the literature. That is competent, but it undersells the stakes. The real pitch is not “we study a portal reform”; it is “we test whether administrative simplification actually creates entrepreneurs in a setting where the common policy rhetoric says it should.”

The first two paragraphs should lead with the broader proposition under test and the fact that the paper delivers a policy-relevant null that meaningfully contrasts with the canonical developing-country evidence.

### The pitch the paper should have

For decades, economists and policymakers have treated business registration costs as a key barrier to entrepreneurship. But almost all of the influential evidence comes from settings where formal entry was extremely cumbersome; we know much less about whether simplifying already-functional registration systems increases firm formation in advanced economies.

This paper studies staggered adoption of one-stop online business registration portals across U.S. states and finds a precise null: simplifying registration does not increase business applications, including applications most likely to become employer firms. The broader implication is that once baseline entry procedures are already relatively light, further administrative streamlining may improve convenience without expanding entrepreneurship.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper’s contribution is to provide evidence from U.S. state portal adoptions that reducing business registration hassle in an advanced-economy setting does not measurably increase new firm formation.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?

Only partly. The paper does differentiate itself from Bruhn/Mexico and Branstetter/Portugal on institutional context, but the differentiation is still mostly “same question, different setting.” That is not enough for AER unless the setting difference is turned into a substantive claim: namely, that the effect of entry simplification is highly non-linear and depends on the baseline level of administrative burden.

Right now the paper says, in effect, “there is evidence from developing countries; here is the U.S.” What it needs to say is: “the canonical claim that registration simplification stimulates entrepreneurship is not portable across institutional contexts; it works when entry barriers are genuinely onerous, but not when reforms merely reduce residual hassle.” That is a bigger contribution.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or filling a gap in a LITERATURE?

Mostly about the world, which is good. The paper’s strongest formulation is: are administrative frictions actually binding for entrepreneurship in the contemporary U.S.? That is better than “first paper to study one-stop portals in the U.S.” But it still slips too often into “there is thin evidence in advanced economies.” The world question is stronger and should dominate.

### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain what’s new?

They could say: “It’s a staggered-adoption paper showing U.S. business registration portals didn’t increase business formation.” That is understandable, but still sounds like “another DiD paper about state policy.”

What they would not yet say is the stronger version: “It shows that administrative simplification only matters when frictions are truly large, so the standard entry-cost narrative has limited external validity in advanced economies.” That is the version worth aiming for.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Most importantly: move from “do portals matter?” to “when does entry simplification matter?” The paper hints at a threshold/nonlinearity story but does not own it.

Specific ways to make it bigger:
- **Exploit variation in reform intensity.** Not all portals are equally meaningful. A binary treatment on “has portal” makes the intervention look thin. A measure of scope/integration/completeness could let the paper say whether deeper integration matters while shallow integration does not.
- **Bring outcomes closer to real employer formation.** BFS applications are a useful early margin, but the paper would be more consequential if it could tie reforms to realized employer births, payroll startups, or early survival. The current contribution risks being heard as “portals don’t affect applications,” which is narrower than “portals don’t affect entrepreneurship.”
- **Frame as heterogeneity by baseline burden.** If the paper can compare states with higher pre-reform complexity or more fragmented systems to those already streamlined, the broader lesson becomes sharper.
- **Strengthen mechanism evidence.** Right now the mechanisms are plausible but generic. The paper needs at least one mechanism angle that is more than informed speculation if it wants to change beliefs.

- Papers on whether regulation or finance is the key barrier to entry.

### How should it position itself relative to those neighbors?

Mostly **build on and qualify**, not attack. The paper should not pretend Bruhn or Branstetter were wrong. It should say their findings are real in high-friction environments, but their policy lesson is context-dependent. That is a constructive and stronger position than “our null contradicts theirs.”

Relative to Djankov-style cross-country claims, it can be somewhat more aggressive: broad claims about entry costs as a general determinant of entrepreneurship likely overstate how much incremental procedural simplification matters once baseline institutions work tolerably well.

### Is the paper positioned too narrowly or too broadly?

At present, a bit **too narrowly in design and too broadly in rhetoric**.

Too narrowly because “one-stop registration portals” is a very specific administrative reform category that risks sounding niche.

Too broadly because the paper sometimes reaches for sweeping claims like “simplifying business registration does not generate new businesses,” which is stronger than the actual evidence. The evidence is about a specific class of U.S. digital administrative reforms, not about all business registration simplification everywhere.

The right position is in between: a paper about the limits of administrative simplification as an entrepreneurship policy tool in low-friction settings.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of, or under-engaged with?

It is somewhat under-engaged with:
- **Business dynamism / startup decline** literature.
- **State capacity / digital government** literature.
- **Formalization vs. true entry** literature: many registration reforms shift formalization or timing rather than underlying economic activity.
- **Entrepreneurship constraints in developed economies**: finance, insurance, labor market risk, and demand uncertainty.

It should also speak more explicitly to the literature on **policy external validity**: why a reform that works in one institutional environment may not travel.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?

Not quite yet. It is currently having the conversation “do these portals work?” The more impactful conversation is “what kinds of frictions actually constrain entrepreneurship in advanced economies, and when should we expect administrative simplification to matter?” That conversation is larger, more AER-relevant, and less vulnerable to the “interesting state policy, but niche” reaction.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

The standard view is that reducing entry costs should increase entrepreneurship. Influential work and policy practice have reinforced the idea that registration procedures are a key determinant of business creation.

### Tension

But the strongest evidence comes from settings with extremely high baseline barriers. In the modern U.S., where registration was already fairly manageable, it is unclear whether consolidating procedures into a one-stop portal removes a genuinely binding friction or merely makes an existing process more convenient.

### Resolution

The paper finds no detectable increase in business applications after portal adoption, including for application types most closely tied to employer businesses.

### Implications

The implication is that administrative simplification may have sharply diminishing returns: it matters when baseline barriers are severe, but not when reforms just shave time and hassle off an already workable process. Policy aimed at increasing entrepreneurship in the U.S. likely needs to target capital, demand, or risk rather than paperwork.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

Yes, but it is not as disciplined as it should be. The paper has the ingredients of a strong arc, but it also reads somewhat like a competent empirical paper arranged around estimator choice and robustness, with a story layered on top. The narrative weakens whenever the prose becomes “here is the ATT, here is the TWFE, here is the Goodman-Bacon decomposition.” That is necessary somewhere, but it should not drive the reader’s understanding of why the paper matters.

### What story should it be telling?

The story should be:
1. Economists and policymakers often talk as if reducing administrative barriers mechanically creates entrepreneurs.
2. That proposition was established mainly in high-friction environments.
3. The U.S. provides a test of whether the same mechanism operates when the reform reduces hassle but not truly prohibitive barriers.
4. It does not.
5. Therefore, the effect of entry reform depends on whether registration is actually the binding margin.

That is a clean story. The current draft partially tells it, but not forcefully enough.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?

“I’d lead with: eleven U.S. states built one-stop business registration portals, and business formation didn’t budge.”

That is a decent hook. Even better: “The classic entry-cost story seems not to travel: in the U.S., making business registration one-click easier doesn’t create more businesses.”

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

Initially, they would lean in, because the topic touches a canonical claim in entrepreneurship and regulation. But the follow-up determines whether they stay engaged. If the presenter quickly moves to “and here are four application series and two estimators,” phones come out. If instead the presenter says “this suggests administrative simplification only matters above a threshold level of friction,” they stay with it.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Almost certainly:
- “Are these portals too minor a reform to test the broader hypothesis?”
or
- “Does this affect actual employer firms, or just application counts?”
or
- “Maybe it changes composition, speed, or compliance rather than total entry?”

Those are exactly the questions the paper needs to anticipate in its framing, even before referees weigh in on the details.

### If the findings are null or modest, is the null itself interesting?

Potentially yes. This is not a random null. It tests a widely held policy intuition and matters because policymakers routinely invest in these reforms while implying they will boost entrepreneurship.

But the paper needs to work harder to establish that this is a **meaningful null**, not just a failed hunt for significance. It does some of that with power language and comparisons to Mexico/Portugal, which is good. Still, the null only becomes truly interesting if the paper is explicit that the reform is a test of the marginal value of administrative simplification in a low-friction environment.

Right now it is close, but not fully there.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

At the moment, this looks more like a solid field-journal paper than an AER paper.

### What is the gap?

Primarily a **framing problem**, secondarily a **scope/ambition problem**.

- **Framing problem:** The paper is better than its current self-presentation. It is not really about portals; it is about the external validity and limits of the entry-cost view of entrepreneurship. That broader claim is present but not centered.
- **Scope problem:** The treatment is narrow and the outcomes are somewhat proximal. To make the paper feel big, the author needs either richer outcomes, richer treatment variation, or a more compelling conceptual synthesis.
- **Novelty problem:** In its current form, some readers will think, “Okay, another policy simplification paper with a null.” The author has to prevent that reaction by making the paper about when and where administrative simplification matters.
- **Ambition problem:** The paper is competent but safe. It does not yet fully exploit the opportunity to say something broader about entrepreneurship policy design in advanced economies.

### Single most impactful advice

Reframe the paper around the boundary conditions of entry-cost reforms—i.e., **not whether one-stop portals work, but when administrative simplification can plausibly increase entrepreneurship, and why the answer in low-friction U.S. settings is no**.

That single shift would improve the title, introduction, discussion, and literature positioning all at once.
