# Review: Taxing the Transition: Do EV Registration Fees Deter Electric Vehicle Adoption?

## 1. Idea Fidelity
The paper adheres closely to the core research question and identification strategy outlined in the manifest, specifically the use of staggered adoption and the Callaway-Sant'Anna (CS) estimator. However, there are three notable deviations from the proposed design. First, the manifest specifies "State-year EV registration counts," which typically implies a flow (new registrations), whereas the paper utilizes cumulative stock data. This shifts the economic interpretation significantly. Second, the manifest explicitly proposed a "Wild cluster bootstrap" for inference given the limited number of clusters (N=51); the paper relies on conventional clustered standard errors. Third, the manifest suggested exploiting fee magnitude heterogeneity via "continuous treatment DiD," but the paper implements the dose-response analysis using a naive TWFE specification rather than a heterogeneity-robust continuous estimator. While these do not invalidate the project, they represent a downgrade in econometric rigor relative to the original proposal.

## 2. Summary
This paper provides the first causal evidence on the demand-side effects of state-level electric vehicle (EV) registration fees. Using staggered difference-in-differences across 50 U.S. states (2016–2023), the author estimates that adopting a registration fee reduces the battery electric vehicle (BEV) stock by approximately 11 percent, though the estimate is statistically imprecise ($p=0.14$). The results suggest a policy tension: fees designed to recoup lost gas tax revenue may inadvertently slow the green transition, with welfare costs potentially offsetting fiscal gains.

## 3. Essential Points
The authors must address three critical issues before the results can be considered credible for policy guidance.

1.  **Economic Magnitude and Elasticity Plausibility:** The point estimate implies an 11% reduction in the BEV *stock* due to a median fee of \$120/year. Given that the average EV purchase price exceeds \$45,000, a \$120 annual fee represents roughly 0.27% of the vehicle cost per year. For this to generate an 11% reduction in the total stock, the implied elasticity of demand is extraordinarily high, or the effect on *new* purchases (flow) must be massive (e.g., >50% reduction sustained over years). This magnitude strains credulity compared to existing literature on fuel prices and subsidies (e.g., Bushnell et al. 2022). The authors must reconcile this magnitude or consider if omitted variables (e.g., state-specific anti-EV sentiment driving both fees and slower adoption) are biasing the result.
2.  **The "Always-Treated" Identification Problem:** The data panel begins in 2016, but policy adoption began in 2012 (Washington). States treated before 2016 have no pre-treatment observations in this sample. In the CS framework, these units cannot contribute to the estimation of pre-trends and may bias the counterfactual if their growth trajectories differed systematically from later adopters. Including them effectively treats them as cross-sectional variation rather than panel identification. The authors should exclude pre-2016 adopters to ensure a valid parallel trends assumption can be tested for all included units.
3.  **Contradictory Dose-Response Specification:** The main result suggests fees deter adoption, yet the dose-response specification (Table 2, Column 4) yields a positive (though insignificant) coefficient. This contradiction undermines the mechanism. Furthermore, using TWFE for the continuous treatment specification reintroduces the bias the paper critiques in the binary case. The dose-response must be estimated using a robust continuous DiD estimator (e.g., Callaway-Sant'Anna with continuous treatment) to be consistent with the main identification strategy.

## 4. Suggestions
The following recommendations are intended to strengthen the econometric validity and economic interpretation of the paper. While not strictly fatal, addressing them will significantly improve the quality of the contribution.

** Econometric Refinements and Inference **
Given the limited number of clusters (G=50), conventional clustered standard errors may lead to over-rejection (Type I error) or unreliable confidence intervals. As originally proposed in the manifest, you should implement a wild cluster bootstrap (e.g., Webb 2014 or MacKinnon-Webb) to construct confidence intervals. This is standard practice in state-level DiD literature with small G. Report the bootstrap p-values alongside the conventional ones. Additionally, regarding the "always-treated" states (WA, CO, NC), I recommend running a restricted sample excluding states treated prior to 2016. This ensures every treated unit has at least one pre-period to establish parallel trends. If the coefficient remains stable, your identification is much more robust. If it changes, the early adopters were driving the result, which would suggest selection bias.

** Data and Outcome Construction **
The use of cumulative stock rather than new registrations (flow) complicates the economic interpretation. A registration fee affects the marginal purchase decision, not the decision of existing owners to deregister (unless they move out of state). Therefore, the effect on stock is a distributed lag of the effect on flow. An 11% drop in stock implies a much larger drop in flow. If possible, obtain the "new registration" flow data from Experian or R.L. Polk (often available via AFDC or state DMV aggregates). If flow data is unavailable, you should explicitly model the accumulation process. For example, acknowledge that $\Delta \ln(Stock) \approx \text{Flow}/Stock$. Since the stock is growing exponentially, a constant negative effect on flow might look like a diminishing effect on stock growth rates. Consider using the growth rate of registrations ($\Delta \ln(Stock)$) as the outcome rather than the level, which aligns better with the flow nature of the policy shock.

** Improving the Dose-Response Analysis **
The current dose-response test uses TWFE, which you correctly identify as biased in the binary case. You should apply the same rigor here. Use the `csdid` package (or equivalent) support for continuous treatments to estimate the effect of fee magnitude (\$50–\$250) on adoption. If the continuous estimator is too noisy, consider binning the fees (e.g., Low vs. High fee adopters) and estimating separate ATTs for each cohort. This would clarify whether the null result in Table 2 is due to specification error or genuine lack of heterogeneity. Additionally, interact the fee treatment with state gas prices. The mechanism suggests fees should matter more in states with high gas taxes (where the fee replaces more revenue) or low gas prices (where the relative cost of EV ownership is higher). This heterogeneity test would bolster the mechanism argument beyond the PHEV placebo.

** Economic Interpretation and Welfare **
The welfare calculation is a strong component but needs refinement regarding the marginal vehicle. You calculate the cost based on the average EV driver. However, the policy affects the *marginal* adopter—the consumer indifferent between EV and ICE. This consumer likely drives fewer miles or has higher discount rates than the average EV owner. Recalculating the welfare loss using the marginal vehicle's expected mileage would provide a more accurate lower bound on the environmental cost. Furthermore, discuss the salience of the fee. Registration fees are paid annually, whereas gas taxes are paid per gallon. Behavioral literature suggests annual lump-sum fees may be more salient than per-unit taxes. If you can cite evidence on tax salience (e.g., Chetty et al. 2009), it would help explain why a small monetary amount generates a large behavioral response.

** Writing and Clarity **
Finally, clarify the distinction between stock and flow in the Introduction and Data sections. Currently, the abstract says "BEV stock," but the manifest said "registration counts." Be precise: "We observe the cumulative stock of registered vehicles, which serves as a proxy for adoption capacity." Also, in the Results section, explicitly discuss the TWFE vs. CS divergence. You note it illustrates bias, but elaborate on *why* the bias is positive (TWFE shows 0%, CS shows -11%). Usually, TWFE biases towards zero when treatment effects are heterogeneous and dynamic. Explain if this aligns with the theoretical expectation that early adopters (who might have higher growth trends) are used as controls for later adopters in TWFE.

By tightening the identification window, correcting the dose-response specification, and refining the economic magnitude argument, this paper has the potential to be a definitive reference on the unintended consequences of EV fiscal policy. The core question is timely and important; ensuring the econometrics match the ambition of the question is the final step.
