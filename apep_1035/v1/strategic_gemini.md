## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
This paper evaluates whether small financial "nudges"—discounts on marriage licenses—induce couples to attend premarital counseling and ultimately reduce divorce rates. Using a staggered difference-in-differences design across ten US states, the author finds a precisely estimated null effect: a $30 incentive is insufficient to shift marital stability or formation. It is a study on the boundary conditions of behavioral interventions for high-stakes life decisions.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this reasonably well by the third paragraph, but the first two paragraphs are a bit "standard." It starts with broad social costs of divorce, which feels like a textbook opening. 
**The Pitch It Should Have:** "Can a $30 coupon save a marriage? Over the last two decades, ten US states have bet that small financial incentives for premarital counseling can curb divorce rates. This paper provides the first causal evaluation of these policies, finding that while the logic of 'nudges' succeeds in low-stakes consumer domains, it fails to move the needle on high-stakes domestic outcomes, ruling out even modest 10% improvements in marital stability."

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**The Contribution:** The paper provides the first rigorous causal evidence that de minimis financial incentives for premarital education fail to reduce state-level divorce rates.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It differentiates itself well from the "psychology" literature (Clyde et al.) by using modern econometrics (Callaway-Sant’Anna) to correct biased TWFE estimates.
*   **World vs. Literature:** It currently sits between the two. It frames itself as "filling a gap" (first evaluation of these laws) but leans into a "World" question in the conclusion regarding the limits of nudges.
*   **The "Smart Economist" Test:** A reader would likely say, "It’s a clean DiD paper that shows small marriage nudges don't work." It risks being seen as a "policy evaluation of a minor policy."
*   **Making it Bigger:** To make this an AER-level contribution, the author needs to lean harder into the *behavioral* mechanism. Why does the nudge fail? Is it a take-up problem (the incentive is too small to change behavior) or a treatment problem (the counseling itself is ineffective)? The paper touches on this in the "Intent-to-treat" section, but elevating this from a policy post-mortem to a fundamental statement on the **limits of nudge theory in the "Economics of Identity"** would broaden its appeal.

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
*   **Neighbors:** Stevenson & Wolfers (2006) on divorce laws; Buckles et al. (2011) on marriage requirements; Thaler & Sunstein (2008) / Beshears et al. (2013) on nudges.
*   **Strategy:** The paper builds on the Family Economics literature but "attacks" the Behavioral Nudge literature by providing a boundary case. 
*   **Narrow vs. Broad:** It is currently a bit narrow (Family Econ). It should speak more to the "Personnel Economics" or "Contract Theory" of the household.
*   **Missing Conversations:** It should connect to the literature on **"Sunk Cost Fallacy" or "Selection into Marriage."** Do these discounts attract the "wrong" couples? Does a discount signal something about the marriage itself? 

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC
*   **Setup:** Divorce is costly; states want to fix it cheaply.
*   **Tension:** We use nudges for 401(k)s and organ donation—why not for the most important contract of a person's life?
*   **Resolution:** The nudge is too small; the "market" for marital stability is too deep for a $30 subsidy to impact.
*   **Implications:** Government "micro-nudges" have a ceiling. If you want to change family structure, you need structural interventions (income, education), not coupons.

**Evaluation:** The arc is clear but lacks "drama." To be AER-worthy, the tension needs to be higher—the author should frame the "Marriage Movement" of the 90s as a major policy shift that this paper finally deconstructs.

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
*   **The Lead Fact:** "States spent 20 years giving out marriage license coupons, and it didn't prevent a single divorce."
*   **Reaction:** Moderate "leaning in." It’s an "obvious in hindsight" result, which is the danger. 
*   **Follow-up:** "Was it because the counseling was bad, or because no one actually took the discount?" The author needs to answer this more definitively to keep the conversation going.

---

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
*   **Front-loading:** The "Why the null?" section (Section 6) is the most intellectually interesting part. Some of that intuition should be pulled into the Introduction.
*   **Appendix:** The detailed state-by-state legislative history (Section 2) could be slimmed down; the reader cares more about the aggregate failure.
*   **Robustness:** The Rambachan-Roth tests are great—keep those prominent to defend the null.

---

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The gap is **Ambition.** Right now, it’s a very competent *Journal of Human Resources* or *Journal of Policy Analysis and Management* paper. 

**Single Most Impactful Advice:** Shift the framing from "evaluating a niche state policy" to **"testing the price elasticity of high-stakes domestic decisions."** If the author can show (perhaps using the ACS data more aggressively) that even in populations where $30-60 matters most (low-income couples), the effect is still zero, it becomes a powerful statement on why "Nudge" policy has failed the American family.
