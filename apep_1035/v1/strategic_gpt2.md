## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether small state financial incentives for premarital counseling—typically a \$20–\$60 discount on a marriage license—reduce divorce. Using staggered adoption across U.S. states, it finds essentially no effect on either divorce or marriage rates, suggesting that token incentives do not meaningfully change high-stakes family behavior.

A busy economist should care only if the paper is framed as a broader test of when low-cost behavioral incentives can move major life outcomes. As currently written, the paper is about a fairly obscure policy lever in family policy; the broader “limits of nudges in consequential domains” angle is present, but it feels bolted on rather than central.

### Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Not quite. The opening is too generic: “divorce is important,” “social costs are large,” “policymakers have searched for interventions.” That is all true, but it is not a sharp AER opening. The paper’s actual hook is more interesting: states tried to prevent divorce with tiny ex ante incentives attached to marriage licensing, and this creates a test of whether behavioral micro-incentives can affect one of the most consequential household decisions.

The introduction should lead with the policy and the broader conceptual question, not with a stock paragraph on why divorce matters.

### The pitch the paper should have

Over the last two decades, ten U.S. states tried to reduce divorce using an unusually light-touch policy: couples who complete premarital counseling receive a small discount on their marriage license. This paper studies whether such micro-incentives can change one of the highest-stakes household outcomes and finds that they do not: state adoption of these policies has essentially no effect on divorce or marriage rates.

The broader lesson is that interventions that can shift low-stakes administrative choices may fail completely when applied to identity-laden, infrequent, high-cost decisions like marriage. If that is the paper’s claim, the paper belongs in a conversation larger than premarital counseling.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### Contribution in one sentence

The paper provides evidence that modest state incentives for premarital counseling do not reduce divorce, positioning this as a test of the limits of behavioral micro-incentives in family decision-making.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?

Only partially. The paper differentiates itself from one direct predecessor in psychology and from the TWFE/staggered-DiD methodological issue. But that is not enough for AER positioning. “No economics paper has estimated this causally” is weak as a contribution statement; it defines novelty by absence, not by importance.

The paper needs to distinguish itself against at least two broader literatures:
1. family law/family policy papers on marriage and divorce,
2. behavioral/public economics papers on the effectiveness and limits of nudges,
3. perhaps program-evaluation evidence on relationship education.

Right now, the differentiation is too methodological and too niche-policy-specific.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or filling a gap in a LITERATURE?

Mostly the latter, which weakens it. The paper repeatedly says variants of “no economics paper has studied this” and “I use modern staggered DiD.” That is a literature gap plus method update. The stronger world question is:

**Can small price incentives induce relationship investments substantial enough to change marital outcomes?**

That is a real-world question with first-order conceptual relevance.

### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain what’s new?

They could say: “It’s a DiD paper showing that marriage-license discounts for premarital counseling don’t affect divorce.” That is intelligible, but it still sounds like “another policy evaluation with a null.”

The introduction does not yet equip the reader to say: “This is evidence that nudges fail in high-stakes family choices, even when targeted at a margin policymakers hoped was cheap and scalable.” That is the version one wants.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Several possibilities:

- **A different framing:** Move from “first economics paper on premarital education incentives” to “test of whether small incentives can generate durable changes in household relationship capital.”
- **A different outcome set:** If possible, show effects on counseling take-up, waiting periods, timing of marriage, or compositional selection into marriage. Right now the outcome space is narrow: divorce and marriage rates. Those are important but blunt.
- **A stronger mechanism angle:** Separate “policy failed because take-up barely changed” from “policy changed take-up but counseling itself had little effect.” Without this, the paper’s broader lesson is underdeveloped.
- **A sharper comparison:** Compare these micro-incentives to more forceful marriage-related policies or barriers—e.g., waiting periods, covenant marriage, no-fault divorce, blood test requirements—to illuminate which policy margins do and do not move family behavior.
- **A broader behavioral claim:** Place the result alongside evidence from retirement, health, education, and administrative hassles to ask when small incentives matter and when they don’t.

The single biggest way to make the contribution larger is to stop treating this as a niche family-policy paper and instead make it a paper about the limits of low-intensity policy for high-intensity life decisions.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

States want to reduce divorce and experimented with cheap ex ante interventions: premarital counseling incentives embedded in marriage licensing. Existing evidence on counseling efficacy is mostly non-economics, often small-scale, and not about real-world policy at scale.

### Tension

The policy is conceptually plausible but economically ambiguous. Marriage is a major life decision, and a tiny financial incentive may be too small to alter counseling take-up or marital behavior. Yet if even low-cost nudges can improve marital stability, the policy would be attractive and scalable.

### Resolution

They do not appear to. Across staggered state adoptions, the paper finds essentially no effect on divorce or marriage rates, with estimates precise enough to rule out moderate population impacts.

### Implications

Cheap, low-salience incentives tied to marriage licensing are not an effective way to alter marital outcomes. More broadly, the result suggests limits to micro-incentives in settings where behavior is infrequent, identity-relevant, and determined by deeper household forces.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

A serviceable one, but not a strong one. Right now it reads more like:
- policy background,
- estimation setup,
- null result,
- robustness,
- some speculative discussion.

It is not quite a collection of unrelated results, but neither is it a compelling narrative with real tension. The tension should be sharpened around the mismatch between **policy intensity** and **behavioral object**. The paper should more explicitly ask:

> Can governments cheaply buy relationship investment at the license counter, and if so, does that investment translate into durable household outcomes?

That is a cleaner story than “here is the first causal estimate of this policy.”

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

### What fact would I lead with?

“Ten states tried to reduce divorce by offering couples about \$20–\$60 off their marriage license if they completed premarital counseling, and it had basically zero effect on divorce.”

That is a decent dinner-party fact. It is concrete and faintly surprising.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

Initially, they might lean in because the policy is unusual and vivid. But they will quickly reach for their phones if the paper cannot answer the obvious next question:

**Was the policy ineffective because almost nobody changed behavior, or because counseling itself doesn’t work?**

That question is central. The paper acknowledges it as ITT vs TOT, but as written it does not fully turn that limitation into insight.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Most likely one of these:
- “Did the subsidy actually increase counseling take-up?”
- “Is \$30 just too small, or is premarital education ineffective more generally?”
- “Do waiting-period waivers matter more than fee discounts?”
- “Were there effects for higher-risk groups, younger couples, or religious states?”

Those are exactly the questions the paper should anticipate more strategically.

### If the findings are null or modest: is the null itself interesting?

Potentially yes, but only under the right framing. A null is interesting if it teaches us that:
1. policymakers chose a margin that looked scalable but was behaviorally irrelevant,
2. cheap interventions cannot move durable outcomes in this domain,
3. implementation at scale differs sharply from efficacy in controlled trials.

At present, the paper partly makes that case, but not forcefully enough. There is still some “failed experiment” energy. To escape that, the paper must more persuasively argue that **learning the limits of low-cost family policy is itself economically important**.
