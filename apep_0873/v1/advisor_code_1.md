**Idea Fidelity**

The submitted paper departs fundamentally from the original manifest. The manifest proposed exploiting quasi-random assignment of appeals to ALJs with varying allowance rates to instrument for SSDI/SSI enrollment and to trace the downstream causal effect on opioid prescribing and mortality. The manuscript, by contrast, works entirely at the state-year level, regressing observed disability prevalence (ACS) on drug-specific mortality with state and year fixed effects and a “difference-in-drugs” placebo. It never constructs or employs the ALJ leniency IV, nor does it link SSA adjudication data to ARCOS, CDC, or Part D prescribing as envisioned. Consequently, the purported causal identification via exogenous ALJ leniency is absent, and the research design only partially addresses (and, in my view, fails to credibly answer) the intended question about insurance-mediated opioid access. This represents a major divergence from the original idea.

**Summary**

The paper investigates whether state-level disability prevalence causally affects opioid overdose mortality through the insurance-mediated prescription channel. Using an OLS panel with state and year fixed effects on CDC provisional death counts (2015–2022), the authors find that the raw cross-sectional correlation reverses sign once fixed effects are added, and that disability prevalence predicts lower mortality across prescription and illicit substances alike. They interpret this as evidence that common economic shocks—not insurance access—drive the disabillity-opioid correlation, particularly in the fentanyl era.

**Essential Points**

1. **Identification is not causal.** The paper claims to test the “pill pipeline,” yet it relies solely on time-series variation in ACS disability prevalence without an instrument or credible exogenous shock. Disability prevalence is endogenous to economic shocks, labor-market conditions, policy changes, and health trends that meanwhile affect overdose mortality. State fixed effects and year dummies cannot fully purge this endogeneity, especially if states experience non-linear or contemporaneous shocks. The manifest’s ALJ leniency IV strategy is absent, so the claim of causality remains unsupported. The authors need to implement the prescribed instrumental-variable strategy or provide another convincing source of exogenous variation in disability receipt.

2. **State-level disability prevalence is too coarse and temporally smoothed to capture the treatment.** ACS 5-year estimates are rolling averages that respond sluggishly to changes, so the independent variable may contain substantial measurement error and misalignment with the hypothesized mechanism (the marginal disability award). Without exploiting within-office variation (e.g., ALJs or recent SSDI awards), the analysis cannot distinguish between permanent state characteristics and true changes in enrollment. This measurement issue undermines both the magnitude and sign of the estimated coefficients.

3. **Placebo design misinterprets common trends.** The “difference-in-drugs” placebo assumes illicit-drug deaths should be unaffected by insurance access, but disability prevalence could still co-move with illicit markets through demand-side despair or policing changes. Finding similar coefficients across substances simply restates the omitted-variable concern rather than isolating the mechanism. This does not validate the causal null; rather, it highlights that omitted confounders (e.g., economic distress) drive both disability and mortality. Without exogenous variation, the uniform response is expected and uninformative.

Given these fundamental limitations, the paper should be rejected unless the authors implement a credible identification strategy consistent with the manifest (e.g., ALJ leniency IV) or otherwise address the endogeneity issues with comparably strong quasi-experimental variation.

**Suggestions**

1. **Implement the ALJ leniency IV or a comparable exogenous source of variation.** Returning to the original idea would substantially strengthen the paper. Aggregate SSA hearing office data can be linked to county (or state) opioid outcomes via geographic crosswalks, and within-office variation in leniency (leave-one-out allowance rates) provides plausibly exogenous shocks to disability awards. First, construct office-by-year leniency measures as in Maestas et al. (2013) and Gelber et al. (2024). Second, instrument for changes in SSDI/SSI enrollment (or local Medicare/Medicaid coverage) using the generated leniency, and estimate the effect on opioid prescriptions (ARCOS, Part D) and deaths (CDC). This approach would directly test whether receiving disability benefits induces greater prescription opioid access and mortality.

2. **Align timing with prescription coverage.** The manifest emphasizes the 24-month Medicare waiting period for SSDI versus immediate Medicaid for SSI as a falsifiable timing test. If the causal pathway is insurance-mediated prescribing, one should see a lagged effect for SSDI but not SSI. Consider constructing separate instruments or proxies for SSDI and SSI awards and checking whether opioid outcomes respond after the expected delay. Similarly, examine whether Medicare/Medicaid enrollment rates (from CMS or SSA data) change predictably with ALJ leniency and mediate the effect.

3. **Leverage more granular data.** The state-year panel obscures the local variation that is the focus of the manifest. County-level ARCOS data and CDC mortality data exist and, when paired with county-level mappings of hearing offices or claimants’ residences, can provide a far better match to the treatment. Quarterly or annual county estimates also allow for event-study dynamics to track the timing of adjudication decisions and subsequent opioid outcomes. A more granular panel would also permit controlling for county-level economic shocks (e.g., plant closures) that might confound disability prevalence.

4. **Clarify mechanism and address selection.** The manuscript currently interprets the negative within-state coefficient as evidence against the insurance channel. However, if disability prevalence increases because of an epidemic itself (which also affects mortality), fixed effects might induce negative bias when the treatment and outcome are trending downward simultaneously. The authors should consider structural modeling or control function approaches that explicitly account for selection into disability. For example, estimating a system of equations where disability prevalence is a function of lagged economic indicators and mortality responds with its own dynamics could help distinguish between reverse causality, omitted shocks, and the proposed mechanism.

5. **Improve placebo design rigor.** Rather than simply showing uniform coefficients across drug types, consider testing whether the insurance-mediated prescription channel is specifically associated with changes in Medicare/Medicaid opioid prescribing. If data permit, regress Medicare Part D or Medicaid opioid prescription rates on your instrumented disability coverage, and compare this to the effect on illicit-drug mortality. Another approach is to exploit variation in policy changes that differentially affected prescription versus illicit markets (e.g., PDMP implementation, opioid litigation settlements) and interact these with disability prevalence to see if the relationship is stronger where prescriptions were more easily accessible.

6. **Enhance robustness and interpretation.** The current tables lack formal tests of the parallel-trends assumption inherent in fixed-effects models, and the interpretation of large negative coefficients is unclear (e.g., does disability prevalence “reduce” deaths, or are we capturing attenuation/measurement error?). Adding event studies, testing for pre-trends, and conducting sensitivity analyses (e.g., Oster bounds, Altonji et al. tests) would improve credibility. Moreover, transparency about data exclusions (41 states) and potential bias from missingness should be clearly discussed.

7. **Reframe the discussion in light of the true identification.** If the ALJ IV approach is adopted, the narrative needs to focus on the specific policy pathway—how adjudication leniency leads to insurance coverage, prescribing, and mortality. The current framing (“pill pipeline is a mirror”) must be adjusted to reflect what is actually being estimated. If the authors persist with the state-year OLS, they should explicitly acknowledge that the results are descriptive, highlight the confounding issues, and suggest that a truly causal test remains an open question.

By addressing these points, particularly by realigning to the ALJ leniency IV strategy and linking it to opioid outcomes, the paper can make a meaningful contribution to the literature on disability insurance and the opioid epidemic.
