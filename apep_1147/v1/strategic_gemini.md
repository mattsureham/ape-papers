### 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

The paper tests a classic tenet of labor economics—that unions act as a "shield" for Black workers—by examining the recent wave of Right-to-Work (RTW) adoptions in the Midwest and Appalachia. Using a triple-difference design on administrative data, the author finds a precise null: weakening unions did not widen the Black-White earnings gap. This suggests that the drivers of racial inequality in the modern U.S. labor market are largely orthogonal to the mechanisms of collective bargaining.

**Evaluation:** The first two paragraphs of the paper are actually quite good at setting this up. However, they lean a bit too heavily on "testing a claim." To truly grab an AER reader, the pitch should emphasize the *disruption* of the conventional wisdom.

*The pitch the paper should have:* "For decades, the 'union shield' has been a cornerstone of our understanding of racial wage compression. This paper shows that in the modern era, this shield has essentially vanished. By exploiting the largest shift in collective bargaining rights in a generation, I find that the racial earnings gap is entirely unresponsive to the weakening of unions, suggesting that the frontier of racial inequality has moved to sectors and mechanisms—like hiring discrimination and occupational sorting—that unions no longer reach."

### 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**State the contribution:** The paper provides a well-powered, quasi-experimental null result that challenges the "union shield" hypothesis, demonstrating that contemporary changes in union bargaining power (via RTW) do not impact the Black-White earnings gap.

*   **Differentiation:** It differentiates itself from **Farber et al. (2021)** by moving from historical long-run trends to modern causal shocks, and from **Fortin et al. (2022)** by shifting the focus from aggregate inequality to the racial dimension.
*   **Question vs. Literature:** It currently sits somewhere in between. It is framed as "testing a claim" (literature gap). It would be stronger if framed as: "Does the decline of American labor institutions explain the persistence of the racial wage gap?"
*   **The "DiD" trap:** A smart economist *might* call this "another DiD paper about RTW" if they only skim the intro. To avoid this, the author needs to emphasize the "precise null" as a substantive discovery about the *limits* of labor institutions.
*   **Making it bigger:** The "urban" result mentioned in the conclusion (and buried in the appendix) is a missed opportunity. If the null is driven by the fact that unions are now too weak to matter *except* in urban hubs, that's a much more nuanced and "AER-sized" story than a flat zero.

### 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

*   **Closest Neighbors:** Farber et al. (2021) on historical unions; Fortin et al. (2022) on RTW/Inequality; Card (1996) on wage compression; and the "New DiD" literature (Sun & Abraham, 2021).
*   **Strategy:** The paper should **synthesize** the historical literature with the modern reality. It shouldn't just say "Farber was right then, I am right now." It should explain the *transition*: why did the shield stop working? 
*   **Niche vs. Broad:** The paper is currently a bit narrow (Labor/Industrial Relations). To broaden it, it should speak to the **Macro-Labor** conversation about the "Great Divergence" and why racial gaps haven't closed despite various institutional shifts.
*   **Missing Conversations:** The paper needs to engage more with the **Sociology** of labor (e.g., Jake Rosenfeld’s work on "What Unions No Longer Do").

### 4. NARRATIVE ARC

*   **Setup:** Unions historically compressed racial gaps.
*   **Tension:** RTW laws arrived in the 2010s, providing a "stress test" for this shield. If the shield works, the gap should widen.
*   **Resolution:** It doesn't. The gap is unchanged.
*   **Implications:** The "shield" is either broken or irrelevant in the 21st-century economy.

**Evaluation:** The arc is clear but "flat." The resolution (a null) is intellectually honest but needs more "tension" in the middle. The author should lean into the *paradox*: Why does the shield work in our history books but not in our current data?

### 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

*   **The Lead Fact:** "When Michigan and Wisconsin went Right-to-Work, the Black-White wage gap didn't budge an inch."
*   **The Reaction:** Lean in—because it contradicts the standard narrative taught in Econ 101. 
*   **The Follow-up:** "Is that because unions are already too weak to matter, or because they only ever protected a small 'elite' of Black workers in manufacturing?" 

The paper passes the "So What?" test because the null result is *unintuitive* given the literature. However, it feels a bit like a "failed experiment" because it doesn't fully explain *why* the result is null.

### 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

*   **Front-load the Heterogeneity:** Move the urban/rural split from the appendix to the main results. If the shield *only* works in cities, that is a much more interesting story than "it doesn't work anywhere."
*   **Mechanism Section:** Section 2 is too brief on mechanisms. It needs a "Why might this be zero?" subsection.
*   **Conclusion:** The conclusion is actually the most interesting part of the paper. The discussion about "upstream mechanisms" (hiring, sorting) should be integrated into the Introduction to set the stage.

### 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The gap is **Ambition**. A precise null on a policy shock is a solid *Applied Economics* paper. An AER paper needs to use that null to change how we think about the *structure* of the labor market.

**The Single Most Impactful Advice:** 
Don't just report the null; explain the **death of the shield**. Specifically, use the QWI's industry-level data to show if the null is due to "compositional shift" (unions moving out of the sectors where they once protected Black workers) or "bargaining erosion" (unions still exist but can no longer extract the racial compression premium).
