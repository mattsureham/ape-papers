## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper studies India’s 2020 farm laws—one of the most politically consequential market reforms in recent years—and asks a simple question: did temporarily dismantling the mandi/APMC system change the prices consumers paid for staple foods? Using cross-state variation in how restrictive agricultural marketing regulations were before the reform, the paper finds essentially no detectable effect on retail food prices during the period the laws were in force or after they were stayed/repealed.

A busy economist should care because this is a high-profile test of a broad proposition: when governments remove longstanding trading restrictions, do markets actually respond in the margins policymakers and protesters claim they will? The paper’s potential contribution is not “another India policy evaluation,” but evidence on when dramatic deregulation fails to move consumer prices.

**Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Partly, but not well enough. The current opening is vivid on politics and chronology, but it does not crisply tell the reader why this episode matters beyond India, nor does it foreground the surprising substantive claim soon enough. It also buries the key tension: a huge reform, huge protests, and yet maybe no retail price effect.

**What the first two paragraphs should say instead:**

> India’s 2020 farm laws were advertised as a transformative deregulation of agricultural markets: by allowing trade outside regulated mandis, they were supposed to lower intermediation costs, improve competition, and ultimately affect prices paid by farmers and consumers. The reform triggered one of the largest protest movements in modern India before being first stayed by the Supreme Court and then repealed—creating an unusually sharp on-off policy episode to test whether dismantling a major trading restriction actually changes market outcomes.
>
> This paper asks whether the farm laws changed retail food prices, the most visible consumer-side margin in the debate. Using monthly price data from 169 markets across 28 states and exploiting cross-state differences in preexisting APMC regulation, I find no evidence of economically large retail price effects during either the reform period or after its suspension. The broader lesson is that removing formal trading restrictions may have little immediate effect on consumer prices when implementation is uncertain, pass-through is limited, or the regulated system provides real market infrastructure.

That is the paper’s best version of itself.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution in one sentence:**  
The paper argues that India’s temporary 2020 agricultural market deregulation did not produce large changes in monitored retail food prices, suggesting that removing legal mandi restrictions alone may not materially affect consumer prices in the short run.

### Evaluation

**Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?**  
Not yet. The introduction lists several literatures, but the paper does not sharply distinguish itself from:  
1. papers on APMC reform and market integration in India,  
2. papers on information frictions and agricultural price dispersion, and  
3. more general reform-evaluation papers with null results.

Right now the paper risks sounding like: “we study a major Indian reform and find no retail price effect.” That is a result, but not yet a differentiated contribution. The author needs to say more clearly: existing papers either study gradual state-level reforms, or information/technology interventions, or farmer-side outcomes; this paper uniquely studies a nationally salient but temporary deregulation and asks whether the most public-facing margin—retail prices—moved at all.

**Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or as filling a gap in a LITERATURE?**  
Mostly literature-gap, with gestures toward world-question. The stronger framing is the world-question: **Do highly salient deregulations of agricultural trade actually lower consumer prices, or are legal barriers often not the binding constraint?** That is much stronger than “there is little evidence on this episode.”

**Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what’s new?**  
At present, maybe, but not confidently. They might say: “It’s a DiD on India’s farm laws using retail price data, and it finds nulls.” That is not enough for AER-level memorability. The introduction needs to make the novelty more conceptual: **a politically explosive deregulation can leave retail prices unchanged; legal reform without institutional take-up may not move consumer markets.**

**What would make this contribution bigger? Be specific.**  
The biggest issue is scope. Retail prices alone may be too downstream to sustain the paper’s ambition. To make the contribution bigger, the author would need at least one of the following:

- **A closer-to-treatment outcome:** wholesale mandi prices, farm-gate prices, procurement margins, or trade volumes through mandis vs non-mandi channels.
- **A mechanism comparison:** show whether reforms mattered more where private trading infrastructure, storage, or procurement networks already existed.
- **A stronger conceptual contrast:** compare this legal deregulation to prior “friction-reducing” interventions like information technology or e-NAM type reforms, asking which kinds of market frictions matter.
- **A sharper welfare framing:** are consumers insulated while producer-side incidence could still be large? Right now the paper says this, but only as a caveat, not as part of the contribution.
- **A more surprising heterogeneity:** perishables vs storable crops, or MSP-intensive vs non-MSP commodities, tied to theory rather than as a late table.

If the author cannot broaden outcomes, then the contribution must be narrowed and sharpened: **this is a paper about the limits of inferring market transformation from high-profile legal reform, as seen through consumer prices.**

That lets the paper connect agricultural development, market design, pass-through, and implementation. The “null results methodology” angle is secondary, not primary.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
India had a heavily regulated mandi system that many economists and policymakers viewed as distortionary. The 2020 farm laws were a dramatic attempt to bypass that system and were widely understood as economically consequential.

### Tension
If the mandi system really imposed major costs on trade, dismantling it should have changed prices—especially in states where preexisting regulation was most restrictive. Yet the reform was short-lived, contested, and possibly weakly implemented, so the key puzzle is whether legal deregulation translated into actual market outcomes.

### Resolution
The paper finds no detectable large effect on retail food prices during the period the laws were in force and no reversal afterward.

### Implications
The paper suggests that formal deregulation may not be enough to change consumer prices when implementation is uncertain, pass-through is weak, or regulated intermediaries provide valuable market functions. More broadly, it cautions against equating legal reform with market transformation.

### Evaluation
The ingredients of a good narrative are here, but the paper is still too much **a collection of null-result diagnostics looking for a story**. After the introduction, the manuscript becomes heavily organized around specification defense and null confirmation. That is understandable, but not strategically optimal.

**What story should it be telling?**  
Not “look how many ways I can show zero.”  
Instead:

- **A huge deregulation happened.**
- **Theory and politics implied price effects.**
- **Retail prices barely moved.**
- **That disconnect is substantively interesting because it tells us legal barriers were not the immediate binding constraint at the consumer margin.**

The robustness should support that story, not become the story.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?
“India briefly dismantled its agricultural mandi system in 2020—a reform that sparked massive protests—and retail food prices appear not to have moved much at all.”

That is a decent opener. People would probably lean in at first because the episode is famous and the result is mildly surprising.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
For the first 30 seconds, they lean in. The risk comes immediately after, when they ask: **“Interesting—but are retail prices the right place to look?”**

That is the central vulnerability of the paper’s strategic positioning. If the answer is “not really, but that’s the data we have,” interest dissipates quickly. If the answer is “yes, because the reform was sold partly on lower consumer prices, and retail pass-through is itself a fundamental margin,” then the paper holds attention.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Almost certainly:
- “What about farm-gate or wholesale prices?”
- “Did anything happen to mandi volumes or private procurement?”
- “Was the reform too short-lived to matter?”
- “Does the null mean legal restrictions weren’t binding, or just that nobody believed the reform would last?”

Those are good questions, but they expose that the paper’s current outcome set may be too limited for a top-field-defining contribution.

### If findings are null or modest, is the null itself interesting?
Yes—but only conditionally. The null is interesting because:
1. the reform was nationally consequential,
2. expectations were strong,
3. the protest politics were enormous, and
4. the policy turned on and off in an unusual way.

However, the paper does not yet make the strongest case for why learning “retail prices didn’t respond” is intrinsically valuable. It edges toward sounding like a failed attempt to detect an effect. To avoid that, the introduction and discussion must be much more explicit: **consumer price non-response is itself a finding about where distortions sit in the supply chain.**

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

Bluntly: in current form, this is **not yet an AER paper**. The main issue is not competence. The issue is that the paper’s empirical object is narrower than its ambitions, and the narrative does not yet fully transform a careful null result into a big economic statement.

### What is the gap?

**Primarily a scope problem, secondarily a framing problem.**

- **Scope problem:** Retail prices in monitored markets may be too far downstream to bear the full weight of the claims. If the paper had wholesale/farm-gate outcomes or direct evidence on channel switching, the contribution would become much bigger.
- **Framing problem:** The paper currently presents itself as “a robust null result about India’s farm laws.” That is respectable, but not enough. It needs to be framed as evidence on the limits of legal deregulation as a tool for reducing consumer prices.
- **Ambition problem:** The paper is careful, but safe. It stops at “no large retail effect,” when the more ambitious paper would use that result to adjudicate among competing views of market distortions.

### What would excite the top 10 people in this field?
One of two things:

1. **Broader outcome scope:** show that the reform had no effect not just at retail, but also in upstream margins where theory says effects should appear; or
2. **A sharper mechanism design:** show that effects are absent precisely where uncertainty, infrastructure constraints, or pass-through frictions should bite.

Without one of those, this remains a well-executed but bounded paper.

### Single most impactful piece of advice
**Either add an upstream outcome/mechanism that gets closer to where the reform should bite, or explicitly recast the paper as a test of consumer-price pass-through from legal market deregulation rather than as a general evaluation of the farm laws.**

That is the fork in the road. If the author can do the former, the paper gets much bigger. If not, then the latter is essential for intellectual honesty and strategic clarity.
