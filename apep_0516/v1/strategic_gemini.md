**SUBJECT:** Strategic Positioning of "Does Geographic Targeting of Housing Subsidies Affect Prices? Evidence from France’s PTZ Reform"

---

### 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper uses a sharp 2018 policy reversal in France to identify the price effects of place-based housing subsidies. By comparing 26,000 communes that lost eligibility for zero-interest loans and tax credits to adjacent communes that retained them, the author finds that subsidy removal caused a 2.4% decline in aggregate housing prices—an effect driven by the non-subsidized (existing) housing stock via demand spillovers.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this reasonably well, but it spends too much time on the mechanics of the French "ABC Zoning" system in the second paragraph. It should pivot faster to the broader economic tension. 

**The pitch the paper should have:**
"Do housing subsidies primarily benefit the buyers they target, or are they simply capitalized into higher prices by sellers? This paper exploits a massive 'de-subsidization' experiment in France—affecting 70% of the country—to show that removing buyer-side subsidies reduces prices not just for new builds, but for the entire local market. The findings suggest that the incidence of housing policy is mediated by demand spillovers across market segments, providing a rare look at the downward price flexibility of real estate in response to policy withdrawal."

---

### 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:** The paper identifies the partial capitalization of buyer-side housing subsidies (12-35% of the economic value) and demonstrates that these effects spill over from the targeted new-build sector into the broader existing housing market.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It differentiates itself from Fack (2006) and Hilber & Turner (2014) by focusing on the *buyer* side (PTZ) rather than the *rental* side or *tax deductions*, and by looking at *withdrawal* rather than implementation.
*   **Question vs. Literature:** Currently, the intro feels 50/50. It needs to lean harder into the "World" question: "What happens to a town’s wealth when the government stops subsidizing its growth?"
*   **"Another DiD?":** A smart economist might dismiss it as "another DiD on French administrative data" unless the **asymmetry** (Existing vs. New) is moved to the center of the contribution.
*   **Bigger Contribution:** The paper would be significantly stronger if it could link the price declines to **local labor market outcomes or migration**. If prices fell 2.4%, did people stop moving there? Did the "vitality" of these communes (as mentioned in the conclusion) actually suffer?

---

### 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

*   **Closest Neighbors:** Fack (2006) on French rental subsidies; Hilber and Turner (2014) on capitalization; Busso, Gregory, and Kline (2013) on place-based policies.
*   **Strategy:** The paper should **attack** the narrow focus of previous literature. Most studies look for price effects only in the *subsidized units*. This paper’s "neighbor" should be the literature on **Market Segmentation** and **General Equilibrium in Housing**.
*   **Missing Conversations:** The paper is silent on the **"Spatially Blind vs. Place-Based"** policy debate (World Bank/World Development Reports). Is it more efficient to subsidize people in high-productivity Paris (B1) or low-demand rural areas (B2/C)? 

---

### 4. NARRATIVE ARC

*   **Setup:** France uses a rigid map to distribute billions in housing aid.
*   **Tension:** The government pulled the rug out from "low-demand" zones to save money, assuming these subsidies were "wasteful" or "inframarginal."
*   **Resolution:** Prices fell. This proves the subsidies *weren't* just wasteful; they were propping up the local asset market.
*   **Implications:** When you cut "targeted" subsidies, you aren't just hitting first-time buyers; you are hitting the wealth of every existing homeowner in the zone.

**Evaluation:** The arc is "Serviceable." To make it "Strong," the author needs to frame the 2024 partial reinstatement (mentioned on page 24) as the "Political Epilogue." The government realized the wealth destruction was politically untenable and blinked.

---

### 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

**The Dinner Party Fact:** "When the French government tried to save money by cutting homebuyer subsidies in rural towns, they accidentally wiped out 2.4% of the housing wealth for every neighbor on the block, even those who never received a cent from the state."

**Reaction:** People lean in. It’s a story about unintended consequences and the "stickiness" of place-based aid.

---

### 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

*   **Front-load the Asymmetry:** The result that existing housing falls while new-build stays flat is the most intellectually interesting part (even if the new-build sample is small). Move Figure 4 earlier.
*   **Appendix:** The Bacon Decomposition and the 2024 discussion are fine as they are, but the "Commercial Property" placebo (Section 6.5) is actually a "Result." If commercial prices also fell, this isn't a placebo—it's evidence of a total local demand shock. This should be a secondary result, not a robustness check.

---

### 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

**The Gap:** The paper currently feels like a very high-quality "Policy Evaluation" (suitable for *AEJ: Policy*). To be *AER*, it needs more **Economic Theory/Mechanism**. 

Why did new-build prices *not* fall while existing prices did? The author suggests "selection" or "demand spillovers." An *AER* paper would attempt to model this. If developers have high fixed costs, they might exit rather than lower prices (quantity adjustment), while the existing stock—which cannot exit—must take the price hit. 

**Single Biggest Advice:** Transform the "Existing vs. New" finding from a "curious decomposition" into the **central theoretical puzzle** of the paper. Use the small sample of new-builds as a feature, not a bug, to discuss how market thickness determines whether a policy shock results in price or quantity adjustments.

*   **Current framing quality:** Adequate
*   **Contribution clarity:** Somewhat fuzzy (is it about capitalization or place-based policy?)
*   **Literature positioning:** Well-positioned
*   **Narrative arc:** Serviceable
*   **AER distance:** Medium
