## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper investigates whether state-level mandates for workplace lactation accommodations—requiring private space and break time—help keep new mothers in the workforce. Using a triple-difference design across 34 states over three decades, the author finds a precisely estimated null effect on aggregate employment, separations, and earnings for women of childbearing age. This suggests that either the mandates are not a binding constraint on the employment margin or that their effects are too small to be detected in broad population data.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates the pitch well in the first two paragraphs, successfully transitioning from a specific, human-interest anecdote (the nurse in Tennessee) to a broad policy gap. No rewrite of the pitch is necessary, though the introduction could benefit from more explicitly stating why a *null* result here is a first-order concern for labor economists.

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution:** The paper provides the first causal evaluation of the labor market impacts of lactation mandates, finding that they do not move the needle on aggregate employment outcomes for women in the childbearing age bracket.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It differentiates itself well from the maternity leave (Rossin-Slater) and breastfeeding duration (Public Health) literatures. However, the differentiation from the "workplace amenities" literature (Mas & Pallais) needs to be more aggressive to explain why this specific mandate failed to produce a result while others (like flexibility) show high valuations.
*   **Question vs. Literature:** It is framed as answering a question about the *world* (Do these laws work?), which is a strength. 
*   **The "Smart Economist" Test:** A reader would likely say, "It's a very clean null result on a policy we all assumed was a 'win-win'."
*   **Making it Bigger:** The contribution would be significantly bigger if the author could move beyond the "population dilution" excuse. To be an AER paper, it needs to solve its own problem: if the effect is masked by aggregation, the author should use another dataset (like ACS or CPS) to look at *actual* postpartum mothers, even if the identification is slightly less "clean" than the QWI administrative data.

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

*   **Closest Neighbors:** Rossin-Slater (2018) on maternity leave; Kleven et al. (2019) on the child penalty; Mas and Pallais (2017) on valuing amenities; and Gruber (1994) on mandated benefits.
*   **Strategy:** The paper currently "fills a gap." It should move toward "challenging the consensus." The consensus is that workplace frictions drive the child penalty; this paper suggests that *this specific* physical friction (pumping space) is not a primary driver of labor market exit.
*   **Conversation:** It is having the right conversation but in a very safe way. It should speak more to the "incidence of mandated benefits" literature. If the laws don't help employment, do they at least shift the wage? (The null on earnings suggests not, which is interesting).

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

*   **Setup:** 34 states passed laws to help breastfeeding mothers stay at work.
*   **Tension:** Despite the intuition that these laws help, we don’t actually know if they affect the "quit" decision.
*   **Resolution:** They don't—at least not at a level that shows up in state-level data.
*   **Implications:** Policy mandates for specific office amenities may be "paper tigers" if they don't address broader constraints like childcare or schedule flexibility.

**Evaluation:** The narrative arc is clear but lacks a "hook" at the end. It feels like a "failed experiment" arc rather than a "surprising discovery" arc. To move to a "surprising discovery," the author needs to lean harder into the "why"—is it because of voluntary compliance or because the laws are too weak?

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

*   **The Fact:** "We looked at 25 years of lactation laws and couldn't find a single person who stayed in their job because of them."
*   **Reaction:** People would reach for their phones. A null result on a popular policy is only interesting if it's definitive.
*   **Follow-up:** "Is it because the data is too coarse, or because the laws are useless?" Currently, the paper admits the data might be too coarse (the 25:1 dilution). This kills the dinner party conversation.

---

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

*   **Section 7.1 (Population Dilution):** This needs to be moved to the *beginning* of the results or empirical strategy section, not the discussion. If the author knows the effect is diluted 25:1, they need to address why this test was the right one to run in the first place.
*   **Heterogeneity:** The paper needs a section on industry-level heterogeneity. QWI has industry codes. If there's no effect in "Healthcare" (female-intensive) vs. "Construction" (male-intensive), the null is much more powerful.

---

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

In its current form, this is a very high-quality "Note" or a solid paper for a field journal (like *J-Labor*). To make it an AER paper, the author must overcome the **ambition problem**. 

AER readers won't accept "I found nothing, but maybe it's because my data is too broad." To get into the AER, the author needs to find a way to see the "treated" people (mothers) or prove that the "dilution" isn't the reason for the null—for instance, by showing that even in industries where 80% of workers are women of childbearing age, there is still a zero effect.

**Single biggest piece of advice:** Conduct an industry-level analysis using the QWI's NAICS disaggregation to show that the null persists even in sectors where the "dilution" problem is minimized (e.g., Education, Nursing).
