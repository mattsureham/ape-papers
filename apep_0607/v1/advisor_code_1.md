**Summary**

The paper studies whether Brazil’s 2012 Forest Code amnesty delivered promised agricultural gains or instead generated pastoral expansion and moral hazard. Using a municipality-level panel (2006–2020) and a continuous-treatment DiD (treatment intensity = 2008 farming share), it finds that amnesty exposure raised cattle herds by about 11% but reduced soybean area, value, and yields—consistent with extensification. Municipalities with larger pre-2008 forest loss also experienced more post-2012 deforestation, suggesting moral hazard. The findings question the productivity rationale for the amnesty and underscore its environmental costs.

**Essential Points**

1. **Treatment Interpretation and Mechanism:** The core identifying variable is the pre-2008 farming share, but the narrative insists that amnesty windfalls were driven by illegal forest clearing conditional on biome-specific legal reserve requirements. The paper should demonstrate more clearly that farming share is a valid proxy for the amnesty windfall and not just a proxy for historical agricultural development. In particular, it should exploit the variation in legal reserve requirements (e.g., biome boundaries, property size exemptions) to show that treatment intensity predicts the size of the amnesty (e.g., hectares forgone restoration) rather than just prior farming levels. Without this, the DiD coefficient may simply capture mean reversion or other convergence dynamics.

2. **Parallel Trends for Crop Outcomes:** The pre-trend evidence for soybean area shows a strong declining trajectory before treatment; the event study reveals non-parallel pre-trends. This undermines causal interpretation of the soy results, which play a central role in arguing against the productivity dividend. The authors must either (a) model and control for the observed trend (e.g., include municipality-specific linear time trends or use a leads-lags specification to account for differential pre-trend slopes), (b) limit causal claims to outcomes with credible pre-trends (e.g., cattle), or (c) re-weight/control to ensure pre-trends are balanced. The regression tables should explicitly report whether adjusting for these trends changes the soy estimates.

3. **Moral Hazard Strategy and Timing:** The moral hazard test is purely cross-sectional (2012–2020 forest loss regressed on 1985–2008 forest loss) with state fixed effects. This raises concerns that the positive coefficient simply reflects persistent drivers (e.g., biophysical suitability) rather than moral hazard. A stronger design would exploit the discontinuity in legal reserve requirements at biome borders or the within-municipality change in enforcement (pre/post-2012) to isolate the incremental behavior induced by the amnesty. Alternatively, the authors could interact treatment intensity with the post-2012 period in a panel that includes pre-2012 deforestation, thereby separating trend from policy response. As it stands, the moral hazard claim remains suggestive rather than causal.

**Suggestions**

1. **Augment Treatment Validation:** Provide a table (or appendix) linking the farming share to actual amnesty windfall (e.g., hectares exempted from restoration) conditional on biome legal reserve requirements. This could involve computing the “restoration requirement gap” by comparing the legal reserve fraction to observed forest cover and showing that municipalities with high farming share (and therefore low forest cover) had larger reductions in required restoration post-amnesty. Alternatively, use PRODES data to measure pre-2008 illegal clearing and show it correlates with the farming share plus higher expected liability. This would make the treatment interpretation more precise and support the policy counterfactual.

2. **Address Soybean Pre-trends:** Given the worrying pre-trend, estimate the soy regressions with municipality-specific linear trends or using the method of \citet{bishara2021calendar} (e.g., flexible time controls or pooled event study) that allow for differential trends. Report whether the main coefficient remains negative/significant. If the effect disappears, reframe the soy results as descriptive or as part of a convergence narrative rather than a causal effect. Additionally, consider using matching or entropy balancing on pre-2012 trends to compare treated and control municipalities with similar pre-trends.

3. **Refine the Moral Hazard Test:** Exploit panel variation in forest loss by estimating a DiD-style specification where post-2012 deforestation (or enforcement activity) is regressed on treatment exposure interacted with a post indicator, controlling for municipality fixed effects and time trends. This would allow the paper to compare the change in deforestation rates before and after 2012 across high- and low-exposure municipalities, more directly tying the moral hazard to the amnesty. Alternatively, use the legal reserve discontinuity: compare municipalities that straddle biome boundaries where the required legal reserve jumps from 35% to 80%—do those on the high-requirement side (hence larger forgone recovery) experience larger increases in post-2012 deforestation relative to their neighbors? Including a placebo test (e.g., evaluating pre-2012 deforestation rates) would strengthen the causal interpretation.

4. **Clarify Mechanisms with Additional Outcomes:** To substantiate the extensification argument, consider including pasture area (if available) or other proxies (e.g., livestock exports, herd productivity). Alternatively, use land cover transitions from MapBiomas to show that high-farming-share municipalities converted remaining forest to pasture after 2012, not to intensive soy. Visualizing the land cover changes would bolster the narrative that the amnesty fueled low-productivity cattle rather than high-value crops.

5. **Revisit Welfare Implications:** The conclusion cites a US$2.4B carbon cost. Provide more detail (possibly in the appendix) on this calculation: the carbon price, assumed sequestration per hectare, and treatment of leakage/spillovers. Since the welfare comparison is central to the policy message, showing the steps (even if stylized) with sensitivity analysis would make the conclusion more credible.

6. **Harmonize Standard Errors with Policy Inference:** As the paper draws policy implications, ensure that inference accounts for the relatively large number of clusters (municipalities) and possible spatial correlation. The robustness appendix mentions state-level clustering, but consider presenting wild-cluster bootstrap \(p\)-values alongside conventional ones for key estimates to reassure readers about statistical significance, especially for the moral hazard result which is based on cross-section.

7. **Discuss Alternative Channels for Cattle Expansion:** The paper attributes cattle herd growth to amnesty exposure, but other factors (e.g., global beef demand, credit access, infrastructure improvements) could also drive differential expansion. Explore whether these correlate with treatment intensity by adding time-varying controls (e.g., road density, access to credit lines) or interacting treatment with a measure of market access. If data are unavailable, transparently discuss the potential omitted variables and their likely direction of bias.

Overall, the paper tackles an important policy question with rich data and a plausible empirical framework. Addressing the above points would solidify the causal claims, clarify mechanisms, and strengthen the policy takeaway.
