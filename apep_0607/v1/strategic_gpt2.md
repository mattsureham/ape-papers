It asks whether that amnesty delivered the promised economic payoff in the form of more productive agriculture, or instead mainly encouraged low-productivity cattle expansion while weakening incentives to preserve forests going forward. Busy economists should care because this is a first-order policy question about whether governments can trade off enforcement credibility for growth—or whether amnesties buy little growth and create dynamic moral hazard.

### The pitch the paper should have

Brazil’s 2012 Forest Code reform legalized millions of hectares of previously illegal deforestation on the theory that clearing legal uncertainty would unlock agricultural productivity. This paper asks whether environmental amnesty can produce a genuine efficiency gain—or whether it instead shifts land into low-productivity cattle use while encouraging future deforestation by signaling that violations will eventually be forgiven. Using municipality-level variation in exposure to the reform, the paper finds little evidence of a productivity dividend, but evidence consistent with pastoral expansion and weakened deterrence.

That is cleaner, more general, and more about the world than about Brazil alone.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:** The paper argues that Brazil’s 2012 deforestation amnesty did not generate the promised agricultural productivity gains, but instead primarily expanded cattle production and may have increased subsequent deforestation by weakening enforcement credibility.

This is a real contribution, but it is only partially differentiated from nearby work.

### Is it clearly differentiated from the closest papers?
Not enough. The paper says it is the “first systematic evidence on the amnesty’s agricultural consequences,” which may be true, but “first evidence on outcome Y” is not a strong top-journal contribution by itself. The stronger differentiation is conceptual:

- prior work on the Forest Code asks whether environmental regulation affects deforestation;
- this paper asks whether **retroactive non-enforcement** generates a productivity payoff large enough to justify the environmental concession;
- it therefore speaks to the economics of **amnesty as a regulatory instrument**, not just to deforestation policy in Brazil.

That distinction is present in embryo, but the introduction does not lean hard enough on it.

### World question or literature-gap question?
It is currently framed as both, but too often slips into literature-gap mode (“first evidence on agricultural consequences,” “contributes to three literatures”). The stronger framing is unequivocally a **world question**:

> When governments legalize past illegal land conversion, what kind of economic activity do they actually unlock, and what happens to compliance incentives afterward?

That is much better than “there is little municipality-level evidence on agriculture after the Forest Code.”

### Would a smart economist know what’s new?
Right now, maybe not. They might say: “It’s a DiD paper on the Brazil Forest Code showing cattle went up and maybe deforestation too.” That is not enough. The paper needs readers to say:

> “It shows that environmental amnesty is a bad bargain: it buys extensification, not productivity, and it may erode deterrence.”

That is a claim about the economics of regulation, not a design.

### What would make this contribution bigger?
Several options:

1. **Reframe around productivity rather than sectoral composition alone.**  
   The big question is not just soy versus cattle; it is whether legalization moved land toward higher-value, more efficient use. Right now “productivity dividend” is asserted, but the empirical center of gravity is still cattle/soy outcomes. The paper needs to elevate productivity, value added, or land-use efficiency as the central object.

2. **Make the mechanism more explicit: amnesty changes the option value of illegal clearing.**  
   The moral-hazard angle is potentially large, but currently underdeveloped and oddly separated from the agricultural results. The paper should more explicitly argue that the same policy had two margins: a static allocation effect and a dynamic compliance effect.

3. **Lean into the policy-design comparison.**  
   The paper could ask: if the state wants agricultural expansion, is amnesty an efficient way to get it relative to enforcement plus formal titling, credit expansion, or infrastructure? Even if not estimated directly, the introduction and conclusion can sharpen this comparison.

4. **Use broader outcomes if possible.**  
   If the authors have any route to measures of land values, credit, investment, slaughterhouse activity, or municipal GDP composition, the paper gets much larger. As it stands, the claim that property-rights regularization failed to deliver productivity is somewhat narrower than it sounds.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Brazil had a stringent forest protection framework with widespread noncompliance. In 2012, the government relaxed the regime and forgave a massive stock of illegal deforestation, with supporters claiming this would unlock productive agricultural use by resolving legal uncertainty.

### Tension
That justification embeds a deep economic tradeoff: amnesty may improve efficiency by regularizing de facto land use, but it may also reward illegality and reduce compliance incentives. It is not obvious ex ante whether the gains from regularization outweigh the dynamic costs to deterrence.

### Resolution
The paper’s answer is that the reform appears to have mainly encouraged cattle expansion rather than crop intensification, while municipalities with greater prior forest loss also saw more subsequent deforestation.

### Implications
If correct, environmental amnesty is a poor bargain: it does not deliver much of the promised productivity dividend, and it may undermine the future credibility of environmental regulation.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
**Serviceable, but not fully integrated.** The paper has the ingredients of a strong narrative, but at present it feels like two related papers stapled together:

1. agricultural consequences of the amnesty;
2. moral hazard in subsequent deforestation.

These belong together, but the bridge is not fully built. The story should be:

- The reform was sold as a productivity-enhancing regularization.
- The paper evaluates that bargain on both margins:
  - static allocation/productivity effects;
  - dynamic compliance effects.
- It finds weak static benefits and potentially important dynamic costs.

That is a coherent arc. Right now the moral-hazard section feels tacked on as an extra result rather than as the second half of the central bargain.

### If it is a collection of results, what story should it be telling?
It should tell the story of **amnesty as a regulatory bargain that failed**.

Not:
- “I estimate several outcomes after the 2012 reform.”

But:
- “Brazil traded away enforcement credibility in exchange for promised agricultural gains. The gains appear concentrated in low-productivity cattle expansion, while the credibility costs show up in subsequent clearing.”

That is the paper.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Likely one of these:

- “Is cattle expansion actually the intended margin, or just the easiest one to move?”
- “How much of this is about property rights versus commodity cycles?”
- “Does this mean amnesties never work, or just that this one worked badly?”
- “What should policymakers have done instead?”

The strongest papers anticipate the best follow-up question in the introduction. This paper should do more of that.

### If findings are modest or mixed, is that okay?
Yes, but only if the paper owns the asymmetry.

The crop results are mixed and partly contaminated by pre-existing trends. That is strategically okay if the paper stops trying to make soy do too much work. The null/ambiguous crop findings can still be useful if they are marshaled in service of a sharper conclusion:

> the promised broad productivity boom does not materialize; the clean effect is on cattle expansion, i.e., on extensification rather than intensification.

That is an interesting “null plus composition shift” result. But the paper must make that case explicitly. Otherwise the mixed crop evidence feels like a failed search for positive outcomes.

Yes, significantly. The paper has an interesting object—a giant environmental amnesty—but it still presents itself too much as a careful reduced-form evaluation of one reform in one country. The AER version is a paper about the economics of regulatory amnesty.

### Scope problem
Also yes. The evidence base is enough for a strong field-journal paper, but for AER the paper would ideally do more to pin down the nature of the “dividend” and the compliance cost. Right now the agricultural side is mostly cattle vs soy, and the dynamic side is suggestive but not fully central.

### Novelty problem
Moderate. The setting is novel enough, but the empirical surface can look familiar. The question must therefore carry the novelty. If the authors do not sharpen the question, readers may see it as “another paper on land-use policy and deforestation in Brazil.”

### Ambition problem
Yes. The paper is more ambitious in title than in argumentative architecture. It wants to say something big—about moral hazard and failed regularization—but too often retreats into narrower empirical bookkeeping. It should be bolder conceptually and more disciplined in selecting only the evidence that advances that concept.

### Single most impactful piece of advice
**Rebuild the paper around one big claim: environmental amnesty is a regulatory bargain—growth in exchange for leniency—and your evidence suggests Brazil got the leniency without much productivity.**

Everything else should serve that claim. That means:
- foreground productivity versus extensification,
- make cattle the empirical centerpiece,
- integrate moral hazard as the second half of the same bargain,
- stop leading with “first municipality-level evidence on agriculture.”
