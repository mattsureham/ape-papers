## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper studies a politically important question: when Brazil’s 2012 Forest Code forgave a large stock of illegal deforestation, did that legalization generate the promised agricultural gains, or did it mainly reward past violators and encourage more clearing? Using cross-municipality variation in exposure to the amnesty, the paper’s central message is that the reform appears to have expanded cattle production more than crop productivity, while places with larger pre-reform forest loss also saw more subsequent deforestation.

A busy economist should care because this is not just a Brazil paper. It speaks to a broad issue in political economy and environmental economics: whether retroactive legalization of illegal activity unlocks productive investment or instead creates a dynamic moral-hazard problem that weakens regulation.

Does the paper itself articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs? Reasonably, but not optimally. The current opening is vivid on institutional detail, but the pitch is still too “Brazil Forest Code” specific and not sharp enough on the general economic question. It also promises two equally strong results when, as written, the paper really has one stronger agricultural result and one more suggestive moral-hazard result.

### The pitch the paper should have

> Governments often respond to widespread regulatory noncompliance by forgiving past violations. The economic case for such amnesties is that they convert de facto activity into secure, investable rights; the concern is that they reward noncompliance and weaken future deterrence.  
>   
> This paper studies that tradeoff in one of the world’s largest environmental amnesties: Brazil’s 2012 Forest Code reform, which legalized a vast stock of previously illegal deforestation on private land. I ask whether the reform produced a genuine productivity dividend in agriculture or instead mainly facilitated low-productivity cattle expansion while weakening incentives to conserve forests. Using municipality-level variation in exposure to the reform, I find evidence more consistent with extensification and moral hazard than with productivity-enhancing regularization.

That is the AER-relevant pitch. It begins with the general question about amnesties, not with a statute.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:** The paper argues that environmental amnesties can expand low-productivity land use rather than productive intensification, and may undermine future compliance by rewarding prior deforestation.

That is potentially a real contribution. But in the current manuscript it is only **partly** clearly differentiated from nearby papers.

### Is it clearly differentiated from the closest papers?
Not yet. The introduction says:
- prior work studies deforestation,
- this paper studies agricultural consequences and moral hazard.

That is directionally right, but still too loose. The reader needs to know exactly what was unknown before:
1. Did legalization of illegal land use increase productivity, not just activity?
2. If activity increased, did it occur on the cattle margin or crop margin?
3. Does amnesty seem to alter subsequent behavior in a way consistent with dynamic noncompliance?

Right now, the paper risks sounding like “another reduced-form paper on the Forest Code” because it does not clearly establish what existing Forest Code papers have already shown about deforestation, enforcement, and land-use responses, and what remains genuinely open.

### World question or literature-gap question?
The paper is strongest when framed as a **world question**:
- What do regulatory amnesties do in practice?
- Do they unlock productive investment or encourage further violation?

That is much stronger than:
- “the literature has not yet studied agricultural outcomes at the municipality level.”

The latter sounds incremental. The former sounds like economics.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new?
At the moment, only somewhat. A smart economist would probably say:
> “It’s a paper on Brazil’s forest amnesty showing more cattle expansion and some evidence of moral hazard.”

That is decent, but still too close to “another DiD paper about deforestation policy.” The paper has not yet made the conceptual novelty memorable enough.

### What would make the contribution bigger?
A few concrete ways:

1. **Move from “agricultural outcomes” to “productivity versus extensification.”**  
   That is much bigger. “Agricultural consequences” is broad but vague. “Did legalization cause intensification or just more pasture?” is sharp.

2. **Put the comparison of cattle versus crops at the center.**  
   This is the most interesting substantive contrast in the draft. If legalization mostly expands cattle rather than high-value crops, that changes the welfare and political-economy interpretation of the reform.

3. **Make the paper about regulatory amnesties, not only deforestation policy.**  
   The paper should connect to tax amnesties, immigration regularizations, and informal-property legalization as a common economic problem: retroactive leniency trades off static efficiency against dynamic incentives.

4. **Strengthen the mechanism framing.**  
   The paper hints at a mechanism—amnesty relaxed constraints on already-cleared land, favoring pasture expansion over investment-driven intensification. That mechanism should be front and center.

5. **Avoid overselling the moral-hazard result unless it becomes central and clean.**  
   As currently presented, the moral-hazard piece reads more like an important extension than the paper’s equally strong second pillar.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Before this paper, the standard defense of the 2012 reform is that regularizing already-cleared land would reduce legal uncertainty and unlock productive agricultural investment. At the same time, critics worried that forgiving violations would weaken the credibility of environmental rules.

### Tension
These two logics point in opposite directions:
- amnesty as efficiency-enhancing regularization,
- amnesty as a reward for noncompliance.

The unresolved question is not just whether deforestation changed, but whether the reform generated a genuine productivity dividend that could justify the environmental cost.

### Resolution
The paper’s resolution is:
- the clearest agricultural response is increased cattle expansion,
- evidence for crop/productivity gains is weak or mixed,
- subsequent deforestation patterns are consistent with moral hazard.

### Implications
If true, the reform did not solve a property-rights distortion in a way that generated major productive gains; instead it facilitated more extensive, lower-value land use and may have weakened regulatory deterrence. That matters for forest policy, for the political economy of environmental enforcement, and for how economists think about amnesties generally.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
It has the bones of one, but it is not fully disciplined. At points it reads like:
- one result on cattle,
- some messy soy results,
- one cross-sectional moral-hazard result,
- a welfare discussion.

That is more a collection of findings than a tightly controlled story.

### What story should it be telling?
The story should be:

> **Regulatory amnesties are often justified as productivity-enhancing regularization. In Brazil’s case, the main detectable response was not productivity-enhancing intensification but expansion along the cattle margin, with suggestive evidence that amnesty also weakened future conservation incentives.**

That is coherent. It also allows the author to demote results that do not cleanly support the story instead of trying to make every table do equal conceptual work.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?
I would lead with:
> Brazil forgave roughly 21 million hectares of illegal deforestation, and the clearest economic response appears to have been more cattle, not more crop productivity.

That is a real hook.

### Would people lean in?
Yes, initially. The topic is inherently salient: amnesty, forests, agriculture, Brazil. Economists will listen. But they will only stay engaged if the paper quickly makes clear why this is a general lesson about amnesties rather than a descriptive postmortem on one law.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Likely:
1. “Why cattle rather than soy?”
2. “Does this mean legalization mainly raised the return to extensive pasture?”
3. “Is the later deforestation truly moral hazard or just persistence/frontier dynamics?”
4. “What is the welfare tradeoff?”

Those are good questions. The paper should be written to anticipate them.

### If findings are null or modest, is that interesting?
The crop side is mixed and, by the paper’s own account, muddied by pre-existing convergence. The author is right not to sell that too hard. The null/weak crop result is interesting **if framed properly**:
> the promised productivity dividend did not materialize where reform exposure was greatest.

That is a meaningful null. But it becomes interesting only if contrasted with the political promise of the reform. The paper should lean on that contrast more explicitly.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

At present, the gap is mostly a combination of **framing**, **scope discipline**, and **ambition**.

### What is the main problem?

#### 1. Framing problem
This is the biggest one. The paper is better than its current framing. It should not be sold as a paper about “the agricultural consequences of Brazil’s Forest Code” in the narrow sense. It should be sold as a paper about **whether regulatory amnesties create productive regularization or merely reward and propagate noncompliance**.

#### 2. Scope problem
The paper currently tries to do several things:
- agricultural effects,
- crop composition,
- intensification vs extensification,
- moral hazard,
- welfare.

That is a lot for a paper whose cleanest, most compelling result is narrower. It needs a more disciplined hierarchy of claims.

#### 3. Novelty problem
There is some novelty, but not enough is made of it. “Another Brazilian deforestation paper” is not an AER pitch. “One of the first papers to show that a major environmental amnesty mainly expanded low-productivity cattle rather than productivity-enhancing investment, with consequences for deterrence” is closer.

#### 4. Ambition problem
The paper is competent but somewhat safe in how it cashes out the result. The bigger ambition would be to use this setting to reshape how economists think about amnesties. That requires a stronger conceptual through-line, not necessarily more tables.

### Does this belong in AER as currently positioned?
Not yet. The topic is AER-worthy. The current manuscript is not yet. It reads more like a solid field-journal paper with top-journal aspirations than a paper that has fully identified its broad economic question.

### Single most impactful advice
**Reframe the paper around one big idea: regulatory amnesties may legalize land use without generating a productivity dividend, instead shifting activity toward extensive cattle production and weakening compliance incentives.**

If the author changes only one thing, it should be that. Everything else—introduction, literature review, ordering of results, conclusion—should be rebuilt around that sentence.
