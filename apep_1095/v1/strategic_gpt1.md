## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether voluntary, operator-led regulation can manage a slow-moving environmental externality when the underlying physical process has long lags. Using induced earthquakes in the Texas Permian Basin, it argues that Texas’s operator-proposed wastewater reduction plans did not visibly bend seismicity trends, in contrast to Oklahoma’s earlier mandatory caps.

A busy economist should care because the paper is trying to speak to a broad question: when does self-regulation fail for stock externalities with delayed feedback? That question travels well beyond earthquakes—to carbon storage, groundwater, methane, banking supervision, and any setting where “compliance” can be observed before outcomes respond.

**Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Partly, but not well enough. The opening is vivid and the topic is interesting, but the introduction slides too quickly into a specific Texas case and a descriptive policy comparison. The broader economic question is there, but it is not cleanly foregrounded. Worse, the paper reveals quite early that the main design is badly contaminated by reactive policy timing, which makes the current pitch feel like: “Here is a striking policy failure, but I cannot really identify it.” That is not a strong first impression for AER.

**What the first two paragraphs should say instead:**  

> Environmental regulation often relies on firms to propose and implement their own compliance plans. Whether that works depends not just on incentives, but on the physics of the externality: if harms respond only slowly to abatement, observed compliance may be a poor proxy for actual risk reduction. This paper studies that problem in the context of induced earthquakes from wastewater injection in the Texas Permian Basin.
>
> Texas responded to rising seismicity by creating Seismic Response Areas in which operators proposed their own injection-reduction plans, rather than facing binding government caps. I show that these plans were introduced precisely where seismicity was already accelerating and were not followed by any visible break in earthquake trends; by contrast, Oklahoma’s mandatory cap regime coincided with a sharp reversal. The paper’s central claim is not a clean causal estimate of one policy coefficient, but a broader lesson: for stock externalities with long geological lags, operator-led compliance can create the appearance of action without timely risk reduction.

That is the pitch the paper wants but does not yet fully earn.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Wastewater injection creates induced earthquakes. Texas adopted a relatively flexible, operator-led response in the Permian Basin. Regulators and industry reported meaningful volume reductions.

### Tension
If operators cut injection, why didn’t earthquake risk visibly fall? More broadly, can voluntary compliance work when the damage process is cumulative and slow-moving? And can observed “compliance” mislead regulators when the environmentally relevant stock has already built up?

### Resolution
The paper shows that SRA areas were on sharply rising seismicity trends before designation and that there is no visible post-designation break. Oklahoma, under a more mandatory regime, experienced a much clearer reversal.

### Implications
For stock externalities with long lags, delegated compliance may be too slow, too heterogeneous, and too easy to mistake for effective regulation. Regulators may need mandatory, earlier, and more uniform intervention.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
It has the ingredients, but not yet the discipline. At present it reads somewhat like:
- interesting intro,
- institutional background,
- a non-causal DiD,
- a placebo showing the DiD is endogenous,
- some heterogeneity,
- a loose Oklahoma comparison,
- broad policy implications.

That is closer to **a collection of suggestive facts looking for a unifying architecture** than a fully coherent narrative.

### What story should it be telling?
The story should be:

1. **Observed compliance is not the same as risk reduction.**
2. **This disconnect is especially likely for stock externalities with delayed physical response.**
3. **Texas is an empirical case where regulators observed sizable operator action but no corresponding break in realized hazard.**
4. **This is not just a Texas story—it is a governance lesson for subsurface environmental risks.**

That is the story. Everything that does not serve that arc should shrink.
