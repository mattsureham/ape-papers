## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether the Clean Water Act’s TMDL program — one of the central planning tools for impaired U.S. waterways — actually improves measured water quality. Using variation in TMDL completion across watersheds in Virginia and North Carolina, it argues that places with more TMDL completion do not experience better dissolved oxygen outcomes, suggesting a major environmental regulatory program may generate paperwork without environmental improvement.

A busy economist should care because this is, in principle, a high-stakes question about whether a flagship environmental planning regime delivers real environmental outcomes rather than administrative output. If true and broadly framed, the paper speaks to state capacity, implementation failure, and the distinction between regulation-on-paper and regulation-in-practice.

### Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Partly, but not well enough for AER. The current opening is competent and immediate, but it is too programmatic and too literature-gap-driven. It says, essentially, “here is a large program, and nobody has causally studied it.” That is not yet a top-journal pitch. The stronger pitch is not “there is no causal evidence on TMDLs”; it is “modern states often substitute plans for implementation, and here is evidence from a canonical environmental program.”

The first two paragraphs should do less institutional throat-clearing and more conceptual work. Right now, the reader learns what TMDLs are before learning the larger question the paper is trying to answer about government.

### The pitch the paper should have

A stronger opening would say something like:

> Governments often respond to environmental problems by producing plans, targets, and compliance documents rather than directly reducing pollution. Whether these regulatory outputs translate into actual environmental improvement is a first-order question for public economics and state capacity.  
>  
> This paper studies the Clean Water Act’s TMDL program, the main U.S. mechanism for restoring already-impaired waters. Despite decades of use and tens of thousands of completed TMDLs, we know little about whether completing a TMDL makes water cleaner. I show that higher TMDL completion is not followed by improved dissolved oxygen in two heavily monitored states, suggesting an important gap between regulatory planning and environmental outcomes.

That is the version that belongs in an AER introduction. The current one is a policy evaluation paper; the stronger one is a paper about the conditions under which regulation changes the world.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper’s contribution is to argue that completing TMDLs under the Clean Water Act does not improve a key measure of ambient water quality, implying that a major regulatory planning instrument may fail to translate into real environmental gains.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?

Not yet. The paper names adjacent literatures, but the differentiation is still fuzzy. As written, the contribution risks sounding like: “another paper evaluating environmental regulation using quasi-experimental variation and physical pollution outcomes.” That is not enough.

What is missing is sharper differentiation along three dimensions:

1. **Policy object**: This is not about the CWA generally; it is about the specific planning-and-allocation stage of the CWA.
2. **Outcome type**: This is about physical environmental outcomes, not housing values, willingness-to-pay, or permit prices.
3. **Conceptual contribution**: The paper is really about the failure of planning mandates absent implementation capacity.

That third element is the one that could make the paper memorable.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or as filling a gap in a LITERATURE?

Mostly as filling a gap in the literature. That is weaker.

The stronger world question is: **When government mandates planning rather than direct action, does that improve environmental quality?** Or even more broadly: **Do compliance-oriented planning regimes work when implementation authority is diffuse and enforcement weak?**

That is much better than “nobody has causally estimated the effect of TMDL establishment on dissolved oxygen.”

### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what’s new?

Not cleanly enough. Right now they might say: “It’s a DiD-ish paper on TMDLs and dissolved oxygen, and it finds no effect or maybe a negative effect.” That is not what you want.

You want them to say: “It shows that one of the core restoration tools in U.S. water regulation seems to produce plans without improvements in water quality — a paper about implementation failure in environmental regulation.”

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Several possibilities:

- **Use outcomes more tightly linked to the actual targeted pollutant loads.** Dissolved oxygen is substantively meaningful, but it is one step removed from many TMDL targets. If the paper showed no effect on the directly regulated pollutant and then also no effect on broader ecosystem outcomes, the contribution would be much larger and harder to shrug off.
- **Open the black box between plan completion and implementation.** The biggest missed opportunity is mechanism. Does TMDL completion lead to permit revisions? enforcement changes? BMP adoption? capital spending? If not, that is the story.
- **Expand beyond two states.** For a paper making claims about a cornerstone federal program, Virginia and North Carolina feel narrow. Even if those are the cleanest settings, the scope currently undercuts ambition.
- **Reframe around state capacity or implementation chains.** The paper hints at this but does not deliver enough evidence to own that territory.
- **Compare TMDLs with more enforceable CWA instruments.** A contrast with direct permitting or enforcement actions would make the paper much more conceptually powerful: planning tools fail where enforceable instruments succeed.

If I had to choose one way to make it bigger, it would be: **show where the chain breaks.** Without that, the paper is just “null/negative reduced form on one water outcome.”

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

### Closest neighbors

The closest neighbors appear to be:

- **Keiser and Shapiro (2019, QJE)** on consequences of the Clean Water Act and water pollution regulation
- **Greenstone and Hanna (2014, AER)** or related environmental regulation papers comparing regulation and pollution outcomes
- **Duflo et al. (2018)** on enforcement and environmental compliance, especially the distinction between rules and actual implementation
- **Craig (2012)** or legal/policy scholarship on the TMDL program itself
- Possibly broader public administration / state capacity work such as **Herd and Moynihan (2018)** and **Christensen et al.** on administrative burden

Some of these are not exact empirical neighbors, but that is the point: the paper’s best positioning may be slightly outside the narrow TMDL literature.

### How should the paper position itself relative to those neighbors?

- **Build on Keiser-Shapiro, not attack it.** Their broad result is that the CWA improved water quality. This paper can say: that success did not extend equally to all instruments; the planning-heavy TMDL component may be the weak link.
- **Build on enforcement papers by contrasting outputs with outcomes.** The conceptual hook is that environmental regulation only works when regulatory acts are backed by implementation and enforceability.
- **Use legal scholarship as institutional context, not as the main conversation.** Right now the paper leans a little too much on the institutional novelty of TMDLs themselves.
- **Borrow from state capacity/administrative burden, but cautiously.** The paper currently invokes that literature too aggressively relative to the evidence presented. It can say “consistent with,” not “this is a paper about administrative burden,” unless it actually measures burden and implementation.

### Is the paper currently positioned too narrowly or too broadly?

Oddly, both.

- **Too narrowly** in empirical scope: two states, one main outcome, one program stage.
- **Too broadly** in claim: “the cornerstone regulatory program of the Clean Water Act appears to clean the list without cleaning the water.” That overstates what the design and scope can support.

The positioning needs to become narrower in claim and broader in intellectual relevance.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?

Two areas stand out:

1. **Implementation and state capacity in regulation**, beyond administrative burden narrowly construed. This includes political economy of bureaucracy, implementation failure, and principal-agent problems in environmental federalism.
2. **Environmental monitoring and nonpoint source regulation**. Since TMDLs often matter most for nonpoint pollution, the paper should likely engage more deeply with that literature. The real issue may be that TMDLs are weak because the regulated margin is hard to enforce, not because “plans” are useless per se.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?

Not quite. It is currently having a conversation about “has anyone causally studied TMDLs?” That is not the highest-value conversation.

The better conversation is:
- when planning mandates work,
- when environmental regulation requires enforceability,
- why some high-profile regulatory instruments generate administrative compliance without environmental improvement.

That is a much more AER-like conversation.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

The U.S. has spent decades using the TMDL program to restore impaired waters. TMDLs are central to the statutory architecture and have generated massive regulatory activity.

### Tension

A TMDL is a plan, not a direct pollution reduction. The puzzle is whether producing these plans changes actual environmental outcomes or merely satisfies legal and administrative requirements.

### Resolution

The paper finds no improvement in dissolved oxygen associated with greater TMDL completion, and perhaps even relative deterioration.

### Implications

Regulatory output should not be conflated with regulatory success. If the result generalizes, a major environmental restoration regime may be hampered by an implementation gap, implying resources may need to shift toward enforceable downstream actions.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the skeleton of one, but not a fully convincing arc. Right now it still reads somewhat like a collection of estimates wrapped in a provocative title. The “paper tiger” story is there, but it is doing too much rhetorical work relative to the evidence actually shown.

The missing element is **resolution through mechanism**. A satisfying narrative arc would be:

1. TMDLs are the federal government’s main restoration planning tool.
2. Planning only matters if it triggers permits, investments, and compliance.
3. We show no improvement in water quality where TMDLs are completed.
4. We then show little downstream implementation response, or weaker effects precisely where implementation capacity is lower.
5. Therefore, the program’s problem is not diagnosis but execution.

Without step 4, the paper’s story is incomplete. It currently resolves with a negative coefficient, not with an explanation.

So yes: at present it is somewhat **results looking for a bigger story**. The story it should be telling is not “TMDLs don’t work,” but “planning-based environmental regulation breaks down when implementation is diffuse and weakly enforceable.”

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?

I would lead with:

> The United States has completed more than 80,000 TMDLs to restore impaired waters, but this paper finds no evidence that higher TMDL completion improves dissolved oxygen in the affected watersheds.

That is the strongest dinner-party fact.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

Initially, they would lean in. The program is large, the title is memorable, and the claim is provocative. But interest will fade fast if the next sentence is just about a two-state design on one outcome.

The first follow-up question will be:

> Is dissolved oxygen actually the right outcome for the pollutants these TMDLs target?

And the second:

> If TMDLs don’t work, where exactly does the chain break?

Those are telling. The paper currently has only partial answers.

### If the findings are null or modest, is the null itself interesting?

Yes, potentially very interesting. A null on a cornerstone environmental planning instrument is valuable. But the paper must work harder to make the null feel informative rather than accidental.

Right now the paper helps itself somewhat by arguing that TMDL establishment is the key policy output and by emphasizing the program’s scale. Still, because the outcome is somewhat indirect and the setting is limited, the null does not yet feel definitive enough to carry an AER-style claim on its own.

The paper would be much stronger if it explicitly made the case that learning “plans do not translate into environmental gains” is the substantive result — and then backed that with either:
- evidence that TMDLs fail to change downstream implementation, or
- evidence across multiple directly relevant outcomes.
