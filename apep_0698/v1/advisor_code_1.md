**Summary**

This paper constructs a new dataset linking PPP loan records to IRS Form 990 financial reports for over 150,000 nonprofits and asks whether the Second Draw PPP’s 25 percent quarterly revenue-decline eligibility threshold created a binding discontinuity in the nonprofit sector. Using annual revenue changes as a noisy proxy for the quarterly threshold, the author finds a precisely estimated null first stage: crossing the −25 percent annual decline cut-off does not change the probability of receiving a Second Draw loan. Conditional OLS associations suggest larger organizations dominated Second Draw take-up, and a placebo test on 2018 employment indicates that these associations reflect selection rather than a causal effect of PPP.

**Essential Points**

1. **Identification Breakdown from Running Variable Measurement.** The RDD hinges on a running variable that determines crossing the 25 percent quarterly threshold, but the paper uses annual revenue change as the running variable. Because that proxy is only loosely related to the actual eligibility criterion and the first stage is essentially zero, the design cannot credibly identify how the statutory threshold affected PPP receipt or employment. Stronger evidence is needed that annual revenue changes provide sufficient variation around the true eligibility cutoff—otherwise the identification claim is not supported and the null result is ambiguous (i.e., is it due to the threshold not binding, or simply the poor proxy?). The paper should either find a closer proxy (e.g., a subset of organizations that report quarterly data, or use external bank-statement documentation if available) or reframe the empirical question to acknowledge that the running variable does not identify the intended threshold and reroute the analysis accordingly.

2. **Interpretation of the Null First Stage Requires More Institutional Support.** The paper concludes that the 25 percent threshold “was invisible” to nonprofits because they report revenue annually, but there are still several mechanisms by which annual filings could correlate with quarterly declines (e.g., nonprofits with large annual drops likely had at least one qualifying quarter, and some nonprofits may have quarterly internal records). Without validating this institutional story—perhaps by comparing matched PPP applications to a sample where quarterly information is documented or by surveying lenders—the null first stage could also reflect measurement error, limited sample variation, or the fact that the threshold was easily met in both tails. More evidence is needed to distinguish these alternatives before the paper can claim a design failure.

3. **The Conditional Association Exercise Could Be Strengthened.** Table 4 shows a standard pre-treatment placebo, but the paper stops short of exploiting available institutional variation to bound causal effects. Given the inability to use the RDD, the author should consider alternative identification strategies (e.g., exploiting variation in bank relationships, timing of lender eligibility certifications, or geographic differences in lender capacity) or, at minimum, present a more detailed matching exercise to describe the selection process. As it stands, readers are left with descriptive conditional associations and a selection test, which do not establish the counterfactual impact of PPP even in a descriptive sense.

**Suggestions**

- **Clarify the Research Question in Light of the Proxy.** If the running variable is annual revenue change, then the question being asked is really whether an annual decline of 25 percent created a discontinuity in Second Draw take-up. The paper should make that explicit from the outset and then translate the institutional implications appropriately: perhaps emergency programs that rely on quarterly data cannot create sharp discontinuities for annual reporters. In doing so, the author could reframe the paper as an evaluation of a mismatch between eligibility design and sectoral reporting rather than as a canonical RDD analysis.

- **Benchmark the Proxy with Alternative Data (if possible).** To increase confidence that annual revenue change is informative about quarterly eligibility, the paper could exploit a subsample with richer information. For instance, some large nonprofits (hospitals, universities) file additional quarterly statements or participate in public financial reporting that may reveal quarterly trends. Even anecdotal evidence—documenting that, within matched records, large annual drops corresponded to actual quarterly declines—would help distinguish measurement error from a genuinely non-binding threshold. If such quarterly data are unavailable, the paper should be transparent about this limitation and perhaps use simulations to show how much attenuation one would expect under various quarterly patterns.

- **Deepen the Institutional Story Around Loan Allocation.** The paper asserts that organizational capacity and banking relationships explain Second Draw allocation. The author could substantiate this by describing the loan approval process in more detail, perhaps using information on lender type or processing time from the SBA data. For example, conditional on similar revenue shocks, do organizations with certain lender types (say, large banks vs. community banks) have higher Second Draw take-up? Are there geographic patterns consistent with differential bank capacity? Adding such auxiliary analysis would strengthen the narrative about selection and make the null first stage more interpretable.

- **Expand the Robustness Appendix for the RDD Claim.** The paper already reports McCrary tests and placebo cutoffs, but given the centrality of the null finding, it would be helpful to show the distribution of annual revenue changes (e.g., kernel density plots) and the probability of Second Draw receipt across the running variable. Visual evidence that the probability is flat—and that there is no discontinuity across a large range—would reinforce the conclusion. Additionally, reporting the fraction of organizations that actually met the 25 percent quarterly drop (to the extent this can be inferred) would help readers assess how “flat” the first stage should be.

- **Discuss External Validity and Generalizability.** The matched sample overrepresents larger organizations (45 percent match rate), which may bias the inference about threshold invisibility. The author should discuss how nonprofits excluded from the sample (e.g., tiny organizations filing 990-N) might differ in their ability to document quarterly declines. Are these smaller groups more or less likely to be constrained by quarterly data? Addressing this would help policymakers understand whether the design failure is a sector-wide problem or concentrated among the matched subset.

- **Consider Alternative Identification or Bounding Strategies for PPP Effects.** With the RDD unavailable, the paper could still provide bounds on the employment effect using instrumental variables exploiting other sources of variation—such as differences in lender exhaustion dates across states or disparities in lender penetration that affected the likelihood of Second Draw receipt. If such instruments are not available, the author could adopt a partial identification approach (e.g., Lee bounds) to illustrate the plausible range of PPP effects given the observed selection.

- **Provide More Detail on the Matching Process and Its Implications.** The matching strategy is described qualitatively, but the paper would benefit from reporting how often matches are ambiguous (multiple PPP records per EIN, for example) and what matching rules were applied when names/ZIPs shifted over time. Given the 44.7 percent match rate, replication would require clear documentation. Additionally, exploring whether matched organizations differ systematically from unmatched ones (in terms of size, revenue patterns, or geographical distribution) would help readers gauge sample representativeness.

- **Strengthen the Discussion of Policy Implications.** The paper raises an important point about aligning eligibility criteria with sectoral reporting, but the current discussion is narrow. The author could sketch specific design recommendations—e.g., how a quarterly requirement could be rewritten to rely on annual revenue and other commonly reported metrics, or how supplementary documentation could be mandated for nonprofits. Comparing the nonprofit case to for-profit firms (where quarterly data exist) would also highlight whether the issue is idiosyncratic or general.

- **Document the Limits of the OLS Associations.** While the selection diagnostic is compelling, readers would benefit from seeing how sensitive the conditional associations are to different control sets (e.g., dropping size bins or including industry fixed effects). Presenting a table showing the coefficients when omitting certain controls would underscore that the positive association is not driven by a single specification choice. Coupling this with a brief discussion of the remaining omitted variables (capacity, bank relationships) would round out the argument.

In sum, the paper raises a novel and policy-relevant concern about the payoffs of designing eligibility rules without considering administrative data structures. To fully deliver on that contribution, the author needs to either strengthen the identification (or justify the shift in the research question), clarify the interpretation of the null result, and deepen the auxiliary analyses that explain selection and policy implications.
