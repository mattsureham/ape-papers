### 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
The paper examines the $67 billion Paycheck Protection Program (PPP) expansion for nonprofits, specifically testing whether the statutory "25% revenue decline" eligibility threshold actually constrained loan allocation. It finds a "precisely estimated zero" first stage—meaning the rule was completely non-binding because it targeted quarterly data while the nonprofit sector operates on an annual reporting cycle. This suggests that the program's primary targeting mechanism was "decorative" rather than functional for a sector employing 12 million Americans.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this well, but it buries the "why" in the third paragraph. The first two paragraphs focus on the scale of the program and the data construction.  
**The Pitch the Paper Should Have:**
"Billions in emergency aid are often distributed using eligibility rules designed for for-profit firms. We show that when these rules are applied to the nonprofit sector, they fail completely: the PPP's 25% revenue-loss threshold had zero impact on loan receipt because it was 'invisible' to the annual administrative data nonprofits generate. Consequently, $67 billion was allocated based on pre-existing banking relationships and organizational capacity rather than financial need, rendering the policy's primary targeting mechanism a ghost in the machine."

---

### 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**One-Sentence Contribution:** The paper demonstrates that statutory eligibility thresholds fail when they are mismatched with a sector’s administrative data infrastructure, causing emergency aid to be allocated by institutional capacity rather than policy-defined need.

- **Differentiation:** Most PPP papers (Autor et al., Chetty et al.) focus on the *effect* of the money. This paper is about the *failure of the gatekeeping mechanism*. It moves beyond "did it work?" to "did the rule even exist in practice?"
- **World vs. Literature:** It answers a question about the world: *Why did the Second Draw fail to target the most distressed nonprofits?* 
- **Bigger Contribution:** To move from "competent" to "essential," the author should emphasize the **"Invisibility of the State"** or **"Administrative Burden"** angle. The contribution is biggest if framed as a cautionary tale for "Plug-and-Play" policy design: you cannot regulate a sector using data that doesn't exist.

---

### 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
- **Closest Neighbors:** Autor et al. (2022) on PPP employment; Granja et al. (2022) on targeting/banking; Bartik et al. (2020) on liquidity; and the nonprofit finance literature (Hansmann, 1980; Calabrese, 2013).
- **Strategy:** The paper should **synthesize** the PPP targeting literature (Granja/Bartik) with the Public Economics literature on administrative burden (e.g., Herd & Moynihan). 
- **The "Right" Conversation:** Currently, it's a bit too "nonprofit-niche." It should speak louder to **Public Economics (Program Design)**. The finding isn't just "nonprofits are different"; it's that "policy-data mismatch can nullify statutory intent."

---

### 4. NARRATIVE ARC
- **Setup:** The government launches a massive relief program with a strict 25% "distress" rule to ensure money goes to the needy.
- **Tension:** Nonprofits are included, but they don't report revenue like firms. Can a quarterly rule work for an annual sector?
- **Resolution:** Total failure. The RDD shows the threshold is a flat line. The "rule" didn't gatekeep anything.
- **Implications:** Allocation defaulted to the "strongest" organizations (those with better banks/staff), not the most "distressed." 

**Evaluation:** The narrative is strong. It’s a "detective story" where the detective finds that the locked door was never actually attached to the wall.

- **Response:** People would lean in. The "precisely estimated zero" is much more interesting than a small positive effect. It suggests a systemic breakdown in the machinery of the state.
