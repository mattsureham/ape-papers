**Idea Fidelity**

The paper largely follows the trajectory laid out in the manifest: it studies the Czech Electronic Records of Sales (EET) rollout using variation in phase-in dates to assess impacts on enterprise counts, draws on Eurostat and CZSO sources, and frames the research within the formalization literature. However, the published version substantially narrows the scope relative to the manifest. The manifest envisioned a “staggered DiD” across Czech municipalities with cross-sectional treatment intensity and several mechanism tests (e.g., municipal-level business counts, unemployment, VAT revenues, comparison to Slovakia as SCM). In the submitted paper the analysis pivots to a cross-country panel of 2-digit NACE divisions, relies primarily on TWFE/event-study designs, and relegates the municipal data to a short post-abolition reversal test. **Summary**

Using Eurostat structural business statistics for five Central European countries, the paper estimates the impact of the Czech Electronic Records of Sales (EET) rollout on the number of registered enterprises in covered sectors. A naïve TWFE estimate suggests a large negative effect, but once unit-specific linear trends are added, the sign reverses to a modest positive effect; placebo and permutation tests are interpreted as evidence that convergence, not EET, drives the naïve result. The paper introduces the notion of a “formalization mirage,” warning that cross-country DiD designs in transition economies can mistakenly attribute convergence to policy shocks.

**Essential Points**

1. **Credibility of Trend Adjustment versus Identification**: The central argument rests on the comparison between the naïve TWFE and the trend-adjusted specification, but the choice of linear trends is ad hoc and risks absorbing post-treatment effects. The authors should provide more systematic evidence that the linear trends are pre-treatment phenomena (e.g., by showing that the trend coefficients are estimated only on pre-treatment data or by using pre-period fits). Without such transparency, the result may be perceived as a mechanical sign reversal rather than an identification strategy—the linear trends could be soaking up treatment effects or other unobserved shocks. Consider estimating the trends using only pre-treatment years (as in \citealt{callaway2021}) or using alternative flexible controls (e.g., pre-treatment principal components) to assess robustness. Moreover, since the event study shows positive pre-trends, the interpretation that convergence explains the negative naïve estimate depends critically on the story that the convergence trend is smooth and extends through the treatment period; this warrants more structural justification or placebo checks beyond the current ones.

2. **Validity of the Control Group and Parallel Trends**: The treated units are only Czech sectors, while controls include all divisions from four other countries. Given structural differences (e.g., levels of development, EU accession timing, country-specific shocks) and the asymmetric convergence argument, it is unsurprising that trends diverge. The placebo on Czech non-EET sectors is helpful, but a more direct test of parallel trends—such as comparing pretreatment growth rates between treated Czech divisions and their foreign counterparts or employing synthetic control methods for each sector—is needed to justify that the trend-corrected estimator identifies the causal effect. Without this, it remains unclear whether the positive trend-adjusted estimate captures formalization induced by EET or a residual convergence pattern unique to the treated Czech sectors.

3. **Interpretation of Mechanisms and Policy Relevance**: The manuscript claims that the positive estimate reflects formalization, but the mechanisms are not empirically substantiated. The VAT revenue estimate is imprecise, and the reversal test is underpowered. The paper would benefit from additional evidence linking the positive effect to formalization (e.g., using business demography to show increased entries relative to exits, or analyzing whether sectors with more informal activity exhibit larger positive effects). As written, the policy lesson—that EET increased registrations—relies mainly on sign-switching rather than on observing the underlying channel. Strengthening the mechanism story is essential for the paper’s contribution to the formalization literature.

If these issues cannot be convincingly resolved, the paper may fall short of publication standards for AER Insights.

**Suggestions**

1. **Clarify the Identification Strategy**: Emphasize the conceptual link between convergence and the need for trends. For instance, present graphical evidence showing that Czech treated sectors were converging toward the control group well before treatment, and demonstrate that similar convergence is absent in the control countries. Consider estimating the trends using only pre-treatment years (e.g., by running a regression of outcomes on a time trend using data up to 2015 for each unit) and then including those fitted values or residuals in the main specification. Alternatively, employ flexible pre-treatment controls (e.g., spline trends, time-varying covariates capturing macro convergence) to show that the sign reversal is not driven by overfitting linear trends on noisy post-treatment data.

2. **Expand the Within-Czech Analysis**: Revisit the manifest idea of exploiting cross-municipality or cross-sector variation within the Czech Republic. Even if data limitations prevent a full municipality-level study, augmenting the current analysis with within-country comparisons (e.g., treated vs. untreated Czech divisions, treated vs. untreated municipalities weighted by sector composition) can help isolate the policy effect without relying on cross-country comparators. If using the Czech non-EET sectors as controls, report more diagnostics (e.g., pre-trend plots, difference-in-differences with sector-time fixed effects) to build confidence that these units provide a reasonable counterfactual.

3. **Reassess the Control Group Selection and Anticipation**: The current control group pools four different countries—Austria, Hungary, Poland, Slovakia—which may have experienced their own reforms or shocks. Consider repeating the main analysis with each country separately (e.g., Czech vs. Poland, Czech vs. Austria) and assessing the stability of the trend-adjusted estimate. Additionally, since different sectors phased in at different times, explore whether the convergence pattern is uniform across cohorts or whether some cohorts show divergent dynamics (which could indicate that the story varies by sector). This could involve estimating separate unit trends for each cohort or applying the synthetic difference-in-differences approach of \citet{arkhangelsky2021}.

4. **Bolster Mechanism Evidence**: The argument that EET formalized businesses would be stronger with direct evidence from business demography. Use the Eurostat birth/death panel to decompose the net effect into entry and exit channels, and examine whether treated sectors experienced increases in births or decreases in deaths relative to controls after 2016. Similarly, if VAT revenue data are too noisy, consider more granular fiscal indicators (e.g., sector-level VAT or cash turnover statistics, if available) to link compliance improvements to enterprise dynamics. The reversal test could be expanded by extending the observation window (if data permit) or by focusing on sectors with clearer pre- and post-abolition comparisons.

5. **Quantify the “Formalization Mirage”**: The conceptual contribution is appealing, but readers would benefit from formalizing it. For example, simulate how divergent pre-trends affect TWFE estimates in a simple convergence model, or demonstrate how different treatment timing amplifies the bias. This would help policy readers understand when standard DiD estimates are misleading and when trend corrections are sufficient. Additionally, clarify why the trend-corrected estimate should be interpreted as causal (e.g., by showing that the residuals satisfy parallel trends after demeaning by the fitted trends).

6. **Address Endogeneity of Abolition and Post-period**: The paper briefly mentions the abolition of EET in 2023 but only tests a reversal over a short window. If the abolition was itself a response to political pressure rooted in perceived harms, the contemporaneous data may reflect anticipatory behavior. Consider whether a longer post-abolition series (if available) or alternative outcomes (e.g., reported VAT compliance) can provide clearer estimates of the policy’s reversal, which would strengthen the causal narrative.

7. **Enhance Robustness Presentation**: The permutation and placebo tests are valuable but could be expanded. For the permutation test, show the distribution of estimated coefficients visually and indicate where the actual estimate lies; for the placebo, compare the pre-treatment trends of treated vs. placebo units explicitly. Also, since linear trends are central, check whether higher-order trends (quadratic, cubic) or splines yield similar estimates, to demonstrate that the positive effect is not an artifact of trend specification.

Overall, the paper poses an interesting question and offers a nuanced interpretation, but it would benefit from deeper engagement with the identification issues and stronger supporting evidence for the proposed mechanism.
