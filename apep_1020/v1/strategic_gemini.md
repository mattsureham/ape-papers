### 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
This paper exploits a unique policy "reversion" in the UK housing market to test whether tax-induced price bunching is a dynamic behavioral response or a static artifact. By tracking the migration of bunching across four simultaneous threshold shifts, the author validates a core (but rarely tested) assumption of structural bunching models: that excess mass moves in lockstep with policy kinks. It addresses the pervasive "round-number" problem in housing by developing a new difference-in-ratios estimator.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this pitch reasonably well by the second paragraph, but it leads too heavily with institutional dates.
**The "Better" Pitch:** "Does tax-induced bunching actually track policy changes, or is it merely an artifact of price points that would exist anyway? This paper exploits a rare natural experiment—the simultaneous shifting of four tax kinks in the UK—to prove that bunching 'migrates' as theory predicts. By introducing a novel estimator that nets out pervasive round-number preferences, I provide the first direct evidence that price-setting behavior is dynamically responsive to marginal tax rate changes rather than fixed price norms."

### 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**One-sentence contribution:** The paper provides the first systematic test of bunching "migration" across multiple moving thresholds while introducing a robust method to disentangle tax-motivated bunching from round-number bias.

*   **Differentiation:** It moves beyond Best and Kleven (2018) by looking at the *dynamics* of the kink rather than just the existence of the notch/kink.
*   **Framing:** It is currently framed as filling a gap in the bunching *literature* (Testing the Saez/Kleven framework). It would be stronger if framed as a question about **Market Frictions and Information**: "How quickly and accurately do agents re-optimize in complex markets when policy moves?"
*   **Clarity:** A smart economist would say: "It’s a validation of the bunching method using a clean 'move-the-goalposts' experiment."
*   **Bigger Contribution:** To make this "AER big," the paper needs to explain *why* it matters that bunching migrates. Does the speed of migration tell us about agent inattention? Does the failure of bunching to disappear at £425k reveal something fundamental about search frictions or first-time buyer ignorance?

### 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
*   **Neighbors:** Saez (2010) [Theory]; Kleven & Waseem (2013) [Frictions]; Best & Kleven (2018) [SDLT context]; Chetty et al. (2011) [Salience/Inattention].
*   **Strategy:** It should "stress-test" the neighbors. It shouldn't just build on them; it should position itself as the *empirical verification* of the assumptions they all rely on.
*   **Missing Conversations:** The paper is silent on the **Inattention/Salience** literature. If bunching at £425k persists even after the kink is gone (as Table 2 suggests), that is a massive result for the salience literature—it suggests "policy scarring" or "sticky price points."

### 4. NARRATIVE ARC
*   **Setup:** Bunching is a standard tool, but we assume it’s caused by current policy.
*   **Tension:** If bunching is just "round numbers" or persistent market norms, then policy evaluation based on bunching is biased.
*   **Resolution:** Bunching *does* move, mostly, but not perfectly.
*   **Implications:** Tax policy can precisely steer prices, but old thresholds might cast long "shadows."

**Evaluation:** The arc is currently "Methodological." It needs to be "Behavioral." The "tension" should be: *Can we trust bunching estimates if we don't know if they are just price-point inertia?*

### 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
*   **Dinner Party Fact:** "When the UK moved its tax thresholds, the 'clump' of house prices moved with them in less than a year, except for first-time buyers who kept clumping at the old, now-irrelevant price."
*   **Reaction:** Lean in—because it speaks to how "rational" or "fooled" home buyers are.
*   **Follow-up:** "Why didn't the £425k bunching disappear?" (This is the most interesting part of the paper and currently a footnote in the results).

### 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
*   **Front-load the "Why":** Move the discussion of the "Migration Hypothesis" to the very first page. 
*   **Appendix:** The "Standardized Effect Sizes" (Table 6) are helpful but the "Classification" (Large/Moderate) feels arbitrary; move to appendix or refine.
*   **The £425k Anomaly:** This needs its own subsection. It’s the "dog that didn't bark." If the goal is to show bunching migrates, the failure to migrate at £425k is a feature, not a bug—it’s an opportunity to discuss information frictions.

### 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The paper is currently a very high-quality "Technical Note" or *AER: Insights* candidate. To be a full **AER** paper, it needs **Ambition**.

**The Gap:** It treats the "migration" as a binary Pass/Fail test of a model.
**Single Biggest Improvement:** Shift the focus from "Does bunching migrate?" to "What does the *asymmetry* and *imperfect migration* of bunching reveal about market intelligence?" Use the £425k "failure" and the Welsh placebo more aggressively to tell a story about why some agents are more "tax-savvy" than others.
