### 1. Idea Fidelity
The paper largely pursues the original idea manifest, exploiting the April 2025 SDLT reversion as a multi-kink natural experiment to test bunching migration, using HM Land Registry Price Paid Data (universe of ~1.56M transactions) and a pre/post difference-in-bunching design with internal replication across thresholds (e.g., £125k new kink, £250k shrinking kink, £425k disappearing kink) and placebos (unchanged £925k/£1.5M, Wales). Key elements like the research question (does bunching migrate as theory predicts?), data source, and policy details are faithfully executed, including confirmation of anticipation via March 2025 spike. However, it misses full cross-kink replication: results emphasize £250k (significant decline) while other treated thresholds yield imprecise or directionally inconsistent estimates (e.g., £425k shows positive ΔR, contrary to prediction of vanishing bunching), and placebos fail (both show declines). The manifest's "6 simultaneous thresholds" and "internal replication" are underdelivered, with analysis narrowing to a round-number-adjusted ratio rather than standard bunching methods. Periods align closely (pre up to anticipation, post from May 2025) but exclude Scotland explicitly (only Wales used).

### 2. Summary
This paper tests whether housing price bunching migrates in response to shifts in the UK's Stamp Duty Land Tax (SDLT) marginal-rate kinks during the April 2025 reversion, using difference-in-ratios of transaction counts at thresholds versus nearby non-tax round-number bins to isolate tax effects from pervasive £5k clustering. The key finding is a significant 40% relative decline in excess bunching at £250k (where the rate jump shrank from 5pp to 3pp), driven by semi-detached homes, with robustness across specifications. Results are directionally consistent but imprecise at other treated kinks, validating (partially) the structural bunching model's prediction that responses track threshold changes.

### 3. Essential Points
**(1) Failed placebos undermine identification credibility.** The £925k unchanged threshold shows a large negative ΔR (-0.277, p<0.10, t≈-1.9), and the Wales £250k geographic placebo also declines sharply (-0.256, p<0.001). These suggest common shocks (e.g., macroeconomic changes in transaction volumes, anticipation spillovers, or post-reversion market slump—note post-period mean price drops 3% and volume halves) drive the results, not tax-induced migration. Authors must show ΔR=0 at true placebos or reject outright; current evidence does not credibly isolate SDLT effects.

**(2) Incomplete multi-kink replication and wrong-signed results at key thresholds.** The manifest promises "cross-kink internal replication" across 4+ kinks, but only £250k is significant and correctly signed; £125k/£300k (new kinks) show small positive ΔR but insignificant (t<1); £425k (disappearing FTB kink) perversely increases (ΔR=+0.068, p<0.10). This mismatches the RQ of testing migration "as theory predicts" and raises doubts about the elasticity's portability. Authors must explain/purify £425k (e.g., via FTB status controls, unavailable in data?) or downplay multi-kink claims.

**(3) Anticipation and period definitions bias pre/post comparison.** Pre-period ends Sep 2024 (stable regime), but anticipation (Oct 2024-Mar 2025) inflates pre-reversion bunching via intertemporal substitution (confirmed March spike). Pooling anticipation into pre overstates baseline bunching, exaggerating ΔR declines. Post-period (May-Dec 2025) has ~70% fewer transactions (376k vs. 1.2M pre), yielding low power and potential seasonality bias. Must re-run excluding anticipation or use dynamic bunching windows.

### 4. Suggestions
The paper is a promising AER:Insights submission—clean experiment, novel estimator, and direct test of theory—with strong potential if essentials are fixed. Below are concrete, prioritized recommendations to strengthen credibility, sharpen the empirical approach to the RQ, and expand appeal.

**Refine the estimator and match to standard bunching canon.** The round-number-adjusted ratio is innovative and well-motivated (addresses a real issue; see Kopczuk & Munroe 2016), but integrate it with canonical polynomial bunching (Saez 2010; Kleven 2016) for comparability. Suggestion: Report excess mass as \( B = [c_k - \bar{c}_j] \times E[\Delta \tau_k / \Delta \tau] \), where \( \bar{c}_j \) is control-bin mean, scaled by rate-jump change for proportionality tests. Fit 7th-order polynomials separately pre/post around each kink (excluding ±£25k windows), then difference excluded mass. This yields elasticity estimates (e.g., \( e = B / (p \cdot \Delta \tau \cdot f_c) \), with counterfactual density \( f_c \)) to benchmark against Best & Kleven (2018). Table would show if \( e \) stable across kinks, directly testing "migration as predicted."

**Handle anticipation and dynamics rigorously.** Use event-study bunching: monthly bins from Jan 2023, with April 2025 as event time 0. Plot ΔR_t vs. t around announcement (Oct 2024) and reversion. Exclude Oct 2024-Mar 2025 from main pre/post, or weight by transaction volume. Add Figure 1: density plots pre/ann/post at £250k vs. £925k/Wales, smoothed with Epanechnikov kernel (h=£2.5k). Quantify anticipation: regress log(transactions) on announcement dummy, interacting with £245k-£255k bin (expect +100% surge, as in smoke test).

**Bolster placebos and falsification.** Expand internals: test all unchanged kinks (£925k, £1.5M explicitly; manifest mentions both) and non-kink round numbers (e.g., £200k, £275k). For Wales, plot full densities and test LTT kinks (£225k unchanged). Add Scotland (manifest placebo, absent here) via postcode filter—its LBTT nil-rate (£145k) unaffected. Cross-region DiD: \( \Delta R_{kt} = \alpha + \beta \text{Post}_t \times \text{TreatedKink}_k \times \text{England}_r + \gamma X + \epsilon \), clustering by month-kink-region. If β significant and placebos=0, identification strengthens.

**Improve power and heterogeneity.** Post-sample thinness hurts; extend to 2026 if data available (manifest has 2025 full-year). Subsample high-propensity margins: restrict to semi-detached/terraced (median ~£250k-£300k). Add FTB proxy: interact with new-build=0 and price<£500k (FTBs overrepresented). Heterogeneity table good; extend to leasehold/freehold, region (London vs. North, varying price distributions).

**Enhance presentation and extensions.** Abstract: quantify elasticity (e.g., "implies e=0.15, half Best&Kleven slab estimate"). Intro: add theory figure—simulate bunching migration under Saez (2010) with £5k round preference (Gaussian spikes). Results: add proportionality plot (ΔR vs. Δ(Δτ)), fit line through £250k/£125k. Appendix: full densities, bin sensitivity (already strong), SDE table useful but clarify SD(Y) computation. Broader impact: discuss implications for stamp duty incidence (e.g., if bunching=5% of mass, deadweight loss=£X billion). Compare to similar reforms (e.g., US Prop 13 reassessments).

**Minor polish.** Table 1: add Wales column. Citations: add recent bunching reviews (Brockmeyer et al. 2023). Word count fits Insights (~4k); trim background if needed. Overall, fixing essentials could make this publishable—exciting validation of bunching portability!
