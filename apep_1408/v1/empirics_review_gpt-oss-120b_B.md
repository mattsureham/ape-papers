**2. Summary**  
The article provides the first causal evidence on Colombia’s nationwide voluntary coca‑substitution program (PNIS). Using a 23‑year, municipality‑level panel of satellite‑detected coca hectares and modern staggered‑DiD estimators, the author finds that PNIS did not generate a lasting reduction in coca cultivation; instead, a temporary dip (or compliance) is followed by a statistically significant spike two years after enrollment and a re‑version to baseline. Wave‑1 municipalities show a marginally negative effect, while Wave‑2 municipalities show no effect. The paper concludes that short‑term cash incentives produced a “substitution mirage” rather than a structural shift to legal agriculture.

**3. Essential Points**  

1. **Missing Labor‑Market Evidence** – The core contribution promised in the manifest (reallocation of agricultural labor toward formal crops, changes in wage bills, credit uptake) is absent. Without ENA‑based outcomes, the paper cannot answer the policy‑relevant question about “rural formal‑ity” and the broader development impact of PNIS.  
2. **Treatment Timing Granularity** – Treatment is coded at the department‑level wave (2017 or 2018) rather than at the exact municipality enrollment date. This coarse timing may blur dynamic effects, especially given that the “payment‑to‑verification” lag is central to the “mirage” narrative. The authors should at least discuss how this aggregation could bias event‑study coefficients.  
3. **Parallel‑Trends Concern** – The pre‑treatment event‑study shows a clear upward trend in coca area for PNIS municipalities relative to controls, reflecting the “peace dividend.” Although the author argues the bias is conservative, the trend also raises the possibility that the treated group was on a different trajectory for reasons unrelated to PNIS (e.g., differential FARC demobilization, early market shocks). The current robustness (Rambachan‑Roth bounds) is helpful, but a more convincing strategy would be to construct a synthetic‑control style counterfactual using a weighted combination of non‑PNIS municipalities that matches the pre‑trend of each treated municipality (or cohort).  

If these three issues cannot be remedied, the paper’s contribution is limited to documenting that “PNIS did not reduce coca at the municipality level,” a finding already suggested by descriptive UNODC reports. In that case, a **reject** would be appropriate.

**Overall Assessment**  
The manuscript delivers a rigorous, modern DiD analysis of PNIS’s impact on coca cultivation and convincingly shows that the program did not generate a lasting reduction at the municipality level. However, it falls short of the broader research agenda outlined in the original idea manifest, namely the labor‑reallocation and formal‑agriculture outcomes. Addressing the three essential points (adding ENA‑based labor outcomes, improving treatment timing, and tightening the counterfactual for parallel trends) will transform the paper from a valuable but narrow case study into a comprehensive evaluation of the program’s development impact. If the authors can incorporate these revisions (or at least the ENA outcomes), the paper would merit publication in a top‑tier development or applied microeconomics journal. If they are unable to do so, the contribution is limited and a rejection is advisable.
