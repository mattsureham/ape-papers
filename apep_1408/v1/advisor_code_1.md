**Idea Fidelity**

The submitted manuscript diverges substantially from the original idea manifest. The manifesto outlined an ambitious “From Coca to Crops” research agenda: use PNIS enrollment as quasi-experimental variation, instrument treatment intensity with pre-2017 coca area, and exploit DANE ENA (and ancillary GEIH) data to study whether voluntaristic coca substitution reallocated agricultural labour toward formal crops, reduced illegal agricultural employment, and shifted farm formality. The paper under review departs from this research question and data plan. It focuses exclusively on municipality-level coca area outcomes, uses only the SIMCI satellite data along with PNIS enrollment data, and traces a “substitution mirage” in coca cultivation dynamics. There is no mention of ENA, labour reallocation, or formal/informal employment outcomes, nor does the manuscript exploit the richer individual- or household-level data that the idea manifest promised. Consequently, while the paper explores an important question within the broader PNIS evaluation space, it fails to pursue the declared labour-market/formality-focused identification strategy and omits key datasets and outcomes from the manifest.

**Summary**

This paper provides a causal evaluation of Colombia’s voluntary coca substitution program (PNIS) by exploiting the staggered rollout across municipalities and 23 years of satellite-based coca area data. Using heterogeneity-robust DiD estimators (Sun-Abraham and Callaway-Sant’Anna) and various robustness checks, it finds no lasting coca reduction: coca hectares rise sharply two years post-enrollment and then revert to baseline, a pattern the author dubs the “substitution mirage.” The paper argues this dynamic reflects temporary compliance tied to payment windows rather than a sustained agricultural transition and notes marginally larger (but still fragile) effects in Wave 1 municipalities.

**Essential Points**

1. **Identification and parallel trends:** The credibility of the staggered DiD hinges on treated and never-treated municipalities sharing trends absent PNIS. The event study shows strong pre-existing upward trajectories in PNIS areas, and the placebo tests reveal marginally significant “effects” prior to treatment. While the paper argues this makes the zero estimate conservative, without a more convincing control for these divergent trends—such as flexible time-varying controls, municipality-specific trends, or synthetic control comparisons—the interpretation remains fragile. In particular, the positive year+2 spike could simply be the continuation of the pre-treatment boom. Please provide either (a) stronger evidence that pre-trends are adequately controlled (e.g., pre-period trend differences absorbed by flexible controls), or (b) show that the dynamic pattern cannot be generated by known aggregate shocks. Otherwise, it is difficult to separate the PNIS effect from the underlying peace-accentuated coca expansion.

2. **Mechanism ambiguity and within-municipality dynamics:** The paper interprets the positive year+2 coefficient as evidence of strategic replanting/compliance cycles, yet the aggregate municipal outcome cannot distinguish among (i) enrolled households reverting, (ii) non-enrolled households expanding, or (iii) changes in detection/reporting intensity. The narrative would be much stronger if the author tied the dynamic pattern to observable implementation details—e.g., staggered payment completion dates, monitoring schedules, or administrative data on compliance verification. At present, the “substitution mirage” story is plausible but not directly tested. Please incorporate household- or program-level timing (if available) or at least show that the event-year spike aligns with payment completion/importantly unique features of PNIS rather than with broader national trends (e.g., Duque-era policy shifts).

3. **Outcome scope mismatch with policy motivation:** The policy framing positions PNIS as a labour-market/legality transition, yet the empirical focus is limited to coca hectares. Without linking to agricultural labour reallocation, wage bills, or farm formality—data sources the manifesto highlighted—the paper risks missing the policy relevance it promises. Does the null coca result translate into null labour reallocation, or might households still shift behavior even if aggregate area doesn’t decline (e.g., cropping mix changes within municipalities)? Please either broaden the analysis to include labour/formality outcomes (ENA/GEIH) or reframe the motivation to match the empirical scope, explicitly noting what questions remain unanswered.

**Suggestions**

- **Strengthen pre-trend controls:** Given the substantial pre-treatment divergence, augment the DiD with municipality-specific linear or quadratic time trends, interacted with pre-2016 coca intensity, to ensure the counterfactual is not simply the continuation of the pre-existing boom. Alternatively, implement a synthetic control for a subset of municipalities (especially Wave 1) to benchmark the dynamic pattern. Including these robustness checks would reassure readers that the event-study spike is indeed post-treatment.

- **Leverage program implementation data:** The paper mentions payment delays and varying implementation quality across waves but does not systematically incorporate these variations into the empirical strategy. If enrollment/payment completion dates or verification schedules are available at the municipality level, consider using them to (a) sharpen treatment timing, and (b) test whether municipalities with longer payment windows or more complete enforcement show different dynamics. For example, if payments ceased in year 2, the spike would align, reinforcing the “mirage” story.

- **Expand outcomes toward labour/formality:** To align with the policy goal, integrate ENA (or GEIH) data by aggregating to the departmental level, as noted in the feasibility assessment. Even if municipal-level matches are imperfect, department-level labour-market outcomes could be linked to PNIS enrollment intensity (e.g., share of families enrolled per department), allowing a difference-in-differences at a higher aggregation that still addresses the research question. Alternatively, construct a synthetic measure of agricultural formality (e.g., inputs, credit usage) from ENA and relate it to PNIS penetration using a dose–response design. This would provide direct evidence on whether PNIS altered the de facto attractiveness of legal agriculture beyond coca area.

- **Address within-municipality displacement:** Since satellite data cannot distinguish enrolled vs. non-enrolled plots, the aggregated null effect might mask household-level success juxtaposed with spillovers. Including additional descriptive evidence—such as variance in coca change correlated with enrollment intensity per municipality (enrolled families per hectare)—could shed light on displacement. If provinces with higher enrollment density had smaller aggregate changes, this would suggest compliance rather than failure. Alternatively, use eradication event data to differentiate manual (PNIS-linked) vs. forced eradication; if manual eradication peaks align with the positive spike, that supports reversion narratives.

- **Clarify identification vis-à-vis other policies:** The manuscript notes PDET overlap but rationalizes it by the slow rollout. It would improve rigor to include explicit controls for PDET investment (e.g., year-by-municipality budgets) or to restrict the comparison set to PDET municipalities to isolate PNIS effects. Additionally, the 2018 Duque administration also ramped forced eradication; include time-varying forced eradication intensity (from the eradication event data) as a control or as part of a triple-difference that compares PNIS municipalities to non-PDET, non-PNIS areas to ensure the spike is not driven by the simultaneous militarized policy.

- **Interpret eradication results carefully:** The conflicting TWFE vs. Callaway-Sant’Anna findings on eradication highlight estimator sensitivity, but the narrative would benefit from a clearer explanation. Consider plotting the raw eradication trends for PNIS vs. non-PNIS areas, and use the classification of eradication method to show whether voluntary eradication (PNIS-linked) rose during the payment window. If voluntary eradication peaks coincide with the coca spike, that would support the suggestion that people were replanting once public attention waned.

- **Discuss heterogeneity beyond waves:** While the wave split is informative, additional heterogeneity (e.g., by baseline coca intensity, proximity to markets, presence of alternative crops) could clarify where the substitution mirage is strongest. The manifest mentions using pre-2016 coca as a dose–response variable; reintroduce this idea by estimating whether municipalities with higher baseline coca saw larger positive spikes, which would reinforce the argument that PNIS failed where it was most needed.

- **Reframe policy implications:** If the paper remains focused on coca area dynamics, explicitly state that it cannot adjudicate whether PNIS improved labour-formality outcomes—unless such results are added. The concluding claims about PNIS failing to transition households to legal agriculture should be tempered unless supported by direct evidence. Alternatively, present the coca dynamics as necessary but not sufficient evidence for policy effectiveness, while delineating what additional data would be required.

- **Enhance transparency on estimator choices:** Given the multiple estimators used, provide a table summarizing when and why each estimator diverges (e.g., the large positive TWFE eradication effect vs. the null heterogeneity-robust effect). This will help readers understand the sensitivity and justify the preference for heterogeneity-robust methods.

By addressing these points—strengthening identification, incorporating richer outcome data, and aligning motivation with empirical scope—the paper can more convincingly inform policy debates on voluntary substitution in post-conflict settings.
