This paper has a potentially interesting subject and a usable headline result, but in its current form it is not yet telling an AER-sized story. The raw ingredients are there: a historically important peace-accord policy, the world’s largest voluntary coca-substitution program, and a provocative claim that payments bought temporary compliance but not durable transition. The problem is that the paper keeps oscillating between three different identities—policy evaluation of PNIS, a methodological note on staggered DiD, and a broader theory-of-incentives paper—and as a result the contribution feels smaller and fuzzier than it should.

## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

### What is this paper about?

This paper studies whether Colombia’s flagship peace-era program to pay coca-growing households to voluntarily eradicate coca actually reduced coca cultivation in the targeted territories. Using municipality-level panel data, it argues that the program did not produce lasting reductions: coca fell, if at all, only temporarily during the payment window, then rebounded, implying that short-term incentives without durable economic alternatives do not transform illicit crop production.

Why should a busy economist care? Because this is not just a paper about one Colombian program. It speaks to a central question in development and political economy: can states buy their way out of illicit production in weak-governance environments, or do temporary transfers merely purchase short-lived compliance?

### Does the paper itself articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Partially, but not well enough. The opening anecdote is vivid and the setting is compelling. But the introduction gets pulled too quickly into program details, estimator names, and literature-gap language. The first two paragraphs should make the world question sharper and more general: when illicit activity is privately profitable and state capacity is weak, can temporary compensation induce durable transition?

Right now the introduction oversells causality mechanics and undersells the core substantive question. It also reveals too much coefficient detail too early, which paradoxically weakens the pitch. The first paragraphs should not sound like a methods section.

### The pitch the paper should have

Here is the pitch the paper should give in the first two paragraphs:

> Governments around the world spend billions trying to induce people to exit illegal but profitable activities, from drug cultivation to informal mining to poaching. The core policy question is whether temporary payments can generate durable occupational change when the underlying private returns to illegality remain high.  
>  
> This paper studies that question in the context of Colombia’s PNIS program, the largest voluntary coca-substitution effort ever attempted and a central pillar of the 2016 peace accord. Using two decades of municipality-level coca monitoring data, I show that PNIS did not deliver lasting reductions in coca cultivation: any apparent compliance was short-lived and cultivation later returned, suggesting that temporary transfers without sustained market alternatives buy compliance during the payment window rather than permanent exit from illicit production.

That is the AER-relevant framing. It is about the economics of state-building, incentives, and persistent illicit activity—not just “the first causal evaluation of PNIS.”

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### Contribution in one sentence

The paper’s contribution is to show that the world’s largest voluntary illicit-crop substitution program did not durably reduce coca cultivation, implying that short-lived payments absent sustained outside options are insufficient to move producers out of illicit agriculture.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest papers?

Not clearly enough. The paper does differentiate itself from work on forced eradication, but the differentiation is too binary: “they study forced eradication; I study voluntary substitution.” That is a start, not a contribution strategy. The introduction needs to explain more sharply what was unknown before this paper and why PNIS is a qualitatively important test case.

The closest comparisons seem to be:
- the Colombia forced-eradication literature,
- alternative development / crop substitution work outside Colombia,
- and perhaps broader transfer/incentives papers.

The current introduction invokes all three, but not in a disciplined way. The result is that the paper looks like it is filling a narrow program-evaluation gap rather than answering a broader economic question.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a gap in a literature?

Too much as the latter. Phrases like “first causal evaluation” and “filling a gap” are secondary-value claims. AER wants the first sentence to be about the world. The stronger framing is:

- weak states often try to replace illicit production with temporary compensation;
- we do not know whether that can work at scale;
- PNIS is a uniquely important test.

That is much stronger than “the literature has not causally evaluated PNIS.”

### Could a smart economist explain what is new after reading the introduction?

Right now they would probably say: “It’s a staggered-DiD paper on Colombia’s coca-substitution program that finds no lasting effect.” That is not enough. The paper risks sounding like “another DiD paper about a policy rollout” because it does not elevate the result into a larger conceptual claim.

What they should instead be able to say is: “This paper shows that temporary transfers in illicit economies can buy compliance but not structural exit, even in a flagship peace-accord program at massive scale.”

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Several possibilities:

1. **Mechanism through payment timing and program completion**
   - The “payment window” idea is the best part of the paper, but it is still mostly interpretive. If the paper can more directly tie dynamic responses to actual payment timing, delays, or rollout intensity, the story becomes much bigger and more distinctive.

2. **A more direct welfare-relevant or state-capacity outcome**
   - If the paper could connect coca dynamics to violence, market access, local development, or state presence, it would move beyond “did coca fall?” to “what did the failure of substitution mean for post-conflict transition?” That would substantially increase ambition.

3. **A tighter comparison between voluntary substitution and forced eradication**
   - Right now forced eradication is background. A more explicit comparative framing—when do carrots outperform sticks, and on what margin?—would make the paper speak to a much bigger audience.

4. **Household-level or plot-level evidence**
   - Even partial evidence distinguishing true household compliance from within-municipality displacement would materially upgrade the paper. At present, “payment-compliance cycle” is a good phrase but not yet a demonstrated mechanism.

5. **Generalization to a class of policies**
   - The authors want this to speak to payment-for-behavior-change programs under weak state capacity. To do that credibly, the paper needs a more disciplined bridge to other settings, not just a few analogies in the discussion.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

### Closest neighbors

The nearest literatures/papers appear to be:

1. **Mejía and Restrepo (2013, 2016)** on coca policy, illegal drug markets, and forced eradication in Colombia.
2. **Reyes (2019)** on the effects of forced eradication on coca cultivation.
3. **UNODC and policy-monitoring work** on coca trends and substitution, though not necessarily causal academic work.
4. **Broader alternative livelihoods / illicit-crop substitution literature** from Afghanistan, Bolivia, Thailand.
5. **Incentives and behavioral persistence literature**, such as Gneezy, Meier, Rey-Biel (2011), and some long-run cash transfer literature.

Depending on field conventions, it may also need to engage:
- state capacity and conflict recovery,
- development economics of illegal markets,
- and perhaps crime/displacement literatures.

### How should the paper position itself relative to them?

Mostly **build on**, not attack.

- Relative to the Colombia eradication papers: “The literature has taught us what coercive eradication does; this paper asks whether cooperation works better.”
- Relative to development/incentives papers: “The paper extends the logic of temporary incentives into illicit economies, where the outside option is unusually profitable and enforcement is incomplete.”
- Relative to peace-building literature: “This is evidence on whether one of the central economic pillars of a peace agreement actually shifted behavior on the ground.”

The paper should not overstate that it overturns prior work. Its value is that it tests a fundamentally different policy approach in a uniquely consequential setting.

### Is the paper positioned too narrowly or too broadly?

Oddly, both.

- **Too narrowly** in that it often reads like a sector-specific evaluation for Colombia drug-policy specialists.
- **Too broadly** in that it gestures at payment for ecosystem services, intrinsic motivation crowd-out, conditional cash transfers, and peace implementation all at once without fully inhabiting any of them.

It needs a primary conversation and one secondary conversation.

My advice: make the primary conversation **development/political economy of illicit production and state-building**, and the secondary conversation **durability of incentive-based behavior change**. Drop the “methodological contribution” as a front-of-house claim.

### What literature does it seem unaware of?

It may be under-engaging with:
- political economy of state presence in post-conflict regions,
- economic transitions out of illegal activity more broadly,
- organizational/market structure of illicit supply chains,
- and perhaps rural development / market access literatures that bear directly on why legal crop transition fails.

It also risks shallow analogizing to cash transfer persistence papers. Those analogies are plausible, but coca eradication is not school attendance. The paper needs stronger intellectual discipline in how it maps this setting to those literatures.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?

Not yet fully. The most impactful framing is probably not “crop substitution” per se. It is:

**Can temporary transfers induce durable exit from profitable illegal production in places where the state cannot fundamentally alter market incentives?**

That conversation reaches development, political economy, crime, and conflict economists. That is the conversation the paper should force itself into.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

The world before this paper: for decades, anti-drug policy in Colombia relied heavily on coercive eradication, which is controversial and often ineffective in the long run. The peace accord introduced a fundamentally different approach: pay farmers to voluntarily exit coca and transition to legal livelihoods.

### Tension

The tension is strong and real: economic logic suggests coca remains more profitable than legal crops, so temporary payments may be too weak to induce lasting transition. But PNIS was unusually large, politically important, and paired with a historic peace process, so if voluntary substitution can work anywhere, this was a plausible place to see it.

### Resolution

The paper’s resolution is that PNIS did not generate durable coca reduction at the municipality level. The dynamic pattern suggests temporary compliance around the payment period, followed by rebound or offsetting expansion.

### Implications

The implication is that substitution policy without sustained market integration or long-horizon support may purchase only temporary behavioral response, not structural change. More broadly, peace-building policies cannot rely on short-duration transfers when illegal production remains the dominant local economic option.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the ingredients of one, but the execution is messy. The paper often feels like a collection of results plus interpretation rather than a tightly driven story. In particular:
- the methods comparison is too prominent relative to the substantive argument;
- the wave heterogeneity, eradication outcome, and trends specification each suggest slightly different stories;
- the discussion introduces many conceptual claims that the earlier paper has not fully earned.

The paper should be telling one clean story:

1. PNIS was a historically important attempt to replace coercion with cooperation.
2. The economic environment made durable substitution difficult.
3. The data suggest temporary compliance rather than lasting exit.
4. Therefore, the failure is informative about the limits of short-run transfer-based substitution under weak state capacity.

Everything should serve that arc. Right now, some sections read like they were written to defend the design, not to tell the story.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?

“Colombia’s peace accord paid nearly 100,000 families to stop growing coca, and the evidence suggests the program bought short-term compliance but not lasting exit from coca.”

That is a good lead.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

Lean in—initially. The setting is important and the policy is memorable. But the next question would come fast: “Is that because the program failed, because farmers replanted, or because neighbors expanded?” And right now the paper does not have a crisp enough answer.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Almost certainly: **what exactly is the mechanism behind the null?**
- Strategic replanting?
- Within-municipality displacement?
- Bad implementation?
- Anticipatory planting?
- National coca boom overwhelming a modest program effect?

The current paper offers all of these, which weakens the takeaway. An AER paper can have multiple mechanisms, but it must rank them or provide sharper evidence on the leading one.

### Are the null/modest findings interesting?

Yes, but only if framed correctly. “We estimate no significant average effect” is not interesting by itself. What is interesting is:
- this was the flagship cooperative alternative to coercion,
- it operated at massive scale,
- and the dynamic pattern suggests not simple ineffectiveness but a particular failure mode: temporary compliance without durable transition.

That is much better than a null. The paper should stop presenting “null effect” as the main fact and instead present the **failure mode** as the main fact.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

At present, the gap is mostly a mix of **framing problem, scope problem, and ambition problem**.

### Framing problem
Yes. The science may be serviceable, but the paper is not consistently framed around the biggest question. It still sounds too much like “the first causal evaluation of PNIS” and too little like “what temporary transfers can and cannot accomplish in illicit economies.”

### Scope problem
Also yes. For an AER audience, municipality-level coca hectares may be too narrow unless the mechanism and broader implications are nailed down. The paper needs either:
- sharper mechanism evidence, or
- a broader set of outcomes tied to peace-building, local economy, or state capacity.

### Novelty problem
Moderate. The setting is novel and important. But “program had no durable effect” in a DiD design is not, by itself, top-journal novelty. The paper needs the failure mode and conceptual lesson to be unmistakably new.

### Ambition problem
Yes. The current paper is competent but safe. It settles for documenting average effects plus heterogeneity. A top paper here would more aggressively answer: **why did voluntary substitution fail at scale, and what does that teach us about the design of incentive-based exits from illegal activity?**

### Single most impactful advice

If the author can change only one thing, it should be this:

**Rebuild the paper around the claim that PNIS reveals a general failure mode of temporary incentives in illicit economies—short-run compliance without durable exit—and then make the empirical presentation serve that single idea.**

That means:
- lead with the world question,
- make the payment-window/reversion dynamic the centerpiece,
- cut the methods-as-contribution framing,
- and either provide more direct evidence on the mechanism or be more disciplined about what can and cannot be claimed.
