### 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
The paper asks whether trade sanctions targeting environmental "bad" behavior actually change that behavior or merely reshuffle global trade flows. Using satellite data to track 190,000 fishing vessels, it finds that while EU "yellow card" sanctions successfully slash seafood exports to Europe by 23%, they have zero detectable impact on actual fishing effort at sea. For the busy economist, this is a cautionary tale about the limits of "market-state" leverage and the prevalence of "paper compliance" in international environmental law.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this very clearly. The first two paragraphs of the current introduction are excellent. They move from the global scale of the problem (IUU fishing) to the specific policy instrument (EU Regulation) and immediately set up the "Trade vs. Behavior" tension. No rewrite is necessary; the author has a keen sense of the AER’s preference for big-picture relevance.

### 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**State the contribution:** The paper provides the first quasi-experimental evidence that international trade sanctions for illegal fishing fail to reduce total extraction, suggesting that sanctioned nations engage in trade diversion and cosmetic regulatory reform rather than substantive enforcement.

*   **Differentiation:** It is well-differentiated from Vatsov (2023), which only looks at trade flows. By moving the "camera" from the customs office to the satellite, it tests the *intended* welfare outcome rather than the *intermediate* policy outcome.
*   **Question vs. Literature:** It is framed as a question about the world: "Do trade sanctions work for conservation?" This is much stronger than "I fill a gap in the IUU literature."
*   **The "Smart Economist" Test:** A reader would explain this as: "It’s the paper that shows EU fishing bans are just 'paper tigers' because the boats keep fishing; they just sell the fish to China or Africa instead."
*   **Making it Bigger:** To make the contribution "AER-sized," the paper needs to lean harder into the **Trade Diversion** mechanism. Currently, it’s a high-quality null result. To push it over the top, the author should use the trade data (Comtrade/BACI) in tandem with the satellite data to show the "leakage" in real-time—proving that the fish not going to the EU are indeed going elsewhere.

### 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
The paper sits at the intersection of **Environmental Economics** and **International Trade**.

*   **Closest Neighbors:** Vatsov (2023) on trade effects; Burgess et al. (2012) on satellite data in environmental econ; He et al. (2020) on "watering down" regulations; and the "Green Protectionism" literature (Costello et al., 2008).
*   **Positioning:** It should—and does—position itself as a bridge. It uses the "gold standard" empirical methods of modern applied micro (Sun-Abraham) to speak to a problem traditionally left to descriptive maritime policy journals.
*   **Conversation:** It is having the right conversation, but it could speak more to the **Sovereign Debt/Sanctions** literature. There is a broad conversation in econ about why sanctions fail (e.g., Iran, Russia). Framing "IUU cards" as a form of "Environmental Sanctions" allows for a richer discussion on why sovereign states choose "paper compliance" over genuine reform.

### 4. NARRATIVE ARC
*   **Setup:** The EU uses its massive market power to police the world’s oceans.
*   **Tension:** We know trade drops, but fish don't report their location to customs agents. Does the policy actually save fish, or just change who eats them?
*   **Resolution:** The satellite "eyes in the sky" show no change in fishing. The "yellow card" is just a warning that triggers a change in paperwork, not a change in power.
*   **Implications:** Market-access leverage is insufficient without direct behavioral monitoring.

**Evaluation:** The narrative arc is strong. The "Paper Card" metaphor is a "sticky" concept that will help the paper be remembered and cited.

### 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
*   **The Lead Fact:** "The EU banned their fish, and they didn't stop fishing for a single hour."
*   **Reaction:** People will lean in. It confirms a cynical (and often correct) economic intuition about "leakage" and "regulatory arbitrage."
*   **The Follow-up:** "Where did the fish go?" and "Does the EU *know* it’s not working?"
*   **Null Result Value:** This is a "Big Null." Because the EU policy is the world’s "flagship" instrument, finding it is ineffective is arguably more important than finding it works. It forces a rethink of the entire global IUU strategy.

### 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
*   **Front-loading:** The paper is well front-loaded.
*   **Appendix:** The Sun-Abraham vs. TWFE diagnostic is necessary but could be even more concise.
*   **Missing Piece:** The paper mentions "Trade Diversion" in the discussion. This should not be a "discussion" point; it should be a **Table**. If the author can show that $\Delta$Exports to EU is perfectly offset by $\Delta$Exports to Non-EU, the paper moves from a "precise null" to a "mechanistic masterpiece."

### 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The gap is **Mechanistic Ambition**. 

Currently, the paper says "X happened (the policy) and Y didn't change (fishing)." To be a "Top 3" paper, it needs to show the *plumbing* of the failure. 
*   **Advice:** The author must integrate trade flow data (HS-6 level) into the main results. If you show me the boats didn't stop (Satellite) AND you show me exactly which new markets the fish flowed to (Trade Data), you have closed the loop. You transform a "failed experiment" into a "success in documenting the failure of international delegation."
