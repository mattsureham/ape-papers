### 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
The paper asks whether raising the tipped minimum wage—a policy often framed as a racial equity tool—actually closes the total welfare gap between Black and White service workers. It finds that while the policy effectively narrows the racial earnings gap (the "price" of labor), it has zero effect on the racial turnover gap (the "quantity" or stability of labor), suggesting that while customer-driven discrimination is mitigated, employer-driven discrimination remains an untouched barrier.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this very clearly. The "Stability Paradox" framing in the first two paragraphs is excellent. It immediately identifies a tension between a popular policy’s stated goals and a previously unmeasured margin of inequality. No rewrite is needed; the "Friday shift" hook is a textbook example of a top-tier intro.

---

### 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**Contribution:** The paper demonstrates that tipped wage policy fixes price-channel discrimination (tips) but fails to address quantity-channel discrimination (turnover), revealing the limits of price-based interventions in achieving labor market equity.

- **Differentiated?** Yes. Most tipping literature focuses on the *act* of tipping (Lynn) or the *level* of the minimum wage (Allegretto). This paper links policy to racial stability gaps using administrative universe data (QWI), which is a significant step up in scale and scope.
- **World vs. Literature:** It answers a question about the **WORLD** (Is One Fair Wage a "remedy" for racial inequity?).
- **Newness:** A smart economist would say: "It’s the paper that proves we can’t 'minimum wage' our way out of employer bias."
- **Bigger Contribution:** To make this even bigger, the author should attempt to bridge the gap between "separations" and "unemployment spells." If Black workers separate and find a new job in a week, the welfare loss is small. If they fall out of the labor force, the "Stability Paradox" is a catastrophe.

---

### 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
- **Neighbors:** Allegretto et al. (2013) on tipped wages; Pager et al. (2009) on audit studies of hiring; Bertrand & Mullainathan (2004) on labor market discrimination.
- **Positioning:** It builds on the "Price vs. Quantity" framework of discrimination. It should position itself as the **empirical bridge** between the tipping literature (customer bias) and the search/matching literature (employer bias).
- **Missing Conversation:** The paper is light on the **Search and Matching** literature. The "turnover gap" is a vacancy/tenure problem. Connecting this to models of "statistical discrimination in retention" would elevate the theoretical weight.

---

### 4. NARRATIVE ARC
- **Setup:** Tipped wages create a two-tiered pay system where Black workers earn less due to customer bias.
- **Tension:** "One Fair Wage" laws are passing to fix this, but earnings are only one part of the job. Does a higher wage matter if you still get fired faster?
- **Resolution:** The laws work for pay but fail for stability.
- **Implications:** Policy must move "upstream" to hiring and scheduling to achieve true equity.

**Evaluation:** The arc is exceptionally strong. It isn't just a collection of results; it's a "warning" to policymakers (as stated in the conclusion).

---

### 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
- **Lead Fact:** "Raising the tipped wage closes the pay gap by 65% but doesn't change the fact that Black servers are 8% more likely to lose their jobs every quarter."
- **Reaction:** People lean in. It challenges the "progressive" win of One Fair Wage by showing it’s a partial victory.
- **Follow-up:** "Why don't they stay longer? Is it the manager's fault or the customers making the environment toxic?" (This is the "So What?" that drives future research).

---

### 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
- **Front-loading:** The paper is well front-loaded.
- **Section 5 (Results):** The DDD and Event Study are a bit repetitive in the text. I would move the "Robustness" (Table 4) to the appendix and use that space to expand on the **Mechanism** (Section 6).
- **Table 2:** Panel A is the "money" result. It needs a visualization (a coefplot) to show the magnitude of the $871 estimate more viscerally.

---

### 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The paper is currently a "very high-quality applied micro" paper. To make it an **AER paper**, it needs more **ambition regarding the "Why."** 

Right now, the "Quantity Channel" is a black box. Is the turnover gap driven by:
1.  **Employer Bias:** Managers firing Black workers more easily?
2.  **Worker Sorting:** Do higher wages in NY attract a different pool of White workers but not Black workers?
3.  **The "Toxic Tip" Environment:** Does the *persistence* of customer bias make the job so unpleasant for Black workers that they quit even when the base pay is higher?

**Single most impactful advice:** Use the QWI data to look at **hires** vs **separations** and **tenure distributions**. If you can show that the "Stability Paradox" is driven specifically by shorter tenures for *newly hired* Black workers in high-wage regimes, you move from "documenting a gap" to "identifying a failure of integration."
