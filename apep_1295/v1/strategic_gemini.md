### 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether the global transition to Automatic Exchange of Information (AEOI) actually dismantles offshore banking hubs or merely triggers cosmetic accounting shifts. By exploiting the staggered bilateral activation of transparency agreements between Liechtenstein and 25 partner countries, the author finds a massive 42-57% drop in bilateral banking positions, suggesting that when secrecy vanishes, so does the capital.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this well in the first two paragraphs, particularly the "efficiency vs. secrecy" tension. However, it frames itself slightly too much as a "Liechtenstein paper." 
**The Revised Pitch:** *“Is the era of bank secrecy over, or has it just moved? This paper uses the bilateral rollout of transparency mandates in Liechtenstein to provide the first clean, country-to-country evidence that automatic information exchange causes a massive repatriation of offshore wealth. The findings suggest that secrecy was the primary comparative advantage of small financial centers, and its removal leads to a structural contraction of their banking sectors.”*

---

### 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution:** The paper provides the first bilateral-level causal evidence of AEOI’s impact on offshore deposits, finding magnitudes significantly larger than previous aggregate studies.

- **Differentiation:** It differentiates well from Johannesen and Zucman (2014) and Menkhoff and Miethe (2019) by moving from aggregate time-series to a bilateral panel. This allows for within-center variation, which is a major empirical step up.
- **World vs. Literature:** It is currently 60/40 Literature/World. It needs to lean more into the "World" aspect—specifically the death of the "Tax Haven" business model.
- **"Just another DiD?":** A cynical reader might say it’s "just another DiD about tax transparency." 
- **Bigger Contribution:** To make this "AER-big," the author needs to explore the **"Waterbed Effect"** (migration to non-AEOI hubs) or **real economy effects** in Liechtenstein. If the banking sector (30x GDP) contracts by 50%, what happens to Liechtenstein’s domestic economy? That is an AER-level story.

---

### 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

- **Closest Neighbors:** Johannesen & Zucman (2014, AEJ: Policy); Alstadsæter et al. (2019, AER); Menkhoff & Miethe (2019, JPubE).
- **Positioning:** It builds on these by improving the identification. It should "attack" the previous aggregate estimates more forcefully as being potentially confounded by global shocks.
- **Niche vs. Broad:** Currently a bit niche (International Tax). To broaden it, it should connect to the **Regulatory Arbitrage** literature (Houston et al., 2012) and the **Inattention/Behavioral** literature (to explain the lack of anticipation effects).
- **Missing Conversations:** The paper misses the "Corporate Tax" connection. Are these household deposits or corporate profit-shifting vehicles? The BIS data conflates these, but the narrative needs to address both.

---

### 4. NARRATIVE ARC

- **Setup:** Liechtenstein as a titan of secrecy (30x GDP).
- **Tension:** The OECD forces the "Sunshine" of AEOI. Does the money stay for the "stability" or flee because the "secrecy" is gone?
- **Resolution:** The money flees. Half of it.
- **Implications:** Secrecy was the product. Without it, the business model is terminal.

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong but the "Resolution" section is weakened by the Sun-Abraham results (Table 2, Col 4), where the ATT becomes insignificant. The narrative currently struggles to reconcile the strong TWFE results with the weaker (but theoretically "better") Sun-Abraham estimates. The author needs a better story for why the EU/EEA cohort (Wave 1) is the "true" effect.

---

### 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

- **The Lead Fact:** "When Liechtenstein started reporting to tax authorities, half of the foreign money left."
- **Dinner Party Reaction:** People lean in. 50% is a "holy shit" number in economics.
- **The Follow-up:** "Where did the money go?" (This is the paper’s biggest weakness. If it just moved to the Cayman Islands, the "Sunshine" didn't work, it just moved the shadow.)

---

### 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

- **Front-loading:** The contrast between the 8% decline in the 2005 Directive and the 50% decline here is the "hook." It should be more prominent.
- **Appendix:** The methodological discussion on TWFE vs. Sun-Abraham is important for a Top 5 journal but disrupts the narrative. Move the heavy econometrics to a "Robustness" section or appendix and keep the focus on the economic magnitude.
- **Conclusion:** It’s currently a summary. It should be a "Look Forward"—what does this mean for the future of the Swiss/Alpine banking model?

---

### 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The gap between this and an AER acceptance is **Scope and Generalizability**.

**The single most impactful piece of advice:** 
Expand the sample to include at least two other major financial centers (e.g., Switzerland or Luxembourg) using the same bilateral framework to prove this isn't just a "Liechtenstein story," and attempt a "Gravity Model" style analysis to see if the lost deposits reappear in non-reporting jurisdictions (The Waterbed Effect).
