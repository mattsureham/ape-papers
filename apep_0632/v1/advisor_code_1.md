**Idea Fidelity**

The paper largely follows the submitted idea manifest. It studies the causal impact of France’s low-emission zone (ZFE) boundaries on Rassemblement National vote shares, uses BNZFE polygons plus commune-level election data, and estimates a spatial difference-in-discontinuities design. However, two deviations are worth noting. First, whereas the manifest envisioned stacking twelve metropolitan ZFEs with staggered adoption dates, the empirical analysis focuses on just five metros with ZFEs active before April 2022, reducing the variation and pre-post comparison scope (especially since Grenoble’s ZFE began in 2019, while several other metros implemented theirs after 2022). Second, the manifest emphasized comparing presidential and legislative outcomes across multiple years; the paper restricts attention to three presidential elections (2012, 2017, 2022) for the main results and relegates the 2024 legislative data to an ancillary appendix. These choices should be justified (e.g., why later metros are excluded and why legislative elections are downplayed), but they do not contradict the core idea.

**Summary**

The paper exploits ZFE boundaries as a spatial discontinuity to test whether vehicle bans increased populist voting for the Rassemblement National. Using a difference-in-discontinuities design on 2017–2022 presidential vote changes across communes near five early-adopting ZFEs, the author finds no significant discontinuity at the boundary—contradicting the narrative that ZFEs triggered a backlash. Robustness checks (placebo pre-trends, bandwidths, specifications, metro fixed effects) and power calculations support the null finding, and the paper diagnoses how omitting metropolitan fixed effects produces a spurious positive effect.

**Essential Points**

1. **Sample Selection and Generalizability:** The analysis sample comprises only five metropolitan areas (Paris, Reims, Saint-Étienne, Toulouse, Grenoble) with ZFEs active before April 2022, excluding the other seven metros mentioned in the manifest. The paper needs to explain (and, if possible, demonstrate) why this restricted sample does not bias the estimates. Were later ZFEs excluded due to data timing, lack of nearby communes, or political events? If they could be included (even with shorter pre/post windows), doing so would bolster the external validity and statistical power. If inclusion isn’t feasible, show that nothing systematic distinguishes the excluded metros (e.g., in terms of voting trends or population) that might affect the estimated backlash.

2. **Measurement of Treatment Exposure:** The treatment indicator is a commune’s centroid falling inside the ZFE boundary. Given that French communes can be large, especially in suburban/rural peripheries, a centroid-based binary indicator could misclassify treatment status (e.g., a commune with most area inside ZFE but centroid just outside). The paper acknowledges the issue but does not quantify its potential bias. Please provide either (a) robustness checks using area-weighted exposure (e.g., proportion of commune area inside the ZFE) or (b) sensitivity analyses restricting to small, densely defined communes where centroid misclassification is unlikely. This is crucial because measurement error in treatment assignment could attenuate the estimated discontinuity, “explaining away” a true backlash.

3. **Multiple Hypothesis Testing and Economic Significance:** The paper conducts many bandwidths, specifications, and outcomes (RN share, far-right total, turnout, placebo). While none of the main estimates is statistically significant, the standardized effect size table classifies the point estimates as “large negative,” which may be misleading without contextualizing statistical uncertainty. The authors should clarify how to interpret economically meaningful changes versus sampling variation. In particular, the power analysis should connect explicitly to the policy question: what magnitude of backlash would have been necessary to motivate policy reversal, and does the confidence interval rule out such magnitudes? Otherwise, the null may be interpreted too strongly.

**Suggestions**

1. **Justify Metro Selection and Report Descriptive Comparison.** The manifest suggested stacking 12 ZFEs, but the paper focuses on five metros. Provide a clear description of the data constraints (e.g., implementation timing, availability of election data, boundary discontinuities) that justify this choice. Adding a supplementary table comparing the excluded metros’ characteristics (population, pre-policy voting trends, Crit’Air thresholds) to the included ones would reassure readers that the inference is not driven by a selective subset. If feasible, consider extending the main sample to include at least some late adopters by using a later pre/post period (e.g., 2022–2024 changes for metros that implemented ZFEs between 2022 and 2024), even if it requires adjusting the empirical strategy (e.g., explicitly modeling variation in implementation timing). This would increase both the sample size and the breadth of the policy conclusion.

2. **Improve Treatment Assignment.** The centroid indicator is a convenient approximation, but it risks misclassification. Computing the proportion of each commune’s area that lies within the ZFE (using spatial overlays) would allow you to (i) construct a continuous treatment variable, (ii) run a fuzzy RD, or (iii) restrict to a sample of “pure” communes (e.g., >90% inside or outside). If spatial overlays are infeasible, construct a “bounding box” measure: only keep communes with population density above a threshold (since high-density communes tend to be smaller). At minimum, report how many communes have more than 10% of their area crossing the boundary to gauge the scale of the issue.

3. **Clarify Timing and Expectation of Treatment Effects.** The difference-in-discontinuities design compares 2017–2022 changes, but some ZFEs (Grenoble, Paris) began earlier, while others (Reims, Saint-Étienne, Toulouse) were implemented in 2022. Specify the exact dates of treatment onset for each metro and discuss whether the 2022 presidential election captures enough exposure. Could the 2022 vote reflect only a few months of enforcement, leaving the main political reaction to future elections (e.g., 2024 legislative)? If so, consider incorporating later outcomes (2022–2024 changes, 2024 legislative results) more centrally, or motivate the assumption that the political effect would manifest quickly upon implementation. This is especially important because the null result could be due to limited exposure rather than absence of backlash.

4. **Address Potential Spillovers and General Equilibrium Effects.** The RD compares communes just inside vs. outside the boundary, but voters just outside the zone also bear spillover costs (e.g., traffic, enforcement) or benefits (cleaner air), and political messaging may operate city-wide rather than at the perimeter. Discuss how such spillovers could bias the estimated discontinuity (e.g., attenuating the difference). If possible, estimate the effect using distance bins farther from the boundary (e.g., 5–15 km) or use a Donut RDD that drops the immediate boundary area to see if remote suburbs show divergence, which might suggest broader political backlash.

5. **Condition on Additional Covariates or Use Event Studies.** The key assumption is that, conditional on metro fixed effects, the change in voting is smooth at the boundary. Yet the covariate balance table shows significant level differences in FN/RN share and population. Controlling for time-varying covariates (e.g., income, share of older vehicles, commuting flows) could improve precision and address concerns about differential trends. While such data may be limited, consider including metro-by-distance trends or interacting the signed distance with pre-treatment covariates to flexibly control for gradients. Alternatively, an event-study-style graph plotting RN vote share by signed distance in each election would illustrate whether there’s a pre-existing trend or discontinuity that the differencing fails to purge.

6. **Reassess Power and SDE Interpretation.** The standardized effect size table labels nearly all point estimates as “large negative,” but the corresponding confidence intervals cover zero. The classification is derived mechanically from the point estimate and the unconditional SD, but it risks overstating substantive effects. Consider reporting and discussing the confidence interval on the standardized effect or the ratio of effect size to standard deviation with uncertainty (e.g., a Bayesian interval or bootstrap). Additionally, the power analysis should translate the minimum detectable effect into policy-relevant terms—what share swing would be needed to plausibly blame ZFEs for electoral outcomes or to sway a close municipal election? This would ground the null result in political context.

7. **Broaden the Discussion of Mechanisms and Policy Implications.** The paper argues that France reversed ZFEs due to a misdiagnosed electoral signal. It would strengthen the contribution to juxtapose this finding with qualitative evidence (e.g., quotes from politicians citing RN performance) or to discuss alternative channels (e.g., nationalized debate, media coverage). Moreover, since the reversal happened after 2022, the paper could speculate on whether the null result would generalize to other countries with LEZs; are French voters uniquely divided, or is this a generalizable null? Adding a short section synthesizing implications for other urban policies would broaden the paper’s audience.

8. **Clarify Discussion of Turnout and Other Outcomes.** The turnout estimates show small negative effects significant at some bandwidths. Discuss whether these are economically meaningful or artifacts of multiple testing. Are the turnout coefficients robust to metro-by-distance trends or to excluding Paris? If turnout is affected, it may indicate mobilization costs rather than ideological backlash—exploring this nuance would enrich the narrative.

By addressing these points, the paper would better establish the credibility of its identification strategy, reinforce the null finding’s policy relevance, and strengthen its contribution to debates on environmental regulation and populism.
