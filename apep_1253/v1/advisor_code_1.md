**Idea Fidelity**

The paper largely follows the original manifest idea: it studies the October 2021 Thrifty Food Plan (TFP) revision using county-level variation in SNAP exposure and QWI employment panels, and it emphasizes industry-specific reallocation patterns. However, it departs from the manifest in two important ways. **Summary**

The paper studies the industry-level labor supply response to the permanent 21% increase in SNAP benefits via the 2021 Thrifty Food Plan revision. Using county-industry-quarter QWI data merged with 2019 poverty rates, it implements a continuous-treatment difference-in-differences with county×industry and industry×quarter fixed effects to quantify employment changes, finding modest negative associations across several industries (notably food services and finance) but no clean causal story due to pre-existing trends. Event studies and robustness checks reveal that the estimated effects likely capture heterogeneous COVID recovery dynamics rather than the SNAP policy shock, leading the author to interpret the results as suggestive associations rather than causal estimates.

**Essential Points**

1. **Parallel Trends Failure and Identification**: The event study shows statistically significant positive pre-trends, which the paper candidly attributes to heterogeneous COVID recovery. This undermines the key identifying assumption of the continuous-DiD design. While the paper discusses the problem, it stops short of providing a convincing strategy (beyond adding state×quarter fixed effects, which do not solve the issue) to restore credibility. The authors need to either (a) demonstrate that the pre-trends are confined to specific periods or subsamples and can be accounted for (e.g., via pre-trend adjustments, leads, or placebo-driven weighting) or (b) adopt a different source of variation (e.g., within-state SNAP caseload changes, administrative SNAP participation data, or an event isolated from COVID shocks). Without addressing this fundamental threat, the estimated coefficients cannot be interpreted even as credible associations.

2. **Treatment Measurement and Interpretation**: Using 2019 SAIPE poverty rates as a proxy for SNAP exposure weakens the manifest’s aim and raises concerns about measurement error and confounding. Poverty rates capture broader county-level socioeconomic status (and, importantly, COVID-related hardship) rather than the specific policy exposure generated by SNAP participation. The paper should (a) better justify this proxy by showing its predictive power for actual SNAP increase exposure (perhaps via prior-year SNAP participation or benefit issuance data), (b) explore alternative proxies (e.g., 2019 SNAP participation rates, 2020 caseload changes) to assess robustness, and (c) perform attenuation bias calculations to quantify how measurement error might affect the reported coefficients. Without these steps, the treatment intensity variable remains a black box, making it hard to interpret the results in terms of SNAP generosity.

3. **Mechanism and Flow Decomposition**: The examination of hires, separations, and earnings is useful, but the narrative that “null on flows implies compositional change rather than labor supply withdrawal” is too speculative. The null effects could be due to limited power at the flow margin, aggregation masking heterogeneous responses, or offsetting dynamics (e.g., simultaneous but opposite shifts in hiring and separations). The authors should strengthen the mechanism section by (a) showing industry-by-industry event studies for hires/separations, (b) decomposing the employment response by firm size or urbanicity to see whether specific labor market segments drive the results, and (c) linking any observed flow changes to SNAP exposure through an explicit hypothesis (e.g., if higher SNAP benefits raise reservation wages, we should see fewer new hires in low-wage sectors). As it stands, the flow evidence neither confirms nor refutes the “reallocation dividend.”

**Suggestions**

1. **Pre-trend Resolution and Alternative Identification**  
   - Given the strong pre-treatment correlations, consider re-estimating the main model with a sample that excludes 2020 observations (or at least 2020Q2–2021Q1) to reduce the influence of the pandemic-induced shock. This would shrink the pre-treatment window to pre-COVID and isolate the pandemic-free period, perhaps restoring plausibility to the parallel-trends assumption.  
   - Explore a “doubly-residualized” specification: residualize county employment changes on pre-2021 trends or include flexible county-specific time trends estimated from the pre-period. While such approaches have limitations, they may help demonstrate robustness if the key coefficient remains stable after removing pre-existing slopes.  
   - Investigate an instrumental-variable approach that uses the mechanical link between poverty rates and SNAP benefit increases but instruments actual poverty–post interactions with (for example) SNAP policy-driven eligibility expansions that vary only by county-specific criteria (such as state-level SNAP policy differences prior to the pandemic). This could help isolate variation orthogonal to COVID recovery.

2. **Improved Treatment Proxy and Exposure Measures**  
   - Merge in actual SNAP participation or benefit data at the county level if possible—2019 SNAP participation counts are often publicly available via FNS administrative tables. Even partial data (e.g., state-level rates disaggregated by demographic group) could help validate the poverty proxy.  
   - Provide scatterplots or correlation matrices showing how 2019 poverty rates forecast SNAP caseloads, benefit amounts, and the magnitude of the October 2021 benefit increase. This would contextualize the treatment intensity variable and reassure readers that high-poverty counties indeed received a larger SNAP income bump.  
   - Consider constructing a “SNAP benefit increase per capita” variable by multiplying the county poverty rate by the per-person benefit change (possibly adjusting for household size). This could improve interpretability and allow effect sizes to be expressed in dollars of increased benefits rather than abstract poverty-percent interactions.

3. **Event Study Transparency and Structural Interpretation**  
   - Expand the event study discussion by plotting the coefficients (with confidence intervals) and overlaying key labor market milestones (e.g., UI expiration dates, major reopening phases). This would help readers visually assess the persistence of pre-trends and the timing of post-treatment convergence.  
   - Estimate separate event studies for counties above/below the median poverty rate or for counties in different regions (e.g., South vs. Northeast) to check whether the pre-trends are isolated to specific subsets. If heterogeneous patterns exist, tailoring the analysis to the subset with stable pre-trends may yield cleaner identification.  
   - The paper currently interprets the DiD as conflating COVID recovery with policy effects. Consider formally estimating how much of the gap can be attributed to COVID by controlling for county-level COVID case/death rates, mobility data, or stimulus payments (if available). Accounting for these can reduce omitted variable bias while still leveraging the targeted policy variation.

4. **Mechanism and Flow Analysis Enhancements**  
   - Instead of (or in addition to) the log-level regressions on hires and separations, examine margin rates (e.g., hiring rate = hires/employment) to account for the scaling effect of the base employment level. This could reveal more nuanced responses, particularly if the employment effect is small but flow rates change proportionally.  
   - Use quantile regression or distributional analysis to see whether the SNAP benefit change disproportionately affects smaller firms or firms with higher turnover. For example, food service establishments with small payrolls might adjust differently than large restaurant chains.  
   - If feasible, incorporate the “firm dynamics” variables mentioned in the manifest (FrmJbGn, FrmJbLs) to see whether the SNAP shock influences firm entry/exit in low-wage industries, offering alternative pathways for reallocation even absent worker-level flow changes.

5. **Policy Discussion and Interpretation**  
   - The conclusion currently leans toward “identification failed; these are associations.” It would be helpful to explicitly state what policy takeaways (if any) can still be drawn: for instance, does the observed negative association (even if not causal) suggest that policymakers should monitor industry composition when raising benefits, or is it simply an artifact of pandemic dynamics? Clarifying this would help readers understand the practical relevance.  
   - Discuss how future data (e.g., post-2023 quarters, SNAP administrative data) could be incorporated to revisit the question. The paper rightly highlights the challenge of the pandemic era, but offering a concrete roadmap (such as exploiting future benefit recalibrations or administrative data releases) would make the contribution more forward-looking.  
   - Since the paper emphasizes the “reallocation dividend,” consider linking the empirical findings to theoretical reservation-wage models. Even if the data cannot identify the mechanism, articulating the expected pattern of employment, hires, separations, and wages under the reallocation scenario versus the withdrawal scenario would provide a clearer benchmark for evaluating the evidence.

Overall, this paper tackles an important question with rich data, but the identification challenges arising from COVID-era confounders and the proxying of SNAP exposure need sharper resolution. Strengthening the treatment measurement, clarifying the causal narrative, and enhancing the mechanism analysis would substantially improve the credibility and usefulness of the results.
