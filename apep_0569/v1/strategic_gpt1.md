## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple, policy-relevant question: when a country suffers a large devaluation, do all imports collapse equally, or are imports that sustain production—intermediate inputs and capital goods—protected relative to consumer goods? Using Egypt’s 2016 devaluation, the paper argues that import compression is structured along the value chain: final goods get squeezed more, while production-related imports prove more resilient.

Why should a busy economist care? Because devaluations are usually discussed as blunt macro shocks, but the paper’s core claim is that they reallocate scarcity within the import basket in a systematic way—with implications for production, pass-through, welfare incidence, and industrial policy.

**Does the paper itself articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Mostly yes, but not optimally. The current opening is vivid and readable, but it leans too hard into a stylized anecdote and too quickly into what standard models “predict,” before clearly stating the empirical fact and why it matters. The paper’s strongest selling point is not “I have a DiD on Egypt,” but “devaluations do not compress imports uniformly; they preserve production inputs more than consumption goods.” That should be the opening fact and framing.

### The pitch the paper should have

“A central question in open-economy macro is what devaluations actually do to import demand. Standard treatments often model devaluation as a broad increase in the domestic price of imports, implying a generalized contraction in import spending. But in real economies, imported goods are not equally substitutable: consumer goods can often be postponed or replaced, while imported intermediates and capital equipment may be necessary to keep production running.

This paper shows that Egypt’s 2016 devaluation generated not uniform import compression, but a systematic reordering of the import basket along the value chain. Using product-level trade data, I find that capital goods and intermediate inputs were substantially more resilient than final consumption goods, with adjustment occurring through lower import values rather than loss of product varieties. The broader implication is that large exchange-rate shocks endogenously protect some productive capacity, making devaluations less like blunt import taxes and more like selective reallocations across uses.”

That is the version a busy economist can immediately place.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:**  
The paper shows that a large devaluation compresses imports unevenly across end uses, with capital goods and intermediate inputs declining less than final consumption goods, implying an endogenous “import hierarchy” along the value chain.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest papers?
Only partially. The paper cites several literatures—pass-through, imported inputs, distributional effects—but the differentiation is not yet sharp enough. Right now, the contribution risks sounding like: “another reduced-form paper showing heterogeneous effects of a macro shock across product categories.” That is not enough for AER-level positioning.

The paper needs to be much clearer on what is new relative to:
1. pass-through heterogeneity papers,
2. imported-input/productivity papers,
3. macro papers on exchange-rate shocks and expenditure switching,
4. crisis/import compression papers.

The real novelty is not “heterogeneity exists,” but **heterogeneity by economic use of the good** in a single large devaluation episode, and the implication that import adjustment protects production more than consumption. That is a world-facing contribution if framed correctly.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or as filling a gap in a LITERATURE?
It is mixed. Too often the paper slips into literature-gap language: “this contributes to three literatures.” The stronger framing is world-facing: **When countries devalue, what gets cut first—consumer imports or production-critical imports?** That is a concrete economic question. The paper should foreground that, and only then locate itself in the literature.

### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what’s new?
They could, but not crisply enough. At present, a smart reader might say: “It’s a paper on Egypt’s devaluation showing intermediates and capital goods are more resilient than final goods.” That is decent. But they might also say: “It’s a DiD on import categories after a currency shock.” That is the danger.

For AER, the reader should come away with a sharper line:  
**“The paper argues that devaluations endogenously ration imports by use value—protecting production inputs relative to consumer goods.”**

That is more memorable and more general.

Several possibilities:

- **Stronger downstream implications.** Right now the paper hints that preserved imported inputs may protect domestic production capacity, but it never really shows that this matters for output, employment, export performance, or inflation. The paper’s current endpoint is import composition. A bigger paper would connect this compositional shift to a first-order economic margin.
  
- **A cleaner mechanism distinction.** The paper currently blends two stories:
  1. demand inelasticity of production inputs,
  2. state-led procurement/infrastructure preserving capital goods.
  
  Those are not the same mechanism. The contribution becomes bigger if the paper can clearly say whether the result is fundamentally a market mechanism, a government allocation mechanism, or an interaction of the two.

- **Comparative framing.** The biggest version of this paper would not be “Egypt is interesting,” but “large devaluations systematically recompose imports toward production uses.” That likely requires either more episodes or a framing that treats Egypt as an especially clean test of a broader mechanism.

- **More consequential outcomes.** If the paper linked input resilience to manufacturing output, firm survival, shortages, price inflation, or export recovery, the contribution would move from trade composition to macro adjustment.

At present, the contribution is respectable but still a bit small for AER.

The more interesting conversation is:

That connects international macro, development, crisis adjustment, and production networks. It is a more distinctive and potentially more important framing than “another pass-through heterogeneity paper.”

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Before this paper, the default mental model is that devaluations make imports more expensive and therefore reduce imports. The literature allows heterogeneity, but the standard high-level intuition is still fairly aggregate and blunt.

### Tension
The puzzle is that not all imports are equally substitutable. Production may depend on imported inputs and machinery in ways consumer demand does not. So do devaluations really compress all imports similarly, or do they selectively spare production-critical goods?

A second tension, which the paper only partly exploits, is that Egypt is a setting with both a large price shock and active state involvement in FX allocation. That creates a substantive question: is the observed hierarchy market-driven, policy-driven, or both?

### Resolution
The paper finds that capital goods, and to a weaker extent intermediates, are more resilient than final consumption goods after the devaluation. Adjustment happens on the intensive margin rather than via collapse in product varieties. There is suggestive evidence that foreign suppliers accommodate via lower unit values.

### Implications
The paper wants the implication to be: devaluations protect productive capacity more than we usually think, because import compression is endogenously sorted along the value chain.

That is potentially interesting. But the implication is still somewhat underdeveloped because the paper does not show what this protection buys the economy in real terms.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
Yes, but it is not yet disciplined. The paper has a real story. It is not merely a collection of tables. But the story is trying to do too much: value-chain demand elasticity, pricing-to-market, public investment buffering, welfare incidence, industrial policy, and imported-input productivity all appear. That makes the arc feel less inevitable than it should.

### What story should it be telling?
The cleanest version is:

1. Devaluations are usually treated as broad import-compression shocks.
2. In reality, imports differ in how essential they are to production.
3. Egypt’s devaluation shows that import compression is systematically ordered by end use.
4. This means macro adjustment reallocates scarce foreign exchange toward production uses—whether through markets, policy, or both.
5. Therefore, understanding devaluation requires studying composition, not just aggregate import decline.

That is the story. Everything else should support it.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?
“After Egypt’s 2016 devaluation, imports tied to production—especially capital goods—fell much less than consumer-goods imports. The devaluation reshuffled the import basket rather than just shrinking it.”

That is a reasonably good dinner-party fact.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
Some would lean in—especially international macro, trade, and development economists—because the idea is intuitive and policy-relevant. But many would immediately ask: **is this a general feature of devaluations, or just Egypt under administrative FX rationing and public-investment push?**

That follow-up question is both the paper’s opportunity and its vulnerability.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Likely one of these:
- “Is this market-driven demand inelasticity, or did the Egyptian government simply prioritize those imports?”
- “Does this happen elsewhere?”
- “Did preserving those imports actually preserve output, employment, or exports?”
- “Why are capital goods more resilient than intermediates?”

These are good questions. The paper should anticipate them much more strategically in the introduction.

### If findings are modest or null
The intermediate result is somewhat modest; the capital result is stronger. The paper is smart to center the capital estimate as the robust anchor. But that also creates a story problem: the conceptual framework suggests intermediates should be especially resilient, yet the cleanest result is on capital. The paper addresses this by invoking government infrastructure demand. That is plausible, but it pushes the story away from a general “value-chain hierarchy” and toward a more Egypt-specific procurement story.

The null extensive-margin finding is potentially interesting, but only as a supporting result. On its own it is not a hook.

One of two upgrades:
