## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple, important question: when police forces lose staff, do they just do less of everything, or do they specifically stop pursuing the kinds of cases that require intensive investigation and victim engagement? Using variation in police staffing across forces in England and Wales during austerity and the later police hiring rebound, the paper argues that officer losses sharply reduced charge rates in victim-based crimes, while leaving non-victim offenses unaffected, consistent with a “triage” of justice away from victims.

A busy economist should care because this is not really a paper about police headcount per se; it is a paper about how state capacity degrades under fiscal pressure, and about which citizens bear that degradation. If true, the paper identifies a hidden cost of austerity: not necessarily more crime, but a weaker ability of the state to convert victim reports into actual case resolution.

### Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Not quite. The current opening is decent, but it leads with a somewhat generic causal question—were officer cuts causally linked to lower charge rates?—rather than the sharper, more distinctive claim: under austerity, police do not merely shrink proportionally; they triage away investigation-intensive victim cases. The title promises a vivid mechanism (“The Triage Trap”), but the opening paragraphs underdeliver on that promise and sound more like a standard reduced-form public finance paper.

### What the first two paragraphs should say instead

The first two paragraphs should make the mechanism and stakes unmistakable:

> Modern policing has two margins: responding to incidents and solving cases. When budgets are cut, police forces may preserve visible frontline response while quietly retreating from the slow, labor-intensive investigative work required to deliver justice for victims. This paper asks whether austerity caused exactly that form of institutional triage in England and Wales.
>
> I show that when forces lost officers, charge rates fell specifically in victim-based crimes, while charge rates in non-victim offenses were unchanged. As staffing later recovered under the Police Uplift Programme, victim-based charge rates recovered as well. The central claim is not simply that “more police means more output,” but that fiscal pressure changes what the state chooses to do: it disproportionately withdraws from victim-dependent, investigation-intensive forms of criminal justice.

That is the paper’s real pitch. It is more vivid, more world-facing, and more memorable.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper’s contribution is to show that police staffing cuts appear to reduce criminal justice performance specifically on the investigative margin—lowering charge rates in victim-based cases but not non-victim offenses—thereby framing austerity as a reallocation of state capacity away from victims.

### Is this clearly differentiated from the closest papers?

Only partially. The paper says it contributes to the literature on police presence and crime rates by shifting attention to “investigation quality,” which is directionally right, but the differentiation is still too generic. As written, a reader could come away with: “This is another paper showing police resources matter.” That is not enough.

The paper needs to be much clearer that its novelty is **not** “more police reduce crime” or even “more police improve enforcement.” It is something narrower and more original:

- police resources affect **case resolution rather than incidence**;
- the effect is concentrated in **victim-dependent cases**;
- austerity may induce **organizational triage**, not just lower average output;
- the relevant object is **state capacity in investigative justice**, not patrol deterrence.

That is the differentiator. Right now it is in the paper, but not cleanly foregrounded.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a gap in a literature?

Mostly a literature gap. That weakens it.

The stronger framing is about the world: **When governments cut police capacity, what actually breaks first?** Do cuts reduce visible presence, deterrence, response, arrest, charge, victim cooperation, or all of the above? This paper’s answer is: the investigative processing of victim-based cases appears especially vulnerable. That is a real-world question about institutional behavior under stress. The introduction should lean into that.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the intro?

At the moment, maybe, but not confidently. They would probably say: “It’s a panel paper on police staffing and charge rates in England and Wales during austerity.” That is too close to “another DiD paper about public sector cuts.”

The goal should be for them to say: “It argues austerity caused police to triage away victim-intensive investigations, and it uses the fact that non-victim cases don’t move as a key contrast.” That is much stronger.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Several possibilities:

1. **Outcome hierarchy**
   The paper currently emphasizes charge rates. That is useful, but a bit narrow. The contribution gets bigger if framed as a broader collapse in the conversion of reported victimization into justice outcomes: suspect identification, referral, charge, victim withdrawal, time to resolution, and perhaps attrition across the case pipeline. If such outcomes exist, they would make the “triage” claim much more concrete.

2. **Offense heterogeneity**
   The paper hints at domestic abuse and interpersonal violence, but cannot directly show it. If the authors can actually isolate domestic abuse, sexual offenses, violent crime, or other victim-dependent categories, the paper becomes much more powerful. “Victim-based crimes” is still somewhat administrative and abstract. “Domestic violence cases were disproportionately triaged under austerity” is much more vivid and important.

3. **Mechanism evidence**
   The paper’s mechanism is organizational triage, but the evidence is still indirect. What would make it bigger is showing which cases or investigative stages are cut first: no-suspect closures, victim attrition, slower case handling, lower detective allocation, lower follow-up intensity. Even one or two direct mechanism indicators would enlarge the contribution.

4. **Broader framing**
   The paper could matter beyond crime economics if reframed as a state-capacity paper: when organizations face fiscal shocks, they protect visible/reactive services and cut invisible/discretionary relational work. That travels to courts, child protection, health inspections, tax enforcement, etc.

- **Criminal case attrition / clearance / prosecutorial pipeline**;

The strongest unexpected literature connection may be to Holmstrom-Milgrom style multitask problems in public organizations: when resources tighten, agencies protect measurable or visible outputs and neglect harder-to-measure relational tasks. That would give the paper more conceptual reach.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?

Not yet fully. It is currently having a somewhat familiar “police resources matter” conversation. The more interesting conversation is: **what part of the state disappears first under austerity?** That is the conversation AER readers are more likely to care about.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Before this paper, we know austerity reduced police staffing in the UK, and we know police resources matter for some outcomes, mostly crime incidence or presence. We also have descriptive concerns that cuts led to “demand management” or triage.

### Tension

The unresolved question is not merely whether fewer officers mean fewer outputs, but whether budget cuts selectively undermine the hardest, most victim-dependent part of policing: sustained investigation leading to charges. Did austerity change the **composition** of criminal justice, not just the quantity?

### Resolution

The paper finds that within-force staffing increases are associated with higher charge rates in victim-based crimes, while non-victim offenses show no such relationship, and the patterns reverse in the expected direction during the officer hiring recovery.

### Implications

If valid, the implication is that austerity has hidden distributional consequences within the criminal justice system. The state does not retreat evenly; it retreats from cases requiring time, persistence, and victim engagement. That matters for welfare, equity, the perceived legitimacy of the justice system, and the evaluation of fiscal consolidation.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the bones of one, but it still reads too much like a collection of plausibly related regression results. The central narrative is there, but not fully dramatized.

The missing element is a stronger **tension**. The paper should more clearly set up the distinction between:
- visible policing versus investigative justice,
- reactive response versus case-building,
- universal degradation versus targeted triage.

Right now the narrative is “staffing fell, charge rates fell, and here are some supporting patterns.” The better story is “fiscal pressure forces a hidden prioritization decision, and victims in complex cases lose.” That story is much sharper and more resonant.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?

I would lead with this:

> In England and Wales, when police forces lost officers, they did not just become a little less effective overall; they became specifically less likely to bring victim-based cases to charge, while non-victim offenses were unaffected.

That is the headline fact. It is intuitive, surprising enough, and politically salient.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

Economists would lean in—if presented that way. If presented as “I estimate a two-way fixed effects model of charge rates on officer FTE,” they will absolutely reach for their phones.

The paper’s current framing is too close to the latter. The job is to make the former unavoidable.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Likely:
1. “Which crimes are driving this—domestic abuse, sexual assault, violent crime?”
2. “Is this really about victim disengagement, suspect identification, or prosecution standards?”
3. “Does restoring officers restore investigative performance one-for-one, or is there hysteresis?”
4. “Are forces protecting emergency response at the expense of investigations?”

Those are good questions. In fact, the paper should anticipate them more explicitly, because they reveal what readers actually care about.

### If findings are modest, is the modesty itself interesting?

The main result is not null, but the event-study evidence is more modest than the prose suggests. The paper currently oversells some of the supporting evidence. Strategically, that is risky. Better to make the main fact do the work and present the other evidence as suggestive. A modest but well-framed finding about selective state retreat can still be interesting. A modest finding oversold as a sweeping causal demonstration will not survive contact with serious readers.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

At present, this is not yet an AER paper in strategic terms. The main gap is not just identification; it is **scope plus framing**.

### What is the main problem?

Mostly a **framing problem**, secondarily a **scope problem**.

- **Framing problem:** The paper has a more interesting idea than it currently advertises. It should be a paper about selective institutional retreat under fiscal stress, not just staffing and charge rates.
- **Scope problem:** The empirical package is still a bit thin for the ambition implied by the title and claims. One main outcome, one key contrast, and a modest event study make this feel closer to a strong field-journal paper unless the mechanism and substantive stakes are made much richer.
- **Novelty problem:** Not fatal, but real. “Police resources affect outcomes” is not novel enough. “Austerity causes criminal justice triage away from victim-based cases” is more novel.
- **Ambition problem:** The paper is competent but somewhat safe. It needs to take on the bigger question more directly.

### What is the gap between the current version and one that would excite the top 10 people in this field?

A version that excites top people would likely do at least two of the following:

1. **Show sharper offense-level heterogeneity**
   Especially domestic abuse, sexual offenses, violent interpersonal crimes, or cases requiring repeated victim-police interaction.

2. **Open the black box of triage**
   Show whether austerity changes suspect identification, victim withdrawal, time-to-close, detective assignment, or closure reasons in a way that directly maps onto triage.

3. **Frame as general state-capacity economics**
   Connect to how governments ration scarce administrative attention across tasks, rather than leaving the paper as a UK policing study.

4. **Use the reversal more substantively**
   Is recovery symmetric? Does capacity rebuild slowly? Are there persistent losses in investigative quality even after staffing returns? That could be very interesting and would deepen the economics.

### Single most impactful piece of advice

If the author can only change one thing, it should be this:

**Reframe the paper around a single big claim—fiscal austerity causes the state to triage away investigation-intensive victim justice—and reorganize the introduction and main results so every piece of evidence serves that claim.**

That one change would clarify the contribution, improve the narrative, and tell the reader why this is more than a policing panel paper.
