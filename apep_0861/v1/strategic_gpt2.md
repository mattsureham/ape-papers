## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple but important question: when police forces lose officers, what part of policing actually gets cut? Using variation in police staffing across forces in England and Wales during austerity and the later hiring rebound, the paper argues that staffing reductions did not just reduce visible police presence—they weakened the state’s ability to convert victim reports into criminal charges, especially in investigation-intensive victim cases.

A busy economist should care because this is really a paper about state capacity under fiscal stress. The broad claim is that austerity does not merely shrink inputs; it changes what the state does, pushing public agencies into triage and selectively withdrawing service from the most demanding users.

### Does the paper articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Not quite. The current opening is competent, but it starts too much in “are these two trends causally linked?” mode and too little in “here is the big fact about how state capacity collapses.” It also frames the stakes somewhat narrowly as “criminal justice quality,” when the bigger hook is selective degradation of state capacity under austerity.

The first two paragraphs should say, more directly:

> Modern states do not respond to budget cuts by reducing all services proportionally. They triage: routine or easily processed cases continue, while labor-intensive services quietly deteriorate. This paper studies that process in policing, asking whether reductions in police staffing caused forces in England and Wales to retreat from victim-based criminal investigations.
>
> I show that when forces lose officers, charge rates fall in victim-based cases but not in non-victim offenses that require less investigative effort. The same relationship reverses when staffing recovers under the Police Uplift Programme. The central implication is that austerity changed not just how much policing was delivered, but what kind of justice the state was able to provide.

That is the pitch the paper should have. Right now the title points in that direction more clearly than the introduction does.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### Contribution in one sentence

The paper’s contribution is to show that police staffing changes affect the investigative margin of policing—measured by charge rates in victim-based cases—suggesting that austerity induces triage within public services rather than uniform deterioration.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?

Only partially. The paper says, essentially, “the police literature studies crime rates, I study investigation quality.” That is true, but still a bit generic. The introduction needs sharper differentiation from at least four strands:

1. **Police quantity and crime deterrence/incapacitation**  
   Papers like Levitt, Chalfin-McCrary, Mello study how police numbers affect crime rates. This paper is not about deterrence or incidence; it is about case processing and resolution.

2. **Policing and clearance/detection**  
   There is work on clearances, detective allocation, investigative productivity, and police management. The paper should make clearer whether its key novelty is the *victim-based vs non-victim-based distinction*, the *austerity-induced triage mechanism*, or the *state-capacity framing*.

3. **Austerity and public service quality**  
   The paper hints at this but underplays it. The deeper contribution may be less “another policing paper” and more “evidence that budget cuts shift public agencies toward rationing hard-to-serve clients.”

4. **Domestic violence / victim-centered criminal justice**  
   The paper invokes this literature, but somewhat opportunistically, since it does not directly test DA outcomes. That weakens rather than strengthens the contribution unless handled carefully.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or filling a gap in a LITERATURE?

Mixed, but too often as literature-gap filling. The stronger world question is:

**When the state loses frontline capacity, which citizens lose access first?**

That is much stronger than: “the literature has studied crime rates more than investigation quality.”

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?

At present, maybe, but not cleanly. They would likely say:  
“It's a panel paper showing police staffing is correlated with charge rates, especially in victim-based cases, in UK austerity.”

That is not enough. The goal is for them to say:  
“This paper shows that austerity changed the composition of policing from victim-facing investigation toward easier-to-process offenses—an example of triage in state capacity.”

Right now it still risks sounding like “another fixed-effects paper on police resources.”

### What would make the contribution bigger?

Three concrete ways:

1. **Make the dependent variable more conceptually central.**  
   If possible, move beyond aggregate charge rates to offense categories where investigative intensity is clearly higher and socially salient—domestic abuse, sexual violence, serious assault, repeat-victimization cases. That would turn the mechanism from plausible to vivid.

2. **Show redistribution within policing, not just degradation.**  
   The paper’s most interesting idea is triage. That implies not merely fewer charges overall, but a shift away from labor-intensive victim cases toward easier or more administratively routinized categories. If the paper can document this reallocation more directly, the contribution gets much bigger.

3. **Reframe around selective state withdrawal.**  
   The big contribution is not “staffing affects outcomes”—everyone already believes that at some level. The bigger claim is that under resource pressure, the state retreats first from services that require sustained human engagement.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

### Closest neighbors

The likely closest neighbors are:

- **Levitt (1997)** on police and crime
- **Chalfin and McCrary (2018)** on police force size and crime
- **Mello (2019)** on police and public safety/crime outcomes
- **Blanes i Vidal and Mastrobuoni-type work** on police resource allocation/productivity/investigations
- **Fetzer (2019)** on austerity and broader downstream outcomes
- Possibly literature on **clearance rates, case attrition, criminal justice processing**, though the paper does not engage much with it

There is also a public administration / political economy literature on **state capacity, bureaucratic overload, rationing, and street-level bureaucracy** that seems highly relevant and is largely absent.

### How should it position itself relative to those neighbors?

Mostly **build on and redirect**, not attack.

- Relative to police-crime papers: “Those papers ask whether more police reduce crime. I ask what happens to the cases that do occur.”
- Relative to policing productivity papers: “Prior work studies tactical deployment or experimental settings. I show how a large fiscal shock changed investigative outcomes systemwide.”
- Relative to austerity papers: “Prior work documents many downstream harms of austerity. I show one mechanism inside the state itself: fiscal pressure induces selective retreat from labor-intensive justice provision.”

The paper should not overclaim that nobody has studied investigation quality. That is too easy to dispute. It should instead claim a distinct combination: **systemwide fiscal shock + victim-case outcomes + triage framing**.

### Is it positioned too narrowly or too broadly?

Currently, a bit of both—an awkward combination.

- **Too narrowly** in some sections, where it reads like a UK policing institutional note.
- **Too broadly** in claiming sweeping lessons about criminal justice quality and domestic abuse without enough direct evidence.

It needs a clearer center of gravity: **public-sector triage under austerity, illustrated through policing**.

### What literature does it seem unaware of?

At least four areas:

1. **State capacity / public administration / street-level bureaucracy**
2. **Case attrition / criminal justice processing / clearance literature**
3. **Rationing and queuing under public-service constraints**
4. **Gendered or victim-centered justice system response**—if it wants to keep the domestic abuse angle

There is likely also relevant UK institutional work on police funding formulas, force-level budget adjustment, and criminal justice performance that should be surfaced more systematically.

### Is it having the right conversation?

Not yet. The paper is currently having a conventional policing conversation. Its most promising conversation is broader and more interesting:

**How do governments ration state capacity under austerity, and who gets rationed first?**

That is the conversation that could make the paper travel outside policing and UK public finance.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

The world before this paper: economists know a great deal about whether police numbers affect crime rates, and there is broader concern that austerity harms public services. But we know less about what budget cuts do to the *intensive margin* of public service delivery—here, the ability of police to investigate and resolve reported crimes.

### Tension

Austerity cut police staffing dramatically, and charge rates in victim-based cases collapsed. But many other things were changing too: crime composition, reporting, administrative practices, social trust, prosecutorial behavior. So the puzzle is whether staffing losses actually caused forces to triage away investigation-intensive victim cases.

### Resolution

The paper finds that within-force staffing is strongly associated with victim-based charge rates, not with non-victim offense charge rates, and that charge-rate gaps widen during cuts and partly reverse during the post-2019 staffing recovery. The interpretive claim is that officer shortages produce triage: when forces are stretched, victim-dependent investigations are what get dropped.

### Implications

The implication is not just that austerity reduced policing. It changed the kind of policing delivered, selectively reducing justice for victims who require sustained investigative effort. More broadly, fiscal shocks may produce hidden forms of state retreat that are not visible in headline input or output measures.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the ingredients, but the arc is still weaker than it should be. Right now it reads somewhat like:

- background on austerity,
- panel regression,
- placebo,
- event study,
- discussion.

That is a results sequence, not a fully realized story.

### What story should it be telling?

The paper should tell a sharper four-part story:

1. **Public agencies under fiscal pressure do not cut evenly; they triage.**
2. **Policing is an ideal setting because some offenses require sustained investigation while others do not.**
3. **The UK austerity and recovery periods reveal that victim-based case resolution tracks staffing, while less investigation-intensive categories do not.**
4. **Therefore, austerity caused selective state withdrawal from victim-centered justice.**

That is a much more memorable story than “staffing affects charge rates.”

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?

I would lead with:

**Police staffing cuts were associated with lower charge rates in victim-based cases, but had essentially no relationship with charge rates in non-victim offenses.**

That is the cleanest fact because it carries the mechanism in a single sentence.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

Some would lean in, but not enough yet. Among policing and public finance economists, yes. Among general-interest economists, maybe not unless the paper sharpens the broader message: this is about how austerity changes the allocation of state capacity.

Right now the core fact is interesting, but not yet obviously AER-interesting.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Almost certainly:

**Which victims or which crimes are most affected?**

And then:

**Is this really about domestic violence / sexual assault / serious interpersonal crimes, or is it just an aggregate charge-rate pattern?**

That is where the paper feels strategically exposed. It wants the reader to picture domestic abuse and interpersonal violence, but the evidence stays at the broad victim-based category. That gap matters narratively.

### If findings are modest, is the modesty itself interesting?

The findings are not null, but they are still in a range where the paper must sell interpretation, not just coefficient size. The paper does a reasonable job showing the result is economically meaningful, but the stronger case is not magnitude per se—it is selectivity. The interesting claim is that cuts fall hardest on demanding victim-facing services.

Yes—the non-victim placebo is central, not supplementary. It should arguably be presented as a main result alongside the baseline, not as a robustness check. If the whole paper is about triage, the comparative contrast is not a robustness exercise; it is the design’s core intellectual move.

### Does the reader have to wade too long before learning something interesting?

Somewhat, yes. The paper needs to give the reader the signature fact immediately:
**staffing matters for victim-based cases, not for non-victim offenses.**

That is the hook.
