**RE:** Strategic Positioning of "The Triage Trap: Police Staffing and the Collapse of Victim-Based Criminal Justice"

---

### 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
This paper asks how fiscal austerity affects the *quality* of public service delivery, specifically within the criminal justice system. Using the UK’s massive 2010–2018 police staffing cuts and subsequent "Uplift" recovery, it demonstrates that reduced staffing forces a "triage" where police deprioritize resource-intensive investigations (like domestic abuse), leading to a collapse in charge rates for victim-based crimes while leaving non-victim crimes (like drug possession) unaffected. It identifies a hidden, distributional cost of fiscal consolidation: the erosion of state capacity to provide justice for interpersonal crimes.

**Evaluation:** The paper does a decent job in the first two paragraphs, but it leads a bit too heavily with the UK-specific institutional context (20,600 officers lost) rather than the economic phenomenon. 

**The Pitch the Paper Should Have:**
"When governments face fiscal crises, they often cut public sector personnel to save costs. While the effect on the *quantity* of public services is well-documented, we know far less about how these cuts reshape the *quality* and composition of service delivery through internal resource triage. This paper uses the dramatic contraction and subsequent expansion of the UK police force to show that staffing levels are a primary determinant of whether investigations result in justice for victims, revealing that the 'savings' of austerity are bought by deprioritizing the most vulnerable citizens."

---

### 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**One-Sentence Contribution:** The paper identifies the "intensive margin" of policing—investigation quality—as the primary casualty of fiscal shocks, driven by a triage mechanism that disproportionately harms victims of interpersonal crime.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It differentiates itself well from the "More COPS, Less Crime" (Levitt, 1997; Mello, 2019) literature, which focuses on crime *rates*. This paper is about *clearance* and *quality*.
*   **Framing:** Currently, it leans toward a UK policy evaluation. To be AER-quality, it must be framed as a paper about **State Capacity and Triage**.
*   **A Bigger Contribution:** To make this "bigger," the author should emphasize the **Distributional Consequences**. Does the triage happen more in poor neighborhoods? Does it affect female victims (domestic abuse) more than others? Linking this to the political economy of who loses when the state retreats would elevate it.

---

### 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
The paper sits at the intersection of **Public Economics (Austerity)** and **Economics of Crime**.

*   **Closest Neighbors:** Mello (2019) on staffing/crime; Fetzer (2019) on austerity/political outcomes; Blanes i Vidal & Mastrobuoni (2020) on police response times.
*   **Strategy:** It should "Attack" the over-reliance on crime rates as the sole metric of police efficacy. The "So What" is that even if crime rates stay flat (or fall), the *system* can still be failing its primary mission if it stops solving cases.
*   **Missing Conversations:** The paper should speak to the **Personnel Economics** of the public sector. How do managers (Police Chiefs) respond to shocks? Is the triage an optimal response to a budget constraint, or a signal of a broken production function?

---

### 4. NARRATIVE ARC
*   **Setup:** The UK implements massive austerity; police numbers drop.
*   **Tension:** Does this actually matter for justice, or were the police just "bloated" with excess capacity?
*   **Resolution:** It matters immensely for *difficult* cases. The charge rate for victim crimes fell by half. When the money came back (2019), the charge rate started to recover—but with a lag.
*   **Implications:** Fiscal consolidation isn't just a transfer of resources; it changes the "menu" of justice the state provides.

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong but the "Resolution" is slightly weakened by the short 2016–2023 panel. The paper admits the bulk of the "tension" (the 2010–2015 cuts) happened before the data begins.

---

### 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
At an economist's dinner party, the lead fact is: **"When the UK cut 15% of its police, the chance of a victim seeing a suspect charged didn't just drop—it plummeted by 10% for every 10% of staff lost, but only for crimes where the police actually have to talk to the victim."**

People would lean in because this explains a "broken system" narrative many feel intuitively. The follow-up question: "If they aren't charging people, what are those remaining 85% of officers actually doing all day?" (The paper needs to answer this better).

---

### 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
*   **Front-loading:** The "Triage" mechanism is the most interesting part. The placebo test (Table 4, Column 4) is the "money" result. It should be elevated and discussed much earlier.
*   **Appendix:** The leave-one-out and London-exclusion results are standard; move them to an online appendix.
*   **Expansion:** The paper needs a more rigorous look at the "Uplift" phase. Is the recovery symmetric? If it takes 5 years for a charge rate to fall but 10 years to recover with the same headcount, that's an AER-level finding on "hysteresis" in state capacity.

---

### 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The paper is currently a very high-quality "Applied Micro" paper (think *Journal of Public Economics* or *AEJ: Economic Policy*). To hit the AER, it needs **Ambition**.

**The Single Most Impactful Advice:**
The author must move beyond the "reduced form" relationship and build/test a more formal model of **Agency Triage**. If I have $X$ resources and two types of tasks (Easy/Non-Victim vs. Hard/Victim), how do I allocate them under a shrinking budget? Proving that the police are "gaming" the stats by maintaining easy charges while letting hard ones slide would be a major contribution to the theory of the firm/bureaucracy.
