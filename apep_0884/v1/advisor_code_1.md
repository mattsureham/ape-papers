**Summary**  
The paper studies Geneva’s CHF 23 minimum wage—the highest in the world—by comparing high-bite (mostly hospitality/retail) to low-bite (finance/pharma/IT) sectors across Geneva and neighboring Vaud in a canton-sector-year panel from 2011–2023. A triple-difference specification with canton × sector, canton × year, and sector × year fixed effects yields no statistically significant employment or establishment declines in treated sectors, while the firm-entry DiD shows a modest 4.3 percent reduction. Event studies and robustness checks reinforce the view that the employment margin remained intact even under an extreme wage floor, with the main labor-market adjustment occurring through entry deterrence.

**Essential Points**

1. **Potential sector-specific shocks not fully addressed.** The identification relies on the assumption that, absent the minimum wage, the relative path of Geneva’s high-bite versus low-bite sectors would mirror Vaud’s. Yet hospitality and retail differ from finance/pharma along dimensions that may evolve differently across cantons—most notably exposure to international tourism, business travel, and pandemic restrictions. Geneva’s high-bite sectors, anchored in a global city with many international firms and cross-border commuters, may have experienced a unique COVID trajectory relative to Vaud. Sector-year fixed effects absorb national shocks, but they do not capture Geneva-specific deviations in high-bite sectors, so the DDD coefficient could still pick up a COVID or tourism-related shock that differentially affected high-bite sectors in Geneva. The paper should systematically assess whether such time-varying canton-specific shocks exist (for example, by interacting indicators for COVID-related stringency or tourism flows with Geneva’s high-bite sectors) and demonstrate that the DDD results are not sensitive to excluding particularly hard-hit sectors or periods.

2. **Inference and variation rely on two cantons, raising concerns about precision and external shocks.** Although there are many canton-sector cells, the ultimate “treatment” variation occurs in a single treated canton (Geneva) relative to one main control (Vaud). The triple-difference interacts this binary treatment with a high-bite sector indicator, so there are effectively only two treated units (Geneva high-bite and Vaud high-bite) and their low-bite counterparts. This concentration raises concerns about cluster inference, especially if cantonal shocks (e.g., migration, cantonal policy changes beyond the minimum wage) coincide with the treatment. The paper acknowledges the two-canton issue but could do more: for instance, formalize a permutation test or use wild-cluster bootstrap to verify that the inference is not driven by idiosyncratic shocks in Geneva; show that the results remain similar when adding additional control cantons (e.g., Fribourg, Valais) not only to the regressions but to the event studies; and demonstrate that no alternative canton-level policy change coincided with the 2020 reform.

3. **Firm entry result needs more contextualization and identification.** The reported 4.3 percent decline in firm births is only estimated in a canton-year DiD that aggregates across all sectors. Because UDEMO lacks sectoral granularity, we cannot tell whether the entry decline is concentrated in high-bite sectors or reflects broader cantonal trends. Without this, it is difficult to attribute the entry effect solely to Geneva’s high minimum wage rather than to other factors (e.g., pandemic-related uncertainty, canton-specific business support programs). The paper should either (a) find a way to isolate the high-bite sector dynamics (perhaps by combining sector-specific firm entry proxies from other administrative sources, by exploiting firm size classes if they correlate with minimum-wage exposure, or by showing that entry declines are strongest in tertiary/riskier sectors); or (b) more strongly qualify the causal interpretation and explore alternative explanations for the entry decline (e.g., is it driven entirely by hospitality firms? is it concentrated among firms employing cross-border workers?). As it stands, the firm-entry finding is intriguing but inadequately anchored to the DDD design.

**Suggestions**

1. **Probe the COVID confounder more directly.** The paper argues that canton-year fixed effects purge Geneva-specific shocks, but the main concern is time-varying shocks that differentially impact high-bite sectors (e.g., international tourism restrictions, cross-border commuting frictions, or cantonal aid programs targeting hospitality). Consider interacting Geneva’s high-bite indicator with year-specific controls such as tourism arrivals, hotel occupancy, or vaccination rates if available, or with a measure of border closures. If data on COVID-related support (e.g., state aid targeting hospitality) exist, including these interactions would help isolate the minimum-wage effect. If such measures are unavailable, discuss more concretely why they are unlikely to drive the results (perhaps by showing that Switzerland’s federal restrictions limited canton-level divergence in these sectors, or by demonstrating parallel trends in tourism arrivals across cantons before 2020).

2. **Expand the event-study discussion around COVID.** The event study reports coefficients from 2011–2023, which is quite impressive. It would be helpful to overlay key policy dates (e.g., start of COVID lockdowns, lifting of restrictions) to show that the post-treatment trend does not coincide with pandemic-induced shocks. Additionally, consider reporting the same event study but separately for the “late post” period (2022 onwards) where COVID was waning; if the coefficients are still growing positive, this strengthens the case that the minimum wage—not pandemic tailwinds—drives the pattern.

3. **Strengthen the definition and measurement of high-bite sectors.** The high-bite set comprises seven NOGA codes, but ensuring that this classification remains stable across years is important. Provide descriptive statistics showing the pre-treatment share of workers earning below CHF 23 within each of the selected sectors in both cantons. If possible, plot the share over time to reassure readers that the “high-bite” label is meaningful and not shifting drastically (e.g., due to compositional changes). If available, use the LSE/SESS microdata to compute the share of low-wage workers by sector for Geneva and show that Vaud’s corresponding sectors have similar distributions; this would bolster the parallel-trends assumption.

4. **Explore heterogeneous effects within Geneva.** Even within the high-bite group, there may be variation in exposure to cross-border workers or reliance on local demand. Consider interacting the treatment with the share of establishments employing cross-border commuters (if such canton-sector-level data exist) or with measures of export orientation. Differential effects in subgroups would illuminate whether the null employment outcome hides offsetting increases and decreases; for instance, high-bite sectors more reliant on locals might have absorbed costs better than those serving tourists.

5. **Elaborate on the link between firm entry and monopsony.** The discussion rightly mentions a monopsony-based mechanism, but it would benefit from concrete evidence. If data allow, compare the entry effect in sectors with strong monopsonistic characteristics (e.g., high share of low-skill work, limited alternative employers in Geneva) versus sectors with more elastic labor supply. Alternatively, discuss how the magnitude of the entry reduction (≈115 firms per year) compares with the total stock of new firms in high-bite sectors. This contextualization helps readers assess whether the entry deterrent is economically significant relative to the overall competitive landscape.

6. **Clarify inference choices.** The paper clusters standard errors at the canton-sector level, which is appropriate given the interaction terms, but it would be helpful to include a supplementary table showing results of alternative inference methods (e.g., wild-cluster bootstrap with canton clusters, randomization inference under few treated units). Such exercises reassure readers that the statistical significance is not unduly sensitive to clustering choices, especially given the small number of treated cantons.

7. **Discuss spillovers to Vaud or cross-border adjustments.** Geneva’s minimum wage could induce spillovers into neighboring Vaud (e.g., firms relocating or raising wages in Vaud to retain workers). Although Vaud has no minimum wage, the paper should discuss whether such spillovers might attenuate the estimated effect and how they might bias the results. If data on Vaud-Vaud commuting flows or firm relocations exist, even descriptive evidence would help gauge the extent of spillovers.

8. **Consider price or wage pass-through channels in the data.** While not required for the core question, exploring whether the minimum wage affected average wages or prices in Geneva’s high-bite sectors would provide a richer picture. For example, if data on average hourly wages by canton-sector are available, show whether wages in high-bite sectors jumped relative to low-bite sectors after 2020. Alternatively, if price indexes or turnover data exist for hospitality/retail, examine whether firms passed costs through to consumers. Even if the results are suggestive, they would complement the employment and entry findings and help interpret the welfare implications.

9. **Address policy generalizability cautiously.** The paper concludes that even the world’s highest minimum wage produced no employment losses, but Geneva is a wealthy, highly regulated, cross-border city with a large public sector. Consider tempering the general-equilibrium implications by acknowledging that the result may not generalize to more rural, less competitive markets. A short subsection comparing Geneva’s institutional context with other high minimum-wage settings (Seattle, Hungary, etc.) would help readers understand where the findings apply most directly.

---

Overall, the paper makes a valuable contribution by studying an extreme minimum-wage experiment with rich administrative data and a clever DDD design. Addressing the above issues—particularly those related to sector-specific shocks, the firm-entry identification, and inference—will enhance its credibility and impact.
