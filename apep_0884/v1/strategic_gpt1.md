## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple, attention-grabbing question: what happens when a rich urban labor market adopts the highest statutory minimum wage in the world? Using Geneva’s 2020 introduction of a CHF 23 minimum wage and neighboring Vaud as a comparison, the paper argues that even this extreme wage floor had little effect on employment, while possibly discouraging new firm entry.

A busy economist should care because the minimum wage debate has largely moved from “do modest minimum wages destroy jobs?” to “how far can you push the policy before the classic tradeoff appears?” This paper is potentially about the outer boundary of minimum-wage policy, not just one more local estimate.

### Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Mostly, but not sharply enough. The current introduction does a decent job of stating the institutional fact pattern, but then slips too quickly into literature review and method. The first two paragraphs should lead much harder with the world question: **how high is too high?** Right now the paper undersells its best asset, which is not the DDD design but the policy margin being studied.

### The pitch the paper should have

Here is what the first two paragraphs should say instead:

> Minimum wages have become politically mainstream because a large empirical literature finds small employment effects at the levels typically observed in the United States and Europe. But that evidence leaves the central policy question unresolved: are these small effects a feature of minimum wages in general, or only of minimum wages that remain well below the point where labor demand meaningfully contracts?
>
> Geneva’s 2020 reform offers a rare test of that boundary. By introducing what was then the world’s highest statutory minimum wage in an integrated regional labor market next to a similar canton with no minimum wage, Geneva created an unusually informative case for asking whether an extremely high wage floor destroys jobs, alters firm dynamics, or instead continues the now-familiar pattern of limited employment effects. This paper shows that employment effects remain small even at this extreme margin, while firm entry may decline.

That is the AER-worthy version of the paper’s opening claim.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### Contribution in one sentence

The paper’s contribution is to provide evidence on the employment and firm-dynamics effects of an unusually high minimum wage—arguably the highest statutory wage floor in the world—showing little employment response but some suggestive decline in firm entry.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?

Only partially. The paper names Card-Krueger, Dube, Cengiz, Jardim, Harasztosi, and others, but it does not yet clearly explain **what margin of external validity** it is adding beyond “another minimum wage study in a new place.” The key distinction should be:

1. **Extreme policy level** rather than a routine increase,
2. **High-cost, high-productivity urban labor market** rather than typical low-wage settings,
3. **Administrative evidence on firm dynamics**, especially entry,
4. **Cross-border labor market with commuter inflows**, which matters for theory.

At present, the paper says some of this, but not in a way that a reader can instantly repeat.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or as filling a gap in a literature?

Too much as filling a literature gap. It repeatedly says things like “the literature has sparse evidence on very high minimum wages” and “the literature focuses on employment more than firm dynamics.” That is fine as support, but the stronger framing is about the world:

- Policymakers are now setting much higher wage floors.
- Existing evidence mostly covers moderate minimum wages.
- We need to know whether the low-employment-effect result survives at the extreme upper tail.

That framing is much stronger.

### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what’s new?

A smart economist would probably say: “It’s a border-based triple-difference paper on Geneva’s minimum wage and finds no employment effect.” That is not quite enough. The current paper risks sounding like “another DiD paper about minimum wages” because the introduction foregrounds design details before fully establishing the conceptual novelty.

The colleague should instead say: “This is one of the few papers asking whether the modern minimum-wage consensus survives when the wage floor is pushed to an extreme level. Geneva suggests it mostly does for employment, though entry may slow.”

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Several possibilities:

1. **Make the boundary question central.**  
   The paper should explicitly cast itself as testing the limits of the small-effects consensus. “How high is too high?” is the big question.

2. **Strengthen the firm side of the paper or drop it.**  
   Right now the entry result is interesting but clearly second-tier and underpowered. If the author can deepen the firm-margin story—entry by sector, size, or organizational form, or connect it to local product-market structure—that would materially enlarge the contribution. Otherwise it risks looking like a loose appendix result.

3. **Connect more clearly to theory.**  
   The paper gestures toward monopsony, but too loosely. A bigger paper would say: this setting is especially informative because high commuting flows and dense urban matching create ambiguous predictions. Why do we still see little employment adjustment? That would help readers update economic beliefs, not just catalog another estimate.

4. **Make the external-validity dimension more explicit.**  
   Is Geneva informative because it is an extreme test case or because it is idiosyncratic? The paper needs to argue the former.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

### Closest neighbors

The obvious nearby papers are:

- **Dube, Lester, and Reich (2010)** — border discontinuities and modern minimum wage evidence.
- **Cengiz et al. (2019)** — distributional employment effects and the “small effects” consensus.
- **Jardim et al. (2022)** — Seattle and high minimum wages.
- **Harasztosi and Lindner (2019)** — Hungary’s large minimum wage increase and adjustment margins.
- Potentially also **Dustmann et al.** on UK minimum wage margins, depending on exact citation.
- On market power / monopsony, the relevant conceptual neighbor is **Azar et al.**, though that is not a direct minimum wage evaluation paper.

### How should the paper position itself relative to those neighbors?

It should **build on**, not attack, the modern minimum-wage literature.

The right positioning is:

- Card-Krueger and Dube et al. established that modest minimum wages need not produce large job losses.
- Cengiz et al. clarified that employment effects are small even where wage compression is real.
- Jardim and Harasztosi moved toward higher policy levels and broader adjustment margins.
- **This paper asks whether that pattern still holds at an even more extreme policy level in a different labor-market environment.**

That is a clean progression. No need for faux aggression.

### Is the paper currently positioned too narrowly or too broadly?

Currently it is a bit **too narrow in method and too broad in claim**.

- Too narrow in method because it spends a lot of space on the DDD mechanics.
- Too broad in claim because phrases like “extends the frontier” and “global maximum” are rhetorically strong, but the evidence base is still one canton and one episode.

The sweet spot is narrower and more disciplined: **a highly informative extreme case that probes the boundary of the existing consensus**.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?

It should engage more with:

1. **General equilibrium and local equilibrium minimum wage debates** — especially whether high local wage floors induce spatial reallocation, commuting adjustments, or business relocation.
2. **Labor market power / monopsony** more seriously — not just as a concluding label.
3. **Firm dynamics / entrepreneurship / entry barriers** — if the entry result stays in the main text, it needs a real conversation with that literature, not just a speculative paragraph.
4. **Urban economics / spatial equilibrium** — Geneva is a cross-border commuter labor market. That is not a sideshow; it is central to how one should interpret high minimum wages in rich metro areas.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?

Not quite. Right now it is mostly having the standard minimum-wage conversation. The more impactful framing may be to connect minimum wages to **the limits of monopsony-based policy in integrated urban labor markets**. That is the more interesting conversation.

A stronger AER conversation would be:
- minimum wages,
- market power in labor markets,
- urban commuting/integrated regional labor markets,
- firm entry and product-market competition.

That is richer than “yet another local minimum wage estimate.”

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

The world before this paper: economists have become increasingly comfortable with the idea that modest to moderately high minimum wages generate small employment effects, but evidence gets much thinner when wage floors become unusually aggressive. Policymakers, however, are moving in exactly that direction.

### Tension

The unresolved question is whether the celebrated “small employment effects” result survives at the extreme upper end of the policy distribution. Geneva’s reform is a stress test: if a minimum wage this high still does little to employment, that meaningfully shifts the policy frontier; if not, then previous consensus may have been about modest policies only.

### Resolution

The paper finds little employment response in the exposed sectors, with confidence intervals that rule out large job losses, and some suggestive evidence that firm entry declines.

### Implications

The implication is not “minimum wages never reduce employment.” It is more subtle: **the range over which employment effects remain small may be wider than many economists thought**, at least in dense, high-income, service-heavy urban labor markets. But the margin of adjustment may shift toward business formation rather than incumbent employment.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the ingredients, but the arc is not fully disciplined. At times it feels like a collection of empirical outputs:
- main DDD,
- event study,
- firm births,
- robustness,
- monopsony interpretation.

The paper should tell one story: **Geneva is a boundary test of the minimum-wage consensus.** Every section should serve that story.

If that becomes the narrative spine, then:
- the employment results are the main answer,
- the event study supports credibility and dynamics,
- the firm-entry result is an adjustment margin,
- the discussion interprets what kind of labor market can sustain such a high floor.

Right now the story is there, but the paper still reads somewhat like “here is the design, here are the coefficients.”

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?

“Geneva adopted the world’s highest minimum wage, and employment in the sectors most exposed to it does not appear to have fallen.”

That is the headline. It is genuinely dinner-party-worthy for economists.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

They would lean in initially, because “world’s highest minimum wage” is a terrific hook. The challenge is what happens next. If the author then says, “we run a triple-difference using sectoral bite,” attention drops. If instead the author says, “this is a stress test of whether the modern minimum-wage consensus breaks at extreme policy levels,” attention stays high.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Probably one of these:

1. “So does this mean the employment elasticity is basically zero even at very high levels?”
2. “Is Geneva just a special high-productivity place where firms can absorb it?”
3. “Did firms adjust on prices, hours, worker composition, or entry instead?”
4. “How informative is one unusual city about the broader policy frontier?”

Those are good follow-up questions. The paper should anticipate them.

### If the findings are null or modest: is the null interesting?

Yes, potentially very much so. But the paper needs to do more to make the null feel like a substantive answer rather than a non-result. The null is interesting because the treatment is **extreme**. A null after a small, routine increase is boring. A null after the world’s highest minimum wage is not.

The paper should therefore repeatedly emphasize:
- this is not a failed attempt to find an effect;
- the economically important fact is that the effect is small **despite** the extremity of the policy.

That is the value proposition.

Primarily:
