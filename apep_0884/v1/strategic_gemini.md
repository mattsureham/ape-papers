**SUBJECT:** Strategic Positioning of "The World’s Highest Minimum Wage"

---

### 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper exploits a unique policy experiment in Geneva—the implementation of the world’s highest minimum wage (CHF 23/hr)—to test the limits of labor market resilience. Using a triple-difference design across the Geneva-Vaud border, the author finds that even at this "extreme" level, employment remains stable, though new firm entry shows signs of slowing. It challenges the "tipping point" narrative by suggesting that the range of monopsonistic absorption is much wider than previously documented.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this pitch excellently in the first two paragraphs. It immediately hooks the reader with the "world's highest" superlative and identifies the specific gap (the lack of evidence on very high floors and firm dynamics). No rewrite is needed.

---

### 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution:** The paper provides the first causal evidence on the employment and firm-entry effects of a minimum wage set at the global maximum, finding that even unprecedentedly high floors do not cause immediate job destruction in a high-cost urban environment.

*   **Differentiation:** It differentiates itself from Jardim et al. (2022) by pushing the "bite" further and from Harasztosi and Lindner (2019) by focusing on a modern, high-income service economy rather than a transitional one.
*   **World vs. Literature:** It is framed as answering a question about the **WORLD** (what happens at the extreme?).
*   **Newness:** A smart economist would say: "It’s the Geneva paper that shows even $25/hr doesn't kill jobs, but it might stop new shops from opening."
*   **Bigger Contribution:** To make this bigger, the paper needs to move beyond "another null employment result" and lean harder into the **mechanism of entry deterrence**. Why does entry fall but exit not rise? This asymmetry is the most theoretically interesting part of the paper.

---

### 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

*   **Neighbors:** Card & Krueger (1994); Cengiz et al. (2019); Jardim et al. (2022); Azar et al. (2024).
*   **Strategy:** It should **synthesize** the "New Minimum Wage" literature. It shouldn't just be "another data point"; it should be the "stress test" that validates the monopsony model at the boundary.
*   **Narrow vs. Broad:** Currently positioned well—broad enough to interest anyone following the "Fight for $15" or labor power debates.
*   **Unexpected Connection:** It should speak more to the **Industrial Organization** literature on "limit pricing" and "entry barriers." If the minimum wage acts as a barrier to entry, it's essentially a regulation that protects incumbents. This is a powerful, under-discussed political economy angle.

---

### 4. NARRATIVE ARC

*   **Setup:** Minimum wages are generally fine at low levels, but economists fear a "cliff" at high levels.
*   **Tension:** Geneva jumped off that cliff (CHF 23). Did they fall?
*   **Resolution:** Employment didn't budge (the "dog that didn't bark").
*   **Implications:** Monopsony power is deeper than we thought, but the cost may be a less dynamic, more incumbent-skewed firm landscape.

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong. However, the "Resolution" section on firm dynamics (Section 5.3) feels like a "side dish." To be an AER paper, the "Entry vs. Exit" asymmetry needs to be the "Second Act" of the story, not a robustness check.

---

### 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

*   **Lead Fact:** "Geneva passed a $25 minimum wage and employment actually went *up* (insignificantly) by 4%."
*   **Reaction:** Lean in. The number is provocative.
*   **Follow-up:** "How did the restaurants survive? Did they just raise prices?" (The paper is currently silent on prices, which is its biggest "So What?" weakness).

---

### 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

*   **Section 5.3 (Firm Dynamics):** This is the "hidden gem." It is currently based on a simpler DiD because of data limitations. The author needs to do everything possible to bring this to the level of the employment analysis. Even if NOGA-2 data isn't available for entry/exit, can they use STATENT to proxy "net entry" at the sector level?
*   **Front-loading:** The paper is well-structured.
*   **Appendix:** The "Standardized Effect Sizes" (Table 6) is very helpful for the "So What?" test and should remain prominent.

---

### 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The gap between "Solid Applied Paper" and "AER Paper" here is **Mechanisms**. A null employment result at a high level is a "neat fact," but the AER wants to know **why**.

**Single Biggest Improvement:** The author must address **Price Pass-Through** or **Profit Compression**. If wages went up 20% for the bottom decile and employment stayed flat, the money came from somewhere (Consumers, Owners, or Productivity). Without at least *some* data on prices (even if hand-scraped from web menus or CPI sub-indices) or a more rigorous exploration of the "Entry Deterrence" theory, the paper remains a high-quality "measurement" piece rather than a landmark "theory-testing" piece.

### Strategic Assessment

*   **Current framing quality:** Compelling
*   **Contribution clarity:** Crystal clear
*   **Literature positioning:** Well-positioned
*   **Narrative arc:** Strong
*   **AER distance:** Medium (Needs more on "Why/How" firms absorbed the cost)
*   **Single biggest improvement:** Elevate the firm-entry asymmetry from a "suggestive result" to a central theoretical pillar, ideally supported by some proxy for price pass-through.
