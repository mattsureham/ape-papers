### 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
This paper asks whether the death of Swiss banking secrecy—specifically the transition to the Common Reporting Standard (CRS)—actually led to the expected flight of capital. By exploiting the staggered bilateral rollout of information exchange agreements, it finds that while a naive model suggests transparency *increased* deposits, a credible identification strategy using only eventually-treated countries yields a precisely estimated null. This suggests that while total offshore wealth in Switzerland may be declining, the policy did not trigger differential capital flight among participating nations, likely because it deterred new inflows rather than displacing existing stocks.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this well in the abstract, but the first two paragraphs of the introduction are a bit "textbook." They spend too much time on the history of 1934 laws and not enough on the specific empirical tension.

**The Pitch the Paper Should Have:**
"Since 2017, Switzerland has dismantled a century of banking secrecy by automatically sharing account data with foreign tax authorities. While theory and aggregate trends suggest a mass exodus of offshore wealth, this paper shows that the bilateral implementation of these rules had zero impact on the composition of Swiss liabilities. I demonstrate that previous findings of large effects are likely artifacts of comparing expanding OECD economies to stagnant developing nations, providing a precisely estimated null that shifts the conversation from capital flight to the deterrence of new inflows."

---

### 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**The Contribution:** The paper documents a precisely estimated null effect of AEOI on bilateral bank liabilities and provides a methodological warning that previous "successes" in this literature may be driven by inappropriate control groups.

*   **Differentiation:** It directly challenges Johannesen and Zucman (2014) and Casi et al. (2020). While those papers find 20–30% drops, this paper argues those results vanish once you compare "like with like" (eventually-treated units).
*   **Question vs. Literature:** It is currently framed more as a "filling a gap/correcting a mistake" paper (Literature-focused). To be AER-worthy, it needs to be more "World-focused": Why do people *not* move their money when the taxman starts watching?
*   **What would make it bigger?** To move beyond "it's another DiD paper," the author needs to explore the *reallocation* mechanism. If the money didn't leave Switzerland, did it move from "named" accounts to shell companies/trusts within Switzerland? A null result on liabilities is only half the story; the AER reader wants to know where the tax evasion went.

---

### 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
The paper sits at the intersection of public economics (tax evasion) and international finance.

*   **Closest Neighbors:** Zucman (2013), Johannesen & Zucman (2014a), Casi et al. (2020), and the de Chaisemartin & D'Haultfœuille (2020) econometrics literature.
*   **Strategy:** It is currently "attacking" the literature by calling previous results "artifacts." This is bold but risky. It should pivot to "reconciling": acknowledge aggregate declines but explain the lack of bilateral variation.
*   **Missing Conversations:** The paper is silent on the *political economy* of why Switzerland chose this staggered order. Was the order of treaty signing endogenous to the amount of "dirty money" from those countries?

---

### 4. NARRATIVE ARC
*   **Setup:** Switzerland was the world’s tax haven; the G20 forced it to open its books.
*   **Tension:** Initial data shows a "transparency dividend" (deposits going up!), which contradicts every economic intuition.
*   **Resolution:** This is a "control group mirage." Using proper econometrics, the effect is actually zero.
*   **Implications:** The "Stock vs. Flow" argument. The policy stopped new money but didn't move the old money.

**Evaluation:** The narrative is strong but "methodology-heavy." The "resolution" feels a bit like a cold shower. To improve the arc, the "Implications" section needs to be much more ambitious. Does this mean AEOI is a failure, or a success that worked through a different channel?

---

### 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
*   **Dinner Party Fact:** "When Switzerland ended banking secrecy, the countries that signed up first actually saw their deposits *increase* relative to the rest of the world—but only because the rest of the world was Syria and Myanmar."
*   **Reaction:** Economists will lean in for the "spurious result" debunking, which is a popular trope.
*   **Follow-up:** "So where did the money go?" If the author can't answer that, the interest might fade. A null result in a vacuum is hard to sell for the AER unless it's a "Big Null" that overturns a major policy assumption.

---

### 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
*   **Front-loading:** The "Control Group Problem" (Section 5.1) is the heart of the paper. It should be mentioned even earlier, perhaps with a motivating figure in the intro showing the raw trends of OECD vs. Non-OECD countries.
*   **Appendix:** The "Wave Decomposition" (Table 5) is actually very interesting and should be elevated to the main text. It shows the "effect" is only in the first wave.

---

### 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The gap is **Ambition.** Right now, this is a very high-quality "Comment" or a "Journal of Public Economics" paper. To be an AER paper, it needs to solve the puzzle it creates.

**The Single Most Impactful Advice:**
Don't just show that the effect is zero; explain *why* it is zero despite the massive change in the cost of evasion. You suggest it might be "deterrence of new inflows"—find a way to test that (e.g., look at the age of accounts or the volatility of flows vs. stocks). If you can prove that the policy killed the "flow" while the "stock" stayed put, you have an AER-level insight into the stickiness of offshore wealth.
