# Referee Report

## 1. Idea Fidelity

The paper partially pursues the original manifest but omits two critical components. First, the manifest explicitly promised downstream analysis of Swiss financial sector employment (BFS NOGA 64) and bank exits (SNB banking statistics). These are entirely absent—the paper stops at bilateral deposit effects without tracing consequences for the real economy. Second, the manifest hypothesized that foreign deposits would *fall* and employment would *contract*, testing whether secrecy reforms actually reduce hidden wealth. The paper finds the opposite (deposits increased 35%), which is scientifically valuable but represents a substantive departure from the original research question. The data source (BIS LBS) and identification strategy (staggered DiD with CS-DiD) match the manifest well. However, the sample period starts in 2010 rather than 2005 as indicated, reducing pre-treatment periods from "12+ years" to approximately 7 years for Wave 1 countries.

## 2. Summary

This paper exploits staggered activation of Switzerland's Automatic Exchange of Information (AEOI) agreements to estimate effects on bilateral cross-border bank deposits using BIS Locational Banking Statistics. The central finding—that transparency increased rather than decreased deposits by approximately 35%—challenges the dominant narrative in tax transparency literature and suggests a "formalization dividend" where legitimate deposits grew faster than secret deposits shrank. The analysis is methodologically sound with appropriate use of Callaway-Sant'Anna estimators and robustness checks, though the mechanism remains partially speculative.

## 3. Essential Points

**1. Control Group Validity and Parallel Trends.** The identification strategy relies on never-treated countries (136 jurisdictions) as controls for early-treated EU/OECD countries. This is problematic: countries that never signed AEOI agreements are systematically different from those that did (many are tax havens, developing nations, or jurisdictions with minimal Swiss banking relationships). The paper acknowledges Wave 1 drives results (Table 4), yet Wave 1 countries (EU/OECD) are compared against a control group that includes fundamentally different jurisdictions. The event study shows flat pre-trends, but this tests only whether trends were parallel *conditional on fixed effects*, not whether the control group provides a valid counterfactual. The authors should either: (a) restrict the control group to countries that eventually adopted AEOI but later (using only variation in timing among treated units), or (b) provide stronger evidence that never-treated countries would have followed similar trajectories absent treatment. A synthetic control approach for key bilateral relationships (e.g., Switzerland-Germany) could strengthen causal claims.

**2. Reclassification Versus Real Inflows.** The paper briefly acknowledges that increases may reflect "reclassification rather than new inflows" if deposits previously booked through intermediaries now appear in bilateral data. This is not a minor caveat—it fundamentally affects interpretation. If AEOI caused German depositors to move funds from Luxembourg subsidiaries to direct Swiss accounts, BIS bilateral data would show increased Switzerland-Germany liabilities without any actual capital inflow to Switzerland. The authors need to address this more rigorously. One approach: examine whether total Swiss foreign liabilities (aggregated across all counterparties) increased, or whether the effect is purely compositional. Another: use SNB data on ultimate beneficial owner nationality versus immediate counterparty to distinguish reclassification from real flows. Without this, the "transparency dividend" claim remains ambiguous.

**3. Missing Downstream Analysis.** The manifest promised analysis of employment and bank exits, which would have completed the causal chain from policy to real economic consequences. The paper's finding that deposits increased is interesting, but without showing whether this translated into sustained banking sector health (or whether employment still contracted despite deposit growth), the policy implications are incomplete. The introduction cites BFS employment dropping 11% (2014-2018), yet this is not integrated into the empirical analysis. At minimum, the authors should add a section correlating bilateral deposit changes with canton-level banking employment, or explain why this downstream analysis was abandoned. For an AER: Insights paper, the contribution feels incomplete without connecting financial flows to real outcomes.

## 4. Suggestions

**Mechanism Evidence.** The formalization channel is plausible but needs direct support. Consider adding: (1) decomposition by account type if available (retail vs. corporate deposits may respond differently to transparency); (2) analysis of whether the effect is larger for countries with stronger pre-existing enforcement capacity (where hiding wealth was costlier even before AEOI); (3) examination of Swiss bank stock prices around AEOI announcement dates to see whether markets anticipated positive or negative effects. The EU interaction is suggestive but could reflect many confounding factors (Eurozone crisis recovery, bilateral treaties, etc.).

**Heterogeneity by Bank Type.** The BIS data aggregates across all Swiss banks. However, private banks (historically the secrecy beneficiaries) may have responded differently than large universal banks (UBS, Credit Suisse). If data permits, separate analysis by bank category would illuminate whether the transparency dividend accrued broadly or concentrated among specific institutions. The SNB banking statistics mentioned in the manifest (foreign-controlled banks 82→60) suggest meaningful heterogeneity that deserves exploration.

**Alternative Explanations.** The paper rules out COVID as a driver but should address other contemporaneous changes: (1) Swiss National Bank negative interest rate policy (2016 onward) affected deposit attractiveness; (2) EU-Swiss bilateral agreements evolved during this period; (3) global wealth growth may have mechanically increased deposits. Including global wealth controls or interacted time trends could strengthen identification. Additionally, consider whether the 2008-2015 period (post-GFC, pre-AEOI) shows different trends that might indicate anticipation effects.

**Magnitude Calibration.** A 35% increase is economically large. The welfare calculation estimates $222 billion across 74 treated countries, but this assumes the effect is additive across countries. Given that deposits are fungible across jurisdictions, the authors should clarify whether this represents new wealth or reallocation from non-AEOI jurisdictions. Comparing Swiss deposit growth to other financial centers (Luxembourg, Singapore) during the same period would help contextualize whether Switzerland gained market share or simply rode global trends.

**Pre-Treatment Window.** The 2010 start date reduces pre-treatment observations relative to the manifest's 2005 proposal. While 7 years is acceptable, extending backward would strengthen pre-trend validation. The BIS data availability claim (2005 onward) suggests this is feasible. If 2005-2009 data were excluded for specific reasons (data quality, structural breaks), this should be explicitly justified.

**Terminology Precision.** The paper alternates between "deposits," "liabilities," and "cross-border banking positions." BIS LBS measures all cross-border liabilities, not just deposits. While the authors acknowledge this, the text sometimes implies deposit-specific effects. Consistent terminology would improve clarity, particularly since AEOI specifically targets deposit accounts rather than all liability types.

**Policy Implications.** The conclusion claims transparency was "an investment in legitimacy," but the analysis doesn't measure compliance costs borne by Swiss banks. A complete welfare assessment would weigh deposit gains against implementation costs. Even a rough estimate (industry reports suggest billions in compliance spending) would contextualize whether the transparency dividend was net positive for the Swiss banking sector.

**Replication Materials.** For an AER: Insights paper, the authors should prepare a replication package with: (1) code for constructing the AEOI activation date variable from ESTV/OECD sources; (2) BIS data extraction scripts; (3) Callaway-Sant'Anna estimation code. The staggered DiD literature has high reproducibility standards, and transparent code would facilitate scrutiny of the wave construction and control group definition.

**Writing and Presentation.** The paper is well-written overall. Minor suggestions: (1) Figure 1 showing the event study coefficients would complement Table 1; (2) a map visualizing treatment waves would help readers grasp the geographic pattern; (3) the abstract claims "0.30 log points" and "35 percent" interchangeably—pick one primary metric for consistency; (4) consider moving the welfare calculation to a dedicated subsection rather than embedding it in Results.

**Overall Assessment.** This is a timely and potentially important paper that challenges conventional wisdom about tax transparency. The empirical execution is generally strong, with appropriate modern DiD methods and thorough robustness checks. However, the identification strategy rests on questionable control group validity, the reclassification issue needs more serious engagement, and the promised downstream analysis is missing. With revisions addressing these points—particularly the control group construction and mechanism evidence—this could be a valuable contribution to the literature on financial regulation and tax policy. I recommend revision and resubmission contingent on addressing the essential points above.
