## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple and policy-relevant question: when states removed Medicare’s physician-supervision requirement for nurse anesthetists, did ambulatory care markets actually change? Its core claim is no: despite years of political conflict over CRNA oversight, the reform did not measurably shift employment, hiring, or labor allocation, suggesting that formal scope-of-practice rules were not the binding constraint.

A busy economist should care because this is potentially a broader point about regulation: high-salience legal rules may be largely symbolic when private institutions, contracting, and organizational norms already determine behavior. If true, that matters well beyond anesthesia.

**Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Mostly, but not optimally. The first two paragraphs are decent and readable, but they pitch the paper as a policy evaluation of a niche scope-of-practice reform rather than as a test of a broader economic proposition: when do formal regulatory constraints matter in labor markets, and when are they shadowed by private governance? The current introduction gets to “this paper tests that assumption,” but it takes too long to elevate the inquiry from CRNAs to a general economics question.

**What the first two paragraphs should say instead:**

> Economists often treat scope-of-practice rules as binding constraints on labor supply, task allocation, and market structure. But in many sectors, formal legal restrictions coexist with private governance—employer credentialing, insurer contracts, referral networks, and malpractice norms—that may determine behavior regardless of what the statute says. A central question, then, is whether removing a formal restriction actually changes the organization of production.
>
> This paper studies that question in a high-stakes setting: state opt-outs from Medicare’s physician-supervision requirement for certified registered nurse anesthetists. The policy was widely understood as a major deregulatory change that should expand independent CRNA practice and shift care toward ambulatory settings. Yet I find no such restructuring. The main implication is that this prominent scope-of-practice rule was not binding in practice: the economics of anesthesia delivery were governed less by formal supervision law than by the private institutions layered on top of it.

That is the pitch the paper should have. Start with the economic question, then introduce the CRNA setting as a sharp test.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
There is an active policy debate over healthcare workforce shortages and scope-of-practice reform. CRNA supervision opt-outs were treated as meaningful deregulatory changes that should expand provider autonomy and ambulatory capacity.

### Tension
Despite the salience of the debate, it is unclear whether the federal supervision rule was ever the true constraint on behavior. The legal rule may have mattered far less than hospitals, physician groups, and payers.

### Resolution
The paper finds no meaningful changes in ambulatory employment, hiring, or labor allocation after opt-out.

### Implications
The implication is not just “this reform didn’t work,” but “formal deregulation may do little when the real constraints are private and organizational.” That should alter how economists think about scope-of-practice reform and perhaps regulatory reform more broadly.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
It has the bones of one, yes. The phrase “supervision illusion” is useful branding. But the arc is not fully realized because the paper oscillates between:
- a narrow reduced-form evaluation of a regulation,
- a larger claim about symbolic law versus binding institutions.

So at present it is **closer to a collection of well-organized results with an interpretive label** than a fully convincing story.

### What story should it be telling?
The story should be:

1. **Everyone thought this rule mattered.**
2. **If it mattered, ambulatory care should have reorganized after removal.**
3. **It did not.**
4. **Therefore the legal rule was not the operative margin; organizational and contractual constraints were.**
5. **This explains why some scope-of-practice reforms look big on paper but are inert in practice.**

That is the narrative. Right now, elements 1–3 are strong; elements 4–5 are asserted rather than fully developed.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?
I would lead with:

> “States spent two decades fighting over whether nurse anesthetists needed physician supervision, but when states dropped the federal supervision requirement, ambulatory care labor markets basically didn’t move.”

That is a good lead. People will understand the intuition immediately.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
Some would lean in, because the result is surprising and the policy debate is real. But many would quickly ask: “Is that because the policy wasn’t binding, or because your outcome is too aggregated to see the effect?” That is the obvious and important follow-up.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Almost certainly:

> “Do you see this in occupation-specific anesthesia measures, or only in broad BA+ ambulatory employment?”

And then:

> “What evidence do you have that hospitals/payers/private rules are what actually blocked adjustment?”

Those are exactly the questions the paper needs to anticipate more forcefully in framing.

### If the findings are null or modest: is the null interesting?
Yes, potentially very much so. But nulls become interesting only when the paper convinces the reader that:
1. people expected a real effect,
2. the test is appropriately aligned with the prediction,
3. the null updates beliefs about the world.

This paper gets (1) mostly right. It gets (3) somewhat right. It is weaker on (2), because the main outcome is broad enough that readers may worry the null says more about measurement than about economic irrelevance.

So the null is potentially interesting, but the paper has not yet fully earned it as a field-shifting null. Right now it is an intriguing null with a plausible interpretation.
