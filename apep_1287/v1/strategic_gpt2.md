## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether a major climate disaster—the 1927 Mississippi Flood—ended up helping some Black sharecroppers escape a coercive low-mobility labor system by forcing them to move. Using linked census records, it argues that flood-induced displacement pushed Black farm workers out of agriculture and into better occupations, suggesting that forced mobility can raise long-run economic status when people are trapped in locally oppressive institutions.

Busy economists should care because this is potentially a paper about more than one historical event: it sits at the intersection of migration, race, labor coercion, and climate displacement, and it could speak to a broad question about when mobility shocks are welfare-improving rather than destructive.

**Does the paper articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Not quite. The opening is reasonably strong, but the pitch is still split between three possible papers:

1. a Great Migration paper,  
2. a climate displacement paper, and  
3. a sharecropping / racial coercion paper.

The paper’s strongest version is not “another historical migration paper using a disaster shock.” It is: **forced displacement can improve economic trajectories when it breaks institutionally enforced immobility.** The introduction gets near that idea, but it doesn’t quite put it front and center soon enough or sharply enough.

### The pitch the paper should have

Here is the first-two-paragraph pitch the paper should be making:

> Many workers do not remain in low-productivity places because those places are good for them; they remain because institutions make exit difficult. This paper studies whether a large exogenous shock can break that kind of mobility trap. I examine Black sharecroppers in the Mississippi Delta, where debt peonage, racial violence, and labor coercion tied workers to agriculture in the decades before the Great Migration.
>
> I show that the 1927 Mississippi Flood forced a subset of these workers to move, and that those displaced workers experienced substantial occupational upgrading over the following decade. The paper’s core claim is not simply that migration helped Black southerners, which we already broadly know, but that **forced displacement can be economically beneficial when it relaxes an institutionally imposed constraint on mobility.** This connects a classic episode in U.S. economic history to a broader question in labor, development, and climate economics: when does displacement destroy welfare, and when does it release people from low-return equilibrium?

That is the AER version. It leads with a general question about the world, then uses the historical setting as the test bed.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:**  
The paper claims to provide individual-level causal evidence that the 1927 Mississippi Flood improved Black sharecroppers’ long-run occupational outcomes by forcibly breaking a racially specific sharecropping mobility trap.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?

Only partially. The paper names some neighbors, but the differentiation is not yet crisp enough. Right now, a reader could summarize the contribution as:

- “Hornbeck-Naidu, but at the individual level,” or
- “a disaster-induced migration paper in the Great Migration era.”

That is not enough for AER. The paper needs to draw a much harder boundary around what is genuinely new:

- relative to **Hornbeck and Naidu (2014)**: they show county-level adjustment and mechanization; this paper claims to show the welfare consequences for the displaced workers themselves;
- relative to **Boustan-style Great Migration work**: this is not about voluntary migration responding to labor demand, but about involuntary migration interacting with institutional labor coercion;
- relative to modern climate migration papers: this is not just “weather causes movement,” but “the welfare effect of displacement depends on whether people were trapped ex ante.”

That distinction is there, but it needs to be made with more force and fewer generic “contributes to three literatures” paragraphs.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or filling a gap in a LITERATURE?

Mixed, but it should be much more world-facing. Its stronger formulation is:

- **World question:** When does forced displacement help rather than harm?  
- **Mechanism in the world:** when people are trapped by coercive institutions, displacement can increase welfare by breaking immobility.

Its weaker formulation is:

- “There is no individual-level paper on the 1927 flood.”

The paper currently contains both. The former is the AER framing; the latter is field-journal framing.

### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain what’s new?

Right now, maybe, but with some fuzziness. They might say:  
> “It’s an IV paper using the 1927 flood to study Black migration and occupational upgrading.”

That is not enough. The answer you want is:  
> “It shows that forced displacement can raise long-run outcomes when people are trapped by racial labor institutions—this is a paper about mobility constraints, not just migration.”

At present, the introduction does not fully force that interpretation.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Several possibilities:

1. **Make welfare, not occupation, the central object.**  
   Right now the paper leans very heavily on occscore/SEI and farm exit. That makes the story feel narrower and somewhat mechanical: “they left farms and got somewhat better occupations.” To become bigger, the paper needs outcomes that more convincingly map into life improvement: urban residence, home ownership, literacy/education for children, occupational rank of children, geographic destination quality, or household composition. Even one or two broader outcomes would help the paper feel less like a coding exercise in occupational status.

2. **Make the mechanism concrete.**  
   The paper asserts “sharecropping trap,” but the evidence shown is mostly differential migration by race and farm exit. A bigger paper would show more directly that the gains came from moving into labor markets with better outside options: manufacturing entry, destination-city characteristics, or larger gains in places with stronger industrial labor demand.

3. **Reframe around constrained mobility rather than one disaster.**  
   The 1927 flood is the event, but the contribution should be about what that event reveals about immobility under coercive institutions. That is the broader lesson.

4. **Contrast with voluntary migrants or non-disaster Black migrants more explicitly.**  
   The paper says forced movers may differ from voluntary movers, but it never converts that into a central comparison. If it could show whether flood-induced migrants look better, worse, or differently sorted than ordinary Great Migration movers, the contribution becomes much sharper.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Before this paper, the conventional view is that migration out of the South improved Black workers’ opportunities, but much of that literature emphasizes voluntary migration and destination pull factors. Meanwhile, forced displacement is usually viewed as harmful on average, and sharecropping is understood as a coercive labor system that kept workers in place.

### Tension
So what happens when a disaster forces movement out of a coercive low-return equilibrium? Does forced migration harm already vulnerable workers because they are displaced under duress, or help them because it breaks an institutional trap?

### Resolution
The paper’s answer is: in this case, forced displacement increased Black workers’ exit from agriculture and improved longer-run occupational status, with no parallel migration response among white workers in the same counties.

### Implications
The implication is that the welfare effect of displacement is not universally negative; it depends on what constraints people faced before moving. More broadly, shocks that relax immobility can raise welfare even if the shocks themselves are destructive.

### Does the paper have a clear arc?

It has the ingredients of a strong arc, but the execution is still somewhat “collection of results looking for a story.” The story is asserted repeatedly (“the flood broke the sharecropping trap”), but the paper does not fully earn that narrative on the page. Too much of the exposition reads like standard empirical packaging: first stage, reduced form, IV, robustness, heterogeneity.

The better story is:

1. **Black sharecroppers were not just poor; they were constrained from leaving.**
2. **The flood generated a plausibly exogenous break in that constraint.**
3. **Those who were pushed out did better afterward.**
4. **Therefore, this episode reveals a general principle: displacement can be beneficial when the status quo is maintained by coercive immobility.**

That should structure the paper from introduction through conclusion.

These are strategic questions, not referee questions, and the current draft does not fully preempt them.

That can still be important, but then the paper should avoid overselling “welfare-improving” unless it can better substantiate welfare. As written, there is a slight mismatch between the grand claim and the evidence shown.

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

### What would make it read better?

1. **Cut the “standard empirical paper” boilerplate in the introduction.**  
   The first-stage F-stat, leave-one-county-out, and balance-test language arrives much too early. That material clutters the pitch. The first 3–4 pages should be question, mechanism, contribution, headline findings, and why they matter.

2. **Move most of the robustness signaling out of the introduction.**  
   “Leave-one-county-out,” “small age difference,” and similar remarks weaken the opening. That is not what makes the paper interesting.

3. **Front-load the conceptual contribution.**  
   The introduction should state clearly that the paper is about **forced mobility under coercive labor institutions**, not just about the 1927 flood.

4. **Shorten the historical background and sharpen its purpose.**  
   The history is useful, but it currently reads like textbook context. It should be more selective and aimed at one function: demonstrating why Black workers were trapped while white workers were not.

5. **Elevate the white comparison conceptually.**  
   Right now the white falsification test is presented as a specification check. Strategically, it is much more than that. It is one of the key pieces that turns this from a geography/disaster paper into a paper about racially differentiated institutions. It should appear earlier and more prominently.

6. **Reorganize results around the narrative, not the econometric sequence.**  
   Instead of first stage → reduced form → IV in a purely technical rhythm, organize as:
   - Did the flood differentially move Black workers?
   - Did it move them out of sharecropping/agriculture?
   - Did those moves improve longer-run economic standing?
   - Why is this consistent with the sharecropping-trap mechanism?

7. **The conclusion should do more than summarize.**  
   It should state what belief changes: economists should stop treating forced displacement as having a sign-known welfare effect; the sign depends on the institutional conditions people are displaced from.

### Are good results buried?

Yes. The **race-specific contrast** is strategically one of the best facts in the paper, and it is buried as a falsification test. That should be a headline result, not a robustness result.

Similarly, the broader conceptual claim about **displacement breaking institutional traps** is present, but the paper treats it as discussion rather than as the main result.

1. **Substantially sharpen the conceptual framing** around institutional immobility and the welfare sign of displacement, or
