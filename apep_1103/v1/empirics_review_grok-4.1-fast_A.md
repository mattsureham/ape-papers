### 1. Idea Fidelity

The paper partially pursues the original idea manifest but deviates substantially from key elements of the identification strategy, data sources, and execution. The research question—estimating cross-system fiscal spillovers from Switzerland's IV reforms (2008 and 2012) on OKP health costs using canton-level variation—remains intact, as do the core outcomes (OKP per-capita costs by canton/year and service type from sources akin to OBSAN/BAG data, 2000–2022). The event study with 8 pre-reform years (2000–2007) matches. However, critical misses include: (1) no two-stage DiD; the paper omits Stage 1 (reform intensity → DI caseloads) and uses a simple interaction of 2009 DI rate (post-reform stock) with Post-2008 as the "dose-response," ignoring promised reform intensity (early intervention/integration recipients per capita); (2) no Bartik instrument for intensity predicted from pre-reform characteristics; (3) no analysis of IV pension/integration spending or OKP premiums from BFS PXWeb, despite being listed as outcomes. These changes weaken credibility relative to the "READY" feasibility grade, shifting from a potentially instrumental-variables design to a basic TWFE DiD vulnerable to trends.

### 2. Summary

This paper examines whether Switzerland's 2008 and 2012 disability insurance (IV) reforms, emphasizing rehabilitation over pensions, spilled over into higher mandatory health insurance (OKP) costs, using canton-level panel data from 2000–2022. Exploiting cross-canton variation in pre-reform (2009) DI pension rates as a treatment dose interacted with post-reform indicators in a TWFE DiD framework, it finds that higher-DI-exposure cantons saw OKP costs per insured rise by ~6.5 CHF per unit DI rate (~2% relative growth for high-exposure cantons), driven by pharmacy, home care, and physiotherapy. Event studies support parallel pre-trends, but results are fragile to canton trends and log specifications, suggesting cost-shifting from IV to OKP rather than a "rehabilitation dividend."

### 3. Essential Points

1. **Endogenous and mismatched treatment dose undermines identification.** The manifest promised a two-stage DiD using reform *intensity* (early intervention/integration recipients per capita, instrumented via Bartik from pre-reform characteristics) as treatment, with Stage 1 validating effects on DI caseloads. Instead, the paper uses the 2009 DI pension stock per 1,000 (~post-2008) as a fixed "dose," interacted with Post-2008. This is likely endogenous (affected by the 2008 reform's early effects on inflows/stocks) and measures baseline disability burden, not reform uptake. Without Stage 1 regressions showing dose → DI reductions (e.g., via BFS caseload data), there's no evidence the dose captures causal reform exposure. Fix by reverting to recipient-based intensity from BFS PXWeb (as in smoke test), instrumenting it, and adding Stage 1; otherwise, the design identifies correlations between baseline DI burden and health trends, not reform spillovers.

2. **Results vanish with canton-specific trends, violating parallel trends in levels.** The event study shows flat pre-trends (good), but adding linear canton trends flips the coefficient from +6.46 (p<0.01) to -0.78 (insig.), implying baseline high-DI cantons had steeper secular OKP growth. This is a fatal threat for a levels DiD with heterogeneous sizes/trends across 26 units; log specs also yield nulls. Authors acknowledge but downplay it—must address head-on (e.g., via synthetic controls, Sun-Abraham estimators, or interactions with pre-trends). Without credible trends control, reject: AER:Insights demands robust quasi-experimental ID matching the short-format ambition.

3. **Insufficient power and inference with N=26 cantons.** Clustered SEs on 26 clusters are unreliable (Cameron et al. 2011); jackknife helps stability but not inference. Small N amplifies fragility (e.g., excluding 2 outliers barely moves β, but trends kill it). Heterogeneity by language/cost splits power further. Essential: report wild clustered SEs (Roodman et al. 2019), randomization inference, or synthetic DiD; if power diagnostics show inability to detect plausible effects under trends, paper cannot credibly claim spillovers.

### 4. Suggestions

**Strengthen institutional narrative and mechanisms.** The intro and background are strong, crisply explaining "rehabilitation before pension" and financing separation (IV payroll-funded vs. OKP premiums)—lean into this for AER:Insights appeal. Elaborate mechanisms: e.g., does 6a reintegration (16,800 target) mechanically boost rehab via OKP-covered physio/home care? Add a simple model (1–2 pages): agents choose DI application/rehab vs. work; reforms raise rehab costs (borne by OKP), predicting positive spillover if rehab > DI health substitution. Use decomposition (Table 3, nice!) to quantify: rehab services explain ~43% of total β—plot shares pre/post by dose tertiles for visuals.

**Expand data and outcomes faithfully to manifest.** Merge promised BFS PXWeb IV spending/integration measures (smoke test confirmed availability 2006–2024) for Stage 1: regress DI caseloads/pensions on intensity → OKP (2SLS). Add OKP premiums (cantonal-level) as outcome—do reforms raise premiums via cost pass-through? Extend to 2024 (manifest says feasible); check balance post-2022 exclusion. Winsorize outliers (e.g., Ticino max=5,890 CHF) or use robust regressions. Compute economic magnitudes better: back-of-envelope total spillover (β × mean dose × insureds ~ CHF X million/year?) vs. IV savings (cite BSV evaluations).

**Refine empirical strategy for robustness.** Baseline TWFE is simple but trend-sensitive—priority: (i) Callaway-Sant'Anna (2021) or Sun-Shao (2021) event studies to handle multi-period stagger (2008/2012 waves); (ii) interact dose with Post-2008 *and* Post-2012 separately (Table 1 col 3 imprecise—stack or use never-treated base). For trends: quadratic canton trends, or group fixed effects (e.g., by language/urban terciles). Placebo more: fake dose (e.g., 2000 DI rate) or pre-2008 "reform" in 2005. Heterogeneity (Table 4): interact dose × high-cost dummy; test if German cantons' aggressive uptake (cite BSV 2012) drives results via triple diff.
