**Idea Fidelity**

The paper largely adheres to the original manifest’s ambition: it studies Ghana’s 2022 Domestic Debt Exchange Programme (DDEP) and its private credit impact, exploits the policy’s quasi-experimental timing, and provides synthetic control and DiD evidence that domestic sovereign restructuring curtailed credit. Key differences from the manifest, however, should be acknowledged. The manifest envisaged a dual empirical strategy: an aggregate SCM and a *cross-bank* difference-in-differences exploiting variation in banks’ sovereign exposure, along with mechanism tests contrasting SME versus corporate lending and evaluating substitution by foreign-owned banks. The submitted manuscript does not implement the bank-level DiD or these borrower/substitution analyses; it instead relies entirely on country-level SCM/DiD and an NPL mechanism. While the synthetic and macro-level evidence is compelling, the omission of the original bank-level design leaves out an important source of causal variation and a richer mechanism exploration promised in the manifest. A short discussion explaining why bank-level data were unavailable and describing what part of the manifest’s planned analysis was not pursued would help clarify the scope.

**Summary**

The paper studies Ghana’s December 2022 Domestic Debt Exchange Programme (DDEP), which imposed a roughly 30% NPV haircut on domestic sovereign bonds held mostly by banks, and asks whether this restructuring caused the dramatic 2023 fall in private-sector credit. Using a synthetic control built from 13 Sub-Saharan African countries and a complementary two-way fixed effects DiD, the author estimates that Ghana’s credit-to-GDP ratio fell between 5 and 7 percentage points (approximately half of its pre-DDEP level) relative to the counterfactual. Non-performing loans rose sharply, consistent with balance-sheet impairment, suggesting that the haircut triggered a “credit desert” by weakening banks’ capital.

**Essential Points**

1. **Synthetic Control Counterfactual is Dominated by Nigeria.** The synthetic control loads 94% on Nigeria, so the estimated counterfactual relies almost entirely on Nigeria’s 2023 trajectory. This raises concern that idiosyncratic Nigerian shocks—not the absence of a DDEP—drive the gap. Even though a leave-one-out exercise was reported, the resulting counterfactual when Nigeria is dropped still places substantial weight on West African peers, and the gap grows larger. However, the paper should show the pretreatment fit and post-period trajectory for the “Ghana-without-Nigeria” synthetic control (i.e., a full “Nigeria excluded” SCM plot). Additionally, if Nigeria in 2023 experienced its own shocks (e.g., naira devaluation, election effects) that pushed credit up, the estimated treatment effect might be inflated. More diagnostics—such as comparing 2023 macroeconomic drivers (inflation, exchange rate changes, monetary policy) between Ghana and Nigeria, or employing a constrained SCM that caps any single donor’s weight—would strengthen the credibility of the counterfactual. Without that, the credibility of the 7.18 pp gap is in doubt.

2. **Pre-treatment Divergence and Confounding Trends.** The event study and DiD indicate significant pre-treatment differential trends during 2020–2021 (COVID period). While the SCM mitigates this by matching on the full trajectory, the SCM’s fit in those years is not shown graphically, and the DiD evidence implies that Ghana’s credit dynamics were already diverging (likely due to pandemic stress and fiscal woes). This reduces confidence that the 2023 gap is purely the DDEP effect. The paper should (a) show the year-by-year gap in the pre-treatment period, (b) report the pre-treatment MSPE ratio (it currently starts at 2023), and (c) if possible, re-estimate the SCM excluding 2020–2021 to see how sensitive the post-treatment gap is to those years. If Ghana’s credit trend would have fallen anyway, the large estimated effect could be conflated with wider fiscal distress.

3. **Mechanism evidence is suggestive but not conclusive.** The increase in NPL ratios is consistent with balance-sheet impairment, but it could also reflect demand-side deterioration or loan classification changes unrelated to the haircut. Because Ghana’s macro environment worsened substantially in 2023 (inflation above 40%, cedi depreciation), those factors could drive higher NPLs and lower credit simultaneously. The paper should, therefore, provide additional evidence linking the haircut to bank-level outcomes, such as changes in bank capital ratios, provisioning, or lending growth in bond-heavy banks. Even if granular data are unavailable, the paper could exploit cross-bank differences in sovereign bond holdings to construct a dose–response within the aggregate framework (e.g., regress annual lending growth on pre-DDEP bond exposure using aggregate sector data or balance-sheet summaries). Without such a concomitant mechanism, it is difficult to rule out that the credit collapse is just another manifestation of Ghana’s broader macro shock.

**Suggestions**

1. **Provide richer SCM diagnostics.** Currently, predictive fit is summarized only through MSPE and the predictor balance table. Please include (i) a figure showing Ghana and synthetic Ghana’s credit-to-GDP trajectories over 2010–2023 (with the post-2022 gap highlighted); (ii) the year-by-year gaps for the pre-treatment period to verify stability; and (iii) a plot of the placebo MSPE ratios over time. These visuals would let readers assess how well the synthetic control tracks Ghana, especially around 2020–2021. It would also help to report the weight distribution when Nigeria is excluded and, if a constrained SCM (e.g., restricting any single donor to ≤60% weight) is feasible, show that the inference is robust to that constraint.

2. **Narrate the donor selection and data limitations more fully.** The manifest expressed the intention to use 15 SSA donors and a bank-level DiD. The main text now only mentions 13 donors. Please clarify in the main body why Cameroon and Ethiopia were excluded (missings? yes, but mention more explicitly) and confirm that no other SSA country had a DDEP-like event. Likewise, if bank-level data are unavailable (private or proprietary), spell out that limitation, as it would help reviewers contextualize why the more granular DiD was not implemented.

3. **Expand the mechanism section.** The argument that the haircut impaired bank balance sheets is plausible but would benefit from direct evidence on bank solvency metrics (capital adequacy ratio, provisioning, profitability) if available. Even aggregated data from the Bank of Ghana’s statistical bulletin—such as total banking sector capital or growth in deposits—could help. If obtaining such data is infeasible, the paper could at least survey existing reports (BoG bulletins, IMF staff reports) showing that capital ratios fell or provisioning rose, and tie those facts more explicitly to the DDEP. Additionally, discussing alternative mechanisms (e.g., demand contraction, fiscal tightening) and arguing why they cannot fully explain the magnitude of the collapse would strengthen the causal interpretation.

4. **Clarify construct validity of the outcome.** Domestic credit-to-GDP is an aggregate ratio; large swings in GDP (due to real GDP contraction or growth) could change the ratio even if credit stays constant. Since Ghana’s GDP growth collapsed in 2023 and inflation soared, the denominator is moving as well. The paper should report the numerator (domestic credit in real terms) and GDP change to show that the credit drop is not just a denominator effect. If data limitations prevent this, an explicit caveat should be added, and maybe a robustness check using credit-to-total-deposits (if available) or parallel indicator.

5. **Address potential spillovers.** What happened to foreign-owned banks or international lenders during the DDEP? Were they able to increase lending and partially offset the contraction? Even if data are limited, referencing sectoral statistics (e.g., Bank of Ghana monthly data on new lending by bank type) would help assess whether the credit desert was generalized. If substitution occurred, it might dampen the net effect; if not, it reinforces the credit desert narrative.

6. **Contextualize policy implications with alternative responses.** The discussion section rightly emphasizes recapitalization, but readers would benefit from contrasting Ghana’s response (capital adequacy easing) with what other countries have tried (sovereign guarantees, targeted lending facilities, or direct recapitalization). This would make the policy lesson operational and provide guidance for countries facing imminent DDEPs.

7. **Revisit inference statements.** The SCM p-value is 0.091—only marginally significant—yet the text sometimes phrases the finding as if it were conclusive. Be precise: the SCM result is statistically suggestive but not definitive, especially given the small number of donor units and the substantial pre-treatment divergence. The paper might also consider a broader falsification strategy (e.g., using other SSA countries that had non-DDEP credit collapses) to bolster confidence.

8. **Enhance conclusion with caveats.** Since only one post-treatment year is available, the paper should be cautious about suggesting long-run policy implications. A sentence delineating that the estimated effect may capture both immediate disruption and the initial phase of adjustment, and that future data may reveal partial recovery, would keep expectations grounded.

In sum, the paper tackles an important question with an innovative natural experiment, but it would benefit from deeper diagnostics of the synthetic control, more detailed mechanism evidence, and clearer exposition of limitations related to donor dominance and pre-existing trends.
