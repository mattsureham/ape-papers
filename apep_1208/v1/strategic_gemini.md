## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper estimates the real-economy costs of domestic sovereign debt restructuring—a growing trend in developing nations—by exploiting Ghana’s 2022 Domestic Debt Exchange Programme (DDEP). Using a synthetic control method, the author finds that forcing a 30% NPV haircut on domestic banks caused a massive "credit desert," reducing the private credit-to-GDP ratio by over 7 percentage points (a 64% decline) compared to a counterfactual Ghana. 

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this clearly. The first two paragraphs successfully move from the specific event (Ghana’s DDEP) to the broader economic question (the "sovereign-bank nexus" and the domestic cost of "preemptive" restructuring). However, the pitch could be sharpened by emphasizing that this is the first quasi-experimental evidence on *domestic* restructuring, which is distinct from the well-studied external default literature.

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution:** The paper provides the first quasi-experimental evidence that domestic sovereign debt restructuring causes a severe contraction in private credit supply through a bank balance-sheet impairment channel.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It differentiates itself well from the "European debt crisis" literature (Popov and Van Horen) and the "external default" literature (Reinhart and Rogoff). It carves out a niche by focusing on *domestic* debt and *developing* economies.
*   **Question vs. Literature:** It is framed as a question about the world (how much does restructuring hurt the real economy?), which is an AER-level strength.
*   **Explanation:** A smart economist would say: "It shows that when a government defaults on its own citizens' banks, it essentially nukes the private lending market." 
*   **Scaling Up:** To make this bigger, the paper needs to move beyond "Ghana vs. Nigeria (Synthetic)." It needs to prove that this isn't just a "Ghana macro-collapse" story. The author mentions bank-level data is unavailable; finding a way to proxy for bank-level exposure—perhaps through annual reports or branch-level activity—would turn a "country case study" into a "seminal mechanism paper."

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

*   **Closest Neighbors:** Gennaioli, Martin, and Rossi (2014) on the sovereign-bank nexus; Reinhart and Rogoff (2011) on the history of domestic debt; and Abadie et al. (2010) for methodology.
*   **Strategy:** The paper "fills a gap" by providing empirical teeth to the theoretical models of Gennaioli et al. This is a safe but effective positioning.
*   **Broad vs. Narrow:** It is currently positioned as a "Developing Economy / Macro-Finance" paper. 
*   **Missing Conversations:** The paper could connect more to the *Political Economy* of restructuring. Why did Ghana choose a domestic exchange? Who loses (the firms that can't get loans) vs. who wins (the government)? 

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

*   **Setup:** Developing countries are increasingly relying on domestic debt.
*   **Tension:** Does restructuring this debt offer a "painless" fiscal fix, or does it destroy the financial plumbing of the private sector?
*   **Resolution:** In Ghana, it led to a "credit desert," where credit-to-GDP fell by 64% relative to its peers.
*   **Implications:** Restructuring without recapitalization is self-defeating for growth.

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong. The "tension" is particularly well-handled in Section 2, where the author explains why Ghana's DDEP was a "natural experiment" driven by external (IMF) pressure rather than internal bank runs.

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

*   **Lead Fact:** "Ghana wiped out 60% of its private credit market overnight just to fix its government balance sheet."
*   **Reaction:** Lean in. The magnitude is "stark" (the author’s own word).
*   **Follow-up:** "Was it the haircut itself, or would the high inflation have killed lending anyway?" (The author anticipates this with the SCM, but needs more robust evidence on the *mechanism* to keep people from reaching for their phones).

---

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

*   **Front-loading:** The paper is well-structured. However, the SCM results (Figure-equivalents) need to be the star. 
*   **Mechanism Section:** Section 5.2 (NPLs) is too short. This is the "AER" part of the paper. It needs more detail on why the credit died—was it capital adequacy (supply) or because the firms themselves were dying (demand)? 
*   **The "Nigeria Problem":** Since the SCM is 94% Nigeria, a whole section should be dedicated to why 2023 Nigeria is a valid counterfactual for 2023 Ghana, given Nigeria's own currency volatility.

---

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The gap is **identification vs. description.** Right now, it looks like a very high-quality "Policy Paper" or a "Regional Journal" paper because it relies on a single country-level SCM.

**The single most impactful piece of advice:** 
Construct a "synthetic bank" or find a source of cross-sectional variation. If the author can show that banks with *high* exposure to the DDEP cut lending more than banks with *low* exposure (even if using a subset of banks or manually scraped data), the paper moves from "a suggestive case study of a country in crisis" to "a definitive proof of the sovereign-bank transmission channel."
