## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple and potentially important question: when the EITC brings low-education women into work, does it also change *where* they work across industries? Using state EITC adoptions and administrative employment data, the paper’s headline claim is that the EITC increases employment but does not meaningfully reallocate women across sectors such as healthcare, retail, and food services.

A busy economist should care because the paper tries to move the EITC conversation from “does it increase work?” to “what kind of work does it generate?” That is a real question about the structure of labor supply responses, not just their level.

**Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Reasonably clearly, but not optimally. The current introduction is competent and readable, but it is still framed too much as “here is an overlooked margin in the EITC literature.” For AER positioning, the first two paragraphs should more sharply foreground the broader economic question: **do work subsidies change the composition of jobs people take, or only the quantity of work?** That is the larger conversation.

### The pitch the paper should have

A stronger opening would say something like:

> The EITC is known to increase employment among low-income women. But an essential question remains unanswered: when work subsidies pull people into the labor market, do they also change the kinds of jobs workers take? If they do, the EITC may affect earnings mobility, sectoral labor supply, and the distribution of workers across low- and higher-wage industries. If they do not, then the EITC is primarily an extensive-margin policy that expands employment without changing the structure of work.
>
> This paper studies whether state EITCs change the industry composition of low-education women’s employment. Using staggered state EITC adoption and administrative state-by-industry employment data, I find that the EITC raises employment but does not systematically shift women into different sectors. The main implication is that one of the largest work-based transfer programs changes how many women work, but not where they work.

That framing makes the paper about the world: what labor supply subsidies do to job allocation.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution in one sentence:**  
The paper’s contribution is to show that state EITCs appear to increase low-education women’s employment without materially changing the industry composition of that employment.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?
Only partially. The paper clearly differentiates itself from the canonical EITC labor-force-participation papers by focusing on industry composition. But “no one has studied industry composition” is not by itself enough. The contribution feels adjacent to, rather than in direct conversation with, the strongest neighboring literatures.

The introduction does distinguish itself from classic EITC papers, but it needs to do more to distinguish itself from:
- papers on occupational/sectoral sorting and job ladders,
- work on labor supply subsidies and job quality,
- work on whether transfer/tax policy changes the *type* rather than merely the *amount* of labor supplied.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or filling a gap in a LITERATURE?
It starts with a world question, which is good, but it keeps slipping back into “an overlooked dimension in the literature.” The stronger version is:

- **World question:** When governments subsidize work, do workers sort into different kinds of jobs?
- **Weaker literature-gap version:** No one has estimated EITC effects on industry shares before.

Right now the paper contains both, but the latter is doing too much of the work.

### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what’s new?
Yes, but probably in a slightly diminishing way: “It’s a paper showing the EITC doesn’t change the sector mix of women’s employment.” That is better than “another DiD paper about X,” but not by a lot. The danger is that the paper reads as a competent extension exercise: take a famous policy, apply modern staggered DiD, check a new outcome margin, get a null.

### What would make this contribution bigger?
Several possibilities:

1. **Reframe around job quality, not just industry shares.**  
   Industry composition is a proxy. The bigger question is whether EITC-induced jobs differ in quality: wages, stability, hours, turnover, advancement, benefits, formal/informal employment. Industry shares are one narrow operationalization.

2. **Show what the EITC does *not* change besides industry.**  
   If the broader claim is “EITC changes employment quantity but not job type,” then add outcomes on earnings ranks, firm quality, job stability, worker age mix, or sector wage premia. This would turn one null into a more general substantive statement.

3. **Connect to worker sorting / job ladder theories.**  
   Right now the mechanism discussion is plausible but generic. A bigger paper would use the findings to say something sharper about whether low-income labor supply responses are constrained by job access, credentials, networks, or salience.

4. **Use within-industry outcomes to sharpen the point.**  
   If women are not reallocating across sectors, are they reallocating toward better employers within sectors? Without that, “same industry mix” may understate meaningful composition effects.

5. **Make the paper about incidence on sectors.**  
   Another route is to ask whether EITC expansions alter sector-specific labor supply elasticities or labor market tightness in female-intensive sectors. That would interest labor and public economists more than simple employment shares.

At present, the contribution is real but modest.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

### Closest neighbors
The closest papers/literatures seem to be:

1. **Eissa and Liebman (1996)** – canonical EITC and labor force participation.  
2. **Meyer and Rosenbaum (2001)** – major evidence on welfare/tax reform and single mothers’ employment.  
3. **Hotz and Scholz (2003/2006 review tradition)** – broader EITC evidence and interpretation.  
4. **Bastian and Jones (2021)** or related recent state EITC papers – state supplements and labor supply/income effects.  
5. On the labor market structure side, papers like **Autor and Dorn (2013)** / **Autor (2015)** are relevant for occupational/sectoral polarization, though they are not especially close in design.

Potentially also:
- **Rothstein (2010)** on EITC incidence and labor market equilibrium,
- **Leigh (2010)** on wage incidence/pass-through,
- broader “job quality” and “sectoral mobility” work,
- perhaps labor market frictions / directed search / constraints papers, depending on how ambitious the framing becomes.

### How should the paper position itself relative to those neighbors?
**Build on**, not attack.

This should not be sold as “the existing EITC literature missed something obvious.” Better: the classic literature established that the EITC changes labor force entry; this paper asks whether those induced entrants are compositionally different in where they work.

Relative to the public finance literature: build on the established reduced-form fact.  
Relative to labor economics: use that policy shock to speak to worker sorting and job allocation.

The attack should be reserved for the methodological side only in a disciplined way: the paper can note that older TWFE approaches would have generated a false sectoral-reallocation result. But that should not become the central identity of the paper.

### Is the paper positioned too narrowly or too broadly?
At present, **too narrowly in topic and too broadly in claimed implication**.

- Too narrowly because it is “state EITCs and industry shares of low-education women.”
- Too broadly because it occasionally implies sweeping conclusions about welfare effects, earnings mobility, and policy design that the evidence doesn’t fully carry on its own.

The paper needs a cleaner middle ground: “This is evidence on whether a major work subsidy changes the sectoral allocation of induced employment.” That is focused but consequential.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of or under-engaged with?
It seems under-engaged with at least four conversations:

1. **Job quality / job ladders / low-wage work**  
   If the paper wants to say something about mobility, it should be engaging more directly with work on job quality and progression, not just sectoral composition.

2. **Occupational choice and labor market frictions**  
   The mechanism story gestures at constraints, but the literature grounding is thin.

3. **Behavioral public finance / salience**  
   Mentioned, but somewhat opportunistically. If salience is central, it needs deeper integration or less emphasis.

4. **Female labor supply and childcare / scheduling constraints**  
   Industry choice among low-education women is plausibly shaped by schedule flexibility, caregiving, commuting, and credentialing. That literature should be more central than some of the current broad industry-structure citations.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?
Not quite. Right now it is mostly having a conversation with the EITC literature and with staggered-DiD econometrics. That is not the highest-value conversation.

The more impactful framing is to connect **public finance** and **labor market allocation**:
- We know work subsidies raise participation.
- Do they change the allocation of labor across jobs?
- This paper says: apparently not, at least across broad industries.

That is a better conversation than “here is another application showing TWFE can be misleading.”

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
We know the EITC increases employment among low-income women. It is one of the most established findings in public economics.

### Tension
But we do not know whether the induced employment is compositionally different. If the EITC changes where women work, then it affects more than participation: it affects job allocation, job quality, and perhaps mobility. If not, its effects are narrower than one might hope.

### Resolution
The paper finds little evidence of industry reallocation. Women work more, but in roughly the same sectoral mix.

### Implications
The EITC appears to be an employment-expansion policy rather than a job-reallocation or job-upgrading policy. This matters for how we think about the channels through which work subsidies reduce poverty and for whether complementary policies are needed to affect job ladders.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
**Serviceable, but not fully convincing.**

The paper does have a setup-tension-resolution structure. The problem is that the **resolution is thin relative to the ambition of the setup**. Industry shares are a fairly coarse object, yet the paper draws implications about earnings mobility, worker constraints, and policy design. That creates a mismatch.

There is also a secondary narrative trying to coexist:
- substantive story: EITC does not change where women work;
- methods story: TWFE would falsely say it does.

The methods story is currently almost as vivid as the substantive story. That is risky. For AER, the econometric point is not new enough to carry the paper, and the substantive contribution is not broad enough to be diluted by a second agenda.

### If it is a collection of results looking for a story, what story should it be telling?
The story should be:

> Work subsidies can increase employment without changing job allocation. The EITC moves women into work, but not into different kinds of sectors. That suggests the margins governing labor force entry are distinct from the margins governing job sorting.

That is a crisp, potentially important story. Everything in the paper should serve it.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?
“I’d lead with: the EITC increases low-education women’s employment, but it does not appear to change the industry mix of that employment.”

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
Mixed. Public finance economists would lean in briefly because it touches a famous policy. Many others would ask, silently, “Why should I care about industry shares in particular?” That is the problem. The finding is respectable, but the object is not inherently electrifying.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Probably one of these:
- “Okay, but does it change job quality within industry?”
- “Does it affect occupations rather than industries?”
- “How precise is the null?”
- “Why should industry shares be the margin we care about?”
- “What does this teach us about labor supply models or worker constraints?”

That is telling: the natural next question is bigger than the current paper.

### If the findings are null or modest: is the null itself interesting?
Potentially yes, but the paper needs to make the case more forcefully.

A null can be interesting when:
1. prior theory or policy rhetoric strongly predicts an effect;
2. the estimate is precise enough to rule out economically meaningful effects;
3. the null adjudicates between competing views.

The paper partially does (1) and (2), but only partially. The theory section gives reasons to expect reallocation, but not strongly enough that the null feels surprising. And the bounds, while useful, are not so tight that they slam the door shut. So right now the null is “interesting enough,” not “decisive.”

It does **not** feel like a failed experiment, but it does feel like a modest finding packaged a bit too aggressively.

   The best fact is already visible, but the paper could be sharper: first page should tell me clearly that employment rises but sector mix does not.

   Event studies, placebo tests, dose response, DDD, etc. are all fine, but from a narrative perspective they produce a defensive, cumulative feel. Referees can decide what is needed econometrically. Editorially, the main text should keep returning to the substantive interpretation.

5. **Demote the TWFE robustness table.**  
   A whole robustness section centered on stability of the biased TWFE estimate is structurally odd. If the point is that TWFE is misleading, one clean comparison is enough. The rest feels like attention paid to the wrong estimate.

6. **Strengthen the conclusion by elevating the general lesson.**  
   The conclusion is decent, but it still reads as a summary. It should end on the bigger takeaway: labor force entry and job allocation are distinct policy margins.

Not really. If anything, too much of the current main text is devoted to methodological comparison. The more important buried point is the paper’s own broader implication: “employment quantity moves, job composition does not.” That should be the organizing principle throughout.

### Is the reader front-loaded with the good stuff?
Mostly yes, but the paper still spends too much time proving competence rather than earning excitement.

### Is the conclusion adding value?
Some, but not enough. It should do more synthesis and less recapitulation.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

Bluntly: in its current form, this is **not yet an AER story**.

### What is the gap?

Mostly a **scope + ambition problem**, with some framing issues.

- **Not mainly a framing problem.** The framing could improve, yes, but better framing alone will not make a narrow industry-share null result into an AER paper.
- **Scope problem.** Industry composition is too narrow an outcome to sustain the broader claims.
- **Ambition problem.** The paper asks a safe, tidy extension question rather than a field-shaping one.
- **Some novelty problem.** The policy and design are familiar, and the econometric angle is no longer novel.

The paper is competent and disciplined. But “first paper on industry composition of EITC-induced employment” is not, on its own, top-journal level novelty. To reach AER territory, the authors would need to enlarge what is at stake.

### What would excite the top 10 people in this field?
A paper that could say something like:

- The EITC raises employment but does not improve job quality, move workers up job ladders, or shift them toward better-paying sectors/firms.
- Or: work subsidies affect entry but not allocation, revealing a major friction in low-wage labor markets.
- Or: induced entrants go to the same industries, same types of firms, same wage ranks, implying policy complements are needed for upward mobility.

That would be a bigger substantive claim.

### Single most impactful advice
**Broaden the paper from “industry composition” to “job composition/job quality of EITC-induced employment,” using industry shares as one piece of evidence rather than the whole contribution.**

That is the one change that could convert a niche extension into a paper with first-order implications.
