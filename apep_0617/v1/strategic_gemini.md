## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
The paper asks whether the Earned Income Tax Credit (EITC), famous for pulling single mothers into the workforce, also influences *which* industries they enter. Using high-quality administrative data and modern staggered DiD methods, it finds a precise null: while the EITC increases total employment, it does not shift the sectoral mix toward higher- or lower-paying industries. This matters because it suggests the EITC’s success in poverty reduction is a function of pure transfers, not a catalyst for upward industrial mobility.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this very clearly. The first two paragraphs effectively set the stakes (extensive margin vs. sectoral sorting) and explain why a null result here is a "dog that didn't bark" for policy design. No rewrite of the pitch is necessary; it is professionally polished and high-signal.

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
The contribution is the first causal evidence that the EITC does not induce sectoral reallocation among low-education women.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It differentiates itself well from the "classic" EITC literature (Eissa & Liebman, Meyer & Rosenbaum) by moving from *whether* they work to *where* they work.
*   **Question vs. Literature:** It is framed as a question about the world—specifically the mechanism of poverty reduction—which is a strength.
*   **The "Smart Economist" test:** A reader would summarize this as "the paper that proves the EITC doesn't change industry sorting." It avoids being "just another DiD paper" by tackling a specific, overlooked dimension of a massive policy.
*   **How to make it bigger:** The contribution would be bigger if it could definitively distinguish between the three proposed mechanisms (salience, constraints, or composition). Currently, it provides a result but cannot fully explain the "why."

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
The paper sits at the intersection of public finance (EITC), labor economics (sectoral sorting), and econometrics (staggered DiD).

*   **Closest Neighbors:** Bastian & Jones (2021) on EITC efficiency; Rothstein (2010) on EITC incidence; Callaway & Sant’Anna (2021) on methodology.
*   **Positioning:** It builds on the extensive-margin consensus while essentially "attacking" the implicit assumption that subsidies might lead to better job matching or "career" tracks in higher-wage sectors like healthcare.
*   **Conversation:** It is having the right conversation. By connecting the null result to the "tax salience" literature (Chetty, Looney, & Kroft), it elevates the paper from a narrow labor study to a broader behavioral public finance inquiry.

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC
*   **Setup:** We know the EITC gets people to work.
*   **Tension:** Does it help them find *better* work (higher-paying industries), or just *any* work? Standard theory suggests they should sort into sectors where the subsidy is most effective.
*   **Resolution:** It finds no reallocation. The sector mix is "sticky."
*   **Implications:** The EITC is a great safety net but a poor industrial policy. To move women into healthcare/higher-wage sectors, you need supply-side interventions (training), not just demand-side subsidies.

**Evaluation:** The narrative arc is very strong. It uses the "failed" TWFE result (which showed a fake healthcare effect) as a dramatic device to highlight why the correct methodology is essential.

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
At a dinner party, I would lead with: "Did you know that despite billions in subsidies, the EITC doesn't change the industry mix of low-income workers at all? They enter retail and food service at the exact same rates as before."

**Evaluation:** Economists would lean in. The follow-up would be: "Is it because they lack the skills for healthcare, or because they don't realize the credit exists when they're job hunting?" The paper anticipates this by discussing salience vs. constraints. The null is interesting because it contradicts a naive "incentives-matter" intuition.

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
*   **Front-loading:** The paper is well-structured. The contrast between Table 2 Panel A (CS) and Panel B (TWFE) is the money shot and it appears early (page 11). 
*   **Appendix:** The standardized effect sizes (Table 6) are excellent for the "informative null" argument and should stay.
*   **Refinement:** The "Institutional Background" (Section 2) is a bit standard. It could be condensed to focus specifically on why the EITC *should* theoretically affect industry choice (e.g., the phase-in slope varying by industry wage).

---

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The gap between this and a "slam dunk" AER is the **depth of the "why."** 

AER reviewers often find pure nulls "descriptive" unless they provide a definitive mechanistic breakthrough. The paper currently offers three plausible theories for the null but cannot adjudicate between them.

**Single Most Impactful Advice:** Use the QWI earnings data or individual-level data (if possible) to test the "Salience" hypothesis more directly—perhaps by looking at whether reallocation is higher in areas with high EITC "knowledge" or higher professional tax prep rates (following the Chetty/Friedman/Saez 2013 logic). If the author can prove *why* the sorting doesn't happen, this becomes a landmark paper.
