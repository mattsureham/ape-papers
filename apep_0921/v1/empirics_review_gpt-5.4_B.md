## 1. Idea Fidelity

Yes—the paper largely pursues the original idea in the manifest. It studies whether state civil asset forfeiture reforms induce agencies to substitute into the federal equitable sharing program, uses the DOJ ESAC FOIA panel as the core data source, and implements staggered-adoption DiD with both TWFE and Callaway-Sant’Anna estimators. The paper also examines heterogeneity by reform stringency and anti-circumvention laws, which were central to the original proposal.

That said, several key elements of the original idea are either weakened or not fully executed. First, the manifest emphasized two margins—more agencies participating and larger payments—but the paper’s agency-level panel is restricted to agencies with at least three years of certification data, which risks eliminating precisely the entry/exit margin that would capture circumvention. Second, the manifest highlighted the possibility of using more granular asset-level data (CATS) to get closer to the mechanism, but the paper relies entirely on ESAC income and cannot distinguish adoptive seizures from joint investigations. Third, the anti-circumvention analysis is much thinner than envisioned, and the coding of reforms appears somewhat inconsistent across the paper (e.g., number and type of strong/weak reforms).

## 2. Summary

This paper asks an important and policy-relevant question: whether state forfeiture reforms are undermined by agencies routing seizures through the federal equitable sharing program. Using agency-year ESAC data and staggered DiD designs, the paper finds little evidence that reform increases equitable sharing receipts and argues that the feared “escape valve” is largely illusory.

The topic is well chosen and potentially publishable in an Insights-style format, but the current evidence is not yet strong enough to support the paper’s headline conclusion. The main concern is that the outcome and sample may be poorly aligned with the mechanism of circumvention the paper wants to test.

## 3. Essential Points

1. **The sample construction may mechanically miss the main behavioral response of interest.**  
   The paper studies “circumvention” partly as increased participation in equitable sharing, yet it restricts the sample to agencies with at least three years of certification data and, more fundamentally, only observes agencies that file ESAC certifications. This creates a serious risk of conditioning on participation in the program and attenuating the very entry/exit response that the paper says it wants to measure. The authors need to show results on a state-year panel that includes all law-enforcement agencies in the denominator, or otherwise demonstrate convincingly that agency entry into ESAC is not the relevant margin.

2. **The paper’s outcome does not isolate the relevant mechanism.**  
   Equitable sharing receipts combine proceeds from joint investigations and adoptive seizures, but the circumvention concern is specifically about adoptive seizures used to evade state law. A null effect on aggregate ESAC receipts is therefore not equivalent to a null effect on circumvention. The authors need either (i) more direct evidence on adoptive seizures, ideally from CATS or another asset-level source, or (ii) a much more careful reinterpretation of the contribution as “no detectable increase in total equitable sharing receipts,” not “no circumvention.”

3. **The paper overstates the precision and strength of the null.**  
   The abstract and introduction describe a “precisely estimated null,” but the Callaway-Sant’Anna estimate is very imprecise, the event-study pretrends are noisy, and the reported minimum detectable effect is still sizable. A design that can rule out only increases larger than roughly 38 percent is informative, but it does not justify the stronger claim that the “escape valve appears to be an illusion.” The framing and conclusions need to be scaled back unless the authors can materially strengthen the design.

## 4. Suggestions

This is a promising paper with a good question, a useful new dataset, and a sensible first-pass design. My view is not that the paper should be abandoned, but that it needs to be reframed and tightened around what the data can truly identify.

First, I would strongly encourage the authors to rethink the unit of analysis for at least part of the paper. The agency-level ESAC panel is attractive, but because it only includes agencies observed in the federal program, it is ill-suited to testing whether reform causes new agencies to start using the program. A state-year design may actually be more credible for the extensive margin: total ES funds, number of filing agencies, share of agencies filing out of all law-enforcement agencies in the state, and perhaps per-capita or per-officer versions. If the authors can build a denominator from Census of Governments, LEMAS, UCR/LEOKA, or another source of agency universe counts, that would substantially strengthen the paper. At minimum, the current participation analysis should be clearly labeled as “among agencies in the ESAC panel,” not participation in the federal program more generally.

Second, the paper would benefit from a much more transparent discussion of what ESAC income measures and what it does not measure. Right now the reader is invited to treat equitable sharing receipts as a direct proxy for circumvention, but that is too quick. Joint investigations could plausibly move differently from adoptive seizures, and state reform could even reduce one while increasing the other. This is where the manifest’s reference to CATS seems especially important. Even if complete adoptive-seizure data are not available for the full panel, a partial validation exercise would help enormously: show whether states with stronger reforms exhibit changes in the composition of federal forfeiture cases, or whether the subset of identifiable adoptive seizures moves in the same direction as the ESAC totals. Without such evidence, the paper should be more modest in its claims.

Third, the treatment coding needs to be made fully reproducible and internally consistent. The paper refers to 36 reform states in some places, 36 plus DC in the manifest, 21 conviction states, 12 burden-of-proof states, 4 reporting-only states, 4 abolition states, and later 20 “strong reform” states and 16 “weak reform” states. Some of these counts do not obviously reconcile. In a short paper this can be fixed efficiently with a clean appendix table listing, for each state: reform date, legal change, effective date, whether it is coded as strong/weak, and whether an anti-circumvention provision exists. I would also separate enactment from effective date where those differ materially.

Relatedly, the paper should engage more with treatment heterogeneity beyond a binary post indicator. The underlying laws vary a great deal: some states abolished civil forfeiture, some required conviction, some only tightened burdens, and some mainly improved reporting. Those are not close substitutes. Rather than pooling all reforms as one “treatment” and then adding a small heterogeneity table, I would make the paper’s main estimating framework distinguish reform intensity from the outset. One could estimate ATT separately by reform type using interaction-weighted/event-study methods, or even present the binary pooled estimate as a descriptive average and put more interpretive weight on the stronger reforms.

I also recommend more caution about the identifying assumptions. The paper says year fixed effects absorb federal changes such as the Holder and Sessions policy shifts because they applied uniformly nationwide. Formally, that is true for common time shocks, but the practical concern is that those federal changes may have had heterogeneous effects depending on states’ existing forfeiture laws and local agency reliance on federal channels. A useful robustness check would interact year effects with pre-period equitable-sharing intensity or with baseline reform propensity. Another would be to show results excluding the most policy-turbulent federal years around 2015–2017.

On inference, 50 state clusters is acceptable but not ideal, especially with staggered policy timing and some small treated subgroups. I would suggest wild cluster bootstrap p-values for the main TWFE specifications and perhaps randomization-inference style placebo reforms at the state level rather than agency-level placebo timing among never-treated units. The current placebo is not very informative because the variation is still constrained within the already-selected ESAC sample.

The event-study evidence should also be presented more carefully. The manuscript describes pretrends as supportive because coefficients are “centered on zero,” but several are large in magnitude and the standard errors are wide. That is not evidence against violations; it is simply low power. I would avoid strong language here. A more useful presentation would show the event-study figure with confidence intervals, report a joint pretrend test, and discuss the amount of pre-treatment support available by cohort.

The “power” section is directionally useful but currently overinterpreted. In a setting with highly skewed outcomes and a mechanism that may operate through a small subset of agencies, a 38 percent MDE is not obviously small. I would rewrite this section in a more neutral way: the design rules out very large average increases in total ESAC receipts, but more modest circumvention effects or composition changes remain possible. That would still be a meaningful contribution.

The state-level results are potentially more interesting than the paper lets on. The positive state-level point estimate on total equitable sharing funds, though imprecise, goes in the opposite direction of the agency-level null/negative results. That divergence deserves interpretation rather than being brushed aside. It may be a clue that changes occur through a few agencies, or through agency entry, or because the balanced agency panel is selected. Lean into that discrepancy: it could become one of the paper’s most informative findings.

I would also tone down the “compliance spillover” interpretation. A marginally significant negative estimate for strong reforms is intriguing, but the mechanism is speculative and the estimate comes from broad legal recoding plus limited statistical power. The paper can mention this as a suggestive pattern without building much of the discussion around it. The same applies to the anti-circumvention result: with only three treated states, it is better viewed as descriptive.

Finally, the paper’s contribution may be stronger if it is reframed slightly. Rather than claiming to have shown that the federal escape valve is an illusion, the authors could claim something like: “Using the universe of ESAC certifications, we find no evidence that state forfeiture reforms produced large average increases in agencies’ reported equitable sharing receipts.” That is already useful, novel, and policy-relevant. Then the limitations section can candidly state that the data do not perfectly identify adoptive seizure circumvention, leaving room for future work with more granular case-level information.

In short, this is a good idea and a potentially valuable dataset, but the paper needs a tighter match between mechanism, sample, and claims. If the authors address the selection issue, sharpen the outcome interpretation, and scale the conclusions to the evidence, the paper could make a credible contribution.
