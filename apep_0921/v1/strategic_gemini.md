### 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
The paper asks whether state-level civil asset forfeiture reforms are rendered toothless by "equitable sharing," a federal loophole that allows local police to bypass state restrictions. Using a comprehensive FOIA-derived dataset of Department of Justice certifications, the author finds that police do not increase their use of the federal program when state laws tighten; in fact, they might even decrease it. This suggests that the widely feared "leakage" or "escape valve" in policing regulation is surprisingly absent, indicating that local reforms actually have "bite."

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this clearly. The first two paragraphs of the Introduction are excellent: they set the stakes ($6.8 billion), identify the specific loophole (equitable sharing), and state the answer immediately (the escape valve did not open). The pitch is AER-ready.

---

### 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
The paper provides the first causal evidence that sub-national civil asset forfeiture reforms do not trigger jurisdictional arbitrage through federal channels.

*   **Differentiation:** It moves beyond the cross-sectional correlations in the criminology and legal literature (e.g., Carpenter et al., 2020) by using a staggered DiD design.
*   **Question vs. Gap:** It answers a question about the **WORLD** (Are police departments circumventing the law?). This is much stronger than merely "filling a gap" in the DiD literature.
*   **The "Smart Economist" Test:** A reader would describe this as "the paper that shows the federal forfeiture loophole is an illusion."
*   **Bigger Contribution:** To make this even larger, the author needs to dig deeper into *why* the leakage doesn't happen. Is it a lack of administrative capacity at the federal level, or a "chilling effect" where state reform changes the culture of the police department entirely?

---

### 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
The paper sits at the intersection of **Public Economics (Intergovernmental Relations)** and **Law and Economics (Policing).**

*   **Neighbors:** Baicker and Jacobson (2004) on fiscal incentives; Fowlie et al. (2014) on regulatory leakage; and the recent DiD methods literature (Callaway & Sant’Anna).
*   **Positioning:** It should position itself as a counter-point to the environmental literature. In environmental econ, leakage is the rule; here, it is the exception. This "dog that didn't bark" story is the most interesting angle.
*   **Missing Literature:** It could benefit from connecting to the literature on **"Organizational Culture"** or **"Public Choice"** within bureaucracies—why do agents not maximize revenue when given the chance?

---

### 4. NARRATIVE ARC
*   **Setup:** States are cracking down on "policing for profit."
*   **Tension:** A massive federal loophole exists that should, theoretically, make these state reforms useless.
*   **Resolution:** The loophole isn't used. In fact, total seizures might even fall.
*   **Implications:** Federalism works better than we thought; local policy isn't easily bypassed by federal "escape valves."

**Evaluation:** The narrative is strong. It’s a "Myth-Busters" story. The only missing piece is a clearer exploration of the "Compliance Spillover" mechanism mentioned on page 3.

---

### 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
*   **The Lead Fact:** "When states banned civil forfeiture, police didn't just switch to the federal version; they actually stopped seizing assets altogether."
*   **The Reaction:** Lean in. This contradicts the standard "rational bureaucrat" model of revenue-maximizing police.
*   **The Follow-up:** "Why? Is it too much paperwork to call the Feds, or are the police just afraid of the bad press?"

---

### 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
*   **Front-loading:** The paper is already well front-loaded.
*   **State-Level Aggregates:** Table 3, Panel B shows a positive (though insignificant) effect on *total* state funds ($0.760$). This slightly contradicts the agency-level null. This needs a very careful reconciliation in the main text to ensure it doesn't undermine the "no leakage" story.
*   **Appendix:** The "Standardized Effect Sizes" in the appendix are helpful and should perhaps be briefly mentioned in the results section to quantify the "null."

---

### 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The distance is **Medium**. The science is sound and the question is important. To move from "competent" to "exciting," the author needs to address the **Ambition Problem**.

**The single biggest improvement:** Move beyond the null result to prove *why* the substitution doesn't happen. The author suggests "administrative friction" vs "institutional signaling." If the author can find a proxy for "administrative friction" (e.g., distance to the nearest federal field office or previous experience with federal joint tasks) and show that substitution *only* happens where frictions are low, the paper moves from a "null result" to a "theory of regulatory leakage."
