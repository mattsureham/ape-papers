## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple, policy-relevant question: when states restrict civil asset forfeiture, do police departments evade those restrictions by shifting seizures into the federal “equitable sharing” program? Using nationwide agency-level data on DOJ equitable sharing payments, the paper’s central claim is that they do not: state forfeiture reforms do not appear to trigger large substitution into the federal channel.

Why should a busy economist care? Because this is not really just about forfeiture. It is about whether subnational regulation has bite in a federal system when a higher-level jurisdiction offers an apparent escape valve. That is a broad and important question about regulatory leakage, implementation, and the limits of jurisdictional arbitrage.

**Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Mostly yes, but not optimally. The first paragraph sets up the policy concern well. The second paragraph gets to the answer quickly, which is good. But the introduction still reads more like “here is the first causal estimate in this policy area” than “here is a broader finding about when federalism undermines state policy and when it doesn’t.” For AER, the paper needs to elevate the stakes faster and more confidently.

**What the first two paragraphs should say instead:**

> In many areas of policy, subnational regulation is thought to fail because regulated actors can route activity through a laxer jurisdiction. Civil asset forfeiture offers a particularly stark version of that concern: even if a state raises evidentiary standards or abolishes civil forfeiture, local law enforcement can potentially transfer seizures into the federal equitable sharing program and retain most of the proceeds under federal law. If that escape valve is widely used, state reform is largely symbolic.
>
> This paper asks whether that escape valve actually opens. Using the universe of DOJ equitable sharing certifications for state and local law-enforcement agencies from 2009–2024, I find no evidence that state forfeiture reform increases agencies’ use of the federal channel. The result suggests that, even in a setting where legal circumvention appears straightforward on paper, overlapping federal jurisdiction does not necessarily undo state policy.

That is the version that belongs in a top-journal introduction.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:**  
The paper provides the first nationwide causal evidence that state civil asset forfeiture reforms do not, on average, induce law-enforcement agencies to substitute into the federal equitable sharing program.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?
Somewhat, but not enough. The paper differentiates itself from descriptive and legal-scholarship claims about circumvention, and from older work on forfeiture incentives. But the differentiation is still mostly methodological: “first causal evidence,” “uses staggered DiD,” “uses the universe of ESAC records.” That is necessary but not sufficient.

What is still missing is a sharper statement of **substantive novelty**:
- Prior work showed equitable sharing exists and creates incentives.
- Prior advocates argued state reform may be evaded.
- This paper shows that the widely feared evasion mechanism is not a first-order empirical response.

That needs to be stated more crisply.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or filling a gap in a LITERATURE?
It is mixed. The paper does ask a real-world question—does the escape valve open?—which is good. But it frequently slips into “first causal estimate” and “contributes to the literature on…” framing. For AER, the world-facing version is much stronger.

The strongest framing is:
- **World question:** Can state governments constrain a revenue-generating policing practice when federal law offers an alternative route?
- **General implication:** Formal legal escape valves may matter less than institutional frictions, political scrutiny, and organizational norms.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the intro?
Yes, but only narrowly. Right now they would probably say:  
“It's a DiD paper on whether state forfeiture reforms increased federal equitable sharing, and it finds no effect.”

That is not terrible, but it is not yet exciting. The desired reaction is:  
“It overturns the conventional wisdom that federal equitable sharing neutralizes state forfeiture reform, and more broadly suggests that overlapping jurisdiction doesn’t automatically generate regulatory leakage.”

### What would make this contribution bigger?
Most importantly: **get closer to the actual escape valve.** Right now the main outcome is total equitable sharing receipts, but the feared mechanism is specifically adoptive seizures. The paper itself admits this limitation. Strategically, that is the biggest reason the contribution feels smaller than the headline.

Specific ways to enlarge the contribution:
1. **Different outcome variable:** Separate adoptive seizures from joint investigations, if possible. That would turn a good “reduced-form rebuttal” into a direct test of circumvention.
2. **Different comparison:** Compare reforms that explicitly target transfer to federal jurisdiction versus those that only tighten state standards. That would sharpen the federalism story.
3. **Different mechanism evidence:** Show whether reforms reduce overall forfeiture activity or merely change the channel. Without seeing the state side, the story remains incomplete.
4. **Different framing:** Connect this to a broader class of policies where state rules are allegedly nullified by federal alternatives. The paper should be selling a general lesson about when escape valves are real versus illusory.

### How should the paper position itself relative to those neighbors?
It should mostly **build on and correct**, not attack.

- Relative to the forfeiture literature: “Existing work established the incentive problem and documented the descriptive possibility of circumvention. We test whether circumvention is quantitatively important when states actually reform.”
- Relative to leakage/federalism papers: “This setting looks ex ante like an easy case for leakage, but unlike emissions or tax-base mobility, the relevant actors do not respond in the predicted way.”

That is a much stronger positioning than “we also contribute to federalism.”

Paradoxically, both.
- **Too narrowly** in the sense that much of the paper reads like a niche forfeiture-policy paper.
- **Too broadly** in the sense that the gesture toward “regulatory leakage” is not yet sufficiently integrated into the actual design and evidence.

The audience is therefore slightly unclear. It is not fully a policing paper, not fully a public finance/federalism paper, and not fully a law-and-econ paper. It needs to pick the main conversation.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?
It should engage more with:
- **Fiscal federalism and intergovernmental constraints**
- **Bureaucratic compliance / street-level bureaucracy**
- **Policy implementation under overlapping jurisdiction**
- **Policing and revenue-motivated enforcement**
- Potentially **legal federalism / forum shopping / jurisdictional arbitrage** beyond the standard environmental leakage examples

The current environmental-leakage analogy is useful but a bit off-the-shelf. The more natural intellectual neighbors may actually be in **public law, law and economics, and political economy of enforcement**.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?
Not quite yet. The most promising conversation is not “another application of DiD to policing policy,” but:

> When policymakers believe a reform is toothless because of a formal legal workaround, are they right?  
> This paper studies a case where the workaround is obvious in law, politically salient, and repeatedly invoked—yet empirically weak.

That is the right conversation.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
States reform civil asset forfeiture because the practice is viewed as abusive and distortionary. But these reforms may be undermined because local agencies can use a federal program that applies looser rules and returns proceeds to them.

### Tension
The entire reform movement may be futile if agencies can easily route seizures through federal authorities. Legal scholars and advocates have repeatedly claimed exactly that. So the key puzzle is whether state policy meaningfully changes behavior in the presence of an apparent federal loophole.

### Resolution
Using nationwide DOJ equitable sharing records, the paper finds no evidence of a post-reform increase in federal equitable sharing receipts; if anything, stronger reforms are associated with declines.

### Implications
State reform appears to retain bite even in a setting with overlapping federal jurisdiction. More broadly, not every formal escape valve translates into large behavioral substitution.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
Yes, more than many papers of this type. There is an actual question, an apparent puzzle, and a resolution. That is a strength.

But the arc is still **underpowered rhetorically**. The paper has the ingredients of a strong story, yet it sometimes lapses into “here are the estimates” rather than fully dramatizing the stakes:
- Before: everyone says the loophole makes reform symbolic.
- After: nationwide evidence says the loophole is much less operative than advertised.
- Therefore: legal possibility is not the same as effective arbitrage.

The paper should lean harder into that “illusion” structure implied by the title. Right now the title promises a debunking paper, but the text often sounds cautious and incremental.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at dinner?
“I looked at the entire federal equitable sharing database, and when states tightened or abolished civil asset forfeiture, local agencies did **not** increase their use of the federal forfeiture channel.”

That is the right lead fact.

### Would people lean in?
Some would. People in public finance, law and economics, political economy, and policing would lean in. But many general-interest economists will only lean in if the presenter immediately adds the broader implication:

> “This is a case where everyone thought overlapping federal jurisdiction would nullify state reform, and it didn’t.”

Without that sentence, phones may come out.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Almost certainly:  
“But is equitable sharing receipts the right measure of circumvention? Can you isolate adoptive seizures specifically?”

That is the central strategic vulnerability. Even if referees judge the design credible, the editorial issue is that the paper’s headline claim is stronger than the directness of the outcome.

### Is the null interesting?
Yes—conditionally. This is one of those cases where the null is genuinely informative because the prior belief is strong, the institutional concern is widely repeated, and the paper can argue that it rules out economically large substitution.

But the paper has to keep making that case. A null result is interesting when it overturns a salient expectation, not when it merely fails to find something. This paper is closer to the former than the latter, but it must insist on that framing.

Right now the null is presented competently. To become publication-grade for a top outlet, it needs to be presented as:
- a test of a very prominent policy critique,
- in a setting where circumvention is legally feasible and publicly feared,
- with evidence sufficient to rule out first-order effects.
