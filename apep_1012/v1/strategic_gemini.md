### 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper uses high-frequency administrative data to revisit a high-stakes debate in labor economics: whether "Ban-the-Box" (BTB) policies accidentally harm Black job-seekers by incentivizing employers to use race as a proxy for criminal history. The author finds a "precisely bounded near-null," suggesting that while statistical discrimination is a theoretical certainty and detectable in lab-like audit studies, it does not manifest as a meaningful aggregate "tax" on Black employment in the actual economy.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this well in the abstract, but the first two paragraphs of the introduction are slightly too "textbook." They lead with a hypothetical anecdote about New York City. For the AER, the pitch needs to be more aggressive about the *empirical tension* between previous literature and this new administrative evidence.

**The pitch the paper should have:**
"A growing body of experimental evidence suggests that Ban-the-Box (BTB) policies backfire by triggering statistical discrimination against Black applicants. However, these findings—often based on callback rates—have yet to be reconciled with aggregate employment outcomes. This paper uses administrative QWI data and a triple-difference design to show that the 'screening tax' predicted by theory is effectively zero in the aggregate, ruling out adverse effects larger than 0.8 log points and suggesting that labor market adjustments or offsetting benefits for ex-offenders neutralize the predicted harm."

---

### 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**The Contribution:** This paper provides the first high-frequency, administrative-data bound on the aggregate employment effects of Ban-the-Box, challenging the "backfire" consensus established by audit studies and lower-resolution survey data.

- **Differentiation:** It differentiates itself from **Doleac and Hansen (2020)** by moving from ACS survey data (annual/noisy) to QWI administrative data (quarterly/comprehensive), and from **Agan and Starr (2018)** by moving from callbacks (a partial-equilibrium signal) to actual employment (the general-equilibrium outcome).
- **Question vs. Literature:** It frames itself as answering a question about the **world** (Does this policy actually hurt people?), which is strong. 
- **The "So What":** A smart economist would say: "It’s the administrative data 'reality check' on the BTB-statistical discrimination literature."
- **Bigger Contribution:** To make this bigger, the author needs to dig deeper into the *why* of the null. Is it because of the "Offsetting Channels" mentioned in Section 6? If the author could decompose the effect into "gain for Black ex-offenders" vs "loss for Black non-offenders," this would be a Lead Article. Currently, it’s a very high-quality "Comment" on a major literature.

---

### 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

The paper sits at the intersection of **Information in Labor Markets** and **Economics of Crime.**

- **Closest Neighbors:** Doleac & Hansen (2020), Agan & Starr (2018), Shoag & Veuger (2021), and Jackson & Zhao (2023).
- **Positioning:** The paper currently "builds and reconciles." It should probably be slightly more "confrontational" with the audit study literature. It needs to explain *why* an AER reader should believe a null result in administrative data over a clean experimental result in an audit study (e.g., general equilibrium effects, firm-size heterogeneity).
- **Missing Conversations:** The paper should speak more to the **Personnel Economics** literature on multi-stage screening. If firms can't screen at the "box" stage, they screen at the interview stage. The null result suggests the *timing* of the information matters less than we thought.

---

### 4. NARRATIVE ARC

- **Setup:** BTB is a popular equity policy.
- **Tension:** Economic theory and audit studies say it’s a "screening tax" that harms the very people it's meant to help.
- **Resolution:** Using the best available administrative data, we see no such tax.
- **Implications:** The "backfire" narrative may be overstated; policy-makers can breathe easier, but also shouldn't expect BTB to be a silver bullet for the racial employment gap.

**Evaluation:** The arc is clean. It’s a classic "Revisiting an Established Fact" story. The resolution is a "precise null," which is a harder sell than a "large effect," so the narrative must emphasize the *precision* and the *scale* of the data.

---

### 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

At a dinner party, you lead with: **"We’ve been telling a story for ten years that Ban-the-Box hurts Black men, but in the actual tax records, that effect disappears."**

People lean in because the "backfire" story is a favorite counter-intuitive "freakonomics" style fact. Debunking or tempering a counter-intuitive fact is itself highly interesting. The "So what" is: the theoretical harm of statistical discrimination might be a second-order concern in the actual macro-economy.

---

### 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

- **Front-loading:** The Sun-Abraham estimates (Table 3) and the precise CI [-0.79, 0.10] are your best assets. They should be the center of the intro.
- **Appendix to Main:** The "Heterogeneity by Black Population Share" (mentioned in Section 6 and Table 5) is actually very important. If the negative effect is concentrated in high-Black-share counties, that's a "smoking gun" for the mechanism even if the aggregate is null. Move that analysis to the main body.
- **Length:** The "Institutional Background" is a bit long for an AER audience who likely knows what BTB is. Trim it.

---

### 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The gap is **Ambition.** Right now, it’s a very competent "Check on a previous paper." To be a "Full AER," it needs to explain the **Mechanism of the Null.**

**The single most impactful piece of advice:**
Don't just report a null; *prove* why the audit studies were "wrong" (or rather, why they don't scale). Use the QWI's industry or firm-size data to show if the "screening tax" exists in some sectors (e.g., small retail) but is offset by others (e.g., large manufacturing with formal HR). If you can show *where* the effect lives and *where* it dies, you move from "it doesn't happen" to "here is how the labor market functions."
