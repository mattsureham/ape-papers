## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper identifies a "composition illusion" in environmental economics: the common practice of using within-facility, multi-medium variation to estimate the effectiveness of media-specific inspections can produce robust evidence of deterrence that is entirely spurious. By analyzing U.S. manufacturing facilities, the author shows that a Clean Air Act inspection appears to reduce air emissions relative to other media, but this is driven by a simultaneous decline in water emissions (due to correlated enforcement) while air emissions remain flat. A busy economist should care because this suggests that many "gold standard" within-unit evaluations of regulation may be misinterpreting relative shifts as absolute policy wins.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this pitch very clearly. The first two paragraphs effectively set the stage of fragmented U.S. environmental law and immediately define the "composition illusion." 

**The pitch the paper should have (slightly more ambitious):**
"Does environmental enforcement actually work, or are we just measuring it wrong? Using a panel of 3,500 U.S. manufacturing facilities, I show that traditional within-facility estimates of inspection effectiveness suffer from a 'composition illusion' where relative shifts in pollution are mistaken for absolute deterrence. This occurs because enforcement programs are correlated across media; a Clean Air Act inspection appears successful only because water emissions are falling faster, while the targeted air emissions do not move at all."

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution:** The paper demonstrates that within-unit relative estimators are biased indicators of media-specific deterrence when multiple enforcement programs overlap, providing an empirical "cautionary tale" for the environmental evaluation literature.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It is well-differentiated. While Sigman (1996) focuses on *strategic substitution* (firms moving dirt), this paper focuses on *measurement error* (evaluators moving the goalposts). It challenges the isolationist assumptions in Shimshack and Ward (2007) and Gray and Shadbegian (2005).
*   **Framing:** It is currently framed as answering a question about the **WORLD** (how effective is enforcement?) but transitions into a methodological critique.
*   **The "Smart Economist" Test:** A reader would explain this as: "It's the paper that shows why you can't use non-air pollution as a control for air inspections, because the EPA often inspects both at once."
*   **Making it Bigger:** To make this an AER "blockbuster," the author needs to connect the "illusion" to a **welfare** or **policy-weighting** consequence. If the air inspection is a "null" but costs $X, and the evaluator thinks it saved 7%, the misallocation of the EPA's budget is the real story.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

*   **Closest Neighbors:** Shimshack (2014) on enforcement; Sigman (1996) and Greenstone (2002) on cross-media effects; Keiser and Shapiro (2019) on the CWA.
*   **Strategy:** The paper currently **synthesizes and corrects**. It takes the "within-facility" trend (a tool favored by the literature) and turns it on its head.
*   **Audience:** The audience is currently narrow (Environmental Econ). To broaden it, the paper should lean harder into the **Generalizability** section (Section 8), speaking to anyone doing "within-unit" DiD (e.g., Labor economists comparing different departments in a firm).
*   **Conversation:** It is having the right conversation, but it should more aggressively "attack" the optimism of the early 2000s enforcement literature.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

*   **Setup:** We evaluate air inspections by looking at air emissions, often using other media at the same facility as a control.
*   **Tension:** If the EPA targets "bad" facilities across all media, our "control" is moving at the same time as our "treatment."
*   **Resolution:** The "relative" effect is -7%, but the "absolute" effect is 0%. The -7% is a mirage created by falling water emissions.
*   **Implications:** Integrated inspection and, more importantly, integrated *evaluation* are necessary to avoid budget misallocation.

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong and remarkably clean. It reads like a detective story where the "suspect" (the inspection) has a fake alibi (the relative differential).

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

*   **The Fact:** "A Clean Air Act inspection can look like it reduced pollution by 7% even if air emissions actually went *up* by 1%."
*   **Reaction:** Economists will lean in because it exposes a flaw in a very common "clever" identification strategy.
*   **Follow-up:** "How much of the existing literature on enforcement is actually just measuring this illusion?" (The author should try to answer this in the discussion).
