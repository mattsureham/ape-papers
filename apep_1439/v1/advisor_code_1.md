**Summary**

The paper evaluates the FCA’s prediction that eliminating loyalty penalties in motor and home insurance would reduce consumer search, exploiting weekly Google Trends for insurance versus non-insurance comparison websites in a cross-product DiD framework. It finds a positive but imprecise treatment effect—insurance search did not decline relative to controls—and emphasizes that the null rules out large reductions in search intensity. The takeaway is that banning price discrimination did not, as predicted, reduce consumer engagement, implying regulators should not rely on that behavioral channel without empirical verification.

**Essential Points**

1. **Limited Outcome Scope and External Validity:** The paper’s outcome proxy—Google Trends for three insurance brands versus two other sites—is fragile. The control keywords cover different markets (energy/broadband/personal finance) and different search motivations than insurance, and there is no discussion of search intensity heterogeneity within insurance (e.g., motor vs. home). Moreover, the main alternative data sources promised in the idea manifest (ABI switching rates, FCA complaints) are absent, so we cannot assess whether the null result holds for actual switching behavior. The paper should either incorporate those additional data sources or convincingly argue that the Google Trends proxy is representative of switching outcomes and that the control group is comparable.

2. **Identification Concerns from Pre-trends and Limited Controls:** The event study exhibits pronounced pre-trends (negative coefficients for multiple quarters), suggesting parallel trends may not hold even in aggregated form. With only five keywords, controlling flexibly for differential trends is difficult. The paper’s reliance on cluster-robust SEs with three treated keywords and two controls further raises concerns about statistical power and inference. The authors should more seriously address the pre-trend violation—perhaps by weighting, de-trending, or exploiting additional control keywords—and provide sensitivity analysis showing the DiD estimate is not driven by evolving seasonality or media coverage differentially affecting insurance brands.

3. **Interpretation of the Null and Policy Implications:** The paper concludes that the loyalty-penalty ban failed to reduce search, casting doubt on the FCA’s behavioral channel. However, even a null effect on Google Trends does not directly imply that switching behavior or welfare effects are unchanged. Without linking search intensity to actual switching rates, the policy conclusion is speculative. The authors should clarify that the results speak to relative search interest, not switching per se, and temper conclusions about welfare or regulatory strategy accordingly. Alternatively, they could attempt to calibrate how much a 3–4 index-point change corresponds to switching behavior or use complementary data (even descriptively) to strengthen the economic interpretation.

Given these issues, the paper is not ready for publication in its current form. However, each concern seems addressable with additional analysis, provided the data exist.

**Suggestions**

1. **Broaden the Outcome and Validate the Proxy:** The manifest promised ABI motor-switching data and FCA complaints data—these were feasible sources. The authors should incorporate at least one of these to lend credibility to the claim about consumer switching. ABI publishes quarterly switching rates; even if policy reasons prevent accessing raw data, publicly released summaries could be digitized or otherwise used to compare with the Google Trends results. Similarly, FCA/FOS aggregate complaints (with categories related to switching difficulties) could provide a corroborating friction measure. Including these outcomes would allow a richer DiD (e.g., insurance vs. non-insurance complaints) and help translate Google Trends findings into welfare-relevant margins. If such data remain inaccessible, the authors should explicitly explain the data gap and justify why Google Trends is an acceptable substitute, perhaps by demonstrating a correlation between search traffic and switching in a pre-policy period.

2. **Expand and Justify the Control Group:** The identification relies on comparing insurance search to non-insurance comparison sites (uswitch, moneysavingexpert). These sites operate across energy, broadband, utilities, and general finance, so their search patterns may respond differently to macro shocks (e.g., energy price spikes, broadband regulatory announcements). Consider expanding the control group to include more insurance-adjacent comparison terms (e.g., pet/travel insurance sites not covered by GIPP) or other financial product comparison platforms less likely to be affected by contemporaneous shocks. Alternatively, weight the controls to mimic pre-treatment trends more closely, or use synthetic control/stacked DiD methods with more granular keywords. At minimum, provide diagnostic plots showing that control keyword trends do not respond to known confounders (e.g., energy-price surges) and that results are insensitive to excluding each control one at a time (beyond the leave-one-out summary).

3. **Address Pre-trends and Flexible Time Variation:** The event study reveals significant pre-treatment differences, which undermines the parallel-trends assumption. Try adjusting the specification to account for those differences—introduce keyword-specific linear/quadratic trends, difference the data (first differences), or estimate the effect using methods robust to pre-trends (e.g., synthetic control with reweighting). If the pre-trends persist, the paper should explicitly acknowledge this limitation and interpret the DiD estimate as descriptive rather than causal. Additionally, the wide confidence intervals (due to few clusters) call for alternative inference strategies. Consider aggregating at the brand level and using randomization inference (more than 500 permutations) or using wild bootstrap clustering methods to better capture uncertainty. Also, why is there a positive point estimate while the event study shows negative post-treatment coefficients? Reconcile these patterns to avoid contradictory narratives.

4. **Calibrate Effect Size and Link to Behavioral Theory:** The interpretation of the null could be strengthened by calibrating what a 3.8-point change in Google Trends means in terms of switching behavior or search frequency. For example, is this difference economically meaningful relative to the standard deviation of insurance search intensity? You already compute standardized effect sizes in the appendix; incorporate that discussion into the main text to help readers interpret the magnitude. Additionally, link the empirical results more explicitly to economic models of search—does a null effect suggest that search costs are invariant to price-walking removal, or that the awareness channel dominated? The discussion currently hints at competing mechanisms without formally connecting to theory. A short conceptual section contrasting the “incentive channel” (less need to search) and “salience/awareness channel” (more search due to publicity) would help frame the empirical findings. Where possible, use the data to test these mechanisms—for example, did media coverage or brand-specific announcements coincide with spikes in insurance search?

5. **Clarify Policy Implications and Limit Generalizations:** The conclusion states that regulators should not expect price-discrimination bans to reduce search, but this is a strong claim grounded in limited evidence. Rephrase to acknowledge the scope: the analysis pertains to insurance comparison-site traffic in the UK post-GIPP, and the null may reflect offsetting mechanisms (awareness vs. incentive) rather than a universal absence of search reduction. Suggest avenues for future research (e.g., linking search to switching, exploring heterogeneity by tenure or risk type) rather than offering definitive policy prescriptions. This will keep the paper’s tone appropriately cautious and scholarly.

6. **Improve Reporting and Transparency:** Provide more detail on the Google Trends data collection (e.g., how did you handle Google’s normalization across queries—did you rescale the 0–100 indexes to make them comparable across keywords?). Also report how you handled weeks with zero searches (if any), and whether the keywords’ search volumes are normalized yearly or over the entire sample. In the robustness checks, explain whether the narrow-window specification simply drops early/late weeks or re-normalizes the index. Transparency will help readers judge the reliability of the proxy.

7. **Consider Additional Placebos or Mechanism Tests:** The placebo test using January 2020 is a good start, but it would be helpful to show that other policy or media events (e.g., FCA announcements, large price increases) did not coincide with the treatment period. A leads-and-lags exercise using multiple fake treatment dates would bolster credibility. Additionally, test whether the ban affected control keywords—if they also exhibit shifts post-2022, this would suggest broader market dynamics at play. Conversely, if insurance keywords reacted more than controls to unrelated shocks (e.g., motor premium publicity in 2023), that would justify including more granular controls.

In sum, the paper tackles an interesting question with potentially important policy implications, but it needs to deliver on the original idea’s richer empirical strategy, bolster identification with additional controls and data sources, and clarify the interpretation of the null. Addressing these suggestions would substantially enhance the paper’s contribution and credibility.
