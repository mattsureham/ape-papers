## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether foreign aid acts as an "insurance policy" for fragile states by examining if subnational aid exposure mitigated the surge in conflict following the 2008 global oil price collapse in Nigeria. Using geocoded project data and a difference-in-differences design, the author finds a precisely estimated null: aid did not buffer the shock, suggesting that development assistance (at least at current scales) is not a substitute for state fiscal capacity in maintaining peace.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this well in the first two paragraphs, successfully transitioning from the macro-fiscal shock of the oil crash to the micro-question of aid effectiveness. However, it could be sharper. 
*   **The pitch the paper should have:** "When commodity prices collapse, resource-dependent states lose the fiscal capacity to buy off challengers or provide public goods, often leading to violence. This paper provides the first subnational test of whether foreign aid buffers this relationship. Using the 2008 oil crash as an exogenous shock to Nigeria’s federal-transfer system, I find that regions with higher aid exposure were no less likely to slide into conflict, suggesting aid is an ineffective substitute for lost resource rents."

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution:** The paper provides a well-powered, subnational null result that refutes the "aid-as-stabilizer" hypothesis during a major macro-fiscal shock.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It is well-differentiated from Dube and Vargas (2013) by moving from *causing* shocks to *conditioning* them, and from Crost et al. (2014) by looking at a macro-fiscal mechanism rather than a local insurgent-targeting mechanism.
*   **Framing:** It is framed as answering a question about the **WORLD** (Can aid prevent the "resource curse" from turning violent during downturns?). This is a high-level, AER-style question.
*   **Explainability:** A smart economist would say: "It's a study showing that having a lot of aid projects in your district doesn't stop the local violence that happens when the national oil budget crashes."
*   **Making it bigger:** To move from "competent paper" to "blockbuster," the paper needs to prove *why* it failed more definitively. Is it just a scale problem (aid is too small)? If so, the result is less surprising. If the author could show that even in places where aid *was* large relative to local budgets, it still failed because of **fungibility** or **timing**, the contribution becomes a theory-testing powerhouse.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

*   **Closest Neighbors:** Bazzi and Blattman (2014) on macro shocks; Berman et al. (2011) and Crost et al. (2014) on subnational aid/conflict; Besley and Persson (2011) on fiscal capacity.
*   **Positioning:** The paper currently builds on these but should more aggressively **synthesize** them. It sits at the intersection of "Fiscal Federalism" and "Conflict Economics."
*   **Niche vs. Broad:** It is positioned broadly enough for the AER, but it needs to speak more to the **Public Economics** literature on fiscal stabilizers.
*   **Missing Conversation:** The paper would benefit from connecting to the literature on **Social Safety Nets** in developing countries. Is aid failing to buffer because it isn't a "transfer," but rather a "project"? Linking this to the "cash vs. in-kind" debate would elevate the narrative.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

*   **Setup:** Nigeria depends on oil; oil prices are volatile; the 2008 crash was a massive fiscal shock.
*   **Tension:** Theory suggests aid should help fill the gap (stabilizer), but micro-evidence on aid and conflict is often pessimistic (incitement/rapacity).
*   **Resolution:** A robust null. Aid does nothing to stop the bleeding.
*   **Implications:** Donors cannot assume "business as usual" development aid provides any security insurance.

**Evaluation:** The arc is clear. However, the "Resolution" currently feels a bit "flat." To strengthen the arc, the "Discussion" (Section 7) needs to be more than a list of excuses for the null; it needs to be a post-mortem that changes how we think about aid delivery.

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

*   **The Lead Fact:** "In Nigeria, when the oil budget collapsed by 40%, having twice as much foreign aid in your state did absolutely nothing to prevent the ensuing violence."
*   **Response:** People would lean in, then immediately ask: "Was the aid just too small to matter?" (The "Size Matters" follow-up). 
*   **The Null Value:** The null is interesting because it contradicts the "Aid for Stability" mantra of the World Bank and USAID. It isn't a "failed experiment"; it's an "indictment of an assumption."

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

*   **Front-loading:** The paper is well-structured. The FAAC mechanism (Section 2.3) is crucial and rightly placed early—it's the "plumbing" that makes the paper's identification work.
*   **Sector Heterogeneity:** Table 5 (Health vs. Governance) is currently a bit of a distraction because of the Boko Haram confound. I would move the detailed sector-by-sector breakdown to the appendix and focus the main text on the aggregate "insurance" failure.
*   **Visuals:** Figure 6 (Parallel Trends) is the most important graph. It needs to be even more prominent.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

**The Gap:** Currently, the paper is a very high-quality "No" to a popular policy assumption. To be an AER paper, it needs to move from "This didn't work" to "This didn't work because [X], and here is the evidence for [X]."

**The Single Most Impactful Advice:** Quantify the "Fiscal Gap." Calculate exactly how many dollars were lost per capita in the FAAC transfers due to the oil crash and compare that to the dollar value of the aid projects. If you can show that even when aid matched 20% or 30% of the lost revenue, conflict still rose, the paper becomes a definitive rejection of aid as a fiscal substitute. Without the dollar-to-dollar comparison, a skeptic will just say, "The aid was a drop in the bucket; of course it didn't buffer."
