## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple, policy-relevant question: when a resource-dependent country is hit by a large commodity price crash, does pre-existing foreign aid help keep conflict from rising? Using geocoded aid and conflict data from Nigerian states around the 2008 oil price collapse, the paper finds no evidence that more aid buffered violence; if anything, higher-aid places saw weakly more conflict.

A busy economist should care because this is really a paper about whether aid functions as insurance in fragile, commodity-dependent states—not just whether aid is “good” on average. That is a live question for development, political economy, conflict, and macro/commodity-shock audiences.

**Does the paper articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Reasonably, but not sharply enough. The current opening is competent and informed, but it reads like a literature-bridging exercise rather than a high-stakes world question. The first two paragraphs should more forcefully foreground the policy claim being tested: donors and policymakers often act as if development aid can stabilize fragile states when fiscal revenues collapse; this paper tests that proposition in a canonical case and finds little support.

**What the first two paragraphs should say instead:**

> Commodity price crashes can destabilize resource-dependent states by abruptly shrinking the revenues that fund public spending, patronage, and coercive capacity. Policymakers often presume that foreign aid can cushion this blow—that aid can act as fiscal insurance and help prevent revenue collapses from turning into violence—but there is little direct evidence on whether aid actually plays this stabilizing role.
>
> This paper tests that proposition in Nigeria, one of the world’s most oil-dependent countries, around the 2008 oil price crash. Combining geocoded aid projects, subnational conflict events, and the national oil shock, I ask whether Nigerian states with greater pre-crash aid exposure experienced less conflict after oil revenues collapsed. They did not: I find no detectable buffering effect of aid, ruling out large stabilizing effects in this setting.

That is the pitch. Clear question, clear world relevance, clear answer.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:**  
The paper provides subnational evidence from Nigeria that pre-existing foreign aid did not measurably cushion the conflict consequences of a large oil revenue shock.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest papers?
Partly, but not enough. The paper does distinguish itself from the “does aid reduce conflict?” literature and from the “do commodity shocks affect conflict?” literature by emphasizing the interaction—aid as a buffer against commodity-revenue collapses. That is the right instinct.

But the differentiation is still somewhat fuzzy because many readers will collapse this into: “another subnational paper on aid and conflict with a diff-in-diff around a shock.” To avoid that, the introduction needs to repeatedly insist that the object of interest is **aid as shock insurance**, not aid as a generic anti-conflict tool.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a literature gap?
It is mixed, leaning too much toward literature-gap framing. Phrases like “these two literatures intersect” and “my contribution is to test a specific mechanism” are acceptable but a bit seminar-room-ish. The stronger framing is a world question:

- When fiscal capacity collapses in resource states, can aid replace missing state capacity?
- Are donors kidding themselves when they imagine ordinary development projects can stabilize violence after a macro shock?

That is much stronger than “there is little empirical work at the intersection of two literatures.”

### Could a smart economist explain what is new after reading the introduction?
Not quite confidently. Right now they might say: “It’s a DiD on aid exposure and conflict in Nigeria around the 2008 oil crash, with a null result.” That is not enough. You want them to say: “It tests whether aid acts as insurance against state-revenue shocks and finds that ordinary project aid does not seem to stabilize conflict.”

### What would make this contribution bigger?
Several possibilities:

1. **Lean harder into the insurance/stabilization framing.**  
   This is the biggest immediate gain. Make the paper about what aid can and cannot do in fiscally fragile states.

2. **Tie aid intensity to the size of the fiscal hole.**  
   Strategically, the paper would feel bigger if it could say not just “no effect,” but “aid was too small relative to lost oil transfers to plausibly offset the shock.” That turns a null into an economic lesson about scale.

3. **Bring in more direct fiscal-state-capacity outcomes before conflict.**  
   If the paper could show whether aid-exposed states experienced different changes in public spending, wage arrears, service delivery, or local government finance after the crash, the contribution would become broader and more structural: not just “aid did not reduce conflict,” but “aid did not replace missing fiscal capacity.”

4. **Clarify mechanisms by aid modality or type.**  
   The current sector splits feel thin and partly post hoc. A more convincing distinction would be between aid that could plausibly substitute for fiscal capacity (budget support, governance, cash-like flows, infrastructure maintenance) and aid that could not.

5. **Widen the comparative frame.**  
   If the authors cannot add another country, they can still position Nigeria as a hard test: extreme oil dependence, large shock, prominent donor presence. That at least elevates the external significance.

That brings in political economy, development, public finance, and conflict. It is the right conversation for a broader economics audience.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Before this paper, we know two things:
1. negative income or commodity shocks can worsen conflict; and
2. aid sometimes reduces conflict, sometimes increases it, depending on context.

Policymakers nevertheless often act as though aid can stabilize fragile, resource-dependent places when revenues collapse.

### Tension
That policy intuition has not really been tested directly. Ordinary development aid may be too small, too fungible, too mistimed, or too poorly targeted to substitute for a sudden collapse in oil-funded state capacity.

### Resolution
In Nigeria after the 2008 oil crash, states with more pre-existing aid exposure did not experience less conflict. Large stabilizing effects can be ruled out; any buffering effect, if present, is small.

### Implications
Development aid should not be assumed to function as automatic stabilization policy in commodity-dependent fragile states. If donors want aid to insure against macro-fiscal shocks, they may need different instruments, scales, or modalities than ordinary project aid.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
It has the pieces, but they are not yet arranged for maximum force. Right now it is a bit too much a collection of estimates around a null. The paper knows what it is doing, but the story is diluted by:

- too much method-detail too early,
- too much defensive prose around assumptions,
- too much literature listing,
- and too little insistence on the central claim: **aid is not fiscal insurance here**.

### What story should it be telling?
Not “we ran a continuous DiD and found a null.”  
It should be:

> Resource-dependent states are vulnerable to revenue collapses. Donors often hope aid can stabilize those moments. Nigeria’s 2008 oil crash provides a hard test. The answer is no: ordinary pre-existing aid exposure did not cushion violence, suggesting aid is a weak substitute for collapsed resource revenues.

That is the story.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?
“I’d lead with: in Nigeria’s 2008 oil crash, places with more aid before the crash were not more stable afterward. Aid did not behave like shock insurance.”

That is much stronger than leading with the coefficient or the p-value.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
Some would lean in—especially development, political economy, and conflict economists—because the premise is intuitively important. But many would reach for their phones if the presenter quickly slides into “continuous DiD using geocoded data…” before explaining why the answer changes how we think about aid.

The idea is more interesting than the current presentation of it.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Almost certainly:
- “Was aid too small to matter?”
- “Is this about aid modality rather than aid per se?”
- “Is Nigeria just a weird case because of Boko Haram and oil federalism?”
- “Does this mean project aid cannot substitute for fiscal capacity?”

Those are good follow-up questions. The paper should anticipate them and make them central.

### If the findings are null or modest: is the null itself interesting?
Yes, conditionally. The null is interesting **if** framed as rejecting an important policy presumption, not merely failing to find an effect. The paper partially makes that case, but it needs to do it more sharply.

Right now the paper sometimes sounds like it wants credit for robustness of a null. That is not enough for AER. AER will care if the paper can persuade readers that:

1. a large stabilizing effect was genuinely plausible ex ante,
2. this setting is a serious test of that hypothesis,
3. ruling out large effects is substantively important,
4. the result teaches us something general about the limits of project-based aid.

Without that, it risks feeling like a failed attempt to find a negative coefficient.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

At present, this looks more like a solid field-journal paper with a clean idea than an obvious AER paper. The main gap is not primarily econometric; it is strategic.

### What is the gap?

**Mostly a framing problem, with some scope/ambition issues.**

- **Framing problem:** The science may be competent, but the paper undersells and mis-centers the question. It should be about the limits of aid as fiscal insurance in fragile resource states.
- **Scope problem:** Single-country, single-shock, null-result papers need an especially strong conceptual payoff. Right now the paper is narrower than its ambitions.
- **Ambition problem:** The paper is careful but somewhat safe. It demonstrates no buffering effect, but it does not yet fully convert that into a bigger statement about aid modality, fiscal substitution, and state capacity.

### What is the gap between current form and something that would excite the top 10 people in this field?
Top people will ask: what did we learn beyond “no significant interaction in Nigeria”?

To excite them, the paper needs to make them believe they learned something general:
- **project aid is not macro-fiscal insurance,**
- **stabilization requires scale/timing/modality that ordinary aid lacks,**
- **the relevant comparison is between aid and lost resource revenues, not just between high-aid and low-aid places.**

If the paper can quantify that mismatch in economic terms—how much fiscal revenue disappeared versus how much aid was present—it becomes much more than a null. It becomes a paper about why the mechanism should fail.

### Single most impactful piece of advice
**Reframe the paper around a bigger claim: ordinary development aid is not a substitute for collapsed fiscal capacity in resource-dependent states, and use Nigeria as a hard test of that proposition—not as just another aid-and-conflict setting.**

That is the one change that would most improve its odds.
