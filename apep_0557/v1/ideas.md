# Research Ideas

## Idea 1: Does Aid Stabilize Government Spending? High-Frequency Evidence from Nigeria's Oil Crisis
**Policy:** Nigeria's 2014-2016 oil price collapse slashed federal revenue by ~60%, forcing severe fiscal contraction. World Bank projects (268 projects, $30B+ in commitments) continued disbursing across sectors, potentially buffering aided ministries from spending cuts. The key policy question: does foreign aid act as a fiscal stabilizer during commodity shocks, or is aid fungible — with governments redirecting domestic funds away from aided sectors?
**Outcome:** (1) Federal government daily spending by Ministry/Department/Agency from Open Treasury Portal (opentreasury.gov.ng), aggregated monthly. This is the first use of Nigeria's daily payment data in economics research. (2) ACLED conflict events by state and month — to test whether spending cuts in non-aided sectors translate to increased conflict. (3) World Bank Indicators for macro controls.
**Identification:** Difference-in-differences exploiting the 2014-16 oil price crash as an exogenous fiscal shock. Treatment: MDAs (ministries) with active World Bank projects during the crisis. Control: MDAs without active WB projects. The oil price crash is driven by global supply factors (US shale boom, OPEC decisions) — exogenous to individual Nigerian ministries. WB project allocation to sectors was determined years before the crisis (project approval dates are 3-5 years pre-disbursement), so the treated/control distinction is predetermined. Built-in placebo: (a) pre-crisis period (2012-2014) where aided and non-aided MDAs should follow parallel spending trends, (b) MDAs with WB projects that closed before the crisis (treat-then-untreated group). Extension: triple-difference adding conflict outcomes — MDA spending cuts × state conflict exposure × oil shock timing.
**Why it's novel:** (1) First paper to use Nigeria's Open Treasury daily payment data for causal analysis — this administrative dataset has been entirely unused in economics. (2) First high-frequency test of the "aid as fiscal insurance" hypothesis — existing fungibility literature uses annual cross-country data. (3) Novel identification: commodity shock + predetermined aid allocation creates clean quasi-experiment. (4) Mechanism: traces the full chain from aid → government spending → conflict, rather than estimating a single reduced-form.
**Feasibility check:** Open Treasury data accessible via direct URL pattern (CSV files since 2019-2020). WB Projects API returns 268 Nigeria projects with approval dates, closing dates, commitment amounts, and sector/theme codes. ACLED API configured and returns Nigeria conflict events. FRED API provides Brent crude oil prices. Mapping WB sectors to Nigerian MDAs requires a manual crosswalk (~20 MDAs), which is feasible. Key concern: Open Treasury data starts ~2019, while the oil crisis was 2014-2016. MUST verify if Open Treasury has historical data back to 2013. If not, can use the 2020 COVID oil crash as an alternative shock (same mechanism, data available). Also check govspend.ng for pre-processed historical data.

## Idea 2: Foreign Aid and Conflict — Geocoded Evidence from World Bank Project Closures in Nigeria
**Policy:** Between 1995-2023, the World Bank implemented hundreds of projects across Nigerian states. When these projects close — ending employment, procurement, service delivery, and institutional support — local areas may experience disruption. This tests the "aid dependency" hypothesis: does long-term aid create fragility by building institutions that collapse upon project termination?
**Outcome:** ACLED conflict events at the state × month level (battles, riots, protests, violence against civilians). Secondary outcomes: disease surveillance from NCDC CKAN API, and nighttime lights as a proxy for economic activity.
**Identification:** Event study around World Bank project planned closing dates. The planned closing date is set at project approval (years in advance) and determined by WB institutional norms (typically 5-7 year project duration), not by local Nigerian conditions. Within-state, before/after design: compare conflict levels in the 12 months before vs. 12 months after a major WB project closure. Staggered closures across states and years provide multiple events. Built-in placebo: (a) states where WB projects continue (no closure shock), (b) project extensions (planned closure didn't actually happen — tests whether the announcement or actual closure matters).
**Why it's novel:** (1) Tests aid dependency directly using exogenous project termination, rather than cross-country aid flow correlations. (2) Within-country variation avoids the cross-country confounds that plague the aid-growth literature. (3) Clean identification from predetermined closing dates. (4) Policy-relevant for WB project design: should projects have longer durations? Tapering? Transition support?
**Feasibility check:** Requires geocoded WB project data at the state level. AidData World Bank Geocoded Release v1.4.2 covers 1995-2014 (5,684 projects, 61,243 locations globally). GODAD extends to 2023 with ADM1/ADM2 aggregation. Both are downloadable as bulk CSV. ACLED API is configured and working. Main risk: AidData may have limited Nigeria-specific geocoded projects post-2014. Need to verify Nigeria project count in the geocoded dataset before proceeding.

## Idea 3: When the Wells Run Dry — Oil Revenue Shocks, Aid Buffering, and State-Level Conflict in Nigeria
**Policy:** Nigeria allocates federal revenue to states via the Federation Account Allocation Committee (FAAC), with oil-producing states receiving 13% derivation. The 2014-16 oil price crash differentially affected oil-dependent states (Bayelsa, Rivers, Akwa Ibom — ~85% oil revenue dependence) versus non-oil states. States with active World Bank/bilateral projects may have been buffered from the fiscal shock and its downstream effects on public order.
**Outcome:** ACLED conflict events at the state × month level. Secondary: NCDC disease reports, World Bank governance indicators.
**Identification:** Triple-difference: (1) oil-dependent vs. non-oil states × (2) high vs. low pre-crisis aid intensity × (3) pre vs. post oil crash. The oil price is exogenous to individual Nigerian states. Oil dependence is geographically determined. Pre-crisis aid intensity is predetermined. The triple-diff isolates the buffering effect of aid during fiscal stress, controlling for both state fixed effects and common time trends. Placebo: (a) non-oil states (no fiscal shock), (b) 2010-2012 period (no oil crisis), (c) sectors not receiving aid.
**Why it's novel:** (1) Tests whether aid provides "fiscal insurance" against commodity shocks — a question with massive policy implications for resource-rich developing countries. (2) Uses triple-diff design that separately identifies oil shock, aid exposure, and their interaction. (3) State-level analysis within a single country avoids cross-country confounds. (4) Directly relevant to ongoing debates about aid allocation to fragile/commodity-dependent states.
**Feasibility check:** ACLED API configured. Oil prices from FRED. State-level oil dependence can be approximated using FAAC allocation data (available from budget documents) or geographic proximity to Niger Delta production. State-level aid data requires geocoded datasets (AidData/GODAD) or manual coding from WB project documents. Main risk: state-level aid intensity data may be sparse. Need at least 20 treated states for credible DiD — Nigeria has 36 states + FCT, but only ~9 are major oil producers, which may limit the oil dependence variation.
