## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether strengthening private enforcement of privacy law has real economic costs. Using the 2019 Illinois Supreme Court *Rosenbach* decision, which made biometric privacy suits much easier to bring under BIPA, the paper argues that litigation risk reduced employment in biometric-exposed industries near the Illinois border while increasing establishment counts, suggesting a shift away from larger employers.

A busy economist should care because the paper is potentially not really about biometrics at all; it is about how the design of enforcement—especially private rights of action with statutory damages—affects firm behavior, employment, and industrial organization. That is a broad and important question.

### Does the paper articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Not quite. The current introduction is competent, but it opens too much as a legal case study and too little as an economics question of general interest. It leads with settlements, legal doctrine, and the fact that no one has measured this exact setting. That is narrower than it needs to be. The paper’s best pitch is not “here is the first study of *Rosenbach*,” but “here is evidence that private enforcement can operate like a tax on technology adoption and labor demand.”

### What the first two paragraphs should say instead

Something like:

> Privacy regulation is increasingly shaped not just by statutory rules, but by how those rules are enforced. In many settings, the economically relevant margin is whether firms face administrative compliance costs or large-scale private litigation risk. Yet we know very little about whether private-right-of-action regimes materially change employment, firm size, or location decisions.
>
> This paper studies that question using the 2019 Illinois Supreme Court decision in *Rosenbach v. Six Flags*, which transformed the Illinois Biometric Information Privacy Act from a largely dormant statute into a major source of litigation by allowing suits without proof of concrete injury. Comparing biometric-exposed industries with exempt industries in Illinois border counties relative to neighboring states, I find that the ruling reduced employment in exposed sectors while increasing establishment counts, consistent with litigation risk pushing activity away from larger employers. The broader implication is that enforcement design—not just regulation on the books—can have first-order effects on economic activity.

That is the pitch. Start with the world, not with Illinois legal trivia.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper’s contribution is to argue that a judicial increase in private enforcement of biometric privacy law reduced employment in exposed Illinois industries and altered industry structure, implying that private litigation risk can function as a “litigation tax.”

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?

Only partially. The paper distinguishes itself from GDPR/privacy papers by saying this is an enforcement shock rather than a legislative enactment, which is sensible. But it does not yet sharply differentiate itself from adjacent “regulation affects firms” papers, nor from a generic state-policy border-county design. Right now the novelty is a bit too dependent on the legal setting itself.

The contribution needs clearer separation along two dimensions:

1. **From privacy regulation papers:**  
   “Those papers study compliance with privacy rules; this paper studies the consequences of private enforcement intensity.”
   
2. **From state-policy / border-county papers:**  
   “Those papers study taxes, labor regulation, or business climate; this paper studies litigation exposure generated by legal enforceability.”

At present, a smart economist might still summarize it as “another quasi-experimental paper showing regulation reduces employment.” That is not enough for AER positioning.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a literature gap?

Mixed, but too often as a literature gap. Phrases like “no empirical study has measured…” are weaker than “policymakers are choosing between enforcement regimes without knowing the real economic incidence.” The latter is a world question. The paper should lean much harder into that.

### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain what’s new?

They could probably say: “It studies BIPA litigation and finds employment effects in exposed sectors.”  
That is not yet good enough. The current framing does not fully protect the paper from sounding like “a DiD paper about a state privacy law.”

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Specific ways to enlarge the contribution:

- **Reframe from biometrics to enforcement architecture.**  
  The paper should be about private rights of action, statutory damages, and legal enforceability, with biometrics as the setting.
  
- **Push harder on industrial organization margins.**  
  The establishment/employment divergence is the most interesting fact in the paper. If credible, that is more distinctive than the employment decline itself. The big question becomes: does private litigation risk reallocate activity from large to small firms, or from integrated to fragmented organizational forms?
  
- **Use outcomes that more directly speak to firm structure and reallocation.**  
  Average establishment size, firm concentration, entry/exit, multi-establishment firms, or border-side relocation would make the story much bigger. Right now the “compositional shift” is suggestive but underdeveloped.
  
- **Tie to technology adoption.**  
  The paper repeatedly says “technology adoption,” but the outcomes are employment and establishments. Unless there is evidence on actual biometric adoption or technology-intensive sectors, that claim feels one step too far. Either measure adoption more directly or stop overselling that margin.
  
- **Make the comparison more conceptually powerful.**  
  The exposed-vs-exempt distinction is a means, not the message. The message should be: when private enforcement makes fixed or scale-dependent legal risk large, firms adjust on size and location margins.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

The world is moving toward stronger data privacy protections, and policymakers increasingly use private rights of action and statutory damages to enforce those protections. Firms face rising legal exposure when they collect sensitive data, including biometrics.

### Tension

We know a fair amount about the effects of privacy rules in principle, but much less about whether **enforcement intensity**, especially via private litigation, materially changes economic activity. The key tension is that privacy protection may generate social benefits, but it may also impose uncertain and potentially scale-dependent costs on firms.

### Resolution

The paper finds that after *Rosenbach*, employment in biometric-exposed sectors in Illinois fell relative to exempt sectors and neighboring states, while establishment counts rose. The paper interprets this as evidence that litigation risk acts like a tax, disproportionately burdening larger employers and shifting industry structure.

### Implications

Enforcement architecture matters. If private-right-of-action regimes induce downsizing, fragmentation, or relocation, then the design of privacy law affects labor markets and firm structure, not just compliance behavior.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

Serviceable, but not fully disciplined. It has the ingredients of a strong narrative, but at times it feels like a collection of plausible empirical findings surrounded by assertive interpretation. The best story is visible, but the paper does not commit to it cleanly enough.

The paper should be telling one story:

> A dormant privacy law became economically important when courts made it privately enforceable at scale. Firms responded not just by shrinking, but by changing organizational form. Therefore, the incidence of privacy regulation depends crucially on enforcement design.

That is much stronger than the current story, which oscillates among privacy regulation, litigation risk, border-county employment, and technology adoption.
