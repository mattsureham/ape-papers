## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple and potentially important question: when governments use digital tax enforcement to push firms into the formal sector, do those gains persist if the enforcement system is later dismantled? Using the Czech Republic’s 2023 abolition of electronic sales registration—the author claims the only such reversal in a developed country—the paper argues that formalization in cash-intensive sectors did not unwind, suggesting a “compliance ratchet,” while business entry rose in less-targeted sectors where the system may have acted mainly as administrative burden.

A busy economist should care because the policy world has embraced digitized enforcement systems on the implicit premise that compliance gains are durable, yet almost all existing evidence studies introduction, not repeal. If this paper really speaks to persistence rather than just adoption effects, it is addressing a first-order question for tax capacity, informality, and state-building.

### Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Mostly yes, but not optimally. The current introduction is already much better than many submissions: it has a clear policy event, a clear question, and an intuitive novelty claim. Still, it oversells uniqueness before locking down why the result changes how economists think, and it gets pulled too quickly into the institutional details of EET. The sharper pitch is not “Czechia did something unique,” but “we finally observe the off-switch on digital enforcement, which lets us ask whether formalization is state-dependent or persistent.”

### What the first two paragraphs should say instead

Governments around the world are investing heavily in digital tax enforcement—e-invoicing, fiscal cash registers, real-time reporting—to bring firms into the formal sector. But the key policy question is not only whether these systems increase compliance when introduced; it is whether formalization persists once the monitoring technology is no longer in place. If firms revert to informality when enforcement is relaxed, digital tax capacity must be maintained indefinitely. If not, such systems may create durable changes in firm behavior.

This paper studies the rare case that identifies that question directly: the Czech Republic’s abolition in 2023 of its real-time electronic sales registration system after seven years of operation. Using cross-country, cross-sector quarterly business registration data, I show that abolition did not lead to a rebound of informality in the cash-intensive sectors the system was designed to police, while registrations rose in other sectors where the system likely imposed administrative burdens. The central implication is that digital enforcement may create persistent formalization in targeted sectors, but at the cost of deterring entry elsewhere.

That is the pitch the paper should own.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### Contribution in one sentence

The paper’s contribution is to use the abolition of a digital tax enforcement system to ask whether formalization persists after enforcement is removed, and to argue that persistence is strongest in the cash-intensive sectors the policy targeted.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest papers?

Partially. The paper does a decent job differentiating itself from the tax-enforcement-introduction literature, but the differentiation is still too mechanical: “they study introduction, I study repeal.” That is novelty, but not yet significance. The paper needs to distinguish itself from at least three adjacent conversations:

1. tax enforcement and third-party reporting,
2. formalization and informality persistence,
3. administrative burden and business entry.

Right now it is closest to a “reverse-treatment” paper in the first literature, but its actual outcome—business registrations—makes it partly a paper about entry costs and extensive-margin formalization. That creates some slippage between the question posed and the evidence delivered.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a gap in a literature?

Mostly as a world question, which is good. “Do formalization gains survive removal of enforcement?” is a world question. The strongest parts of the introduction do this well.

But the paper drifts into literature-gap language too often: “no prior work examines reversal,” “first symmetric test,” etc. “First” is not enough. The key claim should be: policymakers do not know whether digital compliance systems create durable state capacity or only temporary compliance while the machine is switched on.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the intro?

Yes, but with caveats. A good reader could say: “It uses the Czech repeal of e-tax enforcement to see whether formalization persists after the policy is removed.” That is a clean new angle.

But they could also easily say: “It’s another DiD paper on tax administration, except with repeal instead of rollout, and the main outcome is business registrations.” That second version is the danger. The paper is not yet far enough from “another policy-event design.”

### What would make the contribution bigger?

Several specific possibilities:

- **A better-aligned outcome variable.** If the paper wants to claim “compliance ratchet,” registrations are only an indirect proxy. A direct outcome on reported sales, VAT remittances, taxable income, or firm survival/formality status would make the contribution much larger.
- **A cleaner within-country comparison.** The most interesting result is sectoral heterogeneity. The paper should lean much harder into targeted vs non-targeted sectors as the core design, not the aggregate Czech-vs-controls result.
- **A mechanism that disciplines interpretation.** Is persistence driven by sunk compliance investments, buyer-seller network formalization, or norms? Even descriptive evidence distinguishing these would help.
- **A reframing toward dynamic state capacity.** The big question is not just tax enforcement but whether temporary enforcement investments can have durable effects. That would broaden the appeal.

At present, the contribution is interesting but somewhat undercapitalized.

### How should the paper position itself relative to those neighbors?

Mostly **build on**, not attack. The right line is:

- Pomeranz/Kleven/Naritomi show why digital or third-party enforcement works at introduction.
- This paper asks whether those gains are temporary or durable when enforcement is turned off.
- The result suggests that introduction studies may understate long-run returns if effects persist, but also understate non-target administrative costs if enforcement burdens unrelated firms.

That is a constructive extension, not a critique.

### Is the paper positioned too narrowly or too broadly?

Oddly, both.

- **Too narrowly** in the sense that it leans heavily on one quirky institutional event in Czechia and on the “first repeal” angle.
- **Too broadly** in the sense that it invokes formalization, administrative burden, hysteresis, shadow economy, and policy persistence all at once without fully deciding which conversation it wants to lead.

My read: the paper should narrow the **claim** but broaden the **conversation**. Lead with digital enforcement persistence in tax capacity; then use the burden result as a secondary implication.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?

It seems under-engaged with:

- the broader **state capacity** literature;
- the **regulation/entry** literature beyond token citations;
- work on **persistence and hysteresis in institutional change** at the micro level;
- recent public finance work on **e-invoicing / tax digitization in developing countries**.

It also needs to speak more explicitly to the literature on **extensive vs intensive margins of formality**. Since the outcome is registrations, that distinction is not a caveat buried later; it is central to the interpretation.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?

Not quite yet. The current conversation is “tax compliance reversal,” but the data are more naturally speaking to “does digital enforcement create persistent formalization and how are those effects distributed across sectors?” That connects tax administration to business dynamism and state capacity in a way that is more interesting than a narrow tax-compliance note.

The highest-payoff reframing may be: **Can temporary enforcement investments durably reshape formal sector participation, or do they require permanent monitoring to sustain compliance?** That is a bigger and better conversation.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Governments increasingly rely on digital enforcement systems to formalize economic activity, and existing research shows these systems can improve compliance when introduced.

### Tension

But we do not know whether those gains survive once the enforcement technology is removed. That is a major unresolved issue because the long-run value of digital tax infrastructure depends on persistence, not just immediate treatment effects.

### Resolution

The paper finds no reversal in the cash-intensive sectors that EET was designed to police, but positive effects on registrations in less-targeted sectors, suggesting persistent formalization where enforcement mattered and reduced entry barriers where compliance burdens dominated.

### Implications

The policy payoff is twofold: digital enforcement may generate durable formalization in targeted sectors, but policymakers should account for compliance burdens imposed on firms for which the technology generates little enforcement value.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

Yes, but it is not yet disciplined enough. There is a real story here. The problem is that the paper tells **two stories**:

1. a persistence-of-formalization story;
2. an administrative-burden-on-entry story.

Those can coexist, but one must clearly be primary. Right now the paper keeps toggling between them, which makes the narrative feel less crisp than it should.

My advice: make the story:

- **Main question:** Are digital-enforcement gains persistent after repeal?
- **Main answer:** In the sectors most exposed to anti-evasion enforcement, we do not see reversal.
- **Secondary insight:** The repeal increases registrations elsewhere, implying substantial compliance burdens in non-target sectors.

That hierarchy would make the paper feel designed rather than assembled.

At present, there is still some sense of “results looking for a story,” especially because the aggregate positive effect and the key heterogeneity are doing different conceptual work. The heterogeneity is the paper. The aggregate result is mostly context.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?

I would lead with: **When the Czech Republic abolished real-time digital tax reporting, business registrations did not fall in the cash-intensive sectors the system was meant to discipline.**

That is the fact that gives the paper a reason to exist.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

Economists in public finance, development, political economy, and informality would lean in—at least initially—because the policy event is unusual and the question is important. But the room will cool quickly if they realize the evidence is business registrations rather than direct compliance outcomes. The immediate reaction will be interest followed by interpretive skepticism.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Almost certainly: **“But does this measure actual compliance, or just entry?”**

That is the paper’s central vulnerability at the level of positioning, not econometrics. The paper knows this, but it currently treats it as a limitation section issue. It is actually the first question every good audience member will ask, so the introduction should preempt it and explain why registrations are informative for the persistence question.

### If findings are modest or mixed, is that okay?

Yes—if framed correctly. The null in targeted sectors is interesting, not because nulls are inherently exciting, but because here the null is theory-relevant. The paper is strongest when it says: “The absence of reversal is itself informative about persistence.” That is a valuable insight.

But the paper needs to make the null feel like a substantive result rather than a fallback after a weak aggregate design. Right now, because the aggregate result is front and center and then partially softened, the paper risks sounding like: “The main estimate is shaky, but look at this subgroup pattern.” That is not fatal, but it is not ideal. The subgroup pattern should be the headline from the start.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The biggest gap is **not primarily framing**, though framing needs work. It is a mix of **scope** and **novelty depth**.

The event is novel. The question is good. But the paper is still one unusual policy reversal with a somewhat indirect outcome. For AER, novelty of event alone is not enough; the paper needs to change how leading economists think about a broad issue. Right now it suggests an interesting possibility more than it settles a central debate.

### What is the main problem?

If I have to pick one: **scope problem.**

The question is bigger than the evidence currently marshaled to answer it. “Compliance ratchet” is a large claim; business registrations are a relatively narrow window. The paper partly compensates with clever heterogeneity, but not fully.

Secondarily, there is a **framing problem**: the paper has not fully chosen whether it is about persistence of formalization or costs of administrative burden. It should choose the former as the main question and use the latter as an important ancillary finding.

### What is the single most impactful advice?

**Rebuild the paper around the targeted-vs-nontargeted sector contrast as the central result, and explicitly frame the contribution as evidence on the persistence of formalization rather than as an aggregate repeal effect.**

That one change would do several things at once:
- make the story sharper,
- align the evidence more closely with the question,
- reduce dependence on the shakier aggregate comparison,
- and give the paper a cleaner conceptual hook.

If the author can also add more direct evidence on compliance behavior or formal-sector persistence, then the paper moves much closer to top-tier territory. But if only one thing changes, it should be the narrative architecture around the heterogeneity.
