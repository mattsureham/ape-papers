## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper exploits a rare policy reversal—the abolition of digital tax enforcement in the Czech Republic—to test whether the formalization of the shadow economy persists once the threat of enforcement is removed. It asks: Is tax compliance a "ratchet" that stays in place due to sunk costs and social norms, or does it require permanent surveillance?

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this pitch exceptionally well. The first two paragraphs are "AER-ready." They hook the reader with the rarity of the event ("Until now") and immediately connect it to a high-stakes global policy debate (the cost-benefit of digital infrastructure in developing nations). No rewrite of the pitch is necessary.

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution:** The paper provides the first empirical test of "formalization hysteresis" by showing that removing digital sales monitoring does not lead to a drop in formal business registrations in the sectors most prone to tax evasion.

*   **Differentiation:** It is clearly differentiated. While the "Big 3" of this literature (Naritomi 2019, Pomeranz 2015, Kleven 2011) look at the *introduction* of technology, this is the first to look at the *removal*. 
*   **Framing:** It is framed as a question about the world (persistence of behavior).
*   **Clarity:** A smart economist would immediately understand this as the "symmetry check" for the digital enforcement literature.
*   **Expansion:** To make the contribution bigger, the paper needs to move beyond "registrations" (extensive margin) to "revenue" (intensive margin). If the author could show that VAT revenue stayed flat while the enforcement was dismantled, the "ratchet" story would be undeniable.

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

*   **Neighbors:** Naritomi (2019), Pomeranz (2015), Ulyssea (2018), and Slemrod & Yitzhaki (2006).
*   **Positioning:** The paper correctly builds on the first two (as the "missing half" of the experiment) and synthesizes the latter two by showing a trade-off between enforcement and administrative burden.
*   **Conversation:** It is having the right conversation, but it could speak more to the **Industrial Organization** of the shadow economy. Does the ratchet exist because firms have already paid the sunk cost of accounting software, or because their B2B customers now demand formal receipts?
*   **Unexpected Connection:** Connecting this to the literature on **"Institutional Hysteresis"** (e.g., Acemoglu et al.) would elevate it from a Public Finance paper to a general-interest Economics paper.

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

*   **Setup:** The world is rapidly adopting digital tax tools under the assumption that they permanently fix the shadow economy.
*   **Tension:** We don't actually know if this is true, or if we are just creating an "enforcement treadmill" that requires perpetual, costly monitoring.
*   **Resolution:** The Czech case suggests a "Compliance Ratchet": the gains in the shadow economy persist, while the removal of the system actually *boosts* growth in low-evasion sectors by reducing red tape.
*   **Implications:** Governments should focus on "one-time" formalization shocks and then potentially "sunset" the high-burden administrative requirements once a new equilibrium is reached.

**Reaction:** People lean in. The finding is counter-intuitive and suggests a rare "win-win" in policy (keeping the revenue while cutting the bureaucracy).

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

**The Gap:** Currently, the paper relies on "Business Registrations" as a proxy for compliance. An AER audience will worry that registration is just "the first step" and doesn't prove that firms are actually paying their taxes (intensive margin).

**The Single Most Impactful Advice:** Find a way to incorporate **VAT Revenue data**, even if it is at a lower frequency or less "clean" than the registration data. If the author can show that the Czech Treasury didn't see a collapse in VAT from restaurants in 2023, the paper becomes a landmark study on the permanence of institutional change.
