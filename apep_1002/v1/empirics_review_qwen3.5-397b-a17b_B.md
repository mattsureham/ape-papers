2. **Summary**
This paper provides a novel investigation into the "compliance ratchet" hypothesis by examining the Czech Republic's 2023 abolition of its Electronic Sales Registration (EET) system. Using a difference-in-differences framework with five control countries, the author finds that while aggregate business registrations increased following abolition, this effect was driven entirely by non-cash sectors (construction, transport), suggesting relief from administrative burden. Crucially, cash-intensive sectors targeted by EET showed no significant change, implying that formalization gains may persist even after enforcement is removed. The paper offers a unique contribution to the tax compliance literature by being the first to study enforcement reversal, though the findings are constrained by inference challenges inherent to the small number of country-level clusters.

3. **Essential Points**
The authors must address the following three critical issues before publication:

1.  **Inference with Few Clusters:** The identification relies on variation across only six countries. The admission that the wild cluster bootstrap $p$-value is 0.32 (Table 3) is a major limitation that undermines the causal claim for the aggregate effect. Relying on country$\times$sector clustering (54 clusters) assumes independence across sectors within countries, which is dubious for macro-level policy shocks. The paper cannot claim statistical significance at conventional levels for the main aggregate result without further robustness evidence (e.g., Synthetic Control Method permutation tests).
2.  **Outcome Validity (Registrations vs. Informality):** The paper equates "business registrations" with "formalization." However, an increase in registrations after abolishing enforcement could imply reduced administrative burden rather than sustained compliance. Conversely, a null result in cash sectors could mean firms stayed formal, or it could mean informal firms simply stopped registering *and* stopped reporting (disappearing from the data entirely). Without a measure of the tax gap or VAT revenue (which was in the original Manifest), the claim that "formalization persists" is indirect.
3.  **Control Group Homogeneity:** The parallel trends assumption is visually supported only by the event study coefficients, but the control countries (Italy, Poland, Hungary, etc.) all have distinct fiscal systems and economic trajectories. For instance, Italy's electronic invoicing mandates expanded significantly during this period. The paper needs to explicitly rule out concurrent policy shocks in control countries that might drive the divergence in 2023, particularly given the post-pandemic recovery heterogeneity.

4. **Suggestions**
The following recommendations are intended to strengthen the paper's empirical robustness and narrative clarity. While not strictly essential for the core argument, addressing these would significantly elevate the paper's suitability for a top-tier field journal.

    *   **Implement Synthetic Control Method (SCM):** Given the $N=1$ treated unit problem, SCM is ideally suited for this design. Construct a "Synthetic Czech Republic" using a weighted combination of the five control countries to match pre-2023 trends in business registrations. This provides a visual and statistical counterfactual that is often more persuasive than DiD with few clusters. Include a permutation test where the treatment is assigned to each control country to generate a distribution of placebo effects; if the Czech effect lies in the extreme tail, confidence in the result increases despite the low $N$.
    *   **Reintroduce VAT Revenue Data:** The Original Idea Manifest correctly identified VAT revenue (% GDP) as a critical outcome. Business registrations measure *entry*, but VAT revenue measures *compliance intensity*. If formalization persisted (the "ratchet" hypothesis), VAT revenue in Czechia should not have collapsed relative to controls post-2023. If VAT revenue dropped sharply while registrations stayed flat, it suggests firms registered but returned to off-book trading. Adding this series (GOV_10A_TAXAG) would triangulate the findings and address the outcome validity concern.
    *   **Reconcile Raw Data vs. DiD Results:** The Manifest noted a raw 40% spike in accommodation registrations (93.7 to 131.6), while the paper reports a null DiD effect. This discrepancy will confuse readers. Explicitly address this in the text: did control countries also experience a Q1 2023 seasonal spike? Did inflation drive nominal registration value increases? A figure plotting the raw Czech series against the synthetic control or average control series would clarify whether the "smoke test" spike was a common shock absorbed by the fixed effects.
    *   **Visualizing the Event Study:** The event study results are presented in a table (Table 2). For an *AER: Insights* format, a graphical event study plot is standard and essential. Plot the coefficients $\beta_k$ with confidence intervals over time. This allows readers to visually assess the parallel pre-trends and the dynamic onset of the effect (which the text notes was delayed until $t+4$).
    *   **Clarify the Mechanism of "Burden":** The paper argues that non-cash sectors (construction/transport) saw increased registrations due to reduced administrative burden. To strengthen this, consider adding a brief discussion or citation linking EET reporting requirements
