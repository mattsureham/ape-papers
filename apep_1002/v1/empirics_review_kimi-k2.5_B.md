### 1. Idea Fidelity

The paper successfully pursues the core research question articulated in the manifest: exploiting the Czech EET abolition as a unique reversal experiment to test whether digital enforcement creates persistent formalization. The empirical focus on Eurostat's STS\_RB\_Q data and the cross-country difference-in-differences design align with the proposed strategy.

However, the paper deviates from the manifest in three important ways. First, it abandons the within-Czech staggered DiD strategy (exploiting differential exposure across the four EET phases) as a primary identification approach, relegating sectoral phase timing to a heterogeneity analysis only. This is a missed opportunity; the staggered rollout provides within-country variation that could alleviate the severe clustering issues discussed below. Second, the paper fails to utilize the supplementary Eurostat datasets mentioned in the manifest—specifically the business demography data (BD\_9BD\_SZ\_CL\_R2) and VAT revenue series (GOV\_10A\_TAXAG)—which would have been valuable for validating mechanisms beyond business entry. Third, the findings contradict the manifest's specific prediction regarding Phase 1 sectors; where the manifest hypothesized a "post-abolition registration surge" in cash-intensive sectors if formalization were elastic, the paper finds precise nulls. While the paper's actual results are interesting, authors should reconcile this divergence or clarify their theoretical predictions.

### 2. Summary

This paper exploits the January 2023 abolition of the Czech Electronic Sales Registration (EET) system—the first reversal of a functioning digital tax enforcement regime in a developed country—to test whether formalization gains persist after enforcement removal. Using quarterly business registration data from Eurostat (2015–2025) in a difference-in-differences framework comparing the Czech Republic to five control countries, the author finds that aggregate business registrations increased by 35 index points (0.69 SD) following abolition. Critically, this effect is entirely driven by non-cash-intensive sectors (construction, transport), while cash-intensive sectors (accommodation, retail)—the primary targets of EET enforcement—show no significant change, interpreted as evidence of a "compliance ratchet" in formalization behavior.

### 3. Essential Points

**Severe Inference Limitations.** With only six country-level clusters, conventional cluster-robust inference is invalid, and the wild cluster bootstrap $p$-value of 0.32 for the main specification indicates the aggregate effect is not statistically significant under credible inference. While the author acknowledges this limitation, the abstract and introduction nonetheless emphasize the aggregate 35-point increase as a primary finding. The sectoral heterogeneity analysis suffers from the same problem (only 6 clusters), rendering the null result in cash-intensive sectors uninformative—the confidence interval likely includes economically meaningful effects in both directions. The paper requires either substantially stronger identification strategies (e.g., synthetic control methods, additional control countries) or a fundamental reframing that treats the analysis as exploratory/descriptive rather than causal with precise null effects.

**Violation of Parallel Trends.** The event study (\Cref{tab:eventstudy}) reveals significant pre-trends at $t-7$ and $t-5$ (2021), with coefficients of $-24.09$ and $-19.47$ respectively. The author attributes these to "heterogeneous COVID recovery patterns," but this explanation directly undermines the identifying assumption; if Czech sectors were differentially recovering from COVID in 2021, they likely continued to diverge in 2023 for reasons unrelated to EET abolition. The four quarters immediately preceding treatment ($t-4$ to $t-1$) appear flat, but the earlier deviations suggest the common trends assumption is violated. The paper must address this through more flexible trend adjustments (e.g., unit-specific trends interacted with COVID indicators) or alternative estimators robust to staggered differential trends.

**Mismatch Between Outcome and Research Question.** The paper tests "formalization persistence" using business registrations, but registrations measure the *extensive margin* of entry into formality, not the *intensive margin* of compliance behavior by existing firms. The EET was designed to reduce shadow-economy activity among incumbent businesses (cash underreporting), not necessarily to increase business formation. The finding that registrations rose in construction/transport—sectors where EET was not primarily an enforcement tool but an administrative burden—conflates two distinct mechanisms: (1) persistent compliance in cash sectors (the "ratchet"), and (2) deregulation effects elsewhere. Without data on actual tax compliance (e.g., VAT revenues, audit rates, or reported sales), the claim that formalization "persists" in cash sectors relies on an untested assumption that null effects on entry reflect continued compliance by incumbents.

### 4. Suggestions

**Address the Clustering Problem Directly.** Given the $N=6$ cluster constraint, I recommend the authors pivot to a synthetic control design using sector-specific synthetic controls or adopt the imputation estimator of \citet{borusyak2024revisiting}, which can improve precision with staggered adoption designs. Alternatively, exploit the within-Czech staggered phase-in (Phases 1–4) as a separate identification strategy using the stacked DiD approach of \citet{callaway2021difference}. This would leverage 9 sectors $\times$ 4 phases $\times$ 44 quarters ($\sim$1,500 observations) with variation at the sector-quarter level, potentially allowing clustering at the sector level or using two-way clustering.

**Clarify the Mechanism with Additional Data.** The manifest identified Eurostat GOV\_10A\_TAXAG (VAT revenue \% GDP) and BD\_9BD\_SZ\_CL\_R2 (business demography) as available data sources. I strongly urge the authors to incorporate these. If VAT revenues remained stable in cash-intensive sectors relative to controls following abolition, this would provide credible evidence for the compliance ratchet. Similarly, business demography data on firm deaths/exits would help distinguish between "persistence" (firms stay formal) and "irrelevance" (firms ignore the abolition because they had already exited or never complied).

**Reconcile the COVID Suspension.** The EET was suspended from March 2020 to December 2022, meaning the "treatment" is technically the *permanent* abolition following a temporary suspension, not the removal of actively enforced registration. The paper should explicitly model this three-period structure (Enforcement $\to$ Suspension $\to$ Abolition) rather than treating the pre-2023 period as uniformly "treated" in the policy-intensity sense. A triple-difference design comparing Czech sectors by their intensity of EET use during the enforcement period (2016–2020) could isolate whether the permanent abolition mattered differently than the temporary suspension.

**Interpret Nulls Appropriately.** The claim that "the compliance ratchet holds" based on a null effect in cash-intensive sectors ($\hat{\beta} = -7.2$, $p = 0.36$) is statistically inappropriate. With only 6 clusters and wide confidence intervals ($\approx [-21, +7]$), the data cannot rule out meaningful reversals or surges. The abstract should emphasize the *absence of evidence* for reversal rather than evidence of absence. Alternatively, use the non-cash sector results (where the effect is large and positive in some specifications) to argue that the EET imposed heterogeneous administrative costs, while remaining agnostic about persistence in cash sectors pending better-powered tests.

**Improve Control Group Validity.** Sweden is an odd control for the Czech Republic given vast differences in institutional development and shadow economy size. Consider dropping Sweden or demonstrating that results are robust to excluding each control individually (the appendix shows this, but the main text should discuss why Hungary and Poland are better comparators than Sweden). Additionally, verify that control countries did not experience contemporaneous policy changes affecting business registration (e.g., Italy's e-invoicing expansion in 2023).

**Revisit the Staggered Phase Design.** The manifest's original "smoke test" suggested that Phase 1 sectors (accommodation, 6+ years of enforcement) would show the strongest post-abolition response under the "elastic" hypothesis. The finding that Phase 1 shows nulls while Phase 3–4 shows large positive effects is intriguing but underdeveloped. Consider whether this reflects: (a) attrition of the most informal firms during 2016–2020, (b) differential compliance costs across sectors, or (c) that Phase 1 sectors had already adjusted to formality by 2023. A survival analysis of firms born during different EET phases could illuminate this.

**Refine the Theoretical Framework.** The introduction posits a "compliance ratchet" where formalization persists due to sunk costs and network effects, yet these mechanisms are not tested. Consider surveying a sample of Czech businesses post-abolition on their reasons for maintaining formal status (or discuss existing survey evidence). Without such evidence, the persistence interpretation remains speculative.

In summary, this paper addresses a genuinely important and novel question with a fascinating natural experiment. However, the severe inference constraints, pre-trend violations, and measurement challenges currently prevent it from supporting its strong claims about causal effects and behavioral persistence. A major revision that addresses the clustering problem (perhaps through design changes rather than just robustness checks), reconciles the COVID suspension period, and brings in compliance data (VAT revenues) would significantly strengthen the contribution.
