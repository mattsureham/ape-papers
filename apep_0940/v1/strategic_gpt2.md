## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether one of Europe’s most visibly punitive place-based integration policies—Denmark’s 2018 designation of certain housing estates as “parallel societies”/“ghettos”—actually changed where immigrant-origin residents live. Using population registers, it finds no detectable change in municipality-level demographic composition: the state can publicly stigmatize neighborhoods and threaten demolition, yet that does not translate into municipality-wide sorting.

A busy economist should care because the paper sits at the intersection of place-based policy, migration/sorting, and the political economy of integration policy. The broader question is not “what happened in Denmark?” but “can official territorial stigma reshape urban demographic patterns, or is physical housing supply the only margin that matters?”

### Does the paper articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Not quite. The current opening is vivid and morally charged, but the paper’s actual contribution is narrower than the rhetoric: it does **not** show whether residents left designated estates, only whether municipalities changed composition. The introduction gets to that caveat by paragraph four, which is too late given that the caveat is central to the paper’s claim. Right now the paper risks overselling “stigma without sorting” when the actual result is “no municipality-level compositional response.”

### What the first two paragraphs should say instead

The paper should open with the broad economic question and immediately define the margin of adjustment it can observe. Something like:

> Governments increasingly use place-based labeling to change neighborhood composition: they identify areas as troubled, stigmatize them publicly, and often attach sanctions or redevelopment mandates. But it remains unclear whether such designation policies actually induce residential sorting at a meaningful spatial scale, or whether they mainly create political theater unless accompanied by direct changes in housing supply.
>
> This paper studies Denmark’s 2018 “Ghetto Package,” which labeled 29 public housing estates as “parallel societies” and attached unusually strong consequences, including demolition mandates, enhanced penalties, and integration requirements. Using population register data, I ask whether municipalities containing designated estates experienced subsequent changes in their non-Western immigrant-origin population share. I find a precise null at the municipality level: despite an exceptionally salient and punitive designation regime, treated municipalities did not become less immigrant-concentrated.

That pitch is cleaner, more honest about the spatial level, and stronger because it foregrounds the general question: **can symbolic place-based policy move people?**

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### Contribution in one sentence

The paper shows that Denmark’s highly salient neighborhood-labeling policy did not produce detectable **municipality-level** changes in immigrant-origin population composition, suggesting that official territorial stigma alone may be insufficient to generate aggregate residential sorting.

### Is this clearly differentiated from the closest papers?

Only partially. The paper gestures at three literatures, but the differentiation is still muddy. As written, the contribution could easily be heard as “another reduced-form paper on a controversial housing policy.” It needs sharper contrast along two dimensions:

1. **Against neighborhood-effects papers**: this is not another paper on how neighborhood assignment affects outcomes; it is about whether **designation changes sorting patterns**.
2. **Against stigma/territorial-labeling work**: much of that literature is qualitative or descriptive; this paper offers aggregate causal evidence on whether labeling changes composition.
3. **Against Danish policy papers**: it is not about labor-market or criminal outcomes of immigrant policy; it is about whether the state can reengineer urban demography through designation.

Right now these distinctions are present but not crisp.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a literature gap?

It is mixed, but too often it slips into literature-gap language (“first causal estimate in a European register-data context”). That is not an AER-level sell. The stronger framing is world-facing:

- **World question:** Can governments use public labeling and stigma to alter urban demographic patterns?
- **Policy question:** Does symbolic coercion change where people live absent large-scale physical relocation?

That is the version worth foregrounding.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?

Not confidently. They might say: “It’s a DiD on Denmark’s ghetto policy and finds a null.” That is not enough.

The introduction needs to make them say something more like:

> “It’s about whether official neighborhood stigma actually causes residential sorting. In Denmark, even a very aggressive designation regime didn’t move municipality-level composition.”

That is a much better takeaway.

### What would make the contribution bigger?

Most of the ways to make it bigger are about **scope and level of outcome**, not identification.

1. **Move to a more behaviorally direct outcome**  
   The best version would study actual moves: out-migration from designated estates, in-migration elsewhere in municipality, tenure conversion, school entry, or housing transactions. “Population share” is one step removed from the sorting mechanism.

2. **Exploit within-municipality geography**  
   The paper’s biggest limitation is also the obvious path to a larger contribution. If it can show:
   - no municipality-level change,
   - but clear estate-level displacement or re-sorting,
   then the paper becomes substantively sharper: stigma may reallocate people locally without changing city-wide composition.

3. **Separate stigma from demolition/housing-supply channels**  
   The paper wants to say something about stigma, but the policy bundles stigma with hard supply-side intervention. A bigger contribution would frame the paper around the distinction:
   - symbolic designation,
   - versus physical redevelopment.
   If the paper can show that only the latter matters, that is a much stronger statement.

4. **Use a more revealing comparison**
   Compare municipalities with designated estates to municipalities with similar vulnerable estates that narrowly escaped designation, or compare designated estates to non-designated vulnerable estates. Even if not for identification here, narratively that would better isolate the question economists care about: did the label matter above and beyond preexisting disadvantage?

### How should the paper position itself relative to those neighbors?

Mostly **build on and redirect**, not attack.

- Against the neighborhood-effects literature: “That literature studies what neighborhoods do to people; this paper asks whether policy-induced labeling changes who lives where.”

- Against demolition/redevelopment papers: “Those papers often find effects when housing stock changes; my findings suggest designation alone does not imply aggregate compositional change.”

The paper should not pretend to refute the territorial stigma literature broadly. It has too aggregate an outcome for that. Better to present itself as establishing a **boundary condition**: stigma does not obviously show up in municipality-wide composition, even in an extreme case.

- **Too narrowly** in reading like a Denmark-policy note rather than a paper about the economics of symbolic place-based policy.

It should narrow the claim and broaden the question.

It should engage more directly with:

Right now the citations lean heavily sociological/urban-studies on stigma and then classic neighborhood-effects papers. That creates a slight mismatch. Economists will want a clearer bridge to sorting, housing supply, and place-based policy.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?

Not quite yet. The highest-value conversation is not “territorial stigma exists.” It is:

That conversation connects urban economics, public economics, and political economy. It is much more AER-relevant than a narrower “test of territorial stigma theory.”

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Governments want to break up concentrated disadvantage and immigrant enclaves. One increasingly visible tool is public neighborhood designation: naming places as troubled, incompatible, or in need of intervention.

### Tension

Such designation is politically salient and theoretically powerful—if you stigmatize a neighborhood and threaten sanctions or demolition, many observers expect residents to leave and composition to change. But we do not know whether this sort of symbolic-plus-administrative intervention actually produces sorting at a meaningful aggregate scale.

### Resolution

In Denmark’s especially extreme case, municipality-level composition does not change detectably after designation. Whatever else the policy did, it did not transform the demographic makeup of the municipalities containing designated estates.

### Implications

This should update beliefs about the potency of symbolic place-based policy. If policymakers want demographic transformation, labeling alone may be weak; physical changes to housing stock may be the operative channel. It also tells researchers to distinguish sharply between estate-level displacement and city-level demographic change.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the ingredients, but the arc is not fully controlled. Right now it oscillates between three possible stories:

1. **A paper about stigma**
2. **A paper about displacement**
3. **A paper about a null policy effect in Denmark**

Because these are not the same story, the paper sometimes feels like results looking for the right frame.

### What story should it be telling?

The cleanest story is:

> Governments often hope to change neighborhood composition by labeling places as socially problematic. Denmark offers an extreme test. The label was highly salient and backed by serious sanctions, yet there was no municipality-level sorting response. This implies that symbolic designation is not enough to generate aggregate demographic change; if these policies matter, they likely do so through more local or more physical channels.

That story is coherent, disciplined, and appropriately scaled to the data.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?

“Denmark publicly labeled 29 housing estates as ‘parallel societies,’ threatened demolition, doubled penalties in those areas, and still saw no detectable change in the immigrant-origin share of the municipalities that contained them.”

That is the line.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

They would lean in initially because the setting is vivid and politically charged. But interest would fade quickly if the next sentence were, “at the municipality level,” unless the speaker immediately explains why that is still substantively interesting.

So the paper has a good **hook**, but a vulnerable **second beat**. It needs to explain right away why a null municipality-level effect is still informative:
- because the policy’s rhetoric aimed at large-scale transformation,
- because the treatment was unusually salient,
- because seven years is long enough that a meaningful city-wide effect should have shown up if it existed.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Almost certainly:

> “Fine, but did people leave the designated estates and just move elsewhere in the same municipality?”

That is the central follow-up, and the paper must own it rather than treat it as a minor caveat. At present, that is the real question the paper leaves unanswered.

### Are the null findings themselves interesting?

Yes, but only if framed correctly. A null is interesting here because:
- the policy is unusually dramatic,
- the intended demographic target is explicit,
- the policy was politically sold as a transformational intervention,
- and the paper can rule out large municipality-wide effects.

The null becomes uninteresting when the paper sounds like it wanted to measure displacement but only had municipality-level aggregates. Then it feels like a failed experiment. The paper must instead argue that it is testing a distinct hypothesis: **did designation alter aggregate municipal composition?** On that question, the answer is informative.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

At the moment, the main gap is a mix of **framing problem** and **scope problem**.

### Framing problem
The paper has a potentially strong question—can symbolic place-based policy change who lives where?—but it is not yet framed with enough precision and confidence. It drifts between “stigma,” “demographic displacement,” and “Danish policy evaluation.” It needs a tighter conceptual center.

### Scope problem
For AER, the current outcome is probably too coarse on its own. Municipality-level composition is one layer too far from the mechanism. As a result, the paper’s best current claim is a boundary condition, not a definitive answer.

### Novelty problem
Moderate. The setting is novel and striking, but the design and headline finding risk feeling incremental unless the paper can make a broader conceptual point about symbolic versus physical channels in place-based policy.

### Ambition problem
Yes, somewhat. The paper is competent and cautious, but safe. It would be much more ambitious if it asked not only whether the policy changed aggregate composition, but **through which geographic and behavioral margins adjustment did or did not occur**.

### The single most impactful piece of advice

If the author can change only one thing: **reframe the paper around the distinction between symbolic designation and actual spatial re-sorting, and be explicit from page one that the paper identifies a boundary condition—no municipality-level transformation—even under an extreme labeling regime.**

If allowed one substantive expansion, the obvious next step is to add finer geographic evidence. But even without new data, the paper can become much stronger by making the claim narrower, cleaner, and more economically important.
