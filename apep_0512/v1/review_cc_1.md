## Summary

This paper studies France's abolition of the Taxe d'Habitation (TH) using a continuous-treatment DiD design, exploiting cross-commune variation in pre-reform TH rates. Two main findings: (1) modest positive capitalization of the tax cut into apartment prices (β = 0.0014, p = 0.056), and (2) strong fiscal displacement as communes raise Taxe Foncière rates to offset lost revenue (coefficient = 0.646, p < 0.01).

## Strengths

1. **Novel policy setting.** The TH abolition is a €20B+ reform largely unstudied in the English-language literature. The continuous treatment variation is well-motivated by Oates (1969).
2. **Honest engagement with limitations.** The paper transparently discusses the dept×year FE sensitivity, the unbalanced panel, and the wrong-signed γ in the net incidence decomposition.
3. **Fiscal displacement channel.** The displacement result (Table 3) is the strongest finding — highly significant with clean identification.

## Weaknesses

1. **Marginal statistical significance.** The headline capitalization result is p = 0.056 — suggestive but not definitive. The unweighted specification (Column 2) is not significant.
2. **Dept×year FE kills the estimate.** Column 3 of Table 4 shows β = -0.0004 with dept×year FE. This is discussed transparently but remains a concern for identification.
3. **Net incidence decomposition.** Part C is correctly labeled "illustrative" but still occupies significant space for a decomposition the authors acknowledge is unreliable.

## Minor Issues

- Abstract could be tighter (currently ~140 words, could trim to 120)
- The event study figure (Figure 3) shows some pre-trend concerns in 2016 — the commune merger explanation is plausible but not tested

## Recommendation

The fiscal displacement result is strong and novel. The capitalization result is suggestive. Together they tell a coherent story about how local governments respond to centrally imposed tax cuts. Conditional accept — the paper should proceed to external review.
