### 1. Idea Fidelity
The paper deviates substantially from the original idea manifest. The manifest proposed a spatial RDD focused on **teen (15-19) morning-commute fatalities** as the primary outcome, with explicit age interactions (teens vs. adults), time-of-day falsification, DST interactions, and an Arizona placebo test leveraging its no-DST policy. These elements tested the adolescent-specific prediction from circadian biology, using ~1,260 teen morning fatalities near boundaries from FARS 2010-2023. The paper instead estimates effects on **general morning (6-9:59 AM) fatal crashes** (crash-level Pr(morning|fatal) or county-level morning fatality rates per capita), omitting all teen-specific analyses despite noting 572 teen morning fatalities in the RDD sample. No age interactions, DST tests, or Arizona placebo appear. While the core spatial RDD at continental time zone boundaries and FARS geocoded data are retained, the paper pursues a broader (non-teen) question on any morning fatality discontinuity, finding a null, rather than the manifest's targeted teen causal channel.

### 2. Summary
This paper uses a spatial RDD at U.S. continental time zone boundaries to test whether chronic circadian misalignment (social jetlag) on the late-sunset (western) side increases morning traffic fatalities, drawing on 468,213 geocoded FARS crashes (2010-2023). It finds a precise null effect across crash- and county-level specifications, robust to bandwidths, falsification tests (evening crashes, placebo boundaries), and mechanisms (weekday/weekend), ruling out policy-relevant increases. The result bounds the scope of chronoeconomics, suggesting behavioral adaptation or channel-specific effects (metabolic vs. attentional) prevent chronic sleep deficits from elevating acute drowsy-driving mortality.

### 3. Essential Points
1. **Missing teen-specific analysis undermines the core causal claim and novelty.** The manifest's innovation was testing age-specific channels with FARS geocoded data—absent here despite a viable sample (572 teen morning fatalities in ±1.5° bandwidth, per Table 1). Implement the promised RDD on teen morning fatality rates (crash- or county-level), age interactions (e.g., Teen × LateSunset), and power calculations to assess precision. Without this, the paper does not engage the adolescent prediction or differentiate from prior work (e.g., Giuntella & Mazzonna, 2019).

2. **Outcome definition requires clarification for causal interpretation.** The crash-level RDD estimates the discontinuity in Pr(Morning|fatal crash), assuming stable non-morning fatal crashes. This identifies a morning risk increase only if treatment does not differentially affect total fatal crashes or non-morning risk. Report total (any-time) fatality RDDs and morning counts/rates explicitly; the county-level morning rate per capita (with population denominators) is stronger but needs year/boundary FEs consistently across tables. Address whether darker mornings (west side) mechanically reduce non-commute driving, biasing shares downward.

3. **Power and policy relevance claims need calibration.** The paper rules out "3 percentage points (25% of baseline)" but this is for general morning share; compute minimum detectable effects (MDEs) for morning fatality *rates* per capita (e.g., via simulations) and standardize across outcomes. With 63,658 RDD crashes but sparse morning events (8,162), confirm the 95% CI [-0.054, 0.010] meaningfully bounds teen-relevant effects (e.g., 10-20% teen morning rate increase).

These issues are fixable but critical; addressing them would align with the manifest and AER: Insights standards. More than three fixes signal reject outright, but these suffice for revise-and-resubmit.

### 4. Suggestions
**Strengthen identification and robustness.** Add the Arizona no-DST placebo (manifest-specified) by interacting LateSunset with an Arizona indicator or excluding it; this exploits exogenous variation in misalignment. Include DST-season interactions (e.g., standard vs. DST periods), as boundaries interact with biannual shifts. Report boundary-specific RDD plots (e.g., rdplot outputs) for Eastern/Central (highest power) vs. sparser others, and a pooled RDD with boundary FEs. For spatial RDD threats, add county-level covariate balances (e.g., income, urbanicity from ACS) beyond crash traits, and a spatial autocorrelation test (e.g., Moran’s I on residuals).

**Refine data and outcomes.** Use exact FARS person-level data for teen-involved crashes (not just "at least one teen fatal," per Table 1), distinguishing driver fatalities (most relevant for drowsy driving). Normalize morning rates by teen population exposure (ACS 15-19 counts) at county-year level, weighting by morning vehicle miles traveled (VMT) if NHTSA HPMS data available. Explore secondary outcomes: lighting conditions (darker mornings west?), single-vehicle crashes (drowsiness proxy), or FARS drowsiness attributions (though rare). Harmonize morning window (6-9:59 AM) with commute peaks; sensitivity to 6-10 AM vs. 7-9 AM.

**Enhance mechanisms and interpretation.** The weekday/weekend test is excellent but extend to teens only. Test adaptation channels indirectly: RDD on crash severity (e.g., rollover proxy for speed/impairment) or time-of-day gradients within morning hours. Discuss heterogeneous effects by rural/urban (boundaries cross varying densities) or teen driver age (15-17 vs. 18-19). For the null, quantify adaptation via ATUS sleep data (manifest-mentioned) near boundaries, or BRFSS BMI as a positive control (replicating Giuntella & Mazzonna).

**Improve presentation and AER: Insights fit.** Expand Table 1 with means/tests for RDD covariates (e.g., t-tests p-values). Add RD plots (density, outcome, residuals) as Figures 1-2, with optimal bandwidth overlays. Clarify Table 2 units (county-level rates are per 100k pop, but coefficients seem scaled oddly—e.g., -0.54 on what mean?). Move SDE appendix to main text as a power summary. Shorten intro/discussion (e.g., merge institutional background); target 15 pages total. Cite recent chronoeconomics (e.g., Shigeoka et al., 2023 on school start times) and traffic papers (e.g., Bock et al., 2023 on sleep and crashes). Emphasize policy: with null, TZ reassignments (e.g., Florida counties) unlikely to save lives via this channel, but test non-fatal injuries (GES data).

**General polish.** Fix typos (e.g., Table 2 parametric bandwidth "1.50" but N=2129 mismatches ±1.5° crash N; abstract p=0.17 vs. Table 1 p=0.32). Use consistent clustering (county for panels). Replicate Giuntella RDD on FARS BMI proxies (e.g., obesity-related crashes) as placebo. This is a strong null with excellent data/tests—fixing essentials could make it publishable, contributing a clean boundary for causal chronoeconomics.
