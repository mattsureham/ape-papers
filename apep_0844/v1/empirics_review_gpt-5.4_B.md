## 1. Idea Fidelity

The paper clearly pursues the broad topic in the manifest—state disinvestment in public higher education, tuition passthrough, and enrollment composition using IPEDS—but it departs from the core identification strategy in important ways. The manifest proposed an institution-year panel from IPEDS linked to plausibly exogenous state fiscal shocks in a Bartik-style IV design, with the causal chain running from exogenous state fiscal shocks to appropriations, then to tuition and enrollment composition. The paper instead uses a much simpler continuous-treatment DiD based on realized state cut intensity during the Great Recession. That is a substantial change, and it matters because the paper’s own discussion acknowledges that realized cuts are likely correlated with recession severity, which directly affects Pell eligibility, enrollment demand, and student composition.

The paper also drops key elements of the original design that would have been central to making a contribution. There is no first-stage analysis showing how the fiscal shock translated into institution-level state appropriations, no IV design, and no serious heterogeneity analysis by institution type (flagships vs. regionals), even though those margins are central to the policy question. Most importantly, the paper does not estimate the causal effect of appropriation changes on composition; it estimates differential post-2009 changes by states that experienced larger cuts, a design that bundles together funding cuts and broader recession shocks. So the paper is faithful to the topic, but not to the manifest’s main empirical strategy or its promised causal interpretation.

## 2. Summary

This paper uses an IPEDS panel of public four-year institutions from 2004–2022 to study whether states that cut appropriations more sharply during the Great Recession experienced larger tuition increases and changes in student composition. The main finding is a null effect on tuition and positive effects on Pell share and minority share, which the paper interprets as evidence that tuition is “sticky” and that fiscal shocks are absorbed through student-body recomposition rather than price.

## 3. Essential Points

1. **The identification strategy does not support the paper’s causal claims.**  
   The treatment variable—state cut intensity from 2007–08 to 2011–12—is itself an outcome of the recession, not a plausibly exogenous shock. States with deeper downturns likely had larger budget cuts and simultaneously larger changes in family income, Pell eligibility, college-going behavior, and racial composition of applicants. The paper explicitly acknowledges this for the composition outcomes, but then still draws strong conclusions about the effects of state disinvestment. As written, the paper does not identify the causal effect of appropriations on enrollment composition. To be publishable in an AER: Insights-style outlet, the design needs either a substantially stronger source of exogenous variation or a much more modest interpretation.

2. **The central outcome measures are not well matched to the claims being made.**  
   Pell share in IPEDS SFA is for first-time, full-time undergraduates, whereas minority share is based on total enrollment. These are composition measures, but they are not direct measures of access for low-income or minority students, and Pell share is especially contaminated by changes in eligibility rules and recession-induced income declines. A rise in Pell share does not necessarily mean low-income students were less displaced, more represented, or more served; it may simply reflect falling incomes among an unchanged or even shrinking set of entrants. The paper needs to either reframe the outcome as “measured Pell exposure” and avoid access language, or provide stronger evidence using levels, subgroup enrollments, or application/admission proxies if available.

3. **The data construction and descriptive evidence raise reliability concerns that must be resolved before the estimates can be trusted.**  
   Several summary statistics look problematic. The finance variable for state appropriations per student has implausibly enormous standard deviations, and the pre-period sample sizes in Table 1 collapse for Black and Hispanic shares (N = 44), suggesting coding or merge errors. There is also an apparent inconsistency between the claimed time span (2004–2022) and the number of year fixed effects reported (“year (11)”). Before any substantive interpretation, the authors need to audit the panel construction, variable definitions, and missingness patterns and show that the sample is coherent across modules and over time.

## 4. Suggestions

My overall view is that the paper is addressing an important question and uses a promising data source, but in its current form it does not yet make a convincing causal contribution. The best path forward is either to substantially strengthen the design or to reposition the paper as a descriptive/event-study analysis of how institutions in harder-hit states evolved during the recession.

First, I strongly encourage the authors to return to the manifest’s original logic and exploit a source of variation that is more plausibly exogenous than realized cuts. A Bartik-style fiscal exposure measure, an instrument based on pre-recession state budget composition interacted with national shocks, or another state-finance instrument would be much closer to the claimed question. Even if the IV is imperfect, it would at least separate “states had worse recessions” from “states reduced higher-ed support.” At minimum, the paper should show the first stage from the proposed exogenous shock to state appropriations per FTE, and then estimate the effects of predicted appropriations on tuition and composition. Without this, the current estimates are hard to interpret.

Second, even within a DiD framework, the paper should do much more to assess whether high-cut and low-cut states were on similar pre-trends and whether those states differed systematically in recession exposure. The event-study specification is mentioned but not shown; it should be central. The authors should present event-study graphs for tuition, Pell share, minority share, and enrollment, with pre-trend tests reported transparently. They should also add controls for state unemployment, state income per capita, housing-price collapse exposure, and perhaps state-specific linear trends. These additions will not solve the identification problem, but they would help clarify whether the results are mostly tracking differential macroeconomic distress.

Third, the paper’s strongest result may actually be the tuition null, because tuition is more institution- or state-policy-determined than Pell eligibility. But even here, the current interpretation is too strong. “No differential tuition response by cut intensity” is not equivalent to “tuition is sticky” unless the paper can show that institutions actually experienced differential funding shocks and lacked other compensating margins. I would recommend estimating the reduced-form relationship between state cuts and institution-level appropriations per FTE, then between appropriations and tuition revenue, sticker tuition, and net price if possible. If tuition truly did not move, the paper should document where the budget adjustment occurred: instructional spending, staff, faculty composition, student services, or enrollment quantity. Otherwise the “sticky sticker price” framing is under-supported.

Fourth, the composition outcomes need more careful treatment. For Pell share, the paper should emphasize from the outset that this is based on first-time, full-time students and is affected by both eligibility and composition. The authors should test whether total first-time full-time enrollment changed, whether Pell-recipient counts changed in levels, and whether the denominator moved mechanically. If the Pell result is driven by recession-induced income declines, then the finding is interesting but not evidence that disinvestment reshaped access through institutional behavior. Similarly, for minority share, I would like to see separate Black and Hispanic results, preferably in both shares and levels, because the mechanisms and national trends differ sharply over this period.

Fifth, the sample and variable construction need a careful appendix. Right now, Table 1 undermines confidence in the dataset. The authors should provide a merge diagram across IPEDS modules; explain why the institution count rises from 557 to 692 across periods; explain why race-share sample sizes are tiny in the pre-period; verify whether they are using a balanced or unbalanced panel; and report the exact variable names and transformations. The finance variables in IPEDS are notoriously tricky across form changes, and the appropriations measure especially needs validation. A simple figure showing the national time series of mean state appropriations per FTE, tuition, and Pell share in the analytic sample would help readers assess whether the constructed panel behaves plausibly.

Sixth, the paper would benefit from a clearer distinction between “institutional composition” and “student welfare.” A higher Pell share after disinvestment does not imply that disadvantaged students benefited or even that access improved. It may reflect worsening macro conditions, differential departure of non-Pell students, or shrinking institutional quality that especially harms the remaining students. The conclusion should therefore be rewritten more cautiously. The current language—e.g., “the student body changes” and “bearing the burden of fiscal choices”—is plausible, but the paper does not directly show displacement, rationing, or welfare loss. It shows composition changes coincident with larger cuts.

Seventh, the authors should seriously consider heterogeneity, because the average effect across all public four-year institutions may be hiding the most policy-relevant adjustments. The manifest correctly emphasized that flagships, regional comprehensives, and less-selective institutions face very different margins of adjustment. If flagships raised out-of-state enrollment while regionals absorbed low-income students during downturns, the pooled null on nonresident share could be masking offsetting effects. Estimating separate models by selectivity, Carnegie classification, tuition-setting autonomy, or baseline dependence on state funding would make the analysis more informative and more closely aligned with the contribution envisioned in the original idea.

Eighth, I would encourage the authors to compare sticker tuition and net tuition revenue. The paper is framed around the sticker price, but institutions can and do adjust financial aid, fee structures, and discounting. If sticker tuition is politically constrained but net price or tuition revenue changes, then the “sticky sticker price” title would be apt but the incidence story would be different. IPEDS Finance can at least shed some light on tuition revenue; if student-aid modules allow partial reconstruction of grant aid, that would further strengthen the paper.

Finally, the paper would benefit from a substantial reframing of its contribution. The current introduction claims to overturn the “standard narrative,” but the evidence is not yet strong enough for that. A more credible and useful contribution would be: during the Great Recession, states with larger appropriation cuts did not experience larger sticker-tuition increases at public four-year institutions, while Pell and minority shares rose differentially; these patterns suggest that state disinvestment coincided with compositional and possibly quality adjustments, though the design cannot fully separate funding effects from broader recession shocks. That would be a more cautious claim, but one that is actually supported by the current evidence.

In short, there is a potentially interesting paper here, especially if the authors lean into the strengths of the IPEDS panel and either restore the stronger identification strategy from the manifest or substantially moderate the causal language. As it stands, the empirical patterns are suggestive, but the conclusions outrun the design.
