**Subject:** Strategic Assessment of "The Sticky Sticker Price"

---

### 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper challenges the conventional wisdom that state funding cuts to public universities lead to higher tuition and a resulting "price-out" of low-income students. Using the heterogeneous shocks of the Great Recession, it finds that tuition is essentially a "sticky price" due to political constraints; instead of raising prices, universities see a shift in student composition, with an *increase* in the share of Pell Grant and minority students—a result that suggests fiscal shocks may be absorbed through quality degradation or demand-side shifts rather than price hikes.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this pitch fairly well in the first two paragraphs, but it leans slightly too hard on the "uncomfortable arithmetic" metaphor.
**The pitch it should have:** "While the standard narrative of higher education finance assumes a direct pass-through from state budget cuts to tuition hikes, this paper shows that tuition is a politically administered price that remains rigid even during severe fiscal shocks. I document that the 'adjustment' to disinvestment occurs not through the price of entry, but through the composition of the student body, suggesting that the true cost of austerity is borne by institutional quality rather than student wallets."

---

### 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:** The paper identifies that public university tuition is a rigid, administered price that does not respond to state appropriation shocks, forcing adjustment onto the margin of student body composition.

- **Differentiation:** It differentiates itself from **Bound and Turner (2007)** and **Deming and Walters (2017)** by moving from state-aggregate data to institution-level data and by highlighting the *failure* of the price mechanism.
- **Framing:** It is framed as a question about the WORLD (how do institutions respond to shocks?) which is a strength. 
- **The "Smart Economist" Test:** A reader would likely describe it as "the paper showing tuition doesn't actually go up when states cut funding because of political caps." This is a clear, "sticky" finding.
- **Making it bigger:** To truly reach AER status, the "Composition" result needs to be more than a "suggestive" finding. The author currently admits they can't distinguish between a supply-side response (colleges changing who they admit) and a demand-side response (rich kids fleeing to private schools). Solving this—perhaps with application data or SAT score distributions—would be a major leap.

---

### 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

- **Closest Neighbors:** Bound and Turner (2007) on appropriations; Jaquette and Curs (2017) on out-of-state recruiting; Deming and Walters (2017) on price caps.
- **Positioning:** It builds on the "administered prices" literature (Rothstein and Rouse, 2011). It should position itself as an **attack** on the mechanical pass-through assumption used in structural models of higher ed.
- **Missing Literature:** It should speak more to the **Political Economy** of price-setting. Why are these caps there? Are they more prevalent in "purple" states?
- **Unexpected Framing:** Connecting this to the literature on **"Quality Disclosure"** or **"Shadow Prices"** would be powerful. If the sticker price is fixed, the "quality-adjusted price" must be the thing moving.

---

### 4. NARRATIVE ARC

- **Setup:** Public universities rely on state funding; that funding is disappearing.
- **Tension:** We expect tuition to rise to fill the gap, but many states have political tuition freezes. So, how do schools survive?
- **Resolution:** Tuition stays flat. Pell and minority shares rise (perhaps because higher-income students leave for better-funded private schools).
- **Implications:** Policy debates about "tuition-free college" may be missing the point if the real problem is the erosion of the underlying value (quality) of the degree, not the sticker price.

**Evaluation:** The arc is clean. However, the "Resolution" is currently a bit of a "Black Box." The finding that Pell share *increases* is counter-intuitive and needs a more forceful explanation to feel like a complete story.

---

### 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

- **The Lead Fact:** "When a state cuts university funding by 20%, the sticker price doesn't move, but the student body gets significantly poorer and more diverse."
- **Reaction:** People will lean in because it contradicts the "college is becoming unaffordable" headline.
- **Follow-up:** "Is that because the schools are doing a better job at equity, or because the education got so much worse that the rich kids left?"
- **The Null Result:** The null on tuition is the star of the show. It is a "precise zero" that invalidates a common assumption. That is highly valuable.

---

### 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

- **Front-loading:** The "Tuition-setting constraints" (Section 2) is actually the most interesting theoretical part. It should be moved earlier or integrated into the intro to set the stage for *why* we expect a null.
- **Appendix:** Table 4 (Standardized Effect Sizes) is excellent and should be in the main text to help the reader compare the "size" of the tuition null vs. the composition shift.
- **Buried Results:** The "enrollment growth" result in Column 4 of Table 2 is a bit of a puzzle. If funding is cut, why is enrollment growing? This deserves its own subsection.

---

### 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The gap between this and a "Top 5" publication is the **Mechanism.** 

Currently, the paper says "Tuition doesn't move, but composition does, and we don't know exactly why." To be an AER paper, the author needs to nail down the "Quality vs. Eligibility" debate. If they can show that the "quality" of the incoming class (measured by test scores or peer effects) dropped in high-cut states, they have a blockbuster paper about the *hidden* costs of fiscal austerity.

**Single most impactful advice:** Use the IPEDS "Institutional Characteristics" or "Admissions" files to track SAT/ACT percentiles and application volumes to determine if the composition shift is driven by "rich flight" (quality decline) or institutional "access" mandates.

---

### STRATEGIC ASSESSMENT

- **Current framing quality:** Compelling
- **Contribution clarity:** Crystal clear
- **Literature positioning:** Well-positioned
- **Narrative arc:** Strong
- **AER distance:** Medium (Needs a more definitive mechanism)
- **Single biggest improvement:** Isolate whether the composition shift is driven by a decline in institutional quality/demand from high-income students or by changes in state-level eligibility.

**Verdict:** Do not reject. This has the "bones" of an AER paper. The focus on a "precise null" that breaks a standard model is exactly what the journal looks for.
