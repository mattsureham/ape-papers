## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple, policy-relevant question: when states cut funding to public universities, do universities actually offset the shock by raising tuition? Using cross-state variation in Great Recession-era appropriation cuts, the paper’s headline claim is no: sticker tuition appears surprisingly rigid, suggesting that public university prices are politically administered rather than mechanically market-clearing.

A busy economist should care because a huge amount of discussion about higher-ed finance assumes a pass-through chain from state disinvestment to tuition hikes to reduced access. If that first link is weak, then the standard policy narrative is incomplete, and the incidence of fiscal retrenchment must operate through other margins.

**Does the paper articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Not quite. The current opening is reasonably strong, but it commits too early to the “who shows up, not what they pay” line. That is not the paper’s cleanest contribution, because the paper itself later concedes that the composition results are hard to interpret causally due to recession-driven demand shifts. The clean and potentially publishable pitch is tuition rigidity under fiscal shock, not student recomposition.

**What the first two paragraphs should say instead:**

> Public debate over state disinvestment in higher education rests on a simple assumption: when legislatures cut appropriations, public universities make up the difference by raising tuition. That assumption underlies concerns about affordability, the design of federal aid, and arguments for “free college.” But it is not obvious that tuition at public universities is a flexible price. In many states it is set through a political process involving legislatures, governors, boards, caps, and freezes.
>
> This paper studies whether public universities actually pass state funding cuts through into tuition. Using institution-level IPEDS data and cross-state variation in appropriation cuts during the Great Recession, I find little evidence of differential tuition increases in states with larger cuts. The broader implication is that the incidence of state disinvestment may fall less on posted prices than on non-price margins—potentially enrollment composition, enrollment levels, or institutional quality.

That version gives the paper a sharper center of gravity and avoids overclaiming on the composition evidence.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution in one sentence:**  
The paper’s core contribution is to show that state funding cuts to public universities during the Great Recession were not associated with larger tuition increases in higher-cut states, implying substantial rigidity in public university sticker prices.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3-4 papers?
Only partially. The paper cites a literature that often presumes or documents some relation between state support and tuition, but it does not do enough to distinguish its claim from existing work on state disinvestment, tuition pass-through, and enrollment responses. Right now, the reader may not know whether the novelty is:

- institution-level rather than state-level evidence,
- a Great Recession design,
- a “null pass-through” result,
- emphasis on political tuition-setting constraints,
- or the composition outcomes.

That ambiguity hurts.

The paper needs to say more crisply: **existing papers generally document long-run correlations or average national patterns between falling state support and rising tuition; this paper asks a different question—whether cross-state variation in an acute fiscal shock translated into differential tuition increases, and finds surprisingly little pass-through.** That would sound more distinct.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or filling a gap in the LITERATURE?
It is mixed, but currently too literature-gap flavored in parts. The stronger world question is: **When public universities are hit by fiscal shocks, where does the shock go if tuition cannot adjust?** That is better than “this paper provides first institution-level evidence on X.”

The paper should lean harder into the world question and less into “first institution-level evidence on the composition channel,” especially because that channel is not cleanly pinned down.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?
At present, maybe, but not confidently. They would probably say: “It’s a DiD paper on state funding cuts and university outcomes, with a null tuition result and some compositional changes.” That is too close to “another DiD paper about higher-ed finance.”

The author needs a memorable line of novelty:  
**“This paper argues that public-university tuition is an administered price: when states cut funding, tuition does not move much across states, so the fiscal shock shows up elsewhere.”**

That is what a colleague would remember.

### What would make this contribution bigger?
Most importantly: **replace or supplement the ambiguous composition story with a clearer non-price margin.**

Specific ways to make it bigger:
1. **Show where the shock goes if not tuition.**  
   The paper hints at quality margins but does not measure them. Outcomes like instructional spending, faculty composition, student-faculty ratios, adjunct share, completion, or program offerings would make the contribution much larger.
2. **Exploit heterogeneity in tuition-setting authority.**  
   If the tuition rigidity result is stronger in states with caps/freezes/legislative control and weaker where boards have more autonomy, the paper becomes much more than a null—it becomes a political-economy paper.
3. **Distinguish sticker tuition from net tuition / aid.**  
   If sticker prices are rigid but net prices adjust, that’s a major refinement. If neither adjusts, even more interesting.
4. **Reframe composition as secondary.**  
   Right now composition is doing too much narrative work for evidence the author admits is confounded.

**When a publicly governed sector experiences fiscal retrenchment but posted prices are politically constrained, what margins adjust?**

That is a much more interesting AER-style conversation. Higher education is the application; price rigidity in a politically governed sector is the concept.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
The world before this paper: states cut appropriations, tuition rises, affordability worsens, low-income students get squeezed out. This is the standard narrative in higher-ed policy discussion.

### Tension
But public-university tuition is not a normal market price. It is politically visible and often constrained by governance rules, tuition caps, and freezes. So perhaps universities cannot adjust tuition much even under large fiscal shocks.

### Resolution
The paper’s cleanest result is that higher-cut states did not see significantly larger tuition increases. There is also evidence of higher Pell and minority shares, but these are harder to interpret because the recession itself changed incomes and enrollment demand.

### Implications
If tuition is rigid, then the incidence of state disinvestment does not primarily run through sticker price. Policymakers should stop assuming “states cut, colleges charge more” is the whole story. The relevant adjustment margins may be quality, composition, capacity, or hidden price components.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
Only partially. Right now it is **two papers uneasily sharing one design**:

1. A cleaner paper about **tuition rigidity**.
2. A messier paper about **student body recomposition**.

The first is plausible and potentially interesting. The second is suggestive but underidentified by the author’s own telling. Because the introduction leans into the second, the narrative gets weaker as the paper proceeds and starts disclaiming its own interpretation.

This is the central storytelling problem: **the paper’s most prominent claim is not its most believable one.**

### What story should it be telling?
It should be telling this story:

- Public-university tuition is an administered, politically constrained price.
- Therefore fiscal shocks need not pass through to sticker price.
- In the Great Recession, they largely did not.
- That means researchers and policymakers should stop treating tuition as the automatic margin of adjustment and start looking harder at non-price margins.

That is a coherent setup-tension-resolution-implication arc. The composition results can remain as exploratory evidence of one possible non-price margin, but not as the paper’s main act.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at dinner?
“During the Great Recession, states that cut public-university funding more did not raise tuition more.”

That is a good lead fact. It cuts against a very common assumption.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
They would lean in—briefly. The immediate response would be: “Really? Then where did the money come from or where did the cut show up?” That is exactly the right reaction. It means the paper has a real hook.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Almost certainly one of these:
- “Then what margin adjusted—quality, enrollment, student composition, or state/federal aid?”
- “Is this sticker tuition or net tuition?”
- “Is this because states imposed tuition freezes and caps?”
- “Is this a short-run recession result or a broader feature of public higher-ed pricing?”

Those are good questions. But the paper currently does not answer the strongest one—**where did the shock go?** That is why the paper feels promising rather than complete.

### If the findings are null or modest: is the null interesting?
Yes—the tuition null is genuinely interesting. But the author needs to **sell the null as a substantive fact, not an absence of findings.** Right now the paper says “this null rules out the mechanical pass-through narrative,” which is good, but it can go further:

- The null is surprising given policy rhetoric.
- The null is important because the pass-through assumption underlies affordability debates.
- The null is informative because it points to a different model of public-university pricing: politically constrained, not fiscally elastic.

That is how to make a null result feel like a result.

The composition findings are much less dinner-party effective because the first follow-up is “isn’t that just the recession changing family income?” and the paper itself says yes, maybe.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The gap is mainly a mix of **framing problem** and **scope problem**.

### Framing problem
The paper’s best finding is tuition rigidity, but it is packaged inside a broader “student body recomposition” story that the paper itself cannot convincingly identify. That makes the reader distrust the paper’s ambitions even though the cleanest result is interesting.

### Scope problem
For AER, “tuition did not move much” is a good starting point, not a full destination. The natural next question is where the incidence went. Without stronger evidence on the adjustment margin—quality, capacity, net price, governance constraints, or heterogeneity by tuition-setting autonomy—the paper feels incomplete.

### Novelty problem
There is some novelty, but not enough is extracted from it. A null pass-through result in this setting could be quite interesting if tightly positioned against a literature that implicitly assumes pass-through. But as written, it risks sounding like a recession-era reduced-form revisit of known higher-ed patterns.

### Ambition problem
Yes, somewhat. The paper is careful and competent, but it stops where the most interesting question begins. It says, essentially: “Tuition didn’t adjust, composition may have changed, and future work should study quality.” For AER, the paper itself should try harder to answer that future-work question, or at least pin down the political mechanism behind price rigidity.

### Single most impactful piece of advice
**Make this a paper about politically constrained tuition pass-through—not a paper about student-body recomposition—and then add one decisive piece of evidence showing where the shock goes or why tuition is rigid (e.g., heterogeneity by tuition-setting authority/caps/freezes).**

That one change would sharpen the contribution, improve credibility, and move the paper into a much more interesting conversation.
