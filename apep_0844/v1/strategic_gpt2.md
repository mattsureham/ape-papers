This paper asks a simple and policy-relevant question: when states cut funding to public universities, do universities actually raise tuition, or do they adjust on other margins? Using Great Recession-era variation in state appropriations, the paper’s central claim is that tuition was surprisingly unresponsive, while student composition shifted toward more Pell-eligible and minority students.

A busy economist should care because the paper challenges a deeply embedded policy narrative—state disinvestment raises tuition, which then reduces access—and replaces it with a different one: at public universities, tuition may be politically sticky, so fiscal shocks show up elsewhere. That is a potentially important fact if true, because it changes how we think about incidence, access, and the political economy of public higher education.

Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs? Not quite. The opening is better than average, but it overreaches on the composition angle and undersells the cleaner and more distinctive result, which is tuition rigidity. The first two paragraphs currently promise a paper about “where the fiscal shock lands,” then lean into composition before the paper has really earned that claim. But by the author’s own admission, the composition results are hard to interpret because the recession directly affected student incomes and Pell eligibility. So the introduction is building the story around the noisier result rather than the cleaner one.

The first two paragraphs should instead say something like:

> State disinvestment in public higher education is typically assumed to pass through into tuition. That assumption underlies much of the economics and policy discussion of college affordability, yet it has rarely been tested in a setting where states experienced large, heterogeneous funding cuts.  
>  
> This paper shows that during the Great Recession, public universities in states with larger appropriation cuts did not raise tuition significantly more than universities in states with smaller cuts. That finding suggests that tuition at public universities is not a flexible market price but a politically constrained administered price. The paper then examines other margins of adjustment and documents changes in student composition, though those patterns are more descriptive because recession-driven demand shifts cannot be fully separated from institutional responses.

That is the pitch. It is cleaner, more credible, and more AER-facing than “the shock lands on who shows up, not what they pay,” which is too strong for what the paper can actually support.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

Setup:  
Public universities have faced sustained state disinvestment, and the conventional view is that lower state support leads to higher tuition and reduced access.

Tension:  
That standard story assumes tuition can move freely. But at public universities, tuition is often set through a political process with freezes, caps, and oversight. So the core puzzle is: if funding falls and tuition cannot fully adjust, where does the shock go?

Resolution:  
The paper finds little differential tuition response to differential state cuts during the Great Recession. It also documents increases in Pell share and minority share, though these composition shifts are hard to interpret causally because the recession itself changed student demand and Pell eligibility.

Implications:  
The standard tuition-pass-through view may be incomplete. Public universities may absorb fiscal shocks through nonprice margins, and policy debates that focus narrowly on sticker price may miss the real incidence of disinvestment.

Does the paper have a clear narrative arc? It has the outline of one, but the execution is uneven. The paper wants the story to be “the shock lands on who shows up, not what they pay,” but the evidence really supports “the price does not move much, and some other things seem to move, though we can’t cleanly say why.” That is still a valid paper, but it is a different paper.

So yes, right now it is somewhat a collection of results looking for a story. The tuition null is the story. The composition results are supporting evidence that something else may be adjusting, but not yet the resolution of the mystery. The correct story is:

- Economists think fiscal shocks should raise tuition.
- But tuition is politically sticky.
- In a major natural stress test—the Great Recession—tuition did not differentially rise where cuts were deeper.
- Therefore we need to rethink incidence in public higher education and look beyond sticker prices to quality, rationing, and composition.

That story is stronger, more honest, and more important than the current one.

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?

> “During the Great Recession, states that slashed university funding more deeply did not see public universities raise tuition more.”

That is a good fact. People would lean in, because it cuts against a common assumption. It is counterintuitive enough to get attention, and simple enough to travel.

What follow-up question would they ask? Immediately:

> “If tuition didn’t adjust, what did?”

And then:

> “Is that because of actual political caps and freezes, or because universities adjusted quality or aid or enrollment mix instead?”

Those are exactly the right questions—and the paper currently does not answer them strongly enough. The composition results partly try to answer the first one, but because they are entangled with recession-driven demand shifts, they do not fully satisfy the natural curiosity generated by the tuition fact.

Are the findings null or modest? The headline tuition finding is a null, but it is potentially an interesting null because it rejects a widely assumed pass-through mechanism. The paper does make some case for why that null matters, but it needs to do more. Right now the null risks sounding like “we didn’t find the expected effect.” To make it valuable, the paper must insist that the null is not just absence of evidence but evidence of **a different institutional model of price-setting**. That requires stronger conceptual framing around administered prices and political constraints. If the paper does that, the null becomes interesting. If not, it risks reading like a failed test of a familiar hypothesis.
