### 1. Idea Fidelity
The paper faithfully pursues the core idea from the manifest: multi-threshold bunching estimation at the FHOG ($600K), stamp duty exemption ($800K post-reform), and concession phase-out ($1M) using NSW Valuer General PSI data (1.4M transactions post-restrictions, vs. manifest's 2.16M full sample). It fully implements the bunching migration experiment around the July 2023 reform ($650K to $800K shift, with controls at $550K/$750K) and the supply-demand decomposition (vacant land as new-home proxy vs. existing residential). Minor deviations include a narrower sample (2018--2025, $100K--$2M prices) and omission of the promised spatial heterogeneity test (postcode-level correlation with first-home-buyer shares). Placebos on commercial/farm properties are included but not fully leveraged (see Essential Points). Overall, fidelity is high (90%+), with no major misses on identification or research question (subsidy incidence via supply- vs. demand-side bunching).

### 2. Summary
This paper estimates bunching in transaction prices around three overlapping first-home-buyer subsidy thresholds in New South Wales, using the universe of 1.4 million property sales from 2018--2025. A compelling migration test exploits a July 2023 policy reform shifting the stamp duty exemption from $650K to $800K, showing excess mass collapse at the old threshold and rise at the new one (unlike controls), confirming policy-driven distortions. Decomposing by property type reveals stronger bunching in vacant land (new construction proxy) than existing homes, suggesting developers partially capture subsidies via threshold pricing.

### 3. Essential Points
**1. Sensitivity of bunching estimates undermines credibility.** Table 3 shows extreme instability: at $800K, $\hat{b}$ swings from 0.276 (degree 5) to -0.073 (degree 9), and bin-width sensitivity yields 5.225 ($2K bins) to 0.302 ($10K bins). This violates standard bunching diagnostics (e.g., Kleven 2016 requires stability across polynomials 4--9). Authors must present a preferred specification justified by cross-validation or information criteria (e.g., AIC), re-estimate all results, and report confidence intervals accounting for functional form uncertainty (e.g., via supremum tests). Without this, identification is not credible.

**2. Commercial/farm placebo fails, threatening policy interpretation.** Despite facing no first-home-buyer thresholds, commercial/farm transactions show large bunching at $800K ($\hat{b}=3.250$, SE=0.409; Table 3), comparable to or exceeding residential estimates. This suggests unmodeled round-number or market-wide effects (e.g., psychological pricing at $800K). Authors must decompose commercial/farm by pre/post-reform (does it migrate?), compare to policy thresholds explicitly (e.g., difference-in-bunching tests), and rule out confounders like commercial stamp duty kinks. Current presentation buries this as "robustness" without discussion, eroding the claim that residential bunching is subsidy-driven.

**3. Supply-demand decomposition relies on noisy vacant-land proxy without validation.** Vacant land (mean price $445K) shows 2x+ bunching vs. residential, but the paper acknowledges its imperfection (e.g., owner-builder purchases, misclassified house-and-land packages as "residential"). No evidence validates it as a new-construction proxy (e.g., correlation with building permits/postcodes with new developments?). Authors must provide proxy diagnostics (e.g., regression of bunching on postcode new-supply shares) or sensitivity to alternative proxies (e.g., "house and land" text matches in primary purpose). Spatial heterogeneity test from manifest (bunching vs. first-home-buyer shares) is entirely missing and must be added for credibility.

### 4. Suggestions
The paper is well-structured for AER:Insights (concise, clear tables, strong policy hook) and advances a novel design combining multi-cutoff replication, migration, and decomposition. The migration test is particularly elegant—one of the cleanest in housing bunching—and the incidence angle (seller capture) speaks to Hilber-Vermeulen supply debates. Below are targeted improvements to elevate it to publishable form.

**Figures for transparency and intuition.** Add 3--4 figures: (i) pooled histogram with fitted counterfactuals for all three thresholds (stacked pre/post-reform); (ii) migration visuals (side-by-side pre/post densities at $650K/$800K/controls); (iii) supply-demand bars with 95% CIs; (iv) density plots by property type. Use $10K bins for visuals (smoother than $5K). Label counterfactual fits clearly and overlay excess mass shading. This would make results intuitive without text (e.g., "Figure 1 visually confirms migration").

**Expand robustness suite.** Beyond polynomials/bins, test: (i) flexible controls (e.g., local linear, restricted cubic splines via `rdbwselect` in Stata/R); (ii) narrower/wider exclusion windows ($20K--$50K below, $0--$10K above); (iii) subsample by time (e.g., exclude 2020--2021 COVID volatility); (iv) deflate prices to 2023 AUD using CPI/residential index for nominal trends; (v) Donut-hole regression (Saez 2010) above thresholds. Report a robustness table summarizing $\hat{b}$ ranges (e.g., median/IQR across 20+ specs). For placebos, add round-number controls ($500K, $900K) pre/post and test joint hypothesis (no migration at non-policy rounds).

**Strengthen institutional details and external validity.** Clarify FHB eligibility (e.g., income/age caps? % of transactions eligible?) and how sellers observe it (e.g., buyer pre-approval common?). Compute implied elasticities: $\hat{b} \approx 0.47$ at $600K$ suggests elasticity $\epsilon \approx \hat{b}/(1-\hat{b}) \times (subsidy/property\ price) \approx 0.47/0.53 \times (10K/600K) \approx 0.015$—discuss vs. Kleven benchmarks. Compare to UK Help-to-Buy (apep_0492) quantitatively. Add map of bunching intensity by postcode (tease spatial test).

**Data and replication enhancements.** Great data appendix—expand with code link (manifest GitHub). Report FHB-specific subsample if identifiable (e.g., via low-price/new-home filters)? Validate vacant-land proxy: tabulate primary-purpose text matches (e.g., % "vacant land" vs. "house and land package"); regress vacant-land share on developer-heavy postcodes (e.g., Sydney growth corridors). Compute power: with N=1.4M, detect $\hat{b}>0.1$ easily, but stratify by low/high-price postcodes.

**Framing and policy punch.** Tone down abstract claims (e.g., "partially captured by sellers" is indirect evidence; say "consistent with"). Discussion: quantify deadweight loss (e.g., excess transactions $\times$ subsidy value) and simulate reform (e.g., abolish $600K cap). Link to incidence lit: contrast with Susin (2002) vouchers (no thresholds). Broader hook: "This migration design templates bunching anywhere thresholds shift (e.g., US LIHTC)."

**Minor polish.** Fix inconsistencies (abstract: 73% migration collapse vs. intro 73%; data N=1.4M vs. manifest 2.16M—explain drops). Standardize SE reporting (all bootstrap?). Add JEL R38 (housing). Word count fits Insights (~4K); trim intro repeats. Overall, addressing Essentials makes this a strong accept candidate—exciting, credible ID on timely policy.
