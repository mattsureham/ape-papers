## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether first-home-buyer subsidies in Australia actually help buyers, or whether sellers and developers simply price homes up to the eligibility cap and capture part of the transfer. Using three subsidy thresholds in New South Wales and a reform that moved one threshold, the paper shows transaction-price bunching at the caps and argues that the bunching migrates with policy, suggesting that housing subsidies distort pricing and are partially capitalized into seller revenue.

Why should a busy economist care? Because this is a clean, intuitive case of tax/subsidy incidence in a politically important market, and because the paper’s best idea is broader than Australia: threshold-based transfers can create “pricing to the cap,” not just generic price capitalization.

**Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Not quite. The opening is competent, but it starts as “another bunching paper on subsidy thresholds” rather than as a paper about a broader economic phenomenon: targeted subsidies can be converted into seller rents through pricing rules. The first paragraphs currently overinvest in institutional detail and underinvest in the big economic question.

**What the first two paragraphs should say instead:**

> Governments often subsidize buyers through eligibility caps: below a price threshold, you get a grant or tax exemption; above it, you do not. In markets where sellers can anticipate those rules, such policies may not lower buyers’ effective costs at all—they may simply create focal prices at the cap and transfer public money to sellers. Whether this “pricing to the cap” occurs is central to the incidence of housing subsidies and to the design of means-tested transfers more broadly.
>
> This paper studies that question in New South Wales, where first-home-buyer assistance creates multiple price thresholds and a 2023 reform shifted one of the main cutoffs. Using the universe of housing transactions, I show that transaction prices bunch at subsidy caps, that bunching moves when the cap moves, and that the response is strongest in market segments where developers are more likely to set posted prices. The central implication is that threshold-based housing subsidies are at least partly captured on the supply side.

That is the AER pitch. Right now the paper’s version is more “I exploit three overlapping thresholds” than “here is a first-order fact about how targeted subsidies work.”

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Governments use first-home-buyer grants and transaction-tax relief to expand access to homeownership. These policies often rely on transaction-price caps.

### Tension
Those caps may backfire: instead of lowering effective purchase costs, they may induce sellers to cluster prices at the cap and absorb the subsidy. But observed bunching in housing prices is hard to interpret because round numbers are already salient.

### Resolution
In NSW, prices bunch at three subsidy thresholds, and when one threshold moves in 2023, the bunching moves with it. Responses are stronger in property types plausibly tied to developer pricing.

### Implications
Threshold-based buyer subsidies can generate distortions and partial seller capture; policymakers should worry not just about whether subsidies raise prices on average, but about whether they create exploitable caps.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
It has the ingredients, but the story is still somewhat split between:
1. a methodological story about multi-threshold bunching and migration,
2. an incidence story about seller capture,
3. an Australia housing-policy story.

These are related, but the paper has not fully decided which is the trunk and which are branches. Right now it reads a bit like a collection of decent results in search of a hierarchy.

**The story it should be telling:**  
“Threshold-based housing subsidies create a specific distortion—pricing to the cap. NSW gives us an unusually sharp test because the cap moved. Once we see bunching move with the cap, we can interpret threshold bunching as policy-induced rather than generic heaping; the cross-segment patterns then suggest that sellers, especially developers, capture part of the subsidy.”

That is a coherent arc. The migration result should be the centerpiece; the multi-threshold and property-type evidence should support it.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party?
“When the Australian government moved a homebuyer tax-relief cutoff from \$650k to \$800k, the bunching in transaction prices moved with it.”

That is the fact that makes people look up. It is vivid, intuitive, and speaks to mechanism.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
Economists would lean in—at least initially—because the migration fact is clean and memorable. If instead you lead with “I estimate multi-threshold bunching in 1.4 million transactions,” phones come out.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Probably: **“Fine, but does this really show seller capture, or just buyer sorting/negotiation around eligibility thresholds?”**

That is the right question, and the paper should anticipate it more directly. Strategically, this is the main vulnerability in audience reception—not an identification complaint, but a “what exactly have we learned?” complaint.

### If findings are modest or partial, is that okay?
Yes, but only if the paper is honest about the margin of contribution. The really valuable result is not that seller capture is estimated precisely to be X percent. It is that **policy thresholds visibly reshape prices, and threshold shifts reveal that they do.** That is already interesting. The paper does not need to overclaim full incidence to be useful.
