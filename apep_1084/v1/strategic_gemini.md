## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
This paper exploits a unique "two-shock" policy sequence—the public labeling of for-profit college programs as "failing" followed by the rescission of the actual penalties—to isolate the power of information disclosure from the threat of regulation. It finds that even after the threat of losing federal aid was removed, enrollment in failing programs continued to collapse, suggesting that public "stigma" is an irreversible and compounding regulatory tool. Economists should care because this suggests the "information channel" of accountability is not just a precursor to sanctions, but a permanent market-altering force that cannot be undone by simple policy reversal.

**Evaluation:** The paper does a decent job in the first two paragraphs, but it leans a bit too heavily on the "institutional history" of the Trump administration rollback. 

**The pitch the paper should have:**
"When a government labels a product as low-quality but then decides not to punish the producer, does the market eventually forget? This paper identifies the pure effect of reputational stigma by exploiting the 2017 'Gainful Employment' scores, which publicly branded hundreds of for-profit colleges as failing just months before the underlying regulatory penalties were rescinded. I find that information disclosure alone acts as a one-way door: enrollment declines not only persisted but accelerated after the threat of sanctions vanished, proving that once the state 'scars' a firm with a quality label, the damage is irreversible."

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**Contribution:** The paper identifies the "irreversibility" of mandatory information disclosure by separating the reputational effect of a quality label from the regulatory threat of its associated sanctions.

*   **Differentiation:** It moves beyond Jin and Leslie (2003) or Greenstone (2002) by having a clean "off-switch" for the regulation while the information remains public. Most disclosure papers study the *bundle* of info + threat; this unbundles them.
*   **Frame:** It is currently framed as a mix. It’s at its strongest when answering a question about the **WORLD**: Can information be "un-disclosed"? 
*   **The "Smart Economist" Test:** A reader would explain this as "the paper that shows for-profit colleges stayed dead even after Trump tried to save them." It avoids the "just another DiD" trap because the two-stage shock (Info ON, Regulation OFF) is a rare and clever identification hook.
*   **Bigger Contribution:** To make this truly AER-level, the author needs to dig deeper into **mechanisms**. *Who* is using the info? Is it students (demand), or is it guidance counselors and lenders (intermediaries)? If the author could show that the "scarlet score" affected bank lending or employee hiring independently of student enrollment, the "irreversibility" story becomes a much broader theory of firm reputation.

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
The paper sits at the intersection of Information Economics (Stigler, Dranove) and the Economics of Education (Cellini, Deming).

*   **Closest Neighbors:** Jin & Leslie (2003) on hygiene cards; Cellini & Turner (2019) on GE impacts; Dranove et al. (2003) on "report card" unintended effects.
*   **Strategy:** The paper should **build on** the "labeling" literature but **pivot** toward a more general theory of "Policy Irreversibility." It should position itself as the educational equivalent of the "Sunk Cost" or "Hysteresis" literature—where a temporary policy change has a permanent effect on the market equilibrium.
*   **Missing Conversations:** The paper needs to speak to the **Industrial Organization** literature on firm reputation and brand equity. It treats "reputation" as a black box; connecting to IO models of how firms recover from quality shocks would elevate the "Science."

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC
*   **Setup:** The government creates a "quality" metric (D/E ratios) to clean up the for-profit sector.
*   **Tension:** A change in administration removes the "teeth" (sanctions) of the law. Conventional wisdom suggests firms should recover if the threat was the only thing holding them back.
*   **Resolution:** The firms do *not* recover; they die faster. The "Information" was the real weapon all along.
*   **Implications:** Information disclosure is "sticky" and "cheap" regulation that subsequent administrations cannot easily deregulate.

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong. It’s a "David vs. Goliath" story where the data (the score) is more powerful than the politician (the rollback).

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
At a dinner party, I’d lead with: **"Trump tried to save the for-profit colleges by killing the regulations against them, but they died anyway because the 'failing' label he couldn't erase was more powerful than the law he could."**

*   **Reaction:** People lean in. It’s counter-intuitive and politically salient.
*   **Follow-up:** "Did the students go to better schools instead, or did they just stop going to college?" (The paper touches on this, but needs to be more definitive on the "substitution" vs. "market shrinkage" question to satisfy a top-tier audience).

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
*   **Front-loading:** The "Two-Stage Decomposition" logic (Eq 1) is the star. It should be explained even more intuitively in the intro.
*   **Appendix:** The racial composition effects are important but currently feel like a "secondary paper" tacked on. They should either be integrated into a "Welfare" section or moved to the appendix to keep the focus on the *irreversibility* mechanism.
*   **Robustness:** The "Zone" placebo (Table 5, Col 2) is actually a *main result*. It shows a dose-response. Move that to the main results section to bolster the "stigma" narrative.

---

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The gap is **Ambition**. Right now, it’s a very high-quality "Policy Evaluation" paper. To be an "AER Paper," it needs to be an "Economics of Information" paper.

**The single most impactful piece of advice:**
Stop calling this a paper about "For-Profit Higher Education Accountability" and start calling it a paper about **"The Irreversibility of Information Shocks in Regulated Markets."** Use the for-profit colleges as the *laboratory*, but make the *theory* about why "de-regulation" is impossible once the state has already revealed firm quality to the public.
