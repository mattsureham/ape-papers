## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether public disclosure can regulate markets even when formal enforcement disappears. Using the 2017 publication of “failing” debt-to-earnings scores for for-profit college programs, followed quickly by the Trump administration’s rollback of sanctions, the paper argues that stigma from disclosure persisted and even deepened after legal penalties were removed. A busy economist should care because the paper speaks to a broad question: when does information disclosure itself become an irreversible policy intervention?

The paper is closer than most to articulating this clearly, but the first two paragraphs still read a bit like an event-study setup rather than a big economics question. The current introduction gets to the right idea quickly, but it could do a better job making the stakes more general: this is not mainly a paper about gainful employment rule minutiae; it is a paper about whether governments can create durable market responses simply by publishing bad news.

### The pitch the paper should have in the first two paragraphs

“Governments increasingly regulate by disclosing quality information: pollution releases, hospital performance, school scores, restaurant grades, and college outcomes. But a basic question remains unresolved: are these policies effective because they change information available to consumers, or because they signal future sanctions? This distinction matters because sanctions can be repealed, while public information, once released, is hard to take back.

This paper studies a rare setting that separates those channels. In 2017, the U.S. Department of Education publicly labeled hundreds of for-profit college programs as failing based on debt-to-earnings ratios, then shortly afterward suspended and later rescinded enforcement of the rule. I show that labeled programs continued to lose students even after the regulatory threat disappeared, suggesting that disclosure itself functioned as an irreversible form of regulation.”

That is the AER version of the pitch: broad question first, rare design second, headline finding third.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Governments increasingly publish performance information to discipline firms and institutions. In higher education, the Gainful Employment rule publicly labeled some programs as failing and threatened federal-aid loss.

### Tension

The same policy combined two forces: information for consumers and sanctions for providers. Usually those are bundled, so we do not know whether market responses reflect true informational updating or fear of enforcement. Then politics intervened: sanctions were rolled back, but the labels remained public. That creates the core tension.

### Resolution

Programs labeled failing did not rebound after the rollback; if anything, the decline deepened. The paper interprets this as evidence that disclosure itself imposed a persistent reputational penalty.

### Implications

Disclosure can be an enduring regulatory instrument even when legal enforcement is transient. That matters for policy design and for the political economy of deregulation: administrations can rescind rules, but they cannot unpublish bad news.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

Yes, more than many papers. The arc is visible and potentially strong. But the paper occasionally weakens its own story by drifting into a catalog of tables and sub-results. The best story is not “here are main results, event study, race heterogeneity, robustness.” The best story is:

1. Disclosure and sanctions are usually bundled.
2. This case unexpectedly unbundles them.
3. The market reaction persists after sanctions vanish.
4. Therefore disclosure is a durable policy lever.
5. That durability has distributional consequences.

The racial-composition section currently feels a bit tacked on rather than integral to the arc. It is not obvious whether it is a central implication or a side result. The paper should decide. My instinct: keep it, but demote it from co-headline status unless the equity mechanism becomes much richer. Right now the main story is the persistence of disclosure effects, not racial sorting.

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?

“The government publicly labeled some for-profit college programs as failing, then stopped enforcing the penalties—and the labeled programs kept losing students anyway.”

That is a good fact. Better still:

“Deregulation didn’t undo the damage, because the information shock itself stuck.”

That would make people lean in.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

Lean in—if presented at that level of generality. If presented as “a paper about completions at for-profit colleges under gainful employment,” many will mentally file it as a narrow applied-micro paper. The framing makes the difference here.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Probably one of these:

- “So is this really about information, or about institutions spiraling downward once demand falls?”
- “How general is this beyond for-profit colleges?”
- “Who is updating on this information—students, media, accreditors, lenders?”
- “Is the welfare implication that disclosure helps students avoid bad programs, or that it shuts down pathways for marginal students?”

Those are good questions. The paper should anticipate them in the intro and discussion.

### If findings are modest or null

Not applicable—the headline finding is not null. But some of the levels results are noisy, and the sharpest estimates come from logs. Strategically, that is fine if the paper foregrounds the directional and conceptual pattern rather than over-selling precision in every table.
