## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether public disclosure by government can have durable market effects even after the underlying policy is rolled back. Using the 2017 publication of Gainful Employment scores for for-profit college programs, followed quickly by the Trump administration’s suspension of enforcement, the paper argues that public “failing” labels continued to depress program outcomes after formal regulatory threat disappeared—suggesting that information disclosure can operate as an effectively irreversible form of regulation.

A busy economist should care because this is a broad question about the state’s toolkit, not just a higher-education story: can governments regulate markets simply by making performance information public, in ways that outlast political turnover?

### Does the paper articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Mostly yes, and better than many submissions. The first two paragraphs do identify the question, the natural experiment, and the stakes. But they still read a bit like a competent applied micro paper rather than a paper making a general claim about the political economy of regulation. The phrase “two-shock structure” is useful but not memorable; the big idea is stronger than the prose.

The first two paragraphs should do less scene-setting around GE mechanics and more to foreground the general claim:

- governments increasingly regulate via disclosure;
- unlike taxes/subsidies/sanctions, disclosure may be politically irreversible once information is public;
- this setting provides an unusually clean test of whether disclosure has independent bite after sanctions disappear.

### The pitch the paper should have

Governments increasingly regulate not just by taxing or banning, but by publicly labeling firms and products. But does disclosure matter only because market participants expect sanctions to follow, or can disclosure itself permanently change market outcomes even after enforcement is withdrawn? This paper studies a rare case where those channels separate: the U.S. Department of Education publicly labeled hundreds of for-profit college programs as failing in 2017, then a new administration quickly halted enforcement while leaving the labels public. I show that outcomes continued to deteriorate after the rollback, implying that information disclosure can function as an irreversible regulatory technology.

That is the AER pitch. The current version is close, but it still sounds too much like “a paper on Gainful Employment” and not enough like “a paper about the persistence of disclosure-based regulation.”

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### Contribution in one sentence

The paper’s contribution is to show that public quality disclosure can have persistent effects even after formal enforcement is rescinded, implying that disclosure is not merely a complement to regulation but can itself be an effectively irreversible regulatory instrument.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest papers?

Only partially. The paper names disclosure papers and Gainful Employment papers, but the differentiation is not yet sharp enough. Right now the contribution sounds like:

- “another disclosure paper showing labels matter,” plus
- “another for-profit accountability paper.”

The genuine differentiator is narrower and more interesting: **a setting that separates the informational/reputational channel from the sanction channel through a politically induced rollback.** That needs to be the centerpiece. The paper currently says this, but it does not sufficiently hammer home that this is what the closest papers could not do.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a gap in a literature?

Mostly about the world, which is good. The strongest question is: **Can a government create durable regulation simply by publishing information, even when future policymakers try to undo the rule?** That is a world question.

But the introduction drifts into “this contributes to three literatures” mode a bit too quickly. AER introductions should usually delay the literature bookkeeping and keep the reader focused on the question and the answer.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the intro?

A smart economist could probably say: “It’s about Gainful Employment labels for for-profit colleges, and they use the rollback to distinguish reputation from regulation.” That is reasonably good.

But there is still a risk that many readers would summarize it as: “another DiD paper on accountability in higher ed.” That is the danger. The novelty is not the label effect per se; it is the **durability of disclosure under policy reversal**.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Several possibilities, in order of importance:

1. **Broader outcome framing.** The paper is currently written around completions. That makes the finding feel narrower than it is. If the paper can frame the outcome as demand, program survival, or institutional reallocation in a way that speaks to market equilibrium, the contribution gets bigger.

2. **Sharper mechanism framing.** Right now “reputation” is doing a lot of work. The paper would be bigger if it distinguished whether persistence comes from:
   - student demand,
   - media salience,
   - accreditor/employer responses,
   - internal institutional disinvestment after labeling.
   
   Not with a full mechanism paper, but with a more disciplined conceptual discussion.

3. **Comparative framing across policy tools.** The paper should say more explicitly: disclosure differs from ordinary regulation because it is hard to reverse once third parties absorb and retransmit the information. That comparison raises the stakes.

4. **Generalizability.** A stronger discussion of when disclosure should be reversible vs. irreversible would elevate the paper beyond this setting. What features matter—centralized publication, media uptake, salience of the metric, complexity of the product, existence of prior suspicion?

If the author could make one substantive enlargement, I would want the paper to speak more directly to **disclosure as a technology of state capacity under political turnover**, not just to for-profit colleges.

### How should the paper position itself relative to those neighbors?

Mostly **build on**, not attack.

- Relative to the classic disclosure papers: “Those papers show disclosure matters; this paper shows why disclosure may be uniquely durable even when conventional regulation is reversed.”
- Relative to the GE literature: “Those papers study accountability and regulation in for-profit higher ed; this paper uses the unusual sequence of publication then rollback to isolate what the label itself does.”
- Relative to report-card literatures: “This is a high-stakes setting where a public label can persist because government information remains in the public domain.”

The paper should not pretend prior work missed that disclosure has reputational effects. That would overclaim. Its edge is the **rollback-based separation** and the **irreversibility claim**.

### Is the paper positioned too narrowly or too broadly?

Right now it is oddly both.

- **Too narrowly** in outcome and institutional detail: lots of GE specifics, lots of focus on completions.
- **Too broadly** in rhetoric: “information disclosure alone functions as a powerful, irreversible regulatory tool” is a large claim from one setting.

The right positioning is: one unusually informative setting that identifies a broader mechanism. That is a disciplined generalization, not a universal one.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?

Even without exhaustive citation checking, the paper feels light on:

- **public report card / accountability design** beyond the few canonical disclosure citations;
- **political economy of policy durability** or reversibility;
- **stigma and labeling** literatures, including how public labels persist through media, search, and intermediaries;
- possibly **industrial organization of reputation** where bad public signals have long tails.

It may also benefit from speaking to economists interested in **state capacity and administrative tools**. The deeper paper is about what governments can accomplish with information when harder sanctions are politically fragile.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?

Not quite yet. It is currently having a respectable conversation with higher-education and disclosure literatures. The more important conversation is:

**What forms of regulation survive partisan reversal?**

That is the unexpected and more powerful literature bridge. If framed that way, the paper becomes relevant not just to education economists but to public economists, political economists, IO people interested in information, and scholars of regulation.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Governments use disclosure and performance metrics to discipline providers. In higher education, the Gainful Employment rule publicly identified low-performing for-profit programs and threatened future aid loss.

### Tension

Normally, disclosure and sanctions are bundled, so we do not know whether market responses reflect information, fear of enforcement, or both. The unusual twist here is that the Trump administration removed the enforcement threat but could not fully undo the public label.

### Resolution

Programs publicly labeled as failing continued to experience worse outcomes after enforcement was halted, which the paper interprets as evidence of persistent reputational damage from disclosure itself.

### Implications

Disclosure can be a durable—and perhaps politically sticky—regulatory instrument. Policymakers should treat publication decisions as consequential because labels may outlive the legal regimes that created them.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

Yes, more than most submissions. There is a real story here. The “scarlet score” metaphor, while slightly journalistic, does help unify the narrative.

That said, the paper is still somewhat at risk of becoming “a collection of estimates around one event” because the story is not fully disciplined. Specifically:

- the narrative begins as one about **enrollment/demand** but the main outcome is **completions**;
- it becomes a story about **irreversibility**, but the evidence is persistence over a few years, not full permanence;
- it gestures at **equity implications**, but that part feels bolted on rather than integral.

### What story should it be telling?

The cleanest story is:

1. Disclosure and sanctions are usually intertwined.
2. This episode separates them because politics removed one but not the other.
3. Market responses did not unwind.
4. Therefore, disclosure creates a durable informational state that future governments cannot easily reverse.

That is enough. The racial-composition results can remain, but they should not be sold as co-equal to the main contribution unless they are made much more central and much sharper.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?

“I’d lead with: when the government publicly labeled for-profit college programs as failing, the damage did not reverse when the next administration suspended enforcement; if anything, the decline deepened.”

That is a dinner-party fact economists will understand instantly.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

Lean in—at least initially. The underlying question is strong. Economists like settings where politics help separate channels, and they like papers that say something general about how regulation works.

But they will lean in only if the presenter leads with the broad point. If the pitch starts with detailed GE threshold mechanics, phones come out.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Probably one of these:

- “Is this really about disclosure, or about the market inferring future quality decline?”
- “Why should we think this generalizes beyond for-profit colleges?”
- “Are completions the right outcome for demand/reputation?”
- “Who is actually responding—the students, the institutions, or other intermediaries?”

Those are strategic questions, not referee questions. The paper should preempt them in the framing.

### If the findings are modest or null, is that okay?

The findings are not null, but they are somewhat mixed in levels and stronger in logs. Strategically, that is fine if the paper avoids overselling precision and keeps the focus on the temporal pattern rather than any single coefficient.

The result is interesting enough if presented as: **the rollback did not undo the label.** That is the memorable fact. The paper should not make itself vulnerable by pushing every estimate as “striking.”

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

This is not a hopelessly off-target submission. There is a real AER-type idea embedded in it. But the paper in current form is not yet fully there.

### What is the gap?

Primarily a **framing problem**, secondarily an **ambition problem**.

There is also a mild **scope problem**:
- the paper leans heavily on one outcome,
- mechanism is suggestive rather than developed,
- the equity piece is not fully integrated.

I do **not** think the core issue is novelty in the narrow sense. The design idea—publication followed by rollback—is genuinely interesting. The problem is that the manuscript has not yet converted that design into a broad enough economic claim.

### What is the single most impactful piece of advice?

**Rewrite the paper around one big question: when governments disclose damaging quality information, can later political actors actually undo the market consequences?**

Everything should serve that question. The title, first two paragraphs, literature positioning, results ordering, and conclusion should all be rebuilt around it. If the author gets that right, the paper becomes much more legible as a top-journal contribution.
