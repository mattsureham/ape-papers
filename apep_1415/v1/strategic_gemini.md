## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
This paper exploits a unique natural experiment in Morocco—the world’s first geographically restricted crop legalization—to study what happens when a massive informal economy is brought into the light of the law. Using satellite nightlight data and a spatial difference-in-differences design, the author finds that legalization fails to generate a broad economic boom because the removal of the "illicit risk premium" (the legalization discount) collapses prices by 98%, potentially leaving farmers no better off than under prohibition.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this well in the second paragraph, but the first paragraph is a bit dry on institutional detail.
**The Pitch the paper should have:** "When a state legalizes an illicit crop, does it create wealth or simply destroy the risk premiums that sustained the poor? Morocco's 2021 regional cannabis legalization provides a sharp natural experiment to test whether formalization is an engine of growth or a redistribution of value from farmers to the state and logistics hubs."

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
The paper’s contribution is the introduction and empirical testing of the **"legalization discount"**—the theory that formalization can be value-destructive for producers if the resulting price collapse outweighs the benefits of legal certainty.

*   **Differentiated?** Yes. Most literature focuses on the *eradication* of crops (Dube & Naidu) or the *social* effects of legalization in the West. This looks at the *developmental* impact in a major producer state.
*   **World vs. Literature:** It frames itself as answering a question about the **world** (the Rif mountains/drug policy), which is a strength.
*   **What's new?** It’s not "just another DiD." The spatial restriction (legal on one side of a provincial line, illegal on the other) is a "gold standard" setup for development economics.
*   **Bigger Contribution:** To make this an AER "heavyweight," the author needs more than just nightlights. A secondary outcome—perhaps child labor proxies (school enrollment) or market price data from local souks—would prove the mechanism rather than just inferring it.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
The paper sits at the intersection of Development, Law & Economics, and Remote Sensing.

*   **Neighbors:** Dube & Naidu (2015), Mejía & Restrepo (2016) on drug conflict; Henderson et al. (2012) on nightlights.
*   **Positioning:** It builds on the "Nightlights" literature but *attacks* the naive policy assumption that formalization = prosperity.
*   **Right Conversation?** It should speak more to the **Industrial Organization** of illicit markets. The "legalization discount" is essentially a change in market structure and rent extraction.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC
*   **Setup:** The Rif is a global hub of illicit hashish, tolerated but informal.
*   **Tension:** Legalization arrives, but it brings a 1/50th price drop. Can the volume of a legal market offset the death of the risk premium?
*   **Resolution:** No. Aggregate activity is flat; only the border (logistics) shows a spark.
*   **Implications:** Formalization is a "regulatory success" but a "developmental dud" for the smallholder.

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong. It’s a "tragic" story of development policy, which makes for a compelling read.

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
At an economics dinner party, I’d lead with: **"Morocco legalized cannabis and the price for farmers dropped by 98%—satellite data shows the 'boom' everyone expected never actually happened."**

People would lean in. The follow-up would be: "Where did the money go?" The paper’s finding that activity concentrated at the border suggests it went to the "middlemen" and the state, not the mountain farmers. That is a high-value "So What."

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
*   **Front-loading:** The "Legalization Discount" concept is the star; it should be defined more formally in a small theoretical framework before the results.
*   **Appendix:** The RDD results (page 15) are actually very interesting—the fact that there was a *pre-existing* discontinuity is a red flag that needs more airtime in the main text to defend the DiD.
*   **Conclusion:** Currently a bit repetitive. It should expand on the "EU Building Directive" analogy mentioned on page 3—broadening the "formalization trap" to other industries.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The gap is **Scope and Mechanism.**
Currently, the "Legalization Discount" (the 1/50th price drop) is cited from news reports. If the author can find/scrape actual price data or use a secondary proxy for farmer welfare, it moves from "interesting observation" to "fundamental paper on the transition from informal to formal markets."

**Single biggest piece of advice:** Formally model the "Legalization Discount" and find one more data source (even if it's high-frequency vegetable prices or localized migration data) to confirm that farmers are actually feeling the squeeze.
