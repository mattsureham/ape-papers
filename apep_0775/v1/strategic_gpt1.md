## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple, policy-relevant question: when states restore SNAP eligibility to people with drug felony convictions, does that help them reconnect to formal work, or does it instead reduce labor supply through a standard income effect? Using staggered state rollbacks of the 1996 SNAP drug felon ban, the paper argues that restoring benefits modestly reduces formal employment among low-education workers, especially in construction, suggesting that food assistance is not a springboard into formal employment.

A busy economist should care because this sits at the intersection of the safety net, labor supply, and criminal justice reentry, and because the policy rhetoric often claims that benefit restoration promotes work. If true, the paper would discipline that claim.

**Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Not quite. The introduction is competent, but it leads with institutional background and “theoretical ambiguity,” rather than with the sharper, more provocative question: **does food assistance help reentry into work, or does it simply relax labor supply at the margin?** The current version also too quickly turns into a dialogue with one prior paper, which makes the contribution feel literature-reactive rather than world-driven.

**What the first two paragraphs should say instead:**

> Each year, hundreds of thousands of people leave prison facing an immediate economic problem: they need food and they need work. For people with drug felony convictions, U.S. policy has long made those two margins unusually entangled. Under the 1996 welfare reform law, many were barred from SNAP, and in recent years states have begun reversing that exclusion. The core question is straightforward and important: when access to food assistance is restored for this highly disadvantaged group, does formal employment rise because basic needs are stabilized, or fall because nonlabor income reduces the urgency of work?
>
> This paper studies that question using the wave of state rollbacks of the SNAP drug felon ban between 2015 and 2019. I show that restoring eligibility does not increase formal employment; if anything, it modestly reduces employment among low-education workers, with the largest declines in construction. The result suggests that, on the relevant policy margin, SNAP restoration functions more as income support than as an employment-promoting reentry intervention.

That is the pitch. It is clearer, more concrete, and much closer to an AER-style “why this matters in the world” opening.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution in one sentence:**  
The paper claims that restoring SNAP eligibility to people with drug felony convictions modestly reduces formal employment among low-education workers, implying that on this reentry margin the income effect dominates any job-search facilitation effect.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?
Only partially. The paper distinguishes itself from Tuttle on imposition versus rollback, and from broader SNAP labor-supply papers by focusing on a criminal-justice-targeted eligibility rule. But right now the differentiation is thinner than the author seems to think.

A reader may come away with: “This is another paper showing modest labor supply effects of SNAP, in a narrower subgroup.” That is not enough for AER unless the subgroup is itself of first-order importance or the policy setting cleanly answers a larger question. The paper needs to make clearer that this is not just about drug felons; it is about **whether safety-net access complements labor market reintegration among the most marginalized workers.**

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or filling a gap in a LITERATURE?
Mixed, but too much the latter. The strongest framing is world-facing: policymakers and advocates often justify benefit restoration as aiding reentry and employment. The paper should lean hard into testing that real-world claim. Instead, parts of the introduction sound like “Tuttle studied imposition, I study removal,” which is a narrower and less exciting framing.

### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what’s new?
At present, maybe, but not crisply. They would probably say: “It’s a DiD on state rollbacks of the SNAP drug felon ban; they find small negative employment effects for low-education workers.” That is intelligible, but still sounds like “another DiD paper about a targeted policy.”

The paper needs a sharper novelty statement such as:  
**The novel fact is that restoring a highly salient in-kind benefit to a reentry population does not improve formal employment, contrary to the common stabilization narrative.**

### What would make this contribution bigger?
Several possibilities:

1. **A broader outcome frame.**  
   If employment is the only outcome, the paper risks reading as “small negative labor supply effect of SNAP.” Bigger question: what is the overall effect on reentry? Employment is only one margin. If the paper could connect employment to earnings, job stability, formal/informal substitution, public assistance take-up, or recidivism, the contribution becomes much more important.

2. **A stronger mechanism demonstration.**  
   The construction result is suggestive, but still thin. If the story is formal-to-informal substitution, then evidence more directly related to churn, hires/separations, earnings conditional on employment, or sectoral reallocation would make the paper more than a one-coefficient exercise.

3. **A more ambitious framing around reintegration policy.**  
   The paper should argue that this policy is a test case for a larger question: when disadvantaged populations face severe liquidity constraints, does relieving hardship increase attachment to formal labor markets? That is a general economics question, not just a niche correction to one SNAP rule.

4. **A clearer contrast with “work-first” rhetoric.**  
   The contribution gets bigger if the paper explicitly frames itself as evaluating a widely asserted policy mechanism, not just estimating an effect on one subgroup.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

### Closest neighbors
The likely closest neighbors are:

- **East (2020)** on SNAP generosity and labor supply.
- **Ganong and Liebman / Ganong et al.** on SNAP participation and work patterns.
- **Hoynes and Schanzenbach / Hoynes et al.** on SNAP’s broader effects.
- On reentry/labor market barriers: **Doleac and Hansen** on ban-the-box; **Yang (2017)** on criminal background disclosure and labor market outcomes; perhaps **Mueller-Smith** on criminal justice exposure and labor market trajectories.

Depending on exactly what the field considers canonical, the paper might also speak to:
- the welfare and labor supply literature on in-kind transfers,
- the reentry literature on post-incarceration constraints,
- and possibly the behavioral poverty/scarcity literature if it wants the “stabilization” channel in the setup.

### How should the paper position itself relative to those neighbors?
Mostly **build on** and **reconcile**, not attack.

- Relative to **Tuttle**, the paper should say: “That paper studies benefit loss; I study benefit restoration. The two margins need not be symmetric.” This is a sensible and potentially interesting complement.
- Relative to the broader SNAP labor-supply literature, it should say: “This setting isolates a high-need, highly work-marginal population where the sign of the effect is especially uncertain.”
- Relative to reentry papers, it should say: “Most reentry labor papers study employer-side barriers; this paper studies whether consumption-side support changes formal work attachment.”

### Is the paper currently positioned too narrowly or too broadly?
Somehow both.

- **Too narrowly** in the sense that it spends too much time as a paper about one statutory provision and one prior paper.
- **Too broadly** in the sense that it gestures at several literatures without choosing the primary conversation.

The paper needs to choose a lead conversation. My view: the best lead conversation is **reentry and economic reintegration**, with SNAP/labor supply as the economic mechanism. If it instead leads with “another SNAP labor supply paper,” it shrinks itself.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?
The paper underplays at least three conversations:

1. **Reentry and desistance economics**  
   The deeper question is reintegration, not just labor supply. It should engage more with work on post-release instability, formal versus informal work, and the sequencing of housing/food/work constraints.

2. **Informal labor market adjustment**  
   Since the discussion leans heavily on substitution into informal work, the paper should sound more literate in that literature. Right now construction is treated as quasi-self-evident, but the broader literature on off-the-books work, contingent employment, and criminal-record sorting is relevant.

3. **Public economics of in-kind transfers under extreme disadvantage**  
   If the paper wants to make a general contribution, it should connect more explicitly to how in-kind transfers affect labor supply when recipients are severely constrained and administratively excluded.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?
Not quite yet. The highest-impact framing may come from an unexpected bridge:

**This is not mainly a SNAP paper; it is a paper about whether reducing material hardship improves formal labor market reintegration after incarceration.**

That conversation is bigger and more consequential. AER readers will care more about “what helps reentry into the formal economy?” than about one narrow corner of SNAP administration.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Formerly incarcerated people, especially those with drug felony convictions, face extreme barriers to stable work. States have been restoring access to food assistance for this group, often under the claim that basic support helps people get back on their feet.

### Tension
Economic theory points in two directions. Food assistance could stabilize search and improve employment, or it could reduce formal labor supply through an income effect. Existing evidence is limited and mostly on the opposite policy margin.

### Resolution
The paper finds little evidence that benefit restoration increases formal employment; if anything, employment falls modestly for low-education workers, especially in construction and in states with full repeal.

### Implications
The case for restoring SNAP eligibility should not rest on employment gains. More broadly, hardship relief and formal labor-market attachment may not move together in the way advocates sometimes claim.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
It has the ingredients, but the arc is weaker than it should be. At present, the paper often feels like:

- institutional setup,
- regression setup,
- table of modest negative estimates,
- some heterogeneous effects,
- interpretation.

That is serviceable, but not compelling. The main reason is that the findings are modest, so the paper must rely heavily on narrative architecture. Right now the story is underpowered relative to the evidence.

### If it is a collection of results looking for a story, what story should it tell?
The story should be:

**Policymakers often treat hardship relief as a reentry-to-work intervention. This paper shows that on one prominent margin, that claim is wrong: restoring food assistance does not increase formal employment, and may reduce it. The right normative defense of the policy is humanitarian, not work-promoting.**

That is a real story. It has setup, tension, and implications. It also gives the null/modest result a reason to exist.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?
“States restored SNAP eligibility for people with drug felony convictions, and formal employment didn’t rise—if anything it fell a bit among low-education workers, especially in construction.”

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
Top field economists would probably **half-lean in**. The question is interesting, but the headline fact is modest and not obviously field-shifting. This is not one of those results that instantly compels attention on magnitude alone.

What determines whether they lean in further is the next sentence. If the presenter says, “This undermines the common claim that food assistance helps labor-market reentry,” interest rises. If instead the presenter says, “We estimate a small negative coefficient in a staggered DiD,” phones come out.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Probably one of these:
- “So does this mean SNAP harms reintegration, or just shifts people away from formal payroll jobs?”
- “Is the interesting margin formal vs informal work?”
- “How does this square with the earlier paper finding the ban reduced employment?”
- “Why should I care about this subgroup for broader labor or public economics?”

Those are strategic clues. The paper should preemptively answer them in framing, not wait for the reader to ask.

### If the findings are null or modest: is the null interesting?
Potentially yes, but the paper has not yet fully earned it. It says the result is a “hard null,” but that language is a little too self-congratulatory for what is really a modest negative estimate with limited power. The better claim is:

**The paper rejects a common policy narrative, even if it does not show a large effect in either direction.**

That is useful. But to make the null/modest result feel informative rather than disappointing, the paper needs to be much clearer that the relevant counterfactual expectation in the policy discourse was positive employment effects. Then “no boost to work” becomes a substantive finding rather than an underwhelming one.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

Bluntly: in current form, this does **not** yet read like an AER paper.

The main gaps are:

### 1. Framing problem
This is the biggest one. The paper has a potentially interesting fact, but it is wrapped in a small, somewhat mechanical frame. It reads as a competent applied micro paper about a narrow policy rollout, not as a paper answering a first-order question.

### 2. Scope problem
Employment alone, measured at a fairly aggregated level, makes the contribution feel limited. To be AER-level, the paper likely needs either:
- a broader set of outcomes that speak to reentry and welfare more holistically, or
- a much sharper mechanism showing what margin is actually moving.

### 3. Novelty problem
The paper’s core result sits close to standard intuition from labor supply: nonlabor income can modestly reduce work. The special population makes it interesting, but not automatically top-journal interesting. The paper needs to show why this setting changes what we thought we knew, not merely replicates a familiar sign in a special subgroup.

### 4. Ambition problem
The current manuscript is careful and sensible, but safe. It does not yet extract the full conceptual ambition from the setting. The big question is not “what is the coefficient of a SNAP rollback?” It is “does material support facilitate formal economic reintegration after incarceration?” That is an ambitious question. The paper should own it.

### Single most impactful piece of advice
**Reframe the paper around the broader question of whether reducing material hardship promotes formal labor-market reintegration after incarceration, and make the core contribution the rejection of the policy narrative that SNAP restoration helps people return to work.**

That one change would do the most to move the paper upward. If the author cannot make that frame persuasive, the paper will likely remain a niche applied micro study.
