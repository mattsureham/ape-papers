## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether restoring SNAP eligibility to people with drug felony convictions helps them reconnect to formal work or instead reduces labor supply through an income effect. Using state rollbacks of the SNAP drug felon ban from 2015–2019, the paper argues that employment among low-education workers falls modestly after eligibility is restored, especially in construction, implying that food assistance is not a bridge to formal employment.

Why should a busy economist care? Because this is a clean policy margin at the intersection of safety-net design, labor supply, and reentry: do in-kind transfers stabilize fragile workers and raise employment, or do they mainly relax short-run work pressure?

**Does the paper articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Not quite. The opening is competent, but the pitch is still too local and too literature-driven. It spends too much time on the institutional oddity of the drug felon ban and not enough time on the broader economic question: when highly disadvantaged adults gain access to a basic in-kind transfer, what happens to formal labor supply? The introduction also overstates the “empirically unresolved” nature of the question while simultaneously anchoring on one close paper.

**What the first two paragraphs should say instead:**

> Social insurance for highly disadvantaged adults is often defended not only on humanitarian grounds, but also as a tool for reintegration into the labor market. Yet basic theory points in two directions: access to food assistance may improve job search and stability, or it may reduce formal work by relaxing immediate income needs. Which force dominates is especially important for populations at the edge of the formal labor market, where informal work and nonemployment are realistic alternatives.
>
> This paper studies that question using state rollbacks of the 1996 SNAP ban on people with drug felony convictions. I show that when states restore eligibility, formal employment among low-education workers does not rise and may fall modestly, with the largest declines in construction. The result suggests that at this policy margin, SNAP operates more as income support than as a labor-market springboard—sharpening how economists and policymakers should think about the employment consequences of reentry-oriented safety-net expansions.

That version starts with a world question, then uses the policy as the setting, rather than treating the setting as the contribution.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution in one sentence:**  
The paper provides evidence that restoring SNAP eligibility to individuals affected by drug felony bans modestly reduces formal employment among low-education workers, suggesting that the dominant effect of this safety-net expansion is an income effect rather than improved labor-market attachment.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?
Only partly. The paper names some relevant literatures, but the novelty relative to the closest neighbor—Tuttle on the original ban—is not yet sharp enough. Right now the paper risks sounding like: “another staggered DiD on a welfare policy, with small labor-supply effects.” The asymmetry between **imposing** a ban and **removing** a ban is potentially interesting, but the paper has not fully made that asymmetry the centerpiece.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a gap in a literature?
It oscillates, but too often defaults to a literature-gap framing: “Tuttle studied imposition, I study removal.” That is weaker than: “Policymakers are currently restoring eligibility, and the key real-world question is whether access to food assistance helps marginalized ex-offenders reconnect to formal work.”

### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what’s new?
A reasonably smart economist could say: “It studies rollback of the SNAP drug felon ban and finds small negative employment effects.” But they could also easily say: “It’s another DiD paper on a state policy change with modest labor effects.” That is the danger.

### What would make this contribution bigger?
Most importantly, **reframe the estimand and stakes**. The paper is currently selling “employment effects of rollback.” That is too narrow for AER. A bigger version is:

- **A paper about the labor-market consequences of reentry-oriented safety-net access.**
- Or even better: **a paper about whether transfers move marginal workers from formal work into nonwork or into informality.**

Specific ways to make it bigger:
1. **Formal vs informal margin.** The construction result begs for this framing. If the paper can connect the employment decline to substitution away from formal jobs rather than broad withdrawal, the story becomes much more interesting.
2. **Reentry outcomes beyond employment.** If there is any credible evidence on recidivism, program take-up, earnings, UI-covered job attachment, or churn, that would enlarge the question from “employment effect” to “reintegration effect.”
3. **Exploit asymmetry with Tuttle directly.** The big contribution could be that gains and losses of SNAP eligibility have asymmetric effects because newly eligible people are not the same as current recipients losing benefits. That is a substantive behavioral point, not just a new policy episode.
4. **Connect to labor supply under extreme deprivation.** If the paper wants to speak to core labor/public economists, it should emphasize what we learn about transfer effects at the very bottom of the labor market.

### How should the paper position itself relative to those neighbors?
Mostly **build on** Tuttle, but do so more sharply. The paper should not “attack” Tuttle; it should say: Tuttle identifies the consequences of taking away benefits from an already-eligible population, while this paper identifies the consequences of restoring benefits to a population long excluded. Those are different policy margins and likely different behavioral objects.

Relative to the SNAP labor-supply literature, the paper should **synthesize** rather than merely cite. It should say: for populations at the edge of formal work, transfer expansions need not improve employment even when the policy is explicitly justified in reintegration terms.

Relative to the criminal-justice literature, it should **extend** the conversation from labor-demand barriers to material-support barriers.

### Is the paper positioned too narrowly or too broadly?
Currently, oddly, both.

- **Too narrowly** in institutional framing: it is very tied to a specific ban affecting a specific subgroup.
- **Too broadly** in contribution claims: it gestures at safety-net labor supply, criminal justice, null results, and humanitarian policy, but without a single dominant conversation.

It needs one main lane and one secondary lane. My advice:
- **Main lane:** public economics / labor — what happens to formal labor supply when extremely disadvantaged adults gain access to in-kind transfers?
- **Secondary lane:** crime and reentry — what does this imply for reintegration policy?

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?
It needs to speak more directly to:
- The broader literature on **transfer programs and labor supply at intensive/extensive margins**.
- The literature on **informality / off-the-books work / formal job attachment**, especially in sectors like construction.
- The literature on **scarcity, instability, and behavioral constraints** if it wants to keep the job-search facilitation mechanism.
- Possibly the literature on **incarceration spillovers and post-release labor supply** more broadly.

Not quite yet. The most impactful conversation is not “drug felon SNAP bans are weird and under-studied.” It is: **Can the safety net serve as a labor-market reintegration tool for people on the margins of formal employment?** That is a much better AER conversation.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
States have increasingly restored SNAP access for people with drug felony convictions. These policies are often defended as helping reintegration, and economic theory offers competing predictions about labor-market effects.

### Tension
The tension is strong in principle but underexploited in the paper. The real puzzle is not just “sign ambiguous.” It is: **Does material support stabilize reentry enough to increase formal employment, or does it reduce the need to accept low-wage formal jobs, especially where informal work is available?** That is a real economic tension.

### Resolution
The paper finds no employment gains and some evidence of modest declines in formal employment among low-education workers, concentrated in construction and larger where states fully removed the ban.

### Implications
The policy should not be sold primarily as an employment policy. More broadly, transfer expansions for highly marginal workers may reduce formal employment even when they improve welfare.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
It has the skeleton of one, but at present it still reads somewhat like a collection of empirics attached to a plausible story. The strongest narrative thread is **income effect versus reintegration effect**, but the paper diffuses it by spending too much time on narrow institutional detail and by treating “hard nulls” as a contribution in itself. “Hard nulls” is not a narrative; it is at best a fallback justification.

### What story should it be telling?
This paper should tell a **formal-versus-informal labor supply** story among the reentering poor:

- Society often assumes food assistance helps reconnect vulnerable adults to work.
- But among people near the bottom of the labor market, the immediate margin may be formal employment versus informal work/nonwork.
- Restoring SNAP appears to relax pressure to take formal jobs, especially in sectors where informal alternatives are viable.
- Therefore, the welfare case for the policy may be strong even if the employment case is weak.

That story is much more memorable than “we find a small negative coefficient.”

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?
“I’ve got a paper showing that when states restored SNAP eligibility to people with drug felony convictions, formal employment didn’t rise—it modestly fell, especially in construction.”

That is the most interesting fact.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
A subset would lean in—especially public, labor, and crime economists—but many would not unless the framing is sharpened. The current version sounds modest and qualified in a way that drains urgency. The result needs to be sold as surprising relative to the common policy rhetoric that food assistance aids reentry into work.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Immediately: **“Is this people leaving formal work for informal work, or just overall labor supply falling?”**  
That is the key follow-up, and the paper currently cannot answer it directly. That is a strategic vulnerability.

A second follow-up: **“How do you reconcile this with Tuttle’s result that the ban reduced employment?”** The paper has an answer, but it arrives late and reads a bit post hoc. That reconciliation should move to the introduction and become central.

### If findings are modest/null, is that okay?
Yes—but only if the paper convinces the reader that the null/modest result adjudicates an important policy claim. The paper partially does this. It needs to say more clearly: there is an active and consequential claim that restoring basic food assistance helps labor-market reintegration; this paper directly tests that claim and finds little support.

Right now it still risks feeling like a technically competent paper with borderline-significant effects and a lot of defensive prose about why small effects matter.

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

### What should be shorter, longer, moved, or eliminated?

1. **Shorten the institutional background.**  
   It is serviceable but overlong relative to the contribution. This is not a paper where readers need a full policy-history walkthrough before they understand the question.

2. **Shorten methodological throat-clearing in the introduction.**

3. **Move “hard nulls” language out of the contribution section.**

4. **Bring the Tuttle reconciliation earlier.**  
   That is crucial for novelty and should appear in the intro, not mainly in the discussion.

5. **Elevate the construction result if it is the mechanism clue.**  
   Right now it appears as just heterogeneity. If construction is where formal/informal substitution is most plausible, it should be integrated into the main story, not treated as a side result.

6. **Cut repetitive significance language.**  
   Too many sentences are written in the register of coefficient + p-value + reassurance. For AER positioning, the paper should spend more of its scarce introductory real estate on ideas.

### Is the paper front-loaded with the good stuff?
Somewhat, but not enough. The best stuff is:
- the policy question,
- the asymmetry with Tuttle,
- the implication that the safety net may not be a work springboard,
- the construction/informality angle.

Those should all be in the first 2–3 pages in conceptual language.

### Are important results buried?
Yes: the asymmetry interpretation and the formal/informal-margin interpretation are buried in the discussion. Those should be promoted.

### Is the conclusion adding value?
A little, but not much beyond summary. The conclusion should end with one sharper claim about what economists should update on: namely, that for highly marginalized adults, restoring basic consumption support may improve welfare without increasing formal employment, and policymakers should stop conflating those goals.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

Bluntly: in current form, this is **not yet an AER story**. The main gap is not just execution; it is strategic ambition.

### What is the main problem?
Mostly a **framing and ambition problem**, with some **scope problem**.

- **Framing problem:** The paper undersells the world question and oversells the niche institutional setting.
- **Ambition problem:** It is content to present a small negative employment effect and call that informative. That is not enough for AER without a bigger conceptual hook.
- **Scope problem:** The paper wants to claim a mechanism—income effect, formal/informal substitution, asymmetry with benefit loss—but the evidence base presented is too narrow to carry that confidently as a top-journal contribution.

### Is it a novelty problem?
Partly. The exact policy margin is novel enough. But “state policy rollback + small labor-market effect” is not, by itself, top-journal novel. The novelty has to come from the conceptual lesson: **the labor-market consequences of transfer restoration for people at the margins of formal work differ from the consequences of transfer removal, and formal employment may not be the right metric of reintegration success.**

### What is the gap between current paper and one that would excite the top 10 people in this field?
A paper that would excite them would do at least one of these:
1. Cleanly show that restored benefits shift people from formal jobs toward informal work or lower formal attachment.
2. Directly reconcile and generalize the contrast with prior evidence on benefit removal.
3. Broaden the outcome space to reentry-relevant margins—earnings, churn, recidivism, program participation, stability—so the paper speaks to welfare and reintegration, not just employment levels.
4. Use the setting to say something broader about labor supply under deprivation and the behavioral effects of in-kind transfers at the bottom.

### Single most impactful advice
**Reframe the paper around the central economic question—whether safety-net access pushes marginal workers into or out of formal employment—and make the asymmetry with prior evidence plus the construction/informality margin the core story, not a side interpretation.**

That one change would not solve everything, but it would dramatically improve the paper’s strategic position.
