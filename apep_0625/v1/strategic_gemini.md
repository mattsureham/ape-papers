## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether prohibiting employers from asking about salary history (Salary History Bans) actually reduces the gender pay gap or backfires by triggering statistical discrimination. Using administrative data and a staggered DiD design, it finds that these bans narrow the gender gap in new-hire earnings by 2.3% without inducing the negative spillover effects on Black workers commonly seen in other "information removal" policies like Ban-the-Box.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this pitch quite well. The first two paragraphs effectively set up the "ratchet" tension and the "Ban-the-Box" counter-hypothesis. However, it misses a beat by not explicitly stating *why* we should care about the QWI data over previous survey-based studies until the third paragraph.
*   **Revised Pitch:** "Salary History Bans (SHBs) are designed to stop past discrimination from anchoring future wages, but theory suggests they could backfire by forcing employers to rely on demographic stereotypes. Using near-universal administrative data, this paper finds that SHBs effectively narrow the new-hire gender gap by 2.3%—and up to 4.1% over time—while avoiding the statistical discrimination against Black workers observed in other information-restriction policies. This suggests SHBs are a rare 'free lunch' in labor market regulation: reducing inequality without clear efficiency-equity trade-offs."

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:** The paper provides the first comprehensive, administrative-data-based evidence that Salary History Bans narrow the gender gap across industries without triggering statistical discrimination against other marginalized groups.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It differentiates itself well from Barach & Horton (2021) and Agan et al. (2022) by moving from audit/survey data to administrative aggregates (QWI) and applying modern staggered DiD (CS) that reveals effects hidden by previous methods.
*   **Question vs. Literature:** It is framed primarily as a question about the **WORLD** (Do these bans work?).
*   **Clarity:** A smart economist would say: "It uses the latest DiD tools on the best available administrative data to show that salary history bans actually work as intended, unlike Ban-the-Box."
*   **Bigger Contribution:** To truly elevate this, the author needs to dig deeper into the **male earnings increase (3.8%)** mentioned in Section 5.5. This is a massive "So What?" moment. If SHBs raise the floor for everyone, the "contribution" isn't just about the gap; it’s about market-wide wage transparency and bargaining power.

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

*   **Neighbors:** Doleac & Hansen (2020) on Ban-the-Box; Agan et al. (2022) on SHB audits; Callaway & Sant’Anna (2021) on DiD.
*   **Positioning:** It currently "builds" on the SHB literature and "contrasts" with the Ban-the-Box literature. This is the correct move. 
*   **Niche vs. Broad:** The audience is broad (Labor, Public, Law & Econ).
*   **Missing Conversations:** The paper should connect more to the **Pay Transparency** literature (e.g., Mas 2017, Baker et al. 2023). If male wages are also going up, is this an information ban or a de facto transparency mandate? 
*   **Reframing:** The paper could be "The Right Conversation" by framing SHBs not just as a gender policy, but as a test of **Search and Matching** models where information friction benefits firms over workers.

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

*   **Setup:** The "Ratchet" effect—women's lower past pay follows them forever.
*   **Tension:** Will banning this info help (by stopping the ratchet) or hurt (by causing employers to guess race/gender productivity)?
*   **Resolution:** It helps. The gap closes, and there’s no racial backfiring.
*   **Implications:** Information removal is not always "bad" for the groups it aims to help; the nature of the information removed matters.

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong. The "resolution" is clean, though the "tension" regarding the male wage increase in Section 5.5 is a bit of a narrative curveball that needs to be integrated into the main arc rather than treated as a robustness check.

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

*   **The Lead Fact:** "Banning salary history questions closes 8.5% of the gender gap for new hires and actually raises male wages too."
*   **Reaction:** People will lean in. The "male wages rise" finding is the most provocative part of the paper—it suggests the policy changes the *equilibrium* of the bargaining game, not just the distribution.
*   **Follow-up:** "Wait, if male wages go up 3.8% and the gap narrows by 2.3%, does that mean female wages went up 6%? Who is paying for this? Are firms hiring fewer people?" (The paper starts to answer this with hiring rates, but needs more emphasis).

---

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

*   **Front-loading:** Move the male earnings result (Table 5, Col 4) earlier. It is too important to be a "Placebo/Robustness" test. 
*   **Appendix:** The methodological discussion on TWFE vs. CS (Section 5.1) is useful but could be trimmed. We know TWFE can fail; the "AER version" of this paper focuses on the economic mechanism more than the econometric validation.
*   **Mechanism:** Section 5.3 (Industry Heterogeneity) is currently a bit weak (null DDD). This should be moved to the appendix or reimagined.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

**The Gap:** Currently, it reads like a very high-quality *Applied Economics* or *JMP* paper. It is competent and "correct." To make it AER-level, it needs **Ambition.**

**Single biggest improvement:** The author must explain the **Male Wage Increase.** If SHBs increase new-hire wages for *everyone* (while narrowing the gap), the paper is no longer just about "discrimination." It becomes a major paper about **monopsony power and wage setting.** If firms can't anchor, do they have to move to a "posted wage" system that favors the worker? That is a "Top 5" story.
