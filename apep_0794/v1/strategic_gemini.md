### 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper evaluates the "diversity revolution" promised by the mass adoption of test-optional college admissions during COVID-19. It shows that while removing SAT/ACT requirements caused a massive 14% surge in applications, it resulted in a negligible change in the actual racial composition of enrolled students (a "Black enrollment share" increase of only 0.29 percentage points at the most selective schools). The paper argues that standardized tests are merely a mirror of existing inequalities rather than the primary barrier to entry, as the binding constraints on diversity lie "upstream" of the admissions office.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this pitch exceptionally well. The term "Application Illusion" is a "sticky" phrase that captures the essence of the result immediately. The first two paragraphs effectively set the stakes (the largest simultaneous change in admissions history) and deliver the punchline.

### 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:** The paper demonstrates that lowering application barriers through test-optional policies creates a surge in nominal interest ("top of the funnel") but fails to reallocate student enrollment ("bottom of the funnel") because tests reflect, rather than create, the fundamental barriers to college access.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It is well-differentiated. While previous papers use proprietary data (Common App/single institutions), this uses the IPEDS universe, allowing for a general equilibrium-style look at the entire US higher-ed landscape.
*   **World vs. Literature:** It answers a question about the **WORLD**. This isn't about fixing a DiD estimator; it’s about whether a massive policy shift achieved its social goals.
*   **"Another DiD paper?":** A smart economist would recognize this as a "Value of Information" or "Transaction Cost" paper. It’s not just "another DiD"; it’s a cautionary tale about "frictionless" systems.
*   **Making it bigger:** To make this a "slam dunk" AER, the author needs more data on **financial aid**. If the "illusion" exists because the marginal Black applicant was admitted but couldn't afford the net price, that would move this from a "what" paper to a "why" paper.

### 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

The paper sits at the intersection of **Labor Economics (Education)** and **Market Design**.

*   **Closest Neighbors:** *Pallais (2015)* on application costs; *Hoxby & Turner (2013)* on information frictions; *Chetty et al. (2023)* on elite admissions.
*   **Strategy:** It should **synthesize** these. It confirms Pallais’s theory that marginal applicants are sensitive to costs but builds on Chetty by showing that even when you remove the "gatekeeper," the "gates" remain closed due to structural factors.
*   **Missing Conversations:** The paper should speak more to the **Industrial Organization** of higher education. If schools knew this would happen, was "test-optional" a strategic move to lower their "admit rate" (by boosting applications) and thus juice their *US News & World Report* rankings? The "Illusion" might be a feature for the colleges, not a bug.

### 4. NARRATIVE ARC

*   **Setup:** For decades, the SAT was the "Great Gatekeeper" blocking minority access.
*   **Tension:** COVID-19 provided a "forced" natural experiment where the gatekeeper was fired overnight. Proponents expected a diversity surge; critics expected a meritocracy collapse.
*   **Resolution:** Neither happened. Applications went up, but the "same" kids got in and enrolled.
*   **Implications:** Policy focus on "admissions criteria" is likely a waste of time compared to K-12 reform or financial aid.

**Evaluation:** The narrative arc is very strong. It follows a "Myth-Busters" structure which is highly effective for top-tier journals.

### 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

**Dinner Party Fact:** "When colleges dropped the SAT, applications soared by 14%, but at a typical school of 2,000 students, it only resulted in about 3 to 5 additional Black students."

**Reaction:** People will lean in. It is a counter-intuitive finding that challenges the current political zeitgeist and the stated missions of university DEI offices. The follow-up question will be: "So, why did they bother keeping the policy?" (Which leads back to the IO/Rankings story).

### 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

*   **Front-loading:** The paper is already well front-loaded.
*   **Appendix:** The "Binary DiD" results (Table 2, Col 1-3) should be relegated to the appendix earlier. Since the author admits they have pre-trend violations, giving them "Main Table" real estate distracts from the cleaner intensity design.
*   **Mechanism Section:** The "Discussion" is doing too much heavy lifting. I want a small, dedicated section or a few charts showing that **yield rates** fell most for the groups proponents hoped to help.

### 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The "science" is solid, but it feels a bit like a "Letter" right now. To make it a full AER article, it needs to move from **documenting** the illusion to **dissecting** it. 

**The single most impactful change:** Track the "missing" students. Using the IPEDS data on Pell Grants or Net Price, can the author show that the "Application Illusion" is actually a "Financial Aid Illusion"? If the surge in applications came from low-income students who were then "admitted" but given $0 in institutional aid, the story becomes a much more powerful indictment of institutional behavior rather than just a "null result" on testing.
