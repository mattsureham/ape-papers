## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple and important question: when the law forces parties to nominate equal numbers of women and men, do parties also finance women’s campaigns equally? Using candidate-level campaign finance data from Mexican mayoral elections before and after the 2019 “Parity in Everything” reform, the paper shows that legal parity in nominations did not obviously translate into financial parity: women remained substantially underfunded, and any improvement in party transfers was modest and imprecisely estimated.

Busy economists should care because this is a first-order question about whether formal equality changes actual resource allocation inside organizations. The broader issue is not Mexico per se; it is whether quotas alter real power or merely the visible margin of compliance.

**Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Mostly yes, but not sharply enough. The paper has the right ingredients, but it moves too quickly into institutional detail and empirical strategy. The opening should do less scene-setting and more claim-making. Right now, the paper’s most memorable line is “Legal parity arrived. Financial parity did not.” That is good. The rest of the intro should be built around that sentence.

**What the first two paragraphs should say instead:**

> Gender quotas are now common around the world, but they regulate who appears on the ballot, not necessarily who gets the money needed to compete. If parties comply with nomination parity while continuing to steer campaign resources toward men, then legal parity may expand representation on paper without equalizing electoral opportunity in practice.
>
> This paper studies that possibility in Mexico, where the 2019 “Parity in Everything” reform required parties to nominate equal numbers of women and men for mayor. Using candidate-level campaign finance disclosures from the elections immediately before and after the reform, we ask whether legal parity was followed by financial parity. The answer is: only partially. Women’s share of party transfers rose, but they remained substantially underfunded relative to men, and the evidence does not show a clear nationwide break toward equal financing.

That is the pitch the paper should have.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution in one sentence:**  
The paper shows that Mexico’s move to mandatory gender parity in mayoral nominations did not clearly eliminate gender gaps in party-controlled campaign finance, highlighting campaign funding as a distinct and potentially persistent margin of political exclusion.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?
Only somewhat. The paper gestures toward three literatures—gender quotas, campaign finance, and Mexico parity reforms—but the differentiation is still loose. Right now the contribution reads as:

- quotas matter,
- campaign finance matters,
- here is a Mexico application.

That is not enough. It needs sharper differentiation on one of three axes:

1. **Object of study:** most quota papers study representation or policy outcomes; this studies intra-party resource allocation.
2. **Context:** most campaign-finance-and-gender papers are from Europe or the U.S.; this uses a national reform in a major middle-income democracy with strong parity rules.
3. **Conceptual claim:** nomination parity and financial parity are separable institutional margins.

That third point is the real contribution. It should be elevated.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a literature gap?
It is mixed, but too often framed as filling a literature gap. The stronger framing is about the world:

- **Weak:** “We contribute to three bodies of work…”
- **Strong:** “Quota laws can equalize access to candidacy without equalizing access to campaign resources.”

AER wants the latter. The literature review should support the claim, not substitute for it.

### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what’s new?
They could say something like: “It’s about whether gender quotas changed campaign funding in Mexico.” That is decent, but still perilously close to “another DiD/DDD paper about gender quotas.” The introduction does not yet force the reader to see the paper as establishing a broader conceptual distinction between **ballot access** and **resource access**.

### What would make this contribution bigger?
Several possibilities:

- **Bigger outcome variable:** connect campaign finance more directly to electoral performance, win rates, or subsequent political careers. The present paper stops one step short of the really consequential question: does underfunding blunt the substantive effects of parity?
- **Bigger mechanism:** show more directly whether parties use women to fill parity quotas in systematically less competitive municipalities. That mechanism appears in the paper, but currently as interpretation rather than centerpiece.
- **Bigger comparison:** compare party transfers not just to sympathizer donations, but to seat competitiveness, incumbency, or party strategic value of the race. That would deepen the claim from “women get less money” to “parties comply on paper while rationing resources strategically.”
- **Bigger framing:** make the paper about organizational response to regulation. Political parties are one example of a general phenomenon: regulated institutions shift discrimination to less visible margins when formal constraints bind.

If the authors cannot broaden empirically, they should broaden conceptually.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Before this paper, we know that quota laws can increase women’s candidacies and representation. We also know campaign finance is central to electoral competitiveness. But we know less about whether parties redistribute money when forced to redistribute nominations.

### Tension
There is an obvious strategic tension: parties can comply visibly with parity rules while preserving control over less visible margins, especially funding. So formal parity may coexist with substantive inequality.

### Resolution
The paper finds that women’s party transfers increased after the reform, but remained far below men’s, and the evidence does not support a clear national break toward financial parity. The reform appears to have opened the gate to nomination more clearly than the gate to money.

### Implications
If this holds more broadly, then quota reforms may overstate progress if evaluated only by candidate counts. Policymakers may need to regulate or at least monitor resource allocation, not just nominations.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
Yes, but it is only partially realized. The ingredients are there. The problem is that the paper frequently slips from narrative into method memo. It tells the reader too much, too early, about DDD contamination and specification mechanics, and not enough about the substantive tension.

The paper should be telling a cleaner story:

1. **Quota laws changed who must be nominated.**
2. **Parties still control who gets funded.**
3. **That margin is where hidden resistance may live.**
4. **Mexico’s reform offers a chance to test whether formal parity changed substantive support.**
5. **It mostly didn’t—at least not clearly, and not uniformly.**

That is a strong story. The current paper sometimes sounds like a cautiously documented null result in search of a conceptual hook. The hook already exists; it just needs to lead.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?
“After Mexico required gender parity in mayoral nominations, women still received only about 60 cents in party transfers for every peso given to male candidates.”

That is the dinner-party fact. It is concrete, intuitive, and memorable.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
They would lean in initially. The basic fact is striking. But the next sentence matters enormously. If the follow-up is “and then we estimate a triple-difference that is not statistically significant,” attention will drop fast unless the framing is excellent.

The paper therefore must lead with the substantive fact, not the null coefficient.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Probably one of these:

- “Are parties just placing women in unwinnable races?”
- “Does the money gap translate into lower win rates?”
- “Is this Mexico-specific, or a general feature of quota systems?”
- “Does the reform shift discrimination from nominations to funding?”

Those are good questions. The paper should be organized to answer at least one of them more fully.

### If the findings are null or modest, is the null result itself interesting?
Potentially yes—but only if framed correctly. The null is interesting if the prior was that a major constitutional parity reform would force broader institutional change inside parties. Then learning that the funding margin barely moved is informative.

But the paper must avoid sounding like: “We tried to find an effect and didn’t.” It should sound like: “The important fact is that visible legal compliance did not clearly produce invisible organizational compliance.”

The null is not the contribution; the **persistence of a large funding gap after legal parity** is the contribution. The null regression result supports that broader point but should not carry the paper alone.

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

### What would make it read better?

#### 1. Shorten the methodological throat-clearing in the introduction
The introduction currently gets bogged down in caveats about the DDD design too early. Those caveats are honest, but strategically they are misplaced. The first pages should sell the question and the main fact. Readers do not need the entire contamination logic before they are invested.

#### 2. Move part of the contribution paragraph later
The “we contribute to three bodies of work” paragraph is standard but bland. Compress it. Spend more space sharpening the conceptual contribution and less space listing literatures.

#### 3. Front-load the strongest descriptive facts
The most compelling pieces are:

- women got 47 cents on the male peso pre-reform,
- 60 cents post-reform,
- legal parity in nominations did not become financial parity.

These should appear immediately and repeatedly. Right now they do, but the paper then diffuses the force of those facts by quickly shifting to estimator interpretation.

#### 4. Demote some robustness and estimator-detail language
Phrases like “not a fully clean causal design” are commendably transparent but editorially unhelpful in the first pages. Put the caution in one sentence, then move on. Do not let design modesty become the paper’s identity.

#### 5. Elevate heterogeneity only if it tells a substantive story
State-level heterogeneity is potentially interesting, but in the current form it mostly explains imprecision. That is not enough. Either connect heterogeneity to a real organizing hypothesis—party strength, baseline female representation, competitiveness, enforcement intensity—or relegate much of it to an appendix/table mention.

#### 6. The conclusion should do more than summarize
The conclusion is decent, but it could make one bolder general point: institutions often regulate visible margins first, and organizations adapt by preserving discretion on less visible ones. That would help readers leave with a broader lesson.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

At present, the main gap is **framing plus ambition**, more than pure execution.

### Is it a framing problem?
Yes, significantly. The paper has a potentially strong idea but presents itself as a careful empirical note on one reform in one country. That is too small for AER. It should present itself as a paper about **organizational compliance with equality mandates** using politics as the setting.

### Is it a scope problem?
Also yes. The paper’s current scope is narrow: one office level, one country, two election years, one main margin. That can still work if the conceptual payoff is large, but then the paper needs either:

- stronger evidence on consequences,
- clearer mechanism,
- or broader generalization.

### Is it a novelty problem?
Moderately. Gender quotas are a crowded space. “Another paper on quotas and women in politics” is not enough. The novelty has to be the distinction between nomination parity and funding parity, and that distinction needs to feel fundamental, not incremental.

### Is it an ambition problem?
Yes. The paper is intelligent and careful, but a bit too safe. It settles for documenting a partial disconnect between law and money. The AER version would push harder on why that disconnect matters for political competition, organizational behavior, or policy design.

### Single most impactful piece of advice
**Reframe the paper around a general political-economy claim: quota laws regulate ballot access, but parties retain discretion over resource allocation, so formal parity may leave substantive electoral opportunity unequal.**

That is the one change that would most improve its prospects. If the authors can make readers believe that this is not mainly a Mexico quota paper but a paper about how organizations comply strategically with equality mandates, everything else improves.
