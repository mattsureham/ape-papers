## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper investigates whether constitutional mandates for gender parity in political nominations translate into equitable access to campaign funding. Using a massive candidate-level dataset from Mexico, the authors test if political parties—forced to nominate women—systematically underfund them relative to men, thereby maintaining a "funding gate" that undermines formal legal equality.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this well in the first two paragraphs. It immediately hooks the reader with the stark "47 cents on the dollar" fact and transitions quickly to the concept of the "funding gate" as a mechanism for informal exclusion. It successfully frames a technical finance question as a fundamental question of democratic representation.

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution:** The paper identifies that legal mandates for descriptive representation (nomination quotas) fail to close the gender gap in institutional campaign transfers in the short run, documenting a "funding gate" that persists even when the "candidacy gate" is forced open.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It differentiates itself from the "policy outcome" literature (Chattopadhyay/Duflo) by looking at the *upstream* financial viability of candidates. It differs from existing campaign finance papers by using a national constitutional break rather than voluntary or sub-national quotas.
*   **Framing:** It is framed as a question about the world: "Does financial parity follow legal parity?" This is a strong, intuitive hook.
*   **AER Quality:** A smart economist would see this as more than "another DiD." The use of triple-differences (Party Transfers vs. Private Donations) as a way to isolate institutional intent from market viability is clever, even if the "placebo" (private donations) moves.
*   **Making it bigger:** The paper would be significantly stronger if it could link these funding gaps to **electoral success**. If women get 40% less money, do they lose at higher rates? Closing the loop between *Inputs* (Money) and *Outputs* (Election results) would turn this from a descriptive paper about finance into a definitive paper about the failure of representation.

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

The paper sits at the intersection of Political Economy and Gender Economics.

*   **Closest Neighbors:** Besley et al. (2017) on "mediocre men," Folke and Rickne (2016) on the glass ceiling, and the seminal Duflo/Pande work on quotas.
*   **Positioning:** It currently acts as a "bridge" between the quota literature and the campaign finance literature. It should position itself more aggressively as a critique of "Parity in Everything" policies, suggesting that such mandates are toothless without financial teeth.
*   **Missing Conversations:** The paper should connect to the **Political Agency/Selection** literature. Are parties choosing "weak" women on purpose to fulfill the quota? The finding that private donations *fell* for women post-mandate suggests a selection effect (parties running women in unwinnable districts) that deserves a deeper connection to models of strategic candidate selection.

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

*   **Setup:** Mexico passes a world-leading "Parity in Everything" law.
*   **Tension:** Legal parity is easy to measure, but financial support is a hidden "black box" where parties can still exercise bias.
*   **Resolution:** A "Null" result—the gap doesn't close. Furthermore, the "Placebo" finding suggests a darker story: parties may be "maliciously complying" by nominating women in districts where they are doomed to fail financially.
*   **Implications:** Quotas are necessary but insufficient; the next frontier of gender equality in politics is the bank account, not just the ballot.

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong. The paper moves from a hopeful policy change to a sober realization of institutional inertia.

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

**The Dinner Party Fact:** "Mexico mandated 50/50 gender parity for all offices, but parties still gave women only 60 cents for every dollar they gave men—and private donors actually gave women *less* after the law passed."

**Reaction:** People will lean in. The "malicious compliance" or "strategic placement" angle is the most provocative part of the paper. The "null" on the main effect isn't a failure; it’s an indictment of the policy’s scope.

---

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

*   **Front-loading:** The state-level heterogeneity (Table 4) is currently a bit of a "data dump." This should be used to build a "proof of concept"—show one state where it worked and one where it failed to illustrate the mechanisms of party control.
*   **The Appendix:** The standardized effect sizes (Table 5) are very helpful for the "So What?" test and should be integrated into the main results text to show that even if the coefficient were significant, the magnitude of change is tiny compared to the baseline gap.
*   **The Conclusion:** It is currently a bit repetitive. It should lean harder into the policy recommendation: Why did the "40% mandate" mentioned in Section 2 fail to close the gap?

---

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

**The Gap:** Currently, the paper is a very high-quality "Policy Evaluation" of a specific Mexican reform. To be a top-tier AER paper, it needs to move from "What happened in Mexico?" to **"What does this teach us about the limits of institutional design?"**

**The Single Biggest Improvement:** The authors must investigate **Strategic Placement**. If they can show that the funding gap is driven by parties putting women in "unwinnable" districts (using 2018 margin-of-victory as a proxy for district "winnability"), they would move from a descriptive null to a behavioral finding about how organizations subvert mandates.
