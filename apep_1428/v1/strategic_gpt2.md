## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple, important question: when the law forces parties to nominate equal numbers of women and men, do parties also give women equal campaign resources? Using Mexican mayoral candidates before and after the 2019 “Parity in Everything” reform, the paper shows that legal parity in nominations did not clearly translate into financial parity in party funding: women’s share of party transfers rose, but substantial gaps remained and the overall improvement is noisy and uneven across states.

Yes, a busy economist should care. The broader issue is whether formal inclusion reforms change real allocation inside organizations, or whether gatekeepers comply on paper while preserving discretion over the resources that actually matter.

### Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Mostly yes, but not optimally. The opening has a good fact and a clear question. But it dilutes the pitch too quickly by moving into the “campaign spending predicts success” point and then the “funding gate” language before fully establishing the bigger economic question: do anti-discrimination or parity mandates change actual resource allocation within institutions?

The introduction should be sharper, more world-question driven, and less method-forward in the first pages. Right now the paper reaches the right destination, but it takes a slightly side-road route to get there.

### The pitch the paper should have

A stronger first two paragraphs would say something like:

> Gender parity laws are now common around the world, but nomination parity may not be the same as meaningful electoral opportunity. Political parties can comply with quota rules while still steering money, organizational support, and winnable races toward men. If so, reforms that appear to equalize access may leave the real allocation of opportunity largely unchanged.
>
> This paper studies that question in Mexico, where a 2019 constitutional reform required parties to nominate equal numbers of women and men for mayoral races. Using candidate-level campaign finance disclosures from before and after the reform, I ask whether legal parity in nominations was followed by financial parity in party support. The answer is: only partially. Women’s party transfers increased relative to men’s, but sizable funding gaps remained, with no clear evidence of a nationwide closing of the gap and substantial heterogeneity across states.

That is the story. Start with compliance versus meaningful opportunity. Then Mexico is the setting.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Before this paper, the world looks like this: many democracies have introduced parity or quota rules to increase women’s formal political representation. Success is usually measured by nominations or seats. But parties control another crucial margin—money.

### Tension

The puzzle is whether parity reforms change real electoral opportunity or merely ballot composition. A party can satisfy the letter of the law by nominating women while still starving them of resources or assigning them to weaker races. Thus observed parity in candidacies may mask persistent internal gatekeeping.

### Resolution

In Mexico after the 2019 parity reform, women’s nominations moved toward parity and their party funding improved somewhat, but not enough to establish a clear nationwide shift to financial parity. Resource gaps remained large, and state patterns were highly uneven. Meanwhile, women’s relative private donor support may even have worsened, consistent with placement in weaker candidacies.

### Implications

The implication is important: legal parity on the ballot does not guarantee parity in meaningful opportunity. Regulators and researchers should look beyond headline representation statistics to the internal allocation of money, support, and viable candidacies.

### Does this paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the bones of one, but the paper is not fully committed to it. It still reads somewhat like:
- background on reform,
- data description,
- DDD setup,
- null result,
- some heterogeneity.

The deeper story is stronger than the current exposition. The paper should more clearly be telling:

> Parties lost control over the nomination gate, so the question is whether they retained control through the funding gate.

That is a real narrative. Right now the “funding gate” phrase appears, but the paper does not fully organize itself around it.

Also, the paper seems slightly unsure whether its centerpiece is:
- the null DDD,
- the raw persistence of the funding gap,
- the heterogeneity across states,
- or the negative sympathizer result.

That ambiguity weakens the arc. The author needs to choose one headline and subordinate the others.

My advice: the story should be **“visible parity, incomplete opportunity”**, with the null DDD serving as evidence that financial adaptation lagged, and the sympathizer result as suggestive evidence of strategic placement.

I would lead with:

That is vivid and memorable.

An equally strong variant:

The paper already has that sentence. It should lean even harder on it.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

Economists would lean in for the first minute because the fact is striking and the broader issue is important. But they may reach for their phones if the paper presents itself mainly as a technically imperfect DDD with a null coefficient.

The paper’s political economy relevance is stronger than its econometric headline. If the headline becomes “our triple difference is null,” interest will fade. If the headline is “parties complied visibly but not fully where it mattered,” interest rises.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

The immediate follow-up question will be:
> “Were women nominated in worse races?”

That is the key. The negative sympathizer result already points in that direction. If the paper cannot answer it directly, it should at least frame it as the central interpretive question.

A second follow-up:
> “Does the funding gap matter for winning?”

Again, even limited evidence here would enlarge the paper.

### If the findings are null or modest: is the null result itself interesting?

Potentially yes, but only if framed correctly. A bare null is not enough. What makes it interesting is not “we found no statistically significant DDD.” It is:

- a major constitutional reform changed the visible margin,
- but financial allocation remained substantially unequal,
- suggesting organizational gatekeeping persists on less regulated margins.

That is an interesting null in a world where one might have expected formal parity to transform actual support.

But the paper must avoid sounding like a failed attempt to find an effect. It should sound like a paper about **limits of formal reform**.
