## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple, important question: when Spain sharply reduced the use of temporary contracts in 2022, did jobs actually become more secure, or did employers mostly reclassify precarious work into a new “permanent” category? The core claim is that the reform dramatically changed measured contract composition, but likely did much less to change the underlying nature of employment.

Why should a busy economist care? Because this is a clean case of a broader issue: whether labor market regulation changes real economic relationships or just the labels through which those relationships are recorded. That is a first-order question for labor economics, policy evaluation, and the interpretation of official statistics.

### Does the paper articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Mostly yes, but not optimally. The paper opens vividly and the basic issue is there, but it gets pulled too quickly into institutional detail and superlatives (“most ambitious attack… in OECD history”) before fully crystallizing the general question. The first two paragraphs should more forcefully foreground the universal economic stakes: when policy targets a measured margin, does the world change or only the measurement?

### The pitch the paper should have

“Spain’s 2022 labor reform appeared to deliver a stunning policy success: the temporary employment rate fell by roughly a third in a single year. This paper asks whether that drop reflected a real reduction in labor market precariousness or a relabeling of the same jobs into a new ‘permanent-discontinuous’ category. Using regional variation in pre-reform exposure, we show that the reform sharply reduced measured temporariness but left aggregate employment unchanged, suggesting that bans on contract types can improve statistics without much altering the underlying employment relationship.”

That is the pitch. It is sharper, more general, and less wrapped up in the specifics before the reader understands why the specifics matter.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### Contribution in one sentence

The paper’s contribution is to argue that Spain’s 2022 temporary-contract reform substantially changed the recorded composition of contracts without clear evidence of a corresponding change in the underlying structure of employment, consistent with large-scale relabeling rather than deep reform.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest papers?

Somewhat, but not enough. The paper differentiates itself from descriptive post-reform accounts by claiming a design-based regional comparison. That is useful, but from an AER positioning standpoint, “first design-based evaluation” is not the strongest differentiator unless the underlying substantive insight is clearly larger than the method. Right now the paper’s differentiation leans too much on technique and “causal framework,” and not enough on what we learn about labor market institutions.

The closest contrast seems to be:
- descriptive work showing temporary rates fell but job duration did not move much,
- the older Spain dualism literature on temporary contracts and firing-cost gaps,
- cross-country work on EPL reforms.

The paper needs to say more explicitly: prior work established that dual labor markets matter, and some descriptive work suggested Spain’s reform may be cosmetic; this paper’s novelty is to show that the reform’s headline success appears concentrated on the classification margin, which changes how we should interpret one of the most celebrated labor reforms in Europe.

### World question or literature-gap question?

It is mostly framed as a world question, which is good: did the reform change real job quality or just labels? That is much stronger than “there is no causal study yet.” But the introduction keeps drifting back toward literature-gap language (“first design-based evaluation,” “contributes methodologically,” etc.). That weakens the piece.

The paper should relentlessly frame itself around the world question:
**What do we actually learn from a collapse in temporary employment statistics when the legal taxonomy changes at the same time?**

### Could a smart economist explain what is new?

A smart economist could probably say: “It’s a paper on Spain’s contract reform arguing the fall in temporary work was largely relabeling into fijo discontinuo.” That is good. Better than “another DiD paper about X.”

But the risk is that they would also add: “Though I’m not sure the paper can really distinguish relabeling from modest improvements in job quality within the permanent bucket.” That is exactly where the contribution feels smaller than the prose suggests.

### What would make the contribution bigger?

Three concrete ways:

1. **Use an outcome closer to precariousness than total employment.**  
   Total employment is too coarse to bear all the interpretive weight. A much bigger paper would show what happened to tenure, recall patterns, unemployment spells, hours volatility, wage growth, or actual months worked conditional on “permanent” status.

2. **Directly quantify the substitute category.**  
   If the paper could more directly document growth in fijo discontinuo by region, or at least tie the decline in temporary contracts to growth in that specific contract form, the relabeling claim would become much more powerful and less inferential.

3. **Frame the contribution around the economics of policy-target gaming.**  
   The bigger contribution is not “Spain reform paper.” It is: when governments target a statistic rather than the underlying incentive, institutions adapt in predictable ways. That connects labor, regulation, and public economics.

That is an AER-scale conversation. “Here is another paper on Spanish dual labor markets” is not.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Spain has long had one of the most dualized labor markets in Europe, with extensive use of temporary contracts and repeated failed reform efforts. In 2022, the government enacted a major reform that appeared to sharply reduce temporary employment.

### Tension

A dramatic fall in temporary contracts could mean one of two very different things: workers became genuinely more secure, or employers shifted them into a legally “permanent” but still precarious category. The same observed statistic is consistent with very different underlying realities.

### Resolution

The paper finds that areas with more initial exposure saw larger declines in measured temporary contracts, but no corresponding change in total employment, with especially large shifts in seasonal sectors. The paper interprets this pattern as consistent with relabeling.

### Implications

The policy lesson is that banning a contract type may improve official indicators without substantially changing labor market precarity, especially if the underlying incentive structure remains intact.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

Yes, mostly. This is one of the paper’s strengths. There is a real setup-tension-resolution structure.

But the narrative is weakened by two things:

1. **It keeps interrupting itself with methods talk.**  
   The methodological paragraphs in the introduction are too long relative to the simplicity of the story.

2. **It overstates certainty in the resolution.**  
   “The reform changed labels, not jobs” is too definitive relative to the outcomes shown. The better narrative is “the evidence strongly suggests the reform changed labels much more than it changed employment quantities, and is consistent with substantial relabeling.”

The paper is not a collection of random results. It has a story. It just needs to tell that story with more discipline and a bit more humility.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?

“Spain’s temporary employment rate collapsed after the 2022 reform, but this paper argues that much of the apparent success was likely a relabeling of precarious jobs into a new ‘permanent’ category.”

That is a strong dinner-party opener.

### Would people lean in?

Yes, initially. The fact pattern is interesting and policy-relevant. Labor economists, macro-labor people, and empirical micro people would all understand the stakes.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Immediately:  
**“But did workers actually become better off anyway?”**

That is the key vulnerability. If the answer is “we can’t really tell from these data,” then the paper’s ceiling is lower. The current design can show that employment levels did not move; it cannot fully adjudicate whether workers gained meaningful protections, tenure, earnings stability, or recall rights within the permanent bucket.

That follow-up question is not fatal. But it means the paper cannot rest entirely on the null total-employment result as the decisive proof of “illusion.”

### If findings are modest or partly null, is the null interesting?

Yes, potentially. The null on total employment is interesting because the policy debate likely included both job-creation and job-destruction stories. Showing that the major response was on classification rather than aggregate employment is useful.

But the paper needs to make the null more intellectually valuable by saying:
- the key margin of policy response may be **categorical substitution**, not employment destruction or creation;
- therefore standard evaluations focused on employment levels can miss where the action is.

That is how to turn a modest quantitative result into a bigger conceptual point.

Also, the paper’s own caveat—that workers might benefit within the “permanent” bucket even if employment levels do not change—is extremely important and currently arrives too late. That belongs earlier, because it sharpens the question rather than weakens it.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

At present, the gap is mainly a mix of **framing problem**, **scope problem**, and a bit of **ambition problem**.

### Framing problem

The paper has a potentially big idea but packages it as a competent policy evaluation. The bigger idea is about **statistical reform versus real reform**—when legal relabeling can masquerade as economic transformation. That is much more interesting than “we estimate a continuous-treatment DiD of Spain’s labor reform.”

### Scope problem

The current evidence is too narrow for the size of the claim. Contract shares plus total employment are enough for a strong, provocative short paper or field-journal piece. For AER, the paper likely needs outcomes that get closer to what “precarity” actually is.

### Novelty problem

The underlying intuition—firms substitute into a new margin when the old one is banned—is not novel. The novelty has to come from showing it in a way that changes how economists interpret a very prominent reform. Right now the paper is close to that, but not fully there.

### Ambition problem

The paper is careful and competent, but somewhat safe in the evidentiary margin it chooses. It wants to make a strong claim about job quality while staying with outcomes that stop short of job quality.

### Single most impactful piece of advice

**Reframe the paper around the broader economic question—whether policy can improve a headline labor-market statistic without materially improving worker security—and bring in at least one outcome that more directly measures precariousness rather than relying so heavily on total employment as the discriminator.**

If they only change one thing, it should be that. Not more robustness. Not more inference. Not more literature review. They need one sharper substantive bridge from “classification changed” to “workers’ employment relationships did not meaningfully improve.”
