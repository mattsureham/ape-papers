The paper is faithful to the original idea manifest. It exploits the staggered adoption of Right-to-Counsel (RTC) across cities and states, uses HUD Point-in-Time counts aggregated by Continuum of Care (CoC) as the primary outcome, and investigates whether providing legal representation to tenants reduces community-level homelessness. The identification strategy—Callaway-Sant’Anna staggered DiD with never-treated CoCs and various robustness checks—is aligned with the manifest, and the paper explicitly engages with the potential endogeneity of adoption timing and COVID-related confounds highlighted there. **Summary**

The paper examines whether municipal and state RTC policies reduce homelessness at the CoC level by leveraging staggered adoption from 2017 to 2023 and estimating cohort-time average treatment effects via Callaway-Sant’Anna difference-in-differences. While the pooled ATT suggests a statistically significant 13% decline in total homelessness, event-study diagnostics, pre-COVID subsamples, size-matched controls, and placebo tests collectively point to selection-driven pre-trends rather than a causal effect. The cleanest cohorts yield tightly estimated null effects, implying that RTC’s salvation of individual tenants does not translate into measurable reductions in the aggregate homeless population.

**Essential Points**

1. **Distinguishing Mean Reversion from Treatment Effects:** The central identification challenge is that many RTC adopters initiated the program amid rising homelessness. The event-study shows significant positive pre-trends, and the treatment-effect estimates hinge on later cohorts where trends were already upward. The current diagnostics are informative, but the paper should more formally model and rule out mean reversion. For instance, time-varying controls (e.g., trending economic indicators, housing market stressors) or flexible cohort-specific trends could reduce confounding. Without further adjustments, the reader cannot confidently interpret the late-adopter ATT as even a plausible causal estimate, let alone the null in the cleaned subsamples.

2. **Interpretation of Subsample Nulls:** The paper emphasizes the pre-COVID cohort and size-matched nulls as the “cleanest” evidence. However, these subsamples are small (only five city cohorts), and the policy relevance of omitting the majority of adopted jurisdictions is unclear. Are the subsample coefficients estimated with sufficient power to speak to the costs discussed in the paper’s motivation? The authors should quantify how much of the treated population these subsamples represent and discuss whether the nulls could be due to insufficient variation or statistical power rather than the absence of effects.

3. **Mechanism Linkage Missing:** The paper attributes the lack of aggregate effects to a “thin” eviction-to-homelessness pipeline but does not provide direct evidence on intermediate outcomes (e.g., eviction filings, shelter entries, informal displacement). Without showing that RTC actually reduces evictions within these CoCs during the same period, it remains possible that the programs—perhaps underfunded or poorly implemented—never delivered the presumed intermediate treatment, which would explain the null without challenging the more general policy logic. Presenting some evidence (e.g., using Eviction Lab filings or court data) that RTC adoption did reduce evictions in the same jurisdictions—ideally using the same empirical strategy—would strengthen the interpretation.

**Suggestions**

1. **Augment the Identification Strategy with Flexible Trends and Covariates:** Building on the first essential point, consider augmenting the DiD with flexible cohort-specific trends or synthetic controls that explicitly account for the rising trends that drive adoption. For example, estimate specifications that allow each cohort to have its own pre-treatment linear trend (or higher-order polynomial) and verify that results are similar. Alternatively, use inverse-probability weighting or synthetic control methods where treated CoCs are matched to controls on pre-trend trajectories. Including time-varying covariates such as unemployment rates, rental price growth, or shelter availability could also help soak up differential trends, making the assumption of parallel paths more credible.

2. **Clarify Subsample Interpretation and Statistical Power:** Provide more detail on the composition of the pre-COVID cohort sample (number of CoCs, share of overall treated person-years, etc.) and assess whether the null result might suffer from power issues. Reporting the implied minimum detectable effect (MDE) in that subsample would help readers judge whether RTC could still have economically meaningful effects that remain undetected. If the subsample is small but still represents a substantial fraction of treated people (e.g., NYC alone), emphasize that; if it is tiny, be explicit about the limitations.

3. **Explore Heterogeneous Effects and Alternative Outcomes:** Heterogeneity analysis could reveal whether the null masks positive effects in particular types of CoCs (e.g., those with higher shelter capacity, stronger legal aid infrastructure, or more severe housing unaffordability). Even if the aggregate effect is null, detecting heterogeneity would inform theory about where RTC might still reduce homelessness. Additionally, consider alternative outcomes that are more directly linked to eviction prevention—such as shelter entries, overcrowding or doubled-up households (if data allow), or aggregate eviction filings from Eviction Lab—within the same DiD framework. Finding treatment effects on these intermediates but not on the overall stock would substantiate the “thin pipeline” argument.

4. **Strengthen Placebo and Falsification Exercises:** The placebo shifting treatment dates back three years is a good start, but consider additional falsification tests. For instance, implement a “pre-treatment event” analysis where treatment is assigned to future non-adopters, or examine whether RTC predicts future (post-adoption) outcomes that should remain unaffected, such as non-housing-related variables. Another useful exercise would be to test whether the coefficient on post-treatment leads (e.g., t−2, t−1) remains positive and significant even after including flexible trends; persistent pre-trends after such controls would further corroborate selection concerns.

5. **Engage Directly with the Policy Debate:** The discussion rightly emphasizes that cost-benefit analyses often hinge on homelessness prevention. To make this argument more concrete, quantify how the bounds implied by the clean subsamples translate into taxpayer savings (or lack thereof) by comparing the null effect’s confidence interval to the estimated number of shelter-days avoided per treated eviction. This would provide policymakers with a clearer sense of the gap between the policy’s stated justification and the empirical evidence.

6. **Document Implementation Variation:** RTC programs differ in scope, coverage, and resourcing. Providing a short table summarizing key features (e.g., eligibility thresholds, funding levels, rollout phases) could help readers assess whether variations in implementation correlate with the observed effects—or lack thereof. If such data are unavailable, discuss how implementation heterogeneity might bias the estimates (e.g., weaker programs diluting the true effect).

7. **Clarify Treatment Timing Determination:** The mapping from city/state ordinances to CoCs requires judgment, especially when coverage phases in gradually. The paper should clarify how the treatment year is defined (e.g., ordinance passage vs. full implementation) and whether partial rollouts (e.g., phased district-by-district expansion) are treated as immediate. If possible, conduct sensitivity analyses using alternative treatment dates (e.g., one year later) to assess the robustness of the null finding to such choices.

8. **Discuss External Validity in Light of Null Result:** Because most RTC evidence is from NYC-like cities, the null aggregate effect may reflect the specific nature of the U.S. homelessness stock rather than RTC’s intrinsic ineffectiveness. Explicitly discussing how generalizable these findings are to smaller CoCs or to other contexts (e.g., countries with different shelter systems) would help readers situate the results in broader policy debates.

In sum, the paper raises an important question and makes a valuable methodological point about selection in staggered DiDs. Addressing the identification concerns more directly, providing richer evidence on intermediate outcomes, and clarifying the implications of the null findings will substantially strengthen the manuscript.
