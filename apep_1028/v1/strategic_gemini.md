### 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
This paper evaluates whether providing free legal counsel to tenants in eviction court (Right-to-Counsel) reduces aggregate homelessness. While a naive staggered DiD estimate suggests a 13% reduction, the author demonstrates this is a spurious result driven by endogenous policy adoption—cities pass these laws precisely when homelessness is already surging—and shows that the true population-level effect is a precisely estimated zero.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this pitch exceptionally well. The first two paragraphs establish the high fiscal stakes ($166M in NYC alone), the causal logic used by policymakers, and the immediate "spoiler" that the result is a selection artifact.
*   **The Pitch the Paper Should Have:** The current intro is excellent, but to truly grab an AER reader, it could emphasize the "Aggregation Paradox": Why do micro-level studies show individual success while macro-level data shows zero impact? The second paragraph should frame this not just as a policy evaluation, but as a study on the limits of legal interventions in correcting equilibrium social outcomes.

### 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
The paper provides the first population-level causal evaluation of Right-to-Counsel (RTC) laws, debunking the "common sense" assumption that preventing individual evictions meaningfully moves the needle on community-wide homelessness.

*   **Differentiation:** It is clearly differentiated. Unlike Cassidy & Currie (2022) or Collinson & Reed (2023), which look at individual outcomes or rent effects, this paper looks at the *ultimate* welfare target: the homeless count.
*   **Question vs. Literature:** It answers a question about the **WORLD** (does this expensive policy work?), but it uses a **LITERATURE** gap (staggered DiD selection bias) as its primary tool.
*   **The "Smart Economist" Test:** A reader would explain this as: "It’s a paper showing that lawyers in housing court don't actually reduce homelessness because the 'eviction-to-street' pipeline is too thin and the policy adoption is endogenous."
*   **Making it Bigger:** To move from "very good" to "AER-defining," the author needs to dig deeper into the **behavioral substitution** mechanism. If landlords know they will face a lawyer, do they stop filing but increase informal harassment? Evidence of *informal* displacement would make this a massive contribution to political economy/law and economics.

### 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
The paper sits at the intersection of Urban Economics (Homelessness) and Law & Economics (Access to Justice).

*   **Closest Neighbors:** Collinson & Reed (2023, QJE) on evictions; Desmond (2012, 2016) on the sociology of housing; Callaway & Sant’Anna (2021) on DiD methodology.
*   **Strategy:** It should **synthesize** the micro-empirical work (which shows RTC works for individuals) with the macro-theoretical work on homelessness stocks/flows (O'Brien, 2020). It currently builds on these, but could be more aggressive in "attacking" the cost-benefit extrapolations used by advocates.
*   **Unexpected Connection:** The paper could benefit from connecting to the literature on **"Targeting"** in social programs. Is the problem that RTC is a "blunt instrument" helping people who would never have become homeless anyway?

### 4. NARRATIVE ARC
*   **Setup:** Cities are spending hundreds of millions on RTC based on the logic that "Eviction $\rightarrow$ Homelessness."
*   **Tension:** Micro-studies show RTC works in court, yet homelessness continues to rise.
*   **Resolution:** The "success" in the data is a statistical ghost caused by jurisdictions adopting the policy in response to crises. When you isolate clean variation, the effect vanishes.
*   **Implications:** RTC might be "just," but it isn't an anti-homelessness tool. Cost-benefit models are fundamentally broken.

**Evaluation:** The narrative is very strong. It follows a "Detective Story" structure (finding a result, then debunking it) which is highly effective for the AER.

### 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
*   **The Lead Fact:** "New York spends $166 million on eviction lawyers, but it hasn't reduced the number of people on the street by even 1%."
*   **Response:** People will lean in. It challenges a "feel-good" policy that has bipartisan or at least broad urban support.
*   **Follow-up:** "So where does the money go? Is it just a transfer to lawyers?" or "Does it make landlords more likely to just pay 'cash for keys'?"

### 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
*   **Front-loading:** The paper does this well. The "Selection vs. Causation" decomposition in Table 5 is the heart of the paper and should be emphasized even more.
*   **The "Back Half":** The "Discussion" section (6) is actually the most "AER-like" part. The calibration of the "thin pipeline" (page 10) is brilliant. This should perhaps be its own formal section with a small "toy model" to show how the micro-to-macro math fails.
*   **Appendix:** Move Table 6 (Standardized Effect Sizes) to the main body; it helps communicate the "precisely estimated null."

### 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The gap is **Ambition.** Right now, it is a very high-quality "Technical Note" or a "Policy Evaluation." To be an AER staple, it needs to move from "This policy doesn't work" to **"This is a fundamental lesson in how social systems respond to legal mandates."**

**Single Biggest Piece of Advice:** Formalize the "Aggregation Gap" in Section 6. Don't just list three reasons; provide some empirical evidence (even if suggestive) for *which* one is dominant. If you can show that "informal evictions" rose where "formal evictions" fell, you have a Top 5 paper.
