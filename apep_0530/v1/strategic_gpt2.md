## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether being just inside versus just outside France’s priority-neighborhood boundaries is capitalized into housing prices. Using a large national transaction dataset and the 2015 redraw of those boundaries, it shows that homes inside designated neighborhoods sell for meaningfully less than nearby homes outside, suggesting that place-based policy boundaries coincide with economically salient segmentation in urban housing markets.

Why should a busy economist care? Because place-based policy is usually evaluated through jobs, income, or redevelopment; this paper says the policy map itself may be reflected in asset prices, with implications for stigma, sorting, and the equilibrium incidence of urban policy.

### Does the paper articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Not well enough. The opening is vivid, but the paper quickly gets bogged down in design details and caveats before the reader has a crisp sense of the economic question. The introduction currently reads like a cautious empirical note: “here is a boundary design, here are two boundary types, here is why it’s not causal.” That is honest, but strategically weak.

The first two paragraphs should instead say something like:

> Place-based policies target distressed neighborhoods with extra public resources, but they may also do something else: create administrative labels that markets price. When governments draw a line around a neighborhood and designate it “priority,” does that boundary become an economically meaningful division in housing markets?
>
> This paper studies that question using France’s 2015 nationwide redesign of priority neighborhoods. Combining 2.1 million property transactions with the new policy map, I show that homes just inside these boundaries sell for 6–8 percent less than nearby homes just outside. The central message is not merely that poor neighborhoods are cheaper; it is that administrative targeting boundaries line up with sharp local price segmentation, speaking to how place-based policy, neighborhood stigma, and spatial inequality interact.

That pitch leads with the world question, the core fact, and the reason it matters.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### Contribution in one sentence

The paper documents that France’s priority-neighborhood boundaries coincide with sizable local housing price discontinuities, suggesting that place-based policy maps are capitalized into property markets.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?

Only partially. The paper cites several literatures, but the differentiation is still muddled. Right now the contribution risks sounding like: “another geographic boundary paper showing a discontinuity in prices.” That is not enough for AER-level positioning.

The closest neighbors are probably:
- Black (1999) on school attendance boundaries and housing prices
- Bayer, Ferreira, and McMillan (2007) on boundary/hedonic valuation of neighborhoods and schools
- Hanson and Rohlin / Lynch and Zax style enterprise-zone capitalization papers
- Ahlfeldt et al. on urban renewal/redevelopment capitalization
- Possibly flood-zone or historic-district capitalization papers, where labels and regulations get priced

Relative to these, the paper’s novelty is:
1. a **national-scale** administrative boundary setting,
2. in **place-based policy** rather than schools/zoning/disaster risk,
3. with a potentially interesting angle on **label/stigma versus benefits**.

But the paper does not forcefully tell the reader which of these is the main innovation. “National scale” alone is not a top-journal contribution. “Another capitalization estimate” is also not enough. The most promising differentiator is the idea that targeted anti-poverty policy may itself create a priced neighborhood label.

### World question or literature gap?

It oscillates, but too often it is framed as a literature gap. The stronger version is the world question:

- Do place-based anti-poverty boundaries become priced markers of neighborhood status?
- Can urban policy intended to help neighborhoods also reinforce spatial stratification through housing markets?

That is much stronger than “the literature has not studied capitalization of QPV boundaries.”

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the intro?

Not confidently. They might say: “It’s a boundary-discontinuity paper on French priority zones and house prices.” That is exactly the failure mode. They would be less likely to say: “It shows that anti-poverty policy boundaries themselves segment urban asset prices, which matters for the equilibrium effects of place-based policy.”

### What would make the contribution bigger?

Three concrete possibilities:

1. **Reframe around incidence/equilibrium, not boundary design.**  
   The big question is whether targeted neighborhood aid also gets offset in land values. If the paper can connect the price gap to the incidence of policy benefits, it becomes much more important.

2. **Lean harder into heterogeneity that speaks to stigma versus amenity channels.**  
   Right now “gained vs retained” is interesting but not decisive, especially given the coarse classification. More compelling would be heterogeneity by:
   - market thickness/gentrifying versus declining metros,
   - owner-occupied versus investor-heavy segments,
   - areas with visible redevelopment spending versus not,
   - schools/business subsidies/housing renovation intensity.
   That would make the paper about how and when policy maps matter, not just whether they do.

3. **Use outcomes beyond prices if available.**  
   Listings, time-on-market, transaction volume, buyer composition, rental yields, or renovation permits would turn a static capitalization fact into a richer account of market equilibrium and sorting. At present the paper is one sharp reduced-form fact without enough surrounding evidence.

If the author can only stay with price, then the framing must become much more ambitious.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Governments use place-based policies to channel resources into distressed neighborhoods. These programs are defined by administrative boundaries, and those boundaries may matter not only for eligibility but for how neighborhoods are perceived and valued.

### Tension

The same designation that brings subsidies and public investment may also label an area as disadvantaged. So targeted policy could raise amenities while depressing values through stigma or sorting. We do not know whether policy boundaries themselves become priced in local housing markets.

### Resolution

Using France’s 2015 redrawing of priority neighborhoods, the paper finds that properties inside the boundaries sell for less than nearby properties outside, with comparable price gaps across newly designated and long-standing priority areas in the main specification.

### Implications

Place-based policy may shape cities not just through direct transfers and investments, but through capitalization into land values and neighborhood segmentation. That matters for policy incidence, for residential sorting, and for whether these policies mitigate or reinforce spatial inequality.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

Only weakly. It has ingredients for a good story, but the current draft is too defensive and too dispersed. The central narrative gets interrupted by methodological throat-clearing and by a second, underpowered story about “gained” versus “retained” zones.

At present it feels somewhat like a collection of results looking for a story:
- main price gap,
- gained vs retained comparison,
- nonparametric estimates that differ from parametric ones,
- some property-type heterogeneity,
- many caveats.

### What story should it be telling?

The story should be:

1. **Place-based policy creates lines on maps.**
2. **Those lines may themselves alter urban market valuations.**
3. **In France, they line up with sharp price segmentation.**
4. **This means the targeting geography of social policy is part of the policy’s equilibrium effect.**

Everything else should serve that narrative. The gained/retained comparison is secondary—useful as suggestive evidence on persistence/salience, but not the spine of the paper.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?

“Homes just inside France’s anti-poverty policy zones sell for around 6–8 percent less than homes just outside the boundary.”

That is a good fact. It is intuitive, concrete, and easy to repeat.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

Some would lean in—especially urban, public, and applied micro economists—but the next question comes fast: **Is that policy, or just selection?** The paper itself foregrounds that this is not a clean causal effect of designation, which is admirable but also strategically fatal unless the framing shifts. If the finding is “there is a discontinuity at an administratively meaningful line,” that is interesting. If the claim is “policy caused this,” the paper backs away from it. So the dinner-party interest is real but fragile.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

“Do we learn that the designation label itself matters, or only that poor neighborhoods are on one side of the line?”

That is the key question. The paper knows this and says so repeatedly. But because it cannot answer it cleanly, the paper must make the descriptive fact do more conceptual work: not “the causal effect of the label,” but “the economically relevant segmentation induced or codified by targeted policy geography.”

### If the findings are modest: is the modest result itself interesting?

Yes, potentially. A stable 6–8 percent local price gap is not trivial. But the paper must insist on why the descriptive fact matters:
- it quantifies the market salience of policy boundaries,
- it speaks to incidence,
- it suggests that policy targeting can be capitalized into land values,
- it may help explain why place-based policy sometimes disappoints in welfare terms.

Without that argument, it risks feeling like a competent but modest housing-price paper.

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

### What would make it read better?

1. **Shorten the institutional background.**  
   The paper spends too long on the history of French urban policy. For AER readers, they need the essentials quickly: what QPV is, how 2015 changed the map, why the boundaries are economically salient.

2. **Move much of the identification caveat discussion out of the introduction.**  
   The intro currently neutralizes the paper before it has sold it. The honesty is good; the sequencing is bad. The paper should first establish the question and main fact, then discuss interpretation.

3. **Collapse the literature review.**  
   The standalone literature section is conventional but heavy. Much of it could be integrated into the introduction. AER-style papers often position themselves briskly rather than through a separate survey section.

4. **Front-load the best result.**  
   Readers should learn on page 1 that there is a 6–8 percent local price discontinuity at anti-poverty policy boundaries. Right now they get there, but through too much setup and too many caveats.

5. **Demote some robustness material.**  
   The donut results are unstable and somewhat confusing in the current narrative. If they stay, they need interpretation; otherwise much of that can move to the appendix. Same for some of the design-detail exposition.

6. **Clarify the role of the nonparametric RDD results.**  
   At present they almost create a second paper, because they imply a much larger retained-boundary effect than the parametric results. Either integrate this into a coherent story about spatial scale and salience, or downweight it. Right now it muddies the message.

7. **The conclusion should do more than summarize.**  
   It should state what economists should now believe: place-based policy boundaries are not administratively neutral; they are part of the economic environment households price.

### Are there results buried in robustness that should be in the main text?

Potentially the bandwidth stability figure, because it helps establish that the main pattern is not a knife-edge artifact. That is more central than some of the lengthy specification prose.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

Frankly, the gap is substantial.

### What is the main gap?

Mostly **an ambition/framing problem**, with some **scope/novelty** issues.

- **Not mainly a technical problem.** The paper is transparent about limitations.
- **Mainly a positioning problem.** The current paper presents itself as a careful boundary estimate with many caveats.
- **Also a scope problem.** One descriptive price result, however well executed, is usually not enough for AER unless it changes a major conversation.
- **Partly a novelty problem.** Boundary discontinuities in housing prices are an established template. The paper needs a bigger economic question than “is there a discontinuity?”

### What would excite the top 10 people in this field?

A paper that uses this setting to say something bigger about:
- the incidence of place-based policy,
- the interaction of subsidies and stigma,
- capitalization and sorting in urban equilibrium,
- whether policy targeting helps recipients but lowers land values in a way that redistributes gains.

To get there, the paper needs to stop presenting itself as “a boundary discontinuity application to French QPVs” and instead present itself as “evidence that the geography of anti-poverty policy is itself priced.”

### Single most impactful advice

**Rebuild the paper around one big question: how place-based policy targeting is capitalized into land markets, and what that implies for the equilibrium incidence of urban anti-poverty policy.**

That means:
- make the core contribution conceptual, not methodological;
- make gained/retained a secondary heterogeneity exercise, not the centerpiece;
- reduce defensive caveating in the intro;
- and, if possible, add one or two pieces of evidence that speak to incidence/mechanism rather than just documenting the discontinuity.
