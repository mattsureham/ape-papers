## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
This paper asks how much an administrative "priority" (disadvantaged) label depresses property values, using a 2015 French reform that redrew over 1,200 zone boundaries based on a mechanical income formula. By comparing newly designated zones to those with decades of "priority" status, the paper attempts to disentangle the immediate capitalization of a policy label from the long-term stigma of place-based disadvantage. 

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this reasonably well by the second paragraph, but it lacks a "hook" regarding why the 2015 reform's *formulaic* nature is a game-changer for the literature.

**The Pitch the paper should have:**
"Does labeling a neighborhood as 'distressed' create a self-fulfilling prophecy of decline? Using a massive reform in France that redrew 1,200 administrative boundaries based on a transparent, non-political income formula, I provide the first national-scale evidence of how policy labels segment housing markets. By comparing 'newly shamed' neighborhoods to those with 20-year legacies of designation, I show that while market penalties appear quickly, they double in intensity as the boundary becomes socially and informationally entrenched over decades."

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**One-sentence contribution:** The paper documents a 6–8% housing price penalty at priority zone boundaries and shows that this penalty doubles at the sharpest spatial scale for long-standing zones compared to newly designated ones.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It differentiates itself from *Busso et al. (2013)* and *Hanson/Rohlin (2009)* by focusing on the **stigma/signal** of the boundary rather than the labor market outcomes.
*   **World vs. Literature:** It currently leans toward "filling a gap in the literature." To be AER-caliber, it needs to frame this as a fundamental question about **Information and Urban Sorting**: Do administrative lines create "mental maps" that distort markets?
*   **What would make it bigger?** The "Retained vs. Gained" comparison is the most interesting part, but it’s currently a secondary finding. Making this the **central pillar**—treating it as a "speed of stigma" study—would elevate it.

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
*   **Closest Neighbors:** *Black (1999)* on boundaries; *Bayer et al. (2007)* on sorting; *Busso et al. (2013)* on place-based policy; *Duguet et al. (2010)* on French banlieue stigma.
*   **Positioning:** The paper should **attack** the assumption in earlier place-based policy literature that boundaries are just "neutral lines" for treatment. It should argue that the line itself is a treatment.
*   **Missing Literature:** It needs to speak to the **Socio-Economic Geography** and **Real Estate Marketing** literature. How do real estate platforms (e.g., SeLoger in France) display these zones? If the zone status is a filter on a website, that’s the mechanism.

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC
*   **Setup:** Governments draw lines to help the poor.
*   **Tension:** These lines might accidentally "brand" the poor, offsetting the benefits of the funding through market stigma.
*   **Resolution:** Even when lines are drawn by a "fair" formula, the market reacts with a 6-8% penalty.
*   **Implications:** "Hard" boundaries in social policy may be counter-productive; "soft" or "fuzzy" targeting might be welfare-superior.

**Evaluation:** The arc is currently a bit flat. It feels like an evaluation of a specific French policy. It needs to be a story about the **Unintended Consequences of Transparency.**

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
*   **The Fact:** "In France, if the government draws a line through your street saying the other side is 'priority,' your house just lost 8% of its value—and if that line stays there for 20 years, you lose 24%."
*   **Reaction:** Lean in. It suggests that "transparency" in policy (the mechanical formula) might actually be more damaging than "vague" political targeting because it's easier for the market to price.

---

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
*   **Front-loading:** The distinction between "Gained" and "Retained" needs to be moved from a "heterogeneity" check in Section 8 to the primary results in Section 6.
*   **Appendix:** The 15 pages of institutional detail on the ZUS/QPV system can be compressed. Economists want the logic of the formula, not the names of the laws.
*   **Mechanisms:** Section 8 needs a "Real Estate Salience" test. Does this show up on property listings? That is the smoking gun for the "labeling" story.

---

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
**The Gap:** Currently, it’s a very high-quality "Applied Micro" paper that feels like it belongs in *AEJ: Policy* or *JUE*. To get into the *AER*, it needs to move from "Policy Evaluation" to "Economic Theory of Urban Sorting."

**Single Most Impactful Advice:** Shift the focus from the *policy effects* to the *temporal evolution of stigma*. The most striking result is Table 3, where the 17-meter RDD shows a **-24% jump** for old boundaries vs **-11%** for new ones. **That is the paper.** The story is about how "social knowledge" of a boundary hardens over time, creating deep spatial scars.
