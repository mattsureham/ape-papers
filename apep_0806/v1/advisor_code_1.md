**Idea Fidelity**

The paper stays faithful to the original manifest. It implements a staggered difference-in-differences design using Ireland’s Rent Pressure Zone rollout and Callaway–Sant’Anna estimation with publicly available RTB/CSO rent data. The key research questions—whether RPZs slow rent growth and whether they affect rent levels—are addressed, cohort heterogeneity is explored, and the methodological warning about TWFE bias is executed as envisioned. The only minor departure is that the paper operates at the county level rather than the Local Electoral Area (LEA) level (a limitation acknowledged in the discussion), but this reflects the practical data constraint noted in the manifest.

**Summary**

The paper analyses Ireland’s 2016–2021 staggered implementation of Rent Pressure Zones using Callaway and Sant’Anna (2021) to estimate cohort-specific treatment effects on quarterly county-level rents. It finds RPZ designation reduced year-on-year rent growth by about 2.4 percentage points—roughly one-third of the pre-treatment mean—while having no detectable effect on rent levels. Cohort heterogeneity shows stronger effects in urban early adopters, and a traditional TWFE regression produces a misleadingly large positive level effect, underscoring the importance of heterogeneity-robust estimators.

**Essential Points**

1. **County-level Aggregation Weakens the Identification and Interpretation of Effects:** RPZ designation occurred at the LEA level, whereas the analysis is restricted to county averages. Given that counties like Dublin contain LEAs with very different designation dates and housing market conditions, the treatment indicator is noisy, and the comparison between treated and not-yet-treated counties may mix treated and untreated LEAs within the same county. This could blur the timing variation that identification relies upon and attenuate the estimated effects. At minimum, the paper should explain how within-county heterogeneity in treatment timing and pre-existing rent dynamics affect the Callaway–Sant’Anna comparisons, and ideally conduct robustness checks using any available finer-level data (e.g., urban/rural subsets, LEAs with uniform timing, or instrumental proxying for LEA treatment penetration).

2. **Parallel Trends Assumption Requires Stronger Evidence:** The identifying assumption is that, conditional on cohort and time, treatment cohorts would have followed the same rent trajectory as not-yet-treated counties. Yet treated counties were selected precisely because of higher levels and faster growth, raising concerns about differential curvature or mean reversion. The paper notes that pre–treatment event-study coefficients are “flat” for the growth outcome, but no figures or tables of these pre-trends are shown, and a flat aggregate average could mask cohort-specific differences. Please provide cohort-specific event studies over a longer pre-period, disaggregated for both growth and levels, and discuss whether time-varying confounders (e.g., local construction starts, employment shocks) coincident with designation might threaten parallel trends.

3. **Control Group Deterioration as Universal Treatment Approaches:** Using not-yet-treated counties as controls is standard, but by 2021 almost all counties were treated, leaving no pure control group. This raises two issues: (i) Post-2021 estimates rely on comparisons to counties that will soon be treated, potentially contaminating the control path if the policy anticipates future designation or drives spillovers, and (ii) cohort-specific ATTs for the late (Aug 2021) group cannot be estimated because no clean controls remain. The paper should clearly demarcate the time window over which the estimation is valid, report how long the not-yet-treated control pool remains rich, and assess whether the results are sensitive to trimming the sample before the late-wave designations or to rotating the comparison group (e.g., using early treated counties far from the frontier). An explicit discussion of how universal treatment affects identification—and whether any never-treated units exist post-2021—is necessary.

**Suggestions**

1. **Improve Transparency of Event-Study Diagnostics:** Include plotted event studies (with confidence bands) for both outcomes, disaggregated by cohort if possible, to demonstrate the absence of pre-trends. Reporting only aggregate “flat” pre-period ATTs makes it hard to judge whether the parallel-trends assumption holds across heterogeneous cohorts. If sample limitations prevent plotting all cohorts together, consider grouping into early/mid cohorts. Additionally, the appendix could report the number of not-yet-treated counties contributing to each post-treatment period to show when the comparison group begins to thin.

2. **Assess Alternative Mechanisms and Outcomes:** The paper argues that caps reduce growth but not levels because rents continue rising albeit more slowly. To reinforce this interpretation, consider estimating the cumulative difference in log rents between treated and counterfactual paths over time—i.e., integrate the growth effects to show how large level gaps would become if the policy remained unchanged. Moreover, exploring secondary outcomes (e.g., vacancy rates, new listings, turnover) would help address whether the cap induced supply adjustments. If such data are unavailable, explicitly state the limitations and discuss how supply-side responses could bias the estimated growth effects.

3. **Leverage Differential Intensity of Treatment:** Not all counties experienced equally binding caps; the manifest and paper note that early cohorts (Dublin, Cork) were high-growth markets and thus more likely to have binding caps. The current cohort ATTs go some way toward this, but you could strengthen identification by interacting treatment with pre-treatment rent growth (or constructing a “binding” indicator) to test whether the estimated growth reduction is concentrated where the statutory 4% limit was actually binding. Such heterogeneity analysis would also help differentiate policy effects from regression to the mean.

4. **Discuss Policy Interpretation with More Nuance:** The conclusion implies that RPZs are speed bumps that cannot solve the affordability crisis. While this may be true, the paper could better quantify the policy’s welfare implications. For example, what share of tenants remain in rent-controlled units over time, and how does the cap affect typical tenant savings? A simple back-of-the-envelope calculation—e.g., the median rent, average tenancy duration, and the implied dollar savings from slower growth—would illustrate the practical magnitude of the estimated 2.4 percentage point reduction. Similarly, acknowledge that slowing growth may still have distributional benefits for low-income tenants, even if aggregate levels continue to rise.

5. **Strengthen the Link to the Empirical Strategy Literature:** While the paper rightly argues that TWFE is biased, the argument would be stronger if you formally estimate the Callaway–Sant’Anna ATT for the placebo period (as done with TWFE) and contrast those estimates to the main results, thus reinforcing the credibility of the C–S approach. Additionally, you might mention whether the doubly robust specification includes time-varying covariates (e.g., employment, construction permits) that could absorb remaining heterogeneity, or explain why such covariates were omitted.

6. **Clarify the Late/National Cohort Treatment:** The cohort table omits the late-August-2021 cohort because of the lack of clean controls. Readers will benefit from a detailed explanation of how their exclusion affects aggregate interpretation—does the estimated overall ATT primarily reflect early/mid cohorts? If so, state that the results speak to the policy’s impact before universal designation and may not generalize to the later nationwide rollout. If possible, explore whether a synthetic control or other method could recover late-cohort effects using pre-period trends from similar international markets or more distant counties unaffected by RPZ until later.

7. **Address Spillovers More Fully:** The paper briefly argues that spillovers are limited because early RPZs were urban and controls rural. However, population movement and landlord pricing strategies could still push rents in non-RPZ neighbours. Consider testing for spatial spillovers by including a measure of adjacent county RPZ status or distance to the nearest treated county. At a minimum, explain the likely direction of bias if spillovers exist (e.g., upward pressure on controls) and whether the results may accordingly understate the true effect on treated areas.

8. **Provide Access to Data and Code:** Given the policy importance, sharing replication files or a detailed appendix describing how the treatment timing was constructed (including the exact dates of designation per county) would enhance credibility and facilitate future research. If data restrictions prevent full sharing, consider releasing a treatment-timing table and sample code for the Callaway–Sant’Anna estimation.

By addressing these points, especially the concerns about County-level aggregation, pre-trend diagnostics, and the shrinking control group, the paper will offer a much more convincing empirical evaluation of Ireland’s Rent Pressure Zones.
