**Summary**

The paper examines whether conversion to academy status alters school socioeconomic composition, using GIAS administrative snapshots (2021–2026) and predecessor-successor linkage to track schools through conversion. Applying the Sun–Abraham estimator to a panel of 12,173 schools, it finds a modest but statistically significant reduction in FSM shares (−0.34 pp), driven entirely by sponsor-led conversions, with naive TWFE producing the opposite sign. These results are interpreted as evidence of a sorting cost imposed particularly by forcibly restructured schools.

**Essential Points**

1. **Limited Temporal and Treatment Coverage Weakens Generalizability and Identification.** The central idea was to exploit the staggered conversion of over 11,000 schools between 2010 and 2025. The actual analysis covers only 2021–2026 and 1,461 conversions, a small subset concentrated near the end of the reform. The reduced variation constrains the ability to credibly trace parallel trends and undermines claims about the “world’s largest natural experiment.” The authors need to either extend the panel backward (to capture earlier conversion cohorts) or convincingly argue why later conversions alone can identify the relevant sorting effects without broader cohort comparisons. Without this, it is unclear whether the estimated effects reflect general patterns of academy autonomy or idiosyncrasies of the most recent conversions.

2. **Parallel-Trends Assumption Hinges on Few Pre-Treatment Periods and Changing Cohort Composition.** The event study relies on only a few pre-treatment years for each cohort, and the paper does not document how cohorts differ in observable characteristics (e.g., Ofsted rating, prior FSM trends). Given that sponsor-led conversions correspond to failing schools undergoing dramatic restructuring, their pre-treatment trajectories may already diverge from never-treated schools, challenging the parallel-trends assumption even with school FE. The authors should show more granular evidence of pre-trends (e.g., cohort-specific graphs) and, ideally, incorporate additional controls or flexible trends to capture systematic differences between sponsor-led and other schools prior to conversion.

3. **Mechanism Interpretation Requires Direct Evidence on Administrative Disruption.** The paper attributes the sponsor-led effect to disruption from closures/reopenings, but this channel is asserted rather than demonstrated. Moreover, sponsor-led conversions are correlated with high FSM shares and poor performance, so the estimated compositional change might simply reflect catching up toward the system mean (regression to the mean) rather than sorting per se. The authors should provide evidence that the treatment is not merely re-labelling schools, for instance by showing that the same physical schools (predecessors) experience attrition in FSM pupils, or by linking to pupil mobility data if available. Additionally, comparing sponsor-led conversions with converter ones on observables (e.g., Ofsted scores, prior FSM trends) would clarify whether the observed heterogeneity is truly a sorting cost or driven by pre-existing differences.

**Suggestions**

1. **Broaden the Temporal Window to Capture the Full Staggered-Adoption Variation.** The manifest highlights the 2010–2025 conversion wave. If the data allow, the authors should incorporate earlier GIAS snapshots (available annually) and predecessor-successor linkage to include the earliest cohorts. Doing so would increase statistical power, provide more pre-treatment observations, and substantiate claims about heterogeneity and the “world’s largest natural experiment.” If earlier data are unavailable or unreliable, the paper should explicitly state why and discuss the potential implications for identification (e.g., whether earlier cohorts differ systematically from the 2021–2026 sample).

2. **Strengthen the Parallel-Trends Case with Cohort-Specific Diagnostics and Alternative Specifications.** Beyond reporting insignificant pre-treatment coefficients, consider plotting cohort-specific event studies (e.g., sponsor-led vs. converter) to visually inspect trends. Include placebo tests that shift the treatment year earlier or apply the estimator to outcomes that should not change (e.g., non-FSM demographic shares) to bolster confidence. Alternatively, interact cohort indicators with flexible time trends or include school-specific linear trends as robustness checks; though this reduces precision, it can alleviate concerns that sponsor-led schools were already on different trajectories.

3. **Address Possible Attrition or Measurement Changes Due to URN Reassignment.** The paper rightly emphasizes predecessor-successor linkages, but it should transparently describe how it ensures the continuity of school-level characteristics across URN changes. For instance, do predecessor schools drop out of the sample immediately after closure, and if so, how many pre-treatment observations remain? Are there systematic differences between schools with and without linked records? Providing summary statistics on linkage success rate and the stability of other outcomes (such as pupil counts) across the transition would reassure readers that compositional changes are not artifacts of dataset construction.

4. **Clarify the Mechanism Behind Sponsor-Led Sorting.** To support the “disruption displaces disadvantaged pupils” story, consider incorporating auxiliary evidence, such as changes in admission appeals, exclusion rates, or pupil churn rates around sponsor-led conversions (if data permit). Alternatively, compare nearby maintained schools to see whether sponsor-led conversions are associated with increases in FSM shares elsewhere, consistent with spillover sorting. If such data are unavailable, acknowledge the limitation and frame the mechanism as a hypothesis rather than a conclusion.

5. **Re-express Magnitudes in Policy-Relevant Terms.** A 0.34 pp decline in FSM share is described as one fewer disadvantaged pupil per school, but the paper could further explore whether this compositional change meaningfully affects measured attainment. For example, linking to published value-added estimates could illustrate how much of the purported “attainment gains” might be attributed to sorting. Conversely, if the effect is quantitatively small, consider contextualizing it relative to the variation across schools or years to help readers gauge practical significance.

6. **Discuss External Validity and Policy Implications More Nuancedly.** The paper concludes that sponsor-led conversions have a sorting cost, but policymakers will want to know whether this cost is offset by attainment improvements or whether it could be minimized through targeted support. Even if causal attainment estimates are beyond the scope, reviewing the literature to situate the size of the compositional change relative to documented achievement gains would strengthen the policy narrative. Additionally, consider whether the findings generalize to other contexts (e.g., countries with different accountability regimes) and what design features (e.g., mandated continuity plans) might mitigate sorting in forced conversions.

By addressing these points, the paper would better match its empirical strategy to the research question and provide more compelling evidence on the distributional implications of school autonomy.
