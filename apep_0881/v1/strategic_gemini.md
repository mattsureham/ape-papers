**SUBJECT:** Strategic Assessment of "The Sorting Cost of School Autonomy"

---

### 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

The paper investigates whether granting schools greater autonomy leads them to "cream-skim" or shed disadvantaged students. Using the massive UK Academy conversion program as a laboratory, the author finds that conversion—particularly when forced upon failing schools—leads to a significant decline in the share of students eligible for Free School Meals (FSM), suggesting a sorting cost that may bias traditional estimates of "academy value-added."

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this well in the abstract, but the first two paragraphs of the introduction are slightly too focused on the "English academy programme" as an end in itself. For the AER, the pitch needs to lead with the universal economic tension: the trade-off between institutional autonomy and social equity.

*The pitch the paper should have:* 
"Does institutional autonomy in public service provision improve efficiency at the expense of equity? While decentralization is often touted as a driver of performance, it may incentivize providers to improve 'quality' metrics by selecting easier-to-serve clients. This paper uses the world's largest experiment in school autonomy to show that 'success' in autonomous schools is partially driven by the systematic displacement of disadvantaged users, a finding that complicates the global movement toward school choice."

---

### 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**The Contribution:** The paper identifies a "sorting cost" of school autonomy, demonstrating that compositional shifts (shedding poor students) account for a portion of the documented performance gains in England’s academies.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It differentiates itself from Eyles et al. (2018) and Andrews (2017) primarily through a more robust econometric lens (Sun & Abraham) and a focus on the *denominator* of the value-added equation (student composition). 
*   **Framing:** It is currently framed as "answering a gap in the UK literature." To be AER-caliber, it must be framed as a contribution to the global **Theory of the Firm/Public Economics** regarding how agents respond to autonomy.
*   **AER Quality?** Currently, a smart economist might say, "It’s a UK-specific update using modern DiD." To avoid this, the author needs to prove that this sorting is a *feature*, not a *bug*, of autonomy-based reforms.

---

### 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

*   **Neighbors:** Eyles, Machin, & Silva (2018); Hsieh & Urquiola (2006); Sun & Abraham (2021); Burgess (2015).
*   **Positioning:** The paper tries to do too many things. It attacks the old literature (TWFE bias) while building on the sorting literature. 
*   **Missing Conversations:** The paper should speak more to the **Personnel Economics** of school leadership. Why does a "sponsor-led" academy change the composition? Is it the principal? The branding? The paper is currently a bit of a "black box" regarding the *action* taken by the school.
*   **Unexpected Connection:** It could benefit from connecting to the literature on **"Cream-skimming in Privatized Markets"** (e.g., healthcare or job training), elevating it from an "education paper" to a "public incentives paper."

---

### 4. NARRATIVE ARC

*   **Setup:** The global push for school autonomy as a tool for efficiency.
*   **Tension:** The "Incentive-Selection Puzzle"—do schools get better, or do they just get better students?
*   **Resolution:** Specifically "Sponsor-led" (forced) conversions lead to a 1.2pp drop in FSM students.
*   **Implications:** Autonomy-based gains are partly illusory; policy must guard against "disruption-led sorting."

**Evaluation:** The narrative is clear but the "resolution" feels a bit thin. The 0.34pp aggregate effect is small (1.6% of the mean). The story is currently "it happens, but the effect is modest."

---

### 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

*   **The Dinner Party Fact:** "When the UK forced failing schools to become autonomous, they didn't just fix the teaching—they basically 'rebranded' the poor kids out of the building."
*   **The Reaction:** People will lean in for the *mechanism* (How? Did they change the boundary? Did they just change the logo?). They will reach for their phones when they hear the effect size is only 1.2 percentage points.
*   **The Follow-up:** "Where did those kids go?" This is the missing piece of the story. If they just moved to the school next door, the "Sorting Cost" is a local general equilibrium problem that the paper hasn't fully mapped.

---

### 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

*   **Front-load the Mechanism:** Move the "Sponsor-led vs. Converter" distinction to the very beginning. The aggregate result (-0.34) is too small to carry the paper. The heterogeneity *is* the paper.
*   **The TWFE Section:** While methodologically "correct," the discussion of "forbidden comparisons" feels like a student proving they read the latest econometrics blogs. Move the technical "why TWFE failed" to a footnote or appendix. Keep the focus on the world, not the estimator.
*   **Missing Section:** A "Where do they go?" analysis. If FSM students leave treated schools, do they cluster in the remaining "never-treated" schools? Without this, the "Sorting Cost" is only half-told.

---

### 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

**The Gap:** Currently, this is a very high-quality *Journal of Human Resources* or *Journal of Public Economics* paper. To reach the AER, it needs **Ambition**.

**Single Most Impactful Advice:** 
Identify the *behavioral* mechanism—show *how* these schools shed students (e.g., changes in exclusion rates, marketing language, or "soft" admission barriers)—and then link this sorting directly to the previously reported "attainment gains" to show exactly how much of the "Academy Miracle" was just a "Sorting Mirage."

---

### Strategic Assessment

*   **Current framing quality:** Adequate (Needs more "Public Econ" and less "UK Education").
*   **Contribution clarity:** Somewhat fuzzy (Is it a method paper or a policy paper?).
*   **Literature positioning:** Well-positioned within Education; needs work in broader Micro.
*   **Narrative arc:** Serviceable.
*   **AER distance:** Medium.
*   **Single biggest improvement:** Track the "displaced" pupils to other schools to provide a General Equilibrium view of the sorting cost.
