## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple and policy-relevant question: when states ban kratom, a widely used plant-based substance that some users treat as a safer substitute for opioids, do overdose deaths rise because users switch to more dangerous drugs? Using the staggered timing of five state bans, the paper’s core claim is that there is no detectable increase in overdose mortality, suggesting that banning kratom did not generate the large substitution-to-fentanyl effect many observers might fear.

A busy economist should care because this is, at least in principle, a clean test of a broader question: when policymakers restrict access to a lower-risk substitute during a public health crisis, do they inadvertently push people toward more dangerous consumption? That question travels well beyond kratom.

### Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Fairly well, but not optimally. The opening is vivid and policy-forward, which is good. But it gets pulled too quickly into pharmacology and the details of kratom, rather than establishing the broader economic question in a way that signals why this belongs in a top general-interest journal rather than a health-policy niche.

Right now the intro says, in effect: “Here is an obscure substance, here is a plausible substitution story, and no one has tested it.” That is decent. What it should say is: “This is a rare opportunity to test whether prohibiting a lower-risk substitute during a crisis produces the classic unintended consequence economists worry about—substitution into a more harmful illicit market.”

### The pitch the paper should have

When governments restrict access to a lower-risk substitute for a dangerous good, do consumers shift toward the more dangerous product? This paper studies that question in the context of kratom bans during the fentanyl era. Exploiting the staggered adoption of five state bans, I find no detectable increase in overdose mortality, implying that criminalizing this putative harm-reduction substitute did not produce large substitution-driven deaths at the state level.

That pitch is stronger because it leads with the world question, not the substance. Kratom is the setting; the contribution is about substitution under prohibition.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper provides the first causal evidence on whether banning kratom—a putative lower-risk opioid substitute—increases overdose mortality, and finds no detectable state-level mortality effect.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?

Only partly. The paper gestures toward three literatures—cannabis/opioids, prohibition/unintended consequences, and drug-policy substitution—but the differentiation is still a bit fuzzy. A reader could easily come away with: “This is another staggered-DiD paper on a drug policy and overdoses.”

The real differentiator is not just “first paper on kratom.” “First paper on X” is almost never enough for AER unless X is itself first-order important. The differentiator should be:

1. **This is a direct test of substitution within the opioid-like class**, not a looser pain-management substitute like cannabis.
2. **It studies restriction rather than expansion** of access to a lower-risk alternative.
3. **The null is substantively meaningful** because it bounds how large the feared mortality spillover can be.

Those points are all in the draft, but they are not yet distilled sharply enough.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a gap in a literature?

Mixed, but still too much “gap in the literature.” Phrases like “no published study has tested this” and “this paper contributes to three literatures” are useful but second-order. The stronger framing is about the world: what happens when a state criminalizes a lower-risk substitute in the middle of a lethal epidemic?

The paper should lean harder into that real-world question.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?

They could probably say: “It studies kratom bans and overdose deaths, and doesn’t find much.” That is better than nothing, but not yet enough. Too many readers would still summarize it as “another DiD paper about a drug policy.”

What they should be able to say is: “It tests a classic unintended-consequences mechanism—whether banning a safer substitute pushes users to deadlier opioids—and finds that any such effect is not large enough to show up in state-level mortality.”

That’s a much stronger takeaway.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Most importantly: **move from ‘effect of kratom bans’ to ‘how prohibition affects substitution to dangerous illicit markets.’** The setting stays the same; the ambition changes.

More specifically, the contribution would be bigger if the paper:
- Put **more emphasis on margins of adjustment** beyond mortality: treatment admissions, poison-center calls, opioid prescribing, online searches, retail availability, seizures, or self-reported use. Mortality alone is a very distal outcome.
- Sharpened the **asymmetry insight**: access expansion to safer substitutes may reduce harm, but access restriction may not mechanically increase harm. That is a potentially important conceptual point.
- Better established whether the paper is about **harm reduction policy generally** rather than kratom specifically.
- If possible, compared bans to **regulatory alternatives** like Kratom Consumer Protection Acts. That would enlarge the policy conversation from “ban or not” to “ban versus regulate.”

Right now the paper is competent and reasonably motivated, but the scope of outcomes keeps the contribution a bit narrow.

5. More broadly, the prohibition/unintended consequences tradition: **Miron**, and work on drug supply restrictions shifting use toward more dangerous substances.

There is also an adjacent health-econ literature on **harm reduction and risky-substitute access**, even if not explicitly about kratom.

### How should the paper position itself relative to those neighbors?

Mostly **build on and reframe**, not attack.

- Relative to the cannabis-opioid papers: the paper should say, “Those studies ask whether access to a safer alternative reduces opioid harm. I ask the mirror-image question: does removing a safer alternative increase harm?”
- Relative to PDMP/naloxone/substitution papers: “This belongs to the same conversation about behavioral responses to drug policy, but in a setting where the substitute is legal, decentralized, and outside standard medical channels.”
- Relative to prohibition/unintended-consequences work: “This is a concrete contemporary test of whether prohibition predictably pushes consumers into more dangerous black-market goods.”

The paper should not overstate novelty by pretending this is a wholly separate literature. It is more persuasive when presented as a natural extension of existing substitution logic.

### Is the paper currently positioned too narrowly or too broadly?

A bit too narrowly in subject matter and a bit too broadly in literature claims.

- **Too narrowly** because it can sound like a paper for a niche audience interested in kratom regulation.
- **Too broadly** because saying it contributes to three literatures can feel generic rather than strategic.

The right move is to position it tightly around one central conversation: **substitution responses to harm-reduction versus prohibition policies during the opioid crisis**.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?

Two areas could be engaged more explicitly:

1. **Substitution under supply restrictions / product bans** beyond drugs. There is a larger economics tradition on unintended substitutions when a product is taxed, banned, or quality-restricted. Making that link would help generalize the paper.
2. **Harm reduction and informal self-medication.** The paper mentions surveys but doesn’t fully connect to work on how people use quasi-legal or nonmedical products to manage dependence, pain, or withdrawal.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?

Almost. But the most impactful conversation is not “the kratom literature,” which is too small and interdisciplinary, nor “all prohibition papers,” which is too diffuse. The right conversation is:

**When do safer substitutes actually mitigate harm in dangerous markets—and what happens when policymakers remove them?**

That is a live and broadly interesting economics question.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

The world is in an opioid/fentanyl crisis. Kratom is used by some consumers as a lower-risk analgesic or withdrawal-management product. Some states ban it despite claims that it functions as harm reduction.

### Tension

Economic logic suggests that eliminating a lower-risk substitute could push users toward more dangerous opioids, potentially raising mortality. But there is no causal evidence on whether this feared substitution actually occurs.

### Resolution

The paper finds no detectable increase in overdose deaths following state kratom bans. Apparent negative effects in simple models reflect broader differential mortality trajectories rather than a causal reduction in deaths.

### Implications

The paper implies that prohibition of kratom did not create large mortality spillovers at the state level, and that policy debates should focus less on dramatic substitution-to-fentanyl claims and more on questions like product regulation, enforcement, and who actually uses kratom.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

Yes, but it is not fully disciplined. There is a real story here. The problem is that the paper sometimes reads like:
- an introduction with a strong high-stakes claim,
- a results section heavily organized around estimator diagnostics,
- and a discussion section trying to rescue significance from a null.

The paper’s best story is actually quite straightforward:

1. Kratom is commonly described as a harm-reduction substitute.
2. If that is true in a behaviorally important way, banning it should increase deaths.
3. The data do not show that.
4. Therefore, either access wasn’t meaningfully reduced, the affected population is small, or substitution did not run toward deadly opioids.
5. That matters for how economists think about substitutes in illicit markets.

That story should govern the whole paper. Right now some sections feel like a collection of empirical exercises assembled to justify a null. The mechanism decomposition is useful, but the narrative should remain centered on the core world question.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?

“I’d lead with: states that banned kratom during the fentanyl crisis do not show a detectable increase in overdose deaths, despite the common argument that banning kratom would push users into deadlier opioids.”

That is the dinner-party fact.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

Some would lean in—especially health economists, public economists, and applied micro people who care about drug policy. But many general economists might initially reach for their phones if the paper is pitched as “a kratom paper.” The trick is to make them see it as **a substitution-under-prohibition paper**.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Almost certainly: **“Why not?”**
- Was kratom use too small?
- Was the ban not enforced?
- Did users substitute to something else?
- Are mortality data too coarse to see the action?

That is actually a good sign. It means the paper has an interesting finding. But the paper needs to anticipate and own that question more aggressively.

### If the findings are null or modest, is the null itself interesting?

Potentially yes. The null is interesting if framed as:
- a bound on a feared unintended consequence,
- evidence against a widely repeated policy claim,
- and a test of whether safer-substitute logic is symmetric under access expansion and restriction.

But null papers are always fragile editorially. To work at AER level, the null must feel like it changes beliefs. The current draft is close but not fully there. Sometimes it lapses into “informative null” rhetoric without fully earning why the audience should update.

The strongest case is not “we found nothing.” It is:
**“A widely plausible and policy-salient substitution mechanism does not appear to generate large mortality effects in this setting.”**

That is valuable.

4. **The negative-control result should be front and center.**

Some is necessary, but too much gives the impression that the paper is trying to litigate credibility rather than deliver an idea.

6. **Strengthen the conclusion.**  
   The conclusion mostly summarizes. It should instead do more conceptual work: what does this imply about how economists should think about substitute goods in illicit markets, and about the asymmetry between expanding and restricting access to harm-reduction alternatives?

### Are there results buried in robustness that should be in the main results?

Yes: the contrast between the headline naive negative estimates and the null in the preferred design is central to the paper’s story. That interpretive contrast is more important than several of the robustness checks.

### Is the conclusion adding value?

Not enough. It should be more than “we found a null and policy should focus on regulation.” It should give the reader a broader lesson: **not every safer-substitute market generates large prohibition spillovers in aggregate mortality.**
