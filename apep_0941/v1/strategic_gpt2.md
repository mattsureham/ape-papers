## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple but important policy question: when the government shuts down low-quality for-profit colleges, do students move into community colleges, or do they leave higher education altogether? Using county-level variation in the intensity of for-profit college closures after the 2016 ACICS collapse and related regulatory crackdowns, the paper’s core claim is that community college enrollment fell in more-exposed places rather than rising, suggesting that community colleges did not function as the expected “safety net.”

A busy economist should care because this is not really just a higher-ed paper. It is a paper about what happens when regulation removes bad providers in a market serving vulnerable consumers: does demand reallocate to better substitutes, or does participation itself collapse?

### Does the paper articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Mostly yes, but not optimally. The first two paragraphs are reasonably clear and they do contain the surprising fact. But the framing is still too episode-specific and too quickly descends into design. The broader economic question is more interesting than “what happened after ACICS?” The intro should foreground the general problem of policy-induced market exit and failed substitution, then use ACICS as the setting.

### The pitch the paper should have

> Policymakers often assume that shutting down low-quality providers pushes consumers toward better alternatives. In higher education, that logic implied that students displaced by for-profit college closures would move into community colleges. This paper shows that the opposite happened: in places more exposed to for-profit closures after the 2016 regulatory crackdown, community college enrollment declined.  
>  
> The result suggests that removing bad schools did not simply reallocate students to better ones; it appears to have reduced postsecondary participation itself. That has broader implications for regulation in markets with vulnerable consumers, imperfect information, and weak navigational support: eliminating low-quality supply is not enough if the remaining “safety net” is not actually used.

That is the AER version of the story.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper’s contribution is to show that the mass contraction of the for-profit college sector appears to have reduced, rather than redirected, local enrollment into community colleges.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest papers?

Only partially. The paper says “this is the first paper on spillovers to community colleges,” which may be true, but “first” is not a contribution unless the reader immediately understands why that margin matters. Right now the paper differentiates itself from the for-profit literature mostly by topic adjacency rather than by a sharp conceptual contrast.

The closest contrast should be:

- prior work studies returns to for-profit attendance, misconduct, regulation, and student harm;
- this paper studies equilibrium reallocation after provider exit;
- the key question is whether public-sector alternatives absorb displaced demand.

That distinction is potentially important, but it is not currently made forcefully enough.

### Is the contribution framed as a question about the world, or filling a gap in a literature?

It starts as a world question, which is good, but then slips into “this contributes to three literatures” mode. The strongest version is clearly a world question: **When bad providers disappear, do people move to better providers or leave the market?** That is much stronger than “there is no paper on spillovers to community colleges.”

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?

A reasonably smart economist could say: “It’s a DiD showing for-profit closures lowered nearby community college enrollment.” That is decent, but still sounds like “another DiD paper about X.” What they should be able to say is: “It overturns the standard policy assumption that shutting down for-profits automatically benefits community colleges; instead the policy may have reduced college-going.” The paper is close to that, but not fully there.

### What would make the contribution bigger?

Most importantly: **show where the students went, or at least quantify the broader participation margin.** Right now the paper has a striking negative result on community colleges, plus a suggestive four-year placebo/comparison. But the natural next question is overwhelming: did students trade up, move away, switch online, or exit higher education?

Specific ways to enlarge the contribution:

1. **Make total postsecondary enrollment the central outcome**, not just community college enrollment.
   - If total enrollment falls, the paper becomes much bigger.
   - If total doesn’t fall, then this is a substitution paper, not a chilling paper.

2. **Use the four-year result as a substantive comparison, not a placebo.**
   - As written, the “placebo” is actually interesting and not a placebo at all.
   - The positive coefficient suggests reallocation across sectors.
   - That is central to the paper’s meaning.

3. **Sharpen the mechanism or retreat from it.**
   - “Information/stigma” is plausible, but currently speculative.
   - If the paper cannot show mechanism, it should lead with the fact and be modest about channels.
   - If it can show mechanism—media intensity, transfer barriers, recruitment presence, local information frictions—it becomes more than a reduced-form surprise.

4. **Reframe around policy design under provider exit.**
   - The paper becomes more important if it is about how regulation interacts with weak consumer navigation, not just about community colleges per se.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

### Closest neighbors

The likely closest neighboring literature includes:

1. **Deming, Goldin, and Katz (2012)** on the rise and consequences of for-profit postsecondary education.
2. **Cellini and Chaudhary (2014)** on labor market returns and the role of for-profit institutions.
3. **Darolia et al.** / related work on for-profit enrollment, aid, and student outcomes.
4. **Cellini, Darolia, and Turner / Cellini (2020-era work)** on regulation and consequences in the for-profit sector.
5. Work on **college closures and student outcomes**, especially around Corinthian/ITT closures and borrower defense-type settings.

Also relevant:
- community college demand and access literature;
- information barriers in college choice (Hoxby-Turner, Dynarski et al.);
- broader regulation/spillover/reallocation literature.

### How should it position itself relative to those neighbors?

Mostly **build on**, not attack. The paper does not need to claim prior work got the for-profit problem wrong. It should say:

- prior work established these institutions were often low-value and ripe for regulation;
- what remained unknown is what happens to the students and local educational ecosystem when those providers disappear;
- this paper shows that cleanup can reduce participation if substitution to public alternatives is weak.

That is a natural extension, not a repudiation.

### Is it positioned too narrowly or too broadly?

Right now it is oddly both.

- **Too narrowly**: the title and intro are heavily centered on community colleges and ACICS.
- **Too broadly in a vague way**: the “regulatory spillovers” claim reaches for a big literature, but without fully earning it.

The better middle ground is: **a paper on policy-induced provider exit in higher education markets, with community colleges as the key margin of expected substitution.**

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?

It seems under-connected to:

1. **Market exit/reallocation literature** in industrial organization and public economics.
   - What happens when providers are removed from a market?
   - Does demand reallocate efficiently?

2. **School closure and institutional closure literature** beyond for-profits.
   - K-12 school closures, hospital closures, bank branch closures, etc.
   - The common issue is whether replacement institutions function as true substitutes.

3. **Mismatch and navigational-frictions literature**.
   - The claim is effectively about frictions in moving to “better” options.
   - That should connect to consumer information, salience, and administrative burden.

4. **Higher-ed undermatch and application behavior**.
   - If students fail to substitute into community colleges, why?
   - This is closer to the college-choice/frictions literature than the paper currently acknowledges.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?

Not quite. It is currently having a conversation with “for-profit colleges” and “community college access.” That is necessary but insufficient.

The more impactful conversation is:

**What are the unintended consequences of shutting down low-quality providers when consumers face information and transfer frictions?**

That conversation reaches public economics, industrial organization, education, and regulation. It is the right conversation for AER.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

The world before this paper: for-profit colleges serve many disadvantaged students, regulators conclude many are low-quality, and the default policy view is that community colleges can absorb displaced students because they are open-access and cheaper.

### Tension

The puzzle is whether that substitution actually happens. The entire regulatory logic assumes it does. But consumers in this market may face confusion, stigma, transfer barriers, or informational frictions, so shutting down one set of institutions may reduce take-up of the whole sector rather than improve matching.

### Resolution

The paper finds that counties more exposed to for-profit closures saw lower community college enrollment, not higher, with especially large declines among Hispanic students and an effect that appears to grow over time.

### Implications

The implication is that “remove bad providers and students will move to better schools” is not an automatic equilibrium outcome. Regulation may need to be paired with active transition support, outreach, and transfer infrastructure.

### Does the paper have a clear arc?

It has the ingredients of a good arc, but the execution is uneven. The story is there, but the paper sometimes reads like a sequence of empirical sections around a predetermined conclusion (“chilling effect”) rather than a disciplined resolution of the central question.

Two specific narrative problems:

1. **The paper settles too quickly on the “chilling” interpretation.**
   - But the four-year result hints at sectoral reallocation.
   - The narrative should acknowledge that the key issue is not just chilling; it is failed or uneven substitution.

2. **The mechanism discussion outruns the evidence.**
   - The paper wants the story to be information/stigma.
   - But what it really has is a striking reduced-form fact about community colleges.
   - For narrative credibility, it should either produce mechanism evidence or present multiple interpretations more symmetrically.

### What story should it be telling?

The best story is:

> Regulators assumed displaced students would move from low-quality for-profits into community colleges. They did not. Instead, the educational safety net appears weaker than policymakers believed, and the consequences of provider exit depend crucially on whether consumers can navigate to substitutes.

That is cleaner and stronger than “closures chilled postsecondary participation through stigma,” which is one possible explanation, not yet the paper’s proven contribution.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?

“I’ve got a paper showing that when for-profit colleges shut down, nearby community college enrollment falls instead of rises.”

That is a very good lead.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

They would lean in—initially. It is a genuinely surprising sign reversal relative to the policy presumption.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Immediately: **“So where did the students go?”**

That is the central strategic problem of the paper. If the author cannot answer that, the paper is still interesting, but it stays one tier lower in ambition. The paper currently anticipates this only partially.

A second follow-up would be: **“Is this really about the ACICS episode, or about broader trends in urban community college demand?”** The current framing invites that concern because it leans heavily on one episode while the treatment bundles multiple closures and multiple policy shocks.

### If findings are modest, is that a problem?

The estimates are not enormous, but they are surprising. The issue is not statistical modesty; it is interpretive ambiguity. A modest negative community-college effect can still be AER-relevant if it decisively overturns a major policy assumption and is clearly connected to broader theory. Right now it is intriguing but not yet decisive.
