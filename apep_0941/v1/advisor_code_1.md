**Summary**

This paper studies the community college enrollment response to the ACICS accreditation revocation and associated for-profit closures by exploiting county-level variation in closure intensity (2015 enrollment at subsequently closed institutions). Using a county-year DiD with county and year fixed effects, it finds that higher displacement is associated with statistically significant declines in community college enrollment—especially among Hispanic students—and that the effect grows over time. Placebo analyses (four-year publics) and various robustness checks support the interpretation that the community college sector failed to absorb displaced students, contrary to the assumed “safety net.”

**Essential Points**

1. **Spatial definition of treatment and spillovers.** The original manifest emphasized treatment assignment based on proximity (within 25 miles) rather than mere county location, because students may cross county borders to enroll. The current treatment measure imputes displacement to counties that hosted closing institutions, which may misclassify spillover effects (e.g., students in adjacent counties). This could both bias estimated effects if displacement is correlated with county fixed effects and undermine the identification assumption. Please reconcile the treatment definition with the manifest idea (25-mile radius) and, if feasible, consider constructing exposure measures that account for the spatial reach of displaced students (e.g., distance-weighted enrollment within a buffer). Presenting results under both definitions would increase confidence that the estimated effect is not driven by arbitrary county borders.

2. **Parallel trends justification and inclusion of pre-ACICS dynamics.** The credibility of the DiD rests on parallel trends, but the paper offers only a brief description of flat pre-trends and does not show the full event-study figure nor discuss whether the parallel-trend test includes 2015 (when ACICS was flagged) or prior years. Given that the treatment variable is constructed from 2015 enrollment, counties may already differ before 2016 in ways correlated with enrollment growth. Please include the full event-study plot (with confidence intervals) and discuss whether any pre-2016 trends are statistically and economically meaningful. Additionally, consider controlling for county-specific linear trends or testing the robustness to differential pre-period slopes to ensure the identification assumption is not violated by pre-existing trends in community college enrollment.

3. **Mechanism and interpretation: absorption vs. chilling.** The paper interprets the negative effect as a “chilling” of enrollment; however, the identification strategy captures only county-average enrollment changes and cannot distinguish between students who dropped out of higher education, moved to other sectors (e.g., other for-profits or out-of-county community colleges), or indeed reflect capacity constraints or composition changes in community colleges. The “placebo” with four-year publics is helpful but insufficient. To solidify the mechanism, consider (i) examining whether nearby private non-profit or out-of-county community colleges experienced enrollment changes, (ii) exploring whether degree completions or retention rates changed (suggested by the manifest), or (iii) comparing enrollment responses in counties that lost ACICS institutions versus those that lost only non-ACICS for-profit institutions. Without additional evidence, the conclusion that policy “chilled” participation may overstate the inference.

**Suggestions**

- **Revisit treatment definition and exposure measure.** As noted, the manifest described defining treatment via counties within 25 miles of an ACICS institution. Consider constructing a distance-weighted exposure index: for each county, sum displaced enrollment from nearby closures, weighted by inverse distance or travel time. This would capture the fact that students may enroll outside their home county, addressing potential spatial spillovers and reducing measurement error. Compare the main results using both the within-county and distance-weighted definitions to show robustness.

- **Expand on event-study evidence.** Include the event-study figure (coefficients and 95% CIs) in the main text or appendix, with a clear narrative on the pre-2016 dynamics. Report the estimated coefficients for each year relative to the reference period (e.g., 2015). If some pre-trends are non-zero, show robustness to including county-specific trends or restricting the sample to a tighter pre-period (e.g., 2010–2014). This transparency will strengthen the parallel-trend claim.

- **Address potential confounders.** Enrollment trends may respond to local economic conditions or demographic shocks correlated with for-profit presence. Although county FE and year FE absorb some variation, consider controlling for time-varying county characteristics (e.g., unemployment rates, population growth, K–12 graduating cohorts) to rule out confounding. A triple-difference exploiting variation within states (comparing treated and control counties within the same state-year) could further mitigate concerns about state-level policy shifts or macro shocks.

- **Leverage other outcomes to unpack mechanism.** The manifest highlighted the value of examining degree completions. Add analyses of community college completions and (if available) transfer rates to four-year institutions to test whether the decline in enrollment translates into completion losses. Similarly, test whether financial aid receipt, Pell enrollment share, or part-time/full-time mix changed differentially by exposure. These descriptive checks would help distinguish between enrollment declines due to fewer students entering versus shifts in composition (e.g., more affluent students staying).

- **Discuss policy heterogeneity.** County-level responses may depend on the local community college supply or state-level safety-net programs. Explore whether the estimated effect varies with county community college capacity (e.g., number of campuses, seats per capita), state-level outreach programs post-ACICS, or the presence of teach-out agreements. Heterogeneity analysis could identify contexts where the safety net performed better or worse, adding actionable insight for policymakers.

- **Clarify the interpretation of the positive “placebo” coefficient on four-year publics.** The placebo estimate is positive (2.7 pp; marginally significant), which the paper interprets as stronger students “trading up.” It would be helpful to discuss alternative interpretations—e.g., that four-year publics benefited from an unrelated expansion—or relate this to non-ACICS shocks. Providing longer-run placebo checks (e.g., on private non-profits or overall post-secondary enrollment) would contextualize the result.

- **Detail the triple-difference specification.** The paper introduces a DDD but provides little detail on how the race indicator is constructed or how the panel (race × county) is organized. Include the equation, clarify whether race refers to enrollment shares or counts, and report standard errors appropriately (e.g., clustered at state level). Additionally, consider interacting displacement with Hispanic share to test whether counties with more Hispanic students experienced larger declines, supporting the race-specific claim.

- **Explore spillovers to labor market outcomes.** While beyond the current focus, the chilling effect could have wider social implications. If feasible, link to county-level employment or wage data to demonstrate whether enrollment declines coincided with worse labor market outcomes, reinforcing the policy relevance. At minimum, include a discussion of potential downstream effects.

- **Ensure reproducibility.** The paper relies on the IPEDS DuckDB infrastructure. Provide sufficient detail (including code snippets or links) so that readers can reconstruct the county-year panel, treatment variable, and aggregation logic. Clarify any data cleaning steps (e.g., how county attribution was handled for institutions crossing borders).

By addressing these suggestions, the authors can reinforce the credibility of the identification strategy, provide richer mechanism evidence, and better connect the findings to the policy debate on for-profit regulation.
