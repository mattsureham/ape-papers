### 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether the regulatory "cleansing" of the for-profit college sector—specifically the 2016 revocation of ACICS accreditation—achieved its goal of shifting students toward higher-quality public alternatives. Using a geographic DiD, the author finds the opposite: instead of community colleges absorbing displaced students, local community college enrollment actually *declined*, particularly among Hispanic students. This suggests that sector-specific regulation can have "chilling" spillovers that depress overall human capital investment rather than just reallocating it.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this well in the first paragraph, but it buries the "chilling" mechanism a bit too deep. The current pitch focuses on the policy failure. To be AER-ready, the pitch should lead with the **behavioral puzzle**: Why does the death of a competitor (for-profits) reduce demand for a substitute (community colleges)? 

**The pitch the paper should have:** 
"When low-quality firms are regulated out of a market, do consumers switch to higher-quality alternatives or exit the market entirely? Exploiting the mass closure of for-profit colleges, I show that instead of a 'flight to quality' toward community colleges, there is a localized chilling effect that reduces enrollment across the board. This finding suggests that for-profits may provide positive information externalities or recruitment spillovers that, once removed, leave marginal students disconnected from the higher education system entirely."

---

### 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:** The paper documents a negative enrollment spillover from the contraction of the for-profit sector onto the public community college sector, debunking the "safety net" assumption of federal policy.

*   **Differentiation:** It is well-differentiated from Cellini (2010) or Deming et al. (2012) by looking at *aggregate county spillovers* rather than just student-level returns or for-profit entry. 
*   **World vs. Literature:** It frames the contribution as answering a question about the **WORLD** (does policy work as intended?), which is a strength. 
*   **What’s New?** A smart economist would see this as "The Cross-Price Elasticity is the wrong sign" paper. That is a compelling hook.
*   **Make it bigger:** To be truly AER-sized, the paper needs to move beyond documenting the decline to **proving the mechanism**. Is it stigma (information), the loss of for-profit marketing (advertising), or credit transfer frictions? Right now, Section 6 "Discussion" is speculative. If the author could use Google Trends data for "Community College" searches in treated counties or marketing spend data, the contribution would double in size.

---

### 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

*   **Closest Neighbors:** Cellini (2020) on for-profit regulation; Deming, Goldin, and Katz (2012) on the for-profit landscape; and the Hoxby/Turner (2015) work on information interventions.
*   **Positioning:** The paper builds on the for-profit literature but should **attack** the "Optimal Regulation" literature. It needs to position itself as a cautionary tale for Greenstone (2002)-style regulatory studies: fixing a "bad" sector can damage a "good" one if they share a common information environment.
*   **Missing Conversations:** The paper is largely silent on the **Industrial Organization of Education**. It should speak to the literature on "advertising as a public good" (if for-profits grow the whole pie, their exit shrinks it).

---

### 4. NARRATIVE ARC

*   **Setup:** The U.S. cracks down on "predatory" for-profits, assuming community colleges are a waiting safety net.
*   **Tension:** If they are substitutes, CC enrollment should go up. If they are complements or share a brand, CC enrollment might go down.
*   **Resolution:** CC enrollment goes down. The "safety net" is a myth; the "chilling effect" is real.
*   **Implications:** Closing bad schools isn't enough; you need "active" bridge policies.

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong. It’s a "surprising result" paper, which is the best kind of AER narrative. However, the resolution feels incomplete because we don't know *why* it's happening (Mechanism).

---

### 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

*   **The Fact:** "When the government shut down the for-profits, the local community colleges—which are cheaper and better—actually *lost* students."
*   **Reaction:** Lean in. It’s counter-intuitive.
*   **Follow-up:** "Wait, why? Did the for-profits' marketing actually help the community colleges by getting people to think about school at all?"
*   **The Null:** The paper isn't a null; it's a "wrong sign" result. That is highly publishable.

---

### 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

*   **Front-loading:** The paper is well-structured, but the "Placebo Test" on 4-year publics (Section 5.3) is actually a **Main Result**. It shows the effect is specific to the "marginal student" sector. Move it up.
*   **COVID Scrutiny:** The attenuation in the pre-2019 sample (Table 4) is a red flag. The author needs to be much more aggressive in proving the 2017-2019 decline is real and not just a 2020-2022 COVID artifact. If the result is only "significant" because of the pandemic years, the paper is in trouble.
*   **Appendix:** Move the "Standardized Effect Sizes" (Table 5) to the appendix; the coefficients in Table 2 are more intuitive.

---

### 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The gap is **Mechanism**. Currently, we see a "what" but only guess at the "why." 

**The single most impactful piece of advice:**
The author must find a proxy for the "recruitment/information" channel—such as for-profit advertising spend data (Kantar/Vivisig) or local search volume—to show that for-profit marketing was a tide that lifted all boats. Without a clear mechanism, a referee will dismiss this as a "spurious correlation with urban decline" or a "COVID fluke."

---

### Strategic Assessment

*   **Current framing quality:** Compelling
*   **Contribution clarity:** Crystal clear
*   **Literature positioning:** Well-positioned
*   **Narrative arc:** Strong
*   **AER distance:** Medium (Needs mechanism evidence)
*   **Single biggest improvement:** Provide empirical evidence for the "information/recruitment" mechanism (e.g., ad spend or search data) to rule out the possibility that the results are driven by unrelated secular declines in urban community colleges.
