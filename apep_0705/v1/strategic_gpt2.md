## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether governments can reduce informal work not by auditing harder, but by subsidizing formal transactions. Using Sweden’s 2007 RUT tax deduction for household services, it argues that places with higher latent demand for domestic services saw more declared income and more service-sector employment after the subsidy, consistent with some household work moving from the shadow economy into the formal sector.

A busy economist should care because this is a clean, policy-relevant question with broad reach: can tax policy formalize activity in rich countries, not just in developing-country informality settings? If true, that is a useful complement to the much larger enforcement-based compliance literature.

**Does the paper articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?** Not quite. The current introduction gets to the right topic quickly, but the pitch is still a bit diffuse and a little too “literature gap” driven. It takes several paragraphs before the reader understands the central claim: this is a paper about **whether subsidies can formalize informal household labor in a high-compliance economy**. That should be front and center immediately.

**What the first two paragraphs should say instead:**

> Much of the economics of tax compliance asks how governments can deter evasion through enforcement. But in many sectors, especially household services, informality persists not because enforcement is impossible, but because formal transactions are simply too expensive relative to cash payments. This raises a first-order policy question: can governments reduce informality by subsidizing formal market exchange rather than by increasing audits and penalties?
>
> Sweden’s 2007 RUT reform provides a striking test. The reform cut the effective price of household services in half when purchased from registered firms, directly favoring formal over informal domestic work. This paper asks whether that subsidy shifted activity into the formal economy. Using cross-municipality variation in pre-reform demand for household services, I show that municipalities more exposed to the reform experienced faster growth in declared income and service-sector employment after 2007, consistent with formalization of domestic work in one of the world’s highest-compliance economies.

That is the pitch. Clear question, clear setting, clear finding, clear reason to care.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Before this paper, the dominant view in tax compliance is that formalization comes from enforcement, information trails, and monitoring. Household services remain a classic hard-to-tax sector because transactions are private, small-scale, and easy to conduct in cash.

### Tension
But households can also be induced to choose formal market transactions if policy lowers the relative cost of buying services legally. We do not know very well whether that works, especially in rich countries where informality is presumed limited and where household-service subsidies are often justified on labor-supply or equity grounds rather than compliance grounds.

### Resolution
Sweden’s 2007 RUT deduction appears to have increased declared income and service-sector employment more in high-demand municipalities, suggesting that part of the household-services market moved into formal channels.

### Implications
Governments may have another policy tool for shrinking informality: subsidizing formality, not just punishing evasion. More broadly, the boundary between home production, informal work, and formal service markets is policy-sensitive.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
It has the bones of one, but not a fully disciplined arc. Right now it feels somewhat like **a collection of plausible results arranged around a theme**, rather than a paper marching toward one big takeaway.

The main source of narrative weakness is that the paper is split between several possible stories:

1. shadow economy / formalization,
2. tax compliance,
3. service-sector employment,
4. immigration and gender,
5. Sweden-specific policy evaluation.

A strong paper picks one as the spine and makes the others subordinate. Here the spine should be:

**Can a subsidy to formal household services reduce informality by changing relative prices?**

Everything else should serve that story.

The immigration and gender sections, as currently executed, do not materially advance the narrative. They read like add-on mechanism tests rather than essential pieces of the story.

### Is it a scope problem?

### Is it an ambition problem?
Yes. The paper is competent but somewhat safe. It takes a potentially big idea and presents it as a policy evaluation plus literature-gap paper. The AER version would be bolder conceptually.

**Rebuild the paper around the broader economic question—how tax subsidies reshape the boundary between home production, informal work, and formal market services—and then align the main outcomes and mechanisms as tightly as possible to that question.**

That is the one thing. If the author does only one major revision, it should be this.

Right now the paper is trying to prove too much with outcomes that are too indirect. The path upward is not adding more standard tables; it is making the intellectual object clearer and more important.
