**Idea Fidelity**  
The paper closely follows the original idea of exploiting the 2015 franc appreciation to study retail outcomes at the municipal/cantonal level. The author uses STATENT data, focuses on retail employment/establishment destruction, and employs a DID/event-study design with border exposure as continuous treatment—all key elements of the manifest. However, the paper shifts the unit of analysis primarily to cantons and the treatment index is described at the cantonal level. Since the manifest emphasized municipal-level variation, the paper should clarify (and, ideally, exploit) whether the STATENT municipal panel simply aggregates to cantons or whether there is within-canton heterogeneity that could strengthen identification. Additionally, the original idea promised tests on whether lost retail employment was absorbed elsewhere (e.g., non-retail services) and whether loss was permanent; the paper discusses permanence through persistence but does not directly show reallocations toward other service sectors. Including these elements would align even more tightly with the manifest.

**Summary**  
This paper studies the 2015 Swiss franc appreciation as a large, unexpected shock that made cross-border shopping cheaper and, the author argues, led to retail job and firm losses in border cantons. Using STATENT data and a difference-in-differences design exploiting continuous border exposure, the paper documents a statistically significant decline in retail employment (about −6.3% per unit of exposure) and finds persistent post-2015 effects without pre-trends. A triple-difference contrasts retail with non-tradable sectors to argue that the effect is specific to tradable retail activity and not general border-canton malaise.

**Essential Points**  
1. **Municipal vs. Cantonal Variation & Aggregation Bias**  
   The motivation was to exploit municipal-level variation (e.g., distance to crossings), but the empirical work is conducted at the canton level with a single border-exposure index. This raises concerns about aggregation bias and whether key identification comes merely from large cantons (Basel, Geneva, Ticino). The paper should clarify why municipal variation was not used and, if possible, present results at the municipal level or with more granular measures. If municipal-level data are unavailable for employment, explain explicitly why cantonal aggregation still credibly captures the proposed mechanism.

2. **Interpretation of Border Exposure Index**  
   The continuous border exposure measure is central to identification, yet its construction (population-weighted proximity to "accessible cross-border retail destinations") is not fully transparent nor justified econometrically. How sensitive are results to alternative definitions (e.g., border length, share of population within 15km, travel time to nearest crossing)? Without showing robustness to plausible alternatives, it is unclear whether the exposure index genuinely captures the cost reduction from cross-border shopping rather than simply tracking other characteristics of border cantons (e.g., urbanization). Providing more detail and robustness checks for the index is essential.

3. **Mechanism & Reallocation Evidence**  
   The paper asserts retail desertification and hints at reallocation to other services, but empirical support for the mechanism is thin. The employment decline could reflect falling demand for retail or other canton-level shocks coinciding with 2015. Beyond the triple-difference with non-tradables, the paper should present direct evidence on consumer behavior (e.g., border crossing retail sales) or show that other local sectors (e.g., wholesale, transportation) did not trend similarly. Additionally, if “retail jobs were never replaced,” evidence from entry/exit rates or municipal-level retail density would help substantiate the permanence claim. Without such checks, attributing the drop solely to cross-border shopping remains tenuous.

**Suggestions**  
- **Leverage Municipal-Level Variation**: Since STATENT extends to 2,136 municipalities, consider estimating the DID model at the municipality level using cross-border proximity (distance to border crossing or average travel time) as treatment intensity. This would increase the number of clusters and make the identification closer to the original idea. If employment is only available at the cantonal level, explain that limitation explicitly and possibly use municipal counts of establishments or entry/exit rates as supplementary evidence. Combining both levels (municipal for establishments, cantonal for employment) could reinforce the narrative.

- **Detail and Validate the Border Exposure Index**: Provide a fuller explanation of how the exposure score is calculated (e.g., whether population within 15km is summed, whether crossing beds are weighted) and include a table of exposure values for each canton. Consider alternative indices (e.g., inverse distance to nearest crossing, share of population within 15km of the border, travel time-weighted border length) and show that main results are robust. This will reassure readers that the treatment captures the theoretical mechanism—access to cheaper foreign retail—rather than incidental border characteristics.

- **More Direct Mechanism Tests**: Strengthen the causal story by showing that Swiss consumer spending abroad surged following 2015 in border regions. While scanner data may not be accessible, proxies like customs declarations, VAT refunds, or cross-border traffic data could be used. Alternatively, show that retail sales (or employment) declines are concentrated in the branches selling goods most exposed to cross-border substitution (e.g., groceries, clothing) rather than non-durable non-tradables.

- **Control for Other Border Shocks and Trends**: Include controls for potential confounders that differ between border and interior cantons over time, such as growth in tourism, commuting flows, or industry composition shifts. While canton and year fixed effects absorb time-invariant differences, differential trends in tourism or immigration could mimic the border exposure effect. Interacting border exposure with linear time trends or including canton-specific time trends would test whether the results survive more flexible trends. Additionally, explicitly discuss whether any other policy (e.g., cross-border infrastructure investments) coincided with 2015.

- **Revisit Few-Cluster Inference and Power**: With only 26 cantons, randomization inference for the primary employment results is briefly mentioned but not shown in detail. Present the randomization inference distribution (perhaps as a figure) and report the p-values explicitly for each main outcome. If the establishment result becomes non-significant under permutation, discuss interpretation and whether the paper should emphasize employment and share outcomes over establishment count.

- **Explore Reallocation within Municipalities**: The claim that retail jobs were not replaced could be supported by showing that overall municipal employment or establishments did not decline as much, implying job losses were not recouped elsewhere. Use STATENT sector shares or total employment to show that retail lost share without offsetting gains. For example, did wholesale, logistics, or e-commerce-related sectors grow in border areas, signaling substitution? If not, this helps solidify the "desertification" narrative.

- **Extend Triple-Difference with Sectoral Heterogeneity**: The triple-difference is a strong design; consider extending it by interacting retail sub-sectors (food retail vs. other retail) with border exposure to see if the effect is concentrated where substitution is easiest. Moreover, include hospitality and other tradable services as additional comparison groups. This would deepen the understanding of how exchange rate shocks reallocate activity across sectors in border regions.

- **Clarify Policy Implications and External Validity**: In the discussion, briefly comment on whether similar effects might be expected in other countries with sizable border regions (e.g., U.S.–Mexico) and under what conditions. Are there unique Swiss institutional features (cash payments, customs limits, transportation networks) that make the franc shock especially potent? This will help readers gauge the broader relevance of retail desertification from currency appreciation.

- **Tables & Figures**: Consider presenting a map showing cantonal border exposure and retail outcomes to visually convey the spatial pattern. An event-study plot with confidence intervals would also help readers assess pre-trends more easily. Including a table that lists key cantons and their exposure scores, along with their pre- and post-treatment retail changes, would make the narrative more concrete.

Implementing these suggestions would enhance the credibility of the identification strategy, deepen the empirical story, and more fully realize the ambition outlined in the original manifest.
