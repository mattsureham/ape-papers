## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether a large, sudden exchange-rate appreciation can permanently hollow out local retail in places where consumers can easily shop across the border. Using the 2015 Swiss franc appreciation, it argues that border-exposed Swiss cantons saw persistent declines in retail employment and activity because consumers shifted spending to foreign stores, suggesting that exchange-rate shocks can reshape the geography of nontradable sectors, not just manufacturing and exports.

Why should a busy economist care? Because the paper is trying to move exchange-rate economics away from pass-through/export margins and toward local service-sector reallocation: a macro price shock may permanently destroy “nontradable” local jobs when geography makes foreign substitution easy.

**Does the paper articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Almost, but not quite. The opening is vivid and readable, but the paper quickly slides into a literature-gap formulation (“missing piece”) and overstates the novelty (“first causal estimates”) before fully establishing the broader economic question. The paper’s best version is not “here is another DiD around the CHF shock,” but “exchange-rate shocks can reorganize local economic geography through consumer border arbitrage.”

**What the first two paragraphs should say instead:**

> Exchange-rate movements are usually studied through exporters, import prices, and inflation. But in border regions they also change where households shop, potentially shifting demand away from domestic brick-and-mortar retailers and permanently altering the local service economy.  
>   
> This paper studies that margin using the Swiss franc’s sudden appreciation in January 2015. I ask whether making nearby foreign stores dramatically cheaper caused lasting declines in Swiss retail activity in border communities. The answer matters for how economists think about exchange-rate shocks: they may not only redistribute income across firms and sectors, but also create persistent local “retail deserts” in places exposed to cross-border consumer substitution.

That is the pitch. It starts from a world question, not a literature hole.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution in one sentence:**  
The paper claims that a sharp exchange-rate appreciation can cause persistent contraction of domestic retail activity in border-exposed areas by inducing consumers to substitute toward foreign shopping destinations.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest papers?
Only partially. The paper names adjacent literatures, but the differentiation is still fuzzy. Right now, the contribution risks reading as: “we take the Swiss franc shock, use regional exposure, and show retail fell more near the border.” That is intelligible, but not yet sharply distinguished from:
1. Swiss franc shock papers on consumer prices/spending,
2. border discontinuity papers on cross-border shopping,
3. local trade-shock papers on employment reallocation,
4. retail competition papers on entry/exit.

The paper needs to be much clearer that its object is **local nontradable-sector destruction induced by exchange-rate-driven consumer arbitrage**, not pass-through, not firm pricing, and not generic border effects.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a literature gap?
Mixed, leaning too much toward literature-gap language. The stronger framing is world-facing:
- **World question:** When a currency suddenly appreciates, do local service economies in border regions permanently lose activity because consumers can substitute abroad?
- **Weaker literature-gap framing:** No one has causally estimated establishment-level retail destruction after an exchange-rate shock.

The former is AER-relevant; the latter is field-journal language.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?
Not cleanly enough. Right now they might say:  
“It's a DiD paper on the Swiss franc shock showing retail employment fell more in border cantons.”  
That is too close to “another DiD paper about X.”

They should instead be able to say:  
“It shows exchange-rate shocks can act like trade shocks for local retail in border regions, with persistent effects on the geography of nontradable employment.”

That is a more memorable claim.

### What would make this contribution bigger?
Several possibilities:

1. **Consumer-spending reallocation, not just employment.**  
   The paper would feel larger if it connected retail job loss to actual spending shifts across borders, even descriptively. Right now the mechanism is asserted more than shown.

2. **Sharper “local nontradables are tradable at the border” framing.**  
   This is the conceptually big idea. Retail is not always nontradable when geography makes foreign substitution cheap. That could be a more general insight.

3. **Welfare/distributional angle.**  
   Who loses? Small retailers, low-wage workers, specific communities? The paper hints at this but does not develop it into a major implication.

4. **Spatial equilibrium or urban/regional angle.**  
   If the paper could say something about vacancy, town-center decline, or persistent changes in local amenity structure, the story becomes much bigger than “retail employment down 6%.”

5. **Comparison across sectors by tradability-to-consumers.**  
   A more ambitious comparison would rank sectors by consumer substitutability across borders: groceries/pharmacies/apparel vs restaurants/personal services. That would sharpen the mechanism and broaden the contribution.

The biggest missed opportunity is conceptual: the paper should be about **how exchange rates change the effective tradability of local services**.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Exchange-rate shocks are usually understood through exporters, imports, and price pass-through. Retail is often treated as a local nontradable sector whose fortunes depend on domestic demand and local competition.

### Tension
But in border regions, retail may not be meaningfully nontradable: households can arbitrage across national boundaries when relative prices change. So a macro exchange-rate shock may suddenly expose local retailers to foreign competition, with potentially persistent local consequences.

### Resolution
After the 2015 Swiss franc appreciation, more border-exposed Swiss cantons experienced sustained declines in retail employment and a shrinking retail share, consistent with consumer spending shifting abroad and domestic retail activity not recovering.

### Implications
Exchange-rate volatility can reorganize the local service economy, not just tradable sectors. For border regions, macro policy and currency movements may have lasting consequences for community commercial structure and low-skill employment.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
It has the ingredients, but the arc is only **serviceable**, not fully compelling. The paper alternates between three stories:
1. exchange rates and retail,
2. cross-border shopping,
3. hysteresis/permanent desertification.

Those stories are related, but the paper hasn’t decided which one is central. Right now the “desertification” language is doing a lot of work, perhaps too much. The safer and stronger story is:

> A macro relative-price shock made foreign retail newly contestable, causing persistent local service-sector contraction in border communities.

Then “desertification” can be the consequence, not the framing device.

So yes, there is a story, but it needs a firmer spine. At present it is still a bit of a collection of sensible results searching for the most powerful interpretation.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?
“After the franc appreciated in 2015, Swiss border cantons didn’t just see more cross-border shopping — they appear to have lost local retail employment persistently, as if an exchange-rate shock turned neighborhood retail into a tradable sector.”

That’s the interesting fact.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
Lean in — at least initially. The setting is clean and memorable, and the idea that currency shocks can hollow out local retail near borders is intuitive and potentially important.

But the second minute matters. If the presentation quickly becomes “continuous DiD using canton exposure,” attention will drop. If instead it becomes “this changes how we think about the border between tradables and nontradables,” attention stays.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Almost certainly:  
**“Is this really retail desertification, or just a temporary shift in demand/format/composition? And can you show the consumer reallocation mechanism more directly?”**

That is the key strategic vulnerability. Not an identification issue — a storytelling issue. The paper needs to anticipate that the audience wants more than reduced-form employment effects.

### If the findings are modest: is the modesty itself interesting?
Yes, but only if the persistence is credible and central. A 6% employment effect is not earth-shattering on its own. What makes it interesting is:
- it is in a sector many would not think of as exchange-rate-exposed,
- it is geographically concentrated,
- it appears persistent.

Without persistence and mechanism, the result feels like a niche Swiss local-labor-market response. With those, it becomes a meaningful conceptual contribution.
