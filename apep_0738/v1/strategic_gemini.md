### 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
The paper examines how a massive, permanent exchange rate shock (the 2015 Swiss Franc appreciation) destroys domestic retail capacity in border regions. It asks whether "non-tradable" local services become effectively tradable via consumer mobility, leading to permanent "retail desertification" even after currency pressures ease.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this well, but it leads with the institutional history rather than the economic mechanism.
**The "AER Pitch":** "While we traditionally view the retail sector as the quintessential non-tradable employer, consumer mobility across borders renders it vulnerable to exchange rate volatility. Using the 2015 Swiss Franc shock, I show that a 15% appreciation leads to a persistent 10% collapse in border retail employment that fails to recover alongside the currency. This documents a 'spatial hysteresis' where transitory monetary shocks permanently reconfigure the geography of domestic service provision."

---

### 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**One-sentence contribution:** The paper identifies the long-run hysteresis of retail employment and firm survival in response to exchange rate shocks, proving that "non-tradable" sectors face permanent structural scarring from currency volatility.

*   **Differentiation:** It moves beyond the price-pass-through focus of **Gopinath et al. (2010)** and the consumer-side scanner data of **Burstein et al. (2024)** by focusing on the *supply-side* destruction of firms.
*   **Question vs. Literature:** It currently sits between the two. To be stronger, it should frame itself as a study on the **fragility of local labor markets** in a globalized world where "distance" is no longer a shield for services.
*   **What would make it bigger?** The paper needs to show *where the money went*. If retail died, did the workers move to manufacturing? Did they become unemployed? Measuring the **labor reallocation** (the "General Equilibrium" of the canton) would transform this from a retail paper into a top-tier macro-labor paper.

---

### 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
The paper sits at the intersection of International Macro (Exchange rates) and Urban/Regional Economics.
*   **Neighbors:** **Auer et al. (2021)** (Prices), **Burstein et al. (2024)** (Consumer shopping), **Baldwin (1990)** (Hysteresis).
*   **Strategy:** It should **synthesize** the "Hysteresis in Trade" literature (usually applied to exporters) with the "Retail Competition" literature (usually applied to Big Box entry like **Jia 2008**).
*   **Missing Conversations:** The paper should speak to the **"Death of the High Street"** and e-commerce literature. Cross-border shopping is essentially a physical precursor to the Amazon effect. Connecting to the "place-based policies" literature would also increase relevance.

---

### 4. NARRATIVE ARC
*   **Setup:** Swiss retail is a stable, "non-tradable" employer protected by geography.
*   **Tension:** An overnight 15% "discount" at foreign competitors breaks the geographic shield.
*   **Resolution:** Border retail doesn't just dip; it undergoes "desertification"—firms exit and don't return.
*   **Implications:** Monetary policy has permanent, irreversible effects on the spatial distribution of the service economy.

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong. The "tension" is particularly high because of the SNB’s surprise move. The "resolution" (permanence) is the "hook" that makes it AER-worthy.

---

### 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
*   **The Lead Fact:** "When the Franc jumped 15%, border towns lost 1 in 10 retail jobs—and even when the currency came back, the shops stayed closed."
*   **Reaction:** Lean in. It challenges the "neutrality of money" in the long run and the "non-tradable" status of services.
*   **Follow-up:** "Where did the workers go?" (This is the paper’s current weak point).

---

### 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
*   **Front-load the Hysteresis:** Move the discussion of "why they didn't come back" earlier. This is more interesting than the 2015 drop itself.
*   **Appendix/Eliminate:** The "Hospitality" placebo (Table 4) is a bit weak. If people go to Germany to shop, they might eat there too. It's not a "clean" placebo. Focus more on Public Admin or Education.
*   **Buried Treasure:** The finding that employment falls *before* establishments (the intensive vs. extensive margin) is excellent. This needs its own subsection with a clear theoretical link to firm exit models.

---

### 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The science is there, and the setting is "clean." The gap is **ambition regarding the "Death of the Border Town."** 

**The Single Most Impactful Advice:** Expand the welfare and reallocation analysis. If I am the Swiss government, I don't just care that shops closed; I care if the border cantons are now "rust belts" or if they successfully pivoted to other industries. If the author can show that these retail workers ended up in lower-paying sectors or on long-term disability, it becomes a definitive paper on the **spatial costs of currency shocks.**
