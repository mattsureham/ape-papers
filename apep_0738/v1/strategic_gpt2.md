## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether a sharp exchange-rate appreciation can permanently hollow out local retail in border regions by pushing consumers to shop abroad. Using the 2015 Swiss franc appreciation, it argues that Swiss cantons more exposed to cross-border shopping experienced a persistent decline in retail employment and activity, suggesting that exchange-rate shocks can reshape the spatial distribution of nontradable services, not just tradable production.

A busy economist should care because the paper tries to move exchange-rate economics away from pass-through and exporters and toward a neglected margin: consumer arbitrage across borders causing local service-sector decline. If true, that is a real-world mechanism with implications for regional adjustment, urban economics, and the distributional effects of macro shocks.

### Does the paper articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Reasonably well, but not optimally. The current opening is vivid and readable, but it drifts too quickly into “missing piece in the literature” language and “retail is important” rather than locking onto the sharper world-level question: can exchange-rate shocks destroy local nontradable activity in places where national borders are economically porous?

The first two paragraphs should do three things more explicitly:

1. State the world question up front.
2. Make clear why Switzerland is an unusually clean and substantively important setting.
3. Frame the contribution as about **spatial reallocation of local economic activity**, not merely “retail employment fell.”

### The pitch the paper should have

“When exchange rates move sharply, do they merely change trade flows and prices, or can they permanently reallocate local economic activity across national borders? This paper studies the 2015 Swiss franc appreciation and shows that in Swiss communities with easy access to foreign shopping, the shock triggered a durable contraction in domestic retail employment and presence, consistent with consumer spending relocating abroad rather than local demand eventually rebounding.”

“That question matters because retail is usually treated as locally anchored and nontradable. But in border regions, retail is only as local as the exchange rate allows. The paper’s central claim is that a macro-financial shock can produce lasting ‘retail desertification’ in border economies, extending the consequences of exchange-rate movements beyond exporters and import prices to the geography of everyday services.”

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### Contribution in one sentence

The paper’s contribution is to show that a large exchange-rate appreciation can generate persistent decline in domestic retail activity in border regions by making cross-border consumer substitution suddenly much cheaper.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?

Partially, but not yet convincingly enough for AER-level positioning. The paper distinguishes itself from:
- exchange-rate pass-through/manufacturing adjustment papers,
- scanner-data studies of Swiss consumer responses,
- standard retail competition/entry papers.

That is directionally correct, but the introduction still sounds like a familiar “unexpected shock + border exposure + DiD” exercise unless the differentiation is sharpened.

What needs to be clearer is:
- existing Swiss franc papers mostly show **consumer behavior, prices, exports, or manufacturing adjustment**;
- this paper shows **persistent local service-sector contraction at the place level**;
- that distinction matters because it changes how we think about nontradables, local labor markets, and regional persistence.

Right now, a smart economist could still summarize it as “another reduced-form paper on the Swiss franc shock, this time for retail.” The paper needs to make it impossible to say that.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or filling a gap in a LITERATURE?

It is mixed, and it should lean much harder toward the former. The current introduction says “a missing piece connects exchange-rate shocks to the destruction of domestic retail in border regions.” That is literature-gap language. Stronger would be: “Border retail is economically tradable through consumers even when firms do not trade; exchange-rate shocks therefore may reorganize local economies in ways standard trade frameworks miss.”

That is a world claim. AER papers usually win on that dimension.

### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what's new?

Not cleanly enough. They could say:
- “It studies the 2015 Swiss franc shock.”
- “It looks at border cantons.”
- “Retail jobs fell.”

But the “what’s new” is not yet crisp enough to travel. The author wants the colleague to say:
- “It shows that exchange-rate shocks can permanently shrink supposedly nontradable local retail in border regions.”
- “So consumer mobility makes some services effectively tradable, and exchange-rate shocks have local spatial consequences.”

That is a much better takeaway.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Most importantly: connect the retail result to a broader conceptual claim.

Specific ways to make it bigger:
1. **Reframe the outcome as local economic geography, not just retail employment.**
   - Retail share is a start, but the paper needs to treat the question as one of sectoral reallocation within place.
2. **Make consumer-border arbitrage the centerpiece.**
   - The contribution becomes larger if the paper is about when “nontradables” become tradable through household mobility.
3. **Speak more directly to persistence/hysteresis.**
   - If persistence is central, the paper should organize itself around “temporary macro shock, permanent local change.”
4. **Widen the stakes beyond Switzerland.**
   - The paper should say explicitly that many European border regions, and more broadly integrated economic areas, face the same mechanism.
5. **If possible in framing, link to welfare or incidence.**
   - Not by adding a welfare theorem, but by clarifying who gains and who loses: consumers gain purchasing power, local low-skill service employment loses.

The paper does not necessarily need more regressions to sound bigger; it needs a bigger conceptual claim.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

### Closest neighbors

The likely closest neighbors are in several adjacent literatures:

1. **Swiss franc shock / exchange-rate adjustment**
   - Auer, Burstein, Lein-type work on Swiss prices and border effects
   - Kaufmann and coauthors on firm adjustment to the franc shock
   - Burstein et al. on consumer responses / scanner data / expenditure switching
2. **Exchange-rate pass-through and firm adjustment**
   - Gopinath, Itskhoki, Amiti, and related work
3. **Retail competition / entry / local market structure**
   - Jia (Walmart entry)
   - Ellickson and related retail market papers
4. **Spatial economics / local exposure to trade or border shocks**
   - Papers on border discontinuities, consumer shopping across borders, and local labor-market adjustment
5. **Hysteresis / sunk-cost adjustment**
   - Baldwin; Dixit

### How should the paper position itself relative to those neighbors?

Mostly **build on** and **bridge**, not attack.

- Relative to exchange-rate papers: “They show prices, trade, and firm-side adjustment; I show a neglected place-based service-sector margin.”
- Relative to retail papers: “They study domestic competition and entry; I show that foreign retail access, activated by exchange rates, can reshape local retail markets.”
- Relative to hysteresis papers: “I provide place-level evidence consistent with persistence after a temporary relative-price shock.”
- Relative to spatial/local labor papers: “I bring macro shocks into the geography of everyday service employment.”

The paper should not overclaim that nobody has studied cross-border shopping or border retail before; that is unlikely to be literally true and weakens credibility. It should claim instead that the paper provides unusually broad evidence on **persistent establishment and employment adjustment** after a large macro shock.

### Is the paper currently positioned too narrowly or too broadly?

Slightly too narrowly in empirical framing, and slightly too broadly in theoretical ambition.

Too narrow because it often reads as “Swiss border retail after 2015.”  
Too broad because it gestures at grand claims about exchange-rate persistence and hysteresis without fully earning them in the narrative.

The sweet spot is:
- not “a Swiss case study,”
- not “a general theory of hysteresis,”
- but “a clean, highly policy-relevant demonstration that exchange-rate shocks can permanently reorganize local service economies when consumers can arbitrage borders.”

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?

It seems underconnected to:
- **urban/regional economics** on local amenity and commercial composition,
- **consumer mobility / shopping geography** literature,
- **border economics** beyond trade flows,
- **local labor market adjustment** and place-based persistence,
- potentially **European integration / tax and price-border shopping** papers.

At present, the paper speaks mostly to international macro/trade. That is too limited for its best version.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?

Not quite. The current conversation is “exchange-rate shocks affect retail.” The better conversation is “national macro shocks can alter the geography of local nontradable sectors when borders are porous to consumers.”

That broader conversation is more interesting and more AER-relevant.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Retail is generally thought of as locally anchored and largely nontradable, while exchange-rate research has focused on exporters, import prices, and manufacturing.

### Tension

In border regions, that intuition may fail: households can arbitrage national price differences by physically moving their shopping across the border. So a macro shock that changes relative prices may act like an import-competition shock for local retail, potentially with persistent place-based consequences.

### Resolution

Following the 2015 Swiss franc appreciation, more border-exposed Swiss cantons saw persistent declines in retail employment and retail presence relative to less-exposed areas, with patterns consistent with local retail contraction rather than general local decline.

### Implications

Exchange-rate shocks can reshape the geography of nontradable services, not just tradable production. This affects how economists think about regional adjustment, consumer arbitrage, and the distributional consequences of exchange-rate regimes.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the bones of one, but it is not fully disciplined. Right now it is somewhere between a real narrative and a collection of sensible results.

The strongest story is:
1. Retail is usually local.
2. Borders make it conditionally tradable via consumers.
3. The Swiss shock cleanly activated that channel.
4. The effect was not just immediate but persistent.
5. Therefore macro shocks can durably reorganize local service economies.

The paper instead sometimes slips into:
- result narration,
- identification reassurance,
- generic contribution lists.

For an AER audience, the narrative needs to be more conceptual and less procedural.

### If it is a collection of results looking for a story, what story should it be telling?

“Consumer mobility turns local retail into a tradable sector in border regions; a temporary exchange-rate shock can therefore create lasting local service-sector decline.”

That is the story.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?

“A one-day appreciation of the Swiss franc appears to have permanently reduced retail employment in Swiss border areas—because consumers simply moved their shopping abroad.”

That is a good lead fact.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

Lean in, initially. The setting is memorable, the mechanism is intuitive, and the result has bite. But interest will depend on whether the presenter can immediately explain why this is more than a Swiss border curiosity.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Probably one of these:
- “Is this really about retail becoming tradable through consumer mobility?”
- “How much of this is a permanent reallocation of spending versus temporary demand substitution?”
- “Why didn’t activity come back when the exchange rate partially reversed?”
- “Is this a border-specific Swiss story, or should I update my broader beliefs?”

That last question is the key one. The paper needs to answer it in the introduction and conclusion, not force the reader to infer it.

### If the findings are modest: is the modesty itself interesting?

The establishment result is modest; the employment result is more compelling. The paper is smart to lean on employment and persistence. But the term “retail desertification” is somewhat larger than the result as currently presented. A 6–10% employment decline in highly exposed areas is important, but the label risks overselling unless the paper more carefully defines what “desertification” means.

This is not a failed experiment. It has a real and interesting finding. The danger is not nullity; it is mismatch between rhetoric and scale.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

This is mostly a **framing and ambition problem**, with some **scope** concerns.

Not primarily a methods problem from an editorial-positioning standpoint. The empirical design is intelligible and publishable somewhere. The issue is whether the paper currently feels like an AER paper rather than a solid field-journal paper.

### The gap between current form and an AER paper

#### 1. Framing problem
The paper is still framed too much as a Swiss natural experiment and not enough as a broader statement about how macro shocks affect local service economies.

#### 2. Scope problem
The current outcome set is a bit narrow for the rhetorical ambition. If the paper wants to claim “retail desertification,” it needs to keep the focus on persistent place-level reallocation, not just one sectoral employment coefficient.

#### 3. Novelty problem
The setting is famous. Many economists have seen papers on the Swiss franc shock. So the author must work harder than usual to show that this paper changes beliefs rather than merely extending the setting to another outcome.

#### 4. Ambition problem
The paper is competent and professional, but somewhat safe. It does not yet fully cash out the bigger implication that **consumer mobility can endogenize the tradability of local sectors**.

### Single most impactful piece of advice

Reframe the paper around the idea that **exchange-rate shocks can permanently reallocate supposedly nontradable local services across borders through consumer mobility**, and organize every part of the introduction and discussion around that world-level claim rather than around the existence of another clean DiD in Switzerland.
