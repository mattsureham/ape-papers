**Summary** — The paper estimates the impact of Brazil’s 2007 FUNDEB reform on municipal education spending, exploiting the fact that complementação transfers targeted ten states whose pre-reform per-pupil spending fell below a federal floor. Using SICONFI municipal finance data for 4,204 municipalities (2002–2011), the authors implement a two-way fixed effects DiD and find a 12.9 percent increase in log education spending for treated municipalities, accompanied by null effects on secondary spending shares and education’s budget share. Pre-trends, placebo health spending results, and robustness checks are offered in support of a credible causal interpretation.

**Essential Points**  
1. **Mismatch with the approved research question.** The paper needs to explain why it abandoned the original idea of studying FUNDEB’s gender-differentiated effects on secondary schooling and shifted to a general fiscal pass-through analysis. Because the manuscript’s contribution is fundamentally different from the proposal, the authors should either 1) reframe the submission to fit the new research question transparently, or 2) deliver the promised exploration of female secondary outcomes with the continuous treatment strategy described in the manifest. Without this clarification, it is hard to assess the paper’s contribution in the context of the call.  
2. **State-level shocks and limited clusters.** Treatment is effectively assigned at the state level (10 treated vs. 17 controls), causing the identifying variation to come from 27 clusters. While the event study shows balanced pre-trends, the FE specification does not flexibly account for state-specific shocks after 2007 (e.g., regional economic programs or other federal transfers). The authors should either control for state-specific time trends or include state-by-year covariates that could capture contemporaneous shocks. They should also consider whether inference is reliable with only 27 clusters—state-level wild bootstrap or randomization inference would provide more credible p-values.  
3. **Interpretation of the spending increase relative to treatment intensity.** The paper reports a 12.9 percent increase in municipal education spending but never benchmarks this change against the actual transfer amounts. Given that complementação varied by state and was designed to close a per-student gap, the mechanism seems better understood as a continuous funding floor rather than a binary shock. The authors should relate the spending response to the estimated per-pupil shortfall (or the transfer amount) to check whether richer transfers generated larger responses and to evaluate fiscal pass-through more precisely. Without this, the policy interpretation remains incomplete.

**Suggestions**  
- **Clarify the connection to the policy idea and/or reframe.** If the final paper is to remain focused on municipal spending, briefly summarize the original idea (gendered schooling effects) in the introduction and explain why the current analysis offers a better use of the data/policy variation. If the authors still plan to explore gendered outcomes, the paper should integrate INEP School Census data as promised: construct female/male secondary enrollment/completion rates, exploit the pre-reform reverse gender gap, and run the continuous treatment DiD (Gap × Post) that the manifest describes. That exercise would more directly address the research gap noted in the proposal, and it would make better use of FUNDEB’s extension to secondary education.  
- **Strengthen the DiD by addressing state-level time-varying confounders.** Because treatment is determined by states’ 2006 spending levels, it is plausible that treated states also differed systematically in post-2006 time trends (economic growth, demographic shifts, other federal programs). Including state-by-year linear trends or interactions with observable covariates (e.g., state GDP growth, population changes, or political variables) could help guard against such confounding. Alternatively, a synthetic control at the state level (or a weighted DiD with only treated vs. carefully matched control states) would allow treatment on more aggregate units, aligning with the coarse assignment. Clearly reporting how sensitive the estimates are to these specifications would bolster credibility.  
- **Assess response heterogeneity by treatment intensity.** Even though the analysis now uses binary treatment, the complementação transfers depended on the magnitude of the pre-reform per-pupil gap. The authors could exploit that variation by interacting the post indicator with the expected funding gap (or a state-level measure of the federal transfer per student) to estimate a dose–response. This would illuminate whether municipalities that needed more equalization responded more strongly, providing a richer mechanism story. At minimum, plotting the estimated effect against state-level transfer amounts or per-pupil gaps would demonstrate whether the 12.9 percent effect scales with treatment intensity.  
- **Compare expenditure responses to the size of the federal transfer.** Provide a back-of-the-envelope that compares the observed increase in municipal spending to the actual complementação inflows. For example, if treated municipalities received X percent more revenue from complementação, does the 12.9 percent spending increase imply full pass-through, partial, or more than one-to-one? This would also help interpret why education’s budget share remained flat—if complementação expanded total revenues substantially, then a flat share implies the canonical fiscal reaction function.  
- **Address concerns about selection into the balanced panel.** The sample drops municipalities with missing data, possibly skewing toward larger, better-managed units. A descriptive table comparing included vs. excluded municipalities (e.g., by population, region, pre-2007 spending) would show whether attrition biases the estimation. If differences exist, consider estimating the same DiD on the unbalanced panel or using inverse probability weighting to account for the missingness mechanism.  
- **Supplement the placebo analysis.** The health-spending placebo is a helpful sanity check. It would strengthen the empirical claim further to show that other education-related outcomes remain unaffected by complementação when they are not linked to municipal responsibility (e.g., state-level secondary schooling inputs). Another useful placebo is to test whether treated and control municipalities showed different trends in other municipal functions (e.g., sewage or infrastructure) post-2007.  
- **Explain the delay in treatment effects.** The event study shows that the spending increase materializes only after 2007, peaking in 2011. The paper should more explicitly tie this to the disbursement schedule of complementação (possibly including a figure showing the timing of federal transfers) and discuss whether this delay might bias annual averages. Does the effect continue beyond 2011? Mention that the available data limits the analysis to the early years of FUNDEB and whether spending responses might grow further once transfers become fully institutionalized.  
- **Improve discussion of limitations and generalizability.** The limitation section should clearly state that the findings pertain to municipalities within treated states and may not generalize to states that never qualified for complementação. Additionally, the small number of treated states restricts the ability to generalize to other federations. A brief discussion of how Brazilian constitutional mandates (education share floor) interact with intergovernmental grants would help readers understand the broader applicability of the flypaper interpretation.

By addressing these points, the paper would present a more coherent, policy-relevant argument, either by delivering on the originally proposed gender-equality analysis or by more rigorously justifying and interpreting the current fiscal-expenditure focus.
