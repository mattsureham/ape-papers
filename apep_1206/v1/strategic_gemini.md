### 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether subnational corporate tax competition generates real economic growth or merely induces firms to shift their legal addresses. Using a high-resolution census of Swiss establishments, the author shows that municipal tax cuts increase the number of registered firms but have zero effect on total employment, as the new entities are overwhelmingly "employment-light" letterbox companies in the service sector. The paper suggests that local tax competition is a zero-sum game of legal relabeling rather than a driver of regional development.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this pitch excellently. The opening contrast between Zug and Zurich (Page 2) is a "hook" that immediately grounds a dry tax question in a striking real-world puzzle. The first two paragraphs successfully bridge the gap between abstract tax competition theory and the concrete "letterbox" phenomenon.

---

### 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution:** The paper provides the first subnational evidence that corporate tax competition induces a compositional shift toward paper entities (letterboxes) rather than attracting real productive activity.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It is well-differentiated from Krapf and Staubli (2021) by moving beyond "taxable income" (an accounting result) to "employment per establishment" (a physical reality).
*   **Question vs. Literature:** It frames itself as answering a question about the **WORLD** (does tax competition work?), which is its primary strength. 
*   **"Another DiD?":** A smart economist would see this as more than "another DiD." The "extensive vs. intensive" margin decomposition applied to the letterbox problem is a clever, intuitive "trick" that makes the paper feel like a discovery rather than just a measurement.
*   **Making it bigger:** To truly elevate this, the author needs to connect the "zero-sum" finding to the **fiscal externality** on other municipalities. If I move my letterbox to a low-tax town, who did I move it *from*? Quantifying the "siphoned" revenue from neighbors would turn this from a local case study into a general equilibrium statement.

---

### 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

*   **Closest Neighbors:** Brülhart and Jametti (2012) on Swiss tax competition; Tørsløv, Wier, and Zucman (2023) on international profit shifting; Giroud and Rauh (2019) on state taxes and reallocation.
*   **Strategy:** The paper should **synthesize** the international profit-shifting literature with the local public finance literature. Currently, these two "camps" don't talk enough. This paper is the bridge.
*   **Unexpected Connection:** The paper could speak more to the **Urban/Agglomeration** literature. If tax cuts only attract letterboxes, it suggests that "real" firms are anchored by agglomeration economies that taxes can't easily break. This adds a "Why?" to the "What?".

---

### 4. NARRATIVE ARC

*   **Setup:** The Tiebout/Zodrow-Mieszkowski world where capital moves to efficient jurisdictions.
*   **Tension:** If capital is just "paper," the efficiency gains of tax competition vanish, leaving only a race to the bottom.
*   **Resolution:** Evidence shows a 2% rise in firms but a 0% rise in workers. The "growth" is an illusion of ink and paper.
*   **Implications:** Subnational tax floors (Pillar Two at the local level) are not just a global concern but a domestic necessity.

**Evaluation:** The narrative arc is very strong. It follows a "Detective Story" structure: we see the bodies (registrations), but where are the people (workers)? 

---

### 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

*   **The Lead Fact:** "In Switzerland, a 5-point tax cut gets you 2% more companies but exactly zero more jobs."
*   **The Reaction:** People will lean in. Every policymaker believes tax cuts bring jobs; proving they only bring letterheads is a "status-quo-challenging" result.
*   **Follow-up:** "Does the municipality at least make money on the tax revenue from those letterboxes?" This is the missing link the author should address more forcefully.

---

### 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

*   **Front-loading:** The paper is well-structured. Table 2 is the "money" table and it appears early (Page 7).
*   **Appendix:** The "Standardized Effect Sizes" (Table 5) are helpful but belong in the main text or a discussion of magnitudes.
*   **Refinement:** The "Natural-person tax cuts" placebo (Page 8) is excellent and should be highlighted even more in the intro as the "smoking gun" for identification.

---

### 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The paper is highly competent but currently feels like a very "clean" *Journal of Public Economics* paper. To make it **AER**, it needs more **ambition regarding the "Zero-Sum" claim.**

**Single most impactful advice:** Don't just show that one town didn't gain jobs; show that the *neighboring* towns lost the registrations. If the author can use the STATENT data to track "origin-destination" pairs of firm registrations, proving that Municipality A's gain is Municipality B's loss, the paper moves from a "local elasticity study" to a "fundamental critique of fiscal federalism."
