## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple but important question: when local governments cut corporate taxes, do they attract real economic activity or mostly legal entities on paper? Using Swiss municipality-level data that separately measure the number of establishments and total employment, the paper argues that tax cuts increase firm registrations but not jobs, suggesting that a meaningful share of subnational tax competition is about attracting tax domicile rather than production.

A busy economist should care because this goes to the core welfare meaning of tax competition. If low taxes pull in plants, workers, and investment, that is one model of interjurisdictional competition; if they mostly pull in shells, holding companies, and reported profits, that is a very different model with very different incidence and policy implications.

### Does the paper articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Reasonably well, but not optimally. The current opening is vivid, but it starts with a descriptive contrast between Zug and Zurich that feels a bit journalistic and potentially invites immediate skepticism about cross-place comparisons before the paper has earned the reader’s trust. The introduction then gets pulled quickly into classic tax competition theory and international profit shifting. That is all relevant, but the paper’s actual comparative advantage is more direct: it has a clean, intuitive way to distinguish “more firms” from “more jobs per firm.”

The first two paragraphs should do less scene-setting and more thesis-setting. They should foreground the conceptual distinction, the measurement innovation, and the headline finding.

### The pitch the paper should have

When subnational governments cut corporate taxes, what actually moves: production or paperwork? This paper studies Swiss municipalities and uses a simple but powerful decomposition—firm registrations versus employment per establishment—to show that local corporate tax cuts increase the number of establishments without increasing total employment, implying that the marginal entities attracted by tax cuts are unusually employment-light.

This matters because the welfare and policy implications of tax competition depend on whether low-tax jurisdictions attract real activity or just taxable presence. The paper’s core message is that a nontrivial portion of local tax competition appears to reallocate legal domicile and reported tax base rather than workers and production.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper’s contribution is to show, in a subnational setting, that municipal corporate tax cuts appear to attract firm registrations but not jobs, implying that tax competition may operate importantly through the relocation of employment-light entities rather than real activity.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?

Only partially. The paper claims “the first subnational test” of real activity versus letterbox entities, which is directionally plausible, but the differentiation is not yet sharp enough relative to adjacent literatures on:

- Swiss local tax competition and firm location/tax base responses
- U.S. state/local business tax effects on establishments, employment, and income
- international profit shifting and tax haven papers
- broader tax-base mobility work

Right now the introduction risks sounding like: “we take a standard local tax-response question and use a clever outcome ratio.” That may indeed be the right contribution, but then it needs to own that and define exactly why this decomposition changes what we learn relative to existing work.

The paper should say more explicitly:
- prior subnational papers show tax cuts affect firms, taxable income, or local activity;
- they generally cannot separate whether the marginal response is real expansion or legal relabeling;
- this paper’s novelty is that it uses establishment counts jointly with employment to identify the composition of inflows.

That is a clean point. It needs to be made more crisply and with fewer “first” claims.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or filling a gap in a LITERATURE?

Mostly about the world, which is good. The best version is: “What moves when taxes fall?” not “the literature has not yet examined X.” The paper is strongest when it is asking how tax competition works in reality. It gets weaker when it leans too hard on “first subnational test” language.

### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what’s new?

Yes, but not as confidently as one would want for AER. At present the summary would be something like: “It’s a Swiss municipal tax paper showing more establishments but no more employment after tax cuts, so maybe these are shell firms.” That is decent, but it still sounds a bit like “another DiD paper about local taxes,” just with an interesting ratio outcome.

To become memorable, the paper needs a sharper phrase-level contribution:
- “Tax cuts attract taxable presence, not labor demand.”
- “Subnational tax competition relocates legal domicile more than production.”
- “The missing margin in local tax competition is employment intensity.”

### What would make this contribution bigger?

A few concrete possibilities:

1. **Tie the findings more directly to tax-base mobility rather than “letterbox companies” per se.**  
   “Letterbox” is intuitive, but it is also narrower and more speculative than the evidence directly supports. The broader and stronger contribution is about the composition of entities attracted by tax cuts—employment-light, tax-sensitive firms. That framing scales better.

2. **Connect the establishment/employment decomposition to fiscal stakes.**  
   Right now the paper tells us about composition, but not much about why this matters quantitatively. If the authors can show that these municipalities gain corporate tax base or corporate counts disproportionately relative to jobs, the story becomes much bigger: taxes move tax base without moving labor. That would elevate the contribution substantially.

3. **Show where within services the effects are concentrated.**  
   Tertiary is too broad for top-journal storytelling. If the effect is concentrated in finance, holding, management, real estate, headquarters, or business services, the mechanism becomes much more persuasive and more novel.

4. **Reframe from “Do tax cuts work?” to “What is the margin of response?”**  
   The big contribution is not that taxes affect firms—that is known. It is that the margin of adjustment matters and has been misunderstood.

- International tax papers have shown that profits can move without production.
- This paper connects those conversations by showing that even within a country, under common national law, local tax competition may attract entities that are organizationally mobile but labor-light.

That is a synthesis contribution. It is strongest when positioned as a bridge between local public finance and international taxation.

A bit of both.

Two areas feel underdeveloped:

   The paper needs to engage more fully with work on U.S. state/local taxes and firm location/activity. Otherwise readers may think the authors are reinventing a conversation that already happened elsewhere.

Also, the paper might benefit from connecting to the literature on **taxable presence versus real presence**, which is conceptually broader than “letterbox companies.”

### Is the paper having the right conversation?

Almost. The current conversation is “tax competition versus shell companies,” which is interesting but a bit sensationalized. The more durable conversation is:

**What exactly is mobile in response to subnational corporate taxes—jobs, establishments, tax base, or legal domicile?**

That is the right conversation for a broad economics audience.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Subnational governments compete on corporate taxes, and economists debate whether this competition reallocates productive capital and labor or mainly shifts paper profits and registered entities.

### Tension

Existing evidence often shows that tax bases or firms respond to taxes, but those responses are hard to interpret: are places actually attracting real economic activity, or are they mostly attracting entities that bring little employment? That distinction is central for welfare, but hard to observe.

### Resolution

Using Swiss data that separately track establishment counts and employment, the paper finds that municipal corporate tax cuts increase establishments without increasing total employment, lowering employment per establishment and concentrating effects in services.

### Implications

If that interpretation is correct, then local tax competition can change where firms are registered and where profits are reported without materially changing where people work. That weakens standard “growth and jobs” justifications for local corporate tax cuts and changes how we should think about the efficiency and incidence of decentralized business taxation.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

Yes, more than many papers in this genre. There is a real setup–tension–resolution structure. That said, the narrative is somewhat undermined by two choices:

1. **The paper gets too technical too early about Swiss institutional specifics.**
2. **It overstates the specificity of the mechanism (“letterbox companies”) relative to the direct evidence.**

The results clearly show “more establishments, not more jobs.” That is a strong narrative fact. The paper should let that fact carry more of the weight and be a bit more disciplined in the interpretation. Right now it sometimes reads like a collection of suggestive facts assembled to support a vivid label.

### What story should it be telling?

Not “we caught municipalities attracting shell companies.”  
Rather:

**“The response of firms to local corporate tax cuts is compositionally different from what policymakers often imply: taxes move registrations much more than employment.”**

That is the AER-grade story. It is broader, cleaner, and more durable.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would you lead with?

“When Swiss municipalities cut corporate taxes, they get more firm registrations but no more jobs.”

That is the line.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

They would lean in—initially. It is intuitively sharp and policy-relevant. But the very next question would be: “Are these really shells, or just small service firms?” The paper needs to be ready for that. That is the live margin on whether this is merely a neat local public finance paper or something more important.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Probably one of these:

- “Do these entities bring profits/tax base even if they don’t bring jobs?”
- “Which sectors or legal forms are driving this?”
- “How much of the measured response is actual new entry versus re-registration from nearby municipalities?”
- “Is this a Swiss oddity, or a general feature of local tax competition?”

That tells you what the paper is still missing strategically: the audience wants either a tighter mechanism or a bigger implication.

### If the findings are modest: is the modesty itself okay?

Yes, potentially. The point estimate on total employment per establishment is not huge, but the substantive contrast between establishments and employment is what matters. This is not a “failed jobs effect” paper; it is a composition paper. The paper should lean into that. The null on employment is not a disappointment—it is the main fact.

But the authors need to stop apologizing for the modest magnitudes and instead emphasize the conceptual wedge. Even small changes in average employment intensity can imply a stark difference between attracting labor demand and attracting taxable presence.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The gap is not mainly competence; it is ambition and framing.

### Is it a framing problem?

Yes, significantly. The paper has a clean and interesting fact, but it is not yet framed at the highest level. “Letterbox companies” is catchy, but the broader and more important point is about the margin of tax-base mobility in local public finance.

### Is it a scope problem?

Yes, somewhat. For AER, the current scope feels narrow: two cantons, 25 treated municipalities, one main decomposition, broad tertiary/secondary mechanism. That may be enough for a strong field journal if the execution is airtight, but for AER the paper likely needs either:
- broader coverage,
- richer outcome decomposition,
- clearer connection to fiscal consequences,
- or a much more compelling mechanism.

### Is it a novelty problem?

Not exactly, but there is novelty risk. The broad question “do business tax cuts create real activity?” is not new. The novelty lies in the decomposition between establishment counts and employment intensity. That is real, but the authors need to defend why this decomposition materially changes what we know, rather than just repackaging familiar tax-response evidence.

### Is it an ambition problem?

Yes. The paper currently feels like a smart, well-targeted empirical note. AER papers usually either settle a major debate, open a new one, or offer a strikingly generalizable insight. This paper is close to a strong insight but not yet fully scaled to that level.

### Single most impactful advice

**Reframe the paper around the broader claim that subnational corporate tax cuts move taxable presence more than labor demand, and then strengthen that claim with richer evidence on the type of entities or tax base being attracted.**

That is the path from “nice Swiss tax paper” to “paper people in public finance and macro-public will cite when talking about what tax competition actually does.”
