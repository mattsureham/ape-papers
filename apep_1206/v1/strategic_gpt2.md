## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple and important question: when local governments cut corporate taxes, do they attract real economic activity or mostly paper entities that bring profits without jobs? Using Swiss municipal tax changes and establishment-level census counts, the paper argues that tax cuts increase the number of registered establishments but not employment, suggesting that subnational tax competition partly reallocates legal domiciles rather than production.

A busy economist should care because this goes to the core welfare question in tax competition. The standard policy debate is not whether tax rates move firms on paper, but whether low-tax jurisdictions actually attract workers, capital, and output.

**Does the paper articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Almost, but not quite. The current opening is vivid and better than average, but it quickly slips into a literature-and-theory setup before fully locking in the paper’s central empirical fact and why that fact matters. The opening should be less “tax competition theory exists” and more “the welfare implications hinge on whether what moves is jobs or just registrations.”

**What the first two paragraphs should say instead:**

> Municipal governments around the world cut corporate taxes to attract business. But whether these tax cuts improve economic efficiency or merely reshuffle tax bases depends on what actually relocates: real activity with workers and investment, or paper entities with a mailing address and little else.  
>   
> This paper studies that distinction in one of the world’s most decentralized tax systems: Swiss municipalities. Using annual municipal tax changes and census data that separately measure the number of establishments and the number of employees, I show that corporate tax cuts increase firm registrations but not employment. The main margin of response is therefore a decline in employment per establishment, concentrated in services, consistent with municipalities attracting letterbox companies rather than productive activity.

That is the pitch. It is world-first enough, concrete enough, and policy-relevant enough.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:**  
The paper shows that municipal corporate tax cuts in Switzerland increase establishment registrations without increasing employment, implying that a substantial share of subnational tax competition works through the relocation of employment-light legal entities rather than real economic activity.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?
Partially, but not sharply enough. The introduction names broad literatures, but the differentiation is still somewhat generic: international profit shifting looks at countries, Swiss tax competition papers study taxable income or location responses, and this paper decomposes establishments versus employment. That is a real distinction, but the paper needs to sharpen exactly what prior papers could not tell us and what this paper newly reveals.

Right now, the contribution risks sounding like: “another reduced-form tax competition paper, but with a nice outcome variable.” To become AER-credible, it needs to sound like: “existing evidence cannot distinguish real relocation from legal relabeling; this paper directly measures that distinction in a setting where the legal margin is plausibly important.”

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or as filling a gap in a LITERATURE?
It is mixed. The stronger parts frame it as a world question: what actually moves when taxes fall? The weaker parts lapse into gap-filling: “first subnational test,” “contributes to three literatures,” “resolves ambiguity.” The paper should lean much more on the world question. AER wants papers that change how we think about an economic phenomenon, not papers that “add a decomposition” to a literature.

### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what’s new?
Yes, but only barely. The best version of the colleague summary is:  
“Interesting paper: Swiss local tax cuts seem to attract registrations, not jobs, so subnational tax competition may be mostly about paper firms.”

The riskier version is:  
“It’s a DiD on municipal tax cuts with establishments and employment.”

The difference depends entirely on framing. The underlying result is potentially interesting; the current rhetoric has not yet elevated it into a big conceptual point.

### What would make this contribution bigger?
Several possibilities:

1. **A more direct tax-base outcome.**  
   If the paper could connect the increase in registrations to taxable profits, corporate tax revenues, or some measure of reported profits, it would immediately become much bigger. Right now the story is “more establishments, no jobs.” The stronger story is “more establishments, no jobs, but substantial reported tax base,” which would directly establish the paper-entity mechanism rather than infer it.

2. **A cleaner link to the welfare stakes.**  
   The paper should speak more directly to when this kind of competition is socially wasteful versus benign. If all that moves is legal domicile, the classic tax competition story changes. This is the big idea. The paper only hints at it.

3. **A more decisive comparison with real-margin outcomes.**  
   Not just employment, but wages, commuting flows, local commercial rents, business premises, or investment. The more “real activity” margins remain flat, the more the paper becomes a fundamental statement about what local tax competition does.

4. **A more ambitious framing beyond Switzerland.**  
   The current framing is a bit “Swiss municipalities are neat.” The bigger framing is: in a world of increasingly mobile intangible capital, local tax competition may be competition for legal residence rather than production.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Jurisdictions cut corporate taxes to attract firms, and economists debate the welfare consequences of tax competition. But those consequences depend on whether lower taxes attract real production or simply taxable entities on paper.

### Tension
Most evidence shows that tax bases and firm counts respond to taxation, but it is often unclear whether this reflects jobs and production moving or only legal/booking structures moving. In a decentralized country like Switzerland, with local tax-setting and easy municipal relocation of legal domicile, that distinction is especially salient.

### Resolution
Municipal tax cuts increase establishment counts but not employment, lowering employment per establishment, with stronger effects in services. The pattern is consistent with tax competition attracting employment-light entities rather than real activity.

### Implications
If true, some local tax competition is closer to paper-base poaching than productive reallocation. That changes the welfare interpretation of local tax cuts and the way economists should think about subnational competition in an intangible economy.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
Yes, more than many papers do. This is one of the manuscript’s strengths. There is a real story here. But the narrative is still not fully disciplined. The paper occasionally reads like:
- an institutional note on Swiss taxes,
- a DiD study of establishment composition,
- a contribution to profit shifting,
- and a policy comment on OECD reform.

That diffuses the arc. The story should be singular:

> “Tax competition is only socially meaningful if it moves real activity. Here we show that at the municipal level it mainly moves registrations.”

Everything else should serve that story.

### Is it a collection of results looking for a story?
Not quite. There is a story. But the story is doing too much inference from a limited set of outcomes. The sectoral heterogeneity and establishment/employment decomposition are useful, but the paper still needs to avoid over-selling “letterbox companies” as if directly observed. The narrative should say “consistent with employment-light legal entities” more often than “we show letterbox companies moved.”

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?
“Swiss municipal corporate tax cuts raise firm registrations by about 2 percent but do not raise employment at all.”

That is the headline fact. It is clear, memorable, and consequential.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
Among public finance, urban, and international tax economists: lean in. Among the general AER audience: some would lean in, but only if the framing makes clear why this is a first-order welfare distinction and not just a Swiss institutional curiosity.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Immediately:  
**“If not jobs, then what exactly is moving—reported profits, legal domicile, headquarters, or simply reporting units?”**

That is the crucial question. Right now the paper has a suggestive answer, not a definitive one. This is the main strategic vulnerability.

A second likely question:  
**“How much should we generalize from two cantons in Switzerland?”**

Again: the answer must be conceptual rather than representational. The paper need not claim Swiss representativeness; it should claim this is a clean setting illustrating an economically important margin.

### If the findings are modest, is the modesty itself interesting?
Yes, but only because of the asymmetry across outcomes. “Small increase in registrations, zero employment effect” is interesting because it says the margin that moves is composition, not scale. If the paper presents this as “only 0.12 workers per establishment” it risks shrinking its own contribution. The economically interesting point is not the small absolute number; it is the disconnect between establishment counts and employment.
