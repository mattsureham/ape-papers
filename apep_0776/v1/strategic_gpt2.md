## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper studies whether abruptly forcing older people to work longer can increase mortality. It uses Italy’s 2011 Fornero pension reform—which sharply raised retirement ages, especially for women—and regional variation in how much older-worker employment rose after the reform to argue that places with greater reform “bite” saw a larger increase in female mortality relative to male mortality among ages 55–64.

A busy economist should care because governments across Europe are raising retirement ages right now, and the central policy question is not just fiscal sustainability but whether delayed retirement carries real health costs.

**Does the paper itself articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Not well enough. The current opening is vivid but too institutional and too Italy-specific too soon. It leads with the legislative drama and the *esodati* episode rather than the broad economic question. The paper’s actual sell is not “here is a dramatic reform episode,” but “raising retirement ages may save budgets while harming health, and here is unusually sharp quasi-experimental evidence from a major reform.”

**What the first two paragraphs should say instead:**

> Across advanced economies, population aging is pushing governments to raise retirement ages. The fiscal logic is clear, but the welfare consequences are not: if delayed retirement keeps older workers in stressful or physically demanding jobs, pension reform may improve public finances at the cost of worse health and higher mortality. Evidence on this question remains mixed, in part because most retirement reforms are gradual and difficult to distinguish from broader trends in health and labor markets.
>
> This paper studies one of the sharpest pension reforms in Europe: Italy’s 2011 Fornero reform, which abruptly increased retirement ages, with much larger increases for women than for men. I combine this gender asymmetry with regional variation in how strongly the reform increased employment among older workers to ask whether forced work retention increased mortality at ages 55–64. The core finding is that regions more exposed to the reform saw a larger rise in female mortality relative to male mortality, suggesting that abrupt pension austerity can have measurable health costs.

That is the pitch. The paper should get there immediately.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution in one sentence:**  
The paper claims to show that an abrupt increase in retirement ages in Italy raised mortality among near-retirement women relative to men in regions where the reform more strongly increased older-worker employment.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest papers?
Only partially. The paper cites the retirement-health literature, but the differentiation is still generic: “I study delayed retirement rather than early retirement” and “I use gender asymmetry within regions.” That is not yet a sufficiently sharp statement of novelty for AER-level positioning.

The author needs to be much clearer on exactly what is new relative to:

- papers on retirement and mortality/health using pension eligibility rules,
- papers on retirement and cognition in Europe/Italy,
- papers on labor market shocks and mortality,
- papers on pension reform and employment effects.

Right now, a smart economist could summarize it as: **“another retirement-health paper using pension reform variation, this time in Italy and with a DDD.”** That is not enough.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a gap in the literature?
It oscillates, but too often it sounds like gap-filling. The stronger framing is clearly the world question:

- **What are the health costs of raising retirement ages abruptly?**
- **Do pension reforms have mortality consequences, not just labor-supply consequences?**
- **Is policy design speed/abruptness itself consequential?**

That is much stronger than “the literature has not studied a reform that raised ages in this way.”

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new?
Not crisply, yet. They would probably say: “It’s a DiD/DDD paper on the Fornero reform and mortality.” That is a warning sign. For AER, the novelty needs to be translatable in one line:

- “This is evidence that pension austerity can kill, not just delay retirement.”
- or “Abrupt retirement-age hikes have mortality costs concentrated in the more-exposed gender.”
- or “The health effects of pension reform depend on whether workers are forced to remain attached rather than allowed to exit.”

### What would make the contribution bigger?
Several possibilities; any one of these would enlarge the paper substantially:

1. **Mechanisms via cause-specific mortality.**  
   This is the biggest missing ingredient. If the paper could show increases in cardiovascular deaths, suicides, alcohol-related deaths, or stress-related causes, it would move from “interesting reduced form” to “important substantive insight.” Without mechanism, the paper risks feeling like a provocative but blunt mortality correlation.

2. **Link the result to policy design, not just one reform.**  
   The most scalable contribution is not “Italy did X.” It is “abrupt and non-grandfathered retirement-age increases are different from gradual reforms.” The paper hints at this, but does not build the framing around it.

3. **Go beyond female-vs-male relative mortality.**  
   Relative effects are useful for design, but the broad audience will want the paper’s central claim to be about human welfare, not just a gender differential. The paper needs a stronger way to express the implied overall mortality burden, or at least the welfare interpretation of the relative result.

4. **Heterogeneity by occupation, region type, or labor-market structure.**  
   If the effect is strongest in regions with more physically demanding sectors, weaker public-sector presence, or higher pre-reform older-worker attachment, the paper becomes much more about how work harms health.

5. **Connect to political economy/austerity.**  
   There is a bigger paper hidden here: not merely retirement and health, but the human costs of crisis-driven austerity reforms. Right now that connection is gestured at but underdeveloped.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

### Closest neighbors

- **Bloemen, Hochguertel, and Zweerink (2017)** on early retirement and mortality in the Netherlands.

- **Mazzonna and Peracchi (2017)** on retirement and cognition in Italy/Europe.
- **Fitzpatrick and Moore (2018)** on mortality around Social Security eligibility.
- Also adjacent: **Staubli (2013)** and **Mastrobuoni (2009)** on retirement incentives and labor supply.

Depending on ambition, it should also speak to:
- **Ruhm (2000, and follow-ups)** on macro conditions and health,
- **Sullivan and von Wachter (2009)** on job loss and mortality,
- and perhaps the broader literature on **austerity and health**.

### How should it position itself relative to those neighbors?
Mostly **build on and sharpen**, not attack. The right stance is:

- prior work shows mixed evidence on whether retirement helps or harms health;
- this paper isolates a particularly policy-relevant margin: **forced, abrupt delayed retirement**;
- unlike gradual reforms, Fornero offers a setting where reform intensity varied meaningfully and disproportionately by gender;
- therefore the paper informs when and why delayed retirement may be harmful.

The paper should not spend energy claiming to “resolve” the retirement-health literature. It cannot. It can, however, claim to identify one important corner of it: abrupt pension-age hikes under crisis conditions.

### Is the paper positioned too narrowly or too broadly?
At present, it is oddly both:

- **Too narrow** in its empirical storytelling: very tied to Italy, the *esodati*, and regional bite.
- **Too broad** in some rhetoric: phrases like “some workers paying with their lives” reach beyond what the current paper, as written, can persuasively sustain for a top general-interest audience.

It needs a more disciplined broad frame: **the health consequences of retirement-age reform design**.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?
It seems underengaged with at least three conversations:

1. **Austerity and health**  
   The sovereign debt crisis context matters. This reform was not a normal pension reform; it was crisis austerity. There is a broader policy audience there.

2. **Policy salience, legitimacy, and expectation shocks**  
   The paper repeatedly suggests that the breach of expectations matters psychologically. If that is part of the story, it should engage with literatures on expectations, policy credibility, and social insurance contracts.

3. **Gender and work at older ages**  
   If the central reduced-form effect falls on women, the paper needs a more serious conversation with the literature on gendered labor-force attachment, caregiving burdens, and occupational sorting late in the life cycle.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?
Not quite. It is currently in the “retirement and health” conversation, which is the right base camp, but the more interesting conversation may be:

**How should governments design retirement-age reforms when workers are liquidity constrained, health constrained, and close to retirement?**

That is a broader and more policy-relevant conversation. The paper should move there.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Countries face aging populations and rising pension costs. They respond by raising retirement ages. Economists know these reforms affect labor supply and public finances, but there is substantial uncertainty about their health consequences.

### Tension
Most evidence is mixed and comes from reforms that are gradual, partial, or focused on retirement eligibility margins. Italy’s Fornero reform was abrupt and severe, especially for women. That makes it unusually informative—but also hard to study because regions were already on different health trajectories.

### Resolution
Using the reform’s gender asymmetry together with regional exposure, the paper finds that higher-exposure regions experienced a larger rise in female mortality relative to male mortality among ages 55–64.

### Implications
The design of pension reform matters. Abrupt retirement-age increases may impose significant health costs, so policymakers should weigh transition rules, grandfathering, and worker heterogeneity rather than treating retirement-age hikes as a purely fiscal tool.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
It has the ingredients, but the arc is currently cluttered.

The paper now tells three stories at once:

1. the human drama of the *esodati*,
2. a methodological story about why simple DiD fails and DDD works,
3. a substantive story about retirement-age reform and mortality.

The methodological story is currently too dominant in the intro. For an AER-caliber pitch, the substantive story should dominate, and the design should feel like the way the paper answers the question, not the reason the question exists.

In other words: this should not read like “I found a better estimator after the first one failed.” It should read like “Here is a first-order policy question, and this reform offers a rare way to answer it.”

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?
“An abrupt pension reform in Italy that forced women to work years longer appears to have raised female mortality relative to male mortality in more-exposed regions.”

That is a real hook. People will listen.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
They would lean in initially. Mortality effects of pension reform are inherently interesting. But they will quickly ask: **What exactly is the mechanism?** If the answer remains vague—stress, physically demanding work, expectations, income shock, displacement-like conditions—interest will fade.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Almost certainly one of these:

- “Was it cardiovascular deaths, suicide, or something else?”
- “Is this really about work longer, or about being stranded without income?”
- “Why women specifically—occupations, caregiving, or exposure?”
- “Is this about abruptness and lack of grandfathering, rather than retirement age per se?”

Those are good questions. The paper needs to anticipate them as central, not peripheral.

### If the findings are modest, is that okay?
The findings are not modest in rhetorical terms; they are striking. The issue is not statistical punch but interpretability. If the paper cannot show mechanisms or clarify whether this is a work-retention effect versus a broader social-insurance shock, the result may feel more like a provocative first pass than a decisive contribution.

Still, a null would not have been the only interesting outcome here. Learning that a major retirement-age hike *did not* worsen mortality would also be valuable. So the underlying question is strong. The current result is interesting enough; it just needs sharper interpretation.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

At present, the gap is mainly a combination of **framing problem**, **scope problem**, and some **ambition problem**.

### Framing problem
The science may be interesting, but the story is too local and too estimator-centered. AER wants the paper to answer a broad economic question. This one should be framed as: **What are the welfare costs of raising retirement ages abruptly?**

### Scope problem
The paper currently offers one compelling reduced-form result on all-cause mortality. For AER, that is likely too thin. It needs at least one more layer:
- mechanism,
- heterogeneity,
- generalization to reform design,
- or stronger conceptual integration with the broader retirement-health and austerity literatures.

### Novelty problem
The underlying question is important and not exhausted, but the paper risks looking incremental because the literature already contains many retirement/health studies. To stand out, it must claim a distinct margin: **abrupt, crisis-driven, non-grandfathered delayed retirement**, not retirement timing in general.

### Ambition problem
The paper feels like a strong short paper or field-journal paper trying to dress itself up as a general-interest paper through dramatic language. To become an AER paper, it needs to be bigger in substance, not just sharper in prose.

### Single most impactful advice
**Reframe the paper around the welfare consequences of abrupt retirement-age hikes and add mechanism evidence—especially cause-specific mortality or heterogeneity showing why delayed retirement became lethal in this setting.**

That is the one change that would most alter its trajectory. Without mechanism, it is an intriguing design-plus-result paper. With mechanism, it could become a major policy paper.
