**Idea Fidelity** — The paper remains faithful to the original idea manifest. It studies Italy’s 2011 Fornero reform, uses Eurostat mortality and employment data across 21 NUTS2 regions, exploits the “Fornero bite” (change in 55–64 employment) as continuous treatment intensity, and leans on the gender differential in retirement age increases as the key dose-response within-region identification. The primary empirical focus is on age-specific mortality among 55–64 year-olds, with an age-group placebo at 45–54. The triple-difference strategy emphasized in the manifesto is the empirical centerpiece here, and all critical data sources and policy context are explicitly used.

**Summary** — This paper studies whether Italy’s abrupt 2011 pension reform, which raised retirement ages especially sharply for women, increased mortality among 55–64 year-olds. A simple DoD across regions is confounded by divergent regional trends, so the author resorts to a triple-difference that compares female versus male mortality within regions as a function of region-level “bites.” The main finding is that each additional percentage point of bite increases female mortality by about 0.6% relative to male mortality, implying sizable mortality costs of forced work retention.

**Essential Points**

1. **Credibility of the DDD identifying assumption needs bolstering.** The argument rests on the assumption that, absent the reform, the female–male mortality gap would have evolved similarly across high- and low-bite regions. Yet the paper presents no evidence from pre-reform data to show that the gender gap itself exhibits parallel trends. Given that regional labor markets may have gender-specific dynamics (e.g., industrial composition, smoking patterns) that also evolve over time, I would expect an event-study or coefficient plot showing the pretreatment evolution of the gender gap interacted with bite. Without that, the counterfactual in Equation (2) is untested, and the causal claim is weakened. Please present pre-reform gender-gap trends or, at minimum, validate the assumption by showing that the bite is uncorrelated with differential gender trends before 2012.

2. **Measurement of the treatment variable may still conflate reform effects with contemporaneous regional shocks.** Bite is defined as the 2014–2010 change in employment for ages 55–64, which incorporates both the reform and region-specific labor demand shifts over those four years. High-bite regions could also be regions undergoing broader demographic or economic changes that independently affect gender mortality gaps. Since the DDD uses observed bite rather than an instrument derived from the mechanical effect of retirement-age legislation, endogeneity remains a concern. The authors should construct an alternative “predicted bite” based solely on pre-reform age/gender composition and the retirement-age jump (as in a Bartik-style predictor), and show that results are similar when this instrumented treatment is used.

3. **Inferences would benefit from richer robustness/hereogeneity analysis tailored to the mortality mechanism.** The policy claim is that forced prolonged work increased mortality, but the paper leaves open whether this is driven by direct physical strain, psychological stress, or compositional shifts in occupation. Without any analysis of cause-specific mortality, it is hard to rule out other pathways (e.g., changes in traffic exposure or regionally varying healthcare access). The paper states that cause-of-death data exist but are unavailable at the granular level; this should not stop the authors from considering, for example, broader cause categories (cardiovascular versus non-cardiovascular) or using the region-sex aggregate level if counts permit. At minimum, the paper should test whether the bite correlates with other outcomes (hospitalization or welfare take-up) that could confound the mortality finding.

**Suggestions**

1. **Pre-trend diagnostics for the gender-gap specification.** Present a figure or table showing the event-study coefficients for the female–male difference interacted with bite (e.g., regress log mortality on gender × bite × year indicators, including leads and lags). Even if the pretreatment coefficients are noisy, demonstrating that they are small and statistically indistinguishable from zero would greatly improve confidence in the DDD’s identifying assumption. Alternatively, show placebo tests where the triple interaction is estimated using pre-2012 “fake post” periods; if the coefficient remains near zero, that supports the parallel-trends assumption.

2. **Construct a mechanical reform exposure measure.** Consider constructing a “predicted bite” that uses pre-reform age-sex shares and the legislative increase in statutory retirement ages to forecast the increase in employment that should occur had everyone complied. This can be done by simulating how many workers were aged 55–64 as of 2010 and how many would have reached the old-age cutoff under old versus new rules. Using this predicted bite as an instrument for the observed employment change would isolate the reform effect from contemporaneous labor demand shocks and provide a transparency check on the measurement of treatment intensity.

3. **Explore heterogeneity by occupation or economic structure.** If administrative data are not available, use proxy measures such as regional shares of manufacturing, services, or informal employment (perhaps from ISTAT or Eurostat structural indicators) to see whether the bite–mortality relationship is concentrated in regions where older women were more likely to be in physically demanding jobs. This would add credibility to the “forced work” mechanism and help distinguish it from, say, healthcare access differentials.

4. **Leverage cause-of-death data to probe mechanisms.** The manuscript notes that Eurostat provides cause-of-death at the NUTS2 level for 93 ICD-10 categories. If single-year-of-age detail is unavailable, aggregate ages 55–64 (and sexes) by region to examine whether cardiovascular, mental health, or alcohol-related mortality responded most strongly to the bite. Even if this requires aggregating sexes, comparing the female share of deaths across causes could provide suggestive evidence. The paper’s contribution would be strengthened by aligning the mortality result with plausible physiological or psychological pathways.

5. **Provide sensitivity to functional form and clustering.** While robust SEs are clustered at the region level, the data have only 21 clusters. Show results using wild-cluster bootstrap or randomization inference to confirm that significance is not driven by the small number of clusters. Additionally, discuss whether the log specification is influenced by any outliers (even though no zero-death cells exist, some region-year-sex cells could have low denominators). Reporting results with high-leverage points dropped (beyond Sicilia/Umbria, perhaps the five most extreme bite values) would demonstrate robustness.

6. **Clarify the interpretation of the effect size.** The conversion from the relative effect to absolute deaths (about 30 additional female deaths per 100,000) is helpful. Expand this by translating the effect into life expectancy changes or comparing it to the regional baseline death rate trends. Also discuss the practical significance for policy: beyond statistical significance, how does a 7.2% relative increase stack up against other known risks in the same demographic?

7. **Address potential compositional changes in the 55–64 population.** The bite measure captures employment changes but the reform may also have triggered migration (internal or external) or labor force exit for reasons other than retirement (e.g., disability). Provide evidence that the age-sex population denominators are stable or that the composition of the labor force (e.g., share of informal workers) did not change systematically with the bite, to alleviate concerns that mortality effects are driven by selection rather than treatment.

8. **Situate findings within broader policy debates.** The conclusion touches on legitimacy and transition provisions, which are compelling. Consider briefly discussing whether the results imply diminishing returns to raising retirement ages—i.e., is there an “optimal” bite beyond which mortality costs outweigh fiscal savings? Alternatively, highlight how this Italy-based evidence could be compared to other OECD reforms where gender asymmetries exist (e.g., countries delaying women’s retirement in the 1990s). This would draw a clearer line from the empirical result to global policy lessons.

In sum, the paper makes a potentially important contribution by leveraging Italy’s gendered pension reform to estimate mortality consequences of forced work. Strengthening the identification through pre-trend checks, alternative treatment measures, and mechanism evidence—as well as providing additional robustness—would increase confidence in the causal claim and the policy implications.
