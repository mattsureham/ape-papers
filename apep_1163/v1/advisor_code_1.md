**Idea Fidelity**

The paper largely follows the novel design outlined in the manifest: it uses the CPI‑indexed reporting threshold in CMS Open Payments to estimate bunching just below the cutoff, and it focuses on the role of food and beverage payments as the elastic category most amenable to disclosure avoidance. However, key elements of the envisioned identification strategy are missing or underdeveloped. The manifest emphasized exploiting the moving threshold over all available program years (2016–2024) and using the year‑to‑year shift as a falsification to discriminate policy‑driven censoring from fixed round‑number behavior. The submitted paper relies only on four years (2018, 2020, 2022, 2024) and draws a very limited sample (the first 160,000 records per year), which compromises the strength of the moving‑cutoff identification and raises concerns about representativeness. In addition, the manifest promised a mechanism test via linkage to Part D prescriber data; no such linkage appears, so the paper stops short of the richer validation of the regulatory channel. These departures weaken the original idea’s ability to quantify manufacturer strategic avoidance in a credible way.

**Summary**

The paper documents a systematic missing mass just below the CMS Open Payments per-transaction reporting threshold using bunching methods on pooled payment records from 2018–2024. The author finds a statistically significant negative bunching coefficient (pooled \(\hat{b}=-1.11\)), concentrated in food and beverage payments, which is interpreted as evidence that manufacturers optimally size small transfers to avoid disclosure unless aggregate totals exceed the annual threshold. The paper discusses the implications of this “disclosure cliff” for transparency and for descriptive work that uses Open Payments as if it were a census.

**Essential Points**

1. **Selection of the analytic dataset undermines external validity and identification.** The paper restricts attention to the first 160,000 API records per year and four program years, despite manifest evidence that the census contains millions of payments per year. This arbitrary truncation risks biased estimates if the early records differ systematically (e.g., limited to certain manufacturers, geographies, or payment types). Moreover, the moving threshold identification benefits from year‑over‑year coverage and the precise mapping of the discontinuity’s location; reducing the sample to four biennial years blunts this validation and makes it harder to rule out alternative explanations for the missing mass (e.g., stable round‑number clustering near \$11–\$12). You must show that your sample is representative and that the results hold when drawing randomly (or fully) from the complete dataset.

2. **The mechanism linking bunching to disclosure avoidance is asserted but not empirically demonstrated.** The claim is that firms strategically lower payment amounts to evade reporting because the aggregate threshold is rarely reached in low‑intensity relationships. However, the paper lacks any analysis of the aggregate threshold or of physician‑manufacturer pair intensity; it also omits the Part D linkage mentioned in the manifest. Without quantifying how often sub‑threshold payments are reported via the aggregate rule, or demonstrating that missing payments come from low‑frequency relationships (as opposed to e.g., rounding preferences), the causal story remains speculative. Provide empirical evidence that the observed missing mass corresponds to interactions that stay below the annual reporting ceiling—for instance, by estimating the share of payments in the manipulation window that belong to physicians with low cumulative totals, or by replicating the bunching exercise on a sample stratified by aggregate exposure using the Open Payments or Part D data.

3. **Robustness analyses currently raise more concerns than they reassure.** Table 7 shows enormous volatility in estimates across bandwidths, polynomial orders, and exclusion regions (e.g., orders 5 and 6 produce very different magnitudes and sign reversals appear for wider exclusion zones), which indicates the baseline specification may be overfitting. The justification for the chosen polynomial order and bandwidth needs to be strengthened via cross‑validation or pre‑analysis of bias/variance tradeoffs (e.g., the mechanical robustness framework from Kleven and Waseem). Without this, the inference about missing mass is fragile. The paper should present a principled specification search (perhaps guided by McCrary-style balancing) and focus on a specification where the estimate is stable, rather than reporting a wide range of wildly different values.

**Suggestions**

1. **Expand and randomize the sample.** The central premise is that a large fraction of low-dollar payments are missing from the public record. To credibly quantify this, you should sample randomly (or ideally exhaustively) from the full Open Payments database for each year, rather than limiting to the first 160,000 API records. If API constraints prevent full retrieval, describe the sorting order clearly and demonstrate empirically that the first 160k records are representative (e.g., by comparing payment type shares, manufacturers, threats). If the data are systematically ordered (e.g., alphabetically), that must be made explicit because it could bias the density. At a minimum, draw a random subset of payments in each year using a stratified API query, and show that your bunching estimates are unchanged.

2. **Reintroduce the moving-threshold identification as originally conceived.** Use all available program years (2016–2024) so that you can track the threshold as it moves from \$10.22 to \$13.46, which strengthens the argument that the discontinuity is policy-driven and not a static round-number artifact. Pooling across too few years (and skipping odd years entirely) limits the internal validity of the cross-year test. With more years, you can also examine the elasticity of the censoring gap to the magnitude of the threshold shift, which would add credibility to the interpretation.

3. **Link the observed censoring to the aggregate reporting rule and relationship intensity.** The manifest promised linking to Medicare Part D prescribing data; even if doing so is beyond scope, at least exploit the Open Payments data to measure annual cumulative payments per physician-manufacturer pair. Show that the payments just below the threshold occur predominantly in pairs whose annual totals remain below the aggregate ceiling, which is the only circumstance under which they can disappear entirely. Without this, it is difficult to conclude that manufacturers “strategically avoid” disclosure versus simply not needing to report because relationships are genuinely infrequent. Consider conducting the bunching exercise separately for low-annual-total versus high-annual-total physicians, or instrumenting for intensity via prior-year aggregates.

4. **Tighten the specification search for the bunching estimator.** The existing robustness table is informative but also alarming: the coefficient swings wildly across orders, bandwidths, and exclusion widths. Follow the standard bunching literature by (i) justifying each choice based on minimizing mean squared error or using simulated data; (ii) presenting figures showing the histogram and polynomial fit; and (iii) reporting confidence intervals for excluded-region bins to show that the negative gap is not an artifact of overfitting. If the problem is that the density is noisy below \$10, consider smoothing via wider bins or using a spline-based fit. You can also report sensitivity to alternative normalization strategies (e.g., normalizing by total excess mass rather than density at the threshold) to ensure the negative estimate is not driven by a denominator close to zero.

5. **Provide additional placebo/falsification tests.** The placebo at prior year thresholds should be disaggregated by year to show whether the censoring moves with the current threshold; reporting multiyear thresholds on top of each other in a figure could help. Also consider splitting the sample by manufacturer characteristics (e.g., large vs small firms, domestic vs foreign) or physician specialties to see whether the missing mass is concentrated in contexts where meals are more elastic. Another useful falsification would be to test for bunching in a payment amount range well away from any policy threshold (e.g., \$25–\$30) to ensure the estimator does not systematically generate negative estimates.

6. **Discuss implications for aggregate transparency more concretely.** The paper argues that the blind spot matters for research that uses the extensive margin of payments, but it would strengthen the contribution to quantify the size of the bias in a simple illustrative example (e.g., what share of physicians would be misclassified as “never paid” if all meals were counted). Alternatively, use the estimated missing mass to back out the implied number of unreported payments or dollars, contextualizing what a bunching coefficient of -1.11 means for policy.

7. **Clarify limitations and future directions.** The conclusion currently notes that the estimator measures only marginal missing mass. Expand this section to discuss how much of the small-payment universe is still captured (e.g., using the percentage of payments below the threshold that appear in the data via aggregate reporting). Discuss how the approach could be extended to study manufacturer heterogeneity in compliance or to test whether threshold increases reduce avoidance by looking at firms whose payment distributions shift less.

8. **Improve transparency of the code and data process.** Since the paper is computationally complex and uses API draws, provide an appendix describing how the dataset was constructed, how the bins were formed, and include the code used for the bunching estimation. This will make it easier for reviewers and readers to reproduce the results and assess the integrity of the specification, especially given the nonstandard sampling approach.

In sum, the paper tackles an important question with a promising identification strategy, but it must shore up the data representativeness, mechanistic validation, and statistical robustness before it can convincingly estimate the disclosure avoidance elasticity implied by the Open Payments threshold.
