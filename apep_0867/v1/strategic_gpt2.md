## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple, policy-relevant question: when Europe imposed Article 17’s platform copyright obligations—widely attacked as requiring “upload filters” and predicted to crush the digital economy—did digital-sector employment actually fall? Using cross-country variation in implementation timing, the paper’s core claim is no: one of the world’s most controversial platform regulations did not produce detectable job losses in the information sector.

A busy economist should care because this is not really about copyright law per se. It is about a broader and very live question: when governments increase platform liability and compliance obligations, do the real economic costs show up in employment, or are the dire forecasts overstated?

**Does the paper itself articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Mostly, but not optimally. The current opening is vivid and topical, but it slips too quickly into institutional detail and “this paper provides the first causal evidence.” The stronger version would foreground the broader economic question—how platform regulation affects real activity—and make Article 17 the sharp natural test case, not the entire point.

### The pitch the paper should have

> Governments around the world are expanding the legal responsibilities of digital platforms, and industry opponents routinely argue that these mandates will destroy jobs in the digital economy. Yet we have little evidence on whether platform-liability regulation actually contracts real economic activity, as opposed to merely changing compliance practices or shifting costs across margins.  
>
> This paper studies Europe’s Article 17 copyright regime—the emblematic “upload filter” mandate—as a test case. Exploiting staggered implementation across European countries, I show that this highly controversial increase in platform compliance obligations did not produce detectable declines in information-sector employment. The result suggests that one of the central economic objections to platform-liability regulation—large employment losses—may be much weaker than critics claim.

That is the AER version of the story: platform regulation, real economic effects, and a clean factual takeaway.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution in one sentence:**  
The paper’s contribution is to show that a landmark increase in platform copyright liability in Europe did not measurably reduce information-sector employment, challenging a central claim in debates over the economic costs of platform regulation.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?
Only partially. The paper says “first causal evidence” on Article 17 and gestures to GDPR and environmental-regulation literatures, but the differentiation is still a bit thin. Right now the reader can understand that it is new in topic, but not fully why it is new in substance.

The introduction needs a crisper contrast like:

- Existing platform-regulation evidence focuses mainly on privacy rules, market structure, content moderation, or legal doctrine—not real labor-market outcomes.
- Existing work on Article 17 is largely legal/theoretical, with lots of prediction and little ex post evidence.
- This paper does not ask whether Article 17 changed speech, piracy, or platform behavior; it asks whether the regulation had the large real-side employment costs that dominated the policy debate.

That triad would give the contribution shape.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a literature gap?
It is mixed. The strongest parts answer a **world question**: did upload-filter regulation destroy digital jobs? But parts of the introduction lapse into **literature-gap language**: first estimate here, adds to emerging literature there, demonstrates a design. That weakens the ambition.

For AER positioning, the paper should lean harder into the world question:
- What happens to real economic activity when platform compliance obligations rise?
- Are warnings of digital-sector collapse credible?
- Do platforms absorb regulatory costs through employment, prices, quality, entry, or something else?

Right now the paper knows the world question, but does not always trust it enough.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?
At present, they would probably say:  
“It's a staggered DiD on Article 17 and it finds no employment effect.”

That is clear enough, but it still sounds like “another policy-evaluation paper.” The introduction does not yet elevate it to:  
“This is one of the first ex post tests of whether platform-liability regulation has real labor-market costs, and the answer appears to be no.”

That second version has much more intellectual and policy bite.

### What would make this contribution bigger?
Most important: **move beyond sectoral employment as the only payoff.** Employment is a defensible outcome, but as currently framed it is also blunt and somewhat unsurprising. A bigger paper would either:

1. **Show where the costs went instead.**  
   If jobs did not fall, did firm entry, platform availability, creator revenue, user content volume, or concentration change?  
   This would convert a null into an economic reallocation story.

2. **Get closer to the actually treated margin.**  
   NACE J is broad. A stronger contribution would isolate the subset of firms or activities plausibly exposed to user-upload/content-hosting obligations.

3. **Make the broader regulatory comparison explicit.**  
   Put Article 17 alongside other platform regulations conceptually: privacy, content liability, competition mandates. The point becomes not “one EU copyright policy did nothing” but “headline platform regulations may have much smaller employment effects than public debate suggests.”

If the author can only enlarge the paper one way, the best route is: **pair the employment null with evidence on an alternative margin of adjustment.**

That framing would connect the paper to a much broader set of economists.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Before this paper, the world is one in which platform regulation is politically explosive and economically undermeasured. Article 17 became a symbol of fears that regulating user-generated-content platforms would choke off the digital economy.

### Tension
The public debate made large, concrete predictions—job loss, exit, contraction—but there is little ex post evidence on whether those real economic costs materialized. That creates a sharp tension between dramatic ex ante claims and absent ex post measurement.

### Resolution
The paper finds that the regulation did not produce detectable declines in information-sector employment.

### Implications
The central economic objection to this type of platform regulation may be overstated. Policymakers should update away from “these mandates obviously destroy jobs,” and economists should look for effects on other margins besides employment.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
Yes, but only in skeletal form. The ingredients are there. The problem is that the paper often defaults into methodological narration instead of substantive narration.

Too much of the introduction is:
- controversial law
- staggered timing
- estimator
- robustness
- three literatures

The stronger narrative would be:
1. This regulation was sold as economically dangerous.
2. The key measurable prediction was digital job loss.
3. Europe provides the first ex post test.
4. That prediction does not show up in the data.
5. Therefore the economic incidence of platform regulation likely operates elsewhere.

That is the story. The current draft has the story, but lets it get buried under design and contribution bookkeeping.

### If it is a collection of results looking for a story, what story should it be telling?
The story should be:
**“A major platform regulation triggered dire labor-market predictions. Those predictions were wrong, at least on employment.”**

Not:
**“Here is a staggered DiD on Article 17 with a null result.”**

The former is an AER-adjacent story; the latter is a competent field-journal story.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?
I would say:
“Europe imposed the notorious ‘upload filter’ rule that critics said would kill digital jobs, and there’s no evidence it reduced information-sector employment.”

That is a good opening fact. It has salience and intuitive stakes.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
Some would lean in—especially anyone interested in digital platforms, regulation, or political economy. But the enthusiasm would quickly depend on the next sentence.

If the next sentence is:
- “I measure a broad sector and find a null,” people may drift.
If the next sentence is:
- “So the main labor-market argument against platform-liability regulation seems exaggerated, which raises the question of where the costs actually went,” people lean in.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Almost certainly:
**“If jobs didn’t fall, what margin adjusted instead?”**

That is the key. It is both the paper’s opportunity and current weakness.

Other likely follow-ups:
- Was the policy less binding than advertised?
- Did large firms absorb the costs while small platforms suffered?
- Did the effects show up in entry, innovation, content, or creator outcomes rather than employment?

### If the findings are null or modest: is the null itself interesting?
Yes, but only if the paper really earns it.

This null is potentially interesting because:
- the ex ante predictions were loud and concrete;
- the policy was salient and consequential;
- employment is a first-order political argument.

But null papers need discipline. The author cannot just say “we found nothing.” The paper must make a stronger case that:
1. a meaningful predicted effect was on the table,
2. the analysis has enough informativeness to rule out economically relevant declines,
3. this narrows the plausible channels through which regulation matters.

The current draft is aware of this, but should push harder on point 3. Right now it stops at “no jobs effect.” A better paper says: “therefore the action must be elsewhere.”

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

Bluntly: this is **not yet an AER paper** in current form.

The main gap is not basic competence. The paper has a clean question and an intelligible answer. The gap is that the current version feels like a neat reduced-form evaluation of a famous policy, not a paper that changes how economists think about regulation of digital platforms.

### What is the problem?

#### Mostly a framing problem
The science may be fine, but the story is too small. The paper is still written as:
- Article 17
- staggered implementation
- employment null

For AER, it needs to be:
- a test of whether major platform-liability regulation has real labor-market costs,
- using Article 17 as the natural experiment,
- with implications for the broader economics of digital regulation.

#### Also a scope problem
Even with stronger framing, one broad employment outcome is thin for the very top journals. AER papers usually either:
- answer a very big question cleanly, or
- provide a richer mapping from policy to multiple margins and mechanisms.

This paper currently gives one outcome and then speculates about mechanisms. That is not enough.

#### Some novelty problem
The paper is novel in setting, but not yet in conceptual payoff. “Another regulation, no employment effect” is not by itself a top-journal contribution unless the paper can show why this case is especially informative.

#### Some ambition problem
The paper is competent but safe. It takes the most available aggregate outcome and shows no effect. That is useful, but not yet field-defining.

### What is the single most impactful advice?
**Reframe the paper as evidence on the economic incidence of platform regulation, and add at least one outcome that reveals where adjustment occurred if not through employment.**

If the author can only change one thing, that is it.

Because right now the obvious reader reaction is:
“Interesting that jobs didn’t fall—but then what did happen?”

Until the paper has a sharper answer to that, it will struggle to feel bigger than a well-executed null-result policy evaluation.
