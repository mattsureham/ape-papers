## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
This paper provides the first large-scale causal evidence of the biophysical impact of dam removal, a multi-billion dollar restoration strategy. By matching 1,341 dam removals to continuous sensor data, it documents a "slow dividend"—environmental benefits like temperature reduction that compound over a decade—and demonstrates that standard econometric methods (TWFE) fail to capture these gains due to the very nature of ecological recovery.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this well in the abstract, but the first two paragraphs of the introduction are a bit "standard environmental econ." The pitch the paper *should* have is:
> "Economists have long used property values to proxy for the benefits of ecological restoration, yet we lack evidence that the underlying physical environment actually improves at scale. This paper uses the staggered removal of over 1,000 dams to show that while restoration 'works,' the returns are harvested over decades rather than quarters, creating a 'slow dividend' that current policy evaluation windows—and standard econometric estimators—are fundamentally unequipped to measure."

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**Contribution:** The paper documents the long-run biophysical returns to dam removal and proves that traditional TWFE estimation systematically understates the benefits of environmental investments with long recovery horizons.

*   **Differentiation:** It moves beyond the hedonic/capitalization focus of Lewis et al. (2008) and Walls and Lee (2022) by measuring the "first-stage" physical outcomes that supposedly justify these policies.
*   **World vs. Literature:** It frames itself as answering a question about the **world** (Do these billions of dollars actually fix rivers?), which is high-impact.
*   **What would make it bigger?** To be a "slam dunk" AER paper, it needs to bridge the gap back to welfare. If the biophysical benefit takes 10 years to peak, are current hedonic estimates (which often look at immediate sales) missing the "slow dividend" in asset prices too? Connecting the physical "slow dividend" to an "asset price dividend" would be a major leap.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
The paper sits at the intersection of **Environmental Policy Evaluation** (Greenstone & Fan 2023) and **Applied Econometrics** (Sun & Abraham 2021; Goodman-Bacon 2021).

*   **Closest Neighbors:** Keiser & Shapiro (2019) on the Clean Water Act; Marcus (2021) on leaking storage tanks; and the recent wave of staggered DiD papers.
*   **Positioning:** It should position itself as a "bridge" paper. It uses the ecological science of "J-curves" to explain a specific econometric failure (TWFE attenuation).
*   **Unexpected Conversation:** It should speak to the **Public Finance** literature on infrastructure. Dam removal is "de-provisioning" infrastructure; the paper should frame this as a capital investment in a "natural asset" with a specific depreciation/recovery schedule.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC
*   **Setup:** We are tearing down dams at an accelerating rate for "ecological reasons."
*   **Tension:** We don't actually know if it works at scale, and local opposition is high because the costs are immediate while the benefits are invisible or delayed.
*   **Resolution:** It *does* work, but it takes 10+ years to see the full effect (the "Slow Dividend"). 
*   **Implications:** Short-termism in policy evaluation (3-5 year windows) is biased toward finding null results for environmental restoration.

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong. The "Slow Dividend" is a catchy, "AER-style" label for a complex phenomenon.

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
*   **Dinner Party Fact:** "If you evaluate a dam removal after 3 years, you're only seeing 15% of the eventual benefit." 
*   **Reaction:** People lean in. It challenges the "government waste" narrative by suggesting we simply aren't patient enough to measure success.
*   **Follow-up:** "Does the market (home prices) know this, or are people selling too early?" (This highlights the need for a price-linkage).

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
*   **Front-loading:** The Sun-Abraham vs. TWFE comparison is a major selling point. Table 2 is the "money table." I would move the discussion of *why* TWFE fails (the contaminated controls) earlier in the intro.
*   **Buried Results:** The "Dose-Response" (dam height) result in Table 3 is critical for the "Economics" of the paper (returns to scale). It deserves more prominence than a brief mention in the mechanisms.
*   **Appendix:** The leave-one-state-out is a standard appendix item; keep it there.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The gap is currently **Scope**. While the biophysical results are excellent, the AER is a general interest journal. 

**Single Most Impactful Advice:** The author needs to perform a "Policy Back-of-the-Envelope" (BOTE) or a simple CBA. If you take existing hedonic estimates of dam removal and "scale them up" using the 10-year physical recovery curve found here, how much have we been underestimating the ROI of river restoration? Transforming this from a "science" result into a "welfare" result is the key to the AER.
