## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper evaluates whether the massive wave of state-level legislation (34 states) passed to combat the 1,600% surge in catalytic converter thefts actually worked. Using a staggered DiD design, it finds that these laws had zero impact on theft-related search intensity; instead, the "resolution" of the epidemic was driven entirely by the collapse of global palladium prices. It argues that when the material returns to crime are high enough, even felony-level sentencing enhancements fail to deter, creating a "policy attribution error" where politicians take credit for market-driven declines.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this clearly by the second paragraph. However, the first two paragraphs are a bit "newsy." 
**The pitch the paper should have:** 
"Standard Beckerian models predict that criminal activity is a function of both expected penalties and material returns. This paper exploits the 2019–2024 catalytic converter theft epidemic—a rare setting where returns (commodity prices) and penalties (staggered state legislation) fluctuated violently—to test which channel dominates. I find that while state legislatures enacted 34 laws, these interventions provided no measurable 'deterrence dividend' over and above the reduction caused by falling metal prices."

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution:** The paper provides a clean, market-based test of the Becker (1968) model, demonstrating that material returns can completely swamp the deterrence effect of increased criminal penalties in property crime.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It differentiates well from the "policing" literature (Levitt, etc.) by focusing on the *price* of the loot. It builds on Draca et al. (2019) by moving from heterogeneous "burglarized goods" to a singular commodity with a transparent global price.
*   **Question vs. Literature:** It frames itself as a question about the world (Did the laws work?), which is strong.
*   **Clarity:** A smart economist would get it immediately: "It’s a paper showing prices matter more than laws for commodity-based theft."
*   **Bigger Contribution:** To be truly AER-sized, it needs a more robust outcome than Google Trends. The author admits this limitation. If the author could secure NIBRS offense-level data (even for a subset of states) to validate the Google Trends null, the contribution would move from "suggestive" to "definitive."

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

*   **Neighbors:** Becker (1968), Draca et al. (2019) [Prices/Crime], Durlauf & Nagin (2011) [Deterrence], and the recent DiD econometrics (Callaway & Sant’Anna, 2021).
*   **Strategy:** It currently "synthesizes." It should probably "attack" more—specifically attacking the political narrative of legislative efficacy. It should position itself as a critique of "Severity vs. Certainty" in deterrence.
*   **Missing Conversations:** The paper is light on the **Political Economy** of "doing something." Why do 34 legislatures pass laws that don't work? Connecting to the literature on "Symbolic Politics" or "Policy Attribution" would broaden the audience to include political economists.

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

*   **Setup:** A 1,600% spike in theft driven by a 300% spike in palladium prices.
*   **Tension:** States pass laws; theft falls. Politicians say "The laws worked." The economist asks: "Or was it just the price of palladium falling?"
*   **Resolution:** The laws had no effect. The price interaction shows the law only "works" when the crime isn't profitable anyway.
*   **Implications:** Deterrence by severity is a weak tool against high-value commodity theft; we should target the supply chain (the "fence") instead.

**Evaluation:** The arc is exceptionally strong. It reads like a detective story.

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

*   **Lead Fact:** "Thirty-four states passed laws to stop your catalytic converter from being stolen, and they might as well have done nothing—the only thing that saved your car was a price drop in the South African mining sector."
*   **Reaction:** People lean in. It's a "freakonomics" style counter-intuitive truth.
*   **Follow-up:** "Why didn't they work? Was it because no one gets caught, or because the money was just too good?"

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

*   **Front-load:** The price interaction model (Equation 2) is the "meat." It should be teased more prominently in the intro as the way we "break the tie" between the two explanations.
*   **Google Trends:** Spend more time in the main text (not the appendix) defending the proxy. If the AER is going to publish a Google Trends paper, the validation against NICB claims needs to be a centerpiece figure, not just a mention.
*   **Mechanism:** Section 5.3 (Heterogeneity) is currently a bit thin. I want to know more about the "Scrap Dealer" laws vs. "Penalty" laws. Is there *any* hint that regulating the buyers works better than punishing the thieves?

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The paper is currently a "very good" applied micro paper, but it feels slightly "light" for the AER due to the reliance on a single proxy (Google Trends).

**The single most impactful improvement:** The author needs to obtain and incorporate **administrative crime data** (NIBRS/UCR) or **insurance industry data** at the state-month level. If a precisely estimated null on law efficacy holds up using actual theft counts alongside the price interaction, this becomes a high-impact paper on the limits of the criminal justice system.
