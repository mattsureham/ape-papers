## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether the dramatic rise and fall in catalytic-converter theft in the United States was driven by anti-theft laws or by swings in palladium prices, which changed the payoff to stealing converters. The core claim is that the theft wave was primarily a commodity-price phenomenon and that the legislative response added little, suggesting that for some property crimes the return to crime can swamp conventional deterrence policy.

A busy economist should care because this is, in principle, a sharp real-world test of a classic Beckerian idea: when crime becomes temporarily very profitable, do tougher penalties matter at all? That is potentially a broad and important question, well beyond catalytic converters.

### Does the paper articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Partly, but not optimally. The current opening is vivid and readable, and paragraph 2 gets to the right question quickly. But the introduction then drifts into a somewhat standard literature-tour before fully crystallizing the bigger point. The paper’s best pitch is not “here is a DiD on catalytic converter theft laws”; it is “here is a natural episode that lets us compare the return-to-crime channel with the deterrence-through-penalties channel.”

The first two paragraphs should more aggressively foreground:
1. the scale and salience of the theft epidemic,
2. the coincidence of lawmaking and commodity-price reversal,
3. the broader economic question about when deterrence fails.

### The pitch the paper should have

From 2019 to 2022, catalytic-converter theft exploded in the United States as palladium prices soared, then receded as those prices collapsed. In response, dozens of states enacted anti-theft laws, creating a rare setting in which economists can compare two forces emphasized by crime theory: changes in the return to crime and changes in the expected punishment.

This paper argues that the episode was driven overwhelmingly by the first force. The central lesson is not about one quirky crime market; it is that when material returns spike sharply, conventional penalty-based deterrence may have little bite, and policymakers may misattribute crime declines to legislation that merely coincided with falling criminal profits.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### Contribution in one sentence

The paper’s contribution is to use the catalytic-converter theft wave as a case study to argue that variation in the return to crime, induced by commodity prices, dominated variation in criminal penalties in explaining the rise and fall of a major property-crime episode.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest papers?

Not yet clearly enough. The paper names three literatures—deterrence, returns to crime, and catalytic-converter policy—but the differentiation is still somewhat generic. Right now the contribution risks sounding like: “another reduced-form policy evaluation finds limited effects of state laws.” What needs sharpening is the claim that this setting uniquely allows a comparison of two margins in crime supply:
- expected punishment,
- expected returns.

That is stronger than “first multi-state evaluation of converter-theft laws,” which is too small-bore for AER positioning.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a gap in a literature?

It is mixed. The strongest parts answer a world question: what actually drove this crime wave? The weaker parts lapse into gap-filling: first multi-state evaluation, extending prior studies, etc. For AER, the world question is much better:
- When criminal opportunities become highly lucrative, can legislatures deter the resulting crime through harsher penalties?
- Or do commodity markets dominate criminal justice policy?

That is the framing to lean on.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?

At present, maybe—but not crisply enough. The best version would be: “They show that catalytic-converter theft rose and fell with palladium prices, and that the wave of anti-theft laws appears to have mattered little, which they interpret as evidence that returns to crime can dominate deterrence.” The weaker version, which too many readers may currently come away with, is: “It’s a staggered DiD on a recent crime law using Google Trends.”

That second reaction is a problem.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Several possibilities:

1. **Shift the focal outcome from the law evaluation to the crime-market mechanism.**  
   The paper is most interesting when it is about the economics of theft markets, not when it is auditing state legislation.

2. **Deepen the mechanism on criminal returns.**  
   For example, tie theft patterns more directly to vehicle composition, scrap-market structure, converter-specific resale values, or locations with easier fencing. That would move it from a policy null to a richer crime-market paper.

3. **Reframe around policy attribution error.**  
   There is a potentially big idea here: policymakers often claim success when an exogenous shock solved the problem. That could resonate far beyond this setting.

4. **Broaden the comparison class.**  
   If the paper could connect catalytic converters to other commodity-linked crimes—copper theft, cargo theft, battery theft, perhaps metal theft more broadly—it would feel less like a one-off episode and more like a general proposition.

5. **Focus less on “laws do not work” and more on “severity does not bind when returns are extreme.”**  
   That is a larger, more theoretically grounded claim.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

A sudden, highly salient theft epidemic emerges because catalytic converters become unusually valuable thanks to soaring palladium prices. Legislatures react quickly with targeted anti-theft laws.

### Tension

The decline in theft coincides with both the spread of anti-theft legislation and a collapse in palladium prices. So what actually caused the decline: policy or market incentives? More fundamentally, can tougher penalties deter crime when the payoff to crime has temporarily become enormous?

### Resolution

The paper finds little evidence that the laws independently reduced theft-related search intensity, while the timing and interaction patterns point to commodity prices as the dominant force.

### Implications

Policymakers may be fighting the wrong margin. For crimes linked to transparent resale markets and commodity shocks, manipulating penalties may be much less effective than disrupting resale channels or addressing the market that makes the crime profitable in the first place.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the ingredients of a good narrative arc, but the execution is uneven. The story is there; the paper just keeps slipping away from it into specification-by-specification exposition. The introduction is stronger than the rest of the paper in this respect. Once in the results, the paper starts to feel like a series of estimates rather than a tightly unfolding argument.

### What story should it be telling?

Not:
- “We estimate whether state anti-theft laws reduced a Google Trends proxy.”

But:
- “A spectacular crime wave created by a commodity boom gave policymakers a chance to claim they solved the problem. This paper asks whether they did. The answer appears to be no: the market created the wave and the market ended it.”

That is a story with setup, tension, and punch.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?

I would lead with:  
“Catalytic-converter theft rose more than sixteen-fold as palladium prices surged, and the paper argues that dozens of state anti-theft laws did little to change that trajectory.”

That is the dinner-party fact.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

They would lean in initially, because the setting is vivid, recent, and legible. Economists like episodes where textbook incentives show up starkly in the data. But they will lean back quickly if the paper sounds like it is just selling a null result on a niche policy.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Probably one of these:
- “So is the real contribution about commodity prices driving crime?”
- “Does this generalize beyond catalytic converters?”
- “Is the issue that severity doesn’t matter, but certainty might?”
- “Why should I trust the outcome measure enough to believe the broader claim?”

That last question is important strategically. Even though you asked not to assess identification, from an editorial positioning standpoint the use of Google Trends means the paper needs to work harder to persuade readers that the object of interest is important enough and well-enough measured to carry a broad claim.

### If the findings are null or modest, is the null itself interesting?

Potentially yes—but only if framed correctly. Nulls are publishable when they overturn a widely touted policy success or reveal that a policy instrument has little bite in a setting where theory might have predicted otherwise. The paper can make that case, but it should stop treating the null as self-evidently important. The interesting result is not just “no law effect.” It is:
- policymakers likely claimed credit for a decline they did not cause, and
- returns to crime may overwhelm severity-based deterrence in certain markets.

Without that framing, the paper can feel like a failed policy evaluation. With it, the null is substantive.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

This is mostly a **framing and ambition problem**, with some **scope** concerns.

### Framing problem

The science may be competent, but the paper is selling itself too much as a narrow policy evaluation and too little as a paper about the economics of crime under extreme fluctuations in criminal returns. AER will not care much about catalytic-converter statutes per se. It may care about a broader proposition about when deterrence fails.

### Scope problem

The paper is trying to carry a big theoretical and policy claim off a very specific setting and a somewhat indirect outcome. That creates a mismatch: the claims are broad, the evidentiary object is narrow. To close that gap, the paper either needs broader empirical scope or more restrained claims.

### Novelty problem

The underlying intuition—that criminals respond to economic incentives, and that commodity prices can drive crime—is not novel. The novelty must therefore come from the setting and the comparison of margins: laws versus returns. That comparison needs to be sold much more sharply.

### Ambition problem

The paper is competent but currently a bit safe. “First multi-state evaluation” is not top-journal ambition. “A vivid test of whether severity-based deterrence binds when the return to crime spikes” is closer.

### Single most impactful advice

**Rebuild the paper around the broader question—when do changes in criminal returns swamp severity-based deterrence?—and treat catalytic-converter legislation as the setting, not the contribution.**

That one change would improve the title, introduction, literature positioning, and the interpretation of the null all at once.
