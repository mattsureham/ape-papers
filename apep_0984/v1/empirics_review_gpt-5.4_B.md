## 1. Idea Fidelity

The paper only partially pursues the original idea in the manifest, and the deviations are consequential for identification and interpretability. The central original contribution was to use administrative crime data—especially NIBRS state-month counts of “Theft of Motor Vehicle Parts or Accessories,” possibly triangulated with NICB claims—to decompose the decline in catalytic converter theft into a commodity-price effect and a law effect. Instead, the paper replaces the intended outcome with Google Trends searches for “catalytic converter theft,” a much weaker proxy that is mechanically influenced by media coverage, policy salience, and public awareness. This is not a minor implementation change: it undermines the stated goal of measuring theft itself.

Relatedly, the manifest emphasized a decomposition using crime data and staggered DiD, with early adopters providing cleaner identification. The paper does estimate staggered DiD and a law–price interaction, but because palladium price is common across states and the outcome is a search index normalized within state, the exercise does not deliver the clean price-versus-law decomposition promised in the manifest. In short, the paper keeps the broad question but misses the key data source and therefore much of the original identification logic.

## 2. Summary

This paper studies whether state anti-theft laws reduced catalytic converter theft during the 2021–2024 period, when palladium prices first surged and then collapsed. Using state-quarter Google Trends search intensity for “catalytic converter theft,” the author finds little evidence that the laws reduced searches and argues that the decline in theft was driven by falling palladium prices rather than legislation.

## 3. Essential Points

1. **The outcome measure is too weak to support the paper’s headline causal claims.**  
   The paper’s strongest claim is that “commodity prices, not criminal penalties, drove the catalytic converter theft epidemic,” but the analysis does not use theft data. Google Trends for a highly newsworthy phrase is plausibly affected by legislation itself, media reporting on theft waves, insurance-company outreach, and public curiosity. Indeed, the paper itself interprets positive law effects at high prices as “media-salience effects,” which underscores the problem: if the policy can directly affect the outcome through publicity, then a null or positive effect on searches is not evidence of no effect on theft. At minimum, the paper needs to move to direct measures of theft incidence (NIBRS, state police reports, insurance claims, or scrapyard transaction data) or sharply scale back its conclusions.

2. **The identification strategy does not isolate a commodity-price effect in a convincing way.**  
   Palladium prices vary only nationally over time, so with quarter fixed effects the main price effect is absorbed, and the paper relies on a law × price interaction for interpretation. But this interaction is difficult to give a causal “decomposition” interpretation without a state-level source of differential exposure to palladium incentives. Why should a national palladium price shock affect all states equally? States differ in vehicle mix (e.g., Prius prevalence), urban density, scrap market structure, and preexisting theft opportunity. Without exploiting such exposure heterogeneity, the paper cannot credibly separate “price effect” from “law effect”; it can only show that estimated treatment effects differ across moments of the national price cycle.

3. **There are basic coherence and data-reporting problems that need to be fixed before the results can be evaluated.**  
   The paper says 43 states are in the analysis sample after excluding eight state-equivalents, but Table 1 notes “51 states,” and the observation count of 1,734 corresponds to 43 states × roughly 40 quarters, not 51. The text also misclassifies some states’ adoption status relative to the institutional narrative, and the paper alternates between 34 treated states in the text and an analysis sample that excludes treated states due to API failures. More importantly, one robustness specification with state-specific trends yields a large statistically significant negative effect, yet the paper dismisses it as “overfitting” without serious diagnosis. These inconsistencies weaken confidence in the empirical implementation.

## 4. Suggestions

The paper asks a timely and interesting question, and the cross-state policy variation is potentially valuable. But to make a genuine contribution in an AER: Insights-style format, the paper needs a sharper empirical foundation and a narrower interpretation. Here are the most helpful ways to improve it.

First, **replace Google Trends as the primary outcome**. If the intended NIBRS/OpenICPSR data are available, the paper should use state-month counts of motor vehicle parts/accessories thefts as the main outcome and relegate Google Trends to a validation appendix. Even if NIBRS is imperfect for identifying catalytic-converter-specific theft, it is still much closer to actual crime than search behavior. If available, NICB state-level insurance claims would be especially compelling, since the policy debate centered on insured theft claims. If neither source can be assembled credibly, the paper should be reframed as a study of *public search interest* rather than theft itself, and the title/abstract/conclusion should be revised accordingly.

Second, **build a more convincing exposure-based price design**. Because palladium price is national, the paper needs cross-state heterogeneity in how much that national shock should matter. Obvious candidates are hybrid-vehicle share, Toyota Prius registrations, vehicle-age composition, urban parking environments, and scrap metal market density. An empirically stronger design would interact national palladium prices with pre-period state exposure to converter theft profitability, then ask whether high-exposure states experienced larger theft changes over the price cycle and whether laws attenuated that relationship. That would come much closer to the manifest’s promised decomposition and would make the Becker interpretation more credible.

Third, **take policy heterogeneity much more seriously**. The current coding into “enhanced penalty” versus “dealer regulation” is too coarse, especially because these laws likely differ in mechanism. Dealer documentation rules, VIN marking, cash purchase bans, holding periods, and offense reclassification should not be expected to have the same timing or effect size. A more useful approach would be to code specific provisions and estimate effects by mechanism, or at least separate laws aimed at the resale market from laws aimed purely at punishment severity. The discussion already hints that certainty and market disruption may matter more than severity; the empirical design should reflect that.

Fourth, **show the underlying treatment timing and event-study evidence more transparently**. I would want a figure with adoption timing by state, a national time series of palladium prices alongside theft outcomes, and event studies for both the TWFE-compatible specification and Callaway–Sant’Anna group-time effects. The current placebo “4 quarters early” test is not a sufficient pre-trend diagnostic. For a staggered-adoption design, the pre-treatment path by cohort is crucial, especially because treatment adoption was plausibly triggered by rising theft within states.

Fifth, **address endogeneity of adoption more directly**. The paper asserts that timing was driven by legislative calendars and committee priorities, but that is not enough. Many states almost certainly acted because thefts had already surged locally. This does not doom the design, but it means the paper should provide evidence: for example, regress adoption timing on lagged theft/claims trends, show whether early adopters had steeper pre-trends, and use not-yet-treated controls in event-study graphs. If the laws were a response to local spikes, then a simple parallel-trends narrative is not persuasive.

Sixth, **be more careful in interpreting nulls and significance patterns**. The abstract and title overstate what the evidence shows. Even if the law coefficient is near zero in the preferred specification, that is not enough to conclude that the “commodity market solved the problem that the criminal justice system could not.” With a noisy proxy outcome and substantial policy heterogeneity, the more defensible claim is that the paper finds no robust evidence of large average effects of these laws in the available data. Likewise, the significant law × price interaction should be treated cautiously: with no direct theft data and no state-varying price exposure, it is descriptive rather than structural.

Seventh, **resolve all sample and coding inconsistencies**. The paper should clearly state the exact analysis sample, number of states, which treated and untreated states are included, and how observation counts are constructed. Table notes and text should match. If Alabama, Alaska, and Arizona are missing due to API failures, the paper must discuss whether exclusion is random with respect to treatment timing and baseline theft risk. For a short-format empirical paper, these housekeeping issues matter because they directly affect confidence in the implementation.

Eighth, **reconsider the role of the state-specific trends result**. Right now the paper dismisses a large significant estimate as likely overfitting because it conflicts with the preferred null. That is not enough. The authors should show event studies with and without trends, explain what identifying variation remains under trends, and clarify whether the trends specification is absorbing differential preexisting growth in search intensity. If the result is unstable across reasonable specifications, the paper should present that instability honestly rather than treat one estimate as obviously spurious.

Finally, **tighten the paper’s contribution claim**. The current framing promises a decisive causal decomposition and a direct test of Becker. The evidence, as presented, cannot sustain that. A more coherent and potentially publishable paper would either:  
(a) use direct theft data and deliver the intended price-versus-law decomposition; or  
(b) present a narrower claim that anti-theft laws do not appear to shift measured theft-related attention/search behavior once national trends are absorbed, during a period dominated by a major commodity-price cycle.

In its current form, I do not think the paper makes a sufficiently credible causal contribution. But the underlying question is good, and with administrative theft data plus a state-varying exposure design, this could become a useful and interesting short paper.
