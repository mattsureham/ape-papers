## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper studies the UK’s 2019 decision to slash the maximum stake on fixed-odds betting terminals (FOBTs) from £100 to £2, one of the sharpest product-specific gambling regulations ever implemented. The core question is whether targeting a highly addictive retail gambling product actually reduces total gambling activity, or whether consumers simply move online; economists should care because this is a clean case of product regulation in a market with fast-growing digital substitutes.

The paper does articulate a recognizable pitch in the opening paragraphs, and in fact the first paragraph is strong. But the introduction currently mixes three pitches: gambling harm, sin-good regulation broadly, and a sector-level DiD design. What it does **not** yet do crisply enough is state the big economic takeaway in a way that travels beyond gambling: **product-specific regulation can reduce total consumption more than naive substitution measures imply because the substitute channel may have been expanding anyway**. That is the portable idea.

### The pitch the paper should have in the first two paragraphs

“Governments increasingly regulate specific high-harm products rather than banning or taxing entire categories: flavored tobacco, high-proof alcohol, online ads, short-term rentals, etc. But when one product is restricted and nearby substitutes remain available—especially digital ones—the key policy question is whether regulation reduces total consumption or simply reallocates it across channels.

This paper studies an unusually stark case: the UK’s 2019 reduction in fixed-odds betting terminal stakes from £100 to £2, which effectively eliminated a major in-store gambling product overnight. I show that the reform sharply reduced land-based betting revenue and destroyed the targeted machine segment, while the apparent shift to online betting is much smaller once one accounts for the pre-existing growth trend in remote gambling. The broader lesson is that evaluating product-level regulation requires a counterfactual for substitute channels, not just a before-after comparison of raw levels.”

That is the AER-version pitch. It leads with a general question about regulation in a substitutable, digitizing market and uses gambling as the sharp empirical setting.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper’s contribution is to show that a dramatic product-level gambling regulation in the UK sharply contracted the targeted retail gambling product and reduced sector revenue, while true online substitution was much smaller than naive post-reform comparisons suggest once pre-existing digitization trends are taken into account.

### Evaluation

#### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?

Not yet clearly enough. The paper says there is “zero NBER working papers” and “no causal study in a top economics journal,” which is a weak way to establish contribution. AER readers do not care that a specific topic has been neglected if the underlying economic question has already been answered in analogous markets. The paper needs to differentiate itself from work on:

- sin-good taxation/regulation with substitution across products or channels,
- salience and targeted regulation,
- gambling demand and problem gambling,
- digitization / online substitution after offline restrictions.

Right now the differentiation is more “this exact policy hasn’t been studied” than “this paper changes what we know.”

#### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or filling a gap in a LITERATURE?

Mixed, but leaning too much toward literature-gap language. The stronger frame is about the world:

- When policymakers regulate a salient high-harm product but not close substitutes, how much consumption truly disappears?
- In digitizing markets, do product bans mostly reallocate demand online, or do they actually reduce total activity?

That is stronger than “there is little economics on gambling.”

#### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what’s new?

Probably, but not sharply enough. They might say:

> “It’s a DiD on the UK FOBT reform showing betting revenue fell and online substitution was modest after trend adjustment.”

That is decent, but still sounds like “another DiD paper about a regulation shock.” The part that could stick is the **difference between naive and trend-adjusted substitution**. That is the paper’s most exportable contribution and should be elevated from result to organizing idea.

#### What would make this contribution bigger?

Several possibilities:

1. **Make harm, not just revenue, more central.**  
   At present the paper shows effects on GGY and machine counts. That is industry restructuring, not welfare. If the paper had credible evidence on gambling intensity, problem gambling, bankruptcies, treatment referrals, financial distress, or crime, the contribution becomes much bigger. Right now it stops one step short of the question readers most care about.

2. **Make the substitution framework more general.**  
   The paper should explicitly sell a broader conceptual point: in markets with trending substitute channels, observed substitution after regulation is not causal substitution. If formalized even minimally, that would make the contribution more durable.

3. **Use more granular variation.**  
   The paper itself admits the next step: local authority variation in exposure to FOBTs. That would enlarge the contribution because it moves beyond a four-sector comparison and opens the door to outcomes beyond industry revenue.

4. **Compare with a broader class of product regulations.**  
   The paper could be bigger if it framed FOBTs as one case of “regulating salient analog vice in a digital market,” alongside flavored tobacco, sports betting apps, payday lending storefronts, etc.

Most of all, the paper becomes bigger if it stops being “a paper on British gambling” and becomes “a paper on how to think about targeted regulation when substitutes are already diffusing.”

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

A highly lucrative, highly controversial gambling product dominated British betting shops. Policymakers faced pressure to act because FOBTs were visible, politically salient, and associated with harm.

### Tension

Restricting a salient offline product may not reduce overall gambling if users migrate to online channels that are less visible and less regulated. In a rapidly digitizing market, post-reform online growth may reflect either true substitution or business-as-usual trend growth.

### Resolution

The stake cut effectively destroyed the targeted machine segment and reduced non-remote betting revenue substantially. Online betting did grow, but most of that increase appears to have been part of a pre-existing trend rather than induced by the reform, implying much less displacement than naive comparisons suggest.

### Implications

Targeted product regulation can materially reduce market activity even when substitutes exist; but policymakers must distinguish between raw post-policy growth in substitute channels and counterfactual growth that would have occurred anyway. More broadly, visible product regulation may be more effective than critics claim, but it may also redirect attention away from less visible substitute channels.

### Evaluation

The paper has the bones of a real narrative arc, which is a positive sign. But it currently slips into being a collection of sensible empirical sections: main result, mechanism, online substitution, placebo, discussion. The narrative is there, but underexploited.

The story it **should** be telling is not just “the UK cut stakes and revenue fell.” It should be:

1. Policymakers regulate visible addictive products.
2. Critics say demand will just move elsewhere.
3. In digitizing markets, that critique is easy to overstate because substitute channels were already growing.
4. This case lets us separate true substitution from background migration.
5. Once we do, the policy looks substantially more potent than naive readings suggest.

That is a coherent and memorable story.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The main gap is a mix of **framing problem** and **scope problem**, with some **ambition problem**.

### Framing problem

The science, such as it is, is more interesting than the current packaging. The paper’s most AER-relevant idea is not “Britain regulated FOBTs.” It is:

> In markets undergoing digital migration, naive estimates of post-regulation substitution can be badly misleading.

That needs to become the title-level and introduction-level contribution.

### Scope problem

The current outcomes are too close to industry accounting aggregates. That is enough for a solid field journal or policy journal paper, but thin for AER. The paper needs either:

- more granular evidence,
- more outcomes tied to consumer behavior or harm,
- or a stronger conceptual generalization.

### Novelty problem

Not fatal, but present. Product-specific regulation with substitution is a known class of question. The paper’s novelty lies in the extremity of the policy shock and the substitution decomposition. It needs to make that novelty unmistakable.

### Ambition problem

The paper is competent, disciplined, and surprisingly readable. But it is safe. It asks a reasonable question and answers it with the data at hand. AER papers usually either:
- answer a first-order question with unusually compelling evidence, or
- use a narrow setting to deliver a broadly important conceptual lesson.

This paper wants to do the second, but hasn’t yet fully committed.

### Single most impactful advice

**Reframe the paper around a general lesson about measuring substitution under product regulation in digitizing markets, and build every section around that idea rather than around the fact that this is a UK gambling reform.**

If the author can do only one thing, that is it. If they can do two things, the second is: add more direct evidence on total gambling behavior or harm, not just sector revenue.
