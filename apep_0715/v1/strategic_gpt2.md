This paper is trying to do something potentially interesting: use the UK’s dramatic 2019 cut in maximum stakes on fixed-odds betting terminals (FOBTs) to ask whether product-specific regulation meaningfully reduces gambling activity, or simply pushes it online. The core takeaway is that land-based betting revenue fell sharply, the targeted machines essentially disappeared, and online substitution appears much smaller once one accounts for pre-existing digital growth.

My bottom-line editorial view: there is an AER-adjacent idea here, but not yet an AER paper. The current draft has a policy-relevant shock and a clean-sounding result, but the paper is still too close to “a competent DiD on one UK reform” and not yet sufficiently framed as answering a larger economic question about product regulation, substitution, and market restructuring.

## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

### What is this paper about? Why should a busy economist care?

This paper studies whether banning or sharply restricting a specific addictive product reduces total consumption, or whether consumers simply substitute into other channels. Using the UK’s 2019 reduction in maximum stakes on fixed-odds betting terminals, it argues that the regulation largely destroyed the targeted product and that online substitution was much smaller than raw pre-post comparisons suggest.

A busy economist should care because this is fundamentally a paper about the effectiveness of product-level regulation in markets with close substitutes and rapid digitization. The broader issue is not gambling per se; it is whether regulating a harmful product changes total behavior or just reshuffles it across platforms.

### Does the paper articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Partly, but not well enough. The first paragraph is vivid and concrete. The second broadens to a general policy problem. That is good instinct. But the introduction then slips quickly into “this paper provides the first systematic economic evaluation…” and “I exploit…”, which makes the contribution feel method-first and setting-specific rather than question-first.

The introduction should lead more forcefully with the general economic question: when regulators target one harmful product in a multi-channel market, what happens to total consumption and industry structure? The UK FOBT reform is then the ideal test case, not the main event.

### The pitch the paper should have

Here is the pitch the paper should use:

> Governments increasingly regulate specific harmful products rather than banning entire markets: flavored tobacco, high-alcohol drinks, high-fee loans, or high-stakes gambling machines. But product-level regulation only improves welfare if consumers do not simply migrate to close substitutes, especially digital ones.  
>  
> This paper studies one of the world’s sharpest product regulations—the UK’s 2019 cut in maximum stakes on fixed-odds betting terminals from £100 to £2—to ask a broader question: when a highly salient, high-intensity product is effectively banned, does total gambling contract or merely move online? I show that the reform destroyed the targeted machine market and reduced betting revenue substantially, while online substitution was modest once one accounts for the fact that remote betting was already on a strong pre-existing growth path.

That is the paper’s real hook.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### Contribution in one sentence

The paper’s contribution is to show that a sharp product-specific gambling regulation substantially reduced the targeted market and that naive estimates overstate online substitution because they fail to net out underlying digitization trends.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest papers?

Not yet. The draft gestures at three literatures but does not sharply distinguish itself from them. Right now the contribution reads as a combination of:
1. a sin-goods substitution paper,
2. a gambling-policy case study,
3. a simple DiD around a major UK reform.

That is not enough differentiation for a top general-interest journal. “First evaluation of FOBT regulation” is not, by itself, a strong AER contribution. “No NBER paper exists” is definitely not a persuasive way to establish novelty. AER cares whether the paper changes what economists believe about behavior or policy, not whether the niche has a vacancy.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a gap in the literature?

It is mixed, but too often framed as a literature gap. The strongest version is world-facing: do product bans work in markets with digital substitutes? The weaker version is literature-facing: no one has causally studied this particular gambling-machine rule. The paper needs much more of the former.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the intro?

At present, they would probably say: “It’s a DiD paper on the UK FOBT stake cut showing betting revenue fell and online substitution was smaller than it looked.”

That is not bad, but it is still too close to “another policy evaluation paper.” The novel idea is the distinction between naive substitution and trend-adjusted substitution in a digitizing market. That should be the memorable concept. Right now it is presented as one result among several rather than the conceptual centerpiece.

### What would make the contribution bigger?

Several possibilities:

- **Center the paper on total market reallocation, not just treated-sector decline.** The current headline outcome is betting-sector GGY. Bigger question: how much did total gambling contract versus reallocate across channels/products/places?
- **Make substitution the main object, not a side calculation.** If the real contribution is that online displacement is overstated by naive comparisons, then the paper should develop that idea more formally and more generally.
- **Add a welfare-relevant margin.** Revenue is a coarse object. A stronger paper would say something about intensity, composition, losses per user, problem-gambling proxies, bankruptcies, local spending spillovers, or crime if feasible. Not because referees need “more outcomes,” but because the paper’s current outcome variable is very industry-structure oriented and only indirectly about harm.
- **Use heterogeneous exposure.** If the paper could move from four sectors to richer geographic or firm-level variation in pre-reform FOBT dependence, it would feel much more like a market-structure paper and less like a sectoral before/after.
- **Frame it as a general lesson in regulating salient physical products when harm migrates to less visible digital channels.** That would connect to platform substitution and regulatory salience in a broader way.

The single biggest route to “bigger” is: turn this from a narrow gambling paper into a general paper about how to measure substitution after targeted product regulation in markets undergoing secular channel change.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

### Closest neighbors

The closest literatures seem to be:

1. **Sin-good regulation / self-control / internalities**
   - Gruber and Kőszegi (2001, 2004)-type work on cigarette taxation and addiction/self-control
   - Allcott, Lockwood, and Taubinsky (2019) on regressive sin taxes and internalities
   - Bernheim and Rangel (2004, 2005)-style behavioral welfare foundations for addiction

2. **Substitution and unintended consequences of regulation**
   - Adda and Cornaglia on smoking restrictions and substitution margins
   - Adda, Bertrand, Monfardini, and others on policy-induced substitution or displacement
   - Miron and Zwiebel “balloon effect” logic, though that citation is more slogan than core economics anchor

3. **Gambling economics**
   - Kearney (2005) on state lotteries and household spending
   - Grinols and Mustard / Grinols and Omorov on casinos and local outcomes
   - Possibly Humphreys and Perez on sports betting / gambling demand depending on exact scope
   - Any UK-specific gambling policy or machine-gambling public health work, even if not economics

4. **Digitization / channel substitution**
   - Papers on online versus offline substitution under regulation, whether in alcohol, cigarettes, finance, or retail

### How should the paper position itself relative to those neighbors?

Mostly **build on and synthesize**, not attack. The right move is:

- From the sin-goods literature: “We know regulation can curb harmful consumption, but effectiveness depends on substitution.”
- From the substitution literature: “Existing work shows alternative margins matter; here the overlooked issue is secular channel growth, which can mechanically overstate policy-induced substitution.”
- From gambling economics: “This setting is unusually clean because one product was sharply constrained while the broader market remained legal.”
- From digitization/platform work: “In markets moving online, policy evaluation must separate migration caused by regulation from migration that would have happened anyway.”

That is a valuable synthesis if done well.

### Is the paper positioned too narrowly or too broadly?

Currently, oddly, both.

- **Too narrowly** in data and institutional focus: it feels like a UK gambling-industry note.
- **Too broadly** in rhetorical ambition: it jumps from FOBTs to alcohol, tobacco, cannabis, and salience without fully earning those connections.

It needs a more disciplined broad framing: pick one central conversation and stay in it. My advice is to anchor in **product regulation under channel substitution**. That is broad enough for AER and specific enough to be coherent.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?

It seems under-engaged with:
- the economics of substitution under partial prohibition or selective regulation;
- digital-channel migration/platform substitution;
- public health and behavioral economics work on machine gambling specifically;
- local market adjustment and retail closures if it wants to talk about restructuring of British gambling rather than just revenue effects.

The paper also cites “there are zero NBER working papers…” which is a tell that the literature positioning is not yet mature. That sentence should be deleted.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?

Not quite. The paper thinks it is in the gambling-policy conversation. For AER, it needs to be in the **economics of targeted regulation in markets with substitutes** conversation. Gambling is the setting, not the audience.

An even more impactful framing might connect to the economics of regulating **salient offline products when demand can move to less salient digital environments**. That link to salience/platforms is intriguing but currently underdeveloped and tacked on late.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Before this paper, the world looks like this: regulators increasingly target specific harmful products; economists know that substitution can undermine such policies; and many sectors are experiencing rapid movement from physical to digital channels.

### Tension

The tension is straightforward and good: when the UK effectively killed FOBTs, did gambling actually fall, or did it just move online? And more subtly: are standard pre-post comparisons misleading because the online channel was already expanding fast?

### Resolution

The paper’s resolution is that the targeted machine market was almost entirely eliminated, land-based betting fell sharply, and online substitution was much smaller than naive comparisons suggest after accounting for underlying remote-growth trends.

### Implications

The implication is that product-level regulation can have real bite even in the presence of substitutes, but evaluating such policies requires a credible counterfactual for the substitute channel. More broadly, policymakers may overstate the “balloon effect” if they confuse secular platform migration with policy-induced substitution.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the ingredients of one, but the current manuscript is still closer to a collection of results than a fully integrated story. The order is:

- dramatic policy shock,
- sector-level DiD,
- mechanism decomposition,
- online substitution,
- some broader claims.

This is fine structurally, but the narrative hierarchy is not settled. Is the paper mainly about:
1. whether the reform reduced betting revenue?
2. whether it specifically hit machines?
3. whether online substitution was modest?
4. what this says about product regulation more generally?

The answer should be #4, supported by #1-#3. Right now #1 gets top billing because it is the regression result. For AER purposes, the story should be:

- **Setup:** Product regulation may fail because demand shifts to substitutes.
- **Tension:** In digitizing markets, even measured substitution is hard because substitutes were already growing.
- **Resolution:** In this setting, the targeted product disappears and trend-adjusted substitution is limited.
- **Implication:** Economists and policymakers should rethink how they evaluate selective bans in multi-channel markets.

That is the story the paper should tell.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party?

I would lead with: “The UK basically killed off high-stakes betting machines overnight, and the striking thing is that most of the apparent online replacement was probably going to happen anyway.”

That is much better than “betting-sector GGY fell by 24 log points.”

### Would people lean in?

Some would, especially those interested in regulation, behavioral economics, or digitization. But many economists would immediately ask: “Interesting, but is this just a niche gambling policy case?” That is the hurdle.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Probably one of these:
- “Did total gambling actually fall, or just land-based gambling?”
- “Do we learn anything about harm, not just revenue?”
- “How general is this beyond UK gambling?”
- “Why should I believe the online trend adjustment is the right counterfactual?”

Those are exactly the questions the introduction and framing need to anticipate.

### If findings are modest or null, is that okay?

The findings are not null, but they are modest in broader welfare terms because the paper ultimately has revenue effects, not direct harm effects. The paper does make a plausible case that “the balloon effect is overstated” is an interesting finding. But it needs to make that case much more sharply. Otherwise the paper risks reading like: “the policy reduced one sector’s revenues, and online rose somewhat, but maybe not due to the policy.” That feels smaller than it should.
