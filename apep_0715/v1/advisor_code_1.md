**Idea Fidelity**  
The manuscript diverges materially from the idea manifest. The original proposal emphasized exploiting cross-local-authority variation in pre-reform FOBT density to construct a continuous difference-in-differences and to link betting shop closures to outcomes such as local vacancy rates, problem gambling admissions, and changes in media tone. In contrast, the paper presented here operates at the sector level: it compares aggregate GGY in the treated betting sector to three untreated sectors (casinos, bingo, arcades), and it stops short of fielding the promised local- or individual-level outcomes. The richer identification strategy based on within-LA heterogeneity and the broader set of outcomes (e.g., NHS gambling disorder admissions) are absent. As a result, the paper addresses a different question—how the betting sector as a whole responded relative to other land-based sectors—rather than the more precise question raised in the manifest about heterogeneous impacts across local authorities and on harm indicators.

**Summary**  
This paper examines the UK’s April 2019 Fixed-Odds Betting Terminal (FOBT) stake reduction by comparing annual gross gambling yield (GGY) in the treated betting sector to three unaffected sectors (casinos, bingo, arcades) before and after the reform. The author finds that betting GGY fell by roughly 21 percent relative to controls in the year immediately following the reform, that the decline was driven almost entirely by machine revenue, and that remote (online) betting absorbed about 38 percent of the lost land-based revenue, leaving total betting expenditure down only 10 percent. The paper interprets these results as evidence that product-level regulation can eliminate the targeted product but is partially offset by substitution to online gambling.

**Essential Points**

1. **Identification strategy and interpretation of the DiD.**  
   The paper interprets the sector-level DiD as a clean evaluation of the FOBT reform, yet the key identifying assumption is that the untreated sectors—casinos, bingo, and arcades—provide a valid counterfactual for betting. These sectors differ from betting in numerous structural ways (average clientele, regulatory trends, online penetration, geographic concentration, exposure to macro shocks such as COVID), and the paper provides limited evidence that sectoral fixed effects and time dummies alone are sufficient to satisfy parallel trends. Without richer controls or a more granular design, it is difficult to attribute the estimated decline in betting GGY purely to the FOBT rule rather than to sector-specific shocks (e.g., competitiveness from online casino offerings, demographic changes). The placebo tests and event study show flat pre-trends, but with only four sector-level units, these diagnostics have limited power and may not rule out differential trends. I therefore recommend re-specifying the empirical approach to either (a) leverage more granular data (e.g., firms within sectors or geographic subunits) where pre-trends can be more credibly assessed, or (b) augment the sector-level DiD with auxiliary evidence (e.g., matching betting shops to local exposure, exploiting continuous pre-reform FOBT density variation) to better isolate the treatment effect.

2. **Inference with very few clusters and omitted heterogeneity.**  
   The primary analysis relies on 48 sector-year observations (four sectors × 12 years), and the treatment is a single interaction term. This raises red flags about inference: with four clusters (sectors), cluster-robust standard errors are unreliable, but the heteroskedasticity-robust standard errors reported may still understate uncertainty because they do not reflect the panel’s small cross-sectional dimension. Moreover, treating the post-period as beginning FY2020 conflates a one-off shock with a permanent regime change. The wide confidence intervals in the full post-sample (FY2009–2023) underline how sensitive the results are to later years. Consider two remedies: (i) conduct inference via randomization/permutation inference over the treated sector, leveraging the small number of possible “treatment assignments” to construct exact or approximate \(p\)-values; (ii) move to a panel at the operator or local-authority level so there are many treated and untreated units, enabling standard clustered inference. Without such adjustments, the strong claims about precision (e.g., the 24 log-point decline) may be overstated.

3. **Scope of outcomes and welfare interpretation.**  
   The paper identifies a substitution into remote betting but stops short of linking this to the harm-reduction question motivating the policy. The manifest suggested evaluating problem gambling, closures, vacancy rates, and media sentiment; none of these indicators appear in the current version. Moreover, the 38 percent substitution rate is based on a single-year turnaround in aggregate revenues, but it does not tell us whether the remaining 62 percent of lost land-based revenue represents reduced harm or merely a shift to less visible yet still harmful products. The welfare discussion therefore rests on assumptions about harm per pound rather than evidence. To support stronger welfare conclusions, the paper should engage more directly with harm indicators or argue more clearly why the revenue-based substitution rate is informative for welfare. For example, can the author show that NHS hospital admissions fell in areas with more FOBTs, or that betting shop closures coincided with changes in local vacancy rates? If such data are unavailable, the discussion should explicitly state the limits of revenue-based inference.

**Suggestions**

1. **Re-align the empirical strategy with the manifest’s design.**  
   The original idea featured cross-LA variation in pre-reform FOBT density and a continuous treatment intensity, which would have supported richer inference about heterogeneity and treatment effect scaling. If such data are available, I strongly encourage the author to (re-)construct an LA-level panel where the intensity of the stake reduction is measured by pre-reform machine density or GGY. With roughly 370 LAs, this would dramatically increase the number of treated and control units, allow for flexible functional forms (e.g., binning density into quantiles, fitting dose–response curves), and make it feasible to include LA and time fixed effects. This approach would also reduce concerns about differential trends because one could balance on pre-trends across many LAs (e.g., via matching or conditioning on rich controls such as demographics, unemployment, or prior gambling exposure). Even if the focus remains sector-level, presenting evidence from the planned LA-level variation would strengthen the causal story and connect the paper back to the original research question.

2. **Expand the outcome set and mechanism analysis.**  
   The paper already contains useful descriptive statistics on GGY decomposition and machine counts. Building on that, consider incorporating additional well-defined outcome variables to speak to the policy’s welfare implications. Potential candidates include: (i) NHS hospital admissions for gambling disorders (available quarterly), (ii) problem gambling prevalence from the Health Survey for England, (iii) betting shop vacancy rates or retail high-street indicators from local authority data, (iv) licensing data capturing closures and openings, and (v) local-area deprivation indices interacting with treatment intensity to explore distributional effects. Even if these outcomes are not causal, showing that the bet sector decline corresponds with declines in harm-sensitive indicators (or documenting the absence thereof) would make the welfare argument more concrete. If these data are not in hand, the paper should acknowledge those limitations and avoid overreaching on welfare claims.

3. **Refine the substitution analysis.**  
   The finding that remote betting GGY grew while land-based betting fell is compelling, but the interpretation of the 38 percent substitution rate can be clarified. For instance, consider estimating a “revenue transfer function” by regressing remote betting GGY or betting shop closures on pre-reform FOBT density, possibly using IV if there is concern about endogeneity (e.g., areas with more machines might have been more likely to grow online even absent the reform). Additionally, the paper could examine whether online growth was led by firms that closed many shops, or whether remote gains are concentrated in operators active both online and offline. This would help disentangle substitution at the firm versus consumer level. Finally, the remote channel is not affected by FOBTs by design, but online products may have simultaneously experienced regulatory or technological shocks; discussing these in more depth would contextualize the substitution rate.

4. **Strengthen robustness and inference procedures.**  
   To bolster inference given the small number of sectors, I encourage the author to (i) present permutation or randomization-based \(p\)-values by reassigning the treatment label to different sectors and recomputing the DiD coefficient, possibly allowing for a more credible assessment of statistical significance, and (ii) explore alternative control groups (e.g., splitting the pre-reform data into two periods and performing synthetic control-type weights) to assess sensitivity. If additional data permit, cluster at a more granular level (e.g., sector × region) to obtain more than four clusters. If the sector-level design must be retained, be transparent about the limitations of heteroskedasticity-robust standard errors in this context.

5. **Clarify timing and sample selection.**  
   The event study uses fiscal years, meaning the post period includes only one week of the COVID lockdown (March 2020). It would be helpful to clarify how the fiscal year structure interacts with the reform timing and whether any fiscal year (e.g., FY2021) captures substantial pandemic effects that could confound the estimates. Including a plot of the raw GGY series by sector would help readers assess nonlinearity and structural breaks. Additionally, consider re-estimating the main specification with the post period defined more narrowly (e.g., using quarterly data if available) to isolate the immediate effect of the reform and avoid contamination from subsequent shocks.

6. **Improve discussion of external validity.**  
   The paper makes the ambitious claim that the FOBT episode “provides the clearest evidence on what product-level regulation can and cannot achieve.” While the case is interesting, product-level substitution dynamics depend heavily on the regulatory complement (e.g., whether online gambling is also regulated) and market structure. Explicitly discussing the limits of generalizing from the UK betting sector to other addictive goods (e.g., cigarettes, alcohol) would make the policy implications more balanced. For example, highlight differences such as the relative ease of migrating to online platforms in gambling versus cross-border purchasing in alcohol.

Overall, the paper examines an important policy with novel data, but it would benefit from aligning more closely with the original identification strategy, expanding the empirical scope, and strengthening inference given the limited cross-sectional variation.
