**Summary**

Leveraging detailed functional spending data for 162 Zurich municipalities over 1990–2024, the paper uses Callaway and Sant’Anna’s staggered DiD estimator to evaluate the fiscal consequences of eight municipal merger events. The primary finding is that mergers sharply reduce administration spending (≈CHF 120 per capita, a 33% drop) while leaving nine service-delivery categories statistically unchanged, a pattern the author dubs the “overhead illusion.” Robustness checks (Sun–Abraham, leave-one-cohort-out, placebo on equalization transfers) support the administrative savings result.

**Essential Points**

1. **External validity and scope of inference.** The paper’s title and framing imply general lessons about “municipal consolidation” and “merger advocates worldwide,” but the empirical analysis is confined to eight merger events within a single canton. Key contextual drivers (e.g., the nature of services managed, the absence of fiscal distress, the cantonal incentive structure) may not generalize to other Swiss cantons or countries. The manuscript should either broaden the sample (as originally planned) or substantially temper the broader policy claims. If data limitations preclude a national sample, the paper needs to explain why these eight events are representative and discuss how the results might differ in, say, cantons with larger municipalities or different service portfolios.

2. **Credibility of the parallel trends assumption across functions.** The main test of identification is an event study for administration spending, yet the paper draws inference for other functions (education, health, transport, etc.) from zero estimates. If the control group systematically differs from treated municipalities (for instance, in size, fiscal capacity, or pre-trends), the null results may reflect differential trends or low power rather than true zeros. The manuscript currently lacks function-specific pre-trend evidence or placebo tests beyond finance and taxes. Provide event-study graphs (or at least coefficients with confidence intervals) for each key function, or demonstrate parallel trends through other means (e.g., pooled leads, falsification outcomes). Without this, the interpretation that services exhibit “precisely estimated zeros” is tentative.

3. **Small treated sample and choice of control group.** Eight treated municipalities (absorbing 18 dissolved units) may not provide enough variation for precise estimation of heterogeneous effects, particularly for service functions. Moreover, treated municipalities are small rural communes, while the control group comprises all never-merged municipalities in Zurich, many of which are substantially larger and may have different trends. Consider constructing a more comparable control group (e.g., matching on pre-merger spending trajectories, population size, or proximity) or demonstrating robustness to dropping large controls. Additionally, address how the small number of treated units affects the reliability of the bootstrap standard errors in the C&S estimator. If feasible, aggregate some functions or pool similar services to increase precision.

**Suggestions**

1. **Expand the empirical scope or clarify limitations.** If data are available for other cantons (as suggested in the manifest), incorporating additional merger events would strengthen the analysis, increase statistical power, and support broader claims. At minimum, include a table summarizing the nationwide merger variation, explain why the paper focuses on Zurich (data quality, functional disaggregation, institutional similarity), and explicitly state that the results pertain to this canton. A brief discussion comparing Zurich’s mergers to those in other cantons (in terms of size, timing, or incentives) would help readers assess external validity.

2. **Enhance diagnostics for the parallel trends assumption across functions.** Present function-specific event-study plots (or tables of lead coefficients) to reassure readers that treated and control units evolve similarly prior to each merger across all categories, not just administration. Where pre-treatment trends diverge, consider adding function-specific linear time trends or restricting the estimation window. You might also exploit placebo outcomes with similar patterns (e.g., other categories not expected to change) to show the estimator is well-behaved.

3. **Strengthen control group comparability.** Construct “synthetic” control groups by matching treated successor municipalities to never-merged peers on pre-merger characteristics (population, GDP proxies, spending levels, slope of trends). Alternatively, restrict the control sample to municipalities with similar pre-2014 spending profiles or population sizes. Report how these restrictions affect the ATT estimates for administration and other functions. This exercise would also help address concerns that never-merged municipalities are on different trajectories due to size or policy differences.

4. **Report statistical power and precision concerns.** Given the small number of treated municipalities, provide a discussion (perhaps in an appendix) of the minimum detectable effect sizes for key functions. This contextualizes the “null” findings: are you powered to detect, say, a 5% change in education spending? If not, describe the implications for interpreting the absence of service effects. Consider pooling similar functions or expressing results in standardized effect-size terms (already done in the appendix) to aid interpretation.

5. **Clarify mechanism and timing.** The narrative emphasizes overhead elimination as the mechanism, supported by larger effects for larger mergers. To strengthen this claim, examine whether administrative layoffs, council reductions, or staffing changes accompany the spending drops (if data exist) or at least discuss institutional evidence (e.g., cantonal guidelines on administrative structures). Additionally, explore dynamic effects: do administration savings persist, increase, or fade over time? Showing ATT estimates by post-merger year (if not already done in event study) would help determine whether the overhead illusion is immediate or evolves.

6. **Deepen the placebo analysis.** The finance and taxes placebo is useful, but consider additional falsification tests: functions theoretically unaffected by overhead (e.g., culture, environment) should also show null effects—report their event studies. Alternatively, test for pre-treatment leads of treated units in outcomes that should not be affected by mergers (e.g., population growth rates). This reinforces confidence that the administrative effect is not an artefact of confounding trends.

7. **Discuss implications for policymakers more cautiously.** The “overhead illusion” framing is provocative but hinges on interpreting zero service effects as evidence against genuine scale economies. Acknowledge that service effects may materialize slowly or in functions not captured (e.g., infrastructure investment) and that the absence of observed savings does not imply mergers are inefficient overall—especially if they affect outcomes beyond spending (quality, coordination). Likewise, when comparing mergers with inter-municipal cooperation, reference literature on cooperation’s fiscal impact to avoid overstating the case.

In sum, the paper presents an interesting decomposition of municipal merger spending effects using modern estimators, but it should bolster its identification diagnostics, clarify the scope of inference, and address concerns arising from the limited treated sample. Expanding the analysis or tempering claims accordingly will enhance the paper’s contribution to both the municipal finance and DiD literatures.
