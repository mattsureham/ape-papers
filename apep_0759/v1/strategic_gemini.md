### 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper uses a 2020 regulatory shift to test whether "cutting red tape" in federal procurement actually works. By comparing contracts just above and below the new $250,000 "simplified" threshold, the author finds that reducing paperwork requirements had zero effect on the number of bidders, small business participation, or the prevalence of sole-source awards. A busy economist should care because this suggests that for a $15 billion slice of the economy, the entire apparatus of "procedural safeguards" is a massive administrative sinkhole that accomplishes nothing—yet removing it doesn't solve the entry problem either.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this well, but it buries the "so what." It currently frames the null as "inframarginality." To be AER-ready, the pitch needs to emphasize the **institutional waste** and the **puzzle of the missing entry**. 

*Suggested revision for the first two paragraphs:* 
"The U.S. government spends $15 billion annually on contracts in the $150k-$250k range, historically subject to 'full-and-open' competition rules meant to prevent waste and encourage entry. In 2020, these rules were overnight replaced by 'simplified' procedures. I find this deregulation had exactly zero effect on any meaningful outcome. This represents a double-failure: the original regulations provided no measurable protection against favoritism, and their removal provided no measurable relief to the 'paperwork-averse' small businesses policymakers hoped to attract."

### 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:** The paper provides the first causal evidence that for mid-range federal contracts, the "margin of regulation" is allocatively inert—procedural complexity is neither a binding barrier to entry nor a meaningful safeguard against corruption.

*   **Differentiation:** It moves beyond the "active vs. passive waste" of **Bandiera et al. (2009)** by showing a third category: **neutral waste**—regulation that is costly to perform but has no impact on outcomes.
*   **Framing:** It is currently framed as answering a question about the WORLD (how to design procurement), which is a strength.
*   **Clarity:** A smart economist would say: "It’s a DiD on the SAT threshold that finds a precise null across the board."
*   **Make it bigger:** To be truly AER-sized, the paper needs to explore **why** the null exists. Is it because the "simplified" procedures aren't actually simple? Is it because the same bureaucrats are making the decisions? Adding a "Mechanism" section using text analysis of contract descriptions or looking at bureaucrat experience would make the contribution much larger.

### 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

*   **Closest Neighbors:** **Bandiera, Prat, and Valletti (2009)** on waste; **Bosio et al. (2022)** on the Shleifer-style regulation of entry; **Coviello et al. (2018)** on discretion in procurement; **Krasnokutskaya and Seim (2011)** on bid preferences.
*   **Positioning:** It should position itself as a "bridge" between the *Public Choice* view (regulation is for rent-seeking) and the *Public Administration* view (regulation is for accountability). Finding a null for both suggests a new "Bureaucratic Inertia" view.
*   **Missing Lit:** It needs to speak to the **Industrial Organization of Entry**. If the costs of bidding go down but no one new enters, it suggests "Relationship Capital" (e.g., **Best et al. 2023**) is a much higher wall than the FAR. 

### 4. NARRATIVE ARC

*   **Setup:** The government spends $700B; we assume the "rules" of the game (the FAR) matter for who plays and who wins.
*   **Tension:** We expect either a "Deregulation Dividend" (more small biz entry) or a "Corruption Cost" (more sole-sourcing).
*   **Resolution:** Neither happens. The results are a "Striking Null."
*   **Implications:** The "Invisible Barriers" (security clearances, past performance) are the real story, not the forms.

**Evaluation:** The arc is clean but lacks a "Resolution 2.0." An AER paper shouldn't just say "nothing happened"; it should explain why nothing happened.

### 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

**The Dinner Party Fact:** "The federal government stopped requiring 'full-and-open competition' for 75,000 contracts a year, and the number of bidders didn't change by even a fraction of a person."
**The Response:** People would lean in, then immediately ask: *"Wait, so does that mean the 'simplified' procedures are actually just as hard as the 'complex' ones?"* This is the follow-up the paper must answer to be a lead article.

### 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

*   **Front-load:** The "Compliance vs. Oversight" theoretical tension (Section 2.3) should be in the Intro.
*   **Appendix:** The summary statistics (Table 1) are fine, but I want to see a density plot of contract values around the $150k/$250k thresholds (to show no bunching).
*   **Conclusion:** Needs to be more than a summary. It should be a "Call to Action" for procurement research to focus on market structure rather than procedural forms.

### 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

**The Gap:** Currently, this is a very high-quality *Applied Economics Letters* or *Journal of Public Economics* paper. It is a "clean null." To reach the AER, it needs **ambition**.

**The Single Most Impactful Advice:** Open the "Black Box" of what actually changed. Use the text of the solicitations. Did the number of pages in the RFP actually decrease? Did the time from posting to award actually drop? If the "Simplified" procedures didn't actually simplify the *experience* for the firm, the null is mechanical. If they *did* simplify the experience but entry still didn't happen, the null is a profound discovery about the nature of government-firm relationships.
