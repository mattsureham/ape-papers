## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
The paper uses 21 million linked census records to investigate how the massive labor supply shock of the 1924 Johnson-Reed Act affected the career trajectories of native-born American workers. It asks whether the sudden removal of millions of low-skill immigrants allowed native workers to "upgrade" into better jobs or whether it destroyed productive complementarities that helped natives advance. This matters because it provides a historical "smoking gun" for the debate on whether immigration is a zero-sum competition or a source of task-based synergy.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this reasonably well, but it buries the "complementarity vs. competition" tension in the second paragraph. The first paragraph is a bit heavy on historical statistics.
**The pitch it should have:** 
> "Does restricting immigration help native workers move up the economic ladder? Using the 1924 Johnson-Reed Act—the largest labor supply contraction in U.S. history—and 21 million linked individual census records, I show that closing the 'Golden Door' actually slowed native occupational advancement. These results suggest that immigrants and natives are fundamental complements, and that removing the bottom rung of the labor market makes it harder for everyone else to climb."

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**Contribution:** The paper provides the first individual-level longitudinal evidence that the 1924 immigration quotas reduced native occupational mobility by disrupting task complementarities.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It differentiates itself well from Tabellini (2020) and Abramitzky et al. by moving from aggregate city/county outcomes to individual "trajectories." This is a major selling point.
*   **Framing:** It is framed as a question about the WORLD (competition vs. complementarity), which is strong.
*   **Clarity:** A smart economist would say: "It's a huge-N linked data paper showing the 1924 Act backfired for native careers because they lost the manual laborers they were supervising."
*   **Bigger Contribution:** To make it even bigger, the author needs to bridge the gap between their "Table 4" (which looks like competition) and "Table 5" (which shows complementarity). The paper's "aha!" moment is that the cross-section is a lie; the author needs to make that "methodological warning" a more central part of the story.

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
*   **Closest Neighbors:** Tabellini (2020) on 1924 political/economic effects; Abramitzky, Boustan, & Eriksson (2014, 2021) on historical mobility; Foged & Peri (2016) on modern Danish complementarity.
*   **Positioning:** It builds on the "New Economic History" (linked data) to arbitrate a classic Labor debate. It synthesizes the historical 1924 literature with the modern "Task Specialization" literature (Peri/Sparber).
*   **Audience:** It's currently a bit too focused on "Historical Labor." To reach a broader AER audience, it should speak more to **General Macro/Growth**—is immigration a "general purpose technology" for labor markets?
*   **Missing Conversation:** It needs to speak more to the **Internal Migration** literature. If the immigrants disappeared, did the Great Migration of Black southerners fill the gap? The paper briefly mentions this in the robustness section (page 12), but it’s a massive potential confounder or mechanism that deserves more than a paragraph.

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC
*   **Setup:** The U.S. labor market was booming with 20m immigrants; then the 1924 Act shut it down.
*   **Tension:** Does this help natives (less competition) or hurt them (less complementarity)?
*   **Resolution:** At first glance, it looks like it helps (cross-section), but the "truth" revealed by the stacked DDD is that it hurt. The quota acted as a "brake" on native career progression.
*   **Implications:** Immigration policy isn't just about the bottom of the skill distribution; it’s about the structural health of the whole ladder.

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong, but the author spends too much time on the "failed" cross-section. While it’s a good teaching moment, the AER reader wants to get to the DDD resolution faster.

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
*   **The Lead Fact:** "When the U.S. kicked out the immigrants in 1924, native-born supervisors actually saw their career advancement slow down by 8%."
*   **Reaction:** Lean in. It’s counter-intuitive to the "common sense" view that less competition is always better for workers.
*   **Follow-up:** "Wait, if the high-skill natives lost out, who won? Did capital owners just replace them with machines?" (The author should try to answer this).

---

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
*   **Front-loading:** The "failed" cross-section (Section 5.1-5.4) is too long. It feels like the author is showing their work rather than telling the story. Move the bulk of the cross-sectional results to the appendix and start the results section with the DDD.
*   **Mechanisms:** The "Why?" is a bit thin. The author points to "Task Specialization," but with 21 million records, can we see if this happened specifically in manufacturing? Or in cities vs. rural areas? The "Farm Exit" result (Table 3) is fascinating and should be elevated.

---

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The paper is currently a "Very Good Labor Paper." To be an "AER Paper," it needs **Ambition.** 

The single biggest gap is the **"General Equilibrium"** problem. If natives in high-quota counties did worse, did they just move to other counties? Table 3 shows no effect on geographic mobility, which is a start, but the author needs to prove that the 1924 Act didn't just relocate economic activity rather than shrinking it.

**Single most impactful piece of advice:** Focus the entire paper on the "Disruption of the Ladder" narrative—explicitly link the "Farm Exit" (Table 3) to the "High-Skill Loss" (Table 5) to show how immigration restriction froze the structural transformation of the American workforce.
