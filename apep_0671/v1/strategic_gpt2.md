## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a classic first-order question in labor economics and economic history: when the United States sharply restricted immigration in 1924, did native-born workers move up the occupational ladder, or did cutting immigration actually slow native advancement? Using linked individual census records and geographic variation in pre-1924 exposure to the now-restricted immigrant groups, the paper argues that the quota reduced natives’ occupational mobility, implying that immigrant-native complementarities outweighed labor market competition.

A busy economist should care because this is not just “another historical immigration paper.” The 1924 Act is one of the cleanest, largest immigration restrictions in U.S. history, and the paper tries to bring individual-level evidence to a debate usually fought with aggregate outcomes: do natives gain when immigration is shut off?

### Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Not quite. The first two paragraphs are competent, but they are too generic and too theory-forward. They announce a famous policy shock and the competition-vs-complementarity framework, but they do not yet tell the reader what is genuinely new here: this paper uses linked individual records to study **occupational trajectories**, not just wages or employment levels, around one of the biggest immigration restrictions in American history.

The introduction gets stronger in paragraphs 3–5, but by then the paper has already made the reader work too hard. The opening should immediately foreground the striking substantive question and the novel margin of adjustment.

### The pitch the paper should have

“Did closing America’s borders in 1924 help native workers climb the job ladder? This paper studies the largest immigration restriction in U.S. history using linked census records that follow millions of native-born workers over time. I show that in places most exposed to the cutoff in Southern and Eastern European immigration, native occupational advancement slowed rather than accelerated, suggesting that immigrants were often complements in production rather than direct substitutes.”

That is the version that belongs in the first two paragraphs.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### Contribution in one sentence

The paper’s contribution is to provide individual-level evidence that the 1924 Immigration Act reduced native-born workers’ occupational advancement in more exposed places, implying that restricting immigration disrupted complementarities rather than benefiting native competitors.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest papers?

Only partly. The paper knows the broad literature, but the differentiation is still blurrier than it should be. Right now the contribution sounds like a combination of:

- a historical immigration restriction paper,
- a task-specialization/complementarity paper,
- and a linked-census paper.

That mix is potentially strong, but the paper has not yet chosen which of those is the main axis of novelty.

The strongest differentiator is not “first individual-level evidence on the 1924 Act” by itself. “First” claims are often fragile and not enough for AER. The stronger claim is:

- prior work studies wages, employment, politics, assimilation, or aggregate urban outcomes;
- this paper studies **individual occupational mobility** as the key adjustment margin;
- and it does so in response to an unusually large, policy-driven contraction in immigrant labor supply.

That is a real contribution, but it needs sharper contrast with the nearest neighbors.

### Is the contribution framed as a question about the world or a gap in the literature?

Mostly as a question about the world, which is good. “Did restricting immigration help natives?” is much stronger than “the literature has not looked at individual mobility after the 1924 Act.” The paper should lean even harder into the world question and use the literature only to show why existing evidence cannot answer it cleanly.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the intro?

Not confidently. Right now a smart economist might say:

> “It’s a historical immigration paper using linked census data to look at occupational mobility after the 1924 Act.”

That is decent, but it is still one step too close to “another DiD paper about immigration.” What they should be able to say is:

> “It shows that one of the biggest immigration restrictions in U.S. history slowed natives’ upward mobility, and the novelty is that you can see it at the individual occupational-transition level.”

That sharper formulation does not quite come through.

### What would make the contribution bigger?

Most importantly: commit to **occupational mobility as the central object**, and make that feel indispensable rather than incidental.

Specific ways to make it bigger:
1. **Elevate the transition margin over the score change.** Occupational upgrading/downgrading, movement into supervisory/clerical work, and transitions out of manual work are more intuitive and memorable than changes in OCCSCORE.
2. **Make mechanisms concrete.** “Complementarity” is currently abstract. Show the specific ladder: immigrant manual labor supporting native clerical, supervisory, skilled-trade, or sales occupations.
3. **Use a more vivid comparison.** The best version of the paper is not “restriction lowered OCCSCORE by x.” It is “when immigrant inflows were cut, natives were less likely to move from manual to supervisory/clerical roles.”
4. **Clarify whether this is about mobility, productivity, or labor demand structure.** Right now it gestures at all three. Pick one dominant framing.
5. **Resolve internal inconsistencies in results presentation.** As written, the paper at times sounds like a competition paper in the cross-section and a complementarity paper in the stacked design. That may be empirically interesting, but narratively it is muddled.

At present, slightly too broadly in ambition and too narrowly in actual execution.

That is too much for what is currently a single core empirical exercise. The paper should narrow the rhetorical spread and sharpen the target audience: labor economists and economic historians interested in the effects of immigration restriction on native career mobility.

It should speak more directly to:

Right now the paper knows the immigration literature, but not enough the literature on career ladders and occupational transitions.

Almost, but not quite. That is a more distinctive and potentially more impactful conversation.

The unexpected but fruitful connection would be:
- not just immigration and wages,
- but immigration and the organization of work.

That is where the paper has a chance to feel fresher.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Before this paper, we know that the 1924 Act was a massive immigration restriction and that there is a long-running debate over whether immigrants compete with or complement native workers. We also know a fair amount about aggregate local effects of immigration, but much less about how individual native workers’ careers adjusted.

### Tension

The key tension is good: if immigration hurts competing natives, then cutting immigration should help them move up. But if immigrants and natives were organized into complementary tasks, restriction could actually slow natives’ advancement. Aggregate evidence cannot cleanly distinguish these channels, and raw cross-sectional patterns are contaminated by the fact that immigrants settled in dynamic places.

### Resolution

The paper’s intended resolution is: once you compare the pre- and post-quota relationship, natives in more exposed counties experienced slower occupational advancement after the quota. That is the central finding.

### Implications

The implication is that immigration restriction can destroy productive complementarities and reduce natives’ upward mobility, even if a simple competition story predicts gains for low-skill workers.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the pieces of one, but not a clean arc. At the moment it reads too much like:
1. raw result,
2. adjusted result,
3. placebo result,
4. new stacked result,
5. several outcome tables,
6. heterogeneity,
7. discussion.

That is a results sequence, not a fully disciplined story.

More importantly, the paper contains a serious **narrative dislocation**: parts of the introduction and discussion emphasize that the complementarity loss is strongest for high-skill workers and negative across skill groups, while the earlier heterogeneity section in the 1920–1930 cross-section reports the opposite pattern, which is explicitly said to fit a competition model. Even if that is empirically correct, it needs to be narratively integrated as “naive post-period heterogeneity points one way; once benchmarked against pre-trends, the inference reverses.” Right now it reads almost like two different papers stitched together.

### What story should it be telling?

The right story is:

- **Setup:** America shut the door in 1924; economists still argue whether that should have helped natives.
- **Tension:** The obvious post-1924 cross-section is misleading because immigrant-heavy places were already on different trajectories.
- **Resolution:** Comparing the same exposure gradient before and after the Act shows that cutting immigration slowed native occupational upgrading.
- **Implication:** The key margin is not just wages or employment but the career ladder itself; immigration supported native advancement by changing who did which tasks.

That is the story. Everything else should serve it.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?

“I’d lead with: when the U.S. slashed immigration in 1924, native workers in the most exposed places did not climb the occupational ladder faster — they climbed it more slowly.”

That is a good dinner-party fact.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

Economists would lean in, at least initially, because the setting is big and the sign is provocative. The 1924 Act is inherently interesting, and “restriction hurt natives” is counterintuitive enough to command attention.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Probably:  
“Interesting — but what exactly was the mechanism? Which occupations stopped growing, and for whom?”

That is where the paper currently underdelivers. It has suggestive heterogeneity and some binary transitions, but not yet a vivid enough account of the actual reallocation channel.

A second likely question:  
“Is this really about occupational mobility, or is it about broader county trends in industrial restructuring?”  
Again, that is not a referee-style identification complaint so much as a narrative issue: the paper needs to tell readers what concrete margin of adjustment they should take away.

### If findings are null or modest, is that okay?

The main result is not null. But some of the secondary outcomes are modest or mixed, and the paper should be careful not to oversell them. The most compelling fact is the sign reversal once one uses the pre-period and the resulting negative stacked estimate. The paper should not dilute that with too many side outcomes of uneven interpretability.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

At present, this is closer to an interesting field-journal or strong specialist-history/labor paper than an AER paper.

The gap is mainly:

### 1. A framing problem
The science may or may not be there, but the story is not yet maximally sharp. The paper has a big setting and a provocative result, but it has not yet distilled them into one crisp claim that readers will remember.

### 2. A scope problem
The paper wants to make a broad claim about complementarity, but the mechanism evidence is still fairly thin. For AER, “restriction reduced OCCSCORE” is not enough. The paper needs to show more concretely **what type of occupational ladder was disrupted**.

### 3. An ambition problem
The paper is competent but somewhat safe in the way it cashes out the result. The setting is enormous; the narrative should be bolder. It should ask not just whether natives gained, but how immigration shaped the organization of production and upward mobility in industrial America.

### 4. A novelty-positioning problem
“First individual-level evidence on the 1924 Act” is not enough by itself. The paper needs to persuade readers that this changes the way we think about immigration restriction, not merely adds one more estimate in a famous setting.

### Single most impactful advice

**Rebuild the paper around one memorable claim: the 1924 immigration cutoff slowed native workers’ movement into higher-ranked occupations, and show that channel concretely rather than mainly through reduced-form OCCSCORE regressions.**

That is the one change that would most increase the paper’s strategic position. If the author can make the occupational-reallocation mechanism vivid and central, the paper becomes much easier to sell as more than “another immigration design.”
