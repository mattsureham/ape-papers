### 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
The paper examines the employment consequences of automatic wage indexation by exploiting a unique natural experiment in Belgium. During the 2022-23 energy crisis, pre-existing collective bargaining agreements forced different sectors to adopt 10% mandatory wage increases at different speeds (immediate vs. quarterly vs. annual). This provides a rare, high-stakes setting to estimate labor demand elasticities for the entire economy—not just low-wage workers—during a period of high inflation.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this well in the abstract and intro, but it leans too heavily on the "Belgian institutional quirk" rather than the "Global Macro-Labor problem."
*   **The Pitch it should have:** "While economists have long debated the inflationary risks of wage-price spirals, we lack causal evidence on how mandatory indexation affects employment across the whole economy. This paper exploits a historical accident in Belgian bargaining timing to provide the first economy-wide estimate of how rapid, mandatory wage hikes destroy jobs during a cost-of-living crisis."

### 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**One-sentence contribution:** The paper provides a causal estimate of the labor demand elasticity for the aggregate economy (roughly -1.1) by exploiting exogenous timing variation in mandatory wage indexation during a massive inflationary shock.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It differentiates well from the 2015 "wage freeze" study (Vandekerckhove & Cockx) by looking at the *increase* phase, which allows for testing asymmetry.
*   **Framing:** It is currently framed as "answering a gap in a literature" (indexation is understudied). It needs to be framed as "answering a question about the world" (Do wage-price spirals lead to mass unemployment?).
*   **Big vs. Small:** A smart economist would see this as a "very clever DiD." To make it "AER Big," the author needs to connect these micro-elasticities to the macro-debate: does this indexation prevent a "soft landing"?

### 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
The paper sits at the intersection of **Labor Demand** (Cengiz et al., 2019) and **Macro-Inflation** (Blanchard, 2022; Lorenzoni & Werning, 2023).

*   **Closest Neighbors:** Dube (2019) on minimum wages; Bils & Klenow (2004) on price/wage rigidity; and the recent "Greedflation/Wage-Price" debate (Bernanke & Blanchard, 2023).
*   **The Conversation:** The paper is currently too polite. It should "attack" the standard minimum wage literature for only looking at the 10th percentile. This paper is about the *median* worker.
*   **Missing Links:** It needs to speak to the **Search and Matching** literature. If firms aren't firing but are "not hiring," that is a vacancy-channel story that belongs in the conversation with Diamond-Mortensen-Pissarides.

### 4. NARRATIVE ARC
*   **Setup:** The 2022 energy shock hits Europe.
*   **Tension:** Most workers lose real income; Belgian workers are protected by law, but this protection creates a massive cost shock for firms. Does this "protection" actually cause job losses?
*   **Resolution:** Yes. Sectors forced to pay early see immediate, significant employment drops compared to those who can delay.
*   **Implications:** Indexation design (timing) is a "free" policy lever to mitigate unemployment shocks without cutting real wages.

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong. It’s not just a collection of results; it’s a "warning" story for policymakers.

### 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
*   **The Dinner Party Fact:** "In Belgium, because of a 100-year-old law, some firms had to raise wages by 10% in six months while their neighbors waited a year—and the 'fast' firms lost 11% of their workforce relative to the 'slow' ones."
*   **Response:** People will lean in. It's a "clean" experiment for a "messy" global problem.
*   **The Follow-up:** "Did those jobs come back once the other sectors caught up?" (The paper starts to answer this with the "transitory" discussion on page 10, but needs more data).

### 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
*   **Front-load:** The "Institutional Background" (Section 2) is great but long. Move the specific list of NACE codes to an appendix. Get to the TWFE results faster.
*   **The "Private vs. Public" split:** This is a goldmine. It should be a centerpiece, not just a robustness check. It proves the "profit motive" mechanism.
*   **Conclusion:** It’s currently a bit dry. It needs to broaden the scope to the EU-wide debate on "competitiveness" vs. "social protection."

### 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
**The Gap:** Currently, the paper is a very high-quality *Applied Micro* paper. To be an *AER* paper, it needs to bridge to **Macro**.
*   **Single Most Impactful Advice:** Quantify the "Aggregate Counterfactual." Use your micro-elasticities to simulate: *What would Belgian unemployment have looked like if they had moved to an annual system in 2022?* This turns a "Belgian case study" into a "General Theory of Indexation Timing."
