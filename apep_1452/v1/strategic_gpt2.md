## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper studies a rare natural experiment in economy-wide wage setting: during the 2022–23 energy crisis, Belgium’s automatic wage indexation system forced large nominal wage increases, but sectoral bargaining rules caused those increases to hit different sectors at different times. The paper asks whether front-loaded mandatory wage increases reduced employment, and it argues that timing alone mattered: sectors forced to adjust wages earlier saw lower short-run employment.

A busy economist should care because this is one of the few settings where we can observe large, mandated wage increases outside the minimum-wage context, during a major inflation shock, with direct relevance for labor demand, wage rigidity, and the policy debate over indexation.

### Does the paper articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Reasonably well, but not optimally. The opening gets to the episode quickly, and the second paragraph explains the timing variation. Still, the current introduction leans too quickly into institutional detail (“pivot,” “joint committees,” “CP 200”) before fully establishing the broader question. The paper’s best asset is not “Belgian institutional nuance”; it is that Belgium offers a rare test of what happens when inflation mechanically becomes labor-cost growth at scale.

### What the first two paragraphs should say instead

**Paragraph 1:**  
In most countries, inflation erodes real wages before nominal pay catches up. In Belgium, by contrast, inflation is rapidly converted into nominal wage growth through automatic indexation. During the 2022–23 energy crisis, that system generated roughly 10 percent mandatory wage growth across the economy, creating a rare opportunity to study a first-order question in labor economics and macroeconomics: when a large inflation shock is mechanically passed through to wages, how much employment is lost?

**Paragraph 2:**  
The key empirical advantage is that Belgian sectors did not all absorb those mandated wage increases at the same time. Longstanding sectoral bargaining agreements—set well before the crisis—meant some sectors implemented the increases immediately, others quarterly, and others only in an annual January reset. I use this predetermined cross-sector timing variation to estimate the short-run employment effects of mandatory wage growth, and find that sectors forced to raise wages earlier experienced meaningfully lower employment, especially in the private sector.

That version leads with the world question, not the institutional plumbing.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### Contribution in one sentence

The paper provides evidence from Belgium’s 2022–23 inflation episode that earlier exposure to large mandatory wage indexation reduced short-run sectoral employment, offering a rare estimate of labor demand under economy-wide wage pass-through.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?

Only partially. The paper does make one important distinction: this is not a minimum-wage paper, not a firm-level bargaining paper, and not the 2015 Belgian wage-freeze episode. But the differentiation is still somewhat thin. Right now the paper risks sounding like:

- another reduced-form wage/employment elasticity paper,
- in one country,
- using staggered timing variation,
- around a special episode.

That is not yet enough for AER-level distinctiveness unless the paper sharpens exactly what is new about the economic object being estimated.

The closest differentiation should be:

1. **Economy-wide mandated wage growth**, not a wage floor affecting a subset of low-wage workers.
2. **Inflation-driven wage pass-through**, not discretionary policy or bargaining reform.
3. **Timing of wage adjustment**, not just the level of wages.
4. **A modern supply-shock setting**, directly relevant to post-COVID inflation debates.

At present, the paper says some of this, but not crisply enough.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or as filling a gap in a LITERATURE?

Mixed, but too much of the latter. The strongest version is a world question:

> When inflation is automatically converted into nominal wage increases, what happens to employment?

That is much better than:

> There is sparse evidence on automatic wage indexation.

The paper currently contains both framings, but the “sparse evidence” version is more visible than it should be.

### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain what’s new?

Barely. They could say: “It uses Belgium’s indexation timing differences to estimate employment effects of mandatory wage increases.” That is decent. But they might just as easily say: “It’s another DiD/TWFE paper on wage shocks.”

That is a warning sign. The introduction needs to turn the contribution from a design description into a substantive claim about how labor markets work under inflation-indexed wage setting.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Three possibilities, in descending order of value:

1. **Lean harder into the “timing versus level” distinction.**  
   The most original angle here is not merely that wages rose and employment fell; it is that sectors faced the same cumulative wage increase but on different schedules. That lets the paper speak to an underexplored question: does the *temporal structure* of wage adjustment matter for employment? This could be a much bigger contribution if developed as the central theme rather than a side note.

2. **Show adjustment margins beyond total employment.**  
   If the paper can distinguish hiring, separations, vacancies, hours, or temporary contracts—even descriptively—that would elevate it from “employment falls” to “how firms absorb indexed wage shocks.” Right now the mechanism remains generic.

3. **Connect to inflation transmission, not just labor demand.**  
   A bigger framing is: does automatic indexation transmit a supply shock into labor market contraction? That puts the paper in a live macro conversation rather than a narrow labor one.

If the author can only do one thing, it should be the first.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

### Closest neighbors

The paper’s closest neighbors appear to be:

1. **Vandekerckhove (2023)** on Belgium’s 2015 indexation suspension/wage freeze.
2. **Cengiz et al. (2019)** and **Dube (2019)** as points of contrast in the minimum wage literature.
3. Classic indexation papers such as **Fischer (1977)** and **Gray (1976)** for theory/background.
4. Recent macro-inflation discussion pieces such as **Blanchard** and **Lorenzoni** on wage-price spirals/post-COVID inflation.

There are also likely adjacent literatures the paper should engage more directly:

5. **Labor demand / payroll tax / wage-cost shock papers**—the broader literature on firm employment responses to labor cost changes, not just minimum wages.
6. **Collective bargaining and wage rigidity** literature in Europe.
7. Possibly **exchange-rate/pass-through style “timing of adjustment”** papers conceptually, though that is a more distant analogy.

### How should the paper position itself relative to those neighbors?

Mostly **build on** and **bridge**, not attack.

- Relative to **Vandekerckhove**, this paper should say: “That paper studies a wage freeze; I study a crisis-induced wage increase with cross-sector timing variation. Together they bracket labor demand responses to indexation.” That complementarity is potentially nice.
- Relative to the **minimum wage literature**, the paper should clearly distinguish the policy object. This is not a marginal wage floor for the lower tail. It is a broad-based cost shock affecting much of the wage distribution.
- Relative to the **macro wage-price spiral discussion**, the paper should offer micro evidence on one mechanism by which inflation-indexed wage setting can affect real activity.

### Is the paper positioned too narrowly or too broadly?

Right now, oddly, both.

- **Too narrowly** in the institutional exposition: lots of Belgium-specific detail very early.
- **Too broadly** in the contribution claims: it gestures at automatic stabilizers, macro inflation, labor demand, wage indexation, competitiveness, and EU policy reform, without fully owning one audience.

The paper needs one primary audience and one secondary audience. My advice:

- **Primary audience:** labor economists interested in labor demand under broad-based wage shocks.
- **Secondary audience:** macroeconomists interested in inflation transmission and wage rigidity.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?

It likely under-engages with:

- the broader empirical literature on **labor cost shocks outside minimum wages**;
- **European collective bargaining / wage-setting institutions**;
- work on **nominal rigidity and indexation in open economies**;
- potentially the literature on **hours versus heads** if such outcomes are relevant.

The paper also overstates the absence of evidence on indexation. Even if direct employment evidence is sparse, there is a larger ecosystem of papers on indexed wage setting, bargaining, and competitiveness that should be acknowledged.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?

Not quite yet. The most promising conversation is not “Belgium has a quirky institution and I estimated its effect.” It is:

> What happens when inflation shocks are mechanically translated into wage costs, and does the schedule of adjustment shape employment losses?

That is the conversation with real reach.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Inflation surged in Europe in 2022. In most economies, wages adjusted only gradually, cushioning firms in the short run but eroding workers’ real incomes. Belgium is unusual because automatic indexation rapidly turns inflation into nominal wage growth.

### Tension

Economists and policymakers worried that this could trigger a wage-price spiral or damage employment, but there is little credible evidence on the employment consequences of large, automatic, economy-wide wage increases—especially when timing differs across sectors for institutional reasons unrelated to the shock itself.

### Resolution

The paper finds that sectors required to implement wage increases earlier experienced lower short-run employment, with stronger effects in the private sector.

### Implications

The design of wage indexation rules matters, not just whether indexation exists. Timing can materially affect short-run employment adjustment during inflationary episodes.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

Serviceable, but not fully coherent. The paper does have the ingredients of a story, but it drifts between several possible stories:

1. **Is automatic indexation bad for employment?**
2. **What is the labor demand elasticity for mandatory wage increases?**
3. **Does timing of wage adjustment matter?**
4. **What can Belgium teach us about post-COVID inflation?**

Those are related but not identical. Right now the paper is a bit of a collection of results and motivations in search of a single dominant story.

### What story should it be telling?

The strongest story is:

> Belgium’s indexation system turns inflation into a broad-based wage shock. Because sectors faced the same total increase on different schedules, the country offers a rare test of whether the timing of mandatory wage pass-through matters for employment. It does.

That story is cleaner and more original than “estimate the elasticity of labor demand in Belgium.”

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?

“During the energy crisis, Belgium mechanically converted inflation into about 10 percent wage growth, but some sectors had to pay it immediately while others only later. The sectors hit earlier lost employment in the short run.”

That is the hook.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

Some would lean in. The episode is unusual and policy-relevant. But the second sentence needs to come quickly, and it needs to be bigger than Belgium per se. If presented as “a clever Belgian timing DiD,” phones come out. If presented as “a rare test of what happens when inflation is automatically passed into wages,” people listen.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Almost certainly: **“Is this telling me something about the level of wage indexation, or only about the timing?”**

That is the central question, and the paper should confront it much more directly and earlier. At present, that distinction appears, but too late and too softly. It is absolutely the first thing an engaged economist will want to know.

A second follow-up question would be: **“Why should I believe this generalizes beyond Belgium’s peculiar institutions?”** The paper has an answer—this is a broad-based mandatory wage shock during inflation—but it should make that answer more forcefully.

### If the findings are modest or null, is the null interesting?

Not relevant here because the main result is not null. The issue is different: the finding is potentially interesting, but the paper does not yet fully persuade the reader that the estimated effect is the economically central object rather than a temporary timing artifact. That limits excitement.

Yes. The paper is competent but safe. It reads like a solid applied paper with a clear estimate and conventional structure. AER papers usually either:

- use a remarkable setting to deliver broader insight.

This paper is not there yet, but it could move closer if it fully embraces the broader insight: **indexation systems are not just about inflation protection; their adjustment schedule shapes real employment losses.**

That one change would improve the introduction, literature positioning, narrative arc, and policy relevance all at once.
