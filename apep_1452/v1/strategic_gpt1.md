## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple and policy-relevant question: when inflation mechanically forces wages up, how many jobs are lost? It studies Belgium’s automatic wage indexation during the 2022–23 energy crisis and uses pre-existing sectoral differences in the timing of mandated wage adjustments to estimate the employment effects of large, economy-wide wage increases.

Why should a busy economist care? Because this is a rare setting where wage increases are broad-based, legally mandated, and driven by a macro shock rather than firm choice or minimum-wage policy. If credible, it speaks to labor demand, inflation pass-through, wage rigidity, and the design of social insurance in high-inflation episodes.

### Does the paper articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Mostly yes, but not as sharply as it should. The introduction is competent and informative, but it leads with Belgium-specific institutional detail before fully crystallizing the broader economic question. The paper currently sounds like “a careful Belgium study of indexation timing,” when it should sound like “a rare test of what happens when inflation mandates economy-wide wage hikes.”

The first two paragraphs should do three things more forcefully:

1. Start with the big economic question, not Belgium.
2. Make clear why this setting is rare and informative relative to existing wage/employment evidence.
3. State upfront that the paper identifies the employment effects of **mandatory wage growth induced by inflation**, not just the quirks of one country’s bargaining system.

### The pitch the paper should have

When inflation rises, wages usually adjust slowly. But in Belgium, inflation mechanically triggered large, mandatory wage increases across the economy during the 2022–23 energy crisis. This paper asks a first-order question for macro and labor economics: when wage growth is forced by indexation rather than negotiated gradually, how much employment is lost?

I answer this using pre-determined differences in the timing of sectoral indexation embedded in collective agreements signed long before the crisis. Sectors that had to absorb wage increases earlier saw lower employment relative to otherwise similar sectors that received the same cumulative wage increase later, implying sizable short-run labor demand responses to mandatory wage growth. The broader lesson is that the design of wage indexation systems affects not just inflation dynamics, but real employment adjustment.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

In most countries, inflation erodes real wages because nominal wages adjust slowly. Belgium is unusual because automatic indexation forces wages up mechanically when prices rise.

### Tension

Economists and policymakers worry that inflation-indexed wage increases may trigger job losses or wage-price spirals, but there is little direct evidence on the employment effects of such large, broad-based mandatory wage increases. Belgium’s 2022–23 energy crisis created exactly such a test.

### Resolution

Because sectors were bound by pre-existing collective agreements that adjusted wages at different times, the paper compares sectors hit earlier versus later by the same cumulative mandated wage increase and finds lower employment where wage costs rose sooner.

### Implications

The design of wage indexation systems matters for real economic adjustment. Protecting workers from inflation via immediate wage pass-through may carry short-run employment costs, especially in the private sector; timing rules may therefore be a meaningful policy lever.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

Serviceable, but not yet fully compelling. The ingredients are all there. The problem is that the narrative gets diluted by:
- too much institutional detail too early,
- a fairly mechanical “this contributes to three literatures” section,
- and a result presentation that reads more like a seminar paper than a top-journal story.

At present, it is closer to “a collection of sensible results around a Belgian quasi-experiment” than “a paper with a clean, memorable narrative.”

### What story should it be telling?

The story should be:

1. Inflation created a rare economy-wide mandated wage shock.
2. Belgium’s institutions turned that shock into staggered sectoral exposure.
3. That staggered exposure reveals how firms adjust employment when wages are forced up quickly.
4. The key lesson is about the **timing of wage pass-through under inflation**, not just Belgium.

That is the story that belongs in AER territory.

Most likely:

- “Is the important finding that wage indexation reduces employment, or that front-loaded wage adjustment is more damaging than delayed adjustment?”
- “How generalizable is this beyond Belgium’s public/private and bargaining structure?”

Those questions are strategic, not technical. The paper should anticipate them in the framing.

### Is the finding interesting enough?

Yes, potentially. This is not a boring null and not a tiny policy tweak. The setting is intrinsically interesting. But the current version slightly undersells the result by presenting it as a straightforward elasticity estimate. The more interesting fact is the combination of:
- macro inflation shock,
- mandatory wage adjustment,
- economy-wide coverage,
- and staggered timing.

That package is what gets economists’ attention.
