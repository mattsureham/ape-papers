## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a clean and policy-relevant question: when governments create geographically targeted tax zones, do they induce genuinely new business formation or mostly reshuffle firms across space? Using the staggered rollout of eight English freeport tax sites and universe-level company registration data, the paper finds little evidence of increased firm births in treated areas and some suggestive evidence of displacement from neighboring jurisdictions.

A busy economist should care because this is the core margin on which place-based tax policy lives or dies. If freeports do not expand the entrepreneurial pie even on the extensive margin of firm entry, then one of the headline rationales for modern place-based policy weakens considerably.

### Does the paper itself articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Not quite. The current introduction is competent, but it gets pulled too quickly into a generic place-based-policy literature review and then into method. It does not sharpen the central economic question enough, and it does not state the bottom line early enough. The first two paragraphs should make the paper feel like a verdict on a widely used policy idea, not primarily “the first causal evaluation of UK freeports.”

### The pitch the paper should have

“Governments around the world are reviving place-based tax incentives to spark local economic renewal. But the key question is whether these zones create new firms or simply move legal registrations and activity across jurisdictional borders. This paper studies England’s freeports—one of the UK’s flagship post-Brexit industrial policies—and finds no detectable increase in firm births in treated local authorities, alongside suggestive evidence that nearby areas lose registrations. The central implication is that freeport-style tax zones may reallocate entrepreneurship geographically without increasing it in aggregate.”

That is the opening. The “first causal evaluation” point can come after, as supporting novelty, not as the main hook.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper provides evidence that England’s new freeport tax zones did not meaningfully increase firm formation and may have displaced firm registrations from nearby areas.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest papers?

Only partially. The paper names many literatures, but the differentiation is still a bit muddy. Right now, the reader gets: “another place-based policy paper, with staggered DiD, using administrative data, finding small or null effects.” That is not enough for AER positioning.

What should be clearer is the specific comparative advantage of this setting:

- freeports are unusually salient politically,
- the incentives are unusually generous and multi-dimensional,
- the timing is sharp,
- the outcome is a leading extensive-margin response,
- and the paper is testing **creation vs. displacement**, not just “did treated areas go up?”

That last point should be the organizing contribution.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a literature gap?

Too much as a literature gap. Phrases like “first rigorous causal evaluation” and “filling a gap explicitly noted by the IFS” are second-tier framing devices. Stronger framing is world-facing: **What do flagship modern tax zones actually do?**

### Could a smart economist explain what is new after reading the intro?

At present, they might say: “It’s a DiD paper on UK freeports using Companies House data, and the effect on incorporations is basically zero.” That is not a good sign. The introduction does not yet arm the reader with a sharper statement such as: “This is a rare direct test of whether place-based tax incentives create new firms rather than merely relocating them.”

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Several specific possibilities:

1. **Better outcome than incorporations alone.**  
   The biggest limitation strategically is that firm registrations are not the same as economic activity. For a top journal, the paper would be much stronger if it linked freeports to:
   - plant openings,
   - employment,
   - payroll,
   - business rates base,
   - VAT registrations,
   - commercial property values or occupancy,
   - investment/capital expenditure,
   - customs/logistics activity.

2. **Sharper spatial evidence on displacement.**  
   The adjacent-LA result is currently “suggestive.” If the paper could map effects by distance to the tax-site boundary, that would turn a vague displacement claim into the paper’s central contribution.

3. **A stronger mechanism.**  
   The sector angle is underdeveloped. If freeports are supposed to matter especially for trade-intensive, warehousing, or logistics firms, then a clear sectoral pattern—or the absence of one—would materially strengthen the story.

4. **A welfare-relevant framing.**  
   Not just “no effect on incorporations,” but “large fiscal subsidies produced little detectable real entrepreneurial response.” That becomes a statement about the efficiency of place-based tax expenditures.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

### Closest neighbors

The nearest literatures/papers are roughly:

- **Neumark and Kolko (2010)** and **Neumark and Simpson (2015)** on enterprise zones and place-based policy

- **Freedman, Khanna, and others on Opportunity Zones** / the Opportunity Zone literature more broadly  
- **Giroud and Rauh (2019)** or related state tax competition/firm location papers  
- Potentially **Wang (2013)** and the SEZ literature, though that is a somewhat different institutional object

### How should the paper position itself relative to those neighbors?

It should mostly **build on and discipline** them, not attack them.

The right positioning is:

- Relative to enterprise zones and Opportunity Zones: this paper isolates the **firm-birth margin** in a modern, high-profile tax-zone design.
- Relative to broader place-based policy work: it tests whether a heavily subsidized zone can generate new entrepreneurial activity rather than reallocation.
- Relative to tax competition work: it shows that the likely first-order response may be geographic reshuffling, not net creation.

The paper should not oversell freeports as uniquely different from all prior zones. They look like a modern variant of a well-known policy class. The value is that they provide a timely and politically salient test case.

### Is the paper currently positioned too narrowly or too broadly?

Paradoxically, both.

- **Too narrowly** because it often reads as “the first evaluation of UK freeports,” which is a niche national-policy hook.
- **Too broadly** because it lists many place-based literatures without choosing a central conversation.

The paper should choose one main conversation: **Do place-based tax incentives create activity or displace it?** Everything else should support that.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of or under-engaged with?

Two areas feel underexploited:

1. **Spatial equilibrium and tax competition**  
   The paper cites Kline and some relocation work, but the conceptual bridge to tax competition and mobile tax base reallocation could be much stronger.

2. **Business registration vs. real activity**  
   There is relevant work in entrepreneurship, tax, and urban economics on what legal registrations measure and when they diverge from real operating location. Since the entire paper rests on Companies House registrations, it needs to show awareness of that interpretive issue at the framing level, not just as a caveat.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?

Mostly, but not optimally. The strongest conversation is not “UK industrial policy post-Brexit,” even though that is a useful application. The strongest conversation is the broader one in public finance and urban/spatial economics: **when governments subsidize place, do they create new economic activity or just move it?**

That is the conversation AER readers care about.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Governments increasingly use place-based tax incentives to revitalize lagging regions. Freeports are a prominent contemporary example, sold as engines of local renewal.

### Tension

The central uncertainty is whether such policies lower barriers enough to generate genuinely new firms or simply attract mobile firms and registrations from neighboring places. Existing evidence is mixed, and modern freeports are politically important but empirically under-evaluated.

### Resolution

In England, freeport tax zones do not produce a detectable increase in firm births at the local-authority level, and nearby areas may lose activity, consistent with displacement.

### Implications

The paper implies that freeport-style tax subsidies may be a costly way to reshuffle entrepreneurship geographically rather than expand it. That matters for both policy design and the broader case for place-based tax policy.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the ingredients, but not the discipline. At moments it feels like a collection of results appended to a standard reduced-form template rather than a paper driving one core point. The current structure foregrounds estimator choice and institutional detail before fully selling the question and the takeaway.

### What story should it be telling?

The story should be:

1. Freeports are a live global policy instrument.
2. Their key promise is new local business creation.
3. This setting offers a good test of that promise on the extensive margin.
4. The answer is basically no, and maybe worse: neighboring places lose.
5. Therefore, the burden of proof on place-based tax zones should shift from attraction to net creation.

That is a real story. The paper is close to it, but it needs to strip away generic “this paper contributes in three ways” scaffolding.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at dinner?

“I looked at England’s flagship freeport tax zones, and despite generous tax incentives, there’s no detectable increase in firm births—and some evidence nearby places lose firms.”

That is the only lead fact worth using.

### Would people lean in?

Moderately. Economists interested in public finance, urban, and political economy would lean in, especially because freeports are high-profile and the result cuts against policy rhetoric. But the excitement would fade quickly once they hear that the outcome is Companies House incorporations at the local-authority level, because that immediately raises the question: “Are we learning about real economic activity or just legal address changes?”

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Almost certainly:  
**“Is this about real activity, or just registered addresses?”**

And second:  
**“What happens to employment, investment, or establishment openings?”**

That tells you exactly where the paper’s strategic vulnerability lies.

### If findings are null/modest, is the null itself interesting?

Yes, but only if framed correctly. A null on a weak intervention is uninteresting. A null on a generous, flagship, heavily marketed policy is interesting. The paper has the latter, but it does not yet fully exploit it.

To make the null interesting, the paper needs to emphasize:

- these were substantial incentives,
- policymakers expected meaningful local renewal,
- firm entry is an early and plausible response margin,
- and yet there is little detectable response.

Then the null becomes evidence against a widely held policy intuition, not a failed experiment.
