## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple, policy-relevant question: when governments create tax-favored freeport zones, do they generate genuinely new businesses or merely move registrations across nearby boundaries? Using the rollout of eight English freeports and universe-level company registration data, the paper finds little evidence that freeports increased overall firm birth, and some suggestive evidence that they displaced activity from neighboring areas.

A busy economist should care because this is the central question in place-based policy: are spatial tax subsidies creating new economic activity or just reshuffling it? The UK freeport program is politically salient, internationally legible, and sits squarely in the broader debate over enterprise zones, Opportunity Zones, and regional industrial policy.

### Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Reasonably well, but not sharply enough. The opening is competent and policy-aware, but the introduction still reads too much like an empirical paper announcing a design rather than a paper making a punchy claim about the world. The best version of this paper is not “first causal evaluation of UK freeports using staggered DiD”; it is “a high-profile modern place-based tax policy appears not to expand entrepreneurship and may mainly reallocate it.”

The first two paragraphs should do less literature listing and more claim-setting. Right now paragraph 2 broadens too fast into a generic place-based policy survey. It should instead heighten the core tension: freeports are sold as engines of local renewal, but the economically meaningful question is whether they increase aggregate business creation rather than shift activity across space.

### The pitch the paper should have

“Freeports are back as a flagship tool of place-based industrial policy, justified by the promise that tax breaks in lagging regions will attract investment and spark new business creation. But the key economic question is whether these zones create firms that would not otherwise exist, or simply pull activity across nearby borders. Using the staggered activation of eight English freeports and the universe of company registrations, this paper finds little evidence that freeports increased firm births overall, and suggestive evidence that nearby areas lost activity instead. The result casts doubt on the idea that place-based tax incentives alone can expand local entrepreneurship.”

That is the story. Lead with that.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper provides early evidence that England’s freeport tax zones did not meaningfully increase firm creation and may have primarily displaced business registrations from nearby areas.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?

Only partially. The paper names broad neighbors, but the differentiation is still muddy. It says “first causal evaluation of UK freeports,” which is true and useful, but “first evaluation of this policy” is not by itself an AER-level contribution. The paper needs a sharper comparative statement against adjacent literatures:

- versus enterprise zone papers: this is about a newer, more generous, post-Brexit place-based tax package;
- versus Opportunity Zone papers: this studies firm births directly, not mainly investment or property markets;
- versus regional subsidy papers: this directly interrogates creation vs displacement in a modern UK setting.

Right now the contribution is still too easy to summarize as “another staggered-DiD evaluation of a place-based tax program.”

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a gap in a literature?

It does both, but it leans too much on “filling a gap” and “first causal evaluation.” Stronger framing would be about the world:

- Do generous, modern place-based tax zones create entrepreneurship?
- Or do they mostly relabel location choice?

That world question is much stronger than “the UK freeports have not yet been rigorously evaluated.”

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?

Not cleanly enough. They could probably say: “It’s a DiD paper on UK freeports and firm registration.” That is not enough. What they should be able to say is: “It shows that one of the most prominent recent place-based tax policies seems to have generated little new firm creation, suggesting relocation rather than real expansion.”

The author needs to write toward that takeaway.

### What would make the contribution bigger?

Several possibilities:

1. **Make displacement the centerpiece, not a side remark.**  
   The most interesting result in the current draft is the adjacent-LA negative effect. Right now it appears in the discussion almost apologetically. If the paper can convincingly show that treated gains are offset by nearby losses, the contribution becomes much larger: not “null effect on treated units,” but “the policy reallocated entrepreneurship geographically.”

2. **Move from registrations to economically more meaningful entry if possible.**  
   AER readers will instinctively worry that Companies House incorporations capture paperwork more than real activity. If the author can connect firm births to VAT registrations, payroll startups, employer firms, or survival/employment trajectories, the paper gets much bigger. The title promises “firm birth”; the data currently capture “incorporation.” That gap matters strategically.

3. **Say more clearly what type of entrepreneurship freeports should affect.**  
   The current sectoral pitch focuses on logistics and warehousing, which is sensible but somewhat narrow. A stronger angle would distinguish mobile, tax-sensitive, asset-heavy, or property-linked entry from other firms. That would sharpen the economics.

4. **Frame this as a test of a broader policy doctrine.**  
   Don’t sell it as a UK case study. Sell it as a clean test of whether place-based tax incentives alone can overcome local disadvantage absent complementary investments.

If the paper could only get bigger on one margin, it should elevate the creation-vs-displacement margin and make that the central result.

### How should the paper position itself?

Mostly **build on and discipline** this literature, not attack it.

The strongest positioning is:

- Busso/Criscuolo show place-based policies can matter under some conditions.
- Kline/Moretti and enterprise-zone surveys stress the core issue of displacement versus net creation.
- This paper brings a fresh, politically salient modern setting where the policy was unusually visible and generous, and asks whether the entrepreneurship margin responds.

So the paper should not overclaim “the literature is inconclusive and I resolve it.” It should instead say: “This is a useful test of a prominent modern version of place-based tax incentives, and the answer is sobering.”

### Is it currently positioned too narrowly or too broadly?

It is positioned a bit **too narrowly in method** and a bit **too broadly in policy survey mode**.

Too narrow because it sometimes reads like a design paper about staggered treatment timing and administrative data.

Too broad because it invokes everything from EU Cohesion Policy to Chinese SEZs, which are not really close substitutes for English freeports. Those are different objects: infrastructure-heavy development zones, trade zones, broad industrial policies, and regional transfers. Invoking too many cousins weakens the paper’s identity.

The right audience is the place-based policy / public finance / urban-regional audience, with implications for industrial policy. Keep it there.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?

A few areas could strengthen it:

1. **Business dynamism and entrepreneurship measurement.**  
   There is a literature distinguishing legal incorporation from economically meaningful entry. Even if not central empirically, the paper should acknowledge that debate and explain why incorporation is still informative here.

2. **Border and spatial spillover designs in local public finance/urban economics.**  
   Since the displacement question is the juicy part, the paper should sound more connected to work on spatial reallocation, border discontinuities, and local tax competition.

3. **Political economy of place-based policy.**  
   Because freeports were symbolic post-Brexit policy, there is a broader conversation about why governments favor visible spatial policies even when net effects are limited. That could enrich the framing, though probably not dominate it.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?

Almost, but not quite. It is currently having the conversation “Do freeports increase firm registrations?” The higher-impact conversation is “What do modern place-based tax zones actually do: create entrepreneurship or reshuffle it?” That shift matters a lot.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Governments increasingly use geographically targeted tax incentives to revive lagging places. English freeports were introduced with strong claims that they would attract investment and regenerate left-behind areas.

### Tension

Economics gives no free pass here: tax-favored zones may create new firms, but they may also simply pull firms across borders. The policy rhetoric is about local revitalization; the real economic question is whether the entrepreneurial pie expands.

### Resolution

Using the freeport rollout and nationwide registration data, the paper finds little detectable increase in firm births in treated areas and suggestive losses in adjacent areas, consistent with displacement rather than creation.

### Implications

If freeports mainly reallocate activity, then one of the UK’s flagship place-based policies may be an expensive reshuffling device rather than a growth strategy. More broadly, tax incentives alone may be too weak to generate entrepreneurship in structurally disadvantaged places.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the bones of one, but not yet the discipline. The ingredients are there. The problem is that the paper still feels somewhat like a collection of standard empirical sections attached to a more interesting story that is not fully embraced.

In particular:

- The title and abstract promise a bold policy story.
- The body spends a lot of space on standard empirical setup.
- The displacement angle, which should be the narrative climax, is treated as suggestive and late.
- The sector analysis is announced as part of the contribution but then barely appears in the presented results.

That mismatch weakens the arc.

### What story should it be telling?

The story should be:

1. Freeports were sold as engines of new business formation in left-behind areas.
2. The right test is not whether treated places saw more business registrations in isolation, but whether the policy increased net entrepreneurship rather than shifting it around.
3. The evidence suggests little new creation and possible displacement.
4. Therefore, the policy’s economic logic is much weaker than its political rhetoric.

That is a coherent AER-style narrative. Right now the paper gestures at it without fully committing.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?

“I’ve got a paper showing that England’s new freeport tax zones didn’t measurably increase firm births, and the nearby areas may actually have lost them.”

That is the lead.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

They would lean in—briefly. The topic is salient and the creation-versus-displacement question is real. But the second sentence matters enormously. If the follow-up is just “using company registration data and staggered DiD,” interest will fall. If the follow-up is “so this flagship post-Brexit industrial policy looks more like geographic reshuffling than real business creation,” interest stays.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Almost certainly:  
“Are these real operating firms, or just registered addresses?”  

And then:  
“Is the activity simply moving across local boundaries?”  

The draft is somewhat prepared for the first question but not strategically enough. It should anticipate that this is the core external-validity concern and address it directly in the framing, not bury it as a standard data caveat.

### If the findings are null or modest: is the null itself interesting?

Potentially yes. This is not a random failed intervention; it is a prominent, expensive, politically meaningful policy in a mature economy. Learning that a generous and visible place-based tax program failed to boost entrepreneurship is useful.

But the paper needs to do more work to make the null feel informative rather than underpowered. Right now the discussion of detectable effect size helps, but the stronger move is to say: even the point estimates are small, there is no dynamic build-up, and neighboring areas move in the opposite direction. That combination is substantively revealing.

The null becomes interesting when paired with the displacement story. On its own, it risks feeling like “we didn’t find much.”
