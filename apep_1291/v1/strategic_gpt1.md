## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple and policy-relevant question: when a major state-level barrier to corporate ownership of farmland was unexpectedly removed in Nebraska, did farm consolidation actually accelerate? Using the 2006 court invalidation of Nebraska’s anti-corporate farming law and neighboring restriction states as comparisons, the paper’s core claim is that the answer is no: agricultural consolidation appears to be driven by forces other than formal limits on corporate ownership.

A busy economist should care because this is, in principle, a clean test of whether a widely discussed and politically salient legal constraint actually binds. If the result is right, it says something broader than “Nebraska is special”: ownership-form regulation may matter much less than technology, contracting, and capital-market forces in shaping industrial structure.

**Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Not quite. The introduction starts with a generic statement that consolidation is important, then moves to the existence of anti-corporate farming laws. That is competent, but not sharp. The real hook is not “consolidation is important”; it is “states have spent decades trying to stop corporate farming, and we do not know whether these laws do anything.” That should be the opening.

**What the first two paragraphs should say instead:**

> For decades, several U.S. states have tried to protect “family farming” by banning or restricting corporate ownership of agricultural land. These laws play an outsized role in political and legal debates over rural economic change, yet we still do not know a basic fact: do they actually slow agricultural consolidation, or does consolidation proceed through organizational forms and market channels the laws do not reach?
>
> This paper studies the sharpest quasi-experimental test available. When the Eighth Circuit invalidated Nebraska’s Initiative 300 in 2006, a state that had long prohibited non-family corporate farming suddenly deregulated while neighboring states kept their restrictions. Comparing counties along the Nebraska border before and after the ruling, we find little evidence that removing the ban changed farm size, farm counts, or agricultural employment. The central implication is that anti-corporate farming laws may be more symbolic than economically operative.

That is the pitch the paper should own.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution in one sentence:**  
The paper provides the first quasi-experimental evidence that removing a prominent anti-corporate farming law did not measurably change farm consolidation or agricultural employment, suggesting that ownership-form restrictions are not a binding constraint on agricultural structure.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?
Only partially. The paper says it is “the first causal evidence on anti-corporate farming laws,” which may well be true, but that is not enough by itself. “First causal evidence” is a weak organizing principle unless the reader immediately sees why existing adjacent work could not already answer the question. The introduction needs to distinguish more clearly between:

1. descriptive or legal scholarship on anti-corporate farming statutes,
2. broader work on agricultural consolidation and farm size,
3. work on organizational form and ownership restrictions in other sectors.

Right now the differentiation is asserted, not demonstrated.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a gap in a literature?
It oscillates, but too often slips into “gap in the literature.” The stronger framing is about the world:

- Do legal restrictions on who may own productive assets actually shape industry structure?
- Or do firms and households reorganize around them so that the laws become mostly symbolic?

That is much stronger than “no one has causally estimated this policy before.”

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the intro?
They could probably say: “It’s a border DiD on Nebraska’s corporate farming law, and they find no effect.” That is understandable, but it is still perilously close to “another DiD paper about a state policy.” The paper does not yet make the newness feel conceptually large.

### What would make this contribution bigger?
Several possibilities, in descending order of importance:

1. **Make the object of interest more general:** not “anti-corporate farming laws” per se, but **whether ownership-form regulation changes real economic organization**. That opens a much broader audience.
2. **Show adjustment margins closer to the policy mechanism:** if the law targeted ownership, outcomes on land ownership, tenancy, leasing, operator legal form, or entry of out-of-state entities would be more powerful than aggregate farm counts and average acreage alone. Right now the paper risks testing a distal outcome.
3. **Connect to substitution across organizational forms:** if consolidation can occur through leasing, contract farming, LLCs, or trusts, then the paper should frame its contribution as evidence on regulatory circumvention/substitution, not merely null effects on farm size.
4. **Compare symbolic versus binding regulation:** the bigger claim is not only “this law did little,” but “form-based restrictions are poorly aligned with the economic margins along which industries consolidate.”

In short: the paper’s current contribution is real but narrow. To become AER-relevant, it needs to become a paper about **when legal form matters for industrial organization**, with agriculture as the test case.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
States adopted anti-corporate farming laws to protect family farms from corporate takeover. These laws remain politically resonant and are often treated as meaningful brakes on consolidation.

### Tension
But we do not know whether they ever bound in practice. Consolidation may have proceeded through leasing, trusts, LLCs, family control structures, and scale economies regardless of formal restrictions. Nebraska’s judicial deregulation creates a chance to test whether removing the law changed anything real.

### Resolution
The paper finds little evidence that it did: farm size, farm counts, and agricultural employment do not move much.

### Implications
The implication is potentially important: ownership-form restrictions may be largely symbolic if economic actors can reorganize around them and if the underlying drivers of concentration lie elsewhere.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
**Serviceable, but not fully coherent.** It has the ingredients, but the current draft often reads like a competent policy evaluation paper with a pile of nulls, rather than a paper organized around a substantive puzzle.

The main narrative problem is that the paper wants the null result to do too much without enough conceptual scaffolding. A null can be powerful, but only if readers understand what margin should have moved, why many informed observers believed it would move, and what it means that it did not.

Right now the narrative is:

- here is a law,
- here is a court case,
- here is a DiD,
- null result,
- therefore the law was symbolic.

That last step needs a stronger story. The paper should explicitly organize around **three possible worlds**:

1. the law binds and repeal causes consolidation;
2. the law binds only on ownership margins, not operating margins;
3. the law does not bind because actors already operate through substitute forms.

Then the findings are informative because they push the reader away from world 1.

As written, it is somewhat a **collection of results looking for a larger story**. The story it should be telling is: **economically meaningful constraints and legally salient constraints are not always the same thing**.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?
“For decades, states tried to stop ‘corporate farming’ through ownership bans; when Nebraska’s ban was struck down, farm consolidation barely budged.”

That is the fact.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
Some would lean in, but many would not—unless it is framed correctly. “A null result on Nebraska farm policy” is phone-reaching material. “A sharp test of whether legal ownership restrictions actually shape industrial structure” is lean-in material.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Almost certainly: **“If not through ownership, then through what channel does consolidation happen?”**  
That is the crucial follow-up, and the paper is not yet equipped to answer it convincingly. It offers plausible mechanisms—leasing, LLCs, trusts, technology—but mostly as ex post interpretation rather than as an integrated part of the paper’s contribution.

### Are the null findings themselves interesting?
Yes, potentially. But null results have to clear a higher bar. This one can, because the policy is salient, long-standing, and often treated as important. The paper does make some effort to argue that “X doesn’t work” is informative, but it currently does so in a somewhat defensive way (“well-powered nulls,” “symbolic policy”). It would be more convincing if it emphasized the mismatch between the target of the law and the relevant margins of adjustment.

At present, the paper is **not a failed experiment**, but it is not yet a fully compelling null either. It needs a stronger affirmative claim: **the policy targeted the wrong margin.**

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

Be honest: in current form, this is **not yet an AER paper**. The gap is mostly not econometric; it is **framing, scope, and ambition**.

### What is the main gap?
Primarily an **ambition/framing problem**, secondarily a **scope problem**.

- **Framing problem:** The paper is written as a narrow policy evaluation in agricultural economics rather than as an economics paper about the bite of ownership-form regulation.
- **Scope problem:** The outcomes are broad and somewhat distant from the policy mechanism. To persuade a general-interest audience, the paper needs either more mechanism-relevant outcomes or a much stronger conceptual frame around why broad nulls are nonetheless decisive.
- **Novelty problem:** “First causal evidence” is not enough by itself. The question has not been answered in exactly this setting, but adjacent literatures already tell readers that technology and contracts matter a lot. The paper must show why this episode genuinely updates prior beliefs.
- **Ambition problem:** The paper is competent but safe. It does not yet seek a big enough claim.

### What would excite the top 10 people in this field?
A version of this paper that says:

- We have a clean setting to test whether legal restrictions on ownership form actually shape industrial organization.
- They do not, because adjustment occurs through substitute contractual and organizational margins.
- Therefore, policies aimed at legal form can be politically salient yet economically toothless.

That is interesting far beyond agriculture.

### Single most impactful advice
**Reframe the paper around the general question of whether ownership-form regulation changes real economic organization, and make agriculture the test case rather than the whole point.**

If the authors can only change one thing, that is it. Everything else follows from that choice.
