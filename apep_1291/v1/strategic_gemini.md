**RE:** Strategic Positioning of "The Corporate Farm Brake"

---

### 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether state-level bans on corporate farming actually protect the "family farm" from agricultural consolidation. By exploiting a sharp judicial shock—the 2006 invalidation of Nebraska’s strict anti-corporate farming law—the authors find that removing these bans has zero effect on farm size, farm counts, or employment. A busy economist should care because this suggests a major pillar of American agrarian policy is purely symbolic, and that the "death of the family farm" is driven by technology and market forces that law cannot reach.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this well, but it buries the "symbolic vs. binding" tension. The first two paragraphs are a bit too "policy-heavy."
**The pitch it should have:** 
"For nearly a century, American agrarian policy has relied on 'corporate farming brakes' to prevent industrial consolidation and preserve rural communities. We provide the first causal evidence on whether these laws actually work. By analyzing the sudden judicial repeal of Nebraska’s ban, we find that the 'family farm' was neither saved by the law nor destroyed by its absence—suggesting that the organizational form of a farm is irrelevant to the inexorable march of agricultural consolidation."

---

### 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution:** The paper provides the first quasi-experimental evidence that anti-corporate farming laws are non-binding constraints on agricultural consolidation.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It differentiates itself well from the descriptive literature (e.g., MacDonald et al., 2013) by moving from "consolidation is happening" to "policy X doesn't stop it."
*   **Framing:** It is currently framed as answering a question about a specific policy. It would be stronger if framed as a broader inquiry into the **limits of land-use regulation** in the face of technological change.
*   **Clarity:** A smart economist would say: "It’s a border-DiD showing that corporate ownership bans don't actually slow down the growth of large farms."
*   **Bigger Contribution:** To make this "AER big," the authors need to dig deeper into the *why*. Is it because of "shadow" corporate ownership (LLCs/Trusts)? Or is it because "family farms" are now just as large and industrial as "corporate" ones? The "mechanisms" section (2.3) needs to be a primary result, not a discussion point.

---

### 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

*   **Neighbors:** MacDonald et al. (2013) on consolidation; Abadie (2020) on null results; and the broader "Dormant Commerce Clause" legal-econ literature.
*   **Strategy:** The paper builds on the consolidation literature but should **attack** the political economy assumption that these laws are "bulwarks." 
*   **Missing Conversations:** The paper should speak to the **Industrial Organization (IO)** of agriculture. Does the lack of effect mean that "family" and "corporate" firms are technologically identical? It also misses a connection to the **Political Economy** of symbolic legislation—why do these laws persist if they do nothing?

---

### 4. NARRATIVE ARC

*   **Setup:** A world where 9 states have "brakes" on corporate farming to save the family farm.
*   **Tension:** These laws are fiercely debated, but we have no idea if they actually work because they were all passed decades ago. 
*   **Resolution:** Nebraska’s brake was "cut" by a court, and nothing changed. The car kept moving at the same speed.
*   **Implications:** The "family farm" vs. "corporate farm" distinction is a legal fiction that masks the true economic drivers of scale.

**Evaluation:** The arc is clean but lacks "teeth." The paper treats the null as a statistical curiosity. It needs to treat the null as a **profound failure of a century-old policy regime.**

---

### 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

**The Dinner Party Fact:** "Nebraska tried to ban corporate farming for 25 years to save small farms; a court struck it down in 2006, and literally nothing happened to the rate of consolidation."

**Reaction:** Economists will lean in because it confirms a suspicion that "technology is destiny," but they will immediately ask: **"Then how are the corporations getting around it?"** This is the "So What?" hurdle. If the authors can't show the *mode* of circumvention (LLCs, leasing, etc.), the dinner party ends early.

---

### 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

*   **Front-load the QWI data:** The annual frequency of the employment data is a much better "sanity check" for the parallel trends than the 5-year Census data.
*   **Move Section 2.3 to the Results:** Instead of "Why it might not matter," make a section called "Testing Mechanisms of Non-Effect." Use the "Land in Farms" (marginal land) result to tell a story about *extensive* vs. *intensive* margin consolidation.
*   **The Appendix is too thin:** I want to see a map of the border counties. I want to see a list of the "replacement" statutes.

---

### 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The gap is **Ambition**. Currently, this is a very high-quality *Applied Economics Letters* or *AJAE* paper. 

**The Single Biggest Improvement:** Move beyond the "null result for the sake of a null result" and prove the **circumvention/equivalence mechanism**. If the authors can show that "Family LLCs" increased exactly as "Corporations" decreased (or vice versa), they prove that the law was just a name-change. 

**Advice to Author:** "Stop selling the methodology and start selling the autopsy of a failed policy. Don't just tell me the law didn't work; show me the legal loopholes (the 'shadow' corporate structure) that ate the law from the inside out."
