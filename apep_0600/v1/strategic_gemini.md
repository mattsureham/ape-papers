## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
This paper evaluates the impact of the EU Mortgage Credit Directive (MCD) on mortgage lending rates using the staggered timing of its adoption across the Eurozone. It finds a precisely estimated zero effect, ruling out even modest changes in borrowing costs, and argues that this is because the "harmonization" merely codified regulatory standards that most member states had already adopted at the national level. A busy economist should care because it challenges the assumption that top-down legal integration necessarily changes market behavior, suggesting that de facto convergence often precedes de jure mandates.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this pitch very clearly. The first two paragraphs successfully frame the MCD as a "crowning achievement" that the European Commission expected to reshape markets, contrast it with the Commission's own later admission of uncertainty, and then offer a definitive answer ("the directive changed nothing"). No rewrite is needed; the hook is well-executed.

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**Contribution:** The paper provides the first quasi-experimental evidence that EU-wide conduct-of-business harmonization in mortgage markets failed to move interest rates because it ratified existing national practices.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It differentiates itself from macroprudential literature (e.g., Cerutti et al. 2017) by focusing on a *conduct-of-business* directive rather than structural capital requirements.
*   **World vs. Literature:** It is framed as a question about the WORLD—specifically, why a major EU policy failed to achieve its stated goals—which is a strong AER-style framing.
*   **Explanation to a Colleague:** A smart economist would say: "This paper shows that the big EU mortgage directive was a nothingburger for interest rates because the countries were already doing what it required."
*   **Making it Bigger:** To move the needle further, the paper needs to prove the *mechanism* of "codification" more robustly. Currently, it relies on a coarse 3-country "stringency" dummy. A more granular "Regulatory Gap Index" (as the author admits in Section 5.3) would turn this from a single-policy evaluation into a broader theory of "The Effective Margin of International Harmonization."

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
The paper sits at the intersection of Financial Regulation and European Integration.

*   **Neighbors:** Cerutti et al. (2017) on macroprudential effects; Jiménez et al. (2017) on credit supply; Abadie (2020) on the value of null results; and Enriques & Hertig (2015) on EU regulatory theory.
*   **Strategy:** It builds on Abadie (2020) to justify its existence as a "principled null" and uses the macroprudential literature as a benchmark for what a "binding" effect would look like.
*   **Niche vs. Broad:** Currently slightly too focused on the specific EU directive. It should speak more broadly to the **Political Economy of Harmonization**—why do politicians pass laws that change nothing? (i.e., the "Symbolic Politics" angle).
*   **Missing Conversation:** It could benefit from connecting to the literature on "Brussels Effect" (Bradford) or "Regulatory Capture" (Stigler). Is this "codification" a form of industry capture where the law is written to fit the leaders?

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC
*   **Setup:** Post-2008, the EU sought to prevent another crisis by harmonizing mortgage standards across the continent.
*   **Tension:** While the law was a massive undertaking, early indicators suggested its impact was "difficult to isolate."
*   **Resolution:** Rigorous DiD analysis shows a precise null effect on lending rates.
*   **Implications:** Harmonization only works when there is a "regulatory gap." If countries have already converged, the law is just expensive paperwork.

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong. It is not just a collection of results; it is a "whodunnit" where the culprit is pre-existing convergence.

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
*   **The Lead Fact:** "The EU spent a decade on the Mortgage Credit Directive, and it moved interest rates by exactly 1.1 basis points."
*   **Reaction:** People will lean in, but then quickly ask: "Well, did it change *who* got loans (rejection rates)?"
*   **Follow-up:** The "So What" is vulnerable to the "intensive vs. extensive margin" critique. If the paper can't rule out that the law kicked high-risk borrowers out of the market, the null on *rates* (for those who still got loans) is less surprising.

---

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
*   **Front-loading:** The paper is well front-loaded.
*   **Section 5.1 (Standard Errors):** The explanation of the 6-fold difference between TWFE and Sun-Abraham SEs is a bit "inside baseball" for the main text. Move the technical justification of the SE increase to a footnote or appendix and keep the focus on the economic bounds.
*   **Figure 3 (House Prices):** This section should be moved to the appendix or framed purely as a "selection check." Since it fails the parallel trends test so badly, it clutters the main narrative of the "precise null" on rates.

---

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The gap is **Scope and Ambition**. Right now, it’s a very competent evaluation of one (admittedly large) directive. To be an AER paper, it needs to be a paper about the *nature of international harmonization*.

**Single Biggest Improvement:**
The author must move beyond the binary "stringent vs. non-stringent" classification and create a continuous "Regulatory Distance" measure for all 18 countries (based on pre-MCD national laws) to show that the treatment effect scales with the degree of required change. If they can show that the effect is only non-zero in countries that actually had to change their laws, the paper becomes a generalizable study on regulatory efficacy.
