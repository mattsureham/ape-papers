## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether Portugal’s Golden Visa program changed not just overall housing prices, but the composition of housing price growth: did foreign investor demand disproportionately bid up **existing homes** relative to **new construction**? A busy economist should care because that distinction is exactly what determines whether investor migration is mostly a transfer into incumbent-owned urban real estate or a catalyst for new housing supply.

The paper mostly does articulate this pitch early, and the first two paragraphs are better than average. But they still read a bit like an institutional setup plus a “here is my design” paper, rather than a sharp statement of the broader economic question. The best version of the opening would make the world-level question more explicit: when governments sell residency to property investors, do they finance construction or merely inflate the price of scarce existing housing?

### The pitch the paper should have

“Investor visa programs are spreading across rich countries, but their incidence in housing markets is poorly understood. The key question is not simply whether they raise house prices, but **which part of the housing market they raise**: if they direct foreign capital into existing dwellings rather than new construction, they increase the cost of local housing without expanding supply. I study Portugal’s Golden Visa program, which generated a large, policy-induced inflow of foreign real-estate demand, and show that it disproportionately raised the price of existing homes relative to new dwellings.”

That is the AER version of the paper. It moves from Portugal-specific institutional detail to a general economic question about capital inflows, supply, and the internal structure of housing markets.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper’s contribution is to show that a major investor-residency program can reshape housing markets by increasing **the relative price of existing versus new homes**, implying a demand shock that falls on incumbents’ housing stock rather than inducing new supply.

### Evaluation

#### Is this clearly differentiated from the closest papers?
Only partly. The paper says prior work studies aggregate house prices and this paper studies within-market segmentation. That is a real distinction, but right now it is presented as a methodological niche rather than a conceptual advance. The author needs to say more clearly:

- Existing vs. new is not just “one more margin.”
- It is the margin that distinguishes **displacement of domestic buyers** from **supply expansion**.
- Therefore the paper is about the incidence of foreign capital inflows in housing markets, not just one country’s housing-price response.

Without that, the contribution sounds like: “another paper on foreign buyers and house prices, but with a different outcome variable.”

#### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world or filling a literature gap?
It is mixed, but still too literature-gap coded. The strongest parts are world-facing: how program design determines who bears housing costs. The weaker parts are the “no study has exploited within-market segmentation as identifying variation” language. AER intros should not lead with “no one has done this exact design on this exact margin.” They should lead with “this margin answers the economically important question.”

#### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?
A smart economist could probably say: “It’s a DiD on Portugal’s Golden Visa using existing-versus-new house prices.” That is not enough. They should instead be able to say: “It shows these programs may raise prices by reallocating demand into scarce incumbent housing rather than generating construction.”

Right now the introduction leaves too much risk of the former reaction.

#### What would make the contribution bigger?
Specific ways to enlarge it:

1. **Frame the outcome as incidence, not segmentation.**  
   The paper should say: existing-vs-new prices reveal whether capital inflows are absorbed by the existing stock or transmitted into supply. That is bigger than “price divergence.”

2. **Bring in quantities if possible.**  
   Even one serious supply-side quantity outcome—permits, completions, transaction volumes, renovation activity—would elevate the paper from “relative price movement” to “capital inflow without supply response.” That would be a larger and more policy-relevant contribution.

3. **Use the 2023 reform more strategically.**  
   Right now the post-2023 section feels bolted on and muddy. If the reversal does not happen, the paper should not oversell it as a mechanism test. Either sharpen it into a broader point about hysteresis/path dependence in housing demand, or cut it back.

4. **Connect to broader foreign-capital/housing incidence.**  
   The paper should not just be about golden visas. It should speak to what happens when mobile global wealth meets local housing with differentiated supply elasticities.

- Relative to golden-visa papers: “Those papers ask whether these programs affect housing at all; this paper asks whether they inflate scarce existing stock rather than induce new supply.”
- Relative to housing-supply papers: “The relative price of existing and new dwellings offers a useful diagnostic for whether a demand shock is supply-accommodated.”

The current draft is a bit too eager to claim novelty from the design itself. Better to present itself as a clarifying paper that sharpens the economic interpretation of foreign capital inflows into housing.

### Is it positioned too narrowly or too broadly?
Currently too narrowly in design and too broadly in policy rhetoric.

- Too narrowly because it leans heavily on one program, one country, one relative price margin.
- Too broadly because it ends with sweeping statements for “30-plus countries” that the paper has not really earned.

The right middle ground is: one unusually sharp case study that reveals a general mechanism.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?
At least three:

1. **Foreign buyers and global city housing** beyond visas specifically.  
   There is a broader urban/public finance conversation on foreign ownership restrictions, absentee ownership, and local housing affordability.

2. **Asset-market segmentation / composition effects.**  
   The paper is really about composition within an asset class. There may be relevant work in finance and urban economics on heterogeneous housing assets, amenity premia, and market segmentation.

3. **Political economy of housing policy and place-based capital inflows.**  
   The paper could speak to how governments design programs that monetize residency while externalizing costs onto local renters/buyers.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?
Not quite. It is having a respectable conversation with the golden-visa niche. The more powerful conversation is with **how global capital enters local housing markets and whether policy design can direct it toward supply rather than scarcity rents**. That is the conversation AER readers are more likely to care about.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Countries increasingly use investor migration programs to attract capital. In housing markets, the central question is whether that capital finances new supply or bids up existing scarce urban property.

### Tension
Most existing evidence looks at aggregate house prices, which cannot distinguish between those two possibilities. Portugal’s Golden Visa program offers a case where policy-induced investor demand was heavily concentrated in real estate, and plausibly in existing urban dwellings.

### Resolution
The paper finds that after the Golden Visa, prices of existing dwellings in Portugal rose relative to prices of new dwellings, more than in comparator countries, suggesting the inflow was absorbed primarily by the existing stock rather than new construction.

### Implications
This changes how we think about investor-residency programs: the key policy issue is not whether they attract money, but whether program design channels that money into **supply creation** or **scarcity capitalization**. More broadly, it suggests that foreign capital inflows into housing can distort the internal composition of housing prices in ways aggregate indexes miss.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
It has one, but it is not fully disciplined. The core story is there. The problem is that the paper wanders into a few secondary stories:

- randomization inference caveat
- 2023 suspension non-reversal
- “surprising” persistence explanations
- broad claims about investor lock-in, digital nomads, retirees, etc.

Those pieces make it feel like a collection of interesting empirical facts rather than one tightly argued paper.

### What story should it be telling?
The paper should tell one story:

**A policy-induced inflow of foreign housing demand matters because it is directed at existing stock, not new supply; the relative price of existing versus new dwellings reveals that incidence.**

Everything else should serve that story. The 2023 section should be secondary unless the author can make it a sharp statement about persistence/hysteresis. Right now it muddies the main narrative more than it strengthens it.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?
“Portugal’s Golden Visa didn’t just raise housing demand; it appears to have bid up **existing homes relative to new construction**, which is exactly what you’d expect if the policy capitalized scarcity rather than financing supply.”

That is a decent lead fact. Better than “the coefficient is 8.3 index points.”

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
Some would lean in—especially urban, public finance, and international economists—but only if the framing is sharpened. If presented as “a DiD on Portugal’s house prices,” many would tune out. If presented as “a way to distinguish whether global capital inflows expand supply or just inflate incumbent housing,” they lean in.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Almost certainly:  
**“Does this show no supply response, or just a stronger response in existing-home demand than in new construction?”**

That is the right question, and the current paper is not fully prepared for it narratively. It gestures toward this answer, but it needs to own the narrower claim carefully: the program disproportionately affected existing homes, consistent with weak supply accommodation. If the author can show quantity margins, that follow-up becomes much easier to answer.

### If findings are modest or null?
The findings are not null, but the RI result is a strategic issue. A dinner-party economist will ask why the treatment country ranks only sixth in the permutation distribution. That does not kill the paper, but it weakens the force of the “Portugal as singular policy shock” story. The paper needs to decide whether RI is a cautionary footnote or a central qualification. Right now it awkwardly tries to have it both ways.
