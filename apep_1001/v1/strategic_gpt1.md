## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether a labor-market regulation—Poland’s Sunday retail trading ban—had an unintended public-safety effect by changing where and how people spend Sundays. Its central claim is that when large retail destinations close, pedestrian accidents fall substantially, implying that retail-hours regulation can affect road safety through changes in pedestrian exposure around commercial areas.

A busy economist should care because the paper tries to reframe a familiar policy domain—blue laws / Sunday trading restrictions—not as only about labor supply, retail competition, or religiosity, but as a policy that changes urban exposure to risk. That is potentially interesting. The problem is that the paper’s current opening does not present the strongest version of this idea clearly enough.

### Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Not quite. The first paragraph is too scene-setting and slightly melodramatic (“45 million people face a choice”), and it muddies the mechanism: “diverted ... onto roads and into outdoor activities” is not the cleanest or most credible statement of the channel. The second paragraph leads with a sign reversal in the raw data. That is a useful empirical wrinkle, but it is not the main reason to care. Right now the introduction makes the paper sound like “a neat empirical result with a seasonal confound,” rather than “a new way to think about the safety consequences of temporal regulation.”

### What the first two paragraphs should say instead

Poland’s Sunday trading ban was enacted to protect retail workers, but it also changes where millions of people go and how they move through cities on Sundays. When shopping centers and commercial strips close, pedestrian traffic around retail roads and parking areas falls sharply; if those places are important sites of pedestrian-vehicle conflict, then a retail-hours law may also be a road-safety policy.

This paper studies that possibility using nationwide accident records from Poland. I show that non-trading Sundays have substantially fewer pedestrian accidents than legislatively exempt trading Sundays, with little corresponding change in vehicle-only collisions. The core contribution is not that Sunday closures change commerce—that is obvious—but that temporal retail regulation appears to reduce a specific form of urban risk: pedestrian exposure near commercial activity.

That is the pitch they should own.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### Contribution in one sentence

The paper’s contribution is to show that Sunday retail closures can generate a previously overlooked safety externality by reducing pedestrian accidents, suggesting that temporal regulation of commerce changes accident risk through pedestrian exposure rather than through overall traffic volume.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?

Only partially. The paper names several literatures, but the differentiation is still a bit mechanical: “no prior study has examined road safety consequences.” That is true but not enough. “No one has looked at outcome Y in setting X” is rarely an AER-level contribution by itself.

What needs to be differentiated more sharply is the conceptual contribution:
- relative to blue-law papers: this is about nonmarket externalities of shopping-hour regulation, not labor or religiosity;
- relative to road-safety papers: this is about destination closure as a safety lever, not street design, speed, or enforcement;
- relative to urban papers: this is about time-varying land-use activation, not static commercial geography.

That is the triangulation. Right now the paper gestures at all three, but does not fully stake out the intersection.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or as filling a gap in a LITERATURE?

It is mixed, and it should be more firmly about the world. The strongest world-question is:

**Do policies that shut down major commercial destinations reduce pedestrian risk by changing where pedestrian-vehicle interactions occur?**

That is much better than:

**There is no paper on Sunday trading bans and road safety.**

The paper is not hopelessly literature-gap driven, but it still leans too much on “thin but growing literature” language. AER papers typically start from a world fact or policy question, then use literature to clarify what is unknown.

### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what’s new?

At the moment, maybe, but not confidently. A good reader might say: “It’s a paper on Poland’s Sunday trading ban that finds fewer pedestrian accidents when shops close.” A less charitable reader might say: “It’s another reduced-form policy paper with heterogeneous effects.”

The latter risk is real because the paper currently spends too much introduction space on the treatment calendar and empirical specification and too little on the conceptual novelty.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Several possibilities:

1. **Lean fully into pedestrian accidents as the main result.**  
   The total-accident result is modest and narratively fragile. The pedestrian result is much larger and much more intuitively linked to the mechanism. The paper should stop pretending the total-accident effect is the headline.

2. **Tie accidents more tightly to commercial geography.**  
   The current “built-up area” split is too coarse. If the data permit, the big version of this paper would show larger effects near malls, retail strips, large supermarkets, or commercial corridors. That would elevate it from “interesting reduced form” to “evidence on where risk is produced.”

3. **Reframe from “Sunday trading ban” to “temporal activation of risky spaces.”**  
   This would make the paper speak to urban economics, transport, and place-based externalities. The regulation is then a quasi-experiment in turning commercial nodes on and off.

4. **Use more meaningful welfare-relevant outcomes if available.**  
   Fatalities/severe injuries would make the stakes larger. If those are too rare, severity composition could still matter.

If they could enlarge the contribution with one move, it would be: **show that the pedestrian decline is spatially concentrated around commercial destinations.** That would transform the paper.

It should **connect much more aggressively** to urban externalities and activity-density literatures. That is the missing bridge.

Oddly, both.

- **Too narrowly** in that it reads like a Poland-specific policy note about Sunday trading.
- **Too broadly** in that the introduction claims contributions to three literatures, including a vague methodological point about seasonal confounding, without really owning a central audience.

The right audience is not “everyone interested in temporal regulation.” It is economists interested in how policies change the spatial-temporal distribution of risk in cities. That is a sharper and potentially more important conversation.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?

It seems under-engaged with:

The more interesting conversation is: **economic policies that alter when commercial places are active can change the incidence of urban harms by changing exposure, even if they do not obviously change aggregate traffic.** That is a better conversation and a more AER-adjacent one.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Sunday trading restrictions are usually understood as labor-market or cultural regulations. Meanwhile, pedestrian accidents are often understood through street design, speed, and infrastructure. These two domains are typically treated separately.

### Tension

But commercial destinations create dense pedestrian-vehicle interactions. If a policy shuts those destinations on certain days, it may alter accident risk in ways standard policy debates ignore. The tension is that we do not know whether reducing shopping activity lowers accidents, shifts them in time, or simply displaces risk elsewhere.

### Resolution

The paper finds that non-trading Sundays have substantially fewer pedestrian accidents, with little effect on vehicle-only collisions. That pattern suggests the policy primarily affects pedestrian exposure around commercial activity.

### Implications

The implication is that retail-hours regulation has safety externalities that should enter policy evaluation. More broadly, the paper suggests that the timing of economic activity—not just its level—matters for urban risk.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the bones of one, but not the discipline. Right now it feels like a paper with one genuinely interesting result surrounded by several secondary stories:
- sign reversal in aggregate accidents,
- pedestrian mechanism,
- within-day displacement,
- a methodological lesson about seasonal confounding,
- welfare implications.

These do not fully cohere.

The paper should tell a simpler story:

1. Sunday trading laws change the activation of commercial space.
2. Commercial space is where pedestrians and cars mix.
3. When that space goes dark, pedestrian accidents fall.
4. Therefore temporal regulation has safety externalities.

Everything else is subordinate. The total-accident result should be demoted; the “seasonal illusion” point should be a support, not a theme; the within-day analysis seems especially weak and may not help the story.

So yes: at present it is somewhat a collection of results looking for a hierarchy. The story should be the pedestrian-exposure story, full stop.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?

“Poland’s Sunday shopping ban appears to reduce pedestrian accidents by about 20 percent on affected Sundays.”

That is the only number here that gets attention.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

They would lean in initially, because that is surprising and intuitively plausible. But the very next question would be:

**“Is that really about shopping closures, or just holiday timing and weird calendar composition?”**

And that is exactly why the paper cannot headline the aggregate accident result. The pedestrian fact is stronger, more distinctive, and more defensible as a story.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Likely one of these:
- “Is the effect concentrated near malls and commercial roads?”
- “Does foot traffic fall, or does shopping just shift to Saturday?”
- “Is this a pedestrian-exposure story or a holiday/travel-pattern story?”
- “How general is this outside Poland?”

The first of those is the most important. If the author cannot answer it, the paper remains suggestive rather than definitive in its strategic positioning.

### If findings are modest: is the modesty itself interesting?

The total-accident effect is modest and, in the paper’s own telling, not especially secure as the main object. That is fine; the paper should stop trying to sell it. The paper becomes much more convincing if it says:

- the overall accident effect is small and contaminated by calendar composition;
- the robust and policy-relevant result is a large decline in pedestrian accidents.

That is not a failed experiment. It is a narrower but cleaner contribution.

**Rebuild the paper around the claim that temporal closure of commercial destinations reduces pedestrian risk, and provide the strongest possible evidence that the effect is concentrated in commercial space rather than merely around special calendar dates.**
