## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple and important question: when local governments receive large, formula-driven fiscal windfalls, do their communities become safer? Using discontinuities in Brazil’s municipal transfer formula, the paper finds that sizeable increases in municipal resources do not measurably change homicide rates, suggesting that violence in Brazil is not easily reduced by general-purpose local revenue alone.

A busy economist should care because this is, at its core, a question about whether fiscal capacity translates into one of the most fundamental public goods: personal security. If the answer is no, that has implications for public finance, decentralization, and crime policy well beyond Brazil.

**Does the paper articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Not quite. The current opening is competent, but it starts with facts about homicide levels and then moves quickly into theory and design. It does not crystallize the broader stakes sharply enough. The paper’s best asset is not “here is another clean FPM RD,” but “here is evidence on whether money can buy local public safety.”

**What the first two paragraphs should say instead:**

> Governments spend enormous sums trying to make communities safer, yet we know surprisingly little about whether giving local governments more unrestricted money actually reduces violent crime. This is a first-order question for public finance and political economy: if weak local fiscal capacity is an important constraint on public safety, then exogenous revenue windfalls should lower homicide. If violence instead reflects policing institutions, organized crime, or political capture, then more money alone may do little.
>
> This paper studies that question in Brazil, a country with exceptionally high homicide rates and a formula-based system of federal transfers that creates sharp jumps in municipal revenue at population thresholds. I show that these windfalls—large enough to affect employment and corruption in prior work—have essentially no detectable effect on homicide. The result suggests a substantive conclusion about the world, not just this setting: unrestricted municipal fiscal capacity is not, by itself, a reliable lever for reducing lethal violence.

That is the pitch. It is clearer, bigger, and less design-first.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:**  
The paper shows that large exogenous increases in unrestricted municipal revenue in Brazil do not reduce homicide, implying that general local fiscal capacity is a weak instrument for improving public safety in a high-violence setting.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest papers?
Only partially. The paper distinguishes itself from the FPM literature by focusing on homicide rather than employment, corruption, or education. But that differentiation currently reads as “same design, new outcome,” which is a modest contribution unless the outcome is made to seem truly central.

The paper needs to separate itself from two kinds of neighboring work:

1. **FPM-threshold papers** showing effects on spending, employment, corruption, education.
2. **Crime-transfer papers** showing that cash to individuals affects crime.

Right now the paper says, in effect, “I apply the well-known FPM design to homicide.” That is accurate, but not exciting enough for AER.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a gap in a literature?
It oscillates between the two. The stronger framing is the world-question: **Does local fiscal capacity reduce lethal violence?** The weaker framing is: **There is a missing outcome in the FPM literature.** The introduction currently leans too much on the latter by saying it “completes a missing piece of this fiscal design.” That is not an AER sentence.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?
They could probably say: “It’s an RD using Brazil’s FPM thresholds to test whether municipal transfer windfalls affect homicide, and it finds a precise null.” That is clear enough. The problem is that they might also say: “So it’s another Brazil FPM paper, just with crime as the outcome.” That is the strategic risk.

### What would make the contribution bigger?
Be specific:

- **Shift the framing from homicide to public safety capacity.** If homicide is the flagship outcome, fine—but then show whether windfalls affected the proximate margins municipalities actually control: guards, urban lighting, social assistance, prevention programs, local security spending, or coordination with state police. Without that, the paper risks answering a narrow question with an unsurprising null.
- **Lean into heterogeneity where the question is substantively alive.** For example: places with weak state police presence, high baseline violence, strong drug-route exposure, higher municipal administrative capacity, or larger dependence on FPM. If unrestricted money matters anywhere, where?
- **Compare violence to other outcomes in the same paper.** A bigger contribution would be: windfalls affect municipal payrolls / corruption / education / some local services, but not homicide. That contrast would convert the paper from “null on one outcome” into “why some state functions respond to money and others do not.”
- **Mechanism via scope of municipal authority.** The most promising larger point is that municipal governments do not control the core coercive margin in Brazil. If the paper can frame itself as evidence about the limits of fiscal decentralization when the relevant policy instrument is held at another level of government, that is much more interesting.

In short: the current contribution is coherent but modest. To be bigger, it needs to become a paper about the **limits of decentralization and fiscal capacity for violence reduction**, not just homicide near thresholds.

That is stronger than saying “the literature hasn’t looked at homicide yet.”

It is currently **too narrow in identification lineage and too broad in implication**.

Too narrow because it reads like a paper for people who already know the FPM design.  
Too broad because the conclusion reaches for “money doesn’t buy safety” without sufficiently developing the conceptual boundary conditions under which that statement is true.

The right audience is not just applied micro people who like Brazilian RDs. It should be people interested in **public economics, political economy of decentralization, and economics of crime/state capacity**.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?
It could speak more directly to:

### Is the paper having the right conversation?
Not yet. The current conversation is “FPM effects on another municipal outcome.” The more impactful conversation is:

> What kinds of public goods can be bought through unconditional transfers to local governments, and why is lethal violence apparently not one of them?

That is the right conversation.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Brazil has very high homicide rates, and local governments receive large formula-driven transfers. More money could plausibly improve safety through employment, prevention, infrastructure, or public services.

### Tension
But violent crime may not be constrained primarily by municipal resources. It may instead depend on state-level policing, organized crime, institutional quality, or political capture. So does local fiscal capacity matter for homicide at all?

### Resolution
Using discontinuities in federal transfers, the paper finds that large municipal revenue windfalls do not measurably affect homicide rates, including youth homicide.

### Implications
Unrestricted fiscal transfers to municipalities are not enough to reduce lethal violence. If governments want safer communities, they may need targeted safety interventions, stronger policing institutions, or changes in the governance environment rather than simply larger municipal budgets.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
**Serviceable, but not yet strong.** The ingredients are there, but the story is still a bit “design + results + robustness.” The paper has findings looking for a bigger story.

### What story should it be telling?
The paper should tell this story:

1. Economists often infer that greater local fiscal capacity improves local public goods.
2. But not all public goods are equally purchasable, especially when production of the good is institutionally fragmented.
3. Lethal violence is a particularly revealing test because it is a first-order welfare outcome and one on which municipal authority is limited.
4. In Brazil, giving municipalities more unrestricted money changes some things, but not homicide.
5. Therefore, the constraints on safety are not primarily local fiscal ones.

That is a genuine narrative. Right now the paper only half-commits to it.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?
“I’ve got a setting where municipalities get about 20% revenue jumps from a transfer formula, and those windfalls don’t reduce homicide at all—even in a high-homicide country like Brazil.”

That is a decent opening fact.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
Some would lean in initially because homicide is important and the setting is clean. But after the first minute, many would ask themselves: “Isn’t policing mostly state-level in Brazil?” If that intuition dominates, the result becomes less surprising, and the paper risks feeling like a well-executed confirmation of limited municipal leverage.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Almost certainly:

- “If the money doesn’t reduce homicide, where does it go?”
- “Is the null stronger in places where municipalities actually have some prevention capacity?”
- “Does it affect non-lethal crime, police-related measures, or service delivery?”
- “Is this telling us about violence, or just about the limits of municipal control over violence?”

Those are exactly the questions the paper should anticipate and use to strengthen its framing.

### Is the null itself interesting?
Yes, but only conditionally. A null can absolutely be interesting here because the treatment is large, the outcome is important, and the design is familiar enough to be credible. But the paper has to make the null do conceptual work.

At present, the paper says: the null is precise, therefore informative. That is necessary, but not sufficient. For AER, the null must also imply a broader lesson: **general local fiscal resources do not automatically translate into the provision of all local public goods, especially public safety when coercive authority lies elsewhere or violence is driven by organized crime.**

Without that broader lesson, it risks reading like a failed attempt to find an effect.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The gap is mostly a combination of **framing problem, scope problem, and ambition problem**.

- **Framing problem:** The paper is better than its current framing. It has a real question—whether fiscal capacity can buy public safety—but it keeps retreating into “another FPM paper.”
- **Scope problem:** One outcome, one familiar design, one null. That is usually not enough for AER unless the outcome is monumental and the interpretation is deep. Homicide is monumental, but the interpretation is not yet developed enough.
- **Ambition problem:** The paper is competent but safe. It does not yet push toward a larger claim about decentralization, state capacity, or the production of public goods.

I do **not** think the main obstacle is pure novelty in the sense that no one has done this exact outcome before. The problem is that “new outcome on old design” is not by itself top-journal territory.

### Single most impactful advice
**Reframe the paper as evidence on the limits of fiscal decentralization for producing public safety, and build the paper around why homicide is a revealing test of what money can and cannot buy at the municipal level.**

If the author can only change one thing, that is the change. Everything else follows from it: literature choice, intro rewrite, discussion, and what additional evidence would be most valuable.
