---

### 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper tests a foundational but overlooked prediction of the Becker (1957) discrimination model: if employers use wage discounts to offset their "taste" for discrimination, then a binding minimum wage—which removes that discount—should force a change in the racial composition of hiring. Using two decades of administrative worker-flow data, the author finds that while minimum wages successfully compress the racial wage gap, they do not change the racial mix of new hires; instead, they significantly increase the rate at which Black workers exit jobs. This suggests that wage floors don't eliminate discrimination—they just push it from the hiring margin to the retention margin.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this pitch quite well in the first two paragraphs. It immediately grounds the inquiry in a classic theory (Becker) and identifies a clear empirical gap (hiring flow data vs. stock employment).

**The Pitch the Paper Should Have:** (The current pitch is strong, but could be sharpened to emphasize the "Entry vs. Exit" tension).
> "Does the minimum wage equalize the playing field for minority workers or simply change how they are excluded? While theory suggests wage floors should reduce hiring discrimination by eliminating 'discriminatory discounts,' I show that the reality is more lopsided. Using administrative flow data, I find that raising the floor has zero impact on who gets in the door, but it significantly accelerates how fast Black workers are pushed out."

---

### 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:** The paper provides the first direct test of the Becker discrimination channel using administrative worker flows, finding that minimum wages fail to equalize hiring but do increase Black separation rates.

- **Differentiation:** It differentiates itself well from the "employment levels" literature (Cengiz et al. 2019) and "audit studies" (Bertrand & Mullainathan 2004) by looking at the *flows* that create those levels. It is closest to Wursten and Reich (2023) but offers a more granular mechanism (hiring vs. separations).
- **Framing:** It is framed as a question about the **WORLD** (does the floor change who gets hired?) rather than just a gap-filler. 
- **The "Smart Economist" Test:** A colleague would say: "It’s the paper that shows the minimum wage doesn't help Black workers get jobs; it just makes them lose them faster."
- **Making it bigger:** To truly elevate this, the author needs to dig deeper into the *why* of the separation effect. Is it "Last-In, First-Out" (seniority), or is it that the higher wage makes firms "pickier" about performance in a way that correlates with race (statistical discrimination)?

---

### 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

- **Closest Neighbors:** Becker (1957) on theory; Cengiz et al. (2019) on MW employment effects; Dustmann et al. (2022) on reallocation; Lang and Lehmann (2020) on modern discrimination theory.
- **Positioning:** The paper should **synthesize** the MW literature with the discrimination literature. Currently, it sits more in the MW camp. It should "attack" the simplicity of the Becker model—not to dismiss it, but to show that in a world with multi-dimensional job attributes (retention, effort, screening), the 1D Becker prediction fails.
- **Missing Conversations:** The paper needs to speak to the **Search and Matching** literature. Separations are often a function of match quality. Does the MW lead to "bad matches" for minority workers?

---

### 4. NARRATIVE ARC

- **Setup:** Becker says if you can't pay them less, you'll hire them more (or at least the same).
- **Tension:** We've raised the MW many times, but racial hiring gaps persist. Why?
- **Resolution:** The hiring margin is "sticky" or unresponsive, but the separation margin is highly sensitive. The policy compresses wages but destabilizes employment for the very group it intends to help.
- **Implications:** Policy focus should shift from the "point of hire" to "on-the-job" disparities and retention.

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong. It moves from a theoretical puzzle to a surprising empirical bifurcation (Null on Entry / Positive on Exit).

---

### 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

- **Lead Fact:** "Raising the minimum wage doesn't change who gets hired, but it makes Black workers 5% more likely to lose their jobs."
- **Reaction:** People will lean in. It's a "counter-intuitive but plausible" result that complicates the progressive narrative for the MW without resorting to the standard "it kills jobs" argument.
- **Follow-up:** "Is it because they are being fired, or because the higher wage makes them look for even better jobs (voluntary quit)?" This is the "AER-level" question the author needs to answer.

---

### 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

- **Front-loading:** The separation result is actually more interesting than the null hiring result. The paper should lead with the "Entry vs. Exit" dichotomy earlier in the Intro.
- **Appendix:** Move the TWFE vs. CS-DiD debate (Section 5.1) largely to an appendix. By now, AER readers accept CS-DiD; we don't need a tutorial on why TWFE is biased unless it reveals a specific economic insight.
- **Mechanism Section:** Section 5.2 needs more data. Can the author look at "tenure at separation"? If the MW kills "new" Black jobs specifically, that's a different story than if it kills "long-term" Black jobs.

---

### 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The gap is **Ambition**. Currently, it feels like a very high-quality "Technical Note" or a "Short Paper." To be a lead AER article, it needs to move from *documenting* the separation effect to *explaining* it.

**The Single Most Impactful Advice:** 
Distinguish between **voluntary** and **involuntary** separations. If you can't do it with QWI, use a secondary dataset (like CPS or NLSY) to show that the increased separation for Black workers is driven by "discharges" (firms being pickier) rather than "quits" (workers moving to better jobs). Without this, the welfare implication—the "So What?"—remains frustratingly ambiguous.

### Strategic Assessment

- **Current framing quality:** Compelling
- **Contribution clarity:** Crystal clear
- **Literature positioning:** Well-positioned
- **Narrative arc:** Strong
- **AER distance:** Medium (Needs the "Why" of the separation effect)
- **Single biggest improvement:** Use a secondary dataset to determine if the increased Black separations are voluntary (reallocation to better jobs) or involuntary (displacement/discrimination).
