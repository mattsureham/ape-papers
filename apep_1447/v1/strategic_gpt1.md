## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper studies Indonesia’s 2015 shift from politically negotiated local minimum wages to a nationally mandated formula and asks a simple question: when a large developing country replaces discretion with a mechanical wage rule, what happens to labor markets? Busy economists should care because the reform speaks to a broad issue beyond Indonesia—whether rule-based labor regulation in high-informality settings actually changes real employment outcomes, or mostly changes the paperwork.

The paper does **not** articulate this pitch as clearly as it should in the first two paragraphs. The current opening is heavy on institutional detail and quickly slips into design. The first two paragraphs should lead with the bigger question—what happens when a state replaces bargaining with formula—and only then explain why Indonesia provides a useful test case.

### The pitch the paper should have

“Across many developing countries, minimum wages are not just wage floors; they are political institutions. Governments increasingly try to depoliticize wage setting by replacing bargaining and discretion with formula-based indexation, on the theory that predictable rules reduce conflict and improve labor-market performance. But we know surprisingly little about whether such centralization and mechanization actually matter for workers and firms, especially in economies with large informal sectors and uneven enforcement.

Indonesia’s 2015 reform provides a rare opportunity to study this question. The government replaced decentralized, negotiated minimum wages with a national formula tied to inflation and GDP growth, generating differential increases across provinces depending on how far pre-reform wages sat below the formula path. I show that provinces more exposed to the reform did not experience detectable changes in aggregate unemployment, labor-force participation, or employment, suggesting that formula-based centralization may have much smaller aggregate labor-market consequences than either critics or proponents claim.”

That is the AER-facing version. It is about **state capacity, rule-based policy, and labor-market adjustment in developing economies**, not just “I run DiD on Indonesian provinces.”

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:**  
The paper’s contribution is to provide evidence that Indonesia’s shift from discretionary local minimum-wage setting to a national mechanical formula had little detectable effect on aggregate provincial labor-market outcomes.

### Is this clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?
Only partially. The paper distinguishes itself from prior Indonesia minimum-wage papers by focusing on **the institutional reform itself**, rather than ordinary minimum-wage variation. That is the right instinct. But the differentiation is still too thin because the introduction makes it sound like another reduced-form minimum-wage paper in a developing country, except with a different policy episode.

What is potentially new here is **not** “minimum wages in Indonesia again.” It is:
1. a reform that changed the **governance technology** of wage setting;
2. a move from local bargaining to national formula;
3. evidence on whether institutional centralization changes aggregate labor outcomes.

That distinction should be much sharper.

### Is the contribution framed as a question about the world, or as filling a literature gap?
Right now it is framed too much as a literature gap plus policy episode. The stronger framing is a question about the world:

- When governments replace discretionary labor-market institutions with predictable formulas, do real labor markets move?
- In high-informality settings, does centralization matter less than the legal reform suggests?
- Are formula-based minimum wages mostly about political conflict reduction and predictability rather than employment effects?

That is a stronger, more durable contribution.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the intro?
At present, not confidently. A smart economist might say: “It’s another DiD paper on minimum wages, this time in Indonesia, and they find a null.” That is not enough.

The paper needs to make the novelty legible in one line:
> “This is not a paper about one more minimum-wage increase; it is a paper about replacing a decentralized bargaining regime with a centralized indexation rule.”

### What would make this contribution bigger?
Several possibilities:

1. **Different outcome variable:**  
   The biggest limitation to strategic importance is the reliance on aggregate provincial unemployment/LFP/employment. Those are coarse outcomes for a context where the salient margin is likely formal/informal reallocation. A bigger paper would show whether the reform changed:
   - formal vs informal employment,
   - sectoral employment composition,
   - wage compression,
   - labor conflict/strikes,
   - compliance,
   - firm entry/exit,
   - manufacturing employment or payrolls.

2. **Different mechanism:**  
   The paper currently speculates about informality, weak enforcement, and small real bite. A stronger paper would directly show which mechanism dominates. Even one credible mechanism result would help enormously.

3. **Different comparison:**  
   The paper should compare **institutional regimes**, not just treatment intensity. The central contrast is negotiated/discretionary wage-setting versus formula-based wage-setting. That is more interesting than “higher Kaitz vs lower Kaitz provinces.”

4. **Different framing:**  
   Reframe from “effect of PP78 on labor outcomes” to “what formula-based centralization does—and does not do—in weakly enforced labor markets.”

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Before this paper, we know that minimum wages in developing countries can affect formal employment, informality, and wages, but evidence is mixed. We also know much less about whether changing the **institutional process** of wage setting—from bargaining to formula—matters in its own right.

### Tension
The reform looks consequential on paper. Indonesia removed local discretion, imposed a national formula, and created predictable but unevenly binding wage increases. In a country with large informality and variable enforcement, this should either:
- meaningfully change labor-market outcomes, or
- reveal that legal/institutional reform is weaker than it appears.

That is the tension.

### Resolution
The paper finds little detectable effect on aggregate provincial unemployment, labor-force participation, or employment.

### Implications
The implication should be: formula-based centralization may have limited aggregate labor-market effects in settings where adjustment occurs through informality, compliance is incomplete, or the reform mainly changes political process rather than binding economic constraints.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
Only partially. Right now it feels like a competent empirical note with a null finding, not a fully developed narrative. The pieces are there, but the story is underpowered.

The current structure is:
- interesting reform,
- standard DiD setup,
- null results,
- speculative mechanisms.

What it should be telling instead is:
1. **A government tries to depoliticize wage setting through a national formula.**
2. **This is a major institutional change that should matter if legal wage-setting institutions are truly binding.**
3. **Yet aggregate labor markets barely move.**
4. **Therefore, in this setting, the institutional form of wage regulation may be less economically potent than its statutory architecture suggests.**

That is a real story. At present, the paper does not tell it forcefully enough.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Almost certainly:
- “Okay, but did it affect formal versus informal employment?”
- “Did firms comply?”
- “Did wages actually rise where the formula bit more?”
- “If nothing happened, what exactly changed—politics, enforcement, wage setting, or just the law on paper?”

Those are revealing questions. They point to the paper’s current weakness: the most natural follow-up questions are better than the outcomes currently analyzed.

### Is the null itself interesting?
Potentially yes, but only if the paper sells the null correctly. A null is interesting here if it is framed as:
- evidence that formula-based labor-market centralization does not mechanically translate into aggregate employment effects;
- evidence that statutory wage-setting institutions may be weak in high-informality settings;
- evidence against a strong view that depoliticized wage formulas substantially reshape labor markets.

If instead the paper presents the null as “we looked and didn’t find significance,” it reads like a failed experiment. At moments, the paper drifts toward that weaker interpretation, especially when it emphasizes power limitations and pre-trend concerns without compensating with a stronger conceptual payoff.
