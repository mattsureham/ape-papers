## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper studies a major institutional change in Indonesia’s minimum wage system: in 2015 the country replaced decentralized, politically negotiated local minimum wages with a national formula tied to inflation and GDP growth. The paper asks whether this centralization and mechanization of wage setting changed aggregate labor market outcomes across provinces, and finds little detectable effect on unemployment, labor force participation, or employment.

Why should a busy economist care? Because this is not just “another minimum wage paper.” In principle, it speaks to a bigger question: when developing countries replace discretionary, conflict-ridden labor regulation with rule-based national formulas, does that actually change labor markets, or does formality, compliance, and local heterogeneity mute the policy?

### Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Not quite. The current introduction is vivid on institutional detail, but the paper undersells the broader question. It opens like a policy note on Indonesian wage setting rather than a paper about what rule-based labor market governance does in developing economies. The reader learns the institutional background before being told why the reform matters beyond Indonesia.

### What the first two paragraphs should say instead

The introduction should lead with the big world question, then use Indonesia as the clean setting:

> Across developing countries, governments increasingly replace discretionary labor market regulation with formula-based rules intended to reduce political conflict, increase predictability, and reassure firms. But it is unclear whether such institutional centralization actually changes labor market outcomes, especially in economies with large informal sectors and uneven enforcement.  
>  
> This paper studies one of the clearest such reforms: Indonesia’s 2015 shift from decentralized, negotiated minimum wages to a binding national formula indexed to inflation and GDP growth. The reform sharply reduced local discretion and generated differential increases in statutory wage floors across provinces depending on how far pre-reform wages were below the new formula path. I show that provinces more exposed to the reform saw little detectable change in aggregate unemployment, labor force participation, or employment, suggesting that rule-based wage setting may matter less for aggregate labor markets than its political salience would imply.

That is the pitch the paper should have. Right now the paper starts with descriptive local color before landing the conceptual question.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper’s contribution is to estimate the aggregate labor market effects of Indonesia’s 2015 shift from discretionary local minimum wage setting to a centralized national formula, showing limited detectable effects across provinces.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?

Only partially. The paper says it is “most closely related” to prior Indonesia minimum wage work and to developing-country minimum wage papers, but the differentiation is still too procedural: “they study earlier years; I study this reform.” That is not enough for AER-level positioning.

The paper needs to make much clearer that it is about a different object:

- not just a minimum wage increase,
- but a **change in the governance technology of wage setting**,
- not just the level of the wage floor,
- but the shift from local bargaining to national formula.

That distinction is there, but it is not made sharply enough. As written, a smart reader could reasonably summarize the paper as “another reduced-form minimum wage study in a developing country, with null effects.”

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or filling a gap in a LITERATURE?

Mostly as a literature gap. It says, in effect: prior papers study developing-country minimum wages and pre-PP78 Indonesia; I study PP78. That is weaker than a world-question framing.

The stronger framing is: **What happens when a state tries to depoliticize labor regulation by replacing bargaining with rules?** That is a world question. It has implications beyond Indonesia and beyond minimum wages.

### Could a smart economist explain to a colleague what’s new?

At present, not cleanly. They would probably say: “It’s a DiD on Indonesia’s minimum wage formula reform, and it finds no aggregate employment effects.” That is competent but undistinctive.

You want them to say: “It shows that centralizing minimum wage setting into a mechanical national rule—despite large statutory shocks and a major political overhaul—did not visibly change aggregate provincial labor markets. So the binding margin may not be statutory wage setting per se, but enforcement, informality, or within-province adjustment.”

That version sounds like a contribution.

### What would make the contribution bigger?

Most importantly: outcomes and mechanisms.

Specific ways to make it bigger:
1. **Move from aggregate labor market outcomes to margins that matter in developing countries**: formal vs informal employment, wage distributions, compliance, firm entry/exit, sectoral reallocation.
2. **Lean into the institutional change**: measure whether the reform reduced wage dispersion, labor conflict, strikes, union activity, or political volatility in wage setting. If the reform’s real purpose was predictability and depoliticization, the paper should evaluate those margins.
3. **Compare governance regimes, not just wage shocks**: show whether formula-based setting changes the relationship between local conditions and minimum wage updates relative to the discretionary era.
4. **Use heterogeneity strategically**: provinces with stronger manufacturing bases, union presence, enforcement capacity, or formal sector exposure are the obvious places where the institutional reform should matter most.

Right now the paper studies the broadest and bluntest outcomes, which makes the contribution look smaller than it might be.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Before the paper, Indonesia’s minimum wage setting was decentralized, politicized, and locally negotiated. There is a broad concern that minimum wages in developing countries distort labor markets, especially by shifting workers into informality, and a separate policy push toward formula-based, predictable regulation.

### Tension

The tension should be: a reform that looked institutionally major and politically consequential may or may not have been economically consequential. If wage setting becomes more centralized and mechanical, does that materially alter labor markets—or are statutory rules too weak a lever when informality and noncompliance are pervasive?

### Resolution

The paper finds little detectable change in aggregate provincial labor market outcomes in more exposed provinces after the reform.

### Implications

The implications should be: depoliticizing wage setting may change the politics of regulation more than the economics of employment; in developing-country labor markets, the key margins may be compliance, informality, and within-labor-market reallocation rather than aggregate job loss.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

Only partially. The ingredients are there, but the paper currently reads more like:
- institutional background,
- design,
- null estimates,
- possible explanations.

That is serviceable, but not memorable. It feels somewhat like a collection of results around a policy episode rather than a paper driving toward a central conceptual takeaway.

### What story should it be telling?

The story should be:

1. Governments often believe that replacing discretion with formula can tame labor market politics and create predictability.
2. Indonesia provides a rare clean test of that idea.
3. Despite a politically salient institutional overhaul and measurable statutory wage shocks, aggregate labor markets barely moved.
4. Therefore, in settings with informality and weak enforcement, the economics of labor regulation may be much less responsive to changes in statutory rule design than policymakers assume.

That is a coherent narrative. It is stronger than “we estimated the effect and got nulls.”

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Almost certainly:
- “Did it affect formal versus informal employment?”
- “Did wages actually rise where the statutory shock was larger?”
- “Did compliance improve or not?”
- “Did it reduce dispersion or local political conflict?”

Those are the natural questions. The fact that the reader immediately wants outcomes the paper does not have is a sign the current version is strategically incomplete.

### If the findings are null or modest: is the null itself interesting?

Potentially yes, but the paper does not make the strongest case.

The null is interesting **if framed as**:
- a null on a politically major institutional reform,
- in a setting where many expect strong labor market effects,
- implying that statutory redesign alone may not matter absent enforcement or without affecting the margins that actually adjust.

The null is less interesting if framed as:
- another minimum wage paper with imprecise province-level employment estimates.

Right now it drifts toward the second framing too often. The author needs to argue harder that learning “this sweeping institutional centralization did not move aggregate labor market aggregates” is itself an economically meaningful result.
