### 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper exploits a unique policy shift in Indonesia—moving from discretionary, highly political minimum wage negotiations to a mechanical national formula—to estimate the employment effects of minimum wage shocks. Using the "bite" of this formula as a natural experiment, the author finds a precisely estimated null effect on aggregate provincial labor market outcomes. This suggests that even large, formula-driven wage increases may be absorbed by developing economies without significant employment losses, potentially due to high informality or weak enforcement.

**Evaluation:** The paper does a decent job in the first two paragraphs, but it is currently framed as a policy evaluation of Indonesia's PP78 rather than a contribution to a global economic debate. 
**The pitch it should have:** 
"Does the institutional mechanism of wage-setting—discretionary vs. formulaic—influence the employment elasticity of the minimum wage? We exploit a 2015 reform in Indonesia that replaced political bargaining with a mechanical rule, creating exogenous variation in wage growth across provinces. Despite substantial formula-induced shocks, we find a precise null effect on aggregate employment, providing new evidence on the 'range of inaction' for minimum wage policy in high-informality settings."

---

### 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Statement:** The paper provides a precisely estimated null effect of a transition from discretionary to formula-based minimum wage setting on aggregate provincial employment in a large developing economy.

- **Differentiation:** It differentiates itself from Merdikawati & Al Izzati (2025) by looking at the *reform* itself rather than just the levels. It contrasts with Bird & Manning (2008) by using more recent data and a cleaner identification of the "bite."
- **Question vs. Literature:** Currently, it feels like it is filling a gap in the "Indonesian minimum wage literature." This is too narrow for the AER. It needs to be framed as a question about **Rule-based vs. Discretionary Policy** or **Minimum Wage Elasticities in the Presence of Massive Informality.**
- **Clarity:** A smart economist would say: "It’s a DiD on an Indonesian reform that shows the formula didn't kill jobs."
- **Making it bigger:** To make this an AER paper, the author needs to dig into the **informality mechanism**. A null result on *aggregate* employment is only interesting if we know where the bodies are buried. Is there a shift from formal to informal? Is there a change in the *intensive* margin (hours)? Without micro-data, the contribution remains "safe" but small.

---

### 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

- **Neighbors:** Alatas & Cameron (2004), Dube et al. (2019), Harrison & Scorse (2004), and the burgeoning literature on "Rules vs. Discretion" in macro/political economy.
- **Positioning:** The paper should build on the "New Minimum Wage Research" (Dube, Cengiz) but explicitly bridge it to the Development literature on **Informality** (Maloney).
- **Narrow/Broad:** Currently too narrow (field-specific). It speaks to "Indonesianists." It should be speaking to **Labor and Development** economists globally.
- **Unexpected connection:** It should connect to the literature on **Administrative Capacity and Enforcement**. The "formula" only matters if the state can enforce it. A null result might actually be a paper about the *failure of state capacity* to project national formulas into local markets.

---

### 4. NARRATIVE ARC

- **Setup:** Developing countries struggle with political, volatile wage-setting that creates business uncertainty.
- **Tension:** Indonesia tries to solve this with a "mechanical" formula. Theory suggests this could lead to massive disemployment if the formula is too aggressive for local productivity.
- **Resolution:** It doesn't. Employment remains flat.
- **Implications:** Formulas are "safe" for politicians to use, but they might be "paper tigers" if they don't account for the informal sector's ability to absorb shocks.

**Evaluation:** The arc is "serviceable" but lacks "tension." The "So What?" needs to be higher-stakes. Why was the formula controversial? Unions hated it. Why? If the author can show that unions were right (or wrong) about the *welfare* effects, the story gets better.

---

### 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

- **Lead Fact:** "Indonesia automated its minimum wage setting, raising wages by up to 27% in some areas, and absolutely nothing happened to the unemployment rate."
- **Reaction:** Initial interest, followed by: "Wait, did anyone actually get paid that wage?" (The enforcement question).
- **Follow-up:** "Is this a win for workers, or just a sign that the law is a fiction?"

### 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The gap is **Scope and Mechanism.** A provincial-level analysis with $N=34$ is very thin for the AER in 2024. 

**The single most impactful piece of advice:** Use the SAKERNAS micro-data (individual level) to track the **formality transition**. If the author can show that the "formula" pushed workers from formal factories into informal street vending, the "null" on aggregate employment becomes a "significant" finding about the structural transformation of labor.
