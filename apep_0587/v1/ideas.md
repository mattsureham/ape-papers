# Research Ideas

## Idea 1: Avoidance vs. Adjustment at the Child Benefit Notch: Behavioral Responses to the High Income Child Benefit Charge
**Policy:** The High Income Child Benefit Charge (HICBC) was introduced on 7 January 2013 under Finance Act 2012, Schedule 1. It imposes a tax charge equal to 1% of total Child Benefit received per £100 of adjusted net income (ANI) above £50,000, creating a 100% marginal effective tax rate on Child Benefit at £50,000. The threshold was frozen at £50,000 from 2013 to April 2024, when Chancellor Hunt raised it to £60,000 and halved the taper. This reform abolished the original notch at £50,000 and created a new softer notch structure at £60,000-£80,000.
**Outcome:** Primary data: ONS Annual Survey of Hours and Earnings (ASHE) via NOMIS API — annual earnings distribution at fine percentiles for full-time workers (~25M PAYE workers). Secondary: HMRC HICBC administrative statistics (GOV.UK) — counts of taxpayers liable by income band annually (440,000 liable, £525M total in 2022/23).
**Identification:** Bunching estimation at the notch (Kleven & Waseem 2013; Kleven 2016). Counterfactual income distribution estimated by polynomial fit excluding ±£5,000 window. Channel test: self-employed (low frictions) vs. PAYE (high frictions) decomposition separates avoidance from real labor supply. Reform falsification: April 2024 shift from £50k to £60k — bunching at £50k should dissolve post-reform.
**Why it's novel:** No academic paper has applied bunching estimation to the HICBC. Adam, Kelsey, Waters (2020 IFS) analyze UK income tax kinks but explicitly exclude child benefit interactions. The pension-contribution avoidance vs. real labor supply decomposition is novel. The 2024 reform provides unusually clean pre/post falsification.
**Feasibility check:** Variation: confirmed (£50,000 notch legally defined, 11-year pre-reform window). Data access: confirmed (NOMIS/ASHE API returns earnings distributions; HMRC statistics page live). Novelty: confirmed (no academic paper on HICBC bunching). Sample size: ~440,000 HICBC-liable taxpayers; 300,000-500,000 workers near £50k per survey year.
