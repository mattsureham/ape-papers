## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a policy-relevant question: when a government gives legal status and work authorization to a very large population of already-present unauthorized migrants, does that disrupt local labor markets? Using Colombia’s 2021 regularization of Venezuelan migrants, the paper argues that legalization at massive scale had little detectable effect on aggregate employment, unemployment, or labor force participation across departments.

A busy economist should care because the policy margin here is genuinely important and understudied: most immigration papers study inflows, not legalization of workers who are already in the labor market. If the paper is right, it speaks directly to current debates in the U.S., Europe, and middle-income host countries about whether regularization is labor-marketly dangerous.

Does the paper articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs? Only partly. The introduction is competent, but it is too self-conscious about methodology and too quick to define itself against the “arrival” literature in abstract terms. The first two paragraphs should lead with the core world question and the surprising fact: a legalization program covering millions appears not to move aggregate labor market outcomes.

### The pitch the paper should have

“Countries around the world are deciding whether to legalize large populations of unauthorized migrants already living and working within their borders. The central economic question is not whether new arrivals affect labor markets, but whether converting unauthorized workers into legal workers changes competition, formalization, and native labor-market outcomes. This paper studies Colombia’s 2021 regularization of roughly 1.8 million Venezuelans—the largest such program in Latin America—and finds that even at this scale, legalization did not measurably change aggregate employment, unemployment, or labor-force participation across regions.

This result matters because legalization changes legal status, not headcount. In economies with large informal sectors, much of the labor-market adjustment may already have occurred before regularization, implying that fears of large aggregate disruption from legalization are overstated. The paper’s contribution is to isolate this policy margin and show that the effects of ‘letting migrants in’ and ‘legalizing migrants already here’ are not the same question.”

That is the story. Right now the paper gets there, but too slowly and with too much emphasis on design vocabulary.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper’s contribution is to show that Colombia’s mass regularization of Venezuelan migrants appears to have had little effect on aggregate regional labor-market outcomes, suggesting that legalization of already-present migrants can be absorbed without measurable aggregate disruption, especially in a high-informality economy.

### Is it clearly differentiated from the closest papers?

Only somewhat. The paper does make the “arrival versus regularization” distinction, and that is the right axis. But the differentiation is still thinner than it needs to be.

The closest literatures are:
1. Venezuelan inflow papers in Colombia and neighboring countries.
2. IRCA/amnesty papers on legalized workers’ outcomes.
3. Broader immigration-incidence papers on local labor markets.

The paper says, in effect, “those papers study arrivals; I study regularization.” That is true, but not yet enough. A smart reader may still come away with: “Okay, another regional-treatment paper showing a null on aggregate outcomes.” The introduction needs to sharpen the conceptual distinction: arrival changes labor supply quantity and composition; regularization changes legal access, bargaining position, and potentially formal-sector sorting. That is the contribution. The paper should make the reader feel that this is a different policy object, not just a different event.

### Is the contribution framed as a question about the world or a gap in the literature?

Mostly as a question about the world, which is good. The paper is strongest when it says: opponents of regularization fear labor-market disruption; does that actually happen? It is weaker when it leans into “there is little literature on regularization.” AER wants the world question first, literature gap second.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the intro?

They could, but not crisply enough. Right now they might say: “It’s a DiD on Colombian migrant regularization and finds no aggregate labor-market effect.” That is not yet memorable. The introduction should leave them saying: “This paper shows that legalizing unauthorized migrants already in the country is a different margin from immigrant inflows, and in a high-informality setting it may have near-zero aggregate labor-market effects.”

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Most importantly: move beyond aggregate employment/unemployment/participation. The paper itself all but admits the real action could be in composition rather than levels. If legalization matters through formalization, the paper needs outcomes that sit closer to that mechanism:
- formal employment,
- social security enrollment,
- pension/health contributions,
- contract status,
- occupation upgrading,
- sectoral reallocation,
- native formal-to-informal switching,
- migrant wages or job quality.

As currently framed, the paper is trying to infer a mechanism (“informal absorption”) using outcomes that cannot really see it. That makes the contribution smaller than it could be.

A second way to make it bigger would be comparative framing: not just “no aggregate effect,” but “arrival had effect X; regularization had effect ~0, implying the economically salient adjustment occurred before legalization.” That would elevate the contribution from a standalone null to a two-margin insight about immigration policy.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

### Closest neighbors

The closest neighbors appear to be:

- **Borjas (2003)** and subsequent immigration-incidence work.
- **Ottaviano and Peri (2012)** / **Peri and Sparber (2009)** on adjustment mechanisms and specialization.
- **Kossoudji and Cobb-Clark (2002)** on wage effects of legalization under IRCA.
- **Amuedo-Dorantes, Bansak, and Raphael**-type work on legalization/IRCA and immigrant outcomes.
- On the Colombia/Venezuela side: **Caruso, Canon, and Mueller (2021)**; **Lebow**; **Rozo**; perhaps **Peñaloza** if that is indeed the right citation cluster.
- For informality: **Ulyssea (2020)**, **Meghir, Narita, and Robin (2015)**.

### How should the paper position itself?

Build on, not attack.

The paper should say:
- The inflow literature identifies effects of new labor entering a market.
- The regularization literature focuses mostly on legalized workers themselves.
- This paper links the two by asking whether legalization of an already-present population changes aggregate local labor markets.
- In a high-informality economy, the answer may differ sharply from the canonical inflow case.

That is a synthesis story, and it is the right one. There is no need to overstate that “the empirical basis is thin” or imply that prior work missed the important margin. Better to say the literatures have studied adjacent objects, and this paper connects them.

### Is the paper positioned too narrowly or too broadly?

At present, slightly too narrowly in empirical object and slightly too broadly in external claims.

Too narrow because it is really a paper about aggregate regional outcomes in one country and one setting, but it occasionally gestures toward sweeping claims about regularization generally. Too broad because the conclusion jumps quickly from Colombia to the U.S., EU, and Turkey without doing enough to articulate when the Colombia result should and should not travel.

The right positioning is: this is an important test case in a high-informality setting, and that setting is exactly why the result is interesting.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?

It should engage more explicitly with:
- legalization/amnesty studies beyond IRCA wage effects,
- labor-market formalization literature,
- papers on migrant legal status and labor-market segmentation,
- broader state-capacity / labor-regulation enforcement literature,
- perhaps refugee integration / temporary protection literature, especially Europe post-Ukraine.

The paper also should speak more directly to political economy. The stated motivation is political fear of labor-market harm. Then the paper should situate itself in the economics of backlash, perceived competition, and misperception about labor-market incidence.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?

Almost, but not quite. It is currently having a somewhat familiar immigration-labor conversation. The more impactful conversation is: **what does legal status actually do in segmented labor markets?** That connects immigration, informality, labor institutions, and state capacity. That conversation is more distinctive and more AER-relevant than simply “another local labor-market immigration paper.”

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

There are millions of unauthorized migrants in host countries, and governments debate whether legalizing them will hurt native workers and formal labor markets.

### Tension

Economists know a lot about immigrant arrivals, but much less about legalization of people already present. These are different margins, especially where many migrants already work informally. So the core tension is: should we expect legalization to generate a visible labor-market shock, or has the market already adjusted before legal status changes?

### Resolution

In Colombia’s large 2021 regularization of Venezuelans, the paper finds no detectable aggregate effects on employment, unemployment, or participation across departments.

### Implications

The implications should be: fears of large aggregate labor-market disruption from legalization may be overstated in economies where migrants are already working and informality is widespread; the main effects of legalization may instead be compositional, institutional, and welfare-related rather than aggregate employment effects.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the bones of one, yes. But it is not yet fully disciplined. Right now there is a risk that the paper is a collection of null results surrounded by an ex post mechanism. The phrase “regularization illusion” is a good title device, but the paper has not fully earned it. The mechanism—absorption through informality—feels more asserted than shown.

So the story it should be telling is not “here is a null, and I give it a catchy name.” It should be:  
1. Regularization is a distinct policy margin.  
2. In segmented labor markets, theory predicts smaller aggregate effects than in arrival settings.  
3. Colombia is the decisive case because scale is large and informality is high.  
4. Aggregate outcomes indeed do not move.  
5. Therefore the economically meaningful effects of legalization likely lie in formalization and worker welfare, not employment levels.

That is a coherent arc. The current draft is close, but still too results-forward and mechanism-light.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?

“Colombia legalized roughly 1.8 million Venezuelan migrants—one of the largest regularizations anywhere—and aggregate regional employment and unemployment barely moved.”

That is the dinner-party line.

### Would people lean in?

Some would, yes, because the policy scale is eye-catching and the question is very live. But after the first sentence, the key risk is that people immediately ask: “Okay, but did formal employment rise? Did natives get pushed into informality? Did migrants’ wages improve?” If the answer is “this paper can’t tell,” attention will fade.

So the paper passes the first five seconds of the test, but not yet the next thirty.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Almost certainly: **If aggregate outcomes didn’t move, where did the adjustment happen?**

That is the question the paper must answer—or at least orient the paper around. Right now it hints at the answer, but with outcomes too coarse to satisfy the audience.

### Is the null itself interesting?

Yes, but conditionally. A null of this sort is interesting only if:
- the policy is genuinely important,
- effects were widely expected,
- the paper can explain why zero is informative rather than a shrug,
- and the null shifts beliefs.

This paper has the first two. It partially has the third. It does not yet fully deliver the fourth, because the mechanism is more conjectural than demonstrated. As written, the null is interesting enough for a field journal discussion. For AER, it needs to become a belief-changing null: not just “we didn’t see effects,” but “this teaches us that the relevant margin is formalization rather than aggregate labor supply.”

At present, it is not quite there.

Some, but not enough. It currently adds policy rhetoric more than conceptual synthesis. It should more clearly articulate scope conditions: when should one expect null aggregate effects from regularization, and when not?

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The gap is mainly a **scope and ambition problem**, with some framing issues.

The framing is not bad. In fact, the basic question is good and the setting is attractive. But the paper in current form is too safe and too thin relative to the scale of the claim. An AER paper on this topic would need to do more than show no movement in aggregate regional employment rates. It would need to reveal the margin of adjustment.

Right now, the paper’s big claim is effectively: “Legalization had no aggregate labor-market effect, likely because of informality.” But the paper does not really observe the mechanism it elevates. That leaves it between genres:
- too mechanistic to be just a descriptive policy note,
- too reduced-form to settle the mechanism it proposes.

### Is it a framing problem?

Partly. The paper should lean harder into “legal status in segmented labor markets” and less into “a clean null DiD.”

### Is it a scope problem?

Yes, clearly. Aggregate employment, unemployment, and participation are not enough for the story the paper wants to tell.

### Is it a novelty problem?

Moderately. The policy setting is novel enough, but the empirical object—regional aggregate labor outcomes after an immigration-related reform—is familiar. Novelty has to come from the conceptual distinction and the mechanism, not the design alone.

### Is it an ambition problem?

Yes. The paper is competent but conservative. It asks a big policy question, then answers it with outcomes that are too blunt. For AER, it needs to either:
- show the formal/informal mechanism directly, or
- frame itself more narrowly and modestly.

### Single most impactful advice

If the author can only change one thing: **rebuild the paper around formalization and labor-market segmentation rather than aggregate null effects—use outcomes that can show whether legalization changed formal employment, social insurance enrollment, or native/migrant sorting between formal and informal work.**

That is the difference between a respectable null paper and a potentially field-shifting one.
