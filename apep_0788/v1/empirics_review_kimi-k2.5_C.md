### 1. Idea Fidelity

The paper pursues the core empirical strategy outlined in the manifest—a triple-difference design exploiting destination, product, and time variation to test for trade deflection from CBAM. However, it deviates from the original research plan in two consequential ways.

**First**, the manifest specified eight major exporters including India (`CN, IN, TR, RU, UA, VN, TW, BR`), motivated explicitly by India's high carbon intensity (~2.0 tCO\textsubscript{2}/t) which is central to the dose-response test. The paper inexplicably omits India, reducing the sample to seven exporters and severely weakening power for the carbon-intensity heterogeneity analysis. This exclusion is particularly damaging given that India is the second-largest steel producer and a primary target of CBAM compliance pressure.

**Second**, the manifest proposed using HS 7604-7616 (aluminium articles) as an uncovered placebo within the aluminium sector, complementing the HS 72/73 comparison. The paper uses only HS 73 as the uncovered control, failing to leverage the within-aluminium variation that would strengthen placebo credibility for HS 76.

Otherwise, the paper faithfully implements the triple-difference specification, timeline (Oct 2023 cutoff), and data sources (UN Comtrade) as described.

---

### 2. Summary

This paper provides the first empirical test of trade deflection from the EU's Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism using a triple-difference design on monthly bilateral metal trade (2020–2024). During the transitional (reporting-only) phase, the author finds no evidence that CBAM-covered exports redirected from the EU toward non-regulated markets (US, Japan, UK). The point estimate for deflection is economically negligible (0.003 SD) and statistically insignificant, though the EU-bound trade in covered products shows a suggestive decline. The results challenge CGE-model predictions of significant trade reallocation, suggesting either that reporting obligations lack sufficient bite or that metal supply chains exhibit high destination-switching costs.
