**Summary**

This paper is the first empirical test of CBAM-induced trade deflection, focusing on metals during the European Union’s October 2023–December 2024 transitional phase. Using monthly UN Comtrade data for major exporters to the EU versus three non-CBAM destinations and exploiting the within-sector coverage boundary (HS 72/76 covered, HS 73 uncovered) in a triple-difference framework, the author finds no statistically or economically significant redirection of covered exports away from the EU. Robustness checks—excluding Russia/Ukraine, COVID data, and the United States; conducting placebo tests; and estimating a dose–response with carbon intensity—support the null, suggesting that reporting requirements have not yet caused trade deflection.

**Essential Points**

1. **Pre-treatment trends are not convincingly flat.** The event study reports statistically significant coefficients at $t=-9$ and $t=-6$ and a pre-trend joint $F$-test $p=0.041$. These deviations could reflect lingering confounders (e.g., war-induced shocks) that differentially affect covered versus uncovered goods across destinations. The credibility of the triple-difference hinges on parallel pre-trends, so the authors must either better explain or account for these violations—perhaps by modeling the war shock explicitly, restricting the pre-period, or reweighting the event study to show insignificance in the $t=-4$ to $t=-1$ window that matters for anticipation.

2. **The exporter sample excludes key players promised in the manifest, which may bias inference on deflection.** India is explicitly cited in the manifesto’s policy relevance, yet it is omitted from the empirical sample. India is a large exporter to non-EU markets with a distinct carbon intensity profile; excluding it may understate or mischaracterize deflection patterns. The authors should justify this exclusion (data availability, sample coverage) and show that the results are not sensitive to adding or omitting other large exporters (e.g., South Korea, Mexico) to demonstrate the generality of the null.

3. **Identification of the deflection margin relies on trade value rather than relative market shares, risking aggregation bias if overall demand shifts differ across destinations.** The null result could hide compositional effects—the absolute volume exported to the EU may decline while aggregate exports remain flat because global demand fell or because prices adjust, without any actual reallocation to non-EU markets. A more compelling test would compare the share of covered exports going to non-EU destinations (or trade volumes normalized by exporter output) or use quantity data to rule out price-driven value changes. Without this, the claim that “tons that stopped going to the EU did not show up elsewhere” remains underpowered.

**Suggestions**

- **Address pre-trend concerns with additional diagnostics.** The event study shows significant deviations far before treatment. Provide alternative event studies focusing on a shorter pre-period (e.g., 12 months before CBAM) or use interactions with known shocks (e.g., the Russia–Ukraine war or supply-chain disruptions) to demonstrate that the triple-difference slope is stable when those shocks are controlled. Alternatively, conduct a formal test removing the early pre-period data (e.g., start in mid-2021) to see if the $p$-value for the pre-trend null improves and whether the main estimate changes.

- **Clarify the exporter selection decision and expand sensitivity checks.** The manuscript should explain why exporters like India and Vietnam (if data permits) were excluded, given the manifest’s emphasis on China, India, and Turkey. If data limitations drove these exclusions, state that explicitly. If not, re-estimate the baseline with a broader exporter set (including India, South Korea, Mexico, etc.) to ensure the null result does not hinge on a selective sample. At minimum, show that excluding or including additional exporters leaves the estimates qualitatively unchanged.

- **Augment the deflection test by analyzing destination shares or quantities.** Use exporter-product-month-level export shares (e.g., share of covered exports destined for non-EU markets) as dependent variables to see if the CBAM period coincides with any reallocation even if absolute values do not rise. Alternatively, switch to quantity data (kilograms) to control for price movements; if CBAM subtly raises prices to the EU without changing quantities (or vice versa), the value-based triple difference could misrepresent the deflection margin. These exercises would bolster confidence that the null reflects a true absence of global reallocation, not just constant values obscuring compositional shifts.

- **Deepen the carbon-intensity dose–response analysis.** The current interaction coefficient is imprecise, but the manifest’s promised monotonic relationship between exporter intensity and deflection is conceptually strong. Consider grouping exporters into terciles by carbon intensity and estimating the triple difference within each group or interacting a continuous intensity measure with both the triple-difference term and time. Also report the implied marginal effects (with bounds) across the range of intensities to show whether the data are informative about meaningful heterogeneity.

- **Discuss the role of pricing versus reporting in more structural terms.** The paper suggests that deflection may emerge only once the pricing phase begins, but it would be helpful to quantify the expected CBAM cost differential during the transitional phase versus the pricing phase. A brief back-of-the-envelope calculation of the implied “shadow price” (e.g., probability of CBAM applying in pricing phase times ETS price times emission intensity) could contextualize why the reporting phase might have limited bite and therefore limited short-run deflection.

- **Clarify the consequences of the positive yet insignificant triple-difference estimate.** Table~\ref{tab:sde} labels the point estimate as “Large positive,” yet the main text emphasizes it as null; this needs reconciliation. Consider reframing standardized effect sizes to reflect uncertainty (e.g., by presenting confidence intervals around the SDE) and emphasize that the data are consistent with zero deflection rather than declaring a “large positive” standardized coefficient with low precision.

- **Expand on potential lagged responses.** The discussion briefly mentions that the definitive phase may change the dynamics. This could be more tightly linked to the empirical design: for example, by exploring whether any destination switching occurs with a lag (e.g., two or three quarters after CBAM starts) even in the transitional phase, perhaps because firms anticipated the pricing phase. A distributed lag specification or weighing later post-period months more heavily might uncover emerging patterns that a contemporaneous treatment dummy misses.

By addressing these points, the paper would significantly strengthen the credibility and policy relevance of its key conclusion: that the CBAM transitional phase has not yet deflected high-carbon metal trade to non-CBAM markets.
