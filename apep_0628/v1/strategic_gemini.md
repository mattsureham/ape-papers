### 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

The paper investigates how central bank foreign exchange (FX) rationing—restricting certain importers from accessing official currency markets—functions as a massive, "invisible" trade barrier. Using Nigeria’s 2015 ban on 41 product categories as a quasi-experiment, it finds that forcing importers into the high-premium parallel market reduced import volumes by 28%, an effect equivalent to a significant tariff that never appears in official trade databases.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this well. The first paragraph is punchy and identifies the "invisible" nature of the barrier.
**The "Better" Pitch:** To reach AER-level gravity, the pitch should lean harder into the *mismeasurement* of global trade policy. "Standard measures of trade protectionism ignore the banking system. By exploiting a product-level FX ban in Nigeria, we show that central bank rationing creates 'shadow tariffs' that reduce trade by nearly a third, suggesting that current estimates of global trade barriers are systematically understated in developing countries."

### 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution:** The paper provides the first product-level, quasi-experimental evidence that FX rationing acts as a quantitatively large de facto trade barrier.

*   **Differentiation:** It moves beyond the macro-level correlations of Wei (1999) and the credit-constraint focus of Manova (2013) by looking at product-specific *access* to currency.
*   **Question vs. Literature:** It currently straddles both. To strengthen it, the authors should frame it less as "we fill a gap in Nigeria's macro history" and more as "how much trade destruction is hidden in the world's financial plumbing?"
*   **What would make it bigger?** The "So What" would be bigger if they could show **downstream effects**. If these are inputs (cement, steel), what happened to Nigerian manufacturing or construction? A 28% drop in key inputs is a supply shock; tracing that into local prices or production would move this from a "trade paper" to a "top-tier general interest paper."

### 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

*   **Closest Neighbors:** Amiti and Weinstein (2011) on financial shocks; Goldberg and Pavcnik (2016) on trade policy; Caliendo and Parro (2015) on elasticities.
*   **Strategy:** It should **synthesize** the trade and international finance literatures. Currently, it feels like a trade paper that happens to use an FX shock. It should position itself as a critique of how we measure "protectionism"—arguing that the "Trade" field and the "International Finance" field are ignoring each other's data.
*   **Niche vs. Broad:** It risks being seen as a "Nigeria paper." To avoid this, it must emphasize that 20+ African countries (and many others globally) use these "leaky" barriers.

### 4. NARRATIVE ARC

*   **Setup:** Trade economists track tariffs and quotas.
*   **Tension:** However, many countries maintain dual exchange rates that act like tariffs but leave no paper trail in customs.
*   **Resolution:** Nigeria’s 2015 policy provides the "smoking gun" to measure this. The triple-difference reveals a 28% drop hidden by global trends.
*   **Implications:** Trade liberalization metrics are biased if they only look at the border and ignore the central bank.
*   **Evaluation:** The arc is strong. The "twist"—that the simple DiD shows a null result while the DDD reveals the true destruction—is a classic AER-style narrative "reveal."

### 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

At a dinner party, you lead with: **"Nigeria effectively slapped a 40% tariff on rice and steel, but if you look at their official tariff schedule, it looks like nothing changed."**
Economists would lean in. The follow-up: "Did it actually help local industry (import substitution) or just create a massive black market for rent-seeking?" The paper currently hints at the latter ("leaky" barriers), which is the more interesting story.

### 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

*   **Front-load the Mechanism:** Move the discussion of the "parallel market premium" (Section 2.2) even earlier. The fact that this is a "tariff" where the revenue goes to "market intermediaries" rather than the "government treasury" is a huge political economy point that deserves more space.
*   **The Null Result:** Spend more time on Table 2, Column 1. Explaining *why* the simple DiD fails is a great methodological lesson for people working with Comtrade data.

### 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The gap is **Scope and Ambition**. Right now, it’s a very clean, very competent measurement of a specific shock.
*   **The single most impactful change:** Connect the trade destruction to **consumer prices or domestic production**. If the authors can show that the "Invisible Tariff" led to a measurable spike in the Nigerian CPI for those specific goods, or a decline in the output of industries using those inputs, the paper becomes a "must-cite" for both macro and trade.
