1. **Idea Fidelity** — The paper follows the broad spirit of the manifest: it studies Nigeria’s 2015 FX exclusion list using HS-level trade data, compares treated and untreated products before and after the policy, and adds a West African control-country comparison. However, it misses two key elements of the original idea in ways that materially weaken the design. First, the manifest envisioned treatment at the specific banned HS6 products; the paper instead maps the policy to entire HS2 chapters, expanding treatment from roughly 150–200 HS6 codes in the idea to 1,435 HS6 codes in the paper. That is not a minor implementation detail: it substantially changes the estimand and creates large treatment misclassification. Second, the manifest proposed BACI bilateral trade data and domestic substitution/mechanism evidence (e.g., FAO production, parallel premium intensity); the paper uses Comtrade “world” imports and does not actually deliver the mechanism or substitution tests. So the paper pursues the main question, but only a coarse and incomplete version of the original strategy.

2. **Summary** — This paper asks a good question: did Nigeria’s 2015 exclusion of selected imports from the official FX window operate like a hidden trade barrier? Using product-level import data and a triple-difference design relative to Ghana, Côte d’Ivoire, and Senegal, the paper reports a decline of about 0.33 log points in imports of “banned” products into Nigeria. The topic is important and the main magnitude is economically plausible, but the current implementation is too coarse and internally inconsistent to support the paper’s central causal claim.

3. **Essential Points** —  
**1. Treatment assignment is much too crude and likely invalidates the interpretation.**  
The biggest problem is the mapping of 41 named banned categories into 23 entire HS2 chapters, generating 1,435 treated HS6 codes. That is an enormous expansion of treatment status. Entire chapters such as vehicles, aircraft, plastics, paper, iron and steel articles, etc. contain many goods that were plainly not on the exclusion list. This is not just classical attenuation: if the “treated” chapters were selected by Nigeria for broader industrial-policy reasons, the chapter-level classification may load in many other contemporaneous product-specific policies and demand shifts. For an AER: Insights paper, treatment must be mapped much more tightly to the actual banned goods, ideally at HS6 or at least HS4 with a transparent concordance in an appendix. Right now, the paper is best described as “imports in chapters related to banned goods declined,” which is a much weaker and less policy-specific statement.

**2. The identifying assumptions are not yet persuasive because the core comparison may mix the FX policy with other Nigeria-specific interventions on the same goods.**  
The paper explicitly concedes concurrent policies, including rice policy and import-substitution programs, but then says this “would not invalidate the DDD estimate.” I disagree. The DDD recovers the combined effect of all Nigeria-specific post-2015 shocks affecting the selected products relative to controls. If the same goods were simultaneously targeted by tariffs, quantitative restrictions, border enforcement, or agricultural programs, then the paper cannot claim to estimate the trade effect of the FX exclusion itself. This is especially problematic because the CBN likely chose products already under pressure from import-substitution policy. You need either (i) to narrow treatment to products uniquely affected by the FX restriction, (ii) to document and control for other product-level policy changes, or (iii) to soften the claim and present the estimate as the reduced-form impact of the broader policy bundle centered on FX exclusion.

**3. The inference and presentation of results are not reliable enough in the current draft.**  
Several claims are overstated relative to the reported estimates. The paper says the effect operates “entirely on the intensive margin,” but the intensive-margin estimate in Table 1 is not statistically distinguishable from zero. The event-study table contains no coefficients, yet the text discusses detailed dynamics. The robustness table appears inconsistent with the text: the placebo-country coefficient is positive and marginally significant, which is not naturally described as “no spurious effect.” More fundamentally, standard errors clustered at HS2 are not obviously appropriate when treatment is assigned at the chapter level and only 23 chapters are treated. Inference may be quite fragile with a small number of treated clusters and strong serial correlation. At a minimum, I would want wild-cluster bootstrap p-values, randomization inference over treated product sets, and clustering choices justified by the level of assignment and residual dependence.

4. **Suggestions** —  
This is a promising paper, and I think it could become publishable if the empirical design is tightened substantially. My suggestions are as follows.

First, rebuild the treatment coding from the policy text itself. The paper’s credibility would improve dramatically with a product concordance table that maps each of the 41 listed items into HS codes, indicates any ambiguities, and distinguishes “core treated” goods from “possibly affected” goods. I would strongly encourage a narrow baseline sample using only products that can be confidently linked to the circular. You can then use broader HS4/HS2 mappings as robustness checks rather than the main design. Right now the treatment set is so broad that a skeptical reader will not know what was actually banned.

Second, use bilateral BACI data, as the original idea proposed, rather than reporter-world aggregates from Comtrade. Bilateral data would let you test whether the decline is broad-based across suppliers or concentrated among certain origin countries, whether trade diversion toward neighboring entrepôt exporters occurred, and whether composition changes in partners account for some of the result. BACI’s harmonization is also preferable for this type of product-level panel. With bilateral data, your fixed-effects structure can be strengthened considerably: exporter-by-year effects or product-by-exporter effects would help absorb supply-side shocks.

Third, sharpen the identification around treatment timing. With annual data and a June 2015 intervention, excluding 2015 is sensible, but the event-study remains weak because there are few clean pre-periods and substantial macro turmoil in 2015–2016. If quarterly or monthly customs data are unavailable, then you should be more restrained in claiming dynamic validation. In the current draft, the event-study is doing too much rhetorical work without showing the underlying estimates. At minimum, include the full graph with confidence bands, all coefficients in an appendix table, and a clear statement about the limited power of pre-trend tests.

Fourth, address the concern that the policy is endogenous to product trends. A useful exercise would be to compare pre-2015 trends in treated and untreated products within Nigeria and relative to controls using only narrowly coded treated products. Another would be to estimate specifications with product-specific linear trends, although I would treat these as sensitivity checks rather than a panacea. You might also exploit variation in exposure intensity: products with higher import dependence or higher pass-through from FX premia should be more affected if the mechanism is truly foreign-exchange rationing.

Fifth, the paper needs a much stronger mechanism section. The current version asserts that the effect tracks the parallel-market premium, but does not estimate that relationship. A more convincing approach would interact treatment with annual measures of the parallel-market premium, or split the post period into high-premium versus lower-premium years. Likewise, the “domestic substitution” angle from the manifest is missing. If the policy was intended to foster local production, do we see increases in domestic output in rice, cement, textiles, etc.? Even imperfect evidence from FAO, industrial production, or sector reports would improve the economic interpretation. If domestic production did not rise, that would itself be a useful result: the policy destroyed trade without generating visible substitution.

Sixth, revisit the magnitude discussion. A 28 percent decline from a 30–60 percent implicit FX wedge is plausible, not implausibly large. But the tariff-equivalent welfare language currently outruns the evidence. The paper should be careful: the parallel premium is not necessarily one-for-one with the importer’s marginal cost wedge, and not all firms may have paid the spot black-market rate. The elasticity-based translation into “equivalent tariff” should be presented as a back-of-the-envelope calculation, ideally with a range using alternative elasticities and pass-through assumptions. Similarly, the statement that the premium is a “deadweight transfer to intermediaries” is too loose; part of it may reflect scarcity rents, risk compensation, or broader macro distortions. The welfare discussion should be toned down unless formally derived.

Seventh, improve inference and transparency. Report p-values consistently. Use wild-cluster bootstrap by HS2 or, better, by the level of treatment assignment once you redefine treatment more tightly. Consider randomization inference that reassigns treatment across similarly sized product groups. Show robustness to alternative outcome transformations: levels with PPML, inverse hyperbolic sine, log conditional on positive imports, and extensive margin. For trade data with many zeros and heteroskedasticity, PPML is often more natural than log(y+1), especially in the multi-country panel.

Eighth, clean up internal inconsistencies. The summary statistics in the table and text do not match exactly. The event-study table is effectively empty. The paper says placebo tests show “no spurious effect,” but one placebo is positive and significant at conventional levels. The discussion of the intensive margin overstates significance. These are fixable, but they matter because they erode confidence in the econometrics.

Finally, I would encourage the authors to simplify and discipline the paper’s claims. The core contribution could still be interesting even in a narrower form: “Nigeria’s FX exclusion list, combined with contemporaneous product-specific policies, reduced imports in targeted product lines.” That is already useful. But to claim a clean estimate of the causal effect of FX rationing as an “invisible tariff,” the paper needs much more precise treatment coding, cleaner policy isolation, and stronger inference. In its current form, the paper asks an excellent question and finds a plausible effect, but it does not yet convincingly answer the question it poses.
