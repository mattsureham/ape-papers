**Summary**  
The paper documents that Nigeria’s 2015 Central Bank circular barring 41 product categories from official FX access reduced imports of those products by about 28 percent, operating primarily through lower intensive-margin volumes. Using UN Comtrade HS6 data for Nigeria and three West African control countries, the authors implement a product-country-year triple-difference with rich fixed effects, isolating the Nigeria-specific disruption after accounting for global product shocks. The finding highlights “invisible tariffs”—FX rationing—as a potent but under-appreciated trade barrier.

**Essential Points**  
1. **Targeting and Treatment Definition Need Further Justification.** The treatment is mapped at the HS2-chapter level (23 chapters) even though the circular lists only 41 specific product descriptions. As the authors acknowledge, many HS6 codes in those chapters may not have been barred, so the treatment is measured with considerable noise and may even mix treated and untreated products. This imperfect mapping could bias the estimates toward zero, but it also raises concerns about whether the comparison group adequately reflects unaffected products. Please provide more detail on the mapping procedure and ideally robustness checks using narrower subsets (e.g., HS6 codes directly mentioned in the circular, or HS6 codes with high textual similarity) to show results are not driven by misclassification.  
2. **Parallel Trends and Confounders Require Stronger Support.** The DDD relies on the assumption that, absent the FX exclusion, Nigeria’s imports of the treated products would have tracked the control countries’ imports (after absorbing product-year trends). Given the many Nigeria-specific shocks in 2015–2022—tariff changes, the Anchor Borrowers programme, FX recapitalizations, and oil-price-driven demand fluctuations—it is essential to demonstrate that these coincide roughly equally across treated and control products. The current discussion is qualitative; please add empirical tests such as allowing for product-specific trends in Nigeria or interacting country-level macro controls with treatment to show the result is robust.  
3. **Interpretation of the Parallel-Market Premium as a Tariff Equivalent Needs Care.** The welfare interpretation assumes the parallel-market premium is uniformly borne by importers and that goods continued to flow via parallel FX at higher costs. Without direct micro evidence on parallel FX use or import prices, this is speculative. Consider supplementing the analysis with any available data on parallel-market FX volumes/premia or importer surveys to support the tariff-equivalent claim, or at least clearly frame it as a back-of-the-envelope comparison subject to caveats.

**Suggestions**  
- **Clarify the Event-Study Estimates.** Table 3 reports only the pre-trend F-test but does not show the actual coefficient estimates for each event year. Including a figure or table with the coefficients and confidence intervals would help readers assess the dynamics and ensure no pre-trend is lurking in the early panels.  
- **Consider Alternative Control Groups.** While Ghana, Côte d’Ivoire, and Senegal are reasonable choices, their trade structures differ from Nigeria’s in important ways. Adding a sensitivity exercise using other countries (e.g., Benin, Burkina Faso) or constructing synthetic controls for each HS6 product could bolster credibility that the effect is not driven by idiosyncratic shocks in the chosen comparators.  
- **Explore Heterogeneity by Product Demand Elasticity.** If the policy operates like a tariff, products with more elastic demand should exhibit larger declines. Exploit variation in estimated import demand elasticities (from the literature or from cross-sectional price instruments) to test whether the response magnitudes align with theory. This would also help buttress the welfare narrative.  
- **Explicitly Address the Exclusion of 2015.** The paper drops 2015 as a “partial treatment” year, but many product adjustments likely began after June 2015 and could affect annual totals. Consider including 2015 with a fractional treatment indicator (e.g., weight by months) or, at minimum, show that results are unchanged when 2015 is included and coded as post-treatment to reassure the reader that the drop is not driving the findings.  
- **Explain Why the Within-Nigeria DiD is Null but the DDD is Not.** The contrast between Tables 2 and 4 is striking. Add a short discussion of the implied counterfactual trend: how much would banned-product imports have grown absent the policy? Quantifying the forgone growth (e.g., comparing trends in control countries) would help interpret the 28% reduction and clarify why the simple DiD understates the effect.  
- **Address Possible Spillovers.** If importers of banned products shifted sourcing to other countries or switched to domestically produced substitutes, there could be spillovers onto other trade lines or domestic industries. Consider examining imports of close substitutes (e.g., local rice production) or exports to test for compensatory effects, thereby enriching the mechanism section.  
- **Report Standard Errors Appropriately.** Several tables (e.g., Table 2) have large standard errors relative to the point estimates, and the intensive-margin estimate is only marginally significant. Consider bootstrapping or using Wild Cluster Bootstrap to confirm inference, especially given the relatively small number of clusters (23 HS2 chapters).  
- **Discuss External Validity.** The paper argues that FX rationing is common in developing countries. Briefly discuss how Nigeria’s case compares to other countries (e.g., severity of premium, enforcement) and whether the estimated effect is likely representative. This would help policymakers gauge the broader applicability of the results.  

Overall, the paper tackles an important and under-studied form of trade policy. Addressing the above points would strengthen the credibility of the identification and enrich the welfare interpretation.
