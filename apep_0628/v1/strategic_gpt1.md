## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether restricting certain imports from access to official foreign exchange acts like a hidden trade barrier. Using Nigeria’s 2015 FX exclusion list, the paper argues that forcing importers into the parallel market substantially reduced imports of targeted goods, implying that exchange-control policy can function as an “invisible tariff” that standard trade-policy measures miss.

A busy economist should care because this is potentially a broad point: many countries do not just regulate trade at the border; they regulate it in financial markets. If true, that changes how we think about trade policy, exchange-rate regimes, import compression, and the measurement of openness in developing economies.

**Does the paper articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Partly, but not optimally. The opening paragraph is vivid and strong. The second paragraph immediately switches into “first product-level quasi-experimental evidence” and a literature-gap formulation. That is weaker than the world-facing question. The paper should lead less with “no one has studied this” and more with “economists may be mismeasuring trade barriers in a large set of countries.”

**What the first two paragraphs should say instead:**  

> Governments often restrict imports without changing tariffs or quotas: they simply deny importers access to scarce foreign currency at the official exchange rate. In countries with dual exchange-rate systems, that policy can raise the domestic cost of targeted imports by 30–60 percent while leaving no trace in tariff schedules or standard trade-policy databases. If foreign exchange allocation works this way, economists may be missing an important and pervasive form of trade protection.
>
> We study Nigeria’s 2015 decision to exclude 41 product categories from official FX access. The policy did not ban imports, but forced importers of targeted goods to buy dollars on the parallel market at a large premium. Using product-level trade data and regional comparison countries, we show that these FX restrictions reduced targeted imports sharply, implying that exchange controls can operate as powerful “invisible tariffs.” This matters not just for Nigeria, but for how we measure trade policy and import barriers across developing countries.

That is the pitch the paper should have.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:**  
The paper shows that product-specific restrictions on access to official foreign exchange can materially reduce imports, implying that FX rationing functions as an important but largely unmeasured non-border trade barrier.

### Evaluation

**Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?**  
Not yet clearly enough. The paper says, in effect, “trade barriers matter, financial frictions matter, Nigeria is interesting, and this hasn’t been studied directly.” That is serviceable, but for AER the differentiation needs to be sharper. Right now the contribution risks sounding like: “another reduced-form paper showing that making imports more expensive lowers imports.” The real novelty is not the sign of the effect; it is the **policy object** being measured. The paper needs to say more cleanly:

- This is **not** a paper about tariffs, quotas, or general devaluation.
- This is **not** a paper about generic financial frictions.
- It is a paper about **administrative allocation of FX as product-specific trade policy**.

**Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a gap in a literature?**  
Currently too much as a literature gap. “Almost no empirical attention” is not an AER-grade reason on its own. The stronger world question is: **How do governments protect domestic markets when they cannot or do not use formal border instruments?** That framing is much better.

**Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what’s new?**  
At present, maybe, but not confidently. They might say: “It’s a DiD on Nigeria’s FX ban showing imports fell.” That is not enough. You want them to say: “It shows exchange controls are effectively selective trade barriers, so standard measures of trade policy miss a meaningful chunk of protection.”

**What would make the contribution bigger? Be specific.**  
Three possibilities:

1. **Broader framing around measurement of trade policy.**  
   The biggest upside is to turn this from “Nigeria case study” into “evidence on a hidden class of trade barriers.” That means explicitly linking to how economists measure openness, NTBs, and policy wedges.

2. **A stronger mechanism-to-object link.**  
   Not more econometrics, but more conceptual clarity on why this is distinct from a generic macro crisis. If the paper could better connect import declines to the time-varying parallel premium or to goods more dependent on formal banking channels, the contribution becomes more than “trade fell after a bad macro shock.” Even at the framing level, this matters.

3. **More ambitious implications/outcomes.**  
   The paper would feel bigger if it went beyond import values to one of the following:
   - import prices/unit values,
   - domestic prices/inflation for targeted goods,
   - reallocation across source countries or informal channels,
   - downstream production effects for sectors using targeted inputs,
   - welfare/consumer consequences.
   
   Any one of these would elevate it from “trade destruction” to “economic significance.”

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Many developing countries face foreign exchange shortages and operate dual exchange-rate systems. Economists usually measure trade barriers through tariffs, quotas, and NTBs recorded at the border. But governments may instead restrict access to official FX, raising the cost of selected imports without formally changing trade policy.

### Tension
If that is true, then our standard picture of trade policy is incomplete. Yet we have little direct evidence on whether product-specific FX rationing actually changes trade flows in the way a tariff would.

### Resolution
Nigeria’s 2015 exclusion list provides a test: targeted goods lost access to cheap official dollars and, relative to appropriate controls, their imports fell substantially.

### Implications
FX rationing is a meaningful trade barrier, not merely a macro side show. That affects how economists should measure protection, interpret openness, and think about the real effects of exchange controls in developing economies.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the ingredients, but the arc is not fully disciplined. The paper currently feels like:

- striking opening,
- then methods and design,
- then “simple DiD is null, DDD works,”
- then some mechanism language,
- then standard implications.

The **best story is not the econometric twist from null DiD to significant DDD**. That is an internal results narrative, not the substantive narrative. The story should be:

1. Hidden policy instrument,
2. Measurement problem,
3. Natural test in Nigeria,
4. Evidence of large import effects,
5. Implication for how we think about trade policy.

Right now too much of the introduction’s energy is spent narrating the estimator rather than the idea. That is backwards for a top journal pitch.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?
“Nigeria effectively imposed product-specific import taxes of 30–60 percent not through customs, but by denying official foreign exchange, and targeted imports fell by about 28 percent.”

That is a good fact. It has some dinner-party energy.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
Trade people and international macro people would lean in. The broader profession might be interested if the framing emphasizes that this is a **hidden form of protection used across many countries**, not just an idiosyncratic Nigerian episode.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Probably one of these:
- “Is this really an FX-rationing effect or a bundle of import-substitution policies?”
- “How general is this beyond Nigeria?”
- “If this is an invisible tariff, where does the wedge go and what are the welfare consequences?”
- “Why didn’t trade collapse more if the premium was so large?”

That last question is actually useful for positioning: the paper already notes that the implied tariff-equivalent is below the observed premium. That is interesting and underexploited. It suggests leakage, evasion, partial pass-through, or informal financing channels. That could be a substantive angle rather than a footnote.

### If the findings are modest: is the result still interesting?
Yes. A 28 percent effect is not trivial, but it is also not so huge as to close the case. The interesting part is not just the magnitude; it is the existence of a measurable effect from a policy instrument economists usually ignore. The paper can make that case. But if it wants AER-level excitement, it needs to explain why this is not merely a Nigerian curiosity.

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

### 4. Front-load the general-interest implications
The abstract and first pages should emphasize:

- mismeasurement of trade policy,

That is the broad-interest hook.

For an editorial read, the paper feels a bit checklist-like once it gets to placebo/LOO/etc. Since this is not where the paper’s strategic value lies, most of that can be compressed or sent to appendix, unless some result is genuinely conceptually important.

### 6. The conclusion should do more than restate
The current conclusion is clean but thin. It should end on the bigger point: economists and policymakers routinely treat exchange controls and trade policy as distinct domains, but in practice they can be the same instrument. That is the memorable takeaway.

### 7. Remove distracting meta material
The autonomous-generation acknowledgment and repository language are distracting in a top-journal context. Regardless of norms, it shifts attention away from the substance and toward provenance. For editorial positioning, that is not helping.
