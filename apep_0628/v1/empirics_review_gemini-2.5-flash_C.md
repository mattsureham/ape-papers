### Review of "The Invisible Tariff: Nigeria's FX Exclusion List and Product-Level Trade Destruction"

As a seasoned econometrician, I find this paper tackles a highly relevant and underexplored topic with a well-conceived identification strategy. The core idea – treating FX restrictions as “invisible tariffs” – is insightful and has significant policy implications for countries grappling with exchange rate management and trade. My review aims to be direct, constructive, and focused on enhancing the robustness and clarity of your contribution.

---

### 1. Idea Fidelity

The paper largely follows the original idea manifest, capturing its essence and core identification strategy. Key elements, such as the focus on Nigeria's 2015 CBN ban on 41 product categories, the use of product-level HS6 trade data, the DiD/DDD framework comparing banned vs. non-banned products and Nigeria vs. West African controls, and the motivation of "invisible tariffs," are all present and align well with the manifest.

There are a few minor deviations:
*   **Data Source:** The manifest proposed CEPII BACI data, while the paper uses UN Comtrade. While both offer HS6-level trade data, BACI often harmonizes reporter/partner data, which can yield more robust totals than relying solely on one reporter's declaration (as Comtrade allows). However, this is a minor data choice, and Comtrade is certainly a valid source. The choice to aggregate to “World” partner simplifies the panel but might miss granular diversion effects (e.g., if imports from high-cost informal channels are underreported or shift to specific partners).
*   **Mechanism Data Use:** The manifest mentioned using Nigeria parallel market FX premium data and FAO production data. The paper discusses the parallel market premium extensively in the narrative and as justification for the time profile of effects but does not formally incorporate it into the econometric models (e.g., as a time-varying treatment intensity or an interaction term). The domestic substitution test using FAO data (or any other production data) is entirely absent. This was a potentially powerful extension outlined in the manifest that could have strengthened the "invisible tariff" argument.
*   **Treated Units:** The manifest listed "~150-200 treated HS6 codes," while the paper analyzes "1,435 `treated` HS6 product codes in 23 HS2 chapters." This is a significant difference in scope, where the paper chose a broader, HS2-chapter-level definition. While this may increase statistical power, it also introduces more potential heterogeneity and misclassification within broad chapters. This decision should be transparently justified, particularly as it moves away from the "41 specific product categories" mentioned in the manifest (which likely correspond to fewer HS6 codes if mapped directly rather than by HS2 chapter).

Despite these points, the core intellectual contribution and methodological approach remain faithful to the initial concept.

### 2. Summary

This paper investigates the trade-destructive effects of Nigeria's 2015 Central Bank policy, which banned 41 product categories from accessing the official foreign exchange market, effectively imposing a selective tariff equivalent to the parallel market premium. Using a triple-difference design on HS6-level bilateral trade data from UN Comtrade, the authors compare imports of banned versus non-banned products in Nigeria against similar trends in three West African control countries. They find that the FX exclusion reduced imports of banned products into Nigeria by approximately 28%, an effect operating entirely on the intensive margin, highlighting FX rationing as a potent, yet opaque, trade barrier.

### 3. Essential Points

1.  **HS2 Mapping and Misclassification/Heterogeneity:** The decision to treat all HS6 codes within 23 HS2 chapters as "banned" is a critical methodological choice that warrants significantly more discussion and robustness. The original CBN circular explicitly lists 41 product categories, not 23 HS2 chapters. Mapping these categorical descriptions *directly* to specific HS6 codes would be more precise. By using entire HS2 chapters, the paper almost certainly includes many HS6 codes that were *not* explicitly on the ban list. This introduces measurement error in the treatment variable, which – if non-classical – could bias the coefficient. At best, it increases noise and attenuates the effect towards zero.
    *   **Actionable:** Provide a detailed appendix mapping the 41 product categories to *specific* HS6 codes, and re-run the analysis with this more precise treatment definition. This would be a more credible test. If a direct HS6 mapping is not feasible or yields too few treated units, then the current HS2-chapter-level assignment needs a much stronger defense. For instance, show that the selected HS2 chapters *predominantly* contain the banned items, or that the results are robust to dropping HS6 codes within these chapters that are less likely to be affected. The claim that this is "conservative" (attenuating towards zero) is an assumption that needs to be explored. What if the selected HS2 codes contain key *intermediate* inputs for which the ban might have had differential effects, or highly differentiated products which might respond differently?

2.  **Standard Errors & Clustering:** The paper consistently clusters standard errors at the HS2 level. While clustering at a higher geographical or product aggregation level is generally advisable to correct for spatial/group correlation, given that the treatment *itself* is defined at the HS2 level, this choice risks being problematic. If clustering is intended to capture the unit of treatment, then the clusters should be the 23 (or even 41, if mapping to HS2+specific product logic). If shocks are correlated *within* HS2 categories but *not across* them (which is what HS2 clustering assumes), that might be fine. But given the limited number of HS2 clusters (97 for within-Nigeria DiD, but what about the DDD where the treatment is *still* at the HS2 level?), inference could be weak. The "97 clusters" mentioned for the DiD suggests clustering at the HS2 level *across all products*, not just the treated ones. For the DDD, the effective number of units of analysis for the clustered standard error for the `Banned` variable (which is fixed across countries and years) is the number of HS2 chapters in the treated group.
    *   **Actionable:** Reconsider standard error clustering. For the DiD, if the treatment assignment is at the HS2 level, the appropriate level of clustering would likely be at the HS2 level *for the treated group samples*, or at the *country* level (if assuming uniform shocks across products within a country), or a "wild cluster bootstrap" with a small number of clusters. For the DDD, the most conservative approach would be multi-way clustering (product-country, and potentially calendar year), alongside a bootstrap. You might also consider aggregating the data to HS2-level imports, which would directly address the clustering issue for the treatment unit but would lose HS6-level heterogeneity. Explicitly state the number of effective clusters for the key treatment effect for both DiD and DDD, especially given the "97 clusters" result leads to a fairly high p-value (0.031) for a key result.

3.  **Welfare Analysis & Magnitudes:** The paper references potential welfare costs but does not quantify them beyond a qualitative statement about deadweight loss. More importantly, the implied "tariff equivalent" from the 28% reduction needs careful grounding. You state "equivalent to a tariff of 20–35 percentage points" based on "standard trade elasticity estimates."
    *   **Actionable:** Explicitly cite the range of trade elasticities used to derive this tariff equivalent (e.g., from \citet{caliendo_parro_2015} or \citet{costinot_rodriguez_clare_2014} as cited). Crucially, the elasticity of trade with respect to *tariffs* (or prices) is typically a negative number. If a 30-60% parallel market premium is the *cause* of the 28% reduction, then what is the implied elasticity? For example, if -2 is a "standard" elasticity, then a 30% price hike implies a 60% reduction, or a 60% premium implies 120% reduction. Your 28% reduction with a 30-60% premium implies an elasticity of around -0.5 to -1, which is on the lower side of standard estimates for aggregate trade but might be more plausible for specific products. Clarifying this link would significantly strengthen the economic plausibility and impact of your magnitude. This also ties back to the missing use of the FX premium data: an interaction of `Banned x Nigeria x Post x FX_Premium` could directly test the hypothesis that the effect scales with the premium.

### 4. Suggestions

1.  **Refine Treatment Definition and Sensitivity Analysis:** As noted above, the HS2-chapter-level ban is a major assumption.
    *   **Specific Suggestion:** Attempt to map the 41 original product descriptions to the *most specific* HS6 codes possible. This would require careful textual analysis of the CBN circular and comparison with HS codes. If this results in a small number of treated HS6 codes, acknowledge this limitation. Alternatively, perform a sensitivity analysis where you exclude "borderline" HS6 codes within the 23 treated HS2 chapters that are less clearly covered by the product descriptions. This would test if the effect is driven by the most obviously banned products.
    *   **Specific Suggestion:** Clearly state the *number* of distinct banned product *categories* (41 identified by the CBN) versus the HS2 categories (23 in your mapping) versus the derived HS6 codes (1,435). This helps the reader understand the level of aggregation.

2.  **Explicitly Incorporate Parallel Market Premium:** The paper frequently references the parallel market premium as the mechanism.
    *   **Specific Suggestion:** Integrate a measure of the premium into your regressions. For example, you could estimate a model where the treatment effect varies with the parallel market premium: `Banned_p * Nigeria_c * Post_t * Parallel_FX_Premium_t`. This would allow you to directly test if the effect scales with the intensity of the "invisible tariff." The premium also exhibited significant variations over time (10% to 60%), which is a powerful source of variation you are currently underutilizing. This would also tie the "time profile of effects" mentioned in the mechanisms section more tightly to the empirical results.

3.  **Address the "Global Increase" and DiD vs. DDD:** The contrast between the null DiD and significant DDD is a strong point.
    *   **Specific Suggestion:** Present the raw trends in imports for banned vs. non-banned products in Nigeria, and for banned products in Nigeria vs. control countries, perhaps in a figure. This would visually demonstrate the issue of the "global increase" in banned products and why the DDD is necessary. For example, a plot showing `log(Imports)` over time, separately for Nigeria-banned, Nigeria-non-banned, and ControlCountries-banned products would be very illustrative.

4.  **Explore Mechanism: Intensive vs. Extensive Margin at the Exporter Level:** The finding that the effect is entirely on the intensive margin is interesting.
    *   **Specific Suggestion:** Given you're using Comtrade (which allows for exporter-level data, even if you aggregate partners to 'World'), you could investigate whether the FX ban influenced the *extensiveness* of trade relationships, i.e., the number of exporter-product pairs into Nigeria. While you look at product-level extensive margin, it's possible existing exporters reduce quantities (intensive), but new exporters don't enter (extensive margin at exporter-product level). This could be an important nuance.

5.  **Robustness Check: Alternative Time Periods or Control Countries:**
    *   **Specific Suggestion:** Given the policy's duration until late 2023, consider extending the "post" period to include 2023, if data becomes available. Also, try robustness checks using alternative sets of control countries (e.g., Sub-Saharan African countries with flexible exchange rates but no explicit FX bans). The current control countries (Ghana, Côte d'Ivoire, Senegal) are good, but heterogeneity among control countries (e.g., CFA zone vs. flexible exchange rate) could be explored. Perhaps weight control countries by economic size or similarity to Nigeria.

6.  **"Invisible Tariff" & Revenue Argument:** The paper correctly highlights that this "tariff" accrues to intermediaries, not the government.
    *   **Specific Suggestion:** Consider referencing literature on rent-seeking and corruption in dual exchange rate markets. This could deepen the welfare implications beyond just direct deadweight loss, by highlighting unproductive activities aimed at capturing the FX premium.

7.  **Data Appendix Clarity:**
    *   **Specific Suggestion:** In the data appendix, clarify what "Partner is aggregated to 'World' (code 0)" means for the panel construction. Does this mean you sum up all partner imports for each HS6-reporter-year? Or do you treat each reporter-partner-HS6-year as an observation? The current description suggests the latter for the raw data, but the main results are presented at the product and product-country level rather than product-country-partner. Ensure clarity throughout. What about the presence of trans-shipment or illicit trade, especially for banned goods, which might not be captured in official Comtrade data? This is an inherent limitation, but acknowledging it would be good.

8.  **"Product-Level DiD" for Nigeria:** In the DiD, you state "Standard errors are clustered at the HS2 chapter level... yielding 97 clusters." This is a bit confusing since you have 23 treated HS2 chapters and 4,111 control HS6 codes. It seems you are clustering among *all* HS6 codes based on their HS2 chapter, which might dilute the effective number of clusters related to the treatment if the treatment is only targeted at specific HS6 products (or even HS2 chapters). This point ties back to point 2 from Essential Points on clustering.

9.  **Discussion of HS2 vs. HS6 for "Banned":** The text mentions that mapping to HS2 is "conservative" and would "attenuate our estimates toward zero." This is an important assertion.
    *   **Specific Suggestion:** Can you provide a more formal argument or evidence for why misclassification *attenuates* the effect? For instance, if many non-banned products are misclassified as banned, the average effect for the "banned" group would indeed be pulled closer to zero. But if the misclassification is systematic (e.g., only specific subsets within an HS2 chapter are hit), the bias can be in either direction. A brief sensitivity check where you restrict the "banned" HS6 codes to only those with the highest import values *within* the 23 HS2 chapters might be interesting, as it addresses the "large-scale staples" aspect noted in the summary statistics.

---

Overall, this is a strong paper with a compelling narrative and robust identification. Addressing these points, particularly the precision of the treatment definition and the explicit use of the parallel market premium, will substantially enhance its contribution and place it firmly within the top tier of trade policy research.
