## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple, important question: when armed conflict spreads across a poor country, does it raise the price of basic food in local markets, and for which foods? Using market-level price data from Burkina Faso linked to geo-coded conflict events, the paper argues that nearby violence functions like a localized “tax on calories,” especially for domestically traded staples whose supply chains are easiest to disrupt.

Why should a busy economist care? Because this is potentially a clean way to connect conflict to welfare through a concrete market mechanism: not just displacement, not just lost income, but the price of survival itself.

### Does the paper articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Not quite. The current introduction is better than average, but it still reads too much like a competent applied paper and not enough like a paper with a sharp AER-worthy question. It gets bogged down quickly in what the literature has not done and in the estimator, before fully selling why this is a first-order economic question about how conflict distorts markets.

The first two paragraphs should do three things more forcefully:

1. Lead with the core world question, not the data structure.
2. Make the substantive stakes vivid: conflict may raise the effective cost of calories for the poorest households.
3. State the main empirical takeaway in plain English before the methodological details.

### The pitch the paper should have

Here is the version the paper should be selling:

> Armed conflict may hurt households not only by destroying incomes and displacing people, but by making food itself more expensive. This paper asks whether nearby violence raises staple-food prices in local markets, and whether those effects are concentrated in goods whose supply chains depend on vulnerable local transport networks.
>
> Using market-level food prices and geo-coded conflict events from Burkina Faso during the rapid spread of Sahel insurgency, the paper finds that violence close to a market raises staple prices, with larger effects for locally traded cereals than for imported rice. The central message is that conflict operates as a localized market disruption: it does not just destroy output, it also increases the consumer price of calories.

That is a much cleaner front door into the paper.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper’s contribution is to show, using subnational market-level data from Burkina Faso, that nearby conflict raises staple food prices through localized supply-chain disruption, with larger effects for domestically traded cereals than for imported staples.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest papers?

Only partially. The paper tries to differentiate itself along four margins:
- market-level rather than cross-country data,
- within-country spatial variation,
- commodity-level heterogeneity,
- use of modern staggered DiD methods.

Of these, only the first three matter for strategic positioning. The last one is not a contribution in itself for AER. “We use Callaway-Sant’Anna” is not a reason to publish a paper; it is table stakes.

The paper needs to differentiate itself less as “the first market-level Sahel DiD” and more as “evidence that conflict raises the consumer price of calories through a specific market channel.” Right now the contribution is somewhat too design-centric and too region-specific.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a gap in a literature?

It oscillates between the two. The strongest framing is world-facing: does conflict increase the price of food, and through what market mechanism? The weaker framing is literature-gap language: “existing literature is cross-country,” “few studies examine within-country food prices,” etc.

For AER, this must be framed primarily as a question about the world:
- How does insecurity transmit into consumer welfare?
- Does conflict fragment internal markets?
- Are poor households hit not only on earnings but also on local price levels?

That is stronger than “the literature lacks market-level estimates in the Sahel.”

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?

Not fully. Right now a smart economist might say: “It’s a staggered DiD on conflict and food prices in Burkina Faso, with some commodity heterogeneity.” That is not enough. They should instead be able to say: “It shows conflict acts like a localized wedge on staple-food markets, especially for goods dependent on local transport.” That is a real idea.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Several possibilities:

1. **Make calories, not prices, the organizing outcome.**  
   “Food prices” is generic. “The price of calories” is sharper and more welfare-relevant. If the data permit, construct calorie-weighted price indices or the cost of a minimum-calorie basket. That would immediately enlarge the paper.

2. **Lean harder into market integration.**  
   The most interesting big-picture question may be whether conflict segments domestic markets. If the paper can show that nearby conflict widens spatial price dispersion or breaks co-movement across markets, that is a bigger contribution than average price effects alone.

3. **Show substitution patterns across commodities.**  
   If local cereals rise while imported rice is less affected, can households substitute? That would make the welfare story more complete and less mechanical.

4. **Elevate the mechanism from suggestive to central framing.**  
   The commodity comparison is currently presented as mechanism evidence, but it is fairly thin. If the authors can better connect exposure to roads, trader routes, market remoteness, or border/import corridors, the paper’s story becomes much larger.

5. **Reframe from Burkina Faso to conflict-induced market fragmentation.**  
   Burkina Faso is the setting, not the contribution. The contribution should travel.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Before this paper, we know conflict devastates livelihoods, trade, and mobility, but we know less about whether and how it raises the local consumer price of staple foods.

### Tension

Conflict can affect food security through many channels: income loss, displacement, aid, production shocks, transport disruption, trader exit. So the key question is whether there is a distinct price channel, and whether it is a diffuse macro effect or a highly localized market-disruption effect.

### Resolution

The paper finds evidence of positive price effects from nearby violence, especially at short distances and for locally produced cereals relative to imported rice.

### Implications

Conflict may function like a localized wedge in staple-food markets, increasing the effective cost of subsistence for vulnerable households. That matters for how economists think about welfare under conflict and for how policymakers target aid and stabilize supply chains.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the skeleton of one, but not a fully persuasive arc. Right now it feels somewhat like a collection of sensible results:
- average effect,
- commodity heterogeneity,
- alternative radius,
- event study.

The story tying them together is present but underdeveloped.

### What story should it be telling?

The paper should tell a tighter three-step story:

1. **Conflict may not just reduce incomes; it may raise subsistence prices.**
2. **If the mechanism is local market disruption, the effects should be highly localized and strongest for transport-sensitive staples.**
3. **That is exactly the empirical pattern we see.**

That structure would make the 30km result and commodity heterogeneity feel like tests of one coherent mechanism rather than miscellaneous extensions.

One practical note: the current main estimate is modest and imprecise, while the sharpest result is the 30km specification. That creates narrative tension of the wrong kind. The authors should not hide the average effect, but they need to organize the results around the more compelling idea that the relevant economic exposure is local. Otherwise the paper’s “headline” is weak and the interesting finding appears as robustness.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?

I would say: “In Burkina Faso, violence very near a market seems to raise staple food prices by about 6 percent, especially for local cereals rather than imported rice.”

That is the best fact in the paper.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

A subset would lean in—especially conflict, development, and trade/market-integration economists—but the current presentation would not reliably hook the broader room. “Average prices rise 2.2 percent, not statistically significant” is phone-reaching material. “Conflict creates a localized wedge in the price of calories” is more interesting.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Probably one of these:
- Is this really a transport/market-integration story?
- Why is the effect so local?
- Does this show up in the cost of a consumption basket?
- Are households able to substitute toward imported foods?
- Is Burkina Faso special, or is this a general feature of conflict zones?

Those are good questions. The paper should anticipate them and use them to shape the framing.

### If the findings are modest, is that still interesting?

Yes, but only if the paper stops pretending the contribution is the average treatment effect per se. The null-ish overall estimate is not by itself interesting enough for AER. What is interesting is the pattern:
- broad exposure definitions dilute the effect,
- close exposure matters more,
- transport-sensitive staples appear more affected.

That can still be an important result if it is framed as evidence that the relevant economics is highly local market disruption, not nationwide inflation.

Otherwise it risks feeling like a paper that went looking for large effects and found only suggestive ones.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

Bluntly: in its current form, this is not yet an AER paper. It feels like a solid field paper with a plausible result and decent framing, but not yet like a paper that changes how economists think about the economic consequences of conflict.

Mostly a **framing and ambition problem**, with some **scope problem**.

- **Framing problem:** The paper is still written as “a staggered DiD estimate of conflict on food prices in Burkina Faso” rather than “a paper about conflict-induced market fragmentation and the consumer price of subsistence.”
- **Scope problem:** The current outcomes and mechanism evidence are a bit too narrow and suggestive to fully carry the larger claim.
- **Novelty problem:** Subnational conflict shock + local prices is not, by itself, novel enough for AER unless it speaks to a broader conceptual question.
- **Ambition problem:** The paper is competent but safe. It needs one larger idea.

### What is the gap between current form and something that excites the top people in the field?

The top people in this area would want the paper to do at least one of the following:
- redefine the object of interest as the **cost of calories / subsistence basket** rather than generic prices;
- show that conflict **breaks market integration** in a measurable way;
- provide sharper evidence that the mechanism is **transport-network disruption** rather than just “nearby bad things happen”;
- generalize the lesson beyond Burkina Faso into a broader theory of how insecurity creates localized economic wedges.

Right now the paper hints at all of these but fully delivers none.

### Single most impactful piece of advice

**Reframe the paper around conflict-induced market fragmentation—using the 30km and commodity-heterogeneity results as central evidence—and, if possible, elevate the outcome from generic food prices to the cost of calories or a subsistence basket.**

That one change would do the most to convert this from a competent applied paper into a paper with a bigger economic idea.
