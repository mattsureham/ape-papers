### 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper examines how the geographic spread of jihadist insurgency in Burkina Faso (2016–2023) affects local food prices. By matching granular conflict event data with market-level retail prices, the author shows that violence acts as a "tax on calories," specifically increasing the price of bulky, locally produced staples (millet, sorghum) while leaving imported goods (rice) relatively unaffected—a pattern indicative of localized supply-chain destruction.

**Evaluation:** The paper does a decent job in the first two paragraphs, but it leans too heavily on the descriptive "barometer of insecurity" phrasing. It needs to lead with the economic mechanism more aggressively.
*   **The Pitch it should have:** "While we know conflict reduces welfare, we lack evidence on how it distorts internal trade and price formation at the micro-level. This paper exploits the exogenous geographic diffusion of the Sahel insurgency to show that conflict causes a localized 6% spike in food prices, driven by the destruction of transport infrastructure. By showing that this 'conflict tax' falls disproportionately on locally produced staples rather than imports, I isolate supply-chain disruption as a primary channel of conflict-driven welfare loss."

---

### 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution:** The paper provides the first market-level, quasi-experimental evidence of how conflict-induced supply chain disruption creates asymmetric price shocks across different commodity types (tradables vs. non-tradables).

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It differentiates itself from Bazzi & Blattman (2014) by moving from country-level aggregates to market-level geography, and from Aker (2010) by moving from "information" to "physical destruction" as the friction.
*   **World vs. Literature:** It is currently framed more as a gap-filler ("we know surprisingly little about..."). It would be stronger if framed as a question about the **World**: "How does the physical destruction of trade infrastructure in a conflict zone determine which households starve?"
*   **The "Smart Economist" Test:** A reader would likely say, "It's a very clean, modern DiD application that proves conflict makes local food more expensive because it breaks the roads." It avoids the "just another DiD" trap because the commodity-type comparison (rice vs. millet) provides a compelling "internal" placebo/mechanism check.
*   **Bigger Contribution:** The paper needs to quantify the **Welfare** impact. A 6% price increase for a household spending 60% of their income on food is a specific calorie-reduction number. Turning "prices" into "starvation risk" makes this an AER paper.

---

### 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

*   **Closest Neighbors:** Aker (2010) on Sahelian market integration; Dube & Vargas (2013) on commodity shocks; Amodio & Di Maio (2018) on conflict and firm inputs.
*   **Positioning:** It should **synthesize** the "market integration" literature with the "conflict" literature. Currently, it sits in the conflict silo. It should speak more to the trade/development literature on "internal trade costs."
*   **Missing Conversation:** The paper is somewhat silent on the **Urban-Rural** divide. Are prices rising in the cities because the rural areas can't ship out, or are prices rising in the villages because they are cut off? This is a fundamental trade question.

---

### 4. NARRATIVE ARC

*   **Setup:** Stable West African democracy (Burkina Faso) with integrated markets.
*   **Tension:** An external shock (Mali insurgency) spreads like a contagion, but does it just reduce income (demand shock) or break markets (supply shock)?
*   **Resolution:** It breaks the markets. The "tax" is localized (30km) and specific to goods that rely on local trucks and dirt roads.
*   **Implications:** Humanitarian aid shouldn't just be "more money"; it should be "physical protection of corridors" or "subsidizing imports" which are more resilient.

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong. The transition from the 50km null result to the 30km significant result is the "eureka" moment of the paper.

---

### 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

*   **The Fact:** "In Burkina Faso, if a jihadist attack happens within 30km of your market, the price of your dinner goes up 6% immediately, but only if you eat local grain."
*   **Response:** People will lean in. It's a "real world" finding that explains why food security collapses even when harvests might be okay.
*   **Follow-up:** "Does this mean the insurgents are intentionally starving people as a tactic, or is it just collateral damage to the roads?"

---

### 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

*   **Front-loading:** The 30km result (Table 4) is actually the headline result, yet the paper leads with the 50km (insignificant) result. This is a mistake. The 30km "Market Disruption" should be the primary specification.
*   **Appendix:** The Sun-Abraham vs. Callaway-Sant'Anna discussion is important for "econometrics nerds" but distracts from the story. Move the comparison to a robustness section and pick one primary modern estimator for the main text.

---

### 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The gap is **Scope and Ambition**. Right now, it’s a "very good" empirical note. To be an AER paper, it needs to:
1.  **Generalize:** Show that this isn't just a "Burkina Faso story" but a "Sahel story" (perhaps adding Niger or Mali data).
2.  **Welfare:** Use the price elasticities to estimate the actual decline in caloric intake or the increase in poverty headcounts.
3.  **The "Single Tip":** Pivot the paper to lead with the **30km radius** as the main finding and re-frame the entire narrative around the **"Fragility of Internal Trade"** rather than just "Conflict and Prices."
