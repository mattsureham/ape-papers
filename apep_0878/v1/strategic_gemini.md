## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
This paper uses administrative employer-side data (QWI) to test whether state-level EITC supplements—often touted as tools for racial equity—actually narrow the Black-White earnings and employment gaps. It finds a "powered null": these supplements are too small to either move the needle on racial convergence or to trigger the employer wage-capture (incidence) predicted by theory.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this clearly by the third paragraph, but the first two paragraphs are a bit "textbook." They focus on the $70 billion scale of the federal program, which the paper doesn't actually study. 
**The pitch it should have:** "State EITC supplements are primary policy levers for racial equity, yet their impact on racial gaps is theoretically ambiguous: they may encourage work among Black households or allow employers to capture the subsidy through lower wages. Using the first administrative dataset to cross-tabulate race and employer-side outcomes, I show that state-level supplements (5-30% of the federal credit) are effectively 'policy-neutral'—they are too small to trigger either the equity gains promised by proponents or the wage suppression feared by critics."

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**Contribution:** The paper provides the first administrative-data test of EITC racial incidence, finding that state-level supplements have zero effect on racial labor market gaps or pre-tax wages.

- **Differentiation:** It moves beyond the household survey era (Eissa & Liebman) and the race-blind tax data era (Chetty et al.) by utilizing the QWI’s race decomposition.
- **Framing:** It is framed as answering a question about the **WORLD** (does this specific policy tool work for equity?) and a **THEORY** (does Rothstein incidence hold at the margin?).
- **Clarity:** A smart economist would say: "It’s a precisely estimated null showing that state EITCs are too small to matter for market-level racial gaps."
- **How to make it bigger:** To be a "slam dunk" AER paper, the author needs to bridge the gap between their **market-level** null and the **household-level** successes found in other literature. Adding an analysis of "intensity" (e.g., does the null hold even in counties with very high concentrations of EITC-eligible Black households?) would deepen the contribution.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
The paper sits at the intersection of public finance (tax incidence) and labor (racial gaps).
- **Neighbors:** Rothstein (2010) on incidence; Bastian & Jones (2021) on state EITCs; Chetty, Friedman, & Saez (2013) on EITC salience.
- **Positioning:** It currently "challenges" the equity rationale. It should pivot slightly to "delimit" the theory: Rothstein’s mechanism is likely a general equilibrium effect of the massive federal program, not a marginal effect of a 10% state supplement.
- **Missing Literature:** The paper should engage more with the "Occupational Sorting" literature. If the EITC doesn't work because Black and White workers are in different sub-segments of "Retail" that the QWI can't see, that's a vital caveat.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC
- **Setup:** The EITC is a massive anti-poverty tool; states have added supplements to improve equity.
- **Tension:** Theory suggests employers might steal the gains; proponents say it's a ladder for Black workers. We haven't had the data to see who is right.
- **Resolution:** Neither is right at the state level. It's a precisely estimated "nothing."
- **Implications:** State EITCs are useful for income support, but they are not a "structural" labor market intervention.
- **Evaluation:** The arc is strong. It is a "results looking for a story," but the author has found a good one: the "insufficiency of marginal subsidies."

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
At a dinner party: "We've been arguing for a decade about whether employers 'steal' the EITC from workers. It turns out at the state level, the credit is so small that employers don't even bother changing their wage offers, and it doesn't help close the racial gap at all."
- **Reaction:** People will lean in because it simplifies a complex theoretical debate with a "common sense" finding.
- **Follow-up:** "Is it because the credit is too small, or because workers don't know they're getting it until tax season?" (The author discusses this, but could do more).

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
- **Front-loading:** The paper is well-structured.
- **Appendix:** Table 5 (Standardized Effect Sizes) is actually very compelling and helps "sell" the null. This should be moved into the main body or integrated into the results discussion to prove the "powered null" point.
- **Discussion:** Section 6 (Discussion) is the best part of the paper. It should be expanded. The "EU Pay Transparency" connection in the conclusion feels tacked on and should be cut.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The gap is **ambition**. Currently, it feels like a very high-quality *Journal of Public Economics* paper. To make it AER, the author needs to address the "Why" more aggressively. 
**Single most impactful advice:** Perform a "Heterogeneity by Salience" check. Do these nulls persist in states where the EITC is highly publicized vs. not? If the null is driven by a lack of worker/employer awareness, it’s a story about information. If the null persists even when people know about the credit, it’s a story about the structural power of the racial wage gap. That distinction is the difference between a "good" paper and a "great" one.
