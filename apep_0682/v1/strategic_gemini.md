## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper uses the staggered rollout of sewage monitors across 14,000 English storm overflows to isolate the effect of *information* about pollution from the pollution itself. While the average effect of revealing this data on house prices is a precise zero, prices fell by nearly 5% in the Thames Water region, where the data was amplified by intense media scrutiny and a high-profile corporate crisis. It argues that environmental information only capitalizes into markets when an "attention intermediary" (the media) transforms raw data into salient news.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this pitch remarkably well in the first three paragraphs. It successfully pivots from a narrow "pollution study" to a broader "economics of information" paper. 

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution:** The paper demonstrates that mandatory environmental disclosure is insufficient for market capitalization unless accompanied by high media salience and public attention.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It differentiates itself well from Keiser & Shapiro (2019) by isolating the info channel from the physical pollution channel.
*   **Question vs. Literature:** It frames itself around a question about the world (Do markets respond to what is *happening* or what is *known*?), which is its strongest asset.
*   **Explainability:** A smart economist would say: "It's a paper about the 'last mile' of information disclosure—data doesn't matter if no one is looking at it." 
*   **What would make it bigger?** To be a "slam dunk" AER paper, it needs to move beyond the single-WaSC (Thames Water) comparison. The contribution would be much bigger if the author successfully implemented the suggested instrument (Eisensee-Strömberg) to prove the media mechanism across all regions, rather than relying on a London-centric indicator variable that might capture other "Thames Water" shocks (like their debt crisis).

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

*   **Closest Neighbors:** Keiser & Shapiro (2019) on water quality; Greenstone et al. (2006) on mandated disclosure; Mastromonaco (2015) on TRI capitalization; and Eisensee & Strömberg (2007) on media/news.
*   **Strategy:** It currently builds on the environmental hedonic literature but "attacks" the assumption that disclosure implies awareness. It should lean harder into the **Attention Economics** literature (e.g., Gabaix, 2019; Sims, 2003) to broaden its appeal beyond environmental specialists.
*   **Conversation:** It is having the right conversation, but it needs to be careful not to be seen as a "UK-only" paper. It should frame the UK sewage crisis as a generalizable case study for "Information Overload" vs. "Salience."

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

*   **Setup:** Residents assume rivers are clean; regulators quietly start monitoring sewage.
*   **Tension:** The data is public on a government website, but does anyone care? Does "dirty water" matter if it's "quietly dirty"?
*   **Resolution:** On average, no. But when the media creates a firestorm (Thames Water), the market reacts sharply.
*   **Implications:** Benefit-cost analyses of environmental regulations are fundamentally flawed if they ignore the "salience" of the improvement.

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong. It avoids being a "collection of results" by leading with the null and using the heterogeneity to provide the "Aha!" moment.

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

*   **The Lead Fact:** "Telling people there is sewage in their backyard doesn't change house prices unless the BBC writes about it."
*   **Reaction:** People will lean in. It touches on the housing market (universal interest) and the "outrage economy."
*   **Follow-up:** "How do we know it was the media and not just that Thames Water is going bankrupt?" This is the paper's Achilles' heel.

---

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

*   **Postcode District vs. Property Level:** The author admits the 2,900 postcode district level is coarse. For the AER, we usually expect the "frontier" of data. Moving to property-level "buffers" (e.g., houses within 500m of an overflow vs. 2km) is likely necessary for the final version to be taken seriously as a top-tier hedonic study.
*   **Section 5.1:** Move the TWFE results (Column 1-2, Table 3) to the appendix or a very brief mention. We know they are biased; don't waste the reader's time on them in the main text. Lead with the C&S null.

---

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The gap is **identification of the mechanism**. The Thames Water result is a "smoking gun," but it’s a single observation at the company level. 

**Single most impactful piece of advice:** Implement the media salience instrument mentioned in the "Limitations" section. If the author can show that house prices in *any* district (not just Thames) drop only when local news coverage of sewage spikes—specifically using "crowding out" by national news as an instrument for coverage—this moves from a "suggestive case study" to a "definitive proof of the attention channel."
