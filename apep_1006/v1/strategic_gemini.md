## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
The paper investigates whether international "grey-listing" by the Financial Action Task Force (FATF)—a move widely feared to trigger financial "de-risking"—actually increases the costs for migrants sending money home. Using a 14-year panel of remittance prices and modern staggered DiD methods, the author finds a precise zero effect, suggesting that the "retail" price of money transfers is remarkably resilient to the "wholesale" regulatory shocks that hit correspondent banking.

**Evaluation:** The paper does a decent job in the first two paragraphs, but it leans too heavily on the "de-risking" jargon. The pitch it *should* have is:
> "Every year, a billion people rely on $650 billion in remittances, yet they lose 6.4% of that value to transaction costs—a 'tax' often blamed on stringent global anti-money laundering (AML) regulations. This paper provides the first corridor-level test of whether being labeled a high-risk jurisdiction by the FATF actually raises prices for the poor. I find that it does not: while global banks may retreat, the rise of specialized money transfer operators and mobile money has created a retail layer that is functionally immune to sovereign financial stigma."

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**One-sentence contribution:** The paper provides the first empirical evidence that FATF grey-listing has no impact on the retail price or availability of remittances, challenging the dominant policy narrative that AML enforcement harms financial inclusion.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It is well-differentiated from Collin et al. (2016) and Kida/Paetzold (2021) by moving from "wholesale" volumes (SWIFT/Capital flows) to "retail" prices (World Bank RPW).
*   **World vs. Literature:** It frames itself as answering a question about the **world** (does this regulation hurt migrants?), which is a strength.
*   **Clarity:** A smart economist would say: "It's the paper that proves grey-listing doesn't actually raise the price of sending money home."
*   **Making it bigger:** To truly reach AER heights, the paper needs more than just a null; it needs to prove the **substitution**. If banks exit, who enters? The "Mechanism" section (Section 6) is currently speculative. The contribution becomes "AER-sized" if the author can empirically show the MTO/Mobile money volume increasing as bank volume decreases within the same corridor.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
The paper sits at the intersection of **Development Finance** and **Banking Regulation**.

*   **Closest Neighbors:** Collin et al. (2016) on de-risking; Beck et al. (2018) on remittance competition; Callaway & Sant’Anna (2021) on methods.
*   **Positioning:** It should "build on" the de-risking literature but "attack" the policy conclusion. It needs to synthesize the "death of correspondent banking" with the "rise of fintech."
*   **Missing Conversations:** The paper is somewhat silent on the **Industrial Organization of Payments**. It treats the "null" as a surprise, but it might be an expected outcome of a market shifting from bank-intermediated credit to pre-funded fintech rails. Speaking to the "future of payments" literature would broaden the audience.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC
*   **Setup:** Global remittances are vital but expensive; the UN wants costs down.
*   **Tension:** Policy experts claim FATF "grey-listing" makes things worse by scaring away banks (de-risking).
*   **Resolution:** Actually, it doesn't. The prices don't budge.
*   **Implications:** AML reform advocates might be barking up the wrong tree if their goal is to lower costs for the poor; the retail market has already "de-coupled" from the sovereign's reputation.

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong. It is a "myth-busting" story. However, it currently feels like a "result looking for a story" in the discussion section. The author needs to lead with the *why* (market decoupling) rather than making it a post-script.

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
At a dinner party, you lead with: *"We've been told for a decade that AML lists hurt the poor by raising remittance costs. It turns out, they don't. The market has basically built a bypass around the global banking system."*

*   **Reaction:** People lean in. It's a "counter-intuitive null."
*   **Follow-up:** *"Wait, so if it doesn't raise costs, why are countries so afraid of being grey-listed?"* This highlights the paper's limit: it only looks at remittances. The author needs to be clearer that grey-listing still sucks for trade finance and FDI, even if it's fine for remittances.

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
*   **Front-loading:** The "Mechanisms" in Section 6 are the most interesting part of the paper. They should be moved up or integrated into the results.
*   **Appendix:** The standardized effect sizes (Table 5) are excellent for proving the null isn't just a "noisy zero." These should be in the main text to satisfy the "precise null" requirement of top journals.
*   **Scope:** The "Scope limitation" in the Appendix (page 16) is a red flag. The author admits they wanted to use BIS data but didn't. For the AER, they might *need* that BIS data to show the "wholesale" side actually broke while the "retail" side stayed fixed.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The gap is **Ambition**. A precise null on a policy-relevant variable is a *Journal of Development Economics* or *REStat* paper. To make it an *AER* paper, it needs to provide a **General Equilibrium-style insight** about the modern financial architecture.

**The single most impactful piece of advice:**
Don't just report the null; prove the **Structural Decoupling**. Use the corridor-level data to show that the market share of banks drops while MTOs/Mobile money grows following a listing, and show that this "fintech buffer" is exactly why the aggregate price doesn't move. Turn a "failed prediction" into a "new theory of financial resilience."
