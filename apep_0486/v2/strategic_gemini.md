## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
This paper evaluates the impact of "progressive" District Attorneys on mass incarceration and racial equity. Using a staggered difference-in-differences design across 25 U.S. counties, it finds that while these DAs successfully reduce overall jail populations by 20–25% without a detectable spike in homicides, they paradoxically widen racial disparities because the benefits of reform accrue more rapidly to White defendants than Black ones.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this pitch exceptionally well. The first paragraph uses a vivid example (marijuana prosecution) to ground the "equity paradox" in economic logic. It moves immediately from the anecdotal to the systemic, clearly defining the stakes (decarceration vs. racial justice) in the second paragraph. This is exactly how an AER introduction should start.

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**Contribution:** The paper identifies a structural "universalism paradox" where race-neutral prosecutorial reforms widen racial incarceration gaps because they operate on the "wrong margin"—low-level offenses where White defendants are disproportionately represented relative to the serious offenses that drive Black incarceration.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It differentiates itself from *Agan et al. (2023)* and *Petersen et al. (2024)* by moving beyond just "does it work?" to "for whom and why?" The use of race-specific event studies and the formal DDD decomposition of the "ratio" vs. "levels" effect is a high-value addition.
*   **Question vs. Gap:** It frames the contribution as answering a question about the *world*—specifically, why well-intentioned equity reforms fail to close gaps. This is a much stronger positioning than merely "filling a gap in the DA literature."
*   **What would make it bigger?** To reach the absolute top tier, the paper needs to move from *inferring* the mechanism to *showing* it. The author admits they lack case-level data. Linking the county-level results to even a subset of jurisdictions with micro-data on charge-level declinations by race would turn this from a "provocative finding" into an "irrefutable proof."

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
The paper sits at the intersection of Political Economy (elections of local officials), Labor/Public (racial disparities), and the Economics of Crime.

*   **Closest Neighbors:** Agan et al. (2023) on misdemeanors; Ouss and Stevenson (2022) on discretion; Neal and Rick (2014) on the long-run Black-White prison gap.
*   **Strategy:** It builds on the "New DiD" literature (Callaway-Sant'Anna) to correct previous descriptive work. It *synthesizes* the criminology literature with a rigorous econometric framework that the AER audience demands.
*   **Missing Conversations:** The paper could benefit from connecting to the broader public economics literature on "Targeting." This is essentially a paper about the failure of "Universalism" in the presence of "Screening" or "Pre-existing Stratification." Connecting to the literature on universal vs. targeted social transfers would elevate the paper's theoretical weight.

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC
*   **Setup:** Progressive DAs are elected to fix a broken, racially biased system.
*   **Tension:** They succeed in decarcerating (the easy part), but the racial gap—their primary moral mandate—actually worsens.
*   **Resolution:** The paradox is structural. DAs only control the "front door" of the office. If the "inbox" (police arrests) is already stratified, neutral exit rules favor the least-involved populations.
*   **Implications:** True equity requires "race-conscious" policy or "upstream" police reform, both of which are politically and legally more difficult than DA declination.

**Evaluation:** The narrative arc is excellent. It follows a "Puzzle-Mechanism-Implication" structure that is very satisfying to read. It isn't just a collection of coefficients; it’s a story about the limits of institutional power.

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
*   **The Fact:** "Progressive DAs actually *increased* the Black-to-White jail ratio by 3 points."
*   **Reaction:** People lean in. This is a counter-intuitive finding that challenges the "common sense" of a major political movement.
*   **Follow-up:** "Is it because Black people are committing more serious crimes, or because the DAs are only helping the 'low-hanging fruit' (White misdemeanors)?" This leads directly into the paper's mechanism discussion.

---

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
*   **Homicide Section:** This is the weakest part. The data is too thin, and the author is almost too honest about its limitations. It should be shortened significantly or moved to a "Public Safety" subsection within the results. Don't give it equal billing to the jail results if the power isn't there.
*   **Front-loading:** The "Theory of the Paradox" (Section 2.3) is brilliant and should perhaps come even earlier, or be more tightly integrated into the introduction to set the stage for the empirical test.
*   **Appendix:** Move some of the "25 DA" descriptive details to the appendix to keep the main text focused on the aggregate estimation.

---

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
**The Gap:** The paper is currently a very high-quality "Policy Evaluation." To be a definitive "AER Paper," it needs more **Theoretical Ambition**.

**The Single Most Impactful Advice:** Shift the framing from "Progressive DAs" to a broader economic theory of **"The Universalism Paradox in Stratified Systems."** If the author can show that this isn't just about jails—that the same math applies to school choice, healthcare access, or tax credits—the paper becomes a general-interest piece that every economist must cite.
