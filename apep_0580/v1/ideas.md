# Research Ideas

## Idea 1: Follow the Money or Follow the Crime? Police Resource Reallocation After Civil Asset Forfeiture Reform
**Policy:** Civil asset forfeiture allows law enforcement to seize property suspected of involvement in crime without requiring conviction. Since 2014, 37 US states + DC have reformed these laws with heterogeneous intensity: 4 states abolished civil forfeiture entirely (New Mexico 2015, Nebraska 2016, North Carolina longstanding, Maine 2024), 16 states now require criminal conviction, 16 states raised burden of proof, and 8 states + DC closed the federal "equitable sharing" loophole. Multi-dimensional treatment: abolish vs. restrict vs. transparency-only. 13 never-reformed states serve as controls.
**Outcome:** FBI UCR Arrests by Age, Sex, and Race (Jacob Kaplan concatenated files, ICPSR Project 102263, 1974-2020). Agency-level annual arrest counts by offense type: drug arrests vs. property crime arrests vs. violent crime arrests. Secondary: FBI UCR Offenses Known and Clearances by Arrest for clearance rates by crime type.
**Identification:** Callaway and Sant'Anna (2021) staggered DiD. Treatment groups defined by reform year and intensity (4-point scale: no reform, reporting only, conviction requirement, full abolition). The causal channel: forfeiture creates direct financial incentives for drug enforcement — seized assets fund police budgets. Reform removes this incentive. Predicted: (a) decline in drug arrest shares, (b) reallocation toward property/violent crime enforcement, (c) heterogeneity by pre-reform forfeiture reliance. This is a revealed-preference test of police incentive responsiveness.
**Why it's novel:** Kantor, Kitchens & Pawlowski (2021, Economic Inquiry) showed the 1984 federal expansion increased drug arrests by ~37%. No published paper tests the reverse: does removing forfeiture incentives cause police to reallocate away from drug enforcement? McDonald et al. (2026) studied New Mexico only (single state). The multi-state substitution/reallocation question is completely open. Zero APEP papers on asset forfeiture, police resource allocation, or law enforcement incentives.
**Feasibility check:** Confirmed: 37 reformed states with staggered timing 2014-2024 and 13 never-reformed controls. Kaplan UCR files publicly available on openICPSR (1974-2020 clean before NIBRS transition). Institute for Justice Policing for Profit database provides reform dates and forfeiture revenue data. No competing multi-state reallocation study exists.

## Idea 2: Does Legal Sports Betting Cannibalize State Lottery Revenue? Staggered Legalization and Fiscal Substitution
**Policy:** Following the 2018 PASPA Supreme Court decision, ~38 US states legalized sports betting in staggered waves (2018-2025). The substitution question: does legal sports betting cannibalize state lottery revenue (which funds education in many states), or expand the total gambling market?
**Outcome:** State lottery revenue data from NASPL annual reports and Census Bureau state government finances. Sports betting handle/revenue from state gaming commissions. All publicly accessible.
**Identification:** CS-DiD with staggered state adoption. ~38 treated states, ~12 never-treated controls. Built-in dose-response: retail-only vs. online sports betting creates intensity variation. Pre-periods back to 2010 (8+ years).
**Why it's novel:** Fragmented single-state studies exist (Pennsylvania, West Virginia) but no comprehensive multi-state panel on the lottery cannibalization question with modern CS-DiD. First-order fiscal stakes: state lottery revenue funds education.
**Feasibility check:** Data is public but requires manual assembly from state reports. ~38 treated units far exceeds minimum. The consumer finance angle is partially claimed by Hollenbeck et al. (SSRN 2024).

## Idea 3: Can Shorter Workweeks Save Fertility? South Korea's 52-Hour Reform and the Marriage-Birth Response
**Policy:** South Korea's 2018 Amendment to the Labor Standards Act reduced maximum weekly working hours from 68 to 52, implemented in three staggered waves by firm size: 300+ employees (July 2018), 50-299 (January 2020), 5-49 (July 2021). Korea has the world's lowest fertility rate (TFR=0.72 in 2023).
**Outcome:** KOSIS vital statistics (province-level monthly marriages and births via API with `kosis` R package). KLIPS panel data for individual-level analysis (firm size, hours, marriage/fertility).
**Identification:** Multi-cutoff staggered DiD. Three firm-size waves create built-in placebo tests. Province-level industrial composition provides continuous treatment intensity. Individual-level: workers at firms 300+ (treated July 2018) vs. firms 50-299 (not yet treated until Jan 2020).
**Why it's novel:** No causal study links the 52-hour reform to marriage/fertility outcomes. Zero APEP papers from East Asia. First-order policy stakes ($280 billion in Korean pro-natalist spending since 2006).
**Feasibility check:** KOSIS API confirmed accessible. But COVID confounds waves 2-3 (Jan 2020 onwards), limiting clean identification to wave 1 only (firms 300+, July 2018-Dec 2019). Narrow birth observation window (~9 months). Marriage is the more responsive outcome.
