## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
The paper asks whether "competing news"—shocks to the global news cycle—affects democratic participation. By exploiting the linguistic divide in Switzerland, the author shows that earthquakes occurring near language-specific "interest regions" (e.g., Turkey for German-speakers, Francophone Africa for French-speakers) crowd out referendum coverage, raising the information cost of voting and significantly depressing turnout. It suggests that even in a stable, high-information democracy, the "random walk" of global events can undermine the deliberative process of direct democracy.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this well, though it spends a bit too much time on the Turkey example in paragraph one. It should pivot faster to the generalizable theory of **information costs in direct democracy**.

**The Pitch the Paper Should Have:**  
"Does the global news cycle distract voters from local democratic choices? This paper identifies the causal effect of 'competing news' on voter turnout by exploiting exogenous seismic activity and the linguistically segmented media markets of Switzerland. I find that when global earthquakes crowd out referendum-related news, municipal turnout drops by 3.3 percentage points, particularly for low-salience ballot items where information costs are highest."

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**Contribution:** The paper demonstrates that exogenous shocks to the media environment increase the information costs of voting, thereby reducing participation in policy-setting referendums.

**Evaluation:**
- **Differentiation:** It is well-differentiated. While Eisensee & Strömberg (2007) look at policy *response* (relief), this looks at voter *participation*. It moves the "competing news" literature from candidate-based elections to policy-based direct democracy.
- **World vs. Literature:** It frames itself as answering a question about the world (how random events affect democracy), which is a strength.
- **Clarity:** A smart economist would get it immediately. It’s "Eisensee & Strömberg meets Swiss Direct Democracy."
- **Bigger Contribution:** To make this "AER big," the paper needs to prove the **"so what" for policy outcomes**. Does the 3.3% drop in turnout change which laws pass? The author hints at "status-quo bias" in results, but this needs to be the headline, not a secondary result.

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
The paper sits at the intersection of Political Economy and Media Economics.

- **Neighbors:** Eisensee & Strömberg (2007) on competing news; Strömberg (2004) on media and turnout; Funk (2010) on Swiss turnout; Matějka & McKay (2015) on rational inattention.
- **Positioning:** It builds on these effectively. It should "attack" the assumption that voters in direct democracies are fully informed or that turnout is merely a function of policy preferences.
- **Missing Conversations:** The paper should speak more to the **Rational Inattention** literature in macro/theory. If information is a scarce resource, how do global shocks reallocate that resource away from civic duties?

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC
- **Setup:** Swiss voters decide major laws via referendums, aided by a robust media environment.
- **Tension:** Global disasters are "attention shocks." When a disaster hits a region relevant to one language group but not the other, it creates a wedge in the information costs faced by two sets of voters on the *same* ballot.
- **Resolution:** Turnout drops significantly when the "seismic distraction" is high, especially for complex or "boring" items.
- **Implications:** Referendum outcomes are not just reflections of the "will of the people" but are sensitive to the timing of the global news cycle.

**Evaluation:** The narrative is strong. It is not just a "collection of results." The "language-swap placebo" is the narrative climax—it proves the story isn't about "mood" but about the *media market*.

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
At a dinner party, I would lead with: *"Did you know that an earthquake in Turkey can prevent a law from passing in Zurich just because it distracted the voters?"*

Economists will lean in. The follow-up question will be: **"Did it actually change the result of any laws?"** This is where the paper currently feels "medium" rather than "close" to AER. If the author can show a specific law that failed/passed because of a well-timed earthquake, it’s a slam dunk.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
