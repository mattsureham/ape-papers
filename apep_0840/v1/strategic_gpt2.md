## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether random foreign disasters can reduce democratic participation by crowding out political information. Using Switzerland’s segmented French- and German-language media markets, it argues that earthquakes abroad receive different levels of attention across language regions, and that municipalities exposed to more earthquake-salient media environments turn out less in federal referendums, especially on low-salience ballot items.

A busy economist should care because the paper is trying to say something bigger than “media matters”: the effective information set facing voters is partly determined by unrelated global events, which means participation in direct democracy may be more contingent and fragile than we usually assume.

### Does the paper articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Reasonably well, but not optimally. The opening anecdote is vivid, yet the paper slightly buries the punchline by moving too quickly into Swiss institutional detail and the mechanics of language-specific salience. The first two paragraphs should more sharply foreground the broad question: **Can unrelated breaking news suppress democratic participation by displacing information about the vote itself?** Right now, the introduction is more “here is my setting” than “here is the surprising fact about the world.”

### The pitch the paper should have

“Democratic participation depends not just on preferences or institutions, but on what else is competing for citizens’ attention. This paper shows that random foreign earthquakes reduce turnout in Swiss referendums when they crowd out political information in language-specific media markets. Because the same referendum is held nationwide but French- and German-speaking voters consume different news, global disasters generate plausibly exogenous differences in attention that reveal a broader fact: even well-functioning direct democracy is vulnerable to unrelated shocks in the information environment.”

That is the pitch. World first, setting second.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper claims to show that exogenous shocks to competing news from foreign earthquakes depress referendum turnout by crowding out vote-relevant information, with stronger effects for low-salience ballot items.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?

Only partly. The paper names Eisensee and Strömberg, some media-and-politics papers, and a few Swiss referendum papers, but the differentiation is still a bit thin. Right now the contribution risks sounding like:

- “another media attention paper,” or
- “another turnout paper in Switzerland,” or
- “an application of competing-news logic to a new context.”

That may be true, but for AER the paper needs to be more explicit about **why this setting generates a qualitatively new insight**, not just a new estimate.

The strongest differentiation is:
1. same national vote, same day, same institutions;
2. different media environments because of language segmentation;
3. effect concentrated where voter information needs are greatest.

That triangulation is what separates it from a generic media/distraction paper. The paper should lean much harder on that.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or as filling a gap in a literature?

It starts with a world question, which is good, but then quickly defaults to literature-gap language: “extends competing-news framework,” “contributes to three literatures,” “no prior work has applied…” That is not fatal, but it is weaker than it should be.

The stronger framing is not “no one has studied earthquakes and referendums.” It is:  
**How much does democratic participation depend on the accidental allocation of attention in the news cycle?**

That is a first-order question about the world.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the intro?

A smart economist could probably say: “It’s a paper showing that unrelated foreign disasters reduce Swiss referendum turnout through language-specific media crowding.” That is decent. But just as easily they might say: “It’s another media attention paper using quirky quasi-random shocks.”

That is the danger. The paper needs to make the reader remember the finding as a substantive claim about democracy, not as a clever reduced-form design.

### What would make the contribution bigger?

Most importantly: **show the media channel directly.** Right now the contribution is one step short of its natural ambition. Without direct evidence on coverage displacement, the paper is effectively about a salience proxy correlated with turnout. The story is media crowd-out, but the data stop short of proving that story. For top-tier positioning, that gap matters a lot.

Other ways to make it bigger:

- **Outcome expansion:** not just turnout, but information acquisition, campaign coverage, search behavior, or ballot knowledge.
- **Mechanism sharpening:** demonstrate that referendum coverage actually falls when earthquake salience rises, ideally by language market.
- **Comparative framing:** connect to candidate elections or other direct-democracy settings to show the phenomenon is general, not Swiss-specific.
- **Substantive stakes:** show whether outcomes meaningfully change, not just participation. Turnout effects alone are interesting; turnout plus policy consequences is much bigger.
- **Voter heterogeneity:** effects among low-information voters, close votes, or issues requiring more deliberation would enlarge the paper’s conceptual reach.

If they could move from “earthquake salience predicts turnout” to “earthquakes displace referendum coverage, depress attention, reduce turnout, and occasionally alter outcomes,” the paper becomes much more AER-shaped.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Voters need information to participate meaningfully in direct democracy. Media help provide that information. But media attention is scarce and can be diverted by major external events.

### Tension

If the news cycle is crowded by unrelated breaking events, does participation in democratic decision-making fall? This is hard to test because media coverage is endogenous and most elections do not provide clean within-event variation in competing news exposure.

### Resolution

Using Switzerland’s language-segmented media markets, the paper finds that foreign earthquakes that are more salient to one language market reduce turnout in municipalities tied to that market, with larger effects on low-salience ballot items.

### Implications

Democratic participation may be shaped by random attention shocks rather than only political fundamentals. That matters for how we think about media environments, voter information, and the robustness of referendum institutions.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

Yes, more than most papers of this type. There is a real story here. But it is still a bit fragile because the narrative promise is stronger than the evidence directly shown in the paper. The arc currently runs:

1. Earthquakes generate salience.
2. Salience differs by media language.
3. Turnout falls.
4. Therefore media crowd-out raised information costs.

The jump from 2 and 3 to 4 is still inferential rather than demonstrated. That makes the narrative feel slightly like a well-organized collection of suggestive results rather than a fully locked story.

### If it is a collection of results looking for a story, what story should it be telling?

The story should be:

**Attention is an input into democracy. Random shocks to the news agenda can reallocate that input away from politics, reducing participation where voters most need information.**

That is cleaner and more general than “earthquakes reduce turnout.” Earthquakes are the design; attention is the object.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?

“I have a paper suggesting that when a major earthquake abroad dominates one Swiss language market’s news, turnout in that region’s referendum voting falls by about three percentage points relative to the other language region voting on the exact same national ballot.”

That is a pretty good dinner-party fact. It is surprising and intuitive.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

They would lean in initially. The idea is clever and memorable. But the very next question will come fast.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

“Do you actually show that referendum coverage fell in those media markets?”

That is the core issue. If the answer is no, then the paper’s current version will lose some altitude in the room. The next questions would be:
- Could this be diaspora sentiment or emotional response rather than media crowd-out?
- How Swiss-specific is this?
- Do outcomes change, or just turnout?

### If the findings are modest, is the modesty itself interesting?

The turnout effect is not modest in magnitude; if true, it is substantial. The problem is not effect size. The problem is interpretive confidence and external significance. A 3.3 pp turnout effect is large enough to matter. The paper does not need to apologize for modesty; it needs to convince readers the mechanism is real and the finding generalizes beyond a clever Swiss exercise.
