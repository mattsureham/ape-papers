## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple, important question: when the federal government retreats from environmental enforcement, do states step in, or does pollution rise? Using the 2017–2020 contraction in EPA enforcement capacity and cross-region differences in historical reliance on federal inspections, the paper argues that areas more dependent on EPA enforcement saw worse ambient air quality, suggesting that cooperative federalism may break down when the federal partner pulls back.

Why should a busy economist care? Because this is not really a paper about PM2.5 alone; it is a paper about whether decentralized governance can substitute for lost central state capacity. That question travels far beyond environmental economics—to regulation, federalism, bureaucratic capacity, and public economics more broadly.

### Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Mostly, but not sharply enough. The opening is competent and the core question appears immediately, which is good. But the introduction slips too quickly into design language (“natural experiment,” “shift-share”) before fully establishing the bigger question. The best version of this paper is not “a shift-share estimate of EPA staffing decline on PM2.5”; it is “an empirical test of whether cooperative federalism actually provides functional substitution when federal enforcement capacity collapses.”

The first two paragraphs should make the stakes more general and more vivid. Right now the paper knows its result, but it has not fully decided what argument about the world it wants to lead with.

### The pitch the paper should have

American regulation often relies on cooperative federalism: Washington sets the rules, but enforcement is shared with states. That system only works if state agencies can replace federal capacity when the center retreats. This paper tests that premise using the sharp decline in EPA enforcement staffing after 2016 and shows that places historically more reliant on federal enforcement experienced worse air quality, suggesting that federal and state enforcement are not close substitutes in practice.

That is the AER-relevant pitch. Not “I study PM2.5.” Not “I use a shift-share design.” The pitch is: **cooperative federalism may be more fragile than economists and policymakers assume.**

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper provides evidence that reductions in federal environmental enforcement capacity are not fully offset by states, implying limited substitutability between federal and state enforcement under cooperative federalism.

What would make the differentiation clearer is to define the object of interest more precisely:

- Existing work: what is the effect of being inspected or punished?
- This paper: what is the effect of removing one layer of the enforcement hierarchy in a shared-governance system?

That is cleaner and bigger. The novelty is not only “agency production function”; it is **substitution across levels of government when one regulator weakens**.

It is split between the two. The strongest parts ask a world question: if the federal regulator retreats, do states replace it? The weaker parts lapse into literature-gap language: “this contributes to three literatures…” That is standard but not persuasive at the AER level.

The paper should lean much harder into the world question. “Can decentralized enforcement systems absorb shocks to central capacity?” is a question about institutional design, not a gap paragraph.

### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what's new?

At present, maybe. But too many would summarize it as: “It’s another reduced-form paper on environmental regulation using a federal staffing shock.” That is the danger.

The author needs the introduction to make the novelty inescapable: this is a paper about **whether cooperative federalism is resilient**. If a reader comes away with that sentence, the paper has done its job. If they come away with “DiD on EPA and pollution,” it has not.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Specific ways to enlarge the contribution:

1. **Reframe the main outcome as a test of governance substitution, not just air pollution.**  
   PM2.5 is acceptable, but it feels one step removed from enforcement. A bigger contribution would connect the staffing decline to a chain:
   - federal withdrawal,
   - state response or non-response,
   - regulated behavior,
   - ambient outcomes.

2. **Show whether substitution failure is stronger where state capacity is weaker.**

3. **Distinguish where federal enforcement matters most.**

5. **Lean into heterogeneity rather than average effects.**  
   The biggest version of this paper is not “EPA staffing matters.” It is “federal enforcement especially matters where states are least able or willing to replace it.”

3. **Unexpected shocks to government capacity.**

Not quite yet. The current conversation is too much: “environmental enforcement paper with a federalism angle.” The better conversation is: **what happens when a layered regulatory state loses capacity at one level?**

That framing gives the paper a larger audience:

That is the conversation an AER paper needs.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

The world before this paper: environmental regulation in the U.S. is organized through cooperative federalism. The standard presumption is that shared enforcement offers flexibility and redundancy: if one level weakens, the other can at least partially cover.

### Tension

But that presumption is not actually well established. Federal and state enforcement may not be close substitutes because they differ in resources, incentives, political constraints, and jurisdictional reach. The 2017–2020 EPA staffing decline creates a real-world stress test of whether the system has true redundancy or only nominal redundancy.

### Resolution

The paper’s evidence suggests that areas historically more reliant on federal enforcement saw worse air quality after federal enforcement capacity shrank. The intended interpretation is that states did not fully replace the missing federal role.

### Implications

The implication is larger than environmental policy: multi-level governance arrangements may be less robust than they appear. If federal withdrawal cannot be replaced by state action, then decentralization does not merely allocate tasks—it creates dependence on the continued strength of the center.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the ingredients, but the arc is only partially realized. The paper currently reads like:
- institutional background,
- empirical design,
- results,
- caveats.

That is fine as a workshop paper. It is not yet the clean narrative structure of a top general-interest paper.

The missing element is **tension with received wisdom**. The paper should more explicitly say: cooperative federalism is often defended partly on redundancy and local adaptability grounds; here is a rare direct test of whether that redundancy actually exists. Without that, the paper is a collection of plausible results around an interesting episode.

### What story should it be telling?

It should tell this story:

1. Modern regulation often relies on shared enforcement across levels of government.
2. Shared enforcement sounds resilient, but that resilience depends on substitutability.
3. We almost never observe a sharp contraction in one layer that lets us test substitutability directly.
4. The EPA contraction offers that test.
5. The evidence suggests the system was not resilient: federal retreat was not offset locally.
6. Therefore, the design of cooperative federalism may need to account for complementarity, not substitution.

That is a real narrative. Right now the paper gestures toward it but does not fully organize itself around it.
