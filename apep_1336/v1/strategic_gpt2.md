## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple, important question: when the federal government retreats from environmental enforcement, do state regulators replace that lost capacity, or does pollution rise? Using the 2017–2020 decline in EPA enforcement staffing and cross-region differences in historical reliance on federal inspections, the paper argues that places more dependent on the EPA saw worse air quality, suggesting that cooperative federalism may break down when one layer of government withdraws.

A busy economist should care because this is not really just a paper about PM2.5; it is a paper about whether decentralized governance systems are resilient when the center steps back. That question travels well beyond environmental economics to regulation, state capacity, public finance, and political economy.

### Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Reasonably well, but not sharply enough. The current opening has the right ingredients, but it still reads like an environmental enforcement paper rather than a broader paper about the fragility of cooperative federalism. The big idea is stronger than the intro makes it sound.

The first two paragraphs should more explicitly say:

1. Cooperative federalism only works if state and federal enforcement are substitutable at the margin.  
2. The Trump-era contraction of EPA enforcement provides a test of that proposition.  
3. The object of interest is institutional substitutability, with air pollution as the revealed outcome.  

### The pitch the paper should have

“American regulation often relies on cooperative federalism: Washington sets rules, but states and the federal government jointly enforce them. This arrangement is viable only if states can replace federal capacity when the center retreats. This paper tests that premise using the sharp 2017–2020 decline in EPA enforcement staffing and asks whether places historically more reliant on federal inspections experienced worse environmental outcomes when that federal capacity disappeared.

I find that counties in regions more dependent on EPA enforcement experienced larger increases in PM2.5 during the federal pullback. The central message is that regulatory authority may be devolved on paper but not in practice: when federal enforcement weakens, states do not fully fill the gap.”

That is the version that belongs in AER territory. The current version is close, but still undersells the generality.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### Contribution in one sentence

The paper’s contribution is to argue that reductions in federal enforcement capacity can worsen environmental outcomes because state regulators do not fully substitute for federal withdrawal under cooperative federalism.

The sharper contrast is:

- Existing environmental enforcement papers ask whether inspections and penalties deter pollution.
- This paper asks whether the enforcement *system* is robust to the loss of one governmental layer.
- Existing fiscal federalism papers debate when decentralization is efficient.
- This paper tests whether decentralization is operationally feasible when substitution is required in real time.

That is a more distinctive contribution.

It is mixed, and it should lean much harder toward the world question. The strongest version is: **Can states replace the federal government when federal regulatory capacity collapses?** That is a world question. ### What would make this contribution bigger?

Several possibilities, in descending order of importance:

1. **Broader framing around state capacity and multi-level governance.**  
   The paper becomes bigger if it is explicitly about whether overlapping governments are substitutes or complements in implementation. That is much larger than EPA staffing.

2. **Outcomes that sit closer to the mechanism and broader welfare stakes.**

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Environmental regulation in the U.S. relies on cooperative federalism. State and federal agencies share enforcement responsibilities, and much of the normative defense of this system implicitly assumes that these layers can substitute for one another when needed.

### Tension

But that assumption is rarely tested. The 2017–2020 decline in EPA enforcement staffing created a real-world stress test: if federal enforcement capacity shrinks sharply, do states step in, or does regulatory performance deteriorate?

### Resolution

The paper finds that areas historically more reliant on federal inspections experienced worse ambient air quality during the period of federal withdrawal, consistent with incomplete state substitution.

### Implications

The implication is that cooperative federalism may be more fragile than its formal institutional design suggests. If overlapping regulators are complements rather than close substitutes, then federal capacity cuts have first-order consequences for environmental outcomes and for how economists should think about decentralized implementation more broadly.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the makings of one, but in the current draft the arc is **serviceable rather than compelling**. The core story is there, but the paper still reads somewhat like: institutional background, data, design, main result, caveat. It does not yet fully dramatize the tension.

The reason is that the best narrative is not “EPA staffing fell, PM2.5 rose.” The best narrative is:

- We often assume multi-level governance is robust because one layer can replace another.
- Here is a natural stress test of that assumption.
- The evidence suggests the replacement did not happen.
- Therefore, our understanding of decentralization needs to incorporate substitution failures.

That is the story the paper should tell more explicitly and repeatedly.

### If it is a collection of results looking for a story, what story should it be telling?

The story should be: **cooperative federalism is an insurance contract that may fail precisely when it is most needed.** The paper should frame the EPA staffing decline as an institutional stress test, and PM2.5 as the diagnostic outcome revealing whether the insurance contract worked.

That framing is much stickier than the current one.
