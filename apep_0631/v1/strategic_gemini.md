## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
This paper uses a unique policy "round-trip"—the 2018 imposition of the $10,000 SALT cap and its 2025 expansion to $40,000—to test a foundational assumption of spatial equilibrium: the reversibility of tax capitalization. While the initial cap significantly depressed home prices in high-tax zip codes, the subsequent reversal has failed to restore them, suggesting that tax shocks may have "ratchet effects" on the spatial distribution of wealth. This matters because it challenges the Tiebout-Rosen framework and implies that temporary tax policies can have permanent structural consequences for housing markets.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this well, but it could be sharper. It spends a bit too much time on the institutional history of SALT in the first two paragraphs. 
**The Pitch the paper should have:** "Does the value of a tax break stay gone even after the break returns? While standard theory assumes symmetric capitalization, we use the 2018 imposition and 2025 reversal of the SALT cap to show that while house prices fall when taxes rise, they fail to recover when taxes fall. This 'sticky capitalization' suggests that tax policy changes can permanently shift the spatial equilibrium through path-dependent sorting and anchoring."

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
The paper identifies a significant asymmetry in how housing markets process tax shocks, rejecting the null of symmetric, reversible capitalization.

- **Differentiation:** It is clearly differentiated from **Li and Yao (2024)** and **Brinkman and Mangrum (2021)**, which only look at the 2018 "downward" shock. The value-add is the "upward" reversal test.
- **Question vs. Literature:** It frames itself as answering a question about the world (the permanence of policy shocks), which is an AER-level framing.
- **Explainable?** Yes. A smart economist would say: "It's the SALT cap paper that shows prices didn't bounce back after the reversal."
- **Bigger Contribution:** To make this truly "Big AER," the author needs to do more than just document the asymmetry; they need to nail the *mechanism*. Is it purely psychological (anchoring), or is it physical (the high-income people moved and the transaction costs of moving back are too high)? Using migration data or listing-level data to see *who* is buying these houses now would elevate the paper.

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
The paper sits at the intersection of Urban Economics (Tiebout/Oates), Public Finance (Tax Incidence), and Behavioral Economics (Anchoring).

- **Neighbors:** Rosen (1979) on capitalization; Sommer and Sullivan (2018) on SALT; Case and Shiller (1989) on housing market efficiency.
- **Positioning:** It should position itself as a "stress test" of the Tiebout model. It isn't just "building on" the SALT literature; it is using SALT as a laboratory to challenge a core pillar of spatial equilibrium theory.
- **Unaware of:** The paper should connect to the "hysteresis" literature in macro and the "moving cost" literature in urban economics (e.g., Bayer et al.). If the price doesn't recover, it’s likely because the *marginal buyer* changed permanently.

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC
- **Setup:** The world is Tiebout-perfect; taxes are capitalized into prices instantly and reversibly.
- **Tension:** The TCJA capped SALT, prices fell. Then the OBBB reversed it. If the world is perfect, prices should go back up.
- **Resolution:** They didn't. Capitalization is a "one-way ratchet."
- **Implications:** Policy "experiments" aren't free; you can't just "undo" a tax change and expect the market to return to the status quo ante.

**Evaluation:** The arc is exceptionally strong. It is a rare "clean" experiment. The only weakness is the 7-month window for the reversal. Critics will say "it's just slow adjustment, not asymmetry."

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
At an economics dinner party, you lead with: "When Congress gave the SALT deduction back, homeowners didn't get their equity back." 
People would lean in. The follow-up would be: "Is it because they think the cap will come back in 2030 (the sunset)?" This is the paper's biggest hurdle—the reversal itself is temporary. The author needs to address whether the lack of recovery is "stickiness" or just the market pricing in the 2030 sunset.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The distance is **Medium**. The "Science" is solid, and the "Story" is great. However, the "Reversal" is very recent (Jan 2026 data in a March 2026 paper). 

**The single most impactful advice:** Solve the "Sunset vs. Stickiness" problem. You must prove that the lack of price recovery isn't just the market being smart about the fact that the cap returns in 2030. If you can show that even in areas where the AGI phase-out doesn't apply (so the tax break is "more" permanent), prices still didn't recover, you have an AER lead article.
