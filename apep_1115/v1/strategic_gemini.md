### 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper tests a foundational theory in labor/migration economics: the "enforcement tax" hypothesis, which posits that immigration enforcement pushes undocumented workers out of "visible" high-wage sectors (like construction) into "opaque" low-wage sectors (like food service). Using the nationwide rollout of the Secure Communities (SC) program and high-resolution QWI administrative data, the author finds a precisely estimated null result—enforcement does not induce industry reallocation.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this pitch excellently. The introduction uses a vivid opening anecdote followed by a clear theoretical conjecture. 

*The "Should-Be" Pitch (Minor refinement only):*
"Does heightened immigration enforcement distort the labor market by forcing workers into the shadows of the service economy? This paper exploits the staggered rollout of Secure Communities to show that, contrary to theoretical predictions of an 'enforcement tax,' the program had zero impact on the industrial composition of the Hispanic workforce. This suggests that the costs of enforcement are borne through unemployment rather than sectoral reallocation."

---

### 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution:** The paper provides the first large-scale empirical rejection of the industry-reallocation "enforcement tax" hypothesis, demonstrating that internal enforcement mechanisms do not shift workers across the intensive margin of industry choice.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It differentiates itself well from East et al. (2023) by moving from *extensive* margin (total employment) to *intensive* margin (sectoral sorting).
*   **Framing:** It is framed as answering a question about the WORLD (how workers respond to risk) rather than a gap in the literature.
*   **Clarity:** A smart economist would immediately understand: "It's the paper that shows Secure Communities didn't actually move people into lower-profile jobs."
*   **Bigness:** To make this bigger, the author needs to explore *why* the theory fails more deeply. Is it a "stickiness" of human capital, or is the "visibility" of sectors a misclassification by economists?

---

### 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

The paper sits at the intersection of Labor Economics and Law & Economics.

*   **Closest Neighbors:** Chassamboulli and Palivos (2014) [Theory]; East et al. (2023) [SC/Employment]; Cox and Miles (2014) [SC/Deportations].
*   **Positioning:** It acts as an empirical "stress test" for Chassamboulli and Palivos. It should more aggressively frame itself as a challenge to the standard search-and-matching models of the undocumented labor market.
*   **Missing Conversations:** The paper would benefit from connecting to the **"Job Lock"** or **"Specific Human Capital"** literatures. If workers don't move despite a "tax," it suggests the returns to industry-specific experience in construction outweigh the enforcement risk—a major point for labor productivity.

---

### 4. NARRATIVE ARC

*   **Setup:** Enforcement is generally thought to push workers into the "informal" or "opaque" economy.
*   **Tension:** Theoretical models (the "enforcement tax") predict a shift from high-wage visible jobs to low-wage opaque ones, yet we haven't seen the evidence.
*   **Resolution:** A precisely estimated null. Secure Communities triggered fingerprinting at jails, but this didn't change where people worked.
*   **Implications:** The "tax" is a myth; the risk is likely perceived as being tied to *personhood* (arrests) rather than *workplace* (raids), or the costs of switching sectors are prohibitively high.

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong but the "Implications" section is currently a bit thin. The author needs to lean harder into what this means for the welfare of undocumented families—it’s actually a *grimmer* story (they stay in risky jobs because they have no choice).

---

### 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

*   **The Fact:** "When ICE started checking fingerprints in every local jail, not a single construction worker moved to a restaurant job."
*   **Reaction:** Lean in. It’s counter-intuitive.
*   **Follow-up:** "Does that mean they just stayed and took the risk, or did they just leave the country?"
*   **The Null:** This is a "Gold Standard" null. It uses the Abadie (2020) framework correctly to show that the absence of an effect is more informative than a noisy positive result.

---

### 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

*   **Front-loading:** The paper is well-structured.
*   **Section 2 (Institutional):** This needs a bit more detail on *why* we thought construction was "visible" vs. healthcare. Is there data on actual workplace raids during this period?
*   **Conclusion:** The conclusion is a bit summary-heavy. It should spend more time on the "Policy Illusion" aspect—if politicians think they are "nudging" workers out of sectors, they are wrong.

---

### 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The gap is currently **Scope and Ambition**. While the null is "precisely estimated," for the AER, I want to see *why* the mechanism failed. 

**The Single Most Impactful Advice:**
Conduct a heterogeneity analysis by **industry wage-gap** or **task-specificity**; if the "enforcement tax" is real, we should at least see reallocation in counties where the wage gap between visible and opaque sectors is smallest (where the "cost" of switching is lower). If it's zero even there, the theory is truly dead.
