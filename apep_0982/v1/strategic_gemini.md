## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether the liberalization of concealed carry laws (Permitless Carry) creates a "hidden" labor market cost for Black workers by making customer-facing environments—where armed interactions become more likely—less tenable or attractive. Using a triple-difference design, it finds that while adopting states saw broad Black employment growth, this growth systematically bypassed the accommodation and food services sector, suggesting that gun policy indirectly reshapes the racial and sectoral composition of the workforce.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this remarkably well. The second paragraph of the introduction is a textbook example of framing: it moves from the policy change to the specific economic agents most exposed (hotel clerks, servers) and provides a clear theoretical link (racialized perceptions of threat). 

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**The Contribution:** The paper identifies a significant "policy externality" where gun liberalization laws, traditionally evaluated only on crime outcomes, create racially disparate sectoral reallocation in the labor market.

- **Differentiation:** It is well-differentiated. While the "Right-to-Carry" (RTC) literature (Donohue; Lott) is decades deep, it is almost exclusively focused on criminology. This paper successfully colonizes that debate for labor economics.
- **World vs. Literature:** It frames itself as answering a question about the **WORLD** (how do guns in public change who works where?). 
- **The "DiD" Trap:** A skeptical reader might say "it's another staggered adoption paper," but the triple-difference (Race x Sector x Time) is used here to test a very specific behavioral hypothesis about *interactions*, which elevates it above a simple policy evaluation.
- **Growth Opportunity:** To make the contribution "AER-big," the author needs to dig deeper into the **mechanism**. Currently, we don't know if Black workers are quitting (supply) or if employers/customers are discriminating more in the presence of guns (demand). 

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

- **Closest Neighbors:** Donohue et al. (2019) on RTC and crime; Bertrand & Mullainathan (2004) on racial labor gaps; and recent "new DiD" literature (Callaway & Sant’Anna, 2021).
- **Positioning Strategy:** The paper should position itself as a **bridge**. It’s not "attacking" the crime literature; it’s arguing that the crime literature has missed half the story by ignoring the "chilling effect" on the labor market.
- **Missing Conversations:** The paper would benefit from connecting to the **"Compensating Differentials"** literature. Are Black workers being paid more to stay in these roles? Or is the "disamenity" of potential armed conflict so high that it overrides wage signals?

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

- **Setup:** States are rapidly deregulating gun carry.
- **Tension:** Economists only look at crime, but workers—especially Black workers in face-to-face roles—are the ones who actually "absorb" the risk of these laws.
- **Resolution:** Black employment gains in "Red states" are real but are funneled away from service jobs and into "back-room" manufacturing jobs.
- **Implications:** Gun policy is, inadvertently, a tool of occupational segregation.

**Evaluation:** The narrative is strong. It avoids the "result-mining" feel by anchoring the empirics in a very specific sectoral comparison (Accommodation vs. Manufacturing).

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

**The Dinner Party Fact:** "When a state passes permitless carry, Black workers stop showing up for new jobs at hotels and restaurants, even as the rest of the state's economy is booming."

**Reaction:** This is a "lean-in" finding. It touches on three third-rail topics: guns, race, and the "Texas miracle" (growth in adopting states). The immediate follow-up is: "Is it because they are scared, or because customers are more hostile when they are armed?" The paper’s inability to answer this is its biggest hurdle for the AER.

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

- **Front-loading:** The paper is well-structured. Table 2 (the simple DiD) vs Table 3 (the DDD) effectively tells the story of why the triple-difference is necessary.
- **Appendix:** The "2021 cohort" discussion is vital because of COVID-19. I would actually move more of the robustness regarding "urban vs. rural" (if the data permits) to the main text, as gun carry dynamics likely differ wildly between a rural diner and a Chicago-area hotel.
- **Mechanism Section:** Section 5.3 is currently too short. This needs to be the meat of the paper if it wants to move from "interesting find" to "fundamental insight."

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The gap is **Mechanism and Scope.** 

The finding that "growth bypassed the sector" is a "relative" effect. In absolute terms, Black accommodation employment still grew (ATT +0.05). To be an AER paper, the author needs to convince us that this isn't just a statistical artifact of manufacturing growing *unusually* fast in these specific states for unrelated reasons (e.g., the "Sun Belt" boom). 

**Single most impactful advice:** Find a way to proxy for "exposure to the public" within the data. If the author can show this effect is stronger in counties with high baseline crime or higher gun ownership, the "fear/interaction" story becomes undeniable.
