## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether permitless concealed-carry laws have labor-market consequences, specifically whether they shift Black employment away from customer-facing sectors. Using state-by-race-by-industry employment data, it argues that after these laws pass, Black employment growth is relatively weaker in accommodation/food services than in manufacturing, suggesting that gun policy may reshape the sectoral composition of minority employment.

A busy economist should care only if the paper can convince them that this is not just “another policy spillover paper,” but evidence that a major and expanding area of state regulation—gun-carrying rules—changes who works in face-to-face service jobs and thus has distributional consequences outside crime.

**Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Reasonably, but not well enough for AER. The current introduction gets to the question quickly, but the pitch is still framed too much as “crime has been studied, labor markets have not.” That is a literature gap, not a world question. And the mechanism is introduced in a somewhat speculative way before the reader is persuaded the underlying fact is important.

**What the first two paragraphs should say instead:**

> Over the last two decades, more than half of U.S. states have made it legal to carry concealed firearms in public without a permit. Whatever these laws do to crime, they also change the environment of everyday economic interaction: restaurants, hotels, and other service businesses now more frequently operate in settings where customers may be armed. The question is whether that change affects who works in those jobs.
>
> This paper shows that it does. After states adopt permitless carry, Black employment growth becomes relatively weaker in accommodation and food services than in manufacturing, while White employment in accommodation shows no comparable shift. The core contribution is to show that firearm policy has economically meaningful distributional spillovers: it changes the sectoral allocation of minority employment, not just public safety outcomes.

That is the pitch the paper should have. It leads with a world fact and a broad implication, not with “the literature has ignored this.”

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution in one sentence:**  
The paper’s contribution is to claim that permitless carry laws reallocate Black employment away from customer-facing service work relative to other workers/sectors, revealing a racialized labor-market spillover of firearm policy.

### Is this clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?
Only partially. It is differentiated from the gun-policy/crime literature simply because it studies labor markets rather than crime. But that is a low bar. The harder question is whether it is clearly differentiated from:

1. general literature on non-crime spillovers of gun policy,
2. racial labor-market discrimination in customer-facing jobs,
3. papers on how local amenity/safety/perceived-threat environments affect labor supply or sector choice,
4. broad policy-shock papers showing sectoral reallocation for minority workers.

Right now the paper’s novelty is mostly **domain novelty** (“nobody has studied this outcome for this policy”), not yet **conceptual novelty**. That is not enough for AER unless the fact itself is truly striking.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a gap in a literature?
Mostly as filling a literature gap. The intro says labor-market effects are “entirely unstudied.” That is not a strong frame. The stronger frame is: **when states make armed public presence more common, does that change the composition of service-sector employment, and for whom?**

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the intro?
They could say: “It’s a triple-difference paper claiming permitless carry reduces relative Black employment growth in accommodation.” That is not nothing, but it still sounds like “another DiD paper about X” because the actual new idea is not yet distilled. The paper needs a cleaner headline contribution, something like:

- firearm policy changes labor-market sorting in customer-facing sectors;
- those effects are racially uneven;
- the key margin is not total employment loss but **where** employment growth occurs.

The “relative sectoral reallocation” angle is actually the most interesting idea in the paper, and it should be promoted more aggressively.

### What would make this contribution bigger?
Several possibilities:

1. **A sharper outcome variable.**  
   NAICS 72 is broad. The paper would be much bigger if it could isolate truly front-facing occupations: servers, bartenders, front-desk staff, cashiers, retail sales, etc. Right now “accommodation vs manufacturing” is intuitive but blunt.

2. **A stronger comparison sector set.**  
   Manufacturing is clean but too stylized. A more ambitious paper would compare gradients of customer exposure across many sectors or occupations, not one high-contact and one low-contact sector.

3. **A stronger mechanism distinction.**  
   The paper currently says it could be worker exit, employer behavior, or customer discrimination. That is too open-ended. The paper would become more important if it could show whether the action is in hiring, quits, establishment composition, or wages.

4. **A broader framing beyond Black employment alone.**  
   The paper hints at a general class of policies that alter the conditions of public interaction. That could be big. But then it should explicitly frame itself as a paper on how public-safety regulation affects labor allocation in public-facing work.

5. **Better external stakes.**  
   Why should economists revise their beliefs? Is the takeaway that gun policy affects labor supply? discrimination? amenities of work? sectoral matching? The paper currently does not force that choice.

4. **Customer-facing work and labor-market sorting**
   - Literature on emotional labor, public-facing occupations, worker sorting by race/gender, customer prejudice

### How should it position itself relative to those neighbors?
Mostly **build on and bridge**, not attack.

- Relative to the gun literature: “You have treated these laws primarily as crime policy; they also change the conditions of everyday market interaction.”
- Relative to racial labor-market literature: “You have studied hiring discrimination and wage gaps; here is a new policy channel that affects sectoral allocation.”
- Relative to public safety/place literature: “This is another route through which safety regulation affects economic organization.”

It should not over-claim that it overturns the gun literature. Nor should it pick a fight with crime papers. The best positioning is as a **bridge paper** connecting firearm regulation to labor economics and racial inequality.

### Is it positioned too narrowly or too broadly?
Currently **too narrowly in empirical design, too broadly in rhetoric**.

- Too narrow because the empirical comparison is just accommodation vs manufacturing, which feels like a very specific setup.
- Too broad because the rhetoric sometimes gestures toward sweeping claims about “racialized channels” and policy externalities without enough conceptual scaffolding.

### What literature does it seem unaware of?
The paper seems under-engaged with at least four literatures:

1. **Customer discrimination / consumer-facing labor markets**
2. **Compensating differentials / workplace amenity-disamenity literature**
3. **Occupational sorting by race under differential exposure to risk or hostility**
4. **Broader social effects of firearm prevalence beyond crime**, including avoidance behavior, public-space use, and perceived safety

Those conversations could give the paper a much stronger economics identity.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?
Not yet fully. Right now it is having the conversation: “gun policy has an unstudied labor outcome.”  
The better conversation is: **policies that alter the character of public interactions can reallocate labor across sectors, especially for groups exposed to higher perceived threat or discrimination.** Permitless carry is the setting, not the entire intellectual payload.

That framing is more interesting and more portable.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
States increasingly legalize permitless concealed carry, changing the environment in which public-facing businesses operate. The gun debate has focused on crime, while labor economists think less about how such legal changes alter workplace interaction.

### Tension
If these laws make customer-facing environments different in ways that are especially salient for Black workers, then employment effects should appear not in aggregate employment alone, but in the relative attractiveness or viability of service-sector jobs. Yet no paper has documented whether firearm liberalization changes labor allocation by race and sector.

### Resolution
The paper finds that Black employment growth is relatively weaker in accommodation than in manufacturing after adoption, while White accommodation employment shows no parallel pattern. The effect looks like sectoral reallocation rather than an absolute collapse in Black employment.

### Implications
Gun policy may have distributional labor-market spillovers; economists and policymakers should think of firearm regulation as affecting the organization of labor in public-facing sectors, not just crime.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
It has the bones of one, but currently it reads somewhat like a **collection of estimators and contrasts looking for a story**.

The main problem is that the paper has two different stories competing with each other:

1. “Permitless carry reduces Black employment in customer-facing industries.”
2. “Actually Black employment overall rises, but less so in accommodation than manufacturing.”

The second is more accurate and more interesting. The first is simpler but somewhat misleading. The paper has not fully committed to which story it wants to tell.

**What story should it be telling?**  
It should be telling a **sectoral sorting** story:

> Permitless carry does not simply reduce employment; it changes where Black employment growth occurs. In adopting states, growth shifts away from public-facing service work and toward less customer-exposed sectors. That suggests that the spread of guns in public spaces changes the effective working conditions of customer-contact jobs.

That story is cleaner, more intellectually coherent, and more defensible as a broad economics point.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party?
“After permitless carry laws pass, Black employment growth shifts away from accommodation/food service relative to manufacturing, with no comparable shift for White workers.”

Not “7.2 log points.” The fact is about **who works in public-facing service jobs after guns become easier to carry in public**.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
Some would lean in, but only if the framing is sharpened. In its current form, many economists would worry this is a thinly sliced policy application with an underdeveloped mechanism. The topic has salience; the current presentation does not fully cash that in.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Almost immediately: **“Why exactly?”**

Then:
- Is this worker avoidance or employer/customer behavior?
- Why accommodation and not retail?
- Is this about perceived threat, actual gun prevalence, or partisan/state growth differences?
- Why should manufacturing be the benchmark?
- How general is this beyond this one sector comparison?

Those are healthy questions, and the paper needs to anticipate them at the narrative level.

### If findings are modest: is the modesty itself interesting?
The findings are modest in absolute size and more interesting in relative terms. The paper does make a reasonable case that the result is not “job loss” but “reallocation.” That helps. But modest relative reallocation needs a stronger conceptual payoff. Otherwise it can feel like a statistically significant but substantively narrow contrast.

The paper should explicitly argue that **reallocation across sectors is the relevant margin** because that is how workplace amenity shocks often operate. That would make the modesty more meaningful.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

Be honest: in current form, this is **not yet an AER paper**. The gap is mainly one of **ambition and framing**, with some likely scope concerns.

### What is the main problem?

**Mostly an ambition problem, secondarily a framing problem.**

- **Framing problem:** The paper is sold as “an unstudied labor-market effect of permitless carry,” which is too small-bore.
- **Ambition problem:** The empirical object is a single sector pair and a broad mechanism story. For AER, the paper needs either a more general conceptual claim or richer evidence showing this is a meaningful economic margin, not a one-off contrast.

There is also some **novelty risk**: if the paper remains “policy X affects subgroup Y in sector Z,” it will feel incremental even if statistically careful.

### What would excite the top 10 people in this field?
One of two things:

1. **A broader, cleaner claim about public-facing work:**  
   that legal changes affecting the expected prevalence of armed public interactions alter labor sorting across customer-contact occupations, especially for groups facing differential threat/discrimination.

2. **Mechanism-rich evidence:**  
   showing the effect is strongest in the most public-contact occupations, appears in hires/separations or wages in a coherent way, and perhaps extends beyond accommodation to retail, transit, health reception, etc.

Without one of those, the paper risks being seen as an interesting JUE / ILRR / RSUE / field-journal paper rather than AER.

### Single most impactful advice
**Reframe the paper around a general economics question—how laws that change the conditions of public interaction reallocate labor in customer-facing sectors—and then make the evidence match that broader claim, rather than presenting this as a narrow “permitless carry has one previously unstudied labor effect” paper.**
