## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether loosening gun-carry restrictions changes who works in customer-facing service jobs. Using state adoption of permitless carry laws, it argues that Black employment grows less in accommodation and food services than in manufacturing or than White employment does, suggesting that firearm policy may reshape the sectoral composition of minority employment even if aggregate employment does not fall.

A busy economist should care because this is, in principle, a novel way to connect two large conversations that rarely meet: firearm regulation and racial inequality in labor markets. If true, the paper is not about “guns and jobs” in a generic sense; it is about whether changes in the public-interaction environment spill over into racial sorting across sectors.

### Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Partly, but not well enough. The introduction gets to the question quickly, but the pitch is still too much “no one has studied labor-market effects” and not enough “here is a surprising fact about the world.” The first two paragraphs also lean too hard on a speculative mechanism—armed customer-worker interactions and racialized threat perceptions—before the reader is convinced there is a robust empirical pattern worth explaining.

The paper should lead with the fact pattern, not with the literature gap.

### The pitch the paper should have

“Permitless carry laws are usually debated as crime policy. This paper shows they may also change the composition of employment in public-facing service work: after states remove concealed-carry permit requirements, Black employment growth shifts away from accommodation and food services relative to both White workers and Black workers in manufacturing. The broader question is whether policies that alter the perceived safety and social dynamics of public spaces can produce racially uneven labor-market sorting, even without reducing overall employment.”

That version is cleaner, more world-facing, and more AER-like.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper’s contribution is to document that permitless carry laws are associated with a relative reallocation of Black employment away from customer-facing accommodation jobs and toward manufacturing, linking firearm policy to racial sectoral sorting in labor markets.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest papers?

Only somewhat. The paper differentiates itself from the crime literature by saying labor-market consequences are unstudied, but that is a weak form of differentiation. “No one has looked here yet” is not enough. It needs to distinguish itself from:
1. the broad right-to-carry / permitless carry literature,
2. the racial discrimination and labor-market sorting literature,
3. papers on how local safety, disorder, or public-interaction environments affect employment composition.

Right now the contribution risks sounding like: “another reduced-form policy paper finds heterogeneous effects by race and sector.” The specific fact is more interesting than that, but the introduction does not yet elevate it.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a literature gap?

Too much as a literature gap. “The labor market consequences are unknown” is not a strong AER framing. The stronger question is: **Do policies that change the social environment of public-facing work alter racial allocation across sectors?** That is a world question.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?

They could probably say: “It’s a DDD paper showing permitless carry reduced Black employment growth in accommodation relative to manufacturing.” That is intelligible, but it still sounds method-first and niche. They would not yet say: “This changes how we think about the incidence of gun policy.”

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Several possibilities:

- **A sharper outcome variable.** The paper would be bigger if it could show this in occupations with the highest direct customer exposure—front-desk, serving, bartending, retail cashiers—rather than a broad NAICS sector. Right now the main finding lives at a fairly aggregated state × sector × race cell.
- **A more direct margin.** Hires/separations/turnover or worker flows across sectors by race would make the “reallocation” claim much more central and vivid.
- **A stronger comparison sector.** Manufacturing is plausible as low-public-contact, but also economically very different. A larger contribution would compare across multiple sectors ranked by customer exposure, not just accommodation vs manufacturing.
- **A more general framing.** The paper would be more important if it argued that public-space regulatory changes affect labor allocation through perceived safety and social interaction, with permitless carry as a salient case.
- **A more persuasive mechanism frame.** Not necessarily more causal evidence, but a clearer conceptual distinction: is the core story worker avoidance, employer/customer discrimination, or shifts in demand? The paper currently gestures at all three.

The single biggest way to make the contribution feel larger is to move from “one policy, one sector contrast” to “a broader insight about how the social environment of public-facing work shapes racial sorting.”

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Permitless carry laws have spread rapidly, and economists mostly debate them through crime outcomes. Meanwhile, customer-facing service jobs are environments where workers are directly exposed to the public, and racial dynamics may matter especially in those settings.

### Tension

If these laws alter the nature of public interactions, then their incidence may not show up as aggregate employment losses or crime alone. Instead, they may shift where different groups are willing or able to work. The puzzle is whether a public-safety regulatory change generates racial sectoral sorting in labor markets.

### Resolution

The paper finds that after adoption, Black employment grows less in accommodation than one would expect based on comparison groups, while White accommodation employment does not show the same pattern and Black manufacturing employment rises.

### Implications

Gun policy may have distributional labor-market effects that standard crime-centered analyses miss. More broadly, public-interaction policies can reshape the allocation of minority workers across sectors without showing up as straightforward job destruction.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

There is a recognizable arc, but it is not yet fully disciplined. The paper has a real story, but it is still too close to “collection of estimates plus possible mechanisms.”

Three narrative problems:

1. **The central fact is conceptually muddled.** Is the headline “Black accommodation employment falls relative to baseline” or “Black employment gains bypass accommodation”? Those are related, but the paper alternates between them. It should choose one central statement.
2. **The mechanism section is too open-ended.** “Could be worker exit, employer behavior, customer discrimination, or some combination” is honest but narratively diffuse.
3. **The coined term ‘accommodation gap’ is not yet earning its keep.** It sounds catchy, but unless this becomes a more general empirical object across settings, it may feel like branding rather than insight.

### What story should it be telling?

Not “permitless carry hurts Black accommodation employment.” That sounds too literal and invites many objections.

The better story is: **permitless carry appears to change the relative attractiveness or viability of public-facing service employment for Black workers, producing racial reallocation across sectors.** That is subtler and more important.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?

“I’d lead with: after states adopt permitless carry, Black employment growth shifts away from customer-facing accommodation jobs relative to both White workers and Black workers in manufacturing.”

That is a dinner-party fact economists can understand quickly.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

Some would lean in, but only if presented as a surprising incidence result, not as a niche gun-law application. The topic has enough salience, and the racial sorting angle is interesting. But if the presentation slips into methodological language or overuses “DDD,” attention will evaporate fast.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Probably one of these:

- “Why accommodation specifically—do you have occupation-level evidence?”
- “Is this really about customer contact, or is manufacturing just a bad control?”
- “Are these workers leaving the sector, the state, or not entering those jobs?”
- “Is this about perceived risk or broader state political environment?”

Those are all strategic questions about interpretation and scope, not econometric objections.

### If findings are modest, is that okay?

Yes, but only if the paper owns the fact that this is about **composition**, not large aggregate job loss. The effect is not huge. That is fine if the contribution is “policy changes where gains accrue.” It is less fine if the paper tries to imply a first-order aggregate labor-market effect. The paper should not oversell magnitude; it should sell the **pattern**.

At present, the null/modest nature of some margins is not a problem. The problem is that the paper hasn’t fully convinced the reader why a relative reallocation result is economically important. It needs to say more clearly that labor-market incidence often works through allocation and sorting, not just levels.

In other words, the paper needs to become less like “the first labor paper on permitless carry” and more like “a paper about policy-induced social environment and labor allocation, with permitless carry as a sharp case study.”
