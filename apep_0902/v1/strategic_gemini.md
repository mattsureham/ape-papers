### 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
The paper examines the labor market consequences of "Right-to-Farm" (RTF) constitutional amendments—aggressive legal shields that immunize industrial livestock operations (CAFOs) from nuisance lawsuits and local zoning. Using a staggered DiD design, it finds that despite being "sold" to voters as job-creation measures, these amendments had a precisely estimated zero effect on agricultural employment.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this well, but it leads too heavily on the legal "extraordinary instrument" angle. To meet AER standards, the pitch should lean into the **deregulation vs. externalities** trade-off.
*   *Suggested Revision:* "Does removing the threat of litigation and local oversight spur industrial expansion and job growth? This paper evaluates recent constitutional 'Right-to-Farm' amendments—which effectively deregulate the industrial livestock sector—and finds that while they successfully shielded firms from legal costs, they failed to produce the employment dividend promised by proponents."

### 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**One-sentence contribution:** The paper provides the first causal evidence that constitutionalizing industrial agricultural protections fails to increase sectoral employment, challenging the "deregulation-as-job-creator" narrative in a high-externality industry.

*   **Clarity/Differentiation:** It is well-differentiated. While there is a legal literature on RTF and an econ literature on CAFO externalities (e.g., Lawley 2021; Sneeringer 2009), the bridge to labor markets is new.
*   **World vs. Literature:** It currently frames itself as "opening an empirical literature" (Gap-filling). It should frame itself as: "Why do politicians keep promising jobs from deregulation in sectors where capital-labor substitution is the dominant trend?"
*   **The "Bigger" Contribution:** To make this bigger, the author needs to explore the **political economy**. If there are no jobs, why did these pass? Is it because they protected *incumbent profits* rather than *new entry*? Adding a firm-level analysis or a "concentration of ownership" proxy would move this from a "policy evaluation" to a "top-tier mechanism paper."

### 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
The paper sits at the intersection of Environmental Econ (externalities), Law & Econ (nuisance law), and Labor (deregulation).
*   **Neighbors:** Greenstone (2002) and Walker (2013) on environmental regulation/jobs; Callaway & Sant'Anna (2021) on methods; Centner (2002) on RTF law.
*   **Strategy:** It should "attack" the conventional wisdom that environmental deregulation is a "free lunch" for rural employment. It is currently too polite to the proponents of these laws.
*   **Unexpected Connection:** It should speak to the **Industrial Organization** of farming. The "So What" is that technology (automation in CAFOs) makes labor demand inelastic to regulatory costs.

### 4. NARRATIVE ARC
*   **Setup:** Industrial farming is under siege from neighbors' lawsuits.
*   **Tension:** States pass "nuclear-option" constitutional amendments to protect the industry, promising a rural jobs boom.
*   **Resolution:** Precise null. The "shield" (legal protection) did not become a "sword" (economic growth).
*   **Implications:** The trade-off is all pain (externalities) and no gain (jobs).
*   **Evaluation:** The arc is strong. The "Shield/Sword" metaphor is effective and helps carry the narrative.

### 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
*   **The Fact:** "Missouri gave CAFOs constitutional immunity to lawsuits to save rural jobs, and ten years later, not a single net job was created."
*   **Reaction:** Economists will lean in because it confirms a suspicion that these laws are "rent-seeking" rather than "growth-oriented."
*   **Follow-up:** "If jobs weren't created, did the value of the CAFOs go up? Did corporate profits rise while property values fell?" The paper needs to hint at these answers to satisfy the dinner party.

### 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
*   **Front-loading:** The "Institutional Background" is a bit long. Move the specific list of six state bills to a table and keep the text focused on *what* the laws changed legally (the shift from "statute" to "constitutional right").
*   **Appendix:** The "Hispanic vs. Non-Hispanic" result is interesting but feels like a "check." It could be moved to the appendix unless the author can link it to a story about *who* the industry claims to be helping.
*   **The Mechanism Section:** This needs to be its own section. Don't just report a null; explain *why* (e.g., the trend toward capital-intensive consolidation mentioned on page 2).

### 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The gap is **ambition**. As a "Policy Evaluation of X," it's a solid *Applied Economics* or *AEJ: Policy* paper. To be an *AER* paper, it must address the **Political Economy of Rent Seeking**.

**The single most impactful piece of advice:**
Add a section or an auxiliary analysis on **land values or corporate concentration.** If you show that these laws didn't create jobs but *did* increase the land value of existing CAFOs or led to the exit of small-scale competitors, you have a paper about how "job creation" is used as a rhetorical mask for protecting incumbent capital.
