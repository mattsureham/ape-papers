## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple policy-relevant question: when states strengthen Right-to-Farm protections for large-scale animal agriculture, do they deliver the rural jobs supporters promise? Using staggered state policy changes and county-level employment data, the paper finds essentially no employment effect in animal production, suggesting that shielding CAFOs from nuisance suits and local regulation does not translate into local labor-market gains.

Why should a busy economist care? Because this is a clean test of a broad and recurring political claim: deregulation is often sold as job creation, but in a consolidated, capital-intensive sector that logic may fail completely.

The paper **mostly does articulate this pitch clearly**, but not optimally. The current opening is vivid and readable, but it spends too much time on the legal novelty of constitutional protection and not enough time on the bigger economic question. The first two paragraphs should foreground the general-interest issue first: when industries seek legal insulation from regulation and litigation, do local economies benefit, or do rents rise without jobs?

### The pitch the paper should have

> Across many sectors, deregulation is justified as a way to create jobs. This paper studies a stark version of that claim in U.S. agriculture: between 2012 and 2021, six states strengthened Right-to-Farm protections that insulated large animal operations from nuisance lawsuits and local restrictions, with proponents explicitly promising rural employment gains. Using county-quarter employment data and staggered policy adoption, I find no evidence that these legal protections increased employment in animal production.
>
> The result matters beyond agriculture. It suggests that when production is already concentrated and capital-intensive, removing legal constraints may protect incumbents without expanding labor demand. Right-to-Farm laws may therefore redistribute power toward producers and away from communities, but not in the employment-producing way advocates claim.

That version turns the paper from “a paper about RTF laws” into “a paper about when deregulation creates jobs.”

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:**  
The paper provides the first causal evidence that recent state-level Right-to-Farm strengthening measures did not increase employment in animal production.

This contribution is **clear enough**, but it is not yet differentiated sharply enough from adjacent work to feel big. Right now the paper’s contribution reads as: “no one has estimated this before.” That is true, but “first paper on X” is not by itself AER-level positioning unless X is obviously central.

### Is it clearly differentiated from the closest papers?
Not fully. The paper names three literatures—RTF legal scholarship, environmental regulation and employment, and agricultural consolidation—but it does not draw crisp contrasts with the nearest economics papers. It needs to say more explicitly:

- existing environmental-regulation/employment papers are mostly about **imposing** regulation in manufacturing;
- this paper instead studies **removing legal constraints** in agriculture;
- existing agriculture papers document externalities and consolidation, but not whether legal protections generate the promised labor-market benefits.

Right now, a smart economist could say: “It’s a diff-in-diff on an unusual agricultural law with a null employment result.” That is not yet enough.

### World question vs. literature gap
At present, the paper does both, but it leans too much on the literature-gap framing (“first causal evidence on RTF employment”). The stronger framing is a **world question**:

> When politicians weaken local and legal constraints on polluting production, do local jobs actually increase?

That is a real question about the world. It has broad relevance well beyond this policy domain.

### Could a smart economist explain what is new?
Yes, but only in a narrow way: “It studies Right-to-Farm laws and finds no employment effect.”  
The risk is exactly what you flagged: many readers would classify it as “another DiD paper about a state policy change,” unless the introduction gives them a more general intellectual hook.

### What would make the contribution bigger?
Most importantly, the paper needs to show that the null is informative about **what these laws do instead of employment expansion**. Several ways to enlarge it:

1. **Different outcome variable:**  
   Employment is politically salient, but maybe not the margin that moves. If the paper could show effects on:
   - establishment counts,
   - average establishment size,
   - wages/profits proxies,
   - concentration,
   - local tax base,
   - housing/property values,
   it would say much more about what the policy actually changes.

2. **Different mechanism:**  
   The current mechanism is hand-wavy: legal shield → lower costs → maybe more labor demand. The paper would be stronger if it asked whether RTF laws induce:
   - expansion of large operations but not jobs,
   - substitution toward capital-intensive production,
   - survival of incumbents rather than entry.

3. **Different comparison:**  
   Compare counties more exposed to CAFO expansion potential versus less exposed counties. Again, not an ID comment; strategically, that would sharpen the economic story from a broad null to “even where this should matter most, jobs don’t grow.”

4. **Different framing:**  
   Recast as a paper on the political economy of deregulation in concentrated industries. The current title and framing are clever but still too policy-specific.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

### Closest neighbors
The closest literatures seem to be:

3. **Externalities of animal agriculture / CAFOs**

   - Herriges, Secchi, Babcock (2005)
   - Lawley and coauthors on property values
   - Health/environmental justice work such as Sneeringer, Wing, Osterberg

4. **Law and economics / political economy of Right-to-Farm**
   - Hamilton (1998)
   - Centner (2002, 2006)
   These are not economics centerpieces, but they matter for institutional context.

### How should the paper position itself?
It should **build on** the environmental regulation/employment literature, not merely cite it. The strongest move is:

- Those papers ask whether stricter environmental rules reduce employment.
- This paper asks the mirror-image question: when legal constraints are relaxed in a pollution-intensive sector, do jobs rise?
- The answer is no, which suggests asymmetry: deregulation need not reverse the employment effects of regulation in sectors where scale and automation dominate labor demand.

That is a much more interesting conversation than “there is no prior RTF employment paper.”

It should also **synthesize** with the agricultural consolidation literature:
- If the sector is moving toward larger, more capital-intensive units, then reduced legal exposure may increase scale or rents without increasing employment.
- That gives the null a genuine economic interpretation, not just a failed search for significance.

### Is the paper positioned too narrowly or too broadly?
Currently it is **too narrowly positioned for AER**. It sounds like a specialized agricultural policy evaluation with a null result. The underlying question is broader than the presentation.

### What literature does it seem unaware of?
A few broader conversations it should probably engage:

- **Political economy of deregulation / regulatory capture**
- **Local economic effects of environmental externalities**
- **Incidence of legal liability protections**
- **Market power / concentration and pass-through of policy benefits into employment**
- Potentially **place-based policy skepticism**: policies sold as local development tools often benefit capital more than labor

The unexpected but potentially high-return connection is to **the economics of incidence in regulation and deregulation**: when legal constraints are lifted, who captures the gains—workers, consumers, landowners, or incumbent firms?

### Is the paper having the right conversation?
Not yet. It is having the conversation: “What do RTF laws do?”  
It should be having the conversation: “When legal protections for polluting incumbents expand, do local labor markets benefit?”

That is the AER conversation.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
States adopted unusually strong protections for industrial agriculture, justified publicly as necessary to preserve or create rural jobs.

### Tension
But it is unclear whether reducing nuisance liability and local constraints actually increases employment in a sector already dominated by large, capital-intensive operations. The same policy that protects output or profits may have little effect on labor demand.

### Resolution
The paper finds no detectable increase in animal-production employment, hiring, separations, or earnings following RTF strengthening.

### Implications
The central implication is that these policies may redistribute rights and bargaining power toward producers without delivering the labor-market benefits used to justify them. More broadly, deregulation in concentrated sectors may not generate jobs.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
**Yes, but only partially.** The bones of the story are there. The problem is that the paper’s narrative arc is more legal-institutional than economic. It sets up RTF as a dramatic constitutional development; the tension is legal conflict; the resolution is no employment effect. That is coherent, but still niche.

The better story is:

- **Setup:** policymakers often promise jobs from deregulation.
- **Tension:** in modern agriculture, reduced legal constraints may expand scale, not labor.
- **Resolution:** no employment gains appear.
- **Implications:** job-creation arguments for these laws are weak; legal protection can benefit capital without benefiting workers.

At moments the paper feels like a collection of standard empirical sections attached to a nice title. The title and first paragraph suggest a sharp idea; the rest of the paper often relaxes into “here are the estimates.” It needs to keep the reader anchored in the broader puzzle throughout.

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

### What fact would I lead with?
I would lead with:

> Six states gave industrial animal agriculture stronger constitutional or statutory protection from lawsuits and local restrictions, and despite explicit promises of rural jobs, employment in animal production did not increase.

That is the right dinner-party fact.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
Among applied micro, public, environmental, and ag economists, some would lean in. Among the broader economics dinner party, this is borderline. The result is interesting, but the current framing makes it sound sector-specific. To make people lean in, you need the broader line:

> Here’s a case where deregulation was sold as job creation and appears not to have created jobs at all.

That gets attention.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Probably one of these:
- “So what did the laws change, if not jobs?”
- “Did they increase scale, profits, entry, or concentration?”
- “Are these laws just transfers to incumbents?”
- “Maybe employment is the wrong margin—what about output or establishment size?”

That follow-up question is important because it reveals the paper’s current limitation: it has a nice falsification of one political claim, but limited evidence on the policy’s actual incidence.

### If the findings are null or modest, is the null itself interesting?
Yes, **conditionally**. The null is interesting because:
1. the policy was sold on employment grounds;
2. the sector is economically large and politically salient;
3. the null is informative about a broader deregulation claim.

But the paper must work harder to make the null feel like learning rather than absence. Right now the phrase “precisely estimated null” helps, but the paper still risks reading like “we looked for an effect and didn’t find one.” To avoid that, the authors need to argue:

- employment was the principal public justification;
- there are reasons ex ante to expect an effect;
- the absence of an effect is itself discipline on how these policies operate.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

Bluntly: in its current form, this is **not yet an AER paper**. It feels more like a solid field-journal paper in agricultural, environmental, or public economics unless the framing and scope are expanded.

### What is the gap?

#### Mostly a framing problem
The science may be competent, but the paper is underselling the big question. It is currently framed as “the first DiD on Right-to-Farm laws.” That is not enough.

#### Also a scope problem
Employment alone may be too narrow, especially for a null-result paper. If the policy plausibly affects industry structure, local amenities, incumbent rents, or establishment dynamics more than employment, then focusing on employment only leaves the reader asking the obvious “so what did it do?” question.

#### Possibly a novelty problem
Not because anyone has done this exact policy already, but because the empirical template—state policy, staggered adoption, employment outcome, null result—is now familiar. To clear the AER bar, the paper needs either a bigger conceptual point or richer evidence on mechanism/incidence.

#### An ambition problem
Yes. The paper is careful and literate, but safe. It accepts the political claim at face value (“did it create jobs?”), knocks it down, and stops. A more ambitious paper would use this setting to say something larger about how deregulation operates in sectors with consolidation and externalities.

### The single most impactful piece of advice
**Reframe the paper around a broad economic question—whether deregulation in concentrated, pollution-intensive industries creates local jobs—and add at least one outcome or mechanism that shows who benefits if workers do not.**

That is the one thing. If the authors can only change one thing, they should stop selling this as a paper about Right-to-Farm laws per se and start selling it as evidence on the incidence of deregulation.
