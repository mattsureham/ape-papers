**Summary**

This paper investigates whether the municipal-level vote shares on the 2014 Swiss Mass Immigration Initiative—interpreted as public signals of local hostility toward immigrants—affected subsequent foreign resident settlement patterns. Using a continuous-treatment difference-in-differences with municipality and canton-by-year fixed effects, complemented by an RDD at the symbolic 50% threshold and a placebo referendum, the author finds no evidence that higher anti-immigration vote shares deterred foreign population growth. In fact, the coefficient on the interaction is positive, and event-study dynamics suggest pre-existing convergence rather than a causal deterrent effect.

**Essential Points**

1. **Parallel Trends and Identification**: Although the event study shows positive pre-trends, the main identifying assumption of the DID—parallel trends in the absence of the signal—is not convincingly defended. The positive pre-treatment coefficients suggest that municipalities with higher yes-vote shares were already on a trajectory of rising foreign shares, and the paper interprets this as evidence against the hypothesis. However, the signature of differential pre-trends undermines the DID estimate’s causal interpretation: the post-period positive coefficient may merely continue that pre-existing convergence. A formal pre-trend test or alternative approach that directly accounts for trend differences (e.g., flexible municipality-specific trends, synthetic controls, or modeling the endogenous vote share) is needed before dismissing the possibility that vote shares proxy for unobserved dynamics.

2. **Interpretation of the DID Coefficient**: Even if one accepts the identification strategy, the interpretation of the positive coefficient as “no deterrence” is tenuous. The paper’s argument is that hostility does not drive immigrants away; yet a positive coefficient implies that municipalities voting more hostile actually attracted more foreigners. Without a clear theoretical mechanism or further decomposition (e.g., examining inflows vs. outflows, or breaking down by country of origin), the empirical finding may simply reflect omitted economic covariates correlated with both vote share and immigrant attraction. More directly, how can we distinguish between “hostility has no effect” and “hostility is endogenous to prior immigrant inflows”? The paper should more fully explore the possibility that the vote share partly captures the past immigrant dynamics that also determine future settlement, in which case the DID coefficient is not measuring a signaling effect.

3. **External Validity of the RDD and Placebo Tests**: The sharp RDD focuses on the 50% cutoff, yet the policy implication relies on variation across the entire vote-share distribution. The paper interprets the null RDD as a backstop confirming the DID, but the RDD only applies to municipalities near the vote margin, which are likely large, urban, and demographically distinct. Likewise, the placebo using the FABI referendum yields a negative coefficient, but that may simply capture rural-urban differences unrelated to the identification with MEI. Both robustness checks should be more tightly linked to the signal interpretation—e.g., by showing that the MEI vote share effect is robust within urban/rural strata, or by conducting heterogeneity analyses that isolate the signaling interpretation (e.g., controlling for housing markets, labor demand shocks, and actual immigration quotas or sectoral flows). Without this, the policy claims about referendums being “cheap talk” remain under-supported.

**Suggestions**

- **Strengthen Identification via Trend Controls**: Given the event-study evidence of differential pre-trends, consider richer controls for time-varying confounders, such as municipality-level linear trends, interactions of economic covariates (unemployment, housing prices, firm counts) with year dummies, or even using the 2010–2013 trajectories of foreign share growth to reweight observations (e.g., synthetic control or inverse probability weighting). If the positive post-vote coefficient remains after accounting for these trends, the null interpretation strengthens.

- **Instrument or Lag Vote Shares**: The vote share likely reflects prior exposure to foreign residents. One strategy is to instrument the 2014 vote with pre-referendum proxies (e.g., historical voting patterns on immigration-related issues, exposure to Swiss People’s Party campaigns) and estimate the effect of the exogenous component of hostility. Alternatively, lag the vote share in the DID (e.g., interact 2014 vote share with post-2015 while conditioning on 2010–2013 trends) to isolate the “signal” aspect from the “outcome of past sorting.”

- **Decompose the Foreign Population Response**: The foreign population share conflates inflows and outflows, and it may respond slowly. Using flow data (if available), or splitting the outcome into foreign inflows, outflows, and net migration, could illuminate whether the lack of response is driven by new immigrants not caring about hostility, or by the foreign population being relatively stationary. Similarly, investigating heterogeneity by nationality (EU vs. non-EU), by sector (e.g., construction vs. services), or by income level could reveal whether certain groups are more sensitive to democratic signals.

- **Clarify the Mechanism of Signal Transmission**: The paper assumes that referendums generate widely observed municipal-level signals, but it would be useful to provide evidence on whether foreign residents (or prospective migrants) are aware of or pay attention to these results. Consider analyzing media coverage intensity, canton-level dissemination, or using Google Trends for MEI-related queries to proxy for signal salience. Alternatively, compare MEI vote shares to other municipal measures of hostility (e.g., SVP vote shares in national elections) to assess how specific the MEI signal is.

- **Better Alignment Between Robustness Checks and Mechanism**: The FABI placebo is interesting, but the negative coefficient could reflect the fact that railway financing had a very different geographic constituency. Presenting balance tables of observable characteristics across MEI and FABI vote shares, or estimating FABI effects within subsamples that mimic the MEI distribution, would clarify the extent to which the placebo isolates “valence” versus “salience.” Similarly, the RDD at 50% might be more informative if supplemented with covariate balance tests around the cutoff and a “falsification” outcome (e.g., changes in Swiss resident share or local non-immigrant growth) to show the discontinuity is specific to foreigners.

- **Address the Role of Non-Implementation**: The paper mentions that the MEI never resulted in quotas, but it should more carefully articulate how this fact affects the interpretation. If immigrants perceived the vote as “cheap talk,” the null is expected; if, on the other hand, voters knew that implementation was unlikely, then the vote still sends a signal but is less costly. Including a placebo examining periods when the implications of the vote were clearer (e.g., after the 2016 compromise) could test whether signal strength matters. Likewise, exploring whether municipalities that later advocated for stricter limits actually saw different trends would enrich the interpretation.

- **Expand the Discussion on Policy Implications**: The conclusion states that referendums do not affect immigration flows, but the evidence mainly shows no effect of vote shares. It would be helpful to nuance the policy claims: is the key takeaway that democratic signals are weak relative to economic fundamentals, or that the MEI vote—given the lack of implementation—was not taken seriously by migrants? Clarifying this distinction will help place the findings within the broader literatures on direct democracy and immigration-related sorting.

In sum, while the analysis is well-structured and the negative result is interesting, the paper would benefit from deeper engagement with the identification challenges posed by pre-trends and potential endogeneity of vote shares, along with richer exploration of heterogeneity and mechanism. Addressing these points would make the negative finding more convincing and sharpen the contribution to the literature on immigration, attitudes, and residential sorting.
