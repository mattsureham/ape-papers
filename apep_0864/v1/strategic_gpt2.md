## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether publicly revealed anti-immigrant sentiment changes where immigrants choose to live. Using municipal vote shares from Switzerland’s 2014 Mass Immigration Initiative—where the national policy outcome was the same everywhere but local support for restriction varied widely—the paper studies whether immigrants avoided municipalities that signaled greater hostility, and finds essentially no detectable response.

A busy economist should care because the paper speaks to a broad question: do political expressions and social signals reallocate people even absent meaningful policy change? If the answer is no, that tempers stronger claims about the allocative consequences of democratic hostility and sharpens the distinction between preferences, policy, and behavior.

**Does the paper itself articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Mostly, but not optimally. The current introduction is competent and readable, but it is too “literature-gap” first and not “world question” first. It also spends too much of its early capital on the identification setup rather than on why the question is surprising and important. The better version would foreground the broader claim: societies often worry that anti-immigration politics scares immigrants away or redirects them; here is a setting where hostility was publicly measured at fine geographic scale without differential policy implementation; strikingly, it does not seem to matter.

**What the first two paragraphs should say instead:**

> Anti-immigration politics is often presumed to shape not only policy but also where immigrants choose to live. If a community publicly signals hostility—especially through a highly salient democratic vote—one might expect immigrants to avoid it, producing sorting across places even when formal policy remains unchanged. Whether political hostility itself reallocates people is an important but surprisingly unanswered question.
>
> Switzerland’s 2014 Mass Immigration Initiative offers an unusually clean test. The referendum narrowly passed nationally, so the formal policy environment was the same in every municipality, but municipal support ranged dramatically, publicly revealing local anti-immigration sentiment. Because the initiative’s core restrictions were never meaningfully implemented, municipal vote shares operate primarily as a signal of local hostility rather than differential policy exposure. I use this setting to ask a simple question: when places reveal anti-immigrant preferences, do immigrants go elsewhere? The answer is no.

That is the pitch. It is cleaner, world-facing, and more memorable.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution in one sentence:**  
The paper shows that highly visible local anti-immigration signals, as revealed by municipal referendum outcomes in Switzerland, did not meaningfully alter subsequent foreign residential sorting.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?
Only partly. The paper is differentiated in topic, but not yet in intellectual stakes. Right now, the contribution risks sounding like: “we take a referendum, interact vote share with post, and find no effect on immigrant population share.” That is not enough. The author needs to define the contribution more sharply relative to:

1. papers on immigration shaping attitudes,
2. papers on anti-immigrant politics affecting policy or labor outcomes,
3. papers on local sorting in response to amenities/policies,
4. papers on direct democracy as information revelation.

The novelty is not the method. It is the **reversal of direction**: from “immigration affects attitudes” to “revealed attitudes affect immigration.” That is a real contribution, but the paper needs to hit that point relentlessly and cleanly.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or as filling a gap in a literature?
It starts as a literature gap. That weakens it. The stronger framing is about the world:

- Do immigrants respond to hostile political environments?
- Are votes just talk, or do they change settlement patterns?
- How much of spatial allocation is driven by local social climate versus economic fundamentals?

Those are big questions. “The reverse channel remains unexplored” is not.

### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what’s new?
At present, maybe, but not confidently. They might say:

> “It’s a Swiss referendum paper on immigration and local sorting with a null result.”

That is too generic. The introduction should make them say instead:

> “It isolates local political hostility from local policy and shows that revealed hostility alone doesn’t redirect immigrant settlement.”

That is a much better sentence.

### What would make this contribution bigger?
Several possibilities:

1. **Use flow outcomes, not just stocks.**  
   The paper itself admits this. If the outcome were new immigrant arrivals, address changes, permit issuances, school enrollments of foreign-born children, or housing take-up by foreigners, the contribution would become much sharper. Stocks are a blunt object for a question about sorting.

2. **Open the black box of who might respond.**  
   The broad foreign-share outcome is too aggregated. The biggest version of this paper distinguishes:
   - newly arriving immigrants vs established residents,
   - EU vs non-EU,
   - high-skill vs low-skill,
   - nearby internal movers vs cross-border entrants.  
   If nobody responds even among those most likely to be mobile and informed, that is much more powerful.

3. **Say more explicitly what theory is being tested.**  
   The paper hints at Tiebout and signaling, but it needs a clearer conceptual distinction:
   - economic fundamentals drive location,
   - formal policy constraints drive location,
   - social/political hostility may or may not drive location.  
   This paper identifies the third margin. That should be the core conceptual contribution.

4. **Push the implication beyond Switzerland.**  
   Right now it reads very Swiss. The broader claim should be about the limits of political signaling as a sorting force.

- We know far less about whether politics changes immigration absent policy.

Right now, “Tiebout + immigration attitudes + direct democracy” is a bit mechanical. The more interesting conversation may be with work on whether people sort in response to hostile environments when those environments are publicly observable.

Not quite. It is having a respectable conversation, but not the highest-impact one.

The better conversation is not:

It is:

That is the right conversation.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Places reveal preferences about immigrants through politics. Many observers believe such signals matter: hostility should deter settlement, welcoming signals should attract it.

### Tension
But it is hard to know whether immigrants respond to hostility itself or to the policies that hostile places enact. Usually politics and policy move together, so we cannot separate the signal from the substance.

### Resolution
The Swiss referendum provides public local variation in anti-immigration sentiment under a common national policy environment, and the paper finds no meaningful redirection of foreign population toward less hostile municipalities.

### Implications
This suggests that political signals alone may be weaker sorting forces than commonly assumed, and that economic fundamentals or actual policy constraints matter more than publicly revealed local attitudes. It also implies asymmetry in the politics-immigration relationship: immigration may reshape politics more than politics reshapes immigrant location.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
Yes, **potentially**, but in its current form the arc is underexploited. The paper has the ingredients for a good story, but sometimes reads like a set of empirical exercises arranged around a null. The event study, RDD, placebo referendum, and robustness checks are all fine pieces, but the narrative that should bind them is:

> This paper isolates a pure hostility signal from policy, then asks whether that signal changes where immigrants live. It does not.

That story is there, but the draft does not drive it hard enough. Instead, it sometimes slips into “here is the setting, here is the design, here are several results.” The paper needs more tension and clearer intellectual payoff.

### If it is a collection of results looking for a story, what story should it be telling?
The story should be:

1. We often think anti-immigration politics affects immigrant behavior.
2. Usually that belief bundles together policy, enforcement, and social climate.
3. This referendum isolates social/political climate.
4. Even in an unusually salient setting, that signal did not redirect settlement.
5. Therefore, the allocative consequences of political hostility may be much smaller than commentators assume unless hostility translates into concrete policy or market behavior.

That is a much stronger story than “the reverse channel is unexplored.”

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?
I would lead with this:

> Swiss municipalities revealed dramatically different levels of anti-immigrant sentiment in a nationally salient referendum, but immigrants did not subsequently sort away from the more hostile places.

That is the dinner-party sentence.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
Some would lean in—but only if you present it exactly that way. If you lead with “continuous-treatment DiD exploiting municipal vote shares,” they will absolutely reach for their phones. The hook is the contrast between revealed hostility and no sorting response.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Almost certainly:

> “Why not? Did immigrants not know, not care, or not have enough margin to respond?”

That is the key follow-up, and right now the paper cannot answer it well. That is fine to a point—but the discussion section should do more to structure these possibilities and be explicit that the paper identifies the reduced-form irrelevance of the signal, not the exact reason.

Another likely follow-up:

> “Are you sure this isn’t just because you’re using stock population shares rather than inflows?”

That is a serious strategic issue, not merely a technical one. The paper must own this upfront.

### If the findings are null or modest: is the null interesting?
Yes, potentially. But the paper needs to work harder to make the null feel informative rather than merely unsuccessful.

The null is interesting because:
- the signal was public,
- the issue was salient,
- the local variation was large,
- the policy implementation was weak, helping isolate the signal,
- strong priors might predict some sorting response.

What the paper must avoid is the impression of:
> “We tested whether a referendum moved foreign population shares and found nothing.”

To make the null matter, the author should emphasize:
- why one might reasonably have expected an effect,
- why this setting is unusually favorable to detecting one,
- and what classes of theories the null rules out.

In other words: not “no effect,” but “even under unusually visible political hostility, signal-only effects appear limited.”

   Leave-one-canton-out, McCrary details, and perhaps some of the sensitivity discussion belong later or in the appendix. They currently occupy too much rhetorical space for a paper whose main problem is not credibility but importance.

   The discussion section should do more than list three explanations. It should connect the null to a conceptual framework:

   The current conclusion is competent but mostly summarizes. It should end on a sharper takeaway:
   - public hostility is not the same as behavioral exclusion,
   - political symbolism may have weaker allocative effects than we think,

### Is the paper front-loaded with the good stuff?
Somewhat, but not efficiently. The result is technically front-loaded, yet the actual hook is not. The paper needs to lead with the striking substantive fact, not the design.

Possibly the **placebo referendum** result, if it helps sharpen interpretation. But it needs better explanation. As written, it is interesting but also potentially distracting. If retained in the main text, it should be used to make a conceptual point about immigration-specific signaling rather than as one more robustness line.
