### 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
The paper asks whether immigrants avoid living in areas that publicly signal hostility through democratic votes. Using a 2014 Swiss referendum on immigration quotas—which passed nationally but never took effect—the author treats local vote shares as a "pure signal" of local sentiment to see if foreign residents subsequently sorted away from high "Yes" municipalities. The paper finds a precise null: immigrants do not seem to care about the "revealed hostility" of their neighbors, suggesting economic fundamentals (jobs, housing) far outweigh social signals in residential choice.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this clearly by the second paragraph. It successfully frames the 2014 referendum not as a policy change, but as a "natural experiment in revealed hostility." 

### 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**One-sentence contribution:** The paper demonstrates that publicly revealed anti-immigration sentiment has no detectable effect on the geographic sorting of immigrants, suggesting that the causal arrow between immigration and attitudes is strictly one-way (composition drives attitudes, but attitudes do not drive composition).

*   **Differentiation:** It is well-differentiated. While the "immigrant sorting" literature (following Tiebout) is vast, applying it to "revealed preference for hate/hostility" via a failed referendum is a clever twist.
*   **Framing:** It is framed as a question about the world (how do immigrants decide where to live?), which is a strength.
*   **AER Quality?** A smart economist would see this as more than "just another DiD." The use of a "failed" policy to isolate the *signal* from the *substance* is a high-level conceptual move common in top-tier journals.
*   **Making it bigger:** To truly clear the AER bar, the author needs to explore **information.** Do immigrants *know* the vote shares? If the "So What?" is that people ignore signals, we need to know if they ever saw the signal in the first place.

### 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
The paper sits at the intersection of Political Economy (Direct Democracy) and Urban Economics (Sorting).

*   **Closest Neighbors:** Card, Dustmann, and Preston (2012) on compositional amenities; Hainmueller and Hopkins (2014) on immigration attitudes; and the Tiebout (1956) sorting literature.
*   **Positioning:** The paper correctly "inverts" the standard Card/Dustmann question. Instead of "Does X change attitudes?", it asks "Do attitudes change X?". This is the right conversation.
*   **Missing Conversations:** The paper should speak more to the **Economics of Information.** If the signal is "Cheap Talk," is it because it's costless for the voter, or because it's invisible to the immigrant? Connecting to the literature on "Salience" (e.g., Chetty et al.) would broaden the appeal.

### 4. NARRATIVE ARC
*   **Setup:** Immigrants move to cities; locals get angry and vote.
*   **Tension:** We assume immigrants want to avoid hostile neighbors, which should lead to a "flight" from anti-immigrant towns.
*   **Resolution:** Immigrants don't move. The "signal" of the vote is ignored.
*   **Implications:** Voting is "cheap talk." It doesn't affect the real economy of residential patterns. 

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong. However, the "Resolution" is a null result. The author must work harder to prove this is a "Precise Null" that rules out meaningful effects, rather than just a "Zero Result" due to noise.

### 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
At a dinner party, you lead with: *"In Switzerland, they had a massive vote to kick out immigrants. Even in towns where 90% of people voted 'Yes,' the immigrants didn't leave. They didn't even slow down."*

*   **Reaction:** Economists will lean in. The follow-up will be: *"Did the immigrants even know about the vote?"* This is the paper's "Achilles' heel." If the immigrants are unaware of the local municipal vote share, the "null" is trivial.

### 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
*   **Front-loading:** The RDD at the 50% threshold is symbolically the most powerful result but feels like an afterthought. Move the RDD visualization (if it exists) to the front.
*   **Mechanism Section:** Section 6 (Discussion) is currently a list of guesses. The author should try to find data on "Search" or "Information"—perhaps Google Search Trends for the referendum results by region—to see if immigrants were even looking for this information.

### 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The gap is **Ambition.** Right now, it’s a very clean, very competent empirical note. To be an AER paper, it needs to be a definitive statement on the **limits of political signals.**

**Single most impactful advice:** Solve the "Information Problem." Conduct a small survey or use digital proxy data (search/social media) to show whether immigrants are actually aware of local political sentiment. If they are aware and *still* don't move, you have a blockbuster paper on the dominance of economic incentives over social utility.
