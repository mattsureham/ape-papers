## 1. Idea Fidelity

The paper is broadly faithful to the original manifest. It uses the 2014 Mass Immigration Initiative as a source of cross-municipality variation in publicly revealed anti-immigration sentiment, combines municipal referendum returns with BFS municipal population data by citizenship, and estimates a continuous-treatment DiD with municipality fixed effects. It also implements two elements envisioned in the manifest: an event-study/pre-trend assessment and an analysis around the 50% threshold.

That said, several parts of the original identification strategy are either only partially implemented or substantially altered. First, the manifest anticipated stronger attention to confounding by local labor demand and sectoral composition; the paper does not actually include municipal economic controls beyond canton-by-year fixed effects and pre-foreign-share interactions. Second, the manifest proposed a triple-difference or second-signal design using the 2020 Begrenzungsinitiative; this is absent, and its omission matters because the core interpretation hinges on referendum results acting as “signals” rather than just proxies for persistent local conditions. Third, the paper’s framing shifts from “effect of revealed hostility on foreign population changes” to a stronger conclusion that “residential sorting responds to economic fundamentals, not democratic signals,” but the empirical design does not directly observe movers, inflows, or location choice. So the paper follows the original idea, but it does not fully execute the strongest parts of the proposed identification strategy, and some conclusions reach beyond what the implemented design can support.

## 2. Summary

This paper asks whether municipal support for Switzerland’s 2014 anti-immigration referendum deterred foreign residential settlement. Using a municipality-year panel and a continuous-treatment DiD, the paper finds no evidence that more anti-immigration municipalities experienced lower foreign population growth after the vote; the threshold analysis around 50% also yields a null.

The topic is interesting and the setting is potentially attractive because the national policy outcome was common across municipalities while local vote shares varied substantially. However, the causal contribution is currently limited: the paper documents that municipal vote shares do not predict a discrete post-2014 decline in foreign population shares, but the evidence does not yet isolate a causal effect of the referendum signal itself from pre-existing trends and slow-moving local characteristics.

## 3. Essential Points

1. **The main DiD design does not credibly identify a causal effect of referendum “signals.”**  
   The event-study evidence shows non-parallel trends, and indeed the paper itself interprets the positive post coefficient as reflecting pre-existing convergence. Once that is the case, the DiD coefficient is not a causal estimate of the effect of revealed hostility. The paper should either (i) substantially revise the identification strategy, or (ii) recast the paper much more modestly as evidence against a large discontinuous break after the referendum rather than as a causal test of signaling effects.

2. **The outcome is too indirect for the paper’s behavioral claims.**  
   Municipal foreign population share is a stock outcome reflecting inflows, outflows, naturalizations, births, deaths, and baseline size effects. This is a noisy proxy for “residential sorting” or “immigrants avoiding hostile places.” The paper’s strongest claims require flow-based or composition-based evidence, or at least a decomposition showing that stock changes are informative about location choices.

3. **The RDD and placebo exercises do not do as much identification work as the paper claims.**  
   Crossing 50% locally did not change policy exposure, and it is not obvious that immigrants respond discretely to “majority yes” versus “majority no,” especially when municipal vote shares are only one component of local information. Similarly, the significant FABI placebo result cuts against a clean interpretation of referendum vote shares as issue-specific signals; it suggests these variables capture broader spatial structure. The paper needs a more careful discussion of what these tests can and cannot establish.

## 4. Suggestions

The paper has a promising setting and a potentially publishable descriptive fact, but it needs a sharper and more disciplined empirical strategy. My suggestions below are meant to help the authors turn an interesting idea into a coherent short paper.

First, I would strongly encourage the authors to **narrow the claim**. Right now the paper oscillates between three statements: (a) there is no causal effect of revealed hostility on foreign settlement; (b) municipal vote shares are not useful predictors of foreign population changes; and (c) immigrants sort on economic fundamentals rather than democratic signals. Only (b), and perhaps a weaker version of (a), is presently supported. Claim (c) is too strong because the paper does not model or measure the alternative margins of adjustment. A more defensible title and framing would emphasize the absence of a detectable discontinuous post-referendum change in foreign population stocks, rather than concluding that democratic signals do not matter.

Second, I think the paper should **rethink the empirical object of interest**. If the hypothesis is about deterrence or avoidance, the most natural outcomes are annual growth of the foreign population, inflow rates, net inflows, or changes in newly arrived foreign residents. If BFS allows breakdowns by years since arrival, age, nationality group, or permit type, those would be much closer to a sorting mechanism than the level foreign-share outcome. A municipality’s foreign share can rise simply because the Swiss denominator shrinks or because of prior migrant cohorts aging in place. Showing null effects on stocks is informative, but much less so than showing null effects on new arrivals or net foreign inflows.

Relatedly, the authors should **decompose the stock outcome mechanically** if direct flow data are unavailable. For example: do municipalities with high yes-vote shares see differential changes in foreign headcounts rather than shares? What about log foreign population, foreign population growth, and foreign share holding total population fixed? The appendix reports log foreign population, but this should be integrated into the main text, not buried. It would also help to show whether the results differ by municipality size, since tiny municipalities can generate volatile shares.

Third, the paper would benefit from **substantially richer controls for local economic trends**, especially because the central threat is that MEI support proxies for labor-market conditions, industrial structure, or housing-market trajectories. Canton-by-year fixed effects are useful, but they do not absorb within-canton differences in exposure to construction booms, tourism, border commuting, manufacturing cycles, or urban spillovers. At minimum, the authors should add pre-determined municipal characteristics interacted with post or year effects: baseline population, baseline employment structure, unemployment rate if available, distance to major labor markets, urban/peri-urban/rural classifications, and perhaps baseline foreign share bins rather than a single linear interaction. The paper’s interpretation currently relies too heavily on fixed effects to solve what is fundamentally a sorting-on-trends problem.

Fourth, I recommend **replacing the simple event-study discussion with a more formal strategy for non-parallel trends**. The paper cites Rambachan-Roth and says the bounds include zero, which is potentially valuable, but the presentation is too thin to carry the argument. What is the exact estimand under HonestDiD? Which post periods are averaged? What is the economic meaning of the allowed violations? More importantly, if the preferred interpretation is “there is no break at the referendum date,” then a model that explicitly estimates a trend break or level break would be more transparent than a standard DiD coefficient known to be contaminated by pre-trends. For instance, estimate municipality-specific pre-trends as a function of vote share and then test whether there is an additional post-2014 deviation from those extrapolated trends. That would better match the narrative already in the paper.

Fifth, the **RDD should be demoted to a complementary descriptive exercise** unless the authors can make the behavioral discontinuity much more credible. Since local passage at 50% had no local policy consequence, the identifying assumption is not the usual one of treatment assignment changing at the cutoff, but rather that “majority yes” itself is a salient social label. That is plausible, but it needs argument and evidence. Did media report municipalities as “yes” vs “no” rather than by exact vote share? Is there any reason immigrants would process the threshold discretely? If not, a local polynomial around 50% is not a particularly compelling causal design. I would present it as testing one specific salience hypothesis, not as a “sharp RDD” delivering quasi-experimental identification in the standard sense.

Sixth, the **placebo referendum result should be interpreted much more cautiously**. A significant negative FABI coefficient does not “confirm” that MEI is specifically about immigration attitudes. It could instead indicate that referendum vote shares are correlated with spatial structure in complex ways, and that issue-specific vote shares pick up different bundles of municipality characteristics. A stronger placebo design would be to examine several same-day or nearby referenda with varying ideological content and show where MEI fits in that distribution. If many unrelated vote shares also predict foreign-share dynamics, the signaling interpretation weakens. If only immigration-related votes do, that would be more persuasive.

Seventh, the paper should exploit the **2020 Begrenzungsinitiative**, as envisioned in the manifest. This is not just an extra robustness check; it could materially improve the design. If municipalities repeatedly reveal anti-immigration sentiment across distinct votes, then one can ask whether the 2014 vote had an effect above and beyond persistent local preferences, or whether changes around 2020 line up with renewed salience. A panel using both referenda could help distinguish stable municipality ideology from referendum-specific public signals. Even if the 2020 exercise remains descriptive, it would deepen the paper considerably.

Eighth, there is room for **heterogeneity analyses that speak directly to mechanism**. If the signaling story is right, one might expect stronger effects in places where municipal information is more visible or where choice sets are broader: municipalities in integrated housing markets, border areas, large metros, or linguistically mixed regions. One might also expect stronger responses for foreign groups more exposed to anti-immigration rhetoric—e.g., EU versus non-EU nationals, or recent arrivals versus long-settled foreigners—depending on data availability. The current appendix reports language-region heterogeneity, but it is not interpreted. If the effect is actually more positive in French-speaking areas, that is interesting and deserves discussion rather than a brief appendix table.

Ninth, the paper should **clean up some internal inconsistencies and coding/presentation issues**. There are multiple discrepancies in municipality counts and timing: 2,114 in the manifest, 2,098 in the paper, 2,302 BFS units, and some shifting references to pre-period length. The event-study notation is also confusing: it says “\(t-1 = 2013\)” in one place while the table uses “\(t-1\) [Reference]” and labels pre-periods in a way that does not line up cleanly with the 2014 vote date. These are fixable, but in a short paper they matter because readers need to understand exactly what variation identifies the estimates.

Tenth, the authors should consider a **more credible “null” paper structure**. A persuasive null-result paper needs especially transparent diagnostics. I would suggest: (1) show the raw cross-sectional relationship between vote share and pre-2014 foreign-share trends; (2) show the event-study graph prominently, not just a table; (3) estimate a trend-break model; (4) report magnitudes in terms of confidence intervals for economically meaningful deterrence effects; and (5) state clearly what the paper rules out. For example: “We can reject effects larger than X pp on annual foreign population growth for a one-SD increase in anti-immigration vote share.” That would make the contribution much sharper.

Finally, I think the paper can still make a contribution if it is framed as follows: Switzerland’s 2014 referendum generated highly visible local variation in anti-immigration sentiment, but municipal foreign population stocks do not exhibit a detectable negative break afterward, even near the majority threshold. That is an interesting empirical fact. But to be convincing as a causal paper on policy signals and sorting, the current version needs a more defensible identification strategy, outcomes closer to actual residential choice, and a more restrained interpretation of what the null evidence shows.
