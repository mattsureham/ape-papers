 **Referee Report: "Cheap Talk: Anti-Immigration Referendum Signals and Foreign Residential Sorting in Swiss Municipalities"**

1. **Idea Fidelity**

The paper largely pursues the empirical strategy outlined in the original manifest. The author executes the continuous-treatment difference-in-differences design with municipality and canton-by-year fixed effects, implements the placebo test using the FABI railway referendum, and conducts the event study analysis. However, two key elements from the manifest are omitted or substantially altered: (1) the promised triple-difference analysis using the 2020 Begrenzungsinitiative as a second signal is not included, and (2) the RDD analysis deviates from the pre-specified 48-52% close-vote band (266 municipalities) in favor of an MSE-optimal bandwidth of 8.7 percentage points (encompassing 990 municipalities). The manifest also suggested controlling for unemployment and sectoral composition to address labor demand confounds; while canton-by-year fixed effects absorb some of this variation, the paper does not explicitly condition on municipal-level labor market conditions as proposed.

2. **Summary**

This paper tests whether municipal-level anti-immigration referendum signals causally affect foreign residential sorting by exploiting Switzerland's 2014 Mass Immigration Initiative, where national policy was uniform but local vote shares varied from 19% to 94%. Despite a well-powered research design leveraging over 29,000 municipality-year observations, the author finds precisely estimated null effects: higher anti-immigration vote shares did not reduce foreign population growth, with the preferred specification yielding small positive coefficients driven by pre-existing convergence trends. The results suggest that democratic signals of hostility do not translate into allocative consequences for immigrant sorting, challenging informational theories of direct democracy and supporting Tiebout models where economic fundamentals dominate location decisions.

3. **Essential Points**

**Pre-trends and identification.** The event study reveals significant pre-treatment trends: municipalities that would later vote more strongly against immigration were already experiencing relative increases in foreign population shares prior to the 2014 referendum (coefficients decline from 0.0013 at $t-5$ to 0.0000 at $t-2$). This pattern violates the parallel trends assumption required for causal interpretation of the difference-in-differences estimates. While the author acknowledges this as "pre-existing convergence," the paper does not adequately address whether these trends reflect anticipation effects, omitted variable bias, or reverse causality (immigration shaping attitudes). The Rambachan-Roth sensitivity bounds are presented as reassurance, but under substantial pre-trend violations ($M > 0.5$), the confidence intervals would likely include meaningful negative effects, weakening the claim of a precise null.

**RDD specification and manipulation concerns.** The paper deviates from the manifest's promised analysis of the 48-52% close-vote band (266 municipalities), instead employing an MSE-optimal bandwidth of 8.7 percentage points that includes nearly 1,000 municipalities. This choice substantially changes the estimand from a "symbolically charged majority threshold" effect to a local average treatment effect over a much wider range of vote shares. Furthermore, the McCrary density test ($p = 0.40$) provides weak evidence against manipulation given the thin national margin (19,526 votes). With such high stakes, strategic behavior or targeted mobilization around the 50% threshold is plausible, and the density test may lack power to detect sorting into the "close vote" window. The paper should report results for the pre-specified narrow band and implement density tests using data from the 2011-2013 municipal elections as a placebo.

**Interpretation of the null mechanism.** The abstract concludes that "residential sorting responds to economic fundamentals, not democratic signals," but the paper provides no direct evidence on economic fundamentals (e.g., wages, housing costs, job vacancies) to support this strong claim. The interpretation hinges on three untested possibilities (immigrants don't observe votes, discount them, or perceive them as cheap talk), yet the paper does not survey immigrants or analyze search behavior to adjudicate between these mechanisms. Moreover, the consistently positive point estimates in the DiD specifications—while attributed to pre-trends—could mask offsetting effects where the signal deterred some immigrants while attracting others (e.g., high-skill immigrants who vote differently than low-skill immigrants), which would complicate the "cheap talk" interpretation.

4. **Suggestions**

**Develop the conceptual framework distinguishing signal from policy.** Given that the MEI was never implemented, the paper offers a clean test of "pure signaling." However, the theoretical implications of a null result would be sharper if the paper explicitly modeled the signal's informativeness. Consider incorporating a simple Bayesian framework where immigrants update beliefs about local hostility based on vote shares, but face switching costs or have priors that dominate the signal. This would help clarify why we might expect no response even if immigrants observe the votes (the "discounting" explanation).

**Implement the omitted triple-difference design.** The manifest proposed using the 2020 Begrenzungsinitiative as a second signal to strengthen identification. This design would test whether municipalities that sent a consistent anti-immigration signal in both 2014 and 2020 experienced differential foreign population changes compared to those where signals conflicted. This would help isolate the effect of sustained signaling from transient political expression and address concerns about pre-trends by leveraging changes in signal intensity over time.

**Test heterogeneous effects by immigrant nationality and skill level.** The MEI specifically targeted EU/EFTA immigration and free movement. The paper aggregates all foreign residents, but the theoretical prediction should be strongest for EU nationals who faced the threat of quota restrictions. Conversely, non-EU immigrants (who already faced strict quotas) should be less responsive to the MEI signal. Disaggregating the foreign population by nationality (EU vs. non-EU) and permit type (short-term vs. permanent) would provide a more nuanced test of the signaling mechanism and could explain the null result if compositional changes offset quantity changes.

**Address spatial correlation and general equilibrium effects.** Municipalities are embedded in labor market regions (travel-to-work areas). If immigrants avoid high yes-vote municipalities but sort into neighboring low yes-vote municipalities within the same commuting zone, the canton-by-year fixed effects may over-control by absorbing relevant spatial variation. Consider estimating the model with labor-market-region-by-year fixed effects or testing for spatial spillovers using a spatial Durbin model. Additionally, if immigrants are mobile but housing supply is inelastic in urban centers (which voted "no"), general equilibrium effects could mask individual-level deterrence. Analyzing inflows versus outflows separately, or using individual-level geocoded data from the Swiss Labour Force Survey, would clarify whether the null reflects true indifference or compositional offsetting.

**Strengthen the RDD analysis.** Report results for the pre-specified 48-52% bandwidth as a primary specification, with the MSE-optimal bandwidth as a robustness check. Implement placebo cutoff tests at 45% and 55% to verify that the null result at 50% is not mechanically driven by the bandwidth choice. Given the potential for manipulation, conduct the density test using the "running variable" of vote share relative to the municipality's historical SVP support baseline, rather than just the raw vote share distribution.

**Reframe the contribution given the pre-trends.** The finding that anti-immigration vote shares are positively correlated with subsequent foreign population growth—driven by pre-existing convergence—is itself noteworthy. It suggests that rapid immigration causes anti-immigration voting (the canonical "immigration $\to$ attitudes" channel), but this reaction does not slow subsequent inflows. The paper could more prominently position this as evidence for one-directional causality in the immigration-attitudes relationship, using the referendum as a validation that attitudes are responsive to stocks but do not determine flows.

**Validate the assumption of signal awareness.** To support the claim that the referendum represented a "costly signal," provide evidence that the vote was actually salient to immigrants. This could be done using Google Trends data for search terms related to the MEI by canton, or by analyzing residence permit applications in the months immediately following the vote (Q1 2014) to test for immediate deterrence effects. If immigrants did not know about the municipal results, the null finding says less about the power of democratic signals and more about information frictions in housing search.
