## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
This paper examines how the move from direct-democratic "ballot-box" naturalization (where neighbors vote on applicants) to administrative bureaucrat-led procedures affects citizenship acquisition. Exploiting a 2003 Swiss Federal Court ruling that banned the former as unconstitutional, the author finds that removing the ballot increased naturalization rates by roughly 26%, with the effect concentrated in small municipalities where personal gatekeeping was most intense. A smart economist cares because this identifies a "procedural discrimination tax"—showing that institutional design, not just underlying social attitudes, is a binding constraint on immigrant incorporation.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this well in the first two paragraphs. It starts with a vivid anecdote (the Turkish carpenter) and immediately pivots to the causal question: did removing the ballot actually increase naturalization? It clearly sets up the tension between "attitudes" vs. "institutions." No rewrite is needed; the hook is strong.

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**Contribution:** The paper identifies the equilibrium impact of procedural reform on aggregate naturalization flows, moving beyond conditional rejection rates to quantify how institutional gatekeeping suppresses the total stock of new citizens.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It is clearly differentiated from Hainmueller & Hangartner (2013). While those authors showed *that* discrimination happens in the booth, this paper shows *how much* that booth suppressed the total number of Swiss citizens over two decades.
*   **Framing:** It is framed as a question about the WORLD (how do institutions shape belonging?) rather than just a gap in a literature.
*   **Clarity:** A smart economist would understand this immediately as an "institutional barriers to entry" story.
*   **How to make it bigger:** The contribution would be bolstered by data on **applicant composition**. If the author could show that the *type* of person naturalizing changed (e.g., more "disfavored" nationalities), the "procedural tax" story would move from a volume metric to a welfare/equity metric.

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
The paper sits at the intersection of Political Economy (Direct Democracy) and Labor/Migration Economics.

*   **Closest Neighbors:** Hainmueller & Hangartner (2013, 2015) on Swiss discrimination; Goldin & Rouse (2000) on blind auditions; and the broad "Institutions Matter" literature (North, 1990).
*   **Strategy:** It correctly builds on the Hainmueller/Hangartner foundation. It should, however, link more aggressively to the **"Chilling Effects"** literature in law and economics.
*   **Niche vs. Broad:** It risks being seen as a "Swiss niche" paper. To reach the AER, it needs to frame Switzerland as a "laboratory for institutional design" that speaks to universal bureaucratic hurdles (e.g., occupational licensing, zoning).
*   **Unexpected Connection:** It could connect to the literature on **Discretion vs. Rules** in bureaucracy. The ballot is "ultimate discretion"; the bureau is "rule-based."

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC
*   **Setup:** Switzerland has a unique, decentralized, and often discriminatory naturalization process.
*   **Tension:** Does the ballot actually stop people from becoming citizens, or is it just a mirror of unchangeable local xenophobia?
*   **Resolution:** The Court bans the ballot; naturalizations jump 26%; the "institutional" theory wins.
*   **Implications:** Courts can expand civil rights even when the majority is hostile, provided they can break the procedural bottleneck.

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong. It is not just a "results look for a story." The story—that institutions provide a "tax" independent of attitudes—is the star.

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
*   **The Lead Fact:** "When Switzerland banned neighbors from voting on their neighbors' citizenship, the number of new citizens jumped by a quarter overnight."
*   **Reaction:** Lean in. It’s a clean "natural experiment" on a high-stakes social outcome.
*   **Follow-up:** "Did it make the neighbors angrier?" or "Who were the new citizens—the ones the neighbors hated most?"

The result is not null, so it passes. The "So What?" is clear: Procedures are not neutral; they are policy levers for inclusion.

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
*   **Front-loading:** The paper is well front-loaded. The "Turkish carpenter" intro is exactly what AER readers need to stay awake.
*   **The Appendix:** The discussion on municipal mergers (Section 4) is necessary for a referee but should be compressed in the main text to keep the narrative moving.
*   **Heterogeneity:** Moving Figure 6 (size quartiles) earlier might help, as that is the strongest evidence for the mechanism.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The gap is **Ambition/Scope**. Currently, it feels like a very high-quality "short paper." To be a full AER lead, it needs to get closer to the "why." 

**The Single Most Impactful Piece of Advice:** Find a way to proxy for the **nationality** or **socioeconomic status** of the naturalized citizens. If you can show that the increase was driven by the "most discriminated against" groups (e.g., Balkan or Turkish names), you transform a paper about *counts* into a paper about *systemic justice and institutional bias*.
