**1. Idea Fidelity**

The paper diverges substantially from the manifest in both the empirical design and the data it exploits. The original idea emphasised a fine-grained spatial DiD using villages within 0–5 km of toll plazas and distant (20–50 km) matched controls, with SHRUG nightlights as an economic activity proxy, along with distance gradients and traffic-based treatment intensity. The submitted paper instead aggregates to the administrative district level, defines treatment simply as “district contains any plaza,” and relies entirely on Google Community Mobility Reports. The identification strategy lacks the spatial precision and matching discussed in the manifest, and no discussion is provided on the proposed distance gradient or the rich heterogeneity mechanisms (e.g., bypass presence, firm entry) that were central to the original idea. Because of these departures, the submitted paper only partially pursues the manifest’s research question and leaves several key elements of the originally proposed strategy unexplored.

**2. Summary**

The paper evaluates whether India’s February 2021 FASTag mandate—which made RFID-based electronic toll collection mandatory across 718 national highway toll plazas—generated local economic spillovers. Using a difference-in-differences setup with district-level Google Mobility data and a simple treatment indicator for districts containing a toll plaza, the author finds precisely estimated null (or slightly negative) effects on transit, workplace, and retail mobility, even after robustness checks and placebo tests. The conclusion is that the policy, though effective in reducing toll congestion, did not measurably reshape local economic activity at the district scale.

**3. Essential Points**

1. **Weak Treatment Definition and Identification:** Treating entire districts as “plaza” versus “non-plaza” obscures the localised nature of the mandate and introduces severe treatment misclassification. Many treated districts contain only one plaza, but the mobility data average over vast areas that also include non-highway regions. Moreover, the control group includes districts without plazas that may differ systematically in unobserved ways, and the paper relies solely on district and state×week fixed effects plus state-level clustering to identify the effect. Without any matching on observable district characteristics or explicit spatial comparisons (e.g., villages within few kilometres vs. far away), the parallel trends assumption is hard to evaluate and unlikely to hold. Please rethink the treatment definition, ideally at the sub-district or pixel level, and explicitly assess balance/pre-trends conditional on more comparable units.

2. **Outcome Measure Misalignment with Research Question:** Google Mobility measures reflect aggregate movement patterns (including non-highway trips) and are noisy proxies for local economic spillovers concentrated around toll plazas. These metrics are affected by large pandemic-related shocks, various policy responses, and differential smartphone penetration, which makes it difficult to isolate a strictly toll-related effect, particularly since the treatment is spatially thin (only a few kilometres around plazas). The paper should demonstrate that the mobility categories used are sensitive to localized congestion relief and not simply picking up general state-level recoveries or pandemic responses.

3. **Interpretation of a “Hard Null” Requires Stronger Mechanism Checks:** The paper’s central claim—that digitizing toll collection does not generate local economic spillovers—is only credible if alternative explanations (e.g., measurement dilution, treatment heterogeneity, slow adjustment) are ruled out. The current heterogeneity analysis (traffic tertiles) is noisy, and the positive coefficient in medium-traffic districts contradicts the predicted mechanism. Without precise distance gradients or more localized data, the null might simply reflect measurement error or averaging across treated and untreated areas. A clearer articulation of why the null is not just an artifact (e.g., via disaggregated outcomes or instrumental variation in treatment intensity) is necessary before drawing policy lessons.

**4. Suggestions**

- **Move Down to the Spatial Scale of Interest.** The key advantage of the FASTag mandate is its precise treatment geography (toll plazas) with clearly defined catchment areas. Aggregating to districts with 600–1,000 km² masks the treatment entirely. I strongly encourage you to try the village-level nightlights approach sketched in the manifest (SHRUG or VIIRS data), where you can define treated villages as those within, say, 0–5 km of a plaza and use a matched set of control villages 20–50 km away. Even if nightlights data are noisier, they are spatially aligned with the policy and directly measure local economic density. If nightlights are unavailable or infeasible, consider using gridded mobility/air-quality data, or at least leverage road-buffered sub-districts, to ensure the treated units are the areas you claim to analyze.

- **Improve the Identification by Exploiting Spatial Discontinuities.** The current strategy relies on within-state variation in plaza presence, but there is no discussion of the exhibited pre-trends or of how treated districts compare to controls on observables (e.g., pre-pandemic economic level, highway density, urbanization). Construct a convincing counterfactual by matching districts on historical mobility trends, highway connectivity, and COVID incidence, or by using a regression discontinuity (if plazas cluster near certain boundaries). Additionally, you can exploit treatment intensity (traffic capacity) along with distance-from-plaza to trace out the spatial attenuation of the policy. Event study plots of districts stratified by distance to the nearest plaza would help, not just aggregated before/after coefficients.

- **Revisit Outcome Measures and Provide Evidence That They Capture Spillovers.** Google Mobility is aggregate and likely conflates multiple channels. Provide diagnostics showing that mobility at transit stations indeed moves in response to highway friction shocks (e.g., ahead of FASTag, transit mobility is more sensitive in towns located near plazas). Alternatively, leverage other datasets mentioned in the manifest (nightlights, enterprise data, VAT receipts, freight movement) to triangulate the null. Even within the mobile data, explore more targeted categories—such as mobility in small towns located along highways, or separate metrics for freight corridors—if available.

- **Contextualize the Null with Power Calculations and Mechanism Validation.** A null result is meaningful only insofar as the study is powered to detect quantitatively relevant effects. Include a power analysis showing that the confidence interval (e.g., ±1.9 pp) excludes effects of interest (say, a 5–10 % increase in local activity). You can also calculate the expected economic impact based on plausible time savings. Moreover, test whether FASTag actually changed local congestion by looking at pre/post traffic volumes at plazas (available in the manifest data) or at travel time data from other sources; if there is no measurable change in congestion, the null is tautological.

- **Address Clustering and Inference Concerns Transparently.** With only 34 state clusters, state-level clustering may over-reject. Present wild bootstrap p-values or two-way clustering (state×week and district) to show the robustness of your inference. Given the wide geographic dispersal, consider multi-way clustering or spatial HAC to account for spillovers across nearby districts. Also, clarify why residential mobility is treated as a placebo when it is endogenous to many pandemic policies.

- **Expand the Discussion on Alternative Channels and Data Limitations.** The current discussion sketches three possible explanations but leaves the reader wanting more. Which of these is supported by the data? Could treated districts have more industrial activity that offset the expected boost? Are there any local policies (e.g., truck bans, road maintenance) that coincide with toll plazas? A more formal test—such as showing that toll plazas do not correlate with the timing of state-level lockdowns or that truck traffic actually increased post-FASTag—would strengthen the argument.

Finally, if you cannot implement the village-level or nightlights strategy, explicitly acknowledge the limitations of the district-level design and moderate your conclusions accordingly. The present framing suggests a policy-relevant null, but without alignments between treatment, outcomes, and scale, the null is difficult to interpret.
