## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether campaign donors influence government spending not just through individual "quid pro quo" contracts, but by fundamentally altering the institutional rules of procurement. Using a close-election RDD in Colombia, the author finds that electing a donor-funded mayor causes a 19-percentage-point shift in the entire municipality’s procurement portfolio away from competitive bidding and toward discretionary "direct-award" modalities.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this reasonably well by the third paragraph, but the first two paragraphs are a bit bogged down in the institutional specifics of Colombia. 

**The pitch the paper should have:**
"Does political money buy specific favors or the power to rewrite the rules? While the literature focuses on 'transactional' corruption—donors getting specific contracts—this paper identifies an 'institutional' channel. I show that donor-funded mayors systematically dismantle competitive procurement systems, shifting the entire municipality toward discretionary award modalities to create a 'capture premium' that benefits the donor class at scale."

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:** The paper identifies an "extensive margin" of political capture where donor influence leads to the systemic restructuring of procurement rules rather than just the steering of individual contracts.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It is well-differentiated from Gulzar et al. (2022). While Gulzar looks at *who* gets the money, this paper looks at *how* the money is given.
*   **Framing:** It is currently framed as "filling a gap" in the corruption literature. It would be stronger if framed as a question about **State Capacity**: Does campaign finance actively degrade the bureaucratic institutions of the state?
*   **Clarity:** A smart economist would get the "Direct Award vs. Competitive" distinction immediately.
*   **Growth:** To make this "bigger," the author needs to bridge the gap between the *shift in modality* and *welfare/efficiency*. Does this shift lead to higher prices? Worse quality? Or is it just "active waste" (Bandiera et al. 2009)?

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

*   **Closest Neighbors:** Gulzar et al. (2022) on Colombian donors; Baltrunaite (2020) on Lithuania; Bandiera, Prat, and Valletti (2009) on active/passive waste; and Decarolis et al. (2025) on procurement rules.
*   **Strategy:** The paper should **synthesize** these. It should position itself as the missing link between the *political science* of campaign finance and the *public economics* of procurement efficiency.
*   **Missing Conversations:** The paper needs to speak to the **"Rules vs. Discretion"** literature in regulation (e.g., Glaeser and Shleifer). Is discretion always bad, or is it only bad when the "Principal" (the mayor) is captured by donors?
*   **Right Conversation?** Yes, but it should lean harder into the "Institutional Decay" narrative.

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

*   **Setup:** Colombia has a legal loophole (direct awards) used for 80% of contracts.
*   **Tension:** Is this high rate of discretion an administrative necessity or a deliberate political choice to facilitate rent-seeking?
*   **Resolution:** Donor-funded mayors aggressively expand the use of these "exceptions" immediately upon taking office.
*   **Implications:** Auditing individual contracts is a "whack-a-mole" strategy; the real solution requires capping the aggregate share of discretionary spending.

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong. It moves logically from a macro-fact (80% direct awards) to a micro-mechanism (donor-funded winners).

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

**The Dinner Party Fact:** "In Colombia, electing a mayor who relies on donors doesn't just help those donors—it results in 1/5th of the city's entire budget being moved from open competition to secret, direct deals."

**Response:** People will lean in. The "19 percentage point" figure is massive. 

**The Follow-up:** "Does it actually cost the taxpayers more money, or are they just more efficient at getting things done?" This is the question the author *must* answer to secure an AER spot.

---

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

*   **Front-loading:** The distinction between "Transactional" and "Institutional" capture (page 2) is the paper's "killer app." This needs to be the North Star of the whole draft.
*   **Appendix:** The technical details of the SECOP II vs. SECOP I mapping should stay in the appendix. 
*   **Buried Treasure:** The value-weighted results (Column 2, Table 2) are smaller than the count-based results. This suggests the "restructuring" happens on many small contracts. This is a vital nuance about *how* capture works (death by a thousand cuts) and should be discussed more in the main text.

---

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The paper is currently a very high-quality "Applied Micro" paper (think *JAERE* or *AJEPOL*). To make it **AER**, it needs **Ambition**.

**The Gap:** The paper shows the *instrument* of capture (discretionary contracts) but not the *cost* of capture (higher prices/lower quality).

**Single Most Impactful Advice:** Link the "Capture Premium" to a measure of price or efficiency. If the author can show that these 19% additional discretionary contracts also come with a 10% price markup compared to the competitive ones, it becomes an undeniable AER paper about the real-world costs of political finance.
