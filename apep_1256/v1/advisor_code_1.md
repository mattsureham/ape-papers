**Idea Fidelity**

The paper faithfully implements the manifest’s original idea. The key research question—whether electing a donor-funded mayor shifts a Colombian municipality’s procurement composition toward discretionary modalities—is pursued using the proposed data sources (SECOP II, Cuentas Claras, CEDAE) and empirical strategy (close-election RDD with a difference-in-discontinuity panel). The focus on the extensive margin (procurement modality choice) rather than individual donor contracts, the construction of a donor-intensity treatment, and the emphasis on institutional capture are all present. One small deviation: the manifest suggested an index of donor funding (a share), but the paper converts this into a binary treatment based on which candidate is more donor-funded. This simplification deserves more discussion, as it transforms the continuous “capture premium” into a sharp-treatment comparison.

**Summary**

The paper studies whether electing a donor-funded mayor alters municipal procurement from competitive to discretionary modalities in Colombia. Using close elections from 2019 and matching procurement with campaign finance data, the author finds that municipalities where the higher-donor-funded candidate barely wins see an almost 19 percentage point rise in the share of discretionary procurement compared to those where that candidate barely loses. The analysis suggests that donor-dependent mayors reshape the institutional rules governing procurement, not just individual contracts.

**Essential Points**

1. **Justifying the Treatment Definition.** The treatment is defined as the binary event that the candidate with the higher donor share wins, yet the underlying research question centers on donor-funding intensity rather than arbitrary candidate ordering. The paper should clarify why the highest-donor candidate is the relevant comparison (rather than, say, a threshold donor share) and whether the treatment’s sharpness adequately captures variation in donor dependence. If the top-two candidates both have low donor shares, the treatment may not reflect meaningful institutional capture. Consider exploring a treatment that uses a continuous donor share difference or explicitly documenting that the “more donor-funded” winner is meaningfully different than the runner-up in terms of donor reliance.

2. **Local Randomization Assumption and Bandwidth Choice.** While the bandwidth sensitivity table is useful, the identification relies on the assumption that the high-donor candidate’s winning is as good as random near the zero margin. The paper should more concretely assess whether the donor characteristics themselves vary smoothly at the threshold—e.g., is the difference in donor share between the top-two candidates small near zero, or could it systematically correlate with underlying ability/talent? A balance table of predetermined candidate characteristics (beyond procurement) or placebo tests using other post-inauguration outcomes would bolster the claim that only donor intensity (and not some omitted candidate trait) drives the discontinuity. Additionally, given the relatively wide bandwidths used (up to ±30 pp), the paper needs to justify why the core estimate focuses on ±12 pp and show that the running variable’s density and potential covariates remain smooth within that range.

3. **Mechanism and Alternative Interpretations.** The paper interprets the jump in discretionary procurement as institutional capture driven by donor-funded mayors. However, this could also reflect differences in policy preferences or administrative capacity correlated with donor dependence (e.g., donors financing candidates promising targeted services requiring discretion). The paper should more explicitly rule out alternative explanations—perhaps by showing that other mayoral decisions (e.g., procurement volume, sectoral composition) remain unaffected, or by examining whether direct-award contracts disproportionately involve smaller values or specific sectors. Without such checks, the institutional-capture claim remains circumstantial.

**Suggestions**

1. **Clarify the Treatment and Donor Share Distribution.** Include a figure showing the distribution of donor shares for winning and losing candidates near the threshold. This would illustrate whether the “more donor-funded” definition captures substantively higher donor reliance and whether there is sufficient variation to detect effects. If feasible, consider a specification where treatment is defined by exceeding an external-donor-share cutoff (e.g., upper quartile) and explore whether close elections around that threshold produce similar results. Alternatively, allow the treatment effect to vary with the actual donor share of the winner (interact treatment with continuous donor share in the post-period). This would help demonstrate that the effect scales with the intensity of donor dependence rather than simply the ordinal ranking.

2. **Expand Validity Checks.** Beyond the McCrary test, it would strengthen the design to present density/continuity checks for other candidate-level covariates (e.g., incumbency status, party affiliation, prior political experience) around the threshold, ideally in an RD framework. Showing that parties or political experience don’t jump at the threshold would reduce concerns about omitted confounders. Additionally, run placebo regressions on outcomes that should not be affected by the mayor’s donor dependence, such as pre-treatment procurement in non-territorial sectors or municipal investment decisions in non-procurement areas, to further validate the local randomization.

3. **Explore Mechanisms Within Procurement.** The core interpretation rests on the idea that donor-funded mayors actively restructure the modality mix. Providing evidence on how the discretionary contracts differ under treated mayors would make the mechanism more tangible. For example, analyze whether the average contract value in discretionary modalities rises (suggesting that larger contracts are being reclassified) or whether the number of discretionary contracts increases disproportionately in certain sectors. Examine whether the ratio of single-bidder to multi-bidder processes in competitive modalities changes, which would signal broader procurement distortions. If data allow, test whether the increase in direct awards is concentrated in the immediate post-inauguration quarters (consistent with intentional restructuring) versus a gradual trend (which might reflect learning or capacity).

4. **Address Generalizability and Sample Selection.** The sample is restricted to municipalities with complete procurement and finance data and to close elections. It would be helpful to describe how these municipalities differ from the omitted ones (e.g., municipal size, region, institutional capacity). Provide a table comparing observable characteristics between sample and non-sample municipalities, even if only descriptively. This helps contextualize whether the estimated capture premium speaks to Colombia’s broader municipal landscape or only to a subset with better data/reporting.

5. **Discuss Policy Relevance More Precisely.** The discussion section could further elaborate on how anti-corruption institutions might monitor modality composition. For instance, what is the feasible size of an “anomalous discretionary share” threshold, and how might this interact with legal justifications for direct awards? Connecting the findings to existing Colombian institutions (like the Observatorio de Contratación or the Procuraduría) would make the policy implications more concrete. Likewise, consider whether the institutional shift persists beyond the mayor’s first year—does the discretionary share revert when donor dependence declines, or is there path dependence? Even if data limitations prevent a full analysis, outlining such dynamics would help policymakers understand the stability of the capture premium.

Overall, the paper addresses an important question with high-quality administrative data and a plausible identification strategy. Strengthening the treatment definition, augmenting validity checks, and fleshing out the mechanism would bolster the paper’s claims that donor-funded mayors induce institutional capture in procurement.
