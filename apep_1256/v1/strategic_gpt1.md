## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether campaign-finance dependence changes not just who gets public contracts, but how local governments buy in the first place. Using close mayoral elections in Colombia, it argues that municipalities where the more donor-funded candidate barely wins shift procurement toward more discretionary, less competitive award modalities—suggesting political finance can reshape procurement institutions, not merely individual transactions.

A busy economist should care because this is potentially a broader claim than the standard quid-pro-quo story: donor influence may operate at the level of institutional rules and administrative practice, not just contract allocation. If true, that matters for corruption, state capacity, public procurement, and campaign finance regulation.

**Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Pretty well, actually. The opening fact is strong, and the pivot from “individual deal-making” to “system-level restructuring” is the right move. The main weakness is that the introduction spends a bit too much time on Colombia’s legal categories before crisply stating the general question. It gets there, but not quite in the sharpest AER-intro form.

**What the first two paragraphs should say instead:**  

> Public procurement is one of the largest interfaces between the state and the private sector, and in many countries much of it occurs through legally permissible but weakly competitive procedures. The central question is whether this discretion is merely an administrative feature of the procurement system, or whether it is itself shaped by politics—specifically, by the financial dependence of elected officials on private donors.
>
> This paper studies whether donor-funded politicians change the composition of procurement once in office. Using close mayoral elections in Colombia, I show that when the more donor-funded candidate barely wins, municipalities shift procurement toward discretionary award mechanisms and away from more competitive modalities. The key contribution is to show that political finance may influence public spending not only by steering particular contracts to connected firms, but by restructuring the procurement environment for all contracts.

That version foregrounds the world question immediately and makes the paper feel larger than “a Colombian procurement paper.”

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:**  
The paper’s contribution is to argue that campaign-finance dependence affects procurement at the institutional margin—by increasing the use of discretionary procurement modalities—rather than only at the transaction margin of steering contracts to specific donors.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?
Somewhat, but not yet cleanly enough. The paper cites the transactional-corruption literature and repeatedly says “this is different because it’s institutional rather than transactional,” which is the right distinction. But the differentiation remains more asserted than established in the framing. A reader may still wonder: is this substantively a new phenomenon, or just a different outcome variable in the same donor-procurement nexus?

The closest concern is that an economist could summarize it as: “another paper showing donor-linked politicians distort procurement, except the outcome is direct-award share.” That is not fatal, but it is a warning sign. The paper needs to make clearer why procurement modality choice is a first-order object in its own right—one that changes the opportunity set for all future contracting, monitoring, and competition.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or as filling a gap in a LITERATURE?
Mostly the former, which is good. The best line in the paper is essentially: maybe the important corruption margin is not which firm gets the contract, but what kind of contract gets issued at all. That is a world question. The paper should lean even harder into that and spend less time saying “the literature has focused on X, I do Y.”

### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what's new here?
Yes, but with some risk of fuzziness. The good version is: “It shows donor-funded mayors increase the use of discretionary procurement mechanisms, so political capture may operate by changing procurement institutions, not just favoritism within a fixed system.”  
The bad version is: “It’s another close-election procurement paper, but with donor funding as the treatment.”

Right now the paper is in between those two summaries.

### What would make this contribution bigger?
A few concrete possibilities:

1. **Show that this is about the procurement regime, not just contract mix.**  
   If the paper could connect modality shifts to broader consequences—entry, concentration, contractor turnover, local price dispersion, or the share going to politically connected firms—it would feel much bigger. Right now “more discretionary modalities” is interesting, but still one step short of broader welfare or market-structure implications.

2. **Tie modality choice to downstream allocative consequences.**  
   The strongest expansion would be: donor-funded mayors increase discretionary procurement, which then reduces competition / changes vendor composition / concentrates spending / increases connected-firm wins. That would transform the paper from “institutional margin exists” to “institutional margin matters economically.”

3. **Clarify whether the paper is about donor dependence or capture capacity.**  
   External-donor share is a reasonable proxy, but conceptually the paper wants to say something about political dependence on private finance. A stronger framing would distinguish whether donor-funded candidates are more beholden, more politically professionalized, or more embedded in contractor networks.

4. **A more surprising comparison.**  
   If the paper could compare not just high-donor vs low-donor winners, but high-donor winners in legally high-discretion procurement environments versus lower-discretion environments, that would sharpen the institutional argument.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

### Closest neighbors
The nearest neighbors appear to be:

- **Baltrunaite et al. (2020)** on political contributions and procurement/favoritism in Lithuania.

- **Coviello and Mariniello (or related public procurement work)** on procurement institutions and discretion.
- Possibly also **Bandiera, Prat, and Valletti (2009)** / **Best et al. (2023)** as broader procurement-efficiency references.

### How should the paper position itself relative to those neighbors?
**Build on them, not attack them.**  
The right line is not “the prior literature got it wrong,” but “the prior literature documented favoritism within procurement; this paper asks whether politics also changes the procurement architecture itself.” That is additive and plausible.

The paper should explicitly say:
- Gulzar-type papers show **who gets contracts**.
- This paper studies **which contracting modalities are used**.
- Those are complementary margins, and the latter may be the upstream institutional channel that enables the former.

That positioning is much stronger than presenting the paper as merely introducing a new label, “capture premium.”

### Is the paper currently positioned too narrowly or too broadly?
A bit **too narrowly in evidence, too broadly in rhetoric**.

- **Too narrowly in evidence** because the empirical object is one country, one office, one election cycle, one procurement outcome.
- **Too broadly in rhetoric** because the paper sometimes sounds like it has established “institutional capture” in a sweeping sense, when what it has shown is a shift in procurement modalities.

The strategic sweet spot is: “This paper provides credible evidence on one institutional channel through which campaign finance can affect state functioning.” That is ambitious but not inflated.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of? What fields should it be speaking to?
It should speak more directly to:

1. **State capacity / bureaucratic quality**  
   The paper hints at “degradation of institutional procurement capacity,” but doesn’t fully engage that literature. If modality choice is an institutional outcome, this belongs partly in state capacity and political selection.

2. **Political selection and politician characteristics**  
   The treatment is not party but a candidate characteristic. That connects to literature on politician quality, business ties, wealth, criminal links, and financing structure.

3. **Industrial organization of procurement / competition design**  
   If the outcome is competitive versus discretionary modalities, this naturally connects to procurement design and market participation, not just corruption.

4. **Campaign finance and influence**  
   The paper should do more to place itself in the broader campaign finance literature, where the question is whether money buys policy, access, or rents. Here the claim is: money buys administrative discretion.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?
Mostly yes, but it may be having **too familiar** a conversation. Framed as “corruption and procurement in Colombia,” it is a solid field paper. Framed as “campaign finance changes the boundaries between rules-based and discretionary state action,” it becomes a much more central political-economy paper.

That unexpected literature bridge—campaign finance to administrative discretion/state capacity—is where the bigger conversation lies.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
In many settings, procurement law gives officials considerable latitude to use non-competitive procedures. Colombia is an extreme case, with a very high share of contracts awarded without competition.

### Tension
The standard view is that corruption in procurement works through favoritism in specific contracts. But if elected officials can also change the procurement mix itself, then political finance may distort the rules of allocation, not only individual decisions within those rules.

### Resolution
The paper finds that municipalities where the more donor-funded candidate barely wins use substantially more discretionary procurement after the election.

### Implications
Campaign finance may affect institutional functioning, not just distributive favoritism. Anti-corruption policy may need to monitor procurement composition and discretionary authority, not merely contract-level anomalies.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
Yes, more than many submissions. The ingredients are all there. But the arc is not yet fully disciplined. At moments the paper is tightly about a specific institutional margin; at other moments it drifts into very large claims about “capture” and “institutional restructuring” that the current evidence only partially supports.

So: **not a collection of unrelated results**, but **a story that slightly overreaches its evidence**.

### What story should it be telling?
The cleanest story is:

1. Procurement systems often contain legal discretion.
2. Political finance may shape how much that discretion gets used.
3. In Colombia, close elections show that more donor-funded mayors use more discretionary modalities.
4. Therefore, campaign finance can influence not only distributive outcomes but also the effective level of rule-based governance.

That is a strong story. It does not require inventing a grand new term or repeatedly insisting on “capture premium.” In fact, the label may be doing more harm than good by sounding a bit slogan-like relative to the measured evidence.

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

### What fact would you lead with?
“I’d lead with: when the more donor-funded candidate barely wins a mayoral election, the municipality shifts a meaningful chunk of procurement toward discretionary, non-competitive procedures.”

That is the headline fact.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
**Lean in initially.** Procurement, campaign finance, and institutional discretion is a live combination. But they will lean in only if the presenter immediately answers: why is procurement modality choice a first-order object? If that answer is weak, interest will fade.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Almost certainly:  
**“Do those extra discretionary contracts actually go to donors or politically connected firms?”**

That is the natural next question, and the paper itself acknowledges it cannot answer it. This is the biggest limitation in terms of excitement. The paper’s current answer is effectively: “not shown here, but this is a plausible upstream channel.” That is respectable, but it caps the paper’s punch.

A second likely question:
**“Is this really about corruption, or just about different procurement needs/administrative styles?”**

Again, not a reason to reject the framing, but a reminder that the world-level claim needs careful wording.

### If the findings are modest: is the result itself interesting?
Yes. Even if one treats the magnitudes cautiously, the result is still interesting because it shifts attention to the margin of discretionary authority. This is not a failed experiment. But the paper should make the case less through effect size adjectives and more through conceptual importance: changing the procurement rule mix is substantively different from changing award recipients within a fixed rule mix.
