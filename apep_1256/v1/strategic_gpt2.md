## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether campaign finance affects not just who gets public contracts, but how local governments buy in the first place. Using close mayoral elections in Colombia, it argues that municipalities where the more donor-funded candidate barely wins shift procurement toward more discretionary, less competitive contracting methods—suggesting political finance may change the institutional environment for contracting, not merely individual awards.

A busy economist should care because this is potentially a more important margin than classic quid-pro-quo contracting: if donor-backed politicians alter procurement rules or modality mix, the consequences extend beyond favored firms to the whole allocation technology of public spending.

**Does the paper articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Not quite. The opening is vivid, but it takes too long to get to the core question. The paper starts with a striking fact about noncompetitive procurement, then moves into the conventional literature, but the actual punchline—“campaign donors may reshape procurement institutions, not just contracts”—arrives only in paragraph three. That is the real hook and it should appear immediately.

**What the first two paragraphs should say instead:**  
> Public procurement is one of the main ways states convert tax revenue into goods and services, and competition in procurement is a central safeguard against favoritism and waste. Yet in many settings officials retain broad legal discretion to bypass competition. A first-order political economy question is whether that discretion is simply a feature of procurement law, or whether it depends on who wins office and how they were financed.
>
> This paper studies whether donor-funded politicians change the composition of procurement itself. In Colombian mayoral elections, municipalities where the more donor-funded candidate barely wins subsequently rely more on discretionary contracting. The key implication is that campaign finance may affect procurement at an institutional margin—reshaping the rules under which contracts are awarded—rather than only steering contracts to particular connected firms.

That is the pitch the paper should have. It leads with a world question, makes the mechanism legible, and gets to the novelty faster.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution in one sentence:**  
The paper claims that electing a more donor-funded mayor increases a municipality’s reliance on discretionary procurement, implying that campaign finance influences the institutional structure of public contracting rather than only the allocation of specific contracts.

### Evaluation

**Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?**  
Only partly. The paper differentiates itself most directly from work like Gulzar et al. on donors receiving contracts, but the distinction is still thinner than the author thinks. Right now the reader may hear: “previous papers show donations affect procurement outcomes; this paper shows they affect procurement modality.” That is a difference, but not yet obviously a big conceptual leap unless the author can show why modality composition is a distinct and more consequential object.

The phrase **“capture premium”** is meant to do that work, but currently it sounds more like branding than a demonstrated conceptual contribution. The paper needs to show why the extensive margin is economically and institutionally different, not just adjacent.

**Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a gap in a literature?**  
Mostly the former, which is good. The stronger framing is: **Do politically financed mayors alter the amount of discretion embedded in government procurement?** That is a world question. The paper should lean even harder into that and less into “the literature has focused on the intensive margin.”

**Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what’s new?**  
They could, but with some hesitation. The current likely summary is:  
> “It’s a close-election paper on campaign finance and procurement in Colombia; the novelty is that they study contracting modality rather than who gets the contract.”

That is better than “another DiD paper about X,” but it is still not at the level of “this changes how we think about corruption.” The introduction is close, but not there.

**What would make this contribution bigger? Be specific.**
1. **Connect modality changes to real consequences.**  
   The big missing piece is why economists should care about more discretionary procurement beyond corruption symbolism. If the paper could show changes in prices, concentration, bidder entry, contract fragmentation, supplier turnover, project composition, or ex post performance, the contribution would become much larger.

2. **Show whether the effect reflects institutional redesign versus compositional substitution.**  
   Right now it is unclear whether donor-funded mayors are truly changing rules, or just using existing legal categories differently at the margin. If the paper could show changes in formal procurement plans, use of exceptions, contract splitting, oversight flags, or persistence over the term, the “institutional restructuring” claim would be more convincing and more important.

3. **Tie the extensive margin to donor favoritism.**  
   The discussion admits it cannot directly show that the additional discretionary contracts go to donors. That is a major limitation for the current storytelling. If it cannot be shown directly, the framing should be more disciplined. If it can be shown, even imperfectly, the paper becomes far more consequential.

4. **Use value, not just counts, more centrally if possible.**  
   A shift in counts driven by many small contracts is less important than a shift in spending. If the world question is about the allocation technology of public spending, economists will want to know whether the money moved, not just the number of contracts.

### How should the paper position itself?
It should **build on** the donor-procurement literature, not “attack” it. The right move is:

- Prior work shows **who benefits** from political finance in procurement.
- This paper studies whether political finance also changes **how procurement is conducted**.
- Those are complements: allocation to connected firms and expansion of discretion may be part of the same equilibrium.

That is a cleaner and more generous positioning than “the literature misses the more consequential channel.”

### Is the paper positioned too narrowly or too broadly?
At present it is **slightly misbalanced**:
- On one hand, it is **too narrow** in being very tied to Colombian modality categories and the specific donor-share treatment.
- On the other hand, it is **too broad** in some claims about “institutional capture” and “degradation of institutional capacity,” which the current evidence does not yet fully sustain.

The right audience is broader than Colombia procurement specialists, but the paper needs a more careful bridge from local institutional detail to general political economy.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?
A few conversations are underdeveloped:

1. **State capacity and bureaucratic discretion.**  
   The paper should speak more directly to the literature on state capacity, administrative procedures, and bureaucratic control. If the claim is about changing the state’s mode of operation, that is bigger than corruption per se.

2. **Political selection / politician characteristics.**  
   The paper treats donor funding as the politician characteristic of interest, but there is a broader literature on what kinds of politicians change policy or institutions after close wins.

3. **Market design / procurement competition.**  
   The paper should engage more seriously with procurement as a market design problem. AER readers will care more if this is about competition, entry, and allocative efficiency—not just corruption labels.

4. **Campaign finance and governance quality.**  
   There is a wider literature on whether campaign finance distorts policy, regulation, and public spending composition. This paper belongs there too.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?
Not fully. Right now it is mainly talking to **corruption-and-procurement**. That is natural, but somewhat limiting. The potentially more impactful framing is:
- campaign finance alters **administrative discretion**,
- which affects **competition in public spending markets**,
- with implications for **state capacity** and **governance quality**.

That conversation is larger and more AER-relevant than “another corruption paper from a developing country with procurement data.”

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Public procurement law often permits discretion, and in Colombia discretionary procurement is pervasive. Existing work shows politicians may reward donors with contracts.

### Tension
The unresolved question is whether political finance changes only **who gets contracts** or also **how the procurement system operates**. If donor-funded politicians increase reliance on noncompetitive modalities, then campaign finance affects institutions, not just transactions.

### Resolution
Close-election comparisons suggest municipalities where the more donor-funded candidate barely wins later use more discretionary procurement.

### Implications
Campaign finance dependence may expand administrative discretion in public spending. If so, anti-corruption policy should target the structure of procurement systems, not only contract-level favoritism.

### Evaluation
There is a recognizable narrative arc, and the paper is better than many empirical papers on this dimension. But the arc is still somewhat **overstated relative to the evidence**.

The current story says:  
**donor-funded mayors restructure institutions.**

What the evidence more directly shows is:  
**donor-funded mayors are associated with greater use of discretionary contracting modalities.**

Those are related, but not identical. The paper repeatedly escalates from “modality share changed” to “institutional restructuring” and “capture,” and that leap risks making the story feel larger rhetorically than analytically.

So: this is **not** a collection of unrelated results. It does have a story. But the best version of the story is slightly narrower and sharper:

> Politically financed mayors increase the use of legally available discretion in procurement. This is an institutional margin of political influence that standard contract-level analyses miss.

That is strong enough. It does not require claiming a wholesale redesign of municipal procurement systems.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?
I would lead with:
> In close Colombian mayoral races, when the more donor-funded candidate barely wins, the municipality subsequently shifts a sizable share of contracts toward discretionary rather than competitive procurement.

That is the clearest and most interesting fact.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
**Some would lean in, but only if presented well.**  
Procurement and campaign finance are important topics, but they are crowded. The lean-in moment is not “there is corruption in procurement.” Everyone knows that. The lean-in moment is:
> “Political finance may change the amount of discretion embedded in government purchasing.”

That is more interesting.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Almost certainly:
1. **Do those extra discretionary contracts actually go to donors or politically connected firms?**
2. **Is this about meaningful spending or just small contracts?**
3. **Why is donor funding the right measure of capture rather than simply candidate type or local political organization?**

Those are exactly the questions the paper should anticipate in framing, even if not all can be answered in the current draft.

### If findings are modest or borderline, is the result itself interesting?
The result is interesting enough, but the current draft oversells precision. The paper itself acknowledges the estimates are economically large but statistically borderline. For editorial positioning, that is fine if the empirical fact is framed as **opening an important margin** rather than definitively settling a big debate. If the author insists on grand causal rhetoric about “institutional capture,” the borderline precision becomes more problematic. If instead the author says, “we provide evidence that campaign finance affects procurement through discretion, a margin largely overlooked,” then the contribution is much easier to accept.

This does **not** read like a failed experiment. It reads like a paper with a potentially good idea whose claims need to be calibrated and whose broader stakes need to be made more concrete.
