## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether changing **who bears tax-classification risk** can reshape the organization of work, even when statutory tax rates do not change. Using the UK’s IR35 reform, it argues that shifting status determination from contractors to hiring firms caused a large contraction in personal service companies, implying that compliance design—not just tax schedules—can reorganize labor markets.

A busy economist should care because this is potentially a broader point about **policy incidence through administrative design**: the state can change firm boundaries and employment relationships not by changing rates, but by reallocating legal risk.

Does the paper articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs? **Almost, but not quite.** The current opening leads with a dramatic fact, which is good, but then slips quickly into institutional detail. What is missing is a sharper statement of the general economic question and why the case is a vehicle for answering it. Right now the introduction reads as “a paper about IR35 and contractor companies.” It should read as “a paper about how compliance architecture changes organizational form.”

### The pitch the paper should have

“Can governments reorganize labor markets without changing tax rates—simply by changing who is legally responsible for compliance? This paper studies the UK’s IR35 reform, which shifted worker-classification decisions from contractors to client firms, and shows that this seemingly administrative change led to a large contraction in personal service companies. The broader lesson is that the allocation of compliance risk can materially affect organizational form, firm boundaries, and the structure of work.”

That should be in paragraph 1. Paragraph 2 can then explain why IR35 is an unusually clean setting: same tax law, same underlying classification concept, but a sharp reassignment of responsibility and liability.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:**  
The paper claims to show that reallocating tax-compliance risk from workers to firms—holding formal tax rules fixed—substantially changes the organizational form through which labor is supplied.

That is a real contribution in principle. But in its current form, it is **not yet clearly differentiated** from adjacent literatures or from the many papers that show “policy changed, legal form changed.” The paper says “novel mechanism: compliance risk allocation,” which is promising, but it does not yet fully establish how this differs from:

1. classic third-party reporting / enforcement papers,
2. tax-induced organizational form papers,
3. contractor classification / fissured workplace papers,
4. reduced-form labor-market responses to reclassification policy.

A smart economist reading the introduction could probably say:  
“it’s a DiD paper on the UK contractor tax reform showing fewer incorporated contractors.”  
That is not enough for AER. They need to be able to say:  
“it shows that **who makes the classification decision** can matter as much as the tax wedge itself, because firms systematically over-comply when they inherit liability.”

Right now the contribution is framed partly as filling a literature gap (“adds a novel mechanism”) and partly as a world question. The stronger framing is clearly the world question:

- How do legal responsibility and compliance liability shape organizational form?
- When does moving discretion from individuals to firms cause over-compliance and reorganization?

That framing is much stronger than “the literature has not studied compliance risk allocation.”

### Could the contribution be bigger?

Yes. The paper’s current contribution is somewhat narrow because the main outcome is **firm counts**. That is an eye-catching outcome, but still a partial one. To make the contribution feel bigger, the paper would benefit from one of the following expansions:

- **A different outcome variable:** show reallocation into payroll employment, umbrella firms, or some broader measure of work organization. Right now “companies dissolved” is vivid but incomplete.
- **A more direct mechanism:** evidence on blanket “inside IR35” determinations, client policies, or heterogeneity by exposure to large-firm clients would make the “risk allocation” mechanism much more distinctive.
- **A sharper comparison:** contrast sectors or geographies where the reform plausibly bit more because contracting relationships were more dependent on large clients, not just on PSC prevalence.
- **A bigger framing:** position this as a paper on how the state can alter the boundaries between employment and contracting via enforcement design, not as a paper narrowly about UK contractor taxation.

The simplest diagnosis: the paper has an interesting **idea**, but the current execution leaves it sounding like a sectoral policy evaluation rather than a general economics result.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Before this paper, we know that tax rates and enforcement matter for behavior; we also know that firms and workers adjust organizational form to regulation and tax incentives. But we know less about whether merely shifting legal responsibility for classification—without changing nominal tax rates—can reshape the way work is organized.

### Tension
IR35 offers a striking case: no headline tax rate change, but a huge disappearance of incorporated contractors. Is this just a sectoral trend, COVID, or tech-cycle noise? Or did reallocating compliance risk to firms trigger over-compliance and a sharp retreat from the contractor-company form?

### Resolution
The paper finds that after the private-sector reform, treated sectors saw a sizable decline in registered companies, with little offset in sole proprietorships and suggestive evidence of movement toward payroll employment or umbrella-company arrangements.

### Implications
The implication is that compliance architecture may be a first-order policy instrument. Governments can influence firm boundaries and labor-market organization not only through taxes and legal definitions, but through the assignment of liability and decision rights.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

**Serviceable, but not fully disciplined.** The raw materials are there. The issue is that the paper alternates between three stories:

1. “43,000 companies vanished” — dramatic descriptive claim  
2. “IR35 caused a decline in company counts” — policy evaluation claim  
3. “compliance risk allocation is a fundamental economic mechanism” — big conceptual claim

The paper needs to decide that (3) is the main story, and that (1) and (2) are evidence in service of it.

At present it sometimes feels like a collection of results looking for the biggest story available. The decomposition, umbrella-company discussion, and global policy extrapolation are all suggestive, but not yet tightly integrated into one narrative. The story it should be telling is:

- compliance responsibility changed,
- firms responded more conservatively than workers,
- that conservative shift killed a particular organizational form,
- therefore administrative design altered labor-market organization.

That is a coherent arc. Everything in the paper should point toward it.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?
“I can make 43,000 UK contractor companies disappear without changing a single tax rate—just by changing who is liable for deciding whether contractors count as employees.”

That is a very good dinner-party line.

### Would people lean in?
Yes—initially. The opening fact is strong. Economists will lean in because it sounds like a clean test of administrative design. But they will quickly ask whether this is really a general lesson or just a UK-specific contractor-regulation episode.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Probably one of these:

- “Did the work itself disappear, or just the corporate shell?”
- “Is this about tax enforcement, labor reclassification, or firm over-compliance?”
- “How much of this is real reorganization versus relabeling?”
- “Why should I think this speaks beyond IR35?”

Those follow-up questions are exactly where the paper is currently a bit thin strategically. The paper has one compelling fact; it needs to show why that fact changes our understanding of policy design more generally.

This is not a null paper, so the null-result discussion is less relevant. But the analog here is: if the main substantive interpretation is “organizational form changed but work may not have,” the paper has to explain why that is still economically important. On that front, it does okay, but could do more. For AER, “mere relabeling” is not enough unless the relabeling itself is shown to matter for taxation, protections, firm boundaries, or measured entrepreneurship.

   It is clear but somewhat overlong relative to the paper’s conceptual payoff. Much of it can be compressed or moved later. Readers need just enough IR35 to understand the shock.

2. **Front-load the conceptual point earlier.**

4. **Promote the decomposition if it is part of the claim.**

5. **Demote mechanical robustness discussion.**  
   The robustness section reads like a referee prebuttal. That is understandable, but editorially it weakens the paper’s voice. A top-field introduction should sell the insight, not litigate every conceivable concern up front.

6. **Rewrite the conclusion.**  
   The conclusion currently restates the headline. It should instead answer: what did we learn about policy design? under what conditions should this generalize? what should economists now think differently about contractor classification and administrative incidence?

### Is the good stuff front-loaded?
Partly. The very first sentence is strong. But the best idea—compliance-risk allocation—is not as front-loaded as it should be. The paper spends too much of its premium reader attention on institutional detail and empirical packaging.

### Are important results buried?
Yes, somewhat. The “no rise in sole proprietors” and “company share falls” findings matter for interpretation and should be given more prominence in the introduction, because they help move the paper from “counting disappearing shells” to “documenting a shift in organizational form.”

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

Right now, the gap is mostly a combination of **framing problem** and **scope problem**, with a touch of **ambition problem**.

- **Framing problem:** The core idea is better than the current presentation. The paper is not yet fully claiming the right thing in the cleanest way.
- **Scope problem:** The evidence is concentrated on one margin—registered company counts. For AER, that is probably not enough unless the broader organizational consequences are shown more directly.
- **Ambition problem:** The paper is competent and has a strong descriptive hook, but the current design is a bit safe. It stops just where readers will want the next layer: where did these contractors go, and what does that imply?

I do **not** think the main issue is novelty in the narrow sense. The setting is interesting. The issue is that the paper has not yet converted a striking institutional episode into a result of broad enough economic significance.

### What would excite the top 10 people in this field?

A version that more convincingly showed:

1. this is a clean case of **administrative incidence**;
2. the mechanism is **firm over-compliance under transferred liability**;
3. the response reflects a meaningful shift in the employment/contracting boundary, not just entry/exit noise in company registries;
4. the lesson travels to other worker-classification and enforcement settings.

**Reframe the paper around a broader question—how liability assignment changes organizational form—and then add one piece of evidence that traces where the dissolved PSCs went, so the paper is about labor-market reorganization rather than just disappearing companies.**
