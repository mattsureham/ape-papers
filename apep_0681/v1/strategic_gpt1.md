## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether changing **who bears tax-classification risk**—without changing statutory tax rates—can reshape how work is organized. Using the UK's IR35 reform, it argues that shifting responsibility for contractor tax status from workers to hiring firms caused a large contraction in personal service companies, especially in contractor-heavy sectors, implying that enforcement design can alter organizational form at scale.

A busy economist should care because this is potentially a paper about a broad question: **can administrative design, not just tax rates, rewire labor-market organization and firm boundaries?** That is an AER-type question if the paper really owns it.

Does the paper articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs? **Mostly, but not optimally.** The current opening has a strong headline fact, but it then slips quickly into institutional detail and “this paper studies IR35.” What is missing is a sharper statement of the general-interest economic question. Right now the intro reads like a good field-journal paper on UK contractor taxation. It needs to read like a paper about **the power of compliance architecture as policy**.

### The pitch the first two paragraphs should have

> Governments usually change behavior by changing tax rates, subsidies, or legal definitions. This paper shows that they can also change behavior by changing who bears compliance risk. I study a UK tax reform that left tax schedules unchanged but shifted responsibility for classifying contractors from workers to hiring firms.  
>  
> The result was a large reorganization of economic activity: tens of thousands of contractor companies disappeared. This matters beyond the UK and beyond contractor taxation, because it suggests that organizational form in labor markets is highly elastic to enforcement design. Policies that merely reassign liability can have effects comparable to major tax reforms.

That is the version that tells readers, immediately, why this is not just “a paper on IR35.”

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### Contribution in one sentence

The paper's contribution is to show that **reallocating tax-compliance liability from contractors to firms can substantially reduce use of the contractor-company form, even without changing tax rates or the legal definition of employment.**

### Is this clearly differentiated from the closest papers?

**Only partially.** The paper distinguishes itself from classic tax-compliance papers by saying this is about **risk allocation rather than third-party reporting or audit probability**, which is promising. But it does not yet sharply mark the frontier relative to the most relevant neighboring literatures:

1. tax enforcement / compliance design,
2. worker classification / fissured workplace,
3. organizational form and tax arbitrage,
4. gig/platform contractor regulation.

Right now the paper's differentiation is asserted, not fully demonstrated. A smart reader may still say: “So this is a reduced-form DiD showing UK contractor companies fell after IR35.” That is not enough.

### Is the contribution framed as a question about the world or a gap in a literature?

It is **mostly framed as a question about the world**, which is good: who decides classification matters. But parts of the intro drift into literature-gap language (“this paper adds a novel mechanism”) rather than keeping the focus on the substantive world question: **how much does compliance design shape labor-market organization?**

The latter is stronger and should dominate.

### Could a smart economist explain what's new after reading the introduction?

At present, they could probably say one of two things:

- the strong version: “It shows that shifting classification liability to firms caused a huge collapse in UK contractor companies, so compliance design matters for organizational form.”
- the weak version: “It’s another DiD on a tax reform hitting contractors.”

The paper is not safely on the strong side yet. The headline is there, but the framing does not consistently force the reader to see the broader claim.

### What would make the contribution bigger?

A few specific possibilities:

1. **Shift the core outcome from firm counts to labor-market reorganization.**  
   Right now the main fact is “43,000 companies dissolved.” That's vivid, but still one step removed from the economically bigger question: what happened to work relationships? payroll employment? umbrella companies? sectoral output? contractor rates? If the paper can show where these firms/workers went, the contribution gets much bigger.

2. **Make the mechanism more than “risk asymmetry” in words.**  
   The paper wants to say firms overcomply when liability shifts to them. That is the paper's intellectual engine. It needs stronger evidence tied to that mechanism—e.g., heterogeneity by exposure to large clients, public vs private client dependence, or sectors where blanket inside-IR35 decisions were more plausible.

3. **Exploit the 2017/2021 split more conceptually.**  
   Right now 2017 is just a smaller precursor. But strategically, the two-step reform could help the paper argue this is about where liability sits in different buyer environments. That would make it more than a one-off case study.

4. **Reframe from “company dissolution” to “the elasticity of legal form to enforcement incidence.”**  
   That is a much bigger concept. It links tax, labor, and industrial organization.

That is a strong synthesis. It makes the paper about **administrative incidence** in labor markets.

At present it is **a bit too narrowly positioned in UK tax-policy terms**, while occasionally making broad claims it cannot yet fully support. The sweet spot is broader than “UK IR35” but narrower than “all contractor regulation worldwide.”

**Almost, but not quite.**

The more interesting conversation may actually be with work on **the boundaries between employment, contracting, and intermediation**. If reframed well, this paper could connect tax enforcement to the structure of the modern labor market—a more surprising and AER-relevant bridge.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Before this paper, the world looks like this: contractor-heavy sectors make extensive use of personal service companies because those structures reduce tax liability and preserve flexible work arrangements. Standard economic instinct says organizational form responds to tax wedges, legal definitions, and production needs.

### Tension

The puzzle is that here **nothing about tax rates changed**, yet a huge number of companies disappeared. So can mere **reallocation of compliance responsibility**—from the worker to the hiring firm—generate large market-wide organizational change?

That is the paper's real tension, and it is a good one.

### Resolution

The paper finds that after the 2021 reform, company counts in high-contractor sectors fell sharply relative to controls, with no corresponding 2020 effect during the COVID delay year. This is interpreted as evidence that shifting compliance risk to firms led many contractors to abandon the PSC form.

### Implications

The implication is that enforcement architecture can be first-order: who bears liability affects not just reported tax, but the legal organization of work. This matters for tax policy, labor-market regulation, and the design of contractor-classification reforms.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

**Serviceable, but not fully disciplined.**  
There is a story here, but the paper sometimes feels like a collection of sensible sections rather than a tightly orchestrated narrative. The key issue is that the paper has **two possible stories** and has not fully chosen between them:

1. **Tax compliance story:** risk allocation changes compliance behavior.
2. **Labor-market organization story:** enforcement design changes legal form and employment structure.

The best version of the paper integrates them, but the current draft toggles between them. It should tell one clean story:

> Modern labor markets contain many margin calls between employment and contracting. This paper shows that administrative incidence—who must make and defend the classification decision—moves that boundary dramatically.

That is the story. Everything else should support it.

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

### What fact would I lead with?

“I have a paper showing that the UK eliminated roughly a fifth of contractor-company usage in exposed sectors without changing tax rates—just by making hiring firms, rather than contractors, responsible for classification.”

That is the dinner-party line.

### Would people lean in?

**Yes, initially.** The opening fact is strong. The phrase “without changing tax rates” buys attention.

But the next question comes fast:  
**“Did those workers become employees, move to umbrella firms, go informal, or just stop working through companies?”**

That follow-up is exactly where the current paper is too thin. The current answer is mostly inference from legal-form decomposition plus sector patterns. That may be enough for a field paper, but for AER-level excitement, the paper needs a more satisfying answer.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Probably one of these:

- “Is this really about enforcement design, or just about firms becoming more conservative?”
- “Where did the contractors go?”
- “Did total work change, or just the wrapper around it?”
- “Why is this not just a UK-specific institutional episode?”

Those are all framing questions, not referee questions, and the paper needs better front-end answers to them.

### If findings are modest or null

The findings are not null. The main effect is substantively large enough to be interesting. So the issue is not whether there is a result; the issue is whether the result is **economically deep enough**. On current draft, it risks feeling like “a large change in registry counts.” The paper must persuade readers that this is a meaningful reorganization of labor-market institutions, not an accounting reshuffle.
