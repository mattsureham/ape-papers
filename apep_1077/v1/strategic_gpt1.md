## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether the recent wave of U.S. state child labor law rollbacks actually increased teenage employment. That question matters because these reforms were sold as removing binding barriers to work; if employment does not respond, then a highly salient deregulation campaign was targeting constraints that were not economically important.

The paper does articulate the basic question fairly clearly in the first two paragraphs. In fact, the opening is better than average: it starts with a concrete policy change, states the question directly, and tells the reader the answer quickly. But it then drifts into a somewhat overwritten conceptual label—“the protection illusion”—before it has fully persuaded the reader why this is a first-order economics question rather than an op-ed point about state politics.

What the first two paragraphs should say instead:

> Since 2022, a dozen U.S. states have weakened child labor restrictions by extending permissible hours, removing permit requirements, and lowering age thresholds for some jobs. These reforms were justified on the grounds that existing laws were keeping willing teenagers out of work.
>
> This paper asks whether those laws were in fact binding. Using newly enacted rollbacks across states, I test whether teen employment rises in the sectors most exposed to child labor regulation. It does not: across states and reform types, weakening child labor protections has essentially no effect on teenage employment. The result suggests that an important contemporary deregulation effort targeted a margin that was not constraining labor market outcomes.

That pitch is sharper because it frames the contribution as a world question—were these laws binding?—rather than as a slogan.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:**  
The paper provides evidence that recent U.S. state-level child labor law rollbacks did not increase teenage employment, implying that these regulations were not binding on the employment margin in the affected service sectors.

This is reasonably clear, but not yet maximally differentiated from nearby work.

### Is it clearly differentiated from the closest papers?
Only partially. The paper gestures toward:
- historical work on child labor laws,
- developing-country child labor literature,
- broader labor regulation / employment protection papers.

But the actual contribution is more specific: this is a **contemporary U.S. policy paper on whether state-level child labor deregulation changes teen labor market outcomes**. The paper should be much more explicit that, to its knowledge, there is little or no causal evidence on the modern U.S. margin precisely because these laws had been stable for decades. Right now the introduction sounds a bit like “there is literature on child labor, and I am the latest paper,” rather than “this paper studies a newly opened policy margin that the literature could not study before.”

### World question or literature gap?
Mostly a world question, which is good. “Did these rollbacks increase teenage employment?” is the right question. The paper is strongest when framed as: policymakers acted as if these laws constrained work; did they? It is weaker when it lapses into “there is almost no empirical attention” style gap-filling.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new?
Yes, but not as crisply as they should. Right now they might say: “It’s a triple-difference paper on recent child labor law rollbacks and teen employment, and the effect is zero.” That is decent, but still a bit “another DiD paper about X.”

What you want them to say is: “It shows that the biggest recent U.S. rollback of child labor protections had no detectable employment payoff, which tells us those laws were not binding in the modern service-sector labor market.” That is a more memorable contribution because it speaks to the economics of regulation, not just policy evaluation.

### What would make the contribution bigger?
Several possibilities:

1. **Better outcome framing:**  
   Employment headcounts are probably not the most policy-relevant margin. The obvious first-order margin for these laws is **hours and timing of work**, especially evening and school-night work, not just whether someone is employed. If the laws mostly govern scheduling rather than extensive-margin employment, then a null employment effect is less surprising and less consequential. A paper that could show “no effect on employment, hours, or earnings” would be much bigger.

2. **Sharper mechanism between legal redundancy and economic non-bindingness:**  
   The discussion raises an important distinction—federal floor vs. employer/household behavior—but cannot really adjudicate it. If the paper could show that effects are absent even where federal law leaves room for state variation, or absent in periods/occupations where school constraints are weakest, the contribution becomes more structurally interesting.

3. **Heterogeneity by the actually exposed ages/jobs:**  
   The current age bin 14–18 is broad. If the action is really among 14–15-year-olds or in specific occupations newly opened by law, the paper risks testing the wrong margin. Even without changing design, a sharper framing around which legal changes should matter most would make the contribution more persuasive.

4. **Different framing:**  
   The biggest contribution is not “child labor laws don’t matter,” which is too broad and likely false. It is “recent state rollbacks of child labor restrictions did not expand teen employment in low-wage service sectors.” The narrower but more precise claim is actually stronger.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

### Closest neighbors
The paper’s closest intellectual neighbors are probably:

1. **Carolyn Moehling (1999)** on the historical role of child labor legislation in the United States.
2. **Edmonds and Pavcnik (2005)** on child labor and trade / household incentives in developing-country contexts.
3. **Manacorda (2006)** on child labor regulation and labor market equilibrium effects.
4. **Edmonds (2008)** as a broad survey of child labor economics.
5. More distantly, **Acemoglu and Angrist (2001)** or the employment protection / regulation literature on when legal protections bite.

But that set is still unsatisfying, because the paper is really sitting at the intersection of:
- child labor,
- youth employment,
- labor regulation incidence / bindingness,
- contemporary U.S. state policy evaluation.

It should probably also be speaking to the literature on **teen labor supply, schooling constraints, and youth employment**, not only “child labor” in the historical or developing-country sense. As written, it feels oddly detached from the youth labor market literature.

### How should it position itself relative to those neighbors?
**Build on and redirect**, not attack.

- Build on Moehling by saying: the historical literature asks whether child labor laws accelerated long-run declines in child labor; this paper studies whether modern U.S. state-level restrictions still bind in a labor market transformed by schooling, federal regulation, and employer norms.
- Build on Edmonds/Manacorda by saying: in poorer settings, child labor restrictions may bite because household poverty and labor demand make child work an active margin; the modern U.S. is a useful contrast case where the same legal category may be mostly redundant.
- Connect to labor regulation more broadly by emphasizing that the effect of deregulation depends on whether the regulated margin is actually active.

### Too narrow or too broad?
Currently **too broad in rhetoric, too narrow in empirical conversation**.

- Too broad because “protection illusion” and sweeping claims about both sides of the debate risk overselling what is a sector- and margin-specific result.
- Too narrow because the empirical conversation is framed almost entirely around child labor laws, when the result is probably of interest to those thinking about **regulatory redundancy**, **stacked legal constraints**, and **symbolic versus binding policy**.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?
It seems underconnected to:
- youth employment / teen labor market literature,
- work on hours constraints and school-work tradeoffs,
- regulatory overlap / federalism / layered regulation,
- perhaps political economy of symbolic deregulation.

The most promising unexpected conversation may be with the literature on **non-binding regulation and legal redundancy**. That is the paper’s real conceptual hook. “States changed the law, but not the equilibrium” is bigger than “another child labor paper.”

### Is it having the right conversation?
Not quite. It thinks it is in the child labor literature. It is actually more interesting as a paper about **whether highly visible labor regulation matters once other institutions already constrain behavior**. That is the conversation that could broaden its audience.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
A wave of states weakened child labor protections after decades of legal stability. Public debate presumed these restrictions constrained teen employment and that relaxing them would expand opportunities.

### Tension
But it is not obvious these laws were binding in the first place, given federal law, compulsory schooling, employer norms, and modern teen labor supply patterns. The puzzle is whether contemporary state child labor rules actually affect labor market outcomes.

### Resolution
They do not, at least on the employment margin in food service and retail. Rollbacks produce essentially no detectable increase in teen employment, hires, or separations.

### Implications
The immediate policy implication is that this deregulation did not deliver its stated labor market objective. The broader economic implication is that some labor regulations may be politically salient yet behaviorally redundant because other institutions already bind.

This is a decent narrative arc. The paper is not just a collection of results. It has a real story.

That said, the story is currently a little unstable because the paper wants to tell **two stories at once**:

1. a concrete policy-evaluation story about recent rollbacks; and
2. a more abstract theory-of-politics story about “the protection illusion.”

The first is solid. The second is more speculative than the evidence can bear. There is no direct evidence in the paper on beliefs, political attribution, or why the illusion persists. So the paper should tell a cleaner story:

- **Setup:** states rolled back child labor laws to increase youth work.
- **Tension:** it is unclear whether those laws still constrained employment.
- **Resolution:** they did not, at least in the affected service sectors.
- **Implication:** modern state child labor regulation appears redundant on this margin, likely because other legal and economic constraints dominate.

That is enough. The “illusion” framing is catchy, but currently out-runs the evidence.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?
“Twelve states weakened child labor laws in the last two years, and teen employment in the affected sectors did not move.”

That is a good opening fact. Economists would listen.

### Would people lean in?
Yes, initially. The topic is timely, politically salient, and unusual enough to stand out. The null is not inherently boring because the policy debate was loud and the result cuts against the premise of the reforms.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Almost certainly: **“Okay, but did hours worked change rather than employment?”**  
And then: **“Is this because federal law still binds, or because nobody wanted those jobs anyway?”**

Those are exactly the questions the current paper cannot fully answer, and they are the key reason the paper feels one notch below AER level. The main finding is interesting, but the immediate follow-up is more interesting than the answer currently provided.

### Is the null itself interesting?
Yes. This is not a failed experiment. A null result is informative here because:
- the reforms were politically important,
- the stakes were rhetorically high,
- the paper can rule out economically meaningful effects on employment,
- and the null speaks to the bindingness of regulation.

But the paper needs to do more work to sell the null as informative on the right margin. Right now the reader may think: “Maybe employment was just the wrong outcome.” Unless the paper confronts that more directly, the null risks feeling narrower than the title suggests.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

At present, this is **not yet an AER paper**, though it has the seed of one.

### What is the main gap?
Primarily a **scope/framing problem**, with some novelty risk.

- **Framing problem:** The science is organized around a timely and interesting question, but the paper oversells a modest empirical result with the “protection illusion” label instead of drawing out the bigger economic issue: when visible state labor regulations are redundant because stronger or more salient constraints already bind.
- **Scope problem:** The paper studies the extensive margin of employment, but the laws likely operate through hours, scheduling, occupation/task mix, and possibly safety. That makes the result feel incomplete.
- **Novelty problem:** If the contribution is merely “recent child labor law rollbacks didn’t affect teen employment,” that is interesting but maybe a field-journal result. To get to AER, the paper needs to teach us something more general about regulation, youth labor markets, or layered legal constraints.
- **Ambition problem:** The paper is competent but somewhat safe. It answers the easiest version of the question.

### What would excite the top 10 people in this field?
A paper that showed not just no employment effect, but something like:

- these reforms did not affect employment, hours, or earnings;
- because federal rules and school constraints already bind the relevant margin;
- and this pattern generalizes to a broader class of overlapping state labor regulations.

Or alternatively:
- no aggregate employment effect, but real reallocation across ages, hours, occupations, or wages—revealing what deregulation actually changes.

Right now it stops at the first-stage political claim: “the stated objective failed.” That is useful, but not enough.

### Single most impactful advice
**Reframe the paper around the economic question of regulatory redundancy and, if at all possible, add evidence on the actual margin these laws regulate—hours/scheduling or the youngest teens—so the null employment result becomes a broader lesson rather than a narrow non-finding.**
