## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a straightforward and policy-relevant question: when U.S. states recently weakened child labor laws, did teenage employment actually rise? The answer, in the paper’s telling, is no—suggesting that a politically salient wave of deregulation targeted constraints that were not, in practice, binding.

A busy economist should care because this is not really a paper about child labor law minutiae; it is a paper about whether visible labor regulations actually bite. If the laws did not affect employment even when rolled back, that matters for how we think about regulation, symbolic politics, and the margins on which labor markets adjust.

**Does the paper articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Mostly yes, but not optimally. The introduction gets to the question quickly, which is good. But it still reads a bit like a current-events policy note plus a design summary, rather than a broad economics question with a sharp empirical answer. The phrase “protection illusion” is rhetorically punchy, but it arrives before the paper has fully earned it, and risks sounding like branding in search of a contribution.

**What the first two paragraphs should say instead:**  
The opening should start with the broader question: when governments repeal labor protections, do markets move? Then narrow to the recent wave of child labor law rollbacks as a clean setting to test whether politically contentious regulations were binding. The key result—no employment response—should arrive immediately, and the broader implication should be that some highly visible regulations may be largely redundant because other constraints dominate.

**The pitch the paper should have:**

> Recent U.S. states have rolled back child labor protections on the premise that these rules suppress teenage work opportunities. This paper asks whether those protections were in fact binding constraints on teen employment. Using the recent rollback wave, I find no measurable increase in teenage employment in the sectors most directly affected, suggesting that state child labor restrictions had little independent bite once federal law, schooling requirements, employer practices, and teen labor supply decisions are taken into account.
>
> More broadly, the paper uses a salient deregulation episode to study when labor market regulation is real versus symbolic. The findings imply that repealing visible protections need not change market outcomes if those protections are redundant to stronger legal, institutional, or behavioral constraints.

4. **Different framing:**

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### What is the setup?
A wave of states rolled back child labor protections, with a public argument that these laws were suppressing teen employment and should be loosened to expand work opportunities.

### What is the tension?
The political fight presumes the laws mattered, but in a modern rich-country setting there are many reasons they may not be the operative constraint: federal law, schooling, employer norms, parental preferences, limited demand for very young workers.

### What is the resolution?
Teen employment in the most exposed sectors does not rise after the rollbacks.

### What are the implications?
Policymakers may be dismantling visible regulations that are economically redundant. More broadly, economists should be cautious about assuming that contested rules are binding just because political actors talk as if they are.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
It has the ingredients, but not yet a fully coherent arc. Right now it is **a result looking for its largest story**.

The paper’s strongest narrative is:
1. States claimed deregulation would expand teen work.
2. That claim implies the laws were actually constraining employment.
3. The paper tests that proposition directly.
4. It finds no effect.
5. Therefore the relevant barriers to teen work lie elsewhere, and the political salience of these laws overstated their economic importance.

What weakens the arc is the jump to “protection illusion,” which sounds more like a slogan than the culmination of evidence. The paper would be stronger if it told a disciplined story about **regulatory redundancy** and only then, if desired, discussed political salience.

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

### What fact would you lead with?
“Twelve states weakened child labor laws, and teenage employment didn’t budge.”

That is a clean and interesting fact.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
Initially, many would lean in. The topic has salience, and the null is surprising enough to provoke interest. But they will only stay engaged if the presenter immediately answers the next question: **why didn’t it matter?**

Without that, the paper risks feeling like a clever empirical exercise yielding a null on one margin.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Almost certainly:
- “So what was binding instead?”
or
- “Maybe employment didn’t change, but did hours, wages, injuries, or schooling change?”

That is the key. The paper’s current version invites exactly the questions it cannot fully answer.

### Is the null result itself interesting?
Yes, but only if framed correctly. A null in a politically charged deregulation episode can be quite valuable. It is not a failed experiment; it is informative if the paper convinces readers that:
1. a real policy change occurred,
2. the most likely margin was tested,
3. the estimated zero rules out meaningful effects,
4. and the null updates our beliefs about regulatory bite.

The paper mostly does (1)–(3). It needs to do (4) much better.
