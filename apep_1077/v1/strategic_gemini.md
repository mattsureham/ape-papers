### 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper investigates the labor market impact of the 2022–2024 wave of U.S. state-level child labor law rollbacks. Using a triple-difference design, it finds that relaxing these century-old protections had "precisely zero" effect on teenage employment, suggesting that these regulations were no longer binding. For economists, this is a striking "non-event" study that challenges the assumed trade-offs of labor market deregulation in a high-income, modern institutional context.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this pitch excellently. The first two paragraphs of the Introduction are a masterclass in AER-style framing: they start with a specific policy hook (Iowa/Arkansas), move to the broader trend, and immediately state the "simple question" and the "simple answer." No rewrite is necessary; the author knows exactly what the story is.

### 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**The Contribution:** The paper demonstrates that modern state-level child labor regulations are non-binding "protection illusions" whose removal fails to expand labor supply or demand due to overlapping federal floors and social norms.

*   **Differentiation:** It is well-differentiated. While the literature on the *origins* of child labor laws is vast (Moehling, Lleras-Muney), this is the first to rigorously evaluate the *reversal* of these laws in the 21st century.
*   **Framing:** It is framed as a question about the world (the "protection illusion") rather than a literature gap. This is strong.
*   **AER Quality:** A smart economist could explain this in one sentence: "The recent GOP push to relax child labor laws didn't actually increase teen work because those laws weren't what was stopping them."
*   **Making it Bigger:** To make the contribution "top-5" big, the paper needs to lean harder into the *why*. Is it the federal FLSA floor? Is it school-to-work norms? The "Dose-Response" result helps, but a more explicit test of the "overlapping constraints" theory would elevate it.

### 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

*   **Closest Neighbors:** Moehling (1999) on historical non-bindingness; Acemoglu & Angrist (2001) on the ADA (unintended consequences of protection); and the broader minimum wage/employment literature (Neumark & Wascher).
*   **Strategy:** The paper currently builds on Moehling to suggest a long-run continuity of non-bindingness. It should perhaps "attack" the political economy assumption—both the right (who thought this would solve labor shortages) and the left (who thought this would lead to Dickensian factories) were wrong.
*   **Missing Conversations:** The paper should speak more to the **Economics of Complexity/Overlapping Regulations**. In a world of federal laws, state laws, insurance mandates, and school requirements, which one actually bites?

### 4. NARRATIVE ARC

*   **Setup:** 100 years of "Progressive Era" consensus on child labor.
*   **Tension:** A sudden, aggressive wave of rollbacks in 12 states motivated by "labor shortages."
*   **Resolution:** A "precisely zero" result. The laws were a "protection illusion."
*   **Implications:** Deregulation on this margin is a "nothing-burger." Policy energy should shift to other constraints (transportation, education).

**Evaluation:** The arc is very strong. It’s not just a collection of coefficients; the "Protection Illusion" label provides the necessary "hook" to make a null result memorable.

### 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

*   **The Fact:** "Twelve states rolled back child labor laws to solve the labor shortage, and the effect on employment was literally zero."
*   **Reaction:** Lean in. It’s counter-intuitive to the political heat the topic generated.
*   **Follow-up:** "If the laws didn't matter, why did they fight so hard to change them?" (This points to a political economy extension).
*   **Null Result Value:** This is a classic "informative null." It closes a door on a specific policy prescription (deregulation) for teenage labor supply.

### 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

*   **Data Constraints:** The author acknowledges the QWI age-group bundling (14-18). This is the "Achilles heel." If 14-15 year olds saw a 10% increase but are only 1/5th of the 14-18 bin, the effect is 2%. The author should try to find CPS or ACS data to verify the age-specific response, even if the N is smaller.
*   **Front-loading:** The paper is well-structured. Table 2 is exactly where it needs to be.
*   **Appendix:** The "Standardized Effect Sizes" in Table 5 are helpful and should stay.

### 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The science is clean, the framing is sharp, and the topic is timely. The "distance" is mainly **ambition and scope**.

**The Gap:** To truly excite the AER audience, the paper needs to move from "This didn't happen" to "Here is the exact mechanism that prevented it from happening." Is it the FLSA? If we compare states where the rollback stayed above the FLSA floor vs. those that tried to go below it (and were blocked), can we see the "binding" constraint?

**Single Biggest Piece of Advice:** Investigate the **"Why"** more aggressively: Use the heterogeneity in *which* specific provisions were rolled back (hours vs. permits vs. ages) to map out the hierarchy of constraints, proving it was "economic non-bindingness" (norms/insurance) rather than just "federal redundancy."
