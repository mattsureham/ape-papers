### 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper investigates whether restricting retail operating hours destroys jobs by exploiting a unique, phased Sunday trading ban in Poland. Using a regional shift-share design and cross-country comparisons, the author finds a "precisely estimated null," suggesting that retailers adapt through schedule redistribution rather than layoffs.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this clearly, but it leads with the *policy* (Poland’s 2018 Act) rather than the *economic puzzle*. To be an AER paper, the pitch should prioritize the broader theory of labor demand and time-use constraints.

**The "AER" Pitch:** *How do labor markets respond to large-scale temporal shocks in operating capacity? Using the progressive tightening of Sunday trade in Poland, this paper tests whether legislated leisure mandates reduce total employment or merely redistribute labor hours across the week. I find that even a 93% reduction in Sunday availability has no detectable effect on headcount, suggesting high elasticity in consumer and firm temporal substitution.*

---

### 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**The Contribution:** The paper provides empirical evidence that trade-sector employment is resilient to significant operating-hour restrictions, likely due to inter-temporal substitution.

*   **Differentiation:** Most literature focuses on *deregulation* in the West. This looks at *tightening* in an emerging market.
*   **Framing:** Currently framed more as "filling a gap in the literature" regarding Poland. It needs to be framed as answering a question about **Firm Flexibility**.
*   **Clarity:** A smart economist would say: "It’s a Polish DiD that finds a null, but warns that the within-country variation is probably just picking up urban growth trends."
*   **Bigger Contribution:** The contribution would be much bigger if it moved beyond "headcount" (total employment). A "precisely estimated null" on headcount is only exciting if you can show the *margins that did move* (e.g., hours worked, shift intensity, or a surge in e-commerce employment).

---

### 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

*   **Closest Neighbors:** Goos & Manning (2007) on UK deregulation; Skuterud (2005) on Canada; Hamermesh (1999) on the timing of work.
*   **Strategy:** The paper should **synthesize** these. The current "attack" on the shift-share identification (Section 5.3) is actually the most "AER-like" part of the paper. It uses the Polish case to illustrate a broader methodological warning about Bartik instruments in high-growth contexts.
*   **Niche vs. Broad:** Currently too narrow. It feels like a "Policy Evaluation of Poland." It should speak to the **Future of Work** and **E-commerce**. The paper mentions Żabka’s loophole and e-commerce—these shouldn't be footnotes; they should be the core of the "Why the null?" story.

---

### 4. NARRATIVE ARC

*   **Setup:** European countries have a long history of Sabbatarian laws.
*   **Tension:** Unions want rest; retailers warn of a "retail apocalypse" and layoffs.
*   **Resolution:** Nothing happens to employment levels.
*   **Implications:** The "apocalypse" was overblown, or the adjustment happened in unobserved ways (hours/wages).

**Evaluation:** The narrative arc is "Serviceable." The tension is clear, but the resolution is a bit deflating because the author spends a lot of time admitting the identification might be compromised by secular trends. To fix this, the author needs to lean harder into the **Cross-Country** result as the "truth" and the **Within-Country** result as a "methodological lesson."

---

### 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

*   **The Lead Fact:** "Poland banned shopping on Sundays and retail employment didn't budge—but it's not because the ban failed; it's because the market moved the work to Monday."
*   **Reaction:** People might reach for their phones unless the author can explain *why* the retail associations were so wrong.
*   **Follow-up:** "Did hours per worker drop?" or "Did online shopping eat the difference?" If the paper can't answer these, the "So what?" remains weak.

---

### 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

*   **Front-load the Methodological Caution:** The fact that the public sector placebo shows a larger "effect" than the treatment is a brilliant, devastating result. Move this up. It makes the paper a "Methodological Warning" paper rather than just a "Null Policy" paper.
*   **Appendix:** The extended panel through 2022 is a mess due to COVID. Keep it in the appendix; it distracts from the clean 2018-2019 variation.
*   **Mechanism Section:** This needs to be expanded or moved to a "Discussion" that uses secondary data (even if imperfect) on retail sales or e-commerce trends in Poland.

---

### 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The gap is **Ambition**. Right now, this is a very competent "Applied Economics Letters" or "Journal of Policy Analysis and Management" paper.

To make it an **AER** paper:
The author must solve the "Composition Problem." NACE G–I is too broad (it includes "Transportation and Storage"). If the ban killed retail jobs but increased "Storage" jobs (because everyone moved to online shopping), the null is actually a massive structural shift masquerading as "no effect." 

**Single Biggest Advice:** Dig into the sub-sectoral composition—if you can show that **Retail** stayed flat while **Delivery/Logistics** stayed flat, the "Schedule Redistribution" story is a major win for labor demand theory.
