**Idea Fidelity**

The paper diverges from the original manifest. While the manifest proposed using Poland’s phased Sunday trading ban to identify effects on brick-and-mortar employment, shop counts, and e-commerce substitution via monthly GUS BDL data at the voivodeship level (and explicitly from 2015–2021), the submitted paper instead analyzes annual Eurostat NUTS-3 employment data (2013–2019) and focuses narrowly on trade-sector employment. The empirical strategy shifts from the manifest’s suggested “staggered dose-response” across voivodeships with monthly granularity to a cross-region continuous treatment design based on baseline trade shares. Key manifest elements—monthly frequency, GUS BDL data, secondary outcomes (e.g., shop counts or retail sales), and explicit tests for e-commerce substitution or other mechanisms—are not pursued. As a result, the paper only partially implements the original research idea.

**Summary**

The paper studies Poland’s 2018–2019 phased Sunday trading ban using Eurostat regional employment data. Exploiting variation in pre-reform trade-sector shares across 73 NUTS-3 regions and the ban’s temporal intensity, the author estimates a continuous treatment difference-in-differences and finds no evidence that the ban reduced trade-sector employment. The null result is supported by event-study pre-trends, cross-country comparisons with the Czech Republic and Slovakia, and placebo tests on other sectors.

**Essential Points**

1. **Parallel-trends assumption is violated and undermines identification.** The continuous treatment coefficient is positive, and the event study shows significant pre-treatment convergence—high-trade-share regions were already growing faster before the ban. That persistence of heterogeneous trends means the interpretation of the estimated coefficient as the causal effect of the ban is invalid. The placebo evidence reinforces this: the treatment predicts employment growth in unrelated sectors. The paper needs either a credible way to purge these differential trends (e.g., region-specific trends, synthetic controls, or alternative identification) or to acknowledge that the evidence cannot disentangle the ban from secular convergence and therefore cannot answer the stated research question with confidence.

2. **Lack of untreated or less-exposed comparison undermines causal leverage.** Because the policy was national and all regions were treated, identification rests solely on baseline trade shares. This shift-share design requires strong assumptions—and the manifest promised a staggered implementation with variation in “dose,” which would have provided cleaner leverage. Absent such variation, the paper should demonstrate that the baseline share is exogenous to unobservable shocks (e.g., via additional controls or robustness checks) or seek alternative sources of variation (e.g., exploiting exemptions, local enforcement differences, or neighboring countries as controls beyond aggregate comparison). Without that, it is difficult to interpret the estimated treatment effect as anything but a correlation.

3. **Empirical focus omits key mechanisms and outcomes promised in the proposal.** The paper omits the rich monthly GUS BDL data, secondary outcomes (shop closures, retail sales breakdowns), and mechanism checks (e-commerce substitution, firm size heterogeneity) outlined in the manifest. While this shift in scope might be acceptable if clearly justified, the paper does not explain why those richer data or outcomes were not used, nor whether more granular data would change the substantive conclusion. Without engaging these additional margins, the paper offers a limited view of the ban’s impact.

Given these issues, the paper does not yet provide the credible empirical answer it purports to deliver. The identification problem is critical—if unaddressed, it should lead to rejection.

**Suggestions**

1. **At least partially implement the original data strategy.** The manifest explicitly states that the GUS BDL API provides monthly trade-sector employment, shop counts, and retail sales for 16 voivodeships 2015–2021. Using that data would both increase power (1,344 observations versus 511) and allow the author to exploit within-year variation, observe abrupt changes around phase transitions, and test the robustness of the null. If the data are available, I strongly encourage the author to rework the analysis around voivodeship-month-level series. Monthly frequency would allow more credible event-study graphs around each policy phase and reduce concerns about confounding by other annual shocks.

2. **Explicitly model and address heterogeneous pre-trends.** The current event study already shows that high-trade-share regions were growing differently before the reform, yet the paper proceeds with a shift-share specification that assumes parallel trends. To salvage causal interpretation, consider (a) adding region-specific linear or higher-order trends interacted with the trade share to soak up differential growth; (b) implementing a synthetic control or a variant of the “generalized difference-in-differences” (e.g., \citealt{CallawaySantanna2021}) that can flexibly account for heterogeneous dynamics; or (c) using local projections to capture dynamic responses while controlling for pre-trend patterns. The placebo on public administration is a good sanity check, but a structural solution (e.g., de-trending at baseline) is needed before interpreting the main coefficient as causal.

3. **Leverage phase variation more carefully.** The manifest highlights the phased roll-out (2018, 2019, 2020). The current specification uses phase indicators but only two post-policy years (2018–2019) and omits Phase 3 to avoid COVID. If available, use the 2020 data pre-COVID (January) or find ways to model the policy intensity across phases, perhaps by focusing on the first two phases but also showing how the near-total ban in 2020 fits into the trend (e.g., with monthly data up to early 2020). Additionally, consider exploiting exemptions—e.g., the \.{Z}abka loophole or owner-operated stores—by constructing regional measures of exposure to exemption-heavy sectors. This would introduce additional cross-sectional variation and reduce reliance on the shift-share assumption.

4. **Engage with the promised mechanisms and secondary outcomes.** The paper’s conclusion about “schedule redistribution” and “regulatory arbitrage” is intuitive but unsupported empirically. Use the GUS BDL data or other sources to track shop counts (do exposed regions lose retail outlets?), hours worked (if available), or retail sales splits between food and non-food. Similarly, e-commerce data or proxy indicators (e.g., share of postal parcel shipments, platform sales if available) would help assess whether the ban accelerated online shopping—a key policy concern. Even if these data are coarser, documenting no effect on shop closures or small-business survival would strengthen the argument that employment adjusted along the extensive (hours) rather than intensive (headcount) margin.

5. **Clarify the counterfactual implied by the “intensity” measure.** The treatment variable normalizes to the share of Sundays closed, but it is unclear what the counterfactual trade employment trajectory is when ban intensity increases. Does the model assume that a region with zero trade share (i.e., no retailers) experiences no effect? Yes, but if those regions are dark or rural, they may also have different macro trends. To make the counterfactual clearer, consider reporting the implied effect for a representative region (e.g., mean trade share) and show how sensitive results are to alternative normalization (e.g., using trade employment in 2015, 2016). Additionally, provide a simple falsification: regress the same treatment on pre-2018 outcomes (a “lead” test). If the coefficient remains positive and significant, it reinforces the need to model trends more flexibly.

6. **Justify clustering choices and report alternative inference.** With 17 voivodeship clusters, cluster-robust standard errors are borderline. Consider using wild-cluster bootstrap (e.g., \citealt{CameronGelbachMiller2008}) and report those p-values. Also, since the treatment variable varies only at the region×time level through the ban intensity (national) and baseline shares, it may be informative to report “conley p-values” or other corrections to demonstrate robustness to few clusters and serial correlation.

7. **Discuss the practical significance of the null.** While a null result is informative, the paper should quantify the confidence bounds (e.g., what magnitude of employment loss per unit trade share is ruled out?). The standardized effect table in the appendix is a good start—perhaps integrate a discussion into the main text, explaining that the data rule out, say, a 1.5% drop in trade employment for high-exposure regions with 95% confidence. If the data lack precision, state that explicitly and discuss implications for policy debate.

8. **Explain data choices transparently.** Given the manifest’s emphasis on GUS data, the paper should (a) explain why Eurostat data were used instead; (b) discuss coverage, timing, and comparability issues; and (c) cite the manifest indirectly (if part of the same project) by noting that multiple data sources were considered. Transparency will reassure readers that the analysis is not a data “convenience sample” but deliberately chosen to suit the question.

By incorporating these suggestions—especially reintroducing the richer data, addressing pre-trends, and testing key mechanisms—the paper would be much better positioned to deliver a credible answer to whether Poland’s Sunday trading ban affected retail employment.
