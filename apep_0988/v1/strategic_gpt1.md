## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple policy question: when a country sharply restricts Sunday retail trading, do retail-sector jobs disappear, or do firms mostly reshuffle activity across days of the week? Using Poland’s phased Sunday trading ban, the paper argues that there is no detectable effect on aggregate trade-sector employment, suggesting that operating-hour restrictions may change schedules more than headcounts.

Why should a busy economist care? Because Sunday-closing laws are common, politically salient, and sit at the intersection of labor demand, regulation, and consumer behavior. More broadly, the paper is trying to say something general about whether restrictions on when firms can operate meaningfully change employment.

**Does the paper articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Only partly. The current opening is competent, but it spends too much of its scarce rhetorical capital on institutional and historical setup before making the larger economic question vivid. The paper gets to the empirical design quickly, but the pitch is still a bit “here is a policy episode and I estimate its effect” rather than “here is a first-order economic question this episode can answer.”

**What the first two paragraphs should say instead:**

> Governments often restrict firms not by banning what they sell or how much they can charge, but by limiting when they are allowed to operate. Do such operating-hour restrictions reduce employment, or do firms and consumers simply reallocate activity across time? This is an important and unresolved question for labor economics and industrial organization: if hours constraints mostly reshuffle demand, then some highly visible regulations may have much smaller employment consequences than both supporters and critics claim.
>
> Poland’s 2018–2020 Sunday trading ban provides an unusually sharp test. The law progressively eliminated most Sunday retail opening, creating a large and salient contraction in legal operating hours in a modern European economy. This paper asks whether that contraction reduced employment in the trade sector. Using regional employment data and complementary within-Poland and cross-country comparisons, I find no detectable decline in aggregate trade employment, consistent with adjustment through timing and organizational margins rather than headcount cuts.

That is the pitch the paper should have.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution in one sentence:**  
The paper’s contribution is to show that Poland’s phased Sunday retail ban did not produce detectable declines in aggregate trade-sector employment, suggesting that operating-hour restrictions may be absorbed through temporal reallocation rather than job destruction.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?
Not sharply enough. The paper cites the obvious Sunday-trading deregulation papers, but the differentiation is mostly “this is a ban, not a deregulation, and it’s Poland.” That is a start, not a full contribution. Right now, a reader could summarize it as: “another reduced-form paper on retail-hours regulation, with a null.”

To be sharper, the paper needs to distinguish itself on one of three dimensions:
1. **Policy direction**: tightening rather than liberalization.
2. **Economic environment**: post-transition, fast-growing, with modern chains and loophole-rich enforcement.
3. **Conceptual contribution**: operating-hour restrictions may affect timing, format, and margin of adjustment more than employment levels.

The third is the strongest, but currently underdeveloped.

### World question or literature gap?
It is trying to be about the world, which is good, but it repeatedly slips into literature-gap framing. The stronger framing is:

- weak: “there is little evidence on Sunday trading bans in Eastern Europe”
- strong: “we still do not know whether restricting legal operating hours actually destroys jobs, or simply redistributes work across time and formats”

The latter belongs in AER territory; the former sounds field-journal.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?
They could say: “It studies Poland’s Sunday ban and finds no employment effect.”  
That is understandable, but still not memorable. The risk is that they would also say: “so it’s a DiD paper on a policy episode with a null result.” That is exactly the strategic problem.

### What would make the contribution bigger?
Specific ways to enlarge it:

- **Different outcome variable:** The biggest missed opportunity is that employment is probably the wrong flagship outcome for this question. Hours worked, part-time vs full-time composition, wages, schedule stability, store entry/exit, online substitution, or sales reallocation across days would make the contribution much bigger.
- **Different mechanism:** Direct evidence of “schedule redistribution” is needed if that is the intended headline. Right now that mechanism is asserted, not shown.
- **Different comparison:** The paper could more forcefully compare labor-market effects to other margins: employment vs firm counts vs sectoral composition vs e-commerce growth.
- **Different framing:** Move from “effect of Poland’s Sunday ban” to “what margins do operating-hour regulations actually bite on?” That is the paper’s path to importance.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Governments often regulate store opening hours, and these restrictions are politically justified as worker protection but criticized as job-destroying.

### Tension
Theory is ambiguous and prior evidence from deregulation episodes is mixed; moreover, it is not obvious whether reducing legal opening time should reduce jobs or merely concentrate demand into other periods.

### Resolution
In Poland’s phased Sunday ban, the paper finds no detectable decline in aggregate trade-sector employment.

### Implications
If true, operating-hour restrictions may have much smaller employment consequences than headline debates suggest, with adjustment occurring through scheduling, business models, or substitution across channels rather than layoffs.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
**Serviceable, but not yet persuasive.** The paper does have a setup and a result, but the “resolution” is weak because the reader is repeatedly told that the main within-country design is confounded and the cross-country design is bounded. So the paper partly undermines its own climax. It ends up sounding like: “we tried to estimate this important effect, but the best design is contaminated, and the cleaner design is underpowered, so the answer is probably no big effect.” That is honest, but not powerful.

At moments the paper feels like a collection of sensible empirical exercises orbiting a null.

### What story should it be telling?
The paper should tell one of these two stories, and choose decisively:

1. **Economic story:**  
   “Large reductions in legal operating hours do not necessarily destroy jobs; firms absorb them through timing and organizational margins.”

2. **Methodological story:**  
   “National policy shocks combined with regional exposure shares can look informative but are easily confounded by differential growth, and placebo outcomes are essential.”

Right now it tries to do both. The first is more AER-relevant, but it requires stronger mechanism or richer outcomes. The second is cleaner, but much less likely to clear the AER bar unless the methodological lesson is much more general and more powerfully demonstrated.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?
“Poland eliminated most Sunday retail trading, and aggregate trade employment didn’t visibly fall.”

That is a decent opening fact. It has policy salience and some surprise value.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
Some would lean in initially. But the next sentence matters a lot. If the follow-up is “using regional trade-share exposure in a continuous DiD,” interest will drop quickly unless the paper immediately explains why the finding changes how we think about regulation. If instead the follow-up is “which suggests that restricting opening hours shifts activity across time rather than eliminating jobs,” interest stays higher.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Probably one of:
- “Okay, but what happened to hours, wages, part-time work, or store closures?”
- “Did spending move online?”
- “Is this really about retail, given your outcome includes transport and accommodation?”
- “Is the null informative, or is the data too coarse?”

Those follow-up questions reveal the paper’s strategic weakness: the headline naturally begs for mechanism and more retail-specific outcomes, and the paper currently cannot deliver them.

### If findings are null/modest, is the null itself interesting?
Potentially yes. A well-established null can matter a lot here because the policy debate is full of claims of layoffs. But the paper has not fully earned the null as an important result. To make the null interesting, the authors need to make the case that:
- the policy shock was economically large;
- employment was the central policy margin people cared about;
- the design is sufficiently informative to rule out effects of policy-relevant magnitude.

The paper begins to do this with “magnitude bounds,” which is smart. That section should be elevated much earlier and made more central. A null becomes publishable when it is really a **rejected disaster narrative**.

Be blunt: in its current form, this is **not yet an AER paper**. The core issue is not polish. It is that the paper has an interesting policy episode and a respectable null result, but not yet a big enough payoff.

1. **Mechanism-rich evidence that the adjustment occurred through hours/composition/channel substitution rather than jobs.**
