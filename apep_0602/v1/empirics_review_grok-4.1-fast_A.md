### 1. Idea Fidelity
The paper faithfully pursues the core original idea: implementing the first regression discontinuity design (RDD) at the federal 30% cohort default rate (CDR) threshold using College Scorecard CDR data merged to IPEDS outcomes for for-profit colleges, estimating causal effects on enrollment, completions, closures, and related behaviors under Title IV accountability pressure. Key elements like the imprecise-control argument (Lee 2008), manipulation tests (McCrary, donut-hole), bandwidth sensitivity (CCT-optimal), covariate balance, and placebo cutoffs are all executed as specified. Minor deviations include: (i) omission of the secondary 40% single-year cutoff RDD and fuzzy RDD instrumenting for eventual eligibility loss; (ii) narrower outcomes (no tuition changes, limited demographics/graduation rates beyond Pell share); and (iii) a slightly shifted sample period (2011–2019 vs. 2010–2020), but these do not undermine the primary research question or identification strategy.

### 2. Summary
This paper provides the first RDD evidence on the effects of crossing the federal 30% three-year CDR threshold—a regulatory cutoff threatening loss of Title IV aid (70–90% of for-profit college revenue)—on for-profit institutions' enrollment, completion rates, closures, and student composition. Using College Scorecard and IPEDS data for 1,880 institutions (2011–2019), it finds precisely estimated null effects on most outcomes, with a suggestive positive shift in Pell recipient share above the threshold, and no evidence of running variable manipulation. The null is attributed to the multi-year (three consecutive years) sanction rule diluting immediate accountability pressure.

### 3. Essential Points
**1. Identification mismatch with research question and regulatory design.** The paper frames the RDD as estimating the causal effect of "crossing the 30% threshold" (sharp design), but the binding sanction requires *three consecutive years* above 30%, with only immediate administrative consequences (monitoring, provisional certification) upon first crossing. This makes the treatment probabilistic/fuzzy rather than sharp, as the first-year discontinuity captures anticipation or minor responses, not the full "death sentence." The estimand does not cleanly match the policy question of accountability pressure's effects, undermining claims of testing the "cliff." Authors must clarify the treatment definition (e.g., switch to fuzzy RDD instrumenting first-crossing for eventual sanction probability) or use a dynamic/event-study RDD tracking multi-year persistence.

**2. Severe power limitations near the cutoff.** Bandwidth samples are tiny (e.g., 1,843 obs for enrollment but only 60 effective for Pell; closure base rate 0.4%), yielding wide confidence intervals (e.g., enrollment CI rules out only >30% drops) and unreliable point estimates (e.g., donut-hole flips sign). Summary stats show stark pretreatment differences (e.g., above-30% institutions 3x smaller), biasing narrow-bandwidth estimates. Authors must report minimum detectable effects (MDEs) for all outcomes, pool years explicitly if trends are smooth, or reject null claims without power calculations—current evidence cannot credibly rule out meaningful effects.

**3. Missing RD plots and density visualization.** No graphical evidence is provided (e.g., RD plots for outcomes, McCrary density), standard for AER:Insights RDD papers. Tabular results alone cannot validate smoothness or discontinuities; visuals are essential for credibility. Include them or the paper is incomplete.

### 4. Suggestions
The paper is well-written, concise, and novel in exploiting this understudied threshold—potentially publishable with fixes. Expand visuals first: Add \cref{fig:rd_plots} with binned scatterplots for each outcome (local linear fit, CCT bandwidth, 95% CIs), \cref{fig:density} for McCrary test (showing smooth density, perhaps contrasting with 25%/40% for context), and RD histograms. Use `rdplot` (Cattaneo et al.) for automation. These would vividly confirm the null and smoothness, strengthening the "clean" running variable claim.

Broaden outcomes to match the manifest and literature: Include tuition levels/changes (IPEDS `ic_ay`/`ic_py`), full demographics (e.g., % Black/Hispanic, age from `ef_a`/`ef_b`), graduation rates (`gr`/`gr200`), and loan/Pell recipient counts/shares (`sfa`). Test cream-skimming explicitly via changes in these (e.g., expected negative Pell/% minority if skimming). Report all in a main table with standardized effect sizes (already in appendix—move to main text). For closure, use survival analysis (e.g., Cox proportional hazards) or forward-looking windows (1/2/3/5 years) to capture delayed effects.

Refine robustness: (i) Implement fuzzy RDD using first-year crossing as IV for three-year persistence or actual sanction (track eligibility loss via IPEDS `hd` closure or Scorecard flags); (ii) Secondary RDD at 40% (single-year sanction, sharper bite) and 25% (monitoring), as promised—compare coefficients; (iii) Event-study RDD by year relative to first crossing (bin years t-2 to t+3); (iv) Cluster SEs at institution level (current appears observation-level); (v) Local quadratic specs in main table; (vi) Subsample by institution age/size (smaller ones may respond more). Pooling 2011–2019 assumes parallel trends—test with year interactions.

Address power explicitly: Compute MDEs (e.g., via `rdpower`) assuming 80% power/5% significance, report in notes/table (e.g., "CI rules out effects >0.2 SD for enrollment"). Restrict to larger institutions (e.g., CDR denominator >100) for precision. If power remains low, frame as "no evidence of large effects" rather than definitive null.

Deepen discussion: Quantify sanction probability (e.g., % of first-crossers sanctioned within 3 years, from historical data). Contrast with gainful employment (Cellini/Turner: immediate program-level threats work). Explore manipulation more: Test forbearance via auxiliary data (if available) or bunching at other points (e.g., 25%). Discuss for-profit heterogeneity (chains vs. independents, via IPEDS `hd` Carnegie). Policy angle: Simulate single-year vs. multi-year rule effects.

Minor polish: (i) Fix donut-hole table (sign flip unreliable due to small N—drop wide donuts); (ii) Clarify sample (FY2009–2019 in notes vs. 2011–2019 text); (iii) Add figures to appendix if space-constrained; (iv) Bibliography: Cite more RDD higher-ed (e.g., Dube et al. min wage inspiration); (v) Abstract: Tone down "cliff that didn't bite" (results tentative). With these, it fits AER:Insights perfectly—timely policy relevance, clean design, informative null.
