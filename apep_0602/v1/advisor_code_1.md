It uses the 30% cohort default rate (CDR) threshold as a running variable to implement an RDD, draws on College Scorecard CDR data merged with IPEDS for outcomes, focuses on the for-profit sector, and investigates enrollment, completions, closure, and Pell share as outcomes. The manuscript, however, stops short of engaging with the “fuzzy” interpretation the manifest outlined: although it mentions the three-year rule in the discussion, the estimation strategy treats the threshold as sharp, without exploiting the discontinuous jump in the probability of eventual Title IV loss once an institution surpasses 30% for the first time. **Summary**

This paper presents the first regression discontinuity analysis of the federal 30% CDR accountability threshold for for-profit colleges, merging College Scorecard CDRs with IPEDS outcomes over 2011–2019. Despite the extreme sanction tied to crossing the threshold—loss of Title IV eligibility—the estimated discontinuities in enrollment, completion rates, and closure are statistically null, while institutions above the threshold serve a higher Pell share. The author interprets the lack of effects as evidence that the three-consecutive-year rule dilutes the behavioral impact of the threshold.

**Essential Points**

1. **Sharp RDD interpretation ignores the staggered sanction structure.** The paper treats crossing 30% in a single year as the “treatment,” yet the rule only revokes Title IV eligibility after three consecutive years above the threshold. That means the treatment discontinuity at 30% is not clear-cut: surviving the first year above the cutoff merely initiates a countdown, and only repeated crossings trigger the “death sentence.” The current specification therefore captures the impact of a relatively mild signal rather than the sanction itself. The authors should consider explicitly modeling the multiyear rule—either by exploiting the discontinuity in the probability of eventual sanction (a fuzzy design) or by focusing on institutions that cross 30% for the third consecutive year—to clarify what causal effect is being estimated.

2. **Interpretation of null and manipulation tests needs more nuance.** The McCrary test and donut-hole specifications suggest no bunching, but the effective sample near the cutoff is small (only ~380 observations with CDR within the optimal bandwidth) and the running variable is reported annually with substantial noise. Given this limited density, the absence of a detectable discontinuity may simply reflect low power rather than the absence of manipulation. The paper should provide more rigorous sensitivity checks (e.g., plotting the density with confidence intervals, reporting power calculations for detecting plausible effect sizes) and be more cautious in interpreting the “smoothness” result as evidence that forbearance-pushing is ineffective.

3. **Outcome choice and inference are too coarse to capture institutional responses.** Enrollment, completions, and closure are broad outcomes whose changes may lag several years after a signal. The current paper uses annual institution-year data but does not exploit dynamic leads/lags or placebo months to ensure that the timing of the outcomes aligns with the hypothesized response window, nor does it distinguish between immediate behavioral changes (e.g., narrowing recruitment) versus longer-term effects (e.g., financial deterioration leading to closure). Furthermore, the completion rate is defined as completions divided by enrollment, which may mechanically move when low-risk students are excluded; without analyzing the absolute levels of completions or disaggregating by program, the paper cannot distinguish cream-skimming from quality improvement. These issues affect both interpretation and statistical power. The authors should either use more sensitive intermediate outcomes or justify why the current outcomes suffice, including a discussion of the timing of the response and how the estimation accounts for it.

**Suggestions**

1. **Clarify the treatment and explore a fuzzy RDD.** The manifest rightly notes that the binding sanction is a discontinuous jump in the probability of losing Title IV aid once an institution begins a third consecutive year above 30%. Estimate the probability of sanction as a function of first-year CDR crossing and use that as an instrument for subsequent consequences (treating eligibility loss as the outcome). Alternatively, focus on the subsample of institution-years that represent the third consecutive year above the threshold, treating that as the treatment and comparing to similar institution-years just below the threshold. Such an approach would more directly capture the behavioral impact of the actual sanction rather than the warning signal.

2. **Leverage panel structure for dynamic analysis.** Institutions’ behaviors evolve over time. Consider a panel specification that includes leads and lags of crossing the threshold to see whether changes occur before or after the crossing, and whether they accumulate over subsequent years. This would help address concerns that the immediate effect is zero while delayed responses exist (e.g., after accruing two years above 30%). Panel RDD methods (e.g., using event-study-style graphs within the bandwidth and controlling for institution fixed effects) could improve precision and increase interpretability.

3. **Enrich the outcome set to probe mechanisms.** In addition to the aggregate outcomes, consider using more granular, narrowly defined indicators: e.g., admissions volume, application-to-enrollment yield, program-level completions, student demographic composition (beyond Pell share), or measures of student support spending if available. These may be more sensitive to short-run institutional adjustments (cream-skimming, recruitment focus) than aggregate enrollment. If data constraints preclude that, explicitly discuss how the existing outcomes map onto the stated channels (quality improvement, cream-skimming, manipulation) and why they are expected to respond within the studied window.

4. **Assess power and plausible effects.** Given the small number of observations near the cutoff, present a power analysis showing the minimum detectable effect size for key outcomes at conventional significance levels. This context would help interpret the null findings—is the paper ruling out economically meaningful impacts, or only large ones? Additionally, reporting standardized effect sizes for all outcomes (beyond Table A.2) in the main text would clarify whether the null is due to noise or truly minimal shifts.

5. **Discuss general equilibrium and compositional implications more cautiously.** The manuscript interprets the higher Pell share above the threshold as evidence that sanctions threaten high-need students and notes the lack of effects as indicating regulatory design failure. It would be helpful to acknowledge that the lack of a sharp response might reflect effective access-preserving behavior (e.g., institutions maintaining Pell enrollment despite pressure) rather than regulatory impotence. If data permit, examine whether institutions above the threshold alter the composition of their borrowing students (e.g., shift to private loans) or adjust tuition to compensate for reputational damage.

6. **Enhance robustness checks.** The placebo results at 15% and 20% suggest a negative relationship between CDR and outcomes in the interior, which the local polynomial may not fully absorb when only a narrow bandwidth is used. Consider including polynomial terms of higher order, alternative specifications (e.g., global controls for cohort size) to ensure that the local effect is not confounded by broader CDR trends. Also, present graphical RDDs with overlayed confidence bands to reassure readers about the visual fit.

7. **Explain sample selection and potential biases.** The sample excludes institutions with fewer than 30 borrowers in the CDR denominator. Discuss whether this automatically excludes very small or new institutions that might behave differently when near the threshold, and what implications that has for external validity. Similarly, mention attrition due to closures—since the outcome includes closure, institutions disappearing after crossing the threshold could bias the estimated effect upward or downward.

8. **Contextualize within broader regulation.** The policy implication—that grace periods dilute accountability—would be strengthened by comparing the CDR regime to other federal cutoffs (e.g., Gainful Employment, 90/10 rule). A brief discussion on how these rules interact (are institutions near 30% also near other thresholds?) would help practitioners understand whether the null is a unique feature of the CDR design or part of a broader pattern.

By addressing these points, the paper will better align its empirical strategy with the manifest’s ambitious research question and provide clearer guidance on the effectiveness (or lack thereof) of the cohort default rate cliff.
