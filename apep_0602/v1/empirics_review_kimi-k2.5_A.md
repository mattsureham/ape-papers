 **Referee Report: "The Cliff That Didn't Bite: Cohort Default Rates and For-Profit College Behavior at the Federal Accountability Threshold"**

---

### 1. Idea Fidelity

The paper pursues the core empirical agenda outlined in the manifest: implementing the first regression discontinuity (RDD) analysis of the 30% cohort default rate (CDR) threshold using College Scorecard and IPEDS data. The data construction, sample restrictions (for-profit institutions, CDR cohort size $\geq$ 30), and basic empirical framework align closely with the proposal.

However, the paper deviates from the manifest in one crucial respect that undermines the research design. The manifest correctly identified that the binding sanction requires **three consecutive years** above 30%, suggesting both "sharp" (first-year effects) and "fuzzy" (eventual eligibility loss) interpretations. The submitted paper treats the first crossing as a sharp RDD treatment, effectively equating "crossing 30% once" with "facing the death sentence." This conceptual slip is consequential: the paper is not estimating the effect of the Title IV loss (which requires sustained violation), but rather the effect of provisional certification and enhanced monitoring. The manifest's insight that this is a multi-period problem requiring careful handling of the accumulation rule is largely lost. Additionally, while the manifest emphasized documenting manipulation (forbearance pushing) as an informative margin, the paper treats the absence of bunching as mere validation rather than exploring the strategic behavior that generates the null result.

---

### 2. Summary

This paper employs a regression discontinuity design to estimate whether for-profit colleges change behavior when crossing the 30% federal cohort default rate threshold, which triggers a path toward loss of Title IV eligibility. Using administrative data from 2011–2019, the author finds precisely estimated null effects on enrollment, completion rates, and institutional closure, suggesting that the multi-year grace period embedded in the regulation dilutes the immediate accountability threat despite the severity of the eventual sanction.

### 3. Essential Points

**1. Empirical design mismatched to the institutional mechanism.** The paper treats the 30% threshold as a standard sharp RDD, but the regulatory structure creates a **dynamic treatment regime** where the first crossing initiates a probationary period, not the sanction itself. The "treatment" at the cutoff is actually "entering a three-year countdown," not "losing Title IV eligibility." This is conceptually distinct from standard RDD applications (e.g., class size caps, election thresholds) where crossing the boundary triggers immediate consequences. 

The current approach conflates two distinct estimands: (a) the effect of provisional certification/enhanced monitoring (first crossing), and (b) the effect of imminent eligibility loss (third consecutive crossing). The paper's interpretation—that the "cliff didn't bite"—is premature because the empirical strategy does not isolate the actual cliff (the third strike). To credibly estimate the causal effect of the CDR regime, the authors should either: (i) implement a **fuzzy RDD** where first-crossing instruments for eventual sanction probability, acknowledging that many institutions below the threshold in subsequent years never receive the treatment; or (ii) restrict the sample to institutions with two consecutive years above 30% and estimate the effect of the third crossing (the true discontinuity in treatment probability). Without this restructuring, the null result may simply reflect that the "treatment" is weak—provisional certification may not change behavior—rather than that accountability pressure is ineffective.

**2. Severe underpowered inference for rare outcomes.** The three-year closure outcome has a baseline rate of 0.4% (Table 1) with only 466 effective observations (Table 2). The reported confidence interval $[-0.005, 0.010]$ includes economically meaningful effects (e.g., a 1 percentage point increase in closure probability would represent a 250% increase relative to the baseline), yet the paper concludes the "cliff didn't bite" for survival. This is methodologically unsound. For rare binary outcomes with limited observations near the cutoff, the RDD lacks power to detect plausible effects. The authors must conduct formal power calculations and report minimum detectable effects (MDEs) for all outcomes. For closure specifically, given the institutional and policy importance of the "death sentence" claim, the paper should either acknowledge that the data cannot resolve this question or employ a hazard/survival model that uses the full panel structure rather than a binary three-year outcome.

**3. Anomalous robustness checks require explanation.** Table 3, Panel C reports donut-hole specifications excluding observations near the 30% cutoff. The estimates flip from negative/null to large and positive (1.706, $p=0.027$ when excluding $\pm$1.5pp; 1.392, $p=0.078$ when excluding $\pm$2pp). This is not a minor sensitivity check failure—it is a sign reversal with statistical significance. The paper dismisses these as "unreliable" due to small samples, but this is insufficient. If the true effect is zero, excluding the closest observations should attenuate precision but not generate large positive coefficients. This pattern suggests: (a) unmodeled heterogeneity where institutions just above 30% are systematically different from those just below once the "manipulation window" is removed; (b) misspecification of the functional form; or (c) that the local randomization assumption fails at wider bandwidths.
