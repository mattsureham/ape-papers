## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
This paper asks whether the most feared regulatory threshold in U.S. higher education—the 30% student loan default rate that can shut down for-profit colleges—actually forces schools to change their behavior. Using a regression discontinuity design, the author finds a "precisely estimated null": crossing the threshold has no immediate impact on enrollment, completion, or survival. This suggests that the "three-year grace period" built into the law effectively neuters the policy's bite, offering a cautionary tale about how the temporal structure of sanctions can undermine high-stakes accountability.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this pitch excellently. The first two paragraphs of the introduction clearly establish the "death sentence" stakes and the immediate empirical puzzle. 

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
The paper’s contribution is providing the first RDD evidence that the primary federal accountability threshold for student loan defaults fails to trigger immediate institutional change.

- **Differentiation:** It is well-differentiated from Cellini and Turner (2019) by focusing on the "old" CDR regime rather than the newer Gainful Employment rules.
- **Framing:** It is framed as a question about the WORLD (why don't these schools close?) rather than just a gap in the literature.
- **Clarity:** A smart economist would immediately understand the "dog that didn't bark" story. It is not "just another DiD"; it is an RDD of a null effect on a high-stakes policy.
- **Bigger Contribution:** To make this bigger, the paper needs to move beyond "it doesn't work" to a more robust "here is exactly how they evade it." While the paper mentions "forbearance pushing," more granular data on *how* schools manage the three-year clock (e.g., student-level transitions into forbearance right at the 2.5-year mark) would turn a policy evaluation into a major paper on corporate response to regulation.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
The paper sits at the intersection of Higher Ed Economics (Deming, Goldin, Katz) and the broader literature on Performance-Based Accountability (Figlio, Rouse, Jacob).

- **Neighbors:** Cellini & Turner (2019), Looney & Yannelis (2015), and Darolia (2013).
- **Strategy:** It builds on these by testing the "grandfather" of all thresholds. It should perhaps more aggressively synthesize with the K-12 "gaming" literature (Jacob 2005) to frame the null not as a failure of the RDD, but as a strategic success for the colleges.
- **Missing Conversations:** The paper could speak more to the "Regulatory Capture" or "Administrative Burden" literatures. Why was the 3-year rule written this way? Is this a feature or a bug?

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC
- **Setup:** For-profit colleges depend on federal aid; the 30% CDR threshold is a "death sentence."
- **Tension:** Schools near this line should be panicking, yet the RDD shows they don't change their behavior at all upon crossing it.
- **Resolution:** The "three consecutive years" rule creates a "warning signal" rather than a "cliff," allowing schools to cycle in and out of compliance without consequence.
- **Implications:** Policy design matters; severity of a sanction is irrelevant if the timing allows for strategic procrastination.

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong and linear. It is a "mystery" story that the data solves by pointing to the fine print of the law.

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
At a dinner party, the lead fact is: **"Crossing the threshold that is supposed to kill a for-profit college has literally zero effect on its enrollment or survival."**
People would lean in. The follow-up would be: "Then how are they still open?" The paper answers this (the 3-year rule), which is satisfying. The null result is interesting because the *institutional stakes* are so high; a null in a high-stakes setting is often more informative than a positive result in a low-stakes one.

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
- **Front-loading:** The paper is well-structured. However, the Pell Share result (the "one marginally significant result") feels like a distraction in the current draft. It should either be elevated as a "compositional" story or moved to the appendix to keep the focus on the main null.
- **Robustness:** The "Donut-hole RDD" section is a bit defensive. It could be tightened to show that the null is robust even when ignoring the "manipulation zone."

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The gap is **Ambition/Scope**. Currently, it feels like a very high-quality *Journal of Public Economics* or *Journal of Labor Economics* paper. 
To reach AER, it needs to be more than a policy evaluation of one specific rule. It needs to be a broader treatise on the **"Structure of Regulatory Threats."** 

**The Single Most Impactful Advice:** Shift the framing from "the CDR threshold doesn't work" to a general theory of "Intermittent vs. Continuous Accountability." Compare the CDR (3-year rolling average) with other "sharp" shocks in the economy. If the author can show that the *intermittency* of the threat is what allows the "for-profit" sector to thrive despite poor outcomes, it becomes a paper about the architecture of the modern regulatory state.
