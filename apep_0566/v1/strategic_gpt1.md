## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple and potentially important question: when states curb civil asset forfeiture—thereby reducing police agencies’ ability to profit from seizures—does public safety deteriorate, or improve? Using staggered state reforms during the opioid era, the paper argues that restricting forfeiture is associated with *lower*, not higher, drug overdose mortality, suggesting that revenue incentives can distort policing away from harm-reducing activities.

A busy economist should care because the paper sits at the intersection of political economy, public economics, crime, and health: it is really about whether giving public agencies revenue-generating incentives changes what they optimize, and whether those distortions have first-order welfare consequences.

### Does the paper articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Not quite. The current opening is vivid but too legalistic and anecdotal. It begins with an abuse case, then defines forfeiture, and only gradually arrives at the bigger economics question. The paper’s real hook is not “forfeiture is controversial”; it is: **does paying police through seizure revenue make them worse at producing public safety?** That should be front and center immediately.

### The pitch the paper should have

“Police departments are often judged on public safety, but some are also partly financed by what they seize. This paper asks whether that revenue incentive distorts policing in ways that worsen social outcomes. I study a wave of state civil asset forfeiture reforms between 2014 and 2021 and show that limiting police access to forfeiture revenue is associated with lower drug overdose mortality, contrary to claims that reform would weaken drug enforcement. The broader point is that when governments fund agencies through self-generated enforcement revenue, they may induce effort toward cash-generating activity rather than socially valuable activity.”

That is the AER version of the paper. It elevates the paper from “a policy evaluation of forfeiture reform” to “a paper about distorted incentives inside the state.”

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### Contribution in one sentence

The paper’s contribution is to argue that reducing police access to forfeiture revenue lowers drug overdose mortality, implying that revenue-generating incentives can distort law enforcement away from socially productive forms of drug enforcement.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3-4 papers?

Only partially. The introduction does say prior work looks at seizure volumes, arrests, and budgets rather than mortality, which is useful. But the differentiation is still a bit thin. Right now the novelty reads as “same topic, new outcome,” which is not quite enough for AER unless the outcome is truly the outcome that matters and the paper makes a broader conceptual point.

The paper needs to distinguish itself more sharply from work showing:
- forfeiture affects police behavior or seizure activity,
- police respond to fiscal incentives,
- policing affects public safety outcomes,
- opioid-era policies affect overdose deaths.

The author needs to say more explicitly: prior papers show agencies chase revenue; this paper asks whether that incentive distortion matters for an ultimate welfare outcome. That’s a bigger contribution than “no one has studied overdose deaths yet.”

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a literature gap?

Mostly a literature gap, with flashes of a stronger world question. The stronger version is:
- **World question:** When police can fund themselves through seizures, do they pursue the wrong objectives, with deadly consequences?
- **Weaker literature-gap version:** We know about seizures/arrests/budgets, but not overdose mortality.

The paper should lean much harder into the world question. AER cares much more about “what kind of state capacity do revenue incentives create?” than “there is no paper on outcome Y.”

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the intro?

At the moment, maybe—but they might still summarize it as “a DiD paper on forfeiture reform and overdoses.” That is a warning sign. The author has the ingredients for something stronger, but the introduction spends too much time on institutional background and estimator justification relative to the conceptual stake.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Specific ways to make it bigger:

1. **Make the outcome architecture more ambitious.**  
   Right now it is one headline outcome: overdose mortality. That is important, but by itself it risks feeling too reduced-form and one-channel. A bigger paper would connect mortality to intermediate policing or criminal justice outcomes:
   - seizure revenues,
   - drug possession vs trafficking arrests,
   - clearance/composition of drug enforcement,
   - jail admissions,
   - naloxone deployment or diversion referrals if available.

2. **Sharpen the mechanism comparison.**  
   The paper currently implies “less seizure-oriented policing, more harm-reducing policing,” but the mechanism evidence is admitted to be weak. The biggest scope upgrade would be to show *what enforcement margin changed*. Without that, the paper is vulnerable to being read as a provocative reduced-form association rather than a persuasive economics story.

3. **Reframe around public-sector incentives, not just forfeiture law.**  
   The paper gets bigger if it is pitched as evidence on what happens when bureaucracies can self-finance through punitive activity. That connects it to a broader class of institutions: fines and fees, civil penalties, tax farming, private probation, municipal ticketing, etc.

4. **Use comparisons that isolate the incentive channel.**  
   For example, comparing reforms that truly sever agency retention from those that merely tweak procedure is directionally right. But if the paper can more credibly show heterogeneity by pre-reform reliance on forfeiture revenue or equitable sharing exposure, that would make the contribution feel much more economic and much less like generic policy evaluation.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Police departments are tasked with public safety, but civil asset forfeiture gives them a separate financial objective: generating revenue from seizures. This creates a plausible tension between what police are supposed to maximize and what they are incentivized to maximize.

### Tension

Critics of reform claim that taking away forfeiture authority would weaken drug enforcement and worsen overdose deaths; reform advocates claim forfeiture distorts policing toward revenue extraction and away from socially productive activity. The missing evidence is whether these incentive distortions matter for an ultimate social outcome, not just seizures or budgets.

### Resolution

The paper finds that states restricting forfeiture see lower overdose mortality, with suggestive evidence of larger effects under more stringent reforms and in higher-overdose states.

### Implications

The implication is not merely that a controversial policing tool may be harmful. It is that **how the state finances enforcement changes what it does**, and those distortions can have large public-health consequences.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has a decent arc, but it is diluted by too many local detours. The paper often reads like:
- legal background,
- estimator tutorial,
- result,
- mechanism speculation,
instead of a tightly staged narrative.

At times it also feels like a collection of empirics looking for a stronger unifying claim:
- main ATT,
- event study,
- dose response,
- heterogeneity,
- welfare calculation.

The story it *should* be telling is:

1. Police face a multitasking problem with a revenue incentive.
2. Civil asset forfeiture reform exogenously weakens that incentive.
3. If the incentive mattered, the socially relevant outcome should improve, not deteriorate.
4. Overdose mortality does improve.
5. Therefore, this is evidence that revenue-linked enforcement can undermine public safety.

That is a coherent AER-style arc. The current draft gets there, but not cleanly enough.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?

“I’ve got a paper showing that when states stop letting police profit from civil forfeiture, overdose deaths fall rather than rise.”

That is a strong lead. It is surprising, politically charged, and economically meaningful.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

They would lean in—initially. The question is whether the paper can sustain that interest beyond the first fact. Right now the headline is more compelling than the supporting architecture. Economists will immediately ask: “Why would that happen?” If the answer is only “maybe police reallocated effort,” the conversation loses altitude.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Almost certainly: **“What exactly changed in policing behavior?”**

Second likely question: **“Is this really about forfeiture incentives, or is reform proxying for a broader package of opioid/public-health reforms?”**

Those are not identification quibbles so much as strategic positioning issues: the mechanism and comparative interpretation are what the paper needs to own.

### If findings are modest or borderline, is the result still interesting?

Yes, the result is still interesting because it overturns a widely asserted policy claim. “Restricting forfeiture does *not* cost lives—and may save them” is an important policy fact if persuasive.

But the paper should be careful not to oversell. The current prose occasionally sounds more definitive than the evidence architecture can carry. AER readers will tolerate uncertainty if the question is big and the paper is honest. They will not tolerate a paper that over-reads a provocative estimate. The right tone is: “Here is surprising evidence against the standard law-enforcement argument, and it points to a broader distortionary incentive mechanism.”

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

This is currently **not yet an AER paper**, but it is also not hopelessly far. The gap is mainly:

### 1. Framing problem
This is the biggest one. The paper is still written as a policy evaluation in the civil asset forfeiture niche. To belong in AER, it needs to be unmistakably about **incentive design inside public institutions** and the consequences of self-financing enforcement.

### 2. Scope problem
The paper is too reliant on one reduced-form outcome and one inferred mechanism. AER readers will want a more complete picture of what reform changed. Even without adding a new design, the paper needs more outcome breadth or more compelling mechanism evidence.

### 3. Ambition problem
The paper is competent, but safe in an odd way: it has a provocative estimate, yet the surrounding architecture is cautious and narrow. The author should think bigger conceptually, not louder rhetorically.

### 4. Novelty problem, but only partly
The question has not been fully answered before, but adjacent literatures already suggest the broad story that agencies respond to revenue incentives. The paper needs to show why this is not just one more example. The answer is that this one links revenue incentives in coercive state power to a first-order mortality outcome. That point needs to be made much more forcefully.

### Single most impactful advice

**Reframe the paper around a general economics question—whether self-financing enforcement distorts state behavior away from social objectives—and then marshal every result around that claim, especially mechanism evidence on what policing behavior actually changed.**

If the author changes only one thing, it should be this. Not a new robustness check. Not another p-value. The paper’s fate is mostly about whether it can become a paper on distorted public-sector incentives, rather than a solid paper on forfeiture reform.
