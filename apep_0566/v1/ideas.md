# Research Ideas

## Idea 1: Follow the Money or Follow the Crime? Police Resource Reallocation After Civil Asset Forfeiture Reform
**Policy:** Civil asset forfeiture reform across 37 U.S. states (2014-2024). Reforms range from full abolition (New Mexico 2015, Nebraska 2016) to conviction requirements (16 states) to raised burden of proof (16 states + DC) to reporting requirements only (27 states + DC). Staggered timing and heterogeneous intensity create rich treatment variation.
**Outcome:** FBI UCR Arrests by Age, Sex, and Race (1974-2021, Jacob Kaplan/ICPSR 102263): agency-level annual arrest counts by offense type (drug, property, violent). Secondary: UCR Offenses Known and Clearances (ICPSR 100707) for clearance rates. Treatment coding from Institute for Justice reform database.
**Identification:** Callaway-Sant'Anna (2021) staggered DiD. Treatment groups by reform year with intensity coded on 4-point scale: (0) no reform, (1) reporting only, (2) conviction requirement, (3) full abolition. 13 never-reformed states as controls. Built-in placebo: violent crime arrests (no forfeiture revenue connection) should not respond to reform. Heterogeneity by pre-reform forfeiture reliance tests the financial incentive channel directly.
**Why it's novel:** Existing literature asks "does abolishing forfeiture increase crime?" (answer: no). No one has asked the substitution question: when forfeiture revenue disappears, do police shift from drug enforcement toward other crime types? This is a revealed-preference test of police incentive responsiveness, connecting to the economics of bureaucratic incentives (Finan et al. 2017 JEL).
**Feasibility check:** Confirmed: 37 reformed states (4 abolished, 16 conviction requirement), 13 never-reformed controls, staggered 2014-2024. Jacob Kaplan's UCR files publicly available on ICPSR. Institute for Justice reform database public. ~50 states x ~10 years at state level; tens of thousands of agency-years at agency level. No APEP paper on this topic.
