## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether Argentina’s striking soybean monoculture was an efficient response to comparative advantage or a distortion created by export tax policy. Exploiting the Macri government’s 2015 decision to eliminate export taxes on wheat, corn, and sunflower while largely preserving soybean taxes, the paper argues that farmers rapidly reallocated land away from soybeans, implying that monoculture was policy-induced and more reversible than many believed.

Why should a busy economist care? Because this is not just a paper about one country’s farm policy; it is potentially a paper about how strongly production patterns respond to distortions, how quickly “locked-in” specialization can unwind, and whether trade taxes reshape real allocation rather than merely prices and rents.

**Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Pretty well, actually. The opening gets to the central factual pattern and the natural experiment quickly. But it still reads somewhat like a good field-journal introduction rather than a top-general-interest introduction. The current version foregrounds Argentina and soybeans; the stronger version should foreground the broader question: when countries use differential export taxation, do they actually reshape production structure, or are production patterns too sticky to move?

**What the first two paragraphs should say instead:**

> Governments in many developing countries use export taxes to raise revenue and steer domestic prices, but we know much less about whether those taxes actually reshape what the economy produces. In agriculture especially, production patterns often appear “locked in” by agronomy, supply chains, and technology, making it unclear whether specialization reflects true comparative advantage or policy-induced distortion.
>
> This paper studies one of the cleanest recent tests of that question: Argentina’s 2015 reform, which eliminated export taxes on wheat, corn, and sunflower while leaving a substantial tax on soybeans. Because Argentina had come to devote more than half its cropland to soybeans, the reform created a sharp test of whether soybean dominance was structurally inevitable or tax-driven. Using administrative crop data across departments and crops, I show that land reallocated quickly and substantially toward the liberalized crops, implying that a large share of Argentina’s soybean monoculture was created by policy rather than fundamentals.

That version gives the paper a broader economic question first, then uses Argentina as the laboratory.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Argentina became heavily concentrated in soybean production, despite having land suitable for multiple crops. Observers could interpret this concentration as either efficient specialization or policy distortion.

### Tension
Acreage allocation in agriculture may be sticky because of agronomy, fixed investments, know-how, and supply chains. So even large relative tax changes may not move the crop mix much. The 2015 reform offers a rare test: if monoculture is tax-induced, acreage should reallocate; if specialization is structural, little should change.

### Resolution
After the reform, acreage shifted sharply toward corn and wheat relative to soybeans, especially in more soybean-concentrated departments. The pattern suggests soybean dominance was not inevitable.

### Implications
Export taxes can shape real production structure, not just transfer income. More broadly, observed specialization in distorted economies may be a poor guide to underlying comparative advantage.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
Yes, **but only in embryo**. The raw ingredients are there. The paper is not a random collection of tables. Still, the narrative gets diluted in three ways:

1. It slips too quickly into empirical specification language.
2. It presents the result as “striking” before fully establishing why the underlying question matters outside Argentina.
3. It has an unresolved second plotline—sunflower and agronomic complementarity—that may actually be the most interesting part, but is not integrated into the main story.

### If it is looking for a better story, what story should it tell?
This one:

> Argentina looked like a country with an overwhelming comparative advantage in soybeans. But a sudden change in relative export taxation revealed that this apparent specialization was partly artificial. Production patterns in agriculture—even those that seem technologically entrenched—can be highly policy-contingent. Yet that contingency is mediated by agronomic realities, which is why corn and wheat rebound while sunflower does not.

That story has setup, tension, a nuanced resolution, and a broader implication.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Likely one of these:
- “So was Argentina’s comparative advantage in soybeans basically fake?”
- “How much did this change total output versus just reallocate land across crops?”
- “Why no sunflower response?”
- “Does this imply export taxes can create monoculture elsewhere too?”

Those are good questions. The paper should be designed to answer them in the introduction and conclusion.

### If findings are modest or null
Not relevant here; the main effect is not null. But the **sunflower null** is interesting and should be treated as a feature, not clutter. It is the main source of nuance and external validity: price wedges matter, but they interact with agronomic viability.
