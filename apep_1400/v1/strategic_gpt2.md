## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether paid family leave, a policy intended to protect workers, can unintentionally widen racial inequality at the hiring stage. Using staggered state adoption of PFL and administrative hiring data, it argues that PFL reduces Black relative hiring, especially when leave programs are less generous and lack job protection, because employers statistically discriminate against workers they perceive as more likely to take leave.

A busy economist should care because this is a sharp inversion of the standard welfare-state story: a progressive labor policy may have regressive incidence through employer behavior. If true, the paper is not just “another policy evaluation”; it is about when social insurance changes the composition of who gets hired.

**Does the paper articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Partly, but not optimally. The current opening is vivid, but it rushes too quickly to the paper’s preferred interpretation (“the door to employment for Black workers narrows”) before first establishing the broader economic question. It also leads with PFL as a labor policy, when the stronger top-journal framing is: **how mandated benefits reshape hiring incentives and inequality**.

**What the first two paragraphs should say instead:**

> Governments increasingly use mandated benefits to insure workers against family and medical shocks. But when employment protections raise the expected cost of hiring, firms may adjust not only wages or employment levels, but also *who gets hired*. A central unanswered question is whether paid family leave changes racial inequality at the point of entry into jobs.
>
> This paper shows that it does. Using administrative hiring data by race and the staggered adoption of state paid family leave programs, I find that PFL reduces the Black-to-White hiring ratio by about 12 percent, driven by a fall in Black hiring with little change in White hiring. The negative effect is concentrated in low-generosity programs without job protection, while more generous, job-protected programs show no such pattern. The broader message is that the design of social insurance policies can either create or mute incentives for statistical discrimination.

That version makes the paper about a first-order economic question—how labor regulation affects allocation across groups—rather than simply “there is an unexplored racial angle in PFL.”

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution in one sentence:**

1. PFL effects on employment and wages,
2. mandated-benefit incidence more generally,
3. discrimination induced by labor regulation,
4. race-specific hiring outcomes from policy shocks.

Right now the contribution reads as a bundle:
- new policy setting,
- new outcome by race,
- new mechanism,
- new heterogeneity by design,
- new data.

That is too many “contributions,” and it weakens the central one. For AER, the paper needs a single clean contribution: **mandated benefits alter employer screening, and policy design determines whether that screening worsens racial inequality.**

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or filling a gap in a LITERATURE?
Mixed. It starts with a real-world question, which is good, but then lapses into “this literature hasn’t studied race in PFL.” That is a weaker frame. AER papers should answer a question about the world:
- Do worker protections change who gets hired?
- Can the design of social insurance generate or suppress discrimination?
- When do progressive labor policies backfire?

Those are much stronger than “the racial dimension has been absent from this literature.”

### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what’s new?
They could probably say: “It’s a DiD showing PFL may reduce Black hiring relative to White hiring.”  
That is not yet enough. The risk is exactly that the paper sounds like “another staggered DiD with an alarming result.”

To rise above that, the introduction must make the newness more conceptual:
- not merely PFL affects race gaps,
- but **benefit design changes employers’ incentive to statistically discriminate**,
- so **the incidence of social insurance depends on equilibrium screening at hiring**.

### What would make this contribution bigger?
Specific ways to enlarge it:

   Right now the paper is too tethered to PFL. The bigger contribution is about mandated benefits/social insurance and screening. The conclusion gestures at this, but the whole paper should be built around it.

2. **Show the margin more directly.**

Strategically, what would make the story larger is evidence that the policy changes the *composition* of hired workers in ways diagnostic of screening: e.g., by age bands, parental-age workers, industries with high leave exposure, or occupations where replacement costs are high. Even without changing methods, a richer set of outcomes would make the contribution feel less narrow.

3. **Sharpen the design heterogeneity into a policy-design result.**  
   The most interesting part of the paper is not the average negative effect; it is that high-generosity/job-protected systems appear not to produce it. That is a more publishable contribution than “PFL hurts Black hiring.”

4. **Tone down the branding and strengthen the economic concept.**

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

### Closest neighbors
The paper’s closest neighbors are likely in four clusters:

1. **PFL / family leave effects**
   - Rossin-Slater, Ruhm, and Waldfogel (2013) on California PFL
   - Baum and Ruhm / Baum and coauthors on labor-market consequences of leave mandates
   - Byker (2016) on leave and maternal labor supply

2. **Discrimination in hiring**
   - Bertrand and Mullainathan (2004)
   - Altonji and Pierret (2001) / broader statistical discrimination tradition

3. **Labor regulation and protected groups**
   - Neumark and Stock / Neumark and coauthors on disability, accommodation mandates, family leave, or employment protection and hiring
   - Acemoglu and Angrist (ADA-type induced substitution/screening logic, even if not race-focused)
   - Papers on minimum wage / scheduling / benefits and differential incidence across workers

4. **Modern policy-incidence / employer adjustment papers**
   - The literature on mandates altering equilibrium margins beyond wages—e.g., health insurance mandates, sick leave mandates, worker scheduling protections.

### How should the paper position itself relative to those neighbors?
**Build on and synthesize**, not attack.

The right stance is:
- PFL literature tells us much about leave-takers and incumbents.
- Discrimination literature tells us employers screen when group identity proxies for costs.
- Labor-regulation literature tells us mandates can backfire on protected groups.
- **This paper joins them** by showing that a worker-protection policy can alter racial hiring through employer screening, and that policy design mediates the effect.

That synthesis is the conversation. The paper currently positions itself too much as “nobody has looked at race in PFL,” which is true but too incremental.

### Is it currently positioned too narrowly or too broadly?
Oddly, both.

- **Too narrowly** because it is written as a PFL paper with a race angle.
- **Too broadly** because it makes sweeping claims about “mandated benefits” and “general principle” without enough literature scaffolding to support those claims.

The better positioning is: **a labor/public paper about the distributional incidence of social insurance through hiring behavior**. That audience is large, coherent, and AER-relevant.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?
It seems under-engaged with:
- employer adjustment to mandates,
- incidence of social insurance through hiring and job design,
- antidiscrimination consequences of employment protection and accommodation mandates,
- broader labor-market sorting literature.

It should be speaking not only to leave-policy scholars, but to:
- labor economists interested in hiring/sorting,
- public economists studying social insurance design,
- economists of discrimination,
- political economy readers concerned with unintended distributional effects.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?
Not quite. The current conversation is “PFL has an overlooked racial consequence.” The more impactful conversation is:
**How should we design worker protections when firms screen workers on expected utilization?**

That is a much better AER conversation.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Paid family leave is widely understood as a progressive policy that improves worker welfare, especially for parents and caregivers. Existing evidence emphasizes benefits for leave-taking, employment attachment, and medium-run outcomes.

### Tension
Policies that insure workers may also change employer hiring incentives. If employers use group identity to proxy for expected leave-taking or disruption costs, then a policy meant to reduce inequality may widen inequality in access to jobs.

### Resolution
The paper finds that PFL adoption is associated with a decline in Black relative hiring, but this effect is concentrated in less generous, weakly protected programs and disappears in more generous/job-protected ones.

### Implications
The distributional effects of labor regulation depend on design. Social insurance can backfire through screening at hiring, but better-designed programs may avoid that tradeoff.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
Yes, more than many papers do. It is not merely a random collection of tables. There is a real arc:
progressive policy → unintended screening → avoidable through design.

But the paper slightly mishandles the arc in two ways:

1. **It resolves too early and too confidently.**  
   The introduction declares “statistical discrimination” as the mechanism with excessive certainty. Editorially, that is strategically risky. The paper should say “consistent with employer screening/statistical discrimination,” not act as though the mechanism is conclusively proven.

2. **The most interesting part of the story arrives too late.**  
   The design-heterogeneity result is the payoff. In strategic terms, that should be front and center from the opening pages. Right now, the first read is “PFL worsens racial hiring gaps,” which is provocative but also brittle. The richer story is “whether PFL worsens racial hiring gaps depends on design.” That is more credible, more policy-relevant, and more AER-like.

If the paper is “a collection of results looking for a story,” the fix is not hard: make the story **policy design under employer screening**, not just **a negative average effect**.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?
“I’ve got a paper suggesting that paid family leave reduced Black relative hiring by about 12 percent in adopting states—but that the effect disappears in high-generosity programs with job protection.”

That is the version that gets attention. Not just “PFL hurt Black hiring,” but “the design seems to determine whether it does.”

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
They would lean in—initially. The result is surprising and potentially important. But the very next reaction would be skepticism about mechanism and external meaning. That is fine; skeptical engagement is what a good AER paper provokes.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Probably one of these:
- “Why would employers think Black workers are more likely to take leave?”
- “Is this really race-specific employer screening, or some correlated labor-demand shock?”
- “Why would higher generosity reduce discrimination rather than increase employer costs?”
- “Is the big result really driven by early adopters like California?”

Those are exactly the questions the framing should anticipate. Again, not by doing referee work, but by setting expectations properly.

### If findings are modest or null
The null in high-generosity states is actually highly interesting. The paper should treat that as a feature, not a footnote. AER does publish papers where the intellectually important result is that a feared tradeoff is not inevitable. Here the positive insight is:
**PFL need not worsen racial inequality; some program designs appear to avoid it.**

That turns the paper from a “failed progressive policy” story into a “design determines incidence” story.
