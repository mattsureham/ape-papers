## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
The paper examines the 2011 "mass revocation" of tax-exempt status for 377,000 U.S. nonprofits—the largest administrative "death event" in history. It asks why some organizations survived (reinstated) while 90% vanished, finding that survival was driven not by social value or charitable mission, but by "institutional rootedness" (specifically, owning physical assets like land or buildings). Economists should care because this is a massive, exogenous shock that reveals how "neutral" transparency regulations can mechanically and regressively reshape an entire sector by selecting for administrative capacity over social utility.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this quite well. The first paragraph effectively uses the "state of Wyoming" comparison to establish scale. However, it could be sharper on the "why." 
*   **The pitch the paper should have:** "When the government imposes new compliance costs on a sector, does it prune the 'dead wood' or kill the 'fragile but valuable'? Following a 2011 IRS shock that revoked status for 16% of the nonprofit sector, I show that survival was dictated by a 'compliance cliff' where organizations with physical assets were 36% more likely to survive than charities. This identifies a regressive mechanism in regulatory enforcement: the state systematically preserves institutionally embedded entities while liquidating the purely charitable."

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**Contribution:** The paper identifies "institutional rootedness" (proxied by physical assets) as the primary determinant of organizational survival following a mass regulatory compliance shock.

*   **Differentiation:** It moves beyond descriptive work (Blackwood & Roeger, 2011) and small-scale surveys (Hager et al., 2004) by using a census-level administrative "natural experiment."
*   **World vs. Literature:** It frames itself as answering a question about the WORLD (how the nonprofit sector was permanently reshaped), which is a strength.
*   **The "Smart Economist" Test:** A reader would explain this as: "It’s a paper showing that when the IRS tightened the rules, the organizations that owned their own buildings survived because they had 'skin in the game' and better info, while the small, volunteer-run charities—the ones we actually care about—got wiped out."
*   **Bigger Contribution:** To make this truly "AER-big," the author needs to bridge to **Political Economy**. Does this shift the median voter's access to social services? Does it change the landscape of local lobbying? Connecting the "compositional shift" to a real-world outcome (e.g., "In counties where the cliff was steepest, did private social safety nets fail?") would be a massive leap forward.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
The paper sits at the intersection of **Organizational Ecology**, **Public Economics (Regulation)**, and **Nonprofit Economics**.

*   **Closest Neighbors:** Hannan & Freeman (1989) on mortality shocks; Decker et al. (2016) on regressive regulatory burdens; and perhaps the literature on "not-for-profit" vs "for-profit" behavior (Glaeser & Shleifer, 2001).
*   **Positioning Strategy:** It should **synthesize**. It uses an Organizational Ecology event (mass death) to test a Public Economics theory (regressive compliance costs).
*   **Niche vs. Broad:** Currently a bit niche (Nonprofit sector). It needs to speak louder to the "Economics of Regulation" crowd.
*   **Unexpected Connection:** The paper could connect to **Information Economics** (Stiglitz). The "surprise" wave is a textbook case of asymmetric information where the "asset-holding" signal is actually a proxy for "information-processing capacity."

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC
*   **Setup:** A million small nonprofits existed in an "administrative wild west" with no filing requirements.
*   **Tension:** The PPA 2006 creates a "paperwork trap." If you don't file, you die. But the IRS doesn't know where you live to tell you the rules have changed.
*   **Resolution:** 377,000 "die." Only the "rooted" (cemeteries, vets) have the incentive/info to come back.
*   **Implications:** Transparency is not a free lunch; it has a high "social cost of administrative burden" that favors the old and established over the new and mission-driven.

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong. It is not a collection of results; it is a clear story of "The Great Pruning."

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
*   **Lead Fact:** "The IRS accidentally killed 13% of all American social welfare organizations in a single afternoon because they didn't file a digital postcard."
*   **Reaction:** Lean in. It’s a "bureaucracy gone wild" story with a quantitative backbone.
*   **Follow-up:** "Did the services they provided actually disappear, or did people just stop getting tax deductions?" This is the vital follow-up the paper needs to address.

---

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
*   **Front-loading:** The paper is well-structured.
*   **The "Cemetery" Focus:** The cemetery result is the "hook." Keep it prominent—it’s the perfect illustration of "perpetual obligation" vs. "discretionary charity."
*   **Appendix:** The state-by-state robustness is fine in the appendix, but if there's a state with *better* outreach, it should be a main-text case study to show how to mitigate the "cliff."

---

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The science is there, and the story is compelling. The gap is **Ambitious Scope**. Right now, it’s a very high-quality "Event Study" of a specific regulation. To make it AER, it needs to be a paper about **The Regressive Nature of the Modern Regulatory State**.

**The Single Most Impactful Advice:** The author must move beyond "Who reinstated?" to "What was lost?" If they can show that the *services* provided by the 501(c)(4)s that died were not picked up by the 501(c)(3)s that lived, they transform a "tax filing paper" into a "welfare economics paper."
