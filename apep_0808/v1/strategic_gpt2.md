## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper studies a remarkable administrative event: in 2011, the IRS automatically revoked the tax-exempt status of roughly 377,000 nonprofits in one stroke, and only about one in ten ever returned. Using the full revocation list, the paper asks which organizations survived this compliance shock and argues that reinstatement depended less on social mission than on institutional capacity and asset-holding, implying that procedural regulation can selectively kill fragile organizations.

A busy economist should care because the paper is not really about nonprofits per se; it is about how fixed compliance burdens reshape the composition of an organizational sector. That is a potentially broad political economy / public finance / organization story.

**Does the paper articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Not quite. The opening has a strong fact, but the paper waits too long to tell the reader why this matters beyond the nonprofit sector. The current introduction is descriptive first, question second, contribution third. For AER, it needs to get to the general lesson immediately: this is a setting to study how automatic administrative enforcement selects across organizations.

**What the first two paragraphs should say instead:**

> Governments increasingly rely on automatic compliance rules—miss the filing threshold, lose legal status. But we know little about which organizations survive these procedural shocks and whether enforcement disproportionately eliminates low-capacity but socially valuable entities. This paper studies the largest mass organizational revocation in U.S. administrative history: the IRS’s 2011 automatic termination of tax-exempt status for more than 377,000 nonprofits that failed to meet a new filing requirement.
>
> Using the universe of revoked nonprofits linked to subsequent IRS records, I show that survival after this shock was sharply patterned by organizational type and institutional rootedness. Organizations with physical assets and embedded local presence were far more likely to regain status, while the modal revoked charity almost never returned. The event therefore reveals a broader fact about administrative states: when compliance rules impose fixed procedural costs, they do not simply remove inactive organizations—they selectively cull the least administratively resilient.

That is the pitch. The current draft has the raw ingredients, but it is still introducing a nonprofit event rather than a general economics question.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution in one sentence:**  
The paper documents that a massive, automatic IRS compliance shock permanently reshaped the nonprofit sector by disproportionately eliminating low-capacity organizations, with reinstatement strongly concentrated among asset-holding, institutionally embedded groups.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?
Only partially. The paper says prior work is descriptive or state-level and that no published paper has used this event to study survival determinants. That may be true, but the differentiation is still too thin. Right now the contribution reads as: “Here is the first comprehensive administrative description of this event.” That is useful, but not enough for AER unless the paper makes a broader claim.

The draft needs to distinguish itself along one of these dimensions:
1. **Scale/uniqueness of the shock** — a census-level organizational death event.
2. **General mechanism** — fixed compliance costs and information frictions sort organizations.
3. **Compositional consequences** — regulation changes who remains in civil society, not just how many.

At present, it mostly leans on (1), somewhat on (2), and underdevelops (3).

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or filling a gap in a LITERATURE?
It oscillates. The stronger parts are world-facing: what kinds of organizations survive compliance cliffs? The weaker parts are literature-facing: “no paper has exploited this natural experiment.” AER wants the former. “No one has studied this exact IRS list before” is not an AER contribution by itself.

### Could a smart economist explain to a colleague what's new?
Right now, maybe, but with some hesitation. The likely summary would be:  
“It's a descriptive paper on IRS nonprofit revocations showing asset-rich organizations were more likely to come back.”  
That is better than “another DiD paper,” but it is still narrower and smaller than what the paper wants it to be.

The introduction needs to make the novelty legible as:
- an unusually clean window into **selection under administrative burden**, and
- evidence that **procedural enforcement changes sector composition in systematically regressive ways**.

### What would make the contribution bigger?
Most importantly: **move from reinstatement heterogeneity to equilibrium composition and social function.**

Specific ways to make it bigger:
- **Different outcome variable:** not just reinstatement, but what share of local civic/service capacity disappeared. If the authors can connect revoked organizations to mission categories, local public-good provision, or place-level organizational density, the paper becomes much more consequential.
- **Different mechanism:** the current “physical assets” proxy is intuitive but somewhat blunt. A bigger paper would show that what matters is embeddedness, salience, donor interface, or professionalization—not merely asset ownership by legal type.
- **Different comparison:** compare this organizational culling to other sectors or administrative regimes, or at least frame the nonprofit sector as a test case for a general model of automatic enforcement.
- **Different framing:** make the paper about the economics of **administrative selection**. Nonprofits are the setting, not the endpoint.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
The state introduces a universal low-cost filing requirement and automatic revocation for repeated noncompliance. In principle, this is administrative housekeeping: remove dead nonprofits from the rolls.

### Tension
A universal procedural rule may not merely clean the registry; it may selectively kill living but administratively weak organizations. The puzzle is whether survival reflects organizational value/mission or institutional capacity/embeddedness.

### Resolution
After the 2010 shock, only a small minority return, and return is heavily skewed toward asset-holding and embedded organization types. The first revocation wave looks especially selective, consistent with information/capacity frictions.

### Implications
Administrative rules with fixed costs can reorganize sectors by selecting on procedural resilience rather than social value. In civil society, that may alter which kinds of organizations remain active and legally recognized.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
**Serviceable, but not yet fully coherent.** There is a story here, but the draft sometimes feels like a collection of descriptive facts plus a favored mechanism. The strongest story is not “cemeteries survived, charities didn’t.” That is a vivid example, not the core arc.

**The story it should tell:**
1. Administrative enforcement is increasingly automatic.
2. Automatic enforcement can generate compliance cliffs.
3. Compliance cliffs do not simply weed out the inactive; they sort on organizational capacity.
4. This changes the composition of an economically and socially important sector.

That story is stronger, more general, and more AER-facing than the current version.

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?
“In one IRS action, 377,000 nonprofits lost tax-exempt status, and 90% never came back; survival was much more common among organizations with tangible assets and institutional roots than among ordinary charities.”

That is a good opening fact. People would likely lean in initially because the scale is startling.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
They lean in for the first fact. The danger comes on the second sentence. If the conversation becomes “here are reinstatement rates by 501(c) subsection,” phones come out. If instead it becomes “this is a vivid case of administrative burden changing who survives,” they stay engaged.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Probably one of these:
- “Were the organizations actually alive, or was the IRS just clearing deadwood?”
- “Why should we think the ones that disappeared mattered?”
- “Is this about information, fixed costs, or assets?”
- “What broader lesson does this teach us outside nonprofits?”

Those are exactly the questions the framing should anticipate.

### If findings are modest/null
They are not null, but they are somewhat modest in analytical ambition. The paper must make the case that this is not just a post hoc taxonomy of who reinstated. The value lies in learning that **procedural compliance shocks can have durable compositional effects**. That is the high-level “so what.”
