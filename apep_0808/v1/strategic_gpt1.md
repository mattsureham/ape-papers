## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper studies a remarkable regulatory event: in 2011 the IRS automatically revoked the tax-exempt status of roughly 377,000 nonprofits after a new filing rule took effect, and only about one in ten ever returned. Using administrative data, the paper argues that the organizations most likely to survive were not the most socially valuable ones, but the ones with institutional infrastructure—especially those tied to physical assets—suggesting that compliance shocks cull organizations on capacity rather than mission.

A busy economist should care because this is potentially a vivid case of how seemingly low-burden administrative rules can produce large compositional changes in a sector, with selection operating through organizational capacity and information rather than formal policy intent.

### Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Not quite. The current opening is vivid, but it spends too much time on the institutional setup before clarifying the real economic question. The paper currently sounds like “here is a large administrative event in the nonprofit sector” rather than “here is a general lesson about compliance cliffs, organizational selection, and the distributional effects of regulation.” The first two paragraphs need to move faster from event description to economic stakes.

### The pitch the paper should have

In 2011, a new IRS filing requirement triggered the automatic revocation of tax-exempt status for 377,000 nonprofits in the largest one-time administrative purge of organizations in U.S. history. This paper asks a simple but important question: when regulation creates a compliance cliff, which organizations die and which find a way back?

Using the full universe of IRS revocations and later reinstatements, I show that survival was driven less by charitable purpose than by institutional capacity. Organizations tied to tangible assets and embedded local infrastructure were much more likely to reinstate, especially in the first “surprise” wave, implying that even minimal administrative requirements can reshape a sector by selecting on compliance capacity rather than social value.

That is the AER-adjacent version of the paper. It leads with a world question, not an archival fact.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper documents that a massive compliance shock in the U.S. nonprofit sector selected organizations on institutional capacity—proxied by asset-holding organizational forms—rather than on charitable mission, thereby permanently changing sector composition.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?

Only partially. The paper says prior work has described the revocation event or state-level variation, and that no published paper has used it to study “organizational survival determinants.” That is useful, but not yet enough. Right now the paper’s novelty still reads as: “first paper to use this administrative episode and describe heterogeneity in reinstatement.” That is a thin novelty claim for AER.

It needs sharper differentiation along one of these dimensions:

1. **Relative to nonprofit-sector papers:**  
   “Prior work studies nonprofit entry/exit, dissolution, and compliance using surveys or state registries; this paper is the first to exploit a national administrative compliance shock affecting the full sector.”

2. **Relative to compliance-cost papers:**  
   “Prior work shows fixed compliance costs matter for firms; this paper shows the same logic can operate through organizational death and sector composition in nonprofits, where financial incentives are weaker and capacity constraints matter more.”

3. **Relative to organizational-ecology papers:**  
   “Most organizational mortality studies observe diffuse exit over time; this paper observes a sudden, policy-induced mass mortality event and tracks selective resurrection.”

That last phrase—mass death plus selective resurrection—is memorable and potentially distinctive. The paper should lean harder into that.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or filling a gap in a LITERATURE?

It oscillates, and too often slips into gap-filling. The stronger version is clearly a world question:

- What happens when governments impose low-salience compliance requirements on administratively weak organizations?
- Do such rules clean out inactive entities, or do they disproportionately kill fragile but active ones?
- How do compliance cliffs reshape the composition of civil society?

That is much stronger than “the literature has not studied reinstatement patterns after the PPA.”

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?

Not confidently. Right now they would probably say: “It’s an administrative-data paper on nonprofit auto-revocations showing some organizations were more likely to reinstate.” That is not enough. They need to be able to say something like: “It shows that a nominally light-touch filing rule caused a huge culling of nonprofits, and survival was determined by administrative rootedness rather than mission—an example of regressive selection through compliance.”

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Most importantly: **broaden the object of interest from reinstatement to sectoral selection and real organizational consequences.** Right now reinstatement is a useful outcome, but too intermediate and bureaucratic. To make the contribution feel larger, the paper needs one of the following:

- **A stronger outcome:** not just who reinstated, but how the composition of civil society changed—by geography, subsector, community function, donor-facing status, or service orientation.
- **A more persuasive mechanism:** information frictions vs. fixed compliance costs vs. legal-identity value. Right now “physical asset” is a rough proxy, and the mechanism remains suggestive.
- **A more general framing:** connect to the economics of administrative burden, state capacity, and the regulation of small entities.
- **A more consequential welfare angle:** were the lost organizations likely dead shells, or active local institutions? Even partial evidence here would transform the paper.

If the paper could show that the purge disproportionately eliminated organizations serving certain communities or functions—not just certain tax code subsections—it would become much bigger.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Before this paper, one could believe that the PPA filing reform was mostly bureaucratic housekeeping: a way to clean dead entities from the nonprofit rolls at minimal cost, because filing a 990-N is trivial.

### Tension

If the filing burden was truly minimal, why did the first enforcement wave wipe out hundreds of thousands of organizations, and why did so few return? More importantly, were the organizations lost random dead shells, or was there systematic selection in who survived?

### Resolution

The paper finds strong selection: organizations with tangible institutional rootedness—especially asset-linked forms like cemeteries, veterans’ posts, and lodges—were much more likely to reinstate, with the gap concentrated in the first “surprise” wave. The interpretation is that compliance shocks disproportionately kill organizations lacking awareness, capacity, and administrative infrastructure.

### Implications

Administrative rules that look trivial on paper can have large and regressive compositional effects. In civil society, the organizations eliminated may be those least able to navigate bureaucracy rather than those least valuable socially.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the ingredients, but the arc is not fully disciplined. At present it reads somewhat like:
1. astonishing event,
2. descriptive heterogeneity by subsection,
3. a simple asset proxy,
4. a temporal comparison,
5. a broader interpretation.

That is coherent enough, but still feels more like a collection of stylized facts than a tightly advancing story. The central tension should be sharpened around a specific misconception:

> Was this purge merely removing inactive organizations, or did a low-level compliance rule selectively kill live but administratively weak ones?

Then every result should serve that question.

### If it is a collection of results looking for a story, what story should it be telling?

It should tell the story of **administrative burden as an engine of organizational selection**.

- The event is the shock.
- Reinstatement is the revealed measure of survival capacity.
- Asset-linked forms are not the point; they are evidence of rootedness/capacity.
- The later waves test whether surprise and information mattered.
- The real payoff is that regulation reallocated organizational survival toward embedded, legally sticky entities.

That is the story. Right now the paper sometimes instead tells the smaller story “cemeteries came back more than charities.”

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?

“In one administrative action, the IRS effectively killed more than 377,000 nonprofits, and 90 percent never came back; the ones that survived were not the most charitable, but the most institutionally rooted.”

That is a strong opening fact.

### Would people lean in?

Yes—initially. The event is large and surprising. But the second sentence has to deliver a general insight quickly. If the presenter then drifts into subsection codes and Business Master File matching, people will reach for their phones.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Probably one of these:
1. “But were these real organizations or mostly dead shells?”
2. “Why should I think this is about compliance burden rather than just legal stakes of different organizational forms?”
3. “What broader lesson does this teach beyond nonprofits?”

Those are exactly the questions the paper must anticipate in its framing.

### If findings are modest, is the null itself interesting?

The findings are not null, but they are still modest in one sense because the core quantitative object is reinstatement heterogeneity. The paper therefore must persuade readers that this is not just bureaucratic bookkeeping. At the moment it partially succeeds. To fully succeed, it needs to explain why learning about selective resurrection reveals something deep about organizational survival under administrative burden.

The paper cannot afford to feel like a failed experiment on nonprofit compliance. It has to feel like a clean demonstration that low-salience regulation changes who exists.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

Bluntly: in its current form, this is **not yet an AER paper**, but it has the seed of one. The event is extraordinary and the data are interesting. The problem is that the paper currently feels **competent, vivid, and somewhat narrow** rather than field-defining.

### What is the gap?

Mostly a mix of:

- **Framing problem:** yes, strongly.
- **Scope problem:** yes.
- **Novelty problem:** somewhat.
- **Ambition problem:** yes.

#### Framing problem
The paper undersells the general economics question and oversells the IRS episode. It needs to be about administrative burden and organizational selection, not just nonprofit revocation patterns.

#### Scope problem
The paper leans heavily on one outcome—reinstatement—which is only an indirect measure of survival and social consequence. For AER, the authors likely need a broader set of stakes: organizational activity, community role, sector composition, geography, service categories, or long-run ecosystem effects.

#### Novelty problem
“First paper on this administrative event” is not enough. The paper needs to be the paper that uses this event to answer a question economists care about outside the nonprofit niche.

#### Ambition problem
The paper is a bit too safe. It is mostly descriptive plus a simple mechanism test. A top-field audience will ask: what belief should I update? The answer must be bigger than “asset-heavy groups reinstated more.”

### The single most impactful piece of advice

**Rewrite the paper around one big claim: low-burden administrative regulation can function as a regressive selection mechanism that reshapes civil society by screening on compliance capacity rather than mission—and then reorganize the evidence to serve that claim.**

If they can only change one thing, change that. Everything else follows from it: introduction, literature, result ordering, and what additional evidence is worth collecting.
