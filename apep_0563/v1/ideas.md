# Research Ideas

## Idea 1: Eat In or Take Out? Tax Salience and Consumer Switching at Japan's Dual-Rate Consumption Tax Boundary
**Policy:** On October 1, 2019, Japan raised its consumption tax from 8% to 10% but introduced a reduced rate of 8% for takeout food and beverages (excluding alcohol). The same food item faces different tax rates depending on where it is consumed: a convenience store onigiri costs 8% if taken out but 10% if eaten at the in-store seating area. Restaurant meals are 10%; the same meal as takeout is 8%. Alcohol is always 10% regardless. This creates a within-product, within-store price wedge of approximately 1.85%. Legal basis: Act on Special Measures Concerning Taxation. Japan's first dual-rate system since the consumption tax's 1989 introduction.

**Outcome:** Japan's Family Income and Expenditure Survey (FIES), monthly, available on e-Stat (www.e-stat.go.jp). Detailed commodity-classification expenditure data for ~8,000 two-or-more-person households per month. Key categories: "Meals outside the home" (eat-in, 10%), "Cooked food" (takeout/prepared food, 8%), "Alcoholic beverages" (always 10%, serves as within-food control). Secondary: Japan CPI by commodity group (Statistics Bureau), providing price-level changes for food at home vs. food away from home.

**Identification:** Sharp temporal discontinuity + triple difference. October 1, 2019 implementation creates a sharp discontinuity in relative prices between eat-in (10%) and takeout (8%) food. First difference (time): Pre vs. post October 2019. Second difference (category): Takeout food (lower rate) vs. eat-in food (higher rate) — both are "prepared food," isolating the tax differential. Third difference (within-food placebo): Alcoholic beverages went from 8% to 10% regardless of eat-in/takeout — no dual rate. Complementary bunching design: "Takeout declaration" behavior — customers claim takeout to pay 8% but eat in-store.

**Why it's novel:** (1) No empirical study has rigorously estimated the behavioral response to Japan's dual-rate food tax. (2) Unique natural experiment: No other country taxes the same product at different rates based solely on consumption location. (3) First-order policy relevance: Multiple countries debate reduced VAT rates for food. (4) Mechanism richness: Consumer switching, firm pricing, and "eat-in tax evasion" (documented social phenomenon). (5) No APEP papers on Japan, consumption tax design, or tax salience.

**Feasibility check:** Variation: confirmed — Sharp October 1, 2019 implementation. 8% vs 10% dual rate on food. Data access: confirmed — e-Stat has 3,901 downloadable Excel datasets for FIES. Long-term monthly expenditure file downloaded (519KB, 865 rows). Current monthly data confirmed (43KB). Novelty: confirmed — No study on Japan's 2019 dual-rate food tax behavioral response. Sample size: ~8,000 households/month; 24+ months pre and post treatment.
