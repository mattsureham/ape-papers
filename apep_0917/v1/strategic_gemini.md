## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether local police departments circumvent state-level restrictions on civil asset forfeiture by "outsourcing" their seizures to the federal government via the Equitable Sharing program. Using a staggered DiD design and a newly obtained FOIA dataset of over 60,000 agency-year observations, the author finds a precisely estimated null result: state reforms do *not* lead to an increase in federalized seizures. This suggests that the "federal loophole," long decried by civil libertarians and assumed by previous cross-sectional research, does not actually function as a meaningful escape valve in practice.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this pitch excellently. The first two paragraphs provide a compelling hook (the Philadelphia couple), define the mechanism (jurisdictional arbitrage), and state the empirical finding. The "pitch the paper should have" is almost exactly what is written.

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:** The paper provides the first causal evidence that state-level civil asset forfeiture reforms do not trigger regulatory leakage into federal channels.

*   **Differentiation:** It is clearly differentiated from Holcomb et al. (2011) by moving from cross-sectional correlation to a staggered DiD framework with agency-level data.
*   **World vs. Literature:** It is framed as answering a question about the **world** (the effectiveness of state-level police reform) rather than just filling a gap in the literature.
*   **"Another DiD paper?":** A smart economist would recognize this as a "well-powered null on a high-stakes policy question." It isn't just "another DiD" because the sign of the result contradicts the standard political economy intuition of leakage.
*   **Making it bigger:** To make the contribution "AER-big," the author needs to lean harder into the **mechanisms** (Section 6). *Why* is there no leakage? Is it transaction costs, political alignment, or the 2015/2017 federal policy shocks? A more formal test or heterogeneous exploration of *why* the arbitrage fails would elevate the paper.

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

The paper sits at the intersection of **Law & Economics** and **Public Finance (Federalism)**.

*   **Closest Neighbors:** Holcomb et al. (2011) on forfeiture leakage; Makowsky and Stratmann (2009, 2019) on the political economy of police revenue and regulatory shift; and the newer staggered DiD literature (Callaway & Sant’Anna, 2021).
*   **Positioning:** It acts as a "corrective" to the previous literature. It should position itself as a bridge between the "policing for profit" literature and the broader "regulatory leakage" literature.
*   **Missing Conversations:** The paper could engage more with the **Bureaucratic Agency** literature. Why do agents (police) not respond to price incentives as theory predicts? Is it a "sticky" organizational culture?

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

*   **Setup:** States are cracking down on "policing for profit."
*   **Tension:** Economists expect "leakage"—if you tax an activity in one jurisdiction, it moves to another. The federal "Equitable Sharing" program is the perfect shadow jurisdiction.
*   **Resolution:** Surprisingly, the leakage doesn't happen. The "escape valve" is a myth.
*   **Implications:** State reforms are more powerful than we thought, and the case for federal intervention changes from "stopping evasion" to "setting a floor."

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong and punchy. It follows the "Surprising Null" template, which is a classic AER narrative.

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

**The Dinner Party Fact:** "Everyone thinks local cops just bypass state laws by calling the Feds to seize cars and cash for them. We looked at 7,000 agencies over a decade: they don't actually do it."

**Reaction:** People will lean in. The follow-up question is always: "Then where did the money go? Did they stop seizing stuff, or did they just start writing more speeding tickets?" The paper's "So What?" is high because it challenges a very intuitive economic assumption about arbitrage.

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

*   **Front-loading:** The paper is well-structured. Table 2 and the Event Study (Table 3) are where they belong.
*   **Discussion (Section 6):** This is currently the most speculative part. It needs to be the "heart" of the paper if it wants to move from a "good empirical note" to a "major article." I would suggest the author attempt to empirically test the "Holder/Sessions" policy shock interaction more explicitly.
*   **Appendix:** The "Standardized Effect Sizes" (Table 6) are actually very helpful for an AER audience to judge the "preciseness" of the null. I would move a version of this into the main text to bolster the "well-powered" claim.

---

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The gap between "Solid Public Finance Paper" and "AER Paper" is **Generalizability and Mechanism.**

Currently, the paper is a very good "evaluation" of a specific policy. To be a top-tier "economics" paper, it needs to speak to the broader theory of **why jurisdictional arbitrage fails.** Is it because the "costs of the loophole" (federal paperwork/oversight) are higher than we thought? Or is it because the agents (local police) are constrained by local political "norms" that shift alongside the law?

**The single most impactful piece of advice:**
Attempt to "price" the transaction costs of the federal loophole. If the author can show that agencies only use the escape valve when the "spread" between state and federal rules exceeds a certain threshold, the paper becomes a general study of transaction costs in federalism, not just a paper about police seizures.
