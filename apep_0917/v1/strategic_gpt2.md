## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a sharp policy-relevant question: when states restrict civil asset forfeiture, do police departments evade those restrictions by shifting seizures into the federal equitable sharing program? Using newly assembled national agency-level data, the paper’s central claim is no: the widely feared federal “escape valve” does not appear to be used at meaningful scale after state reform.

A busy economist should care because this is really a paper about whether subnational regulation in a federal system is undermined by jurisdictional arbitrage. If the answer is no in a setting where circumvention seemed especially plausible, that matters beyond forfeiture.

**Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Reasonably well, but not optimally. The current opening is vivid and the basic question appears quickly, but the introduction still reads a bit like a policy-note-plus-methods-summary rather than a top-journal statement of a broader economic question. The first two paragraphs should more explicitly elevate the paper from “forfeiture reform” to “when does federalism create an escape valve, and when does it not?”

### The pitch the paper should have

> In federal systems, state regulation is often thought to fail because affected actors can shift activity into a more permissive federal or neighboring jurisdiction. Civil asset forfeiture is a stark test of that logic: as 38 states tightened forfeiture laws, critics argued that police would simply reroute seizures through the federal equitable sharing program, preserving revenues while evading state constraints.  
>   
> This paper shows that this widely cited escape valve does not, in practice, appear to operate. Using the universe of agency-level federal equitable sharing records, I find that state forfeiture reform does not increase agencies’ use of the federal channel. The contribution is not just to forfeiture policy: it is to show that legal arbitrage opportunities that look obvious on paper may be constrained in practice by transaction costs, institutions, and local political incentives.

That version makes the paper sound less like “a DiD on forfeiture revenues” and more like “a test of regulatory leakage in a federal system.”

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:**  
The paper provides national causal evidence that state civil forfeiture reforms did not induce law-enforcement agencies to shift seizure activity into the federal equitable sharing program.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?
Partly, but not sharply enough. The paper differentiates itself from Holcomb et al. by saying that prior evidence was cross-sectional and this is causal. That is useful but still a little thin as a top-journal distinction. “We do the same question better” is not enough unless the question is central and the answer is surprising.

The paper should more cleanly distinguish itself from:
1. prior **forfeiture-policy** work asking whether forfeiture creates incentives or affects crime,
2. prior **cross-sectional circumvention** claims,
3. broader **regulatory leakage/federalism** papers documenting substitution across jurisdictions,
4. recent **policing-for-revenue** work on institutional incentives.

Right now the paper is too reliant on “causal identification” as the differentiator. Referees can assess that. Strategically, the differentiator should be: **this paper overturns a canonical policy objection to state reform in a setting where circumvention looked almost tailor-made to occur.**

### World question or literature-gap question?
It is mostly framed as a **world question**, which is good: does state reform leak into federal channels? But it periodically slips into literature-gap language (“bring causal identification to the circumvention question”). The stronger framing is clearly the world one.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the intro?
Yes, but with some risk. A smart economist could say: “It tests whether police circumvent state forfeiture reform via federal equitable sharing and finds no evidence.” That is intelligible and crisp.

But another smart economist could also summarize it dismissively as: “It’s another staggered DiD on a policy reform with a null effect.” That is the strategic danger.

### What would make the contribution bigger?
Specific ways to enlarge it:

1. **Shift the object from revenues to behavior.**  
   Revenue is an imperfect proxy for circumvention. A bigger paper would get closer to seizure counts, case composition, or the share of forfeitures routed federally versus state channels. If the paper could show that actual routing behavior does not change, not just revenue, the contribution becomes much stronger.

2. **Show where the missing substitution went.**  
   If agencies do not substitute into federal equitable sharing, what do they do instead? Do total forfeitures fall? Do criminal forfeitures rise? Do fines/fees rise? Do drug-enforcement patterns shift? A top-field-reader will immediately ask: “Okay, no federal leakage—so what margin adjusts?”

3. **Exploit heterogeneity tied to actual circumvention feasibility.**  
   The strongest version would test whether effects are larger where federal partnership is easiest—e.g., agencies with prior federal task-force participation, border/interdiction exposure, DEA/FBI presence, or pre-period equitable-sharing dependence. If even those agencies do not substitute, the null becomes far more informative.

4. **Reframe around a broader economic concept.**  
   The paper should not merely say “the loophole didn’t matter here.” It should say: **formal arbitrage opportunities do not necessarily generate realized leakage when implementation is organizationally costly.** That is a much bigger claim.

5. More broadly, papers on **regulatory leakage/jurisdictional arbitrage in federal systems**—banking, environmental regulation, tax competition, etc.

(If this were moving forward, I would want the authors to tighten the citations here; the current list is a bit eclectic and occasionally drifts toward legal scholarship rather than the core empirical-economic conversation.)

### How should the paper position itself?
Mostly **build on and revise**, not attack.  
The right posture is:

- prior work established that forfeiture creates strong revenue incentives;
- critics plausibly inferred that agencies would exploit the federal channel;
- this paper shows that this particular arbitrage margin did not materialize after reform.

That is stronger and more persuasive than “prior literature was wrong because it was only cross-sectional.”

### Too narrow or too broad?
Currently it is **narrowly positioned in topic, but broadly gesturing in implication**.  
That combination creates tension. The paper is about one institutionally specific loophole, but it wants to speak to “regulatory leakage in federal systems” generally. It has not yet earned that generality.

The better approach is to make a more disciplined general claim: this is an unusually clean case where legal arbitrage was salient, lucrative, and loudly predicted—and yet absent. That is enough.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?
It could do more with:

- **Organizational economics / state capacity / implementation**: why agencies fail to exploit available legal margins.
- **Law and economics of federalism**: not just leakage, but overlapping authority, enforcement complementarity, and transaction costs across sovereigns.
- **Public finance / tax evasion / regulatory arbitrage** analogies: when nominal legal substitution margins bind versus remain latent.
- **Crime and policing institutional design**: especially papers on task forces, federal-local cooperation, and discretionary enforcement structures.

Right now the paper is speaking mainly to forfeiture specialists and some criminal-justice economists. It should also speak to economists interested in **when policy escape valves actually matter**.

### Is it having the right conversation?
Almost, but not quite. The best conversation is not “civil forfeiture is bad/good” and not “here is a cleaner DiD.” The right conversation is:

> When subnational governments restrict a revenue-generating enforcement practice, do agencies arbitrage into a more permissive jurisdiction—or do institutional frictions block that response?

That would connect this paper to a wider, more important set of readers.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
States reformed civil forfeiture because it was seen as abusive and distortionary. Critics responded that state reform may be ineffective because agencies can circumvent it through federal equitable sharing.

### Tension
The loophole story is highly plausible on paper and has become conventional wisdom in policy and legal debates. If true, state-level reform may be mostly symbolic.

### Resolution
Using national agency-level data, the paper finds no meaningful increase in federal equitable sharing after state reform.

### Implications
The obvious federal escape valve may not actually function as assumed. State reform may therefore have more bite than critics claim, and more broadly, legal arbitrage opportunities may be less important in practice than institutional constraints.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
Yes, more than many empirical papers. It is not a random collection of tables. There is a real story here.

But the arc is still **more serviceable than memorable**. The tension is strong, the resolution is clear, but the implications are underdeveloped. The paper wants the reader to conclude something bigger than “null result on one administrative outcome,” but it has not fully built the bridge.

### If it is a collection of results looking for a story, what story should it tell?
Not exactly a collection of results, but it is currently overinvested in proving the null repeatedly. The story should be:

1. **This loophole was the central objection to state reform.**
2. **This is the first national test using the universe of federal participation records.**
3. **The objection fails empirically.**
4. **The reason matters: implementation frictions can shut down even seemingly lucrative arbitrage.**

That story is stronger than “baseline, event study, heterogeneity, robustness, more robustness.”

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?
“I looked at the full national record of federal equitable-sharing payments and found that when states restrict civil forfeiture, police departments do not respond by shifting into the federal program.”

That is the sentence to lead with.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
A decent number would lean in—especially economists in public finance, law and economics, crime, and political economy—because the loophole is intuitive and the null is somewhat surprising.

But they will only lean in if the paper is presented as overturning a key policy premise, not as “another null DiD.” Presentation matters enormously here.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Almost certainly:
- “Then where did the activity go?”
- “Are revenues the right measure of circumvention?”
- “Is the null driven by small agencies, federal policy changes, or inability to use the federal channel?”
- “Does total forfeiture fall, or does it just get relabeled?”

Those are strategic questions, not econometric ones. The paper needs better anticipation of them in the framing.

### Is the null itself interesting?
Yes—but only conditionally. A null can be AER-worthy if it overturns a widely held and policy-consequential belief. This paper is trying to do exactly that. The problem is that the paper currently spends too much time documenting the null and too little time persuading the reader that this was a belief worth overturning at scale.

It should make much more of:
- how central the circumvention claim has been to actual reform debates,
- how plausible the claim seemed ex ante,
- why learning that the leakage channel is absent changes what policymakers should believe.

Otherwise the null risks feeling like a failed search for an effect rather than a successful test of a high-stakes hypothesis.

4. **Shorten the methodology literature paragraph.**  
   The introduction’s final paragraph on staggered DiD methodology is generic and low-return for AER readers. It makes the paper feel method-led rather than question-led. It can be reduced sharply or moved.

5. **Discussion section should do more interpretive work.**  
   This is where the paper could become bigger. Right now the mechanisms are plausible but generic. The discussion should more directly answer: why didn’t the obvious arbitrage happen, and what does that teach us about federalism and enforcement incentives?

6. **Conclusion is too brief to add strategic value.**  
   It mostly summarizes. It should end with a broader implication: state reform can matter even without federal harmonization, and analysts should be more skeptical of “leakage by assumption” arguments.

Mostly yes. The question and answer arrive early. That is good.

### Are important results buried?
Not badly, but the paper’s most important “buried” result is conceptual, not empirical: the null in a setting where circumvention should have been easy. That point should be elevated more aggressively.

### What should be shorter/longer?
- **Shorter:** methodological throat-clearing, repeated robustness summaries, some table-by-table narration.
- **Longer:** framing of the policy premise being overturned, and interpretation of what the null means for regulatory leakage more generally.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

At present, the gap is mainly a mix of **framing problem** and **scope problem**.

### Framing problem
The science may be competent, but the paper undersells the conceptual point and oversells the econometric machinery. It still reads too much like a careful field-journal paper and not enough like a paper changing how economists think about regulatory leakage.

### Scope problem
The paper answers one substitution question on one outcome. For AER, readers will want either:
- stronger evidence on the underlying behavior, or
- a more expansive accounting of adjacent margins.

Right now the paper says “no substitution into federal equitable sharing.” The natural reaction is “fine, but what *did* happen?” Without at least some answer, the paper remains narrower than the journal typically wants.

### Novelty problem?
Moderate. The question is not wholly new; the setting is known; the empirical result is a cleaner answer to a prominent claim. That can still be publishable at a high level if the paper convincingly shows that a widely accepted objection is false. But the novelty is not self-executing.

### Ambition problem?
Yes, somewhat. The paper is solid and tidy, but safe. It proves one thing over and over. A more ambitious paper would use this setting to speak to a bigger question: when do formal escape valves fail to generate actual circumvention?

### Single most impactful advice
**Reframe the paper around the general lesson that apparent jurisdictional arbitrage opportunities often do not bind in practice, and back that claim with at least one additional piece of evidence on where behavior did—or did not—shift instead.**

That one change would do more than any amount of polishing around the edges.
