**Idea Fidelity**

The paper adheres closely to the manifest’s headline empirical idea—using Japan’s October 2019 dual-rate consumption tax to estimate the price wedge between restaurants (10%) and food-at-home (8%). The authors exploit the same OECD CPI data source, implement the prescribed DiD and triple-difference design leveraging the 2014 uniform hike as a placebo, and focus on the sharply divergent rates for identical prepared food consumed in different settings. However, the analysis stops short of several key elements highlighted in the idea. In particular, the promised investigation of the “takeout substitution effect” and the welfare implications that depend on household expenditure behavior are not carried out: there is no use of the Statistics Bureau’s FIES monthly panel that differentiates eating out versus takeout, and the paper does not quantify any behavioral response or substitution elasticity. When the story frames the reform as presenting a clean test of substitution between eat-in and takeout, the lack of micro spending evidence feels like a departure from the manifest’s scope.

**Summary**

The paper documents that Japan’s 2019 dual-rate consumption tax introduced a roughly 1.8 log-point divergence in CPI between restaurants (10% rate) and food-at-home (8% rate), consistent with near-complete pass-through of the 2-percentage-point differential. A built-in placebo using the uniform 2014 hike confirms that the restaurant-food gap would have been null absent the split-rate reform, and a triple-difference further isolates the differential effect. The findings are interpreted as evidence that the dual-rate structure created a meaningful price wedge with potential welfare and distortionary consequences.

**Essential Points**

1. **Quantifying the behavioral margin is necessary before speaking to substitution or welfare.** The paper frames the reform as creating a wedge that induces substitution between eating in and taking out, yet the empirical analysis only documents price changes. The manifest promised to use the FIES household expenditure data to discern how spending shifted across eat-in versus takeout categories. Without any evidence on quantities, it is premature to claim the reform created a meaningful substitution effect or to assess welfare costs.

2. **The identification strategy needs more rigorous credibility checks.** The core DiD relies on one treated category (CP11) versus one control (CP01) over a very short post-reform window (Oct 2019–Jan 2020). While the 2014 placebo is reassuring, the paper should present an event-study-style assessment to demonstrate the absence of differential pre-trends within the pre-period (e.g., July–Sept 2019) and to show the jump is unique to October 2019. Right now the paper implicitly assumes that any sudden gap is due to the tax change; additional dynamics would increase confidence.

3. **The “restaurant” category is heterogeneous and includes elements unrelated to dining (e.g., hotels).** CP11 contains both restaurants and hotels, and does not isolate the fast-food/eat-in venues most likely affected by the takeout boundary. Because hotels may have had idiosyncratic price movements or different exposure to other shocks, this raises concerns that the estimated effect mixes the tax response with unrelated dynamics. The paper should either use more disaggregated subindices (if available) or provide robustness checks that isolate food-related subcomponents within CP11.

**Suggestions**

- **Integrate household expenditure data to measure substitution.** The idea manifest indicated access to the FIES monthly panel with categories such as “eating out” (1.12) and “takeout/prepared food” (1.9). Incorporating this data would allow you to estimate how quantities responded to the price wedge and to compute substitution elasticities, enabling a welfare discussion grounded in consumer behavior. Even in the absence of precise price data, a before‑after comparison of spending shares would demonstrate whether the tax differential induced meaningful reallocation between takeout and dine-in. If FIES is unavailable, explain explicitly why and consider alternative micro datasets that capture consumption mode (e.g., convenience store sales, survey data).

- **Conduct a fuller event-study analysis and extend pre-period checks.** With only 13 CPI categories, it is feasible to plot the restaurant minus food gap for many months before and after the reform (not just July–October 2019). Presenting leads and lags of the treatment indicator would reveal whether any divergence began before October 2019 (anticipatory behavior) or whether other shocks coincided with the reform. In addition to the placebo around the 2014 hike, this would strengthen the credibility of the parallel-trends assumption. Consider also controlling for seasonality (e.g., month-of-year fixed effects if not already fully absorbed) and checking whether other major CPI divisions experienced discontinuities around the same time.

- **Disaggregate the “restaurants and hotels” category or use auxiliary data to focus on the eating-in margin.** If COICOP subcategories within CP11 are available (e.g., “restaurants and cafés” vs “hotels”), re-estimating the DiD on the restaurant-specific subindex would reduce contamination from hotel pricing and better capture the relevant margin. Alternatively, supplement the CPI analysis with point-of-sale data (e.g., convenience store chains) that report separate eat-in versus takeout prices. If finer CPI detail is unavailable, at least provide evidence that hotel prices were stable around October 2019 so that CP11 mainly reflects restaurant behavior.

- **Clarify the interpretation of the triple-difference.** The paper’s specification stacks the 2014 and 2019 windows but does not elaborate on whether the triple interaction controls for time-varying shocks that affected restaurants differently in 2014 versus 2019 (e.g., consumption trends, tourism). Discuss what variation is being differenced out and whether any remaining differences could confound the estimate. For instance, if October 2019 saw demand shifts unrelated to the tax (e.g., a change in inbound tourism), these could bias the triple-difference. Consider augmenting the model with controls for tourism indicators or constructing a placebo triple-difference using another category-pair that should remain unaffected.

- **Address the interpretation of heterogenous treatment effects across categories.** Column (2) in Table 2 averages CP02–CP12 against CP01 and finds an insignificant negative coefficient, which you attribute to heterogeneity. It would be helpful to unpack this further: Present the individual DiD estimates for other 10%-rate categories (CP02, CP03, etc.) to illustrate which ones stray from the expected pattern and why. This would also clarify whether restaurants are unique in their response or if the tax differential had broader effects.

- **Discuss potential measurement issues with CPI and tax pass-through.** The CPI is a chained index; if the tax change altered the composition of goods consumed within a category (e.g., consumers shifting from restaurant meals to cheaper ones within CP11), the index may reflect quality or basket changes rather than pure price pass-through. Address whether the IO components of the CPI were stable and whether the index construction could bias the estimate. You may also consider re-weighting or using producer price indices if available.

- **Provide more detail on the welfare implications and compliance costs.** The discussion mentions the “rice ball” problem and suggests distortionary substitution. If you cannot measure substitution directly, frame these as hypotheses or provide supporting evidence (qualitative or from market reports) on how consumers or retailers responded (e.g., increased eat-in declarations?). If compliance issues were reported (as suggested by \citet{nikkei2019confusion}), cite concrete numbers or anecdotes that demonstrate the practical distortions of the split-rate system.

- **Tone down overstatements about policy lessons in the conclusion.** While the price wedge is robust, stating that “reduced rates achieve their price objectives, but at the cost of creating price distortions along economically meaningless margins” overreaches without evidence on the actual economic costs or consumer behavior. Consider concluding that the reform produced a sharp price wedge that merits further investigation of behavioral responses before endorsing broad policy judgments.

Overall, the paper offers a neat application of Japan’s dual-rate reform to price pass-through, but strengthening the credibility of the identification, incorporating behavioral evidence, and clarifying the scope of the conclusions will significantly improve its contribution.
