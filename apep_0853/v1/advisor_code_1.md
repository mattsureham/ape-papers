**Idea Fidelity**  
The paper broadly delivers on the manifest. It studies staggered U.S. state adoption of cottage food laws using Census Nonemployer Statistics, exploits a CS-type DiD with event-study checks, and assesses outcomes relevant to grassroots food entrepreneurship. The manifest’s emphasis on both extensive (first adoption) and intensive (expansions) margins is reflected in the robustness table, and the placebo on employer establishments and the focus on NAICS 311 align with the proposed identification strategy. One gap: the manifest mentioned CDC NORS outbreak data (as a welfare check), but the submitted paper does not analyze foodborne outbreaks; incorporating even preliminary results or explaining why they were dropped would better honor the original plan.

**Summary**  
The paper documents that staggered liberalization of state cottage food laws raised nonemployer food manufacturing establishments by roughly 8 percent, with effects growing over five years, using Callaway–Sant’Anna DiD and multiple robustness checks. The estimated response is driven by the micro-entrepreneurial margin—bakeries in particular—while employer food firms show no response, supporting the interpretation that deregulation unlocked previously constrained home-based production. The paper contributes to literature on occupational licensing and regulatory barriers to entrepreneurship by highlighting regulation of facilities (rather than people) as a meaningful fixed-cost constraint.

**Essential Points**  
1. **Parallel Trends and Comparison Group Composition.** While the event study shows flat pre-trends, the comparison group includes states with pre-existing cottage food laws, potentially differing systematically (e.g., already higher entrepreneurship or demand for local foods). The paper should provide more evidence that these states are valid counterfactuals—perhaps through propensity-score weighting, covariate balance on pre-trends, or by showing that the estimator yields similar results when restricting to clean never-treated states (e.g., New Jersey prior to 2021) rather than states with early cottage food regimes.  
2. **Treatment Timing Definition and Heterogeneous Effects.** “Significant expansion” is defined qualitatively, yet the results hinge on pooling first-adoption and expansion events. It is unclear how sensitive findings are to the precise coding (e.g., how large must a sales cap change be, which expansions count). A more transparent rule, possibly with an index of permissiveness as hinted in the manifest, would make the treatment assignment more defensible. Additionally, the weaker response for expansions (Column 5) suggests heterogeneity; the paper should explore heterogeneous ATT estimates more thoroughly (e.g., by sales cap increases or product scope) to clarify what aspect of deregulation drives the results.  
3. **External Validity of Nonemployer Establishments as a Proxy for Cottage Food Activity.** NES NAICS 311 aggregates all nonemployer food manufacturers, including not just home bakers but also food trucks, home caterers, or solo artisanal producers. The paper notes this limitation, but the ATT is interpreted as the effect of cottage food deregulation. The authors should strengthen the case that the observed response indeed comes from cottage food producers—perhaps by showing similar results for NAICS 3118 (bakeries) when controlling for broader industry trends, examining textual evidence (state-level permits or registrations, if available), or interacting treatment with characteristics that proxy for cottage food take-up (e.g., rural share, SNAP participation). Without this, the link between policy and outcome remains suggestive rather than precise.

If addressing these central issues is not feasible, the paper requires major revisions before publication.

**Suggestions**  
1. **Expand the discussion of the comparison group and explore alternative control sets.** The manifest emphasized never-treated New Jersey, but the paper’s core estimators use not-yet-treated states, many of which already had some cottage food law features. Consider replicating results using only states that never had any cottage food law before treatment to show that estimated ATT does not vanish when restricting to “pure” untreated states. Additionally, present pre-treatment trends for treated versus comparison states separately (e.g., average outcomes in treated cohorts and in comparison states) to reassure readers that the parallel-trends assumption is credible outside of model-based event studies.

2. **Quantify the treatment severity with an index.** The paper mentions “composite permissiveness index” in the manifest but never constructs it. Even if the index is only used in robustness checks (e.g., regressing nonemployer establishments on the index rather than a binary), it would help differentiate between modest expansions and wholesale deregulation (like Wyoming’s act). A continuous measure could also enable dose–response analysis: do states that liberalized more (higher sales caps, broader product lists, more venues) experience larger jumps in micro-entry?

3. **More explicitly connect treatment to the target population.** Because NAICS 311 is a broad category, consider merging in auxiliary data that isolates cottage food-like activity. Publicly available state cottage food registries (where they exist) might provide counts of registered cottage food businesses over time; even snapshots could corroborate the NES result. Alternatively, use industry subcategories (e.g., NAICS 3118 for bakeries, 311811 for retail bakeries, 311820 for cookie/bakery mix production) or product-level receipts to show that the growth is concentrated in expected lines. Another angle is to test whether the effect is stronger in states with larger rural populations or fewer commercial kitchens (proxied by the density of licensed food facilities) to bolster the mechanism that facility requirements were binding.

4. **Address potential spillovers or supply-side constraints.** The paper interprets the growing effect over five years as new entry rather than one-time formalization. Yet it could also reflect the gradual diffusion of policy awareness or supply-side bottlenecks (limited commercial kitchen capacity). Discuss alternative interpretations and consider supplementary analyses, such as whether states with prior awareness campaigns (e.g., Institute for Justice outreach) saw faster responses, or whether the effect is conditioned on commercial kitchen availability (e.g., incubator kitchens per capita).

5. **Ensure robustness to pandemic-related disruptions.** The 2012–2021 panel includes 2020–2021 when the pandemic affected both entrepreneurship and reporting. The event study and placebo help, but consider re-estimating the main models excluding 2020 and/or 2021 to assess whether results hinge on pandemic years. If coefficients change materially, explain why (e.g., cottage food laws adopted during the pandemic may have different effects). Alternatively, include controls for pandemic-related federal aid programs or mobility restrictions to ensure the estimated treatment effect is not conflated with those shocks.

6. **Clarify inference procedure for Callaway–Sant’Anna results.** The notes mention bootstrap inference but do not specify whether bootstraps are clustered or stratified by treatment cohort. Given the small number of states and clustered nature of the data, detailing the bootstrap structure (e.g., wild bootstrap, block bootstrap) will help readers assess the reliability of the stated standard errors.

7. **Discuss welfare or health implications, even if only exploratory.** The manifest proposed using CDC NORS outbreak data to evaluate food safety, which is absent here. Even if the data are not used in formal estimation, summarizing whether cottage food states experienced any detectable increase in outbreaks (per capita or relative to other settings) would enrich the policy discussion. If the data are too sparse, explicitly state that and outline why the analysis was infeasible.

8. **Report more granular coverage of treatment timing events.** A table listing states, treatment years, and a brief description of the legal change (first adoption, sales cap increase, product scope expansion) would aid reproducibility and help readers assess heterogeneity. This could also feed into heterogeneity tests by grouping states by treatment type.

Overall, the paper is a promising contribution. Addressing these suggestions would deepen the credibility of the identification strategy, sharpen the link between deregulation and cottage food activity, and better situate the findings within the broader occupational licensing and entrepreneurship literatures.
