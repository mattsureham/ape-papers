## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple and potentially important question: when states make it legal to sell certain foods from a home kitchen, do they create new entrepreneurs? Using staggered adoption of cottage food laws across U.S. states, the paper argues that removing commercial-kitchen and licensing requirements increases entry into very small food businesses. A busy economist should care because this is a clean case of a fixed-cost regulatory barrier at the very bottom of the firm-size distribution: not “does regulation matter?” in general, but whether modest legal barriers prevent economically meaningful market entry by would-be micro-entrepreneurs.

The paper **mostly** articulates this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs, but not as sharply as it could. The current opening is vivid, which is good, but it gets pulled quickly into institutional detail before fully stating the broader economic question. The first two paragraphs should do less scene-setting and more work telling the reader why this is a first-order economics question rather than an interesting state-policy episode.

### The pitch the paper should have in the first two paragraphs

For many potential entrepreneurs, the main barrier to entry is not skill, finance, or demand, but a fixed regulatory cost of becoming legal. Cottage food laws provide a sharp test of this idea: by allowing people to sell low-risk foods from home kitchens, states remove one of the smallest yet most concrete regulatory barriers to market entry. This paper asks whether doing so actually creates new businesses.

I study staggered adoption and expansion of cottage food laws across U.S. states and show that deregulating home-based food production increases nonemployer food businesses by about 8 percent, with effects growing over time. The broader point is that regulation shapes entrepreneurship not only for conventional firms, but also at the micro-enterprise margin where legal entry may hinge on something as simple as access to a certified kitchen.

That is the AER-relevant pitch. It leads with a world question, not a policy curiosity.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### Contribution in one sentence

The paper’s contribution is to show that removing home-kitchen production requirements through cottage food deregulation increases entry into nonemployer food businesses, suggesting that fixed regulatory costs meaningfully suppress micro-entrepreneurship.

### Is it clearly differentiated from the closest papers?

Only partially. The paper says there is “zero prior economics research” on cottage food laws, which may be true in a narrow sense, but that is not enough. “Nobody has studied this exact statute” is not a strong top-journal contribution by itself. The paper needs to differentiate itself from adjacent literatures in a more conceptually ambitious way.

Right now, the differentiation is:

- not occupational licensing exactly,
- not firm registration costs exactly,
- not informality exactly.

That is directionally right, but still defensive and literature-gap driven. A reader could still come away thinking: “This is another staggered-adoption regulation-and-entry paper, just in food.”

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a gap in a literature?

Too much as filling a literature gap. The strongest version is a world question:

- **Do fixed compliance costs prevent ultra-small businesses from entering the formal market?**
- **Can deregulation generate genuinely new business formation at the micro-enterprise margin?**
- **Where are the extensive-margin gains from deregulation largest?**

That framing is much stronger than “first paper on cottage food laws.”

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the intro?

At present, maybe, but not confidently. They could probably say: “It’s a DiD paper showing cottage food laws increase nonemployer food businesses.” That is understandable, but it is not yet memorable. They are less likely to say: “This paper identifies a distinct type of entry barrier—facility-based fixed costs—and shows that removing it creates new firms among people who otherwise would never show up in standard entrepreneurship data.” That second version is what the introduction should make effortless.

### What would make the contribution bigger?

Most importantly: **move from documenting entry to characterizing what kind of entry this is.** Specific ways:

1. **Sharper outcome targeting.**  
   NAICS 311 is broad. If the paper could get closer to the products actually permitted under cottage food laws—baked goods, jams, candy, etc.—the contribution becomes much more than “food manufacturing went up.”

2. **Stronger heterogeneity tied to policy intensity.**  
   Not all cottage food laws are alike. The paper hints at this but does not build the contribution around it. The bigger question is whether entry responds more when deregulation is truly meaningful: no permit, higher sales caps, broader products, online sales allowed. A dose-response framing would substantially raise the paper’s ambition.

3. **Mechanism through formalization vs new creation.**  
   The paper gestures at this using dynamic effects, but this should be central. If it can more convincingly argue these are newly created legal businesses rather than relabeling of informal activity, the contribution becomes much more important.

4. **Distributional or geographic heterogeneity.**  
   If the effect is larger in rural states, lower-income areas, or places with less access to commercial kitchen infrastructure, then the paper speaks to entrepreneurship opportunity, not just regulation.

   - Lafortune et al. or related formalization-policy work
   - De Giorgi and Rahman / Bruhn-style papers on formalization and registration if the paper wants a broader entrepreneurship audience

4. **Home-based business / self-employment / microenterprise**
   - This literature is less centrally cited here but may be more relevant than the paper acknowledges, especially work on constraints to self-employment and household enterprise.

### How should it position itself relative to those neighbors?

It should **build on and bridge**, not attack.

Best positioning:

- Relative to the entry-regulation literature: “Most evidence concerns conventional firms facing registration or labor-market rules; this paper studies a margin below the conventional firm, where the key constraint is a fixed production-facility requirement.”
- Relative to occupational licensing: “Unlike licensing, which often bundles skill-screening and quality regulation, cottage food laws primarily relax a facility-based fixed cost, offering a cleaner case of regulation as a barrier to ultra-small-scale entry.”
- Relative to informality/formalization: “This is a rich-country, low-stakes setting in which legalization may pull household production into observable markets, helping distinguish between relabeling and genuine market creation.”

That is a coherent conversation. It lets the paper speak to industrial organization, labor/public economics, and development-adjacent formalization literatures.

### Is it currently positioned too narrowly or too broadly?

A bit too narrowly in topic, and a bit too broadly in method. It overexplains the estimator and underexplains the substantive conversation. The audience currently feels like “people interested in staggered DiD and niche food regulation.” It should instead be “economists interested in entrepreneurship, regulation, informality, and market access.”

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?

At least three areas need more engagement:

1. **Home-based business / household enterprise / self-employment constraints**
   - The paper should look beyond food and beyond regulation. There is likely work on zoning, home-based work, household production, and barriers to self-employment.

2. **Business dynamism at the low end of the size distribution**
   - There is a broad literature on declining entrepreneurship and the composition of small-firm entry. This paper could speak to that.

3. **Gender and entrepreneurship**
   - This is the dog that didn’t bark. Cottage food production is widely understood to be a domain with many women entrepreneurs and caregivers seeking flexible income. Even if the paper cannot measure gender directly, the framing should at least acknowledge this possible connection. That could widen the conversation considerably.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?

Not quite. Right now it is having the “state policy deregulation” conversation. The higher-impact conversation is: **what kinds of regulation choke off the smallest forms of market participation, and what happens when those barriers are relaxed?** That is the conversation top readers care about.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Before this paper, the world looks like this: many states impose commercial-kitchen, inspection, and licensing requirements on people who want to sell low-risk homemade foods. These requirements may be trivial for established firms but large for tiny would-be entrants.

### Tension

The tension is whether these small-seeming rules actually bind. Do they suppress real entrepreneurship, or do they mostly regulate an activity that would be tiny, informal, or nonexistent anyway? More broadly, does deregulation at the extreme small-business margin create real market activity or merely legalize what was already happening?

### Resolution

The paper’s resolution is that cottage food deregulation increases nonemployer food establishments by around 8 percent, with effects that grow over time and do not appear in employer food manufacturing.

### Implications

The implication is that fixed regulatory costs matter even for the smallest businesses, and deregulation can create economically meaningful entry in places standard firm data often miss. That has implications for how economists think about entrepreneurship, informality, and the incidence of regulation on marginal entrants.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has a **serviceable** one, but it is still too much “collection of credible empirical exercises” and not enough “sharp story.” The main issue is that the tension is underdeveloped. The introduction moves very quickly from anecdote to design to estimates. It needs a clearer motivating puzzle:

- Why has economics underappreciated the smallest-scale regulatory barriers?
- Why might the effect here be surprisingly large or surprisingly small?
- Why does this setting let us learn something general?

The paper should be telling this story:

> We often debate whether regulation impedes entrepreneurship, but most evidence concerns conventional firms. This paper shows that at the micro-enterprise margin, the most binding barrier may be a simple fixed cost of legality. Cottage food laws offer a rare test of that proposition, and the results suggest that many “missing firms” are missing because regulation prevents them from ever appearing in official data.

That is a much stronger arc than “states changed cottage food laws and we estimated the effect.”

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?

I would lead with: **making it legal to sell baked goods and similar foods from a home kitchen increased sole-proprietor food businesses by about 8 percent.**

That is clean, intuitive, and dinner-party legible.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

Some would lean in, but many would not—unless the presenter immediately translates the finding into a broader claim. “Cottage food laws” alone sounds niche. “A small fixed compliance cost can prevent a large number of tiny businesses from existing legally” is more compelling. The current draft does not yet make that conversion forcefully enough.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Probably one of these:

1. “Are these genuinely new businesses or just formalized hobbyists?”
2. “Does this matter economically, or are these tiny side hustles?”
3. “What type of regulation matters most—the kitchen requirement, inspections, sales caps, or product restrictions?”
4. “Who are these entrepreneurs?”

Those are exactly the questions the framing should anticipate.

### If the findings are modest, is that okay?

Yes. An 8 percent increase is not trivial, but it is not a blockbuster headline on its own. The paper therefore needs to make the null alternative more salient: many economists might expect almost no effect because these are tiny businesses, often informal, with limited scale. If the author persuades the reader that such small regulatory changes are usually dismissed as first-order irrelevant, then learning that they generate meaningful entry becomes interesting.

Right now the paper somewhat undersells why the magnitude is surprising. It should make clearer that:

- the regulated activity is tiny and decentralized,
- the barrier itself is not a large tax or licensing fee,
- yet the entry response is economically noticeable.

That is the “so what.”

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

### What would make it read better?

1. **Shorten the estimator discussion in the introduction.**  
   The current introduction spends too much precious space on Callaway-Sant’Anna, Sun-Abraham, Bacon decomposition, and estimator comparison. That is not what sells the paper. For an editor deciding whether the paper belongs in the AER conversation, this is all secondary.

2. **Move most estimator-validation material out of the intro.**  
   The detailed discussion of downward bias in TWFE, decomposition weights, and alternative estimators belongs later. The introduction should state the design and the headline result, then turn quickly to why it matters.

3. **Strengthen and move up heterogeneity in law intensity.**  
   The paper’s best underexploited asset is variation in how permissive the laws are. If there is a compelling result there, it should move into the main results, not sit as a side robustness.

4. **Compress the institutional background.**  
   It is useful, but currently reads like a policy brief. Tighten it around the specific dimensions that map to an economic mechanism: fixed costs, allowable product scope, sales channels, and sales caps.

5. **Elevate the “formalization vs new entry” issue.**  
   This is the core interpretive question. It should be foregrounded much earlier and organized as a central implication, not a tertiary discussion point.

6. **Conclusion should do more than summarize.**  
   Right now the conclusion mostly restates the findings. It should end with a broader implication for how economists think about entry barriers and the visibility of microenterprise in administrative data.

### Is the paper front-loaded with the good stuff?

Reasonably, but still too much of the good stuff is hidden beneath methodological throat-clearing. The reader learns the main estimate early, which is good. But the paper takes too long to explain why that estimate changes anyone’s beliefs.

### Are results buried that should be in the main text?

Yes: the decomposition between first adoption and major expansion, and ideally any richer treatment-intensity analysis if available. Those are more conceptually important than some of the methodological cross-checks.

Because the paper’s claim is still too local:

- a specific deregulation,
- in a specific niche sector,
- measured in a broad administrative category,
- with a contribution defined too much by “first evidence on this policy.”

For AER, the paper has to become a paper about something bigger than cottage foods.

### What would excite the top 10 people in this field?

One of two paths:

1. **A bigger conceptual claim:**  
   This is a clean case showing that the relevant barrier to entrepreneurship for many marginal entrants is a fixed cost of legal production, not financing or skill. That would need much stronger development and sharper evidence tied to policy intensity/mechanism.

2. **A richer empirical portrait of the margin:**  
   Who enters, where, under what regulatory design, and whether the effect reflects formalization or genuinely new activity. That would convert a neat reduced-form result into a paper that teaches us something general.

### Single most impactful piece of advice

**Reframe the paper around a broader economic question—whether small fixed regulatory costs suppress entry at the microenterprise margin—and reorganize the evidence to show not just that cottage food laws matter, but what they reveal about the nature of entrepreneurial barriers.**

That is the one thing. If the paper can do that successfully, everything else starts to look more important.
