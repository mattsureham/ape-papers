## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether sharp regulatory thresholds distort the size distribution of nonprofits. Using the universe of UK charity filings, it shows that charities cluster just below the income threshold that triggers a mandatory audit, suggesting that fixed compliance costs can discourage organizational growth even outside the standard firm/tax setting.

Why should a busy economist care? Because this is, at least potentially, a clean and intuitive example of a broader phenomenon economists care a lot about: when regulation is designed with hard cliffs, organizations may change real behavior to stay small.

Does the paper itself articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs? **Mostly, but not quite.** The current opening is vivid and readable, but it starts too small-bore and then moves too quickly into “I apply bunching methods to these thresholds.” That makes the paper sound like an application of a familiar design rather than a paper about an important economic fact. The first paragraphs should lead with the world question: **Do compliance cliffs shrink organizations?** The charity setting is then the test case, not the whole point.

### The pitch the paper should have

“Many regulatory systems impose fixed compliance requirements once organizations cross a size threshold. If those requirements are costly, they can create an ‘audit cliff’ that discourages growth and distorts the scale of economic activity. This paper studies that question in the UK charitable sector, where charities above £1 million in annual income must obtain a full statutory audit. Using the universe of Charity Commission filings, I show substantial excess mass just below the audit threshold, consistent with charities avoiding growth that would trigger costly oversight. The broader lesson is that threshold-based regulation can distort organizational scale not only for firms and taxpayers, but also for nonprofits.”

Then, if the author wants, paragraph two can introduce the lower threshold and the dose-response comparison as the paper’s way of distinguishing compliance costs from generic round-number behavior.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:**  
The paper documents that UK charities bunch below the £1 million mandatory audit threshold, suggesting that fixed regulatory compliance costs distort nonprofit scale.

That is the real contribution. The paper currently tries to make a somewhat larger claim—about “systematically manipulating reported income” and about a broad dose-response across thresholds—but the lower-threshold evidence and reform evidence are too muddy, by the paper’s own telling, to carry as much weight as the author wants. Strategically, the contribution is strongest when centered on the **audit threshold fact**.

### Is the contribution clearly differentiated from the closest papers?
**Only partially.** The paper does differentiate itself from tax bunching papers and from nonprofit reporting papers, but the differentiation is still a bit generic:

- “This is bunching, but in charities.”
- “This is about nonprofit regulation rather than taxes.”
- “This uses the full UK register.”

That is fine as a start, but not yet enough for AER-level positioning. The introduction should more sharply distinguish:
1. **Tax/notches and kinks literature**: mostly households/firms responding to taxes;
2. **Firm-size regulation literature**: mostly labor laws, administrative thresholds, and formal firms;
3. **Nonprofit governance/regulation literature**: mostly reporting quality and accountability, not organizational scale distortions.

The paper’s distinctive angle is not simply that the sector is new. It is that **a sector widely treated as mission-driven and non-profit-maximizing nonetheless responds strongly to sharp administrative costs**. That is the claim worth highlighting.

### World question or literature gap?
Right now it is split between both, and too often it lapses into the weaker version: “there is no published bunching study using the full UK charity register.” That is not an AER-worthy reason on its own.

The paper should be framed much more clearly as a **question about the world**:
- Do compliance cliffs inhibit organizational growth?
- Are nonprofits, like firms, distorted by fixed regulatory thresholds?
- What does oversight design trade off against scale?

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the intro?
At present, they could probably say: “It’s a bunching paper on UK charity audit thresholds.” That is not enough. They should instead say: “It shows that even charities stay small to avoid audit cliffs, which says something general about how fixed compliance costs shape organizational scale.”

### What would make the contribution bigger?
Several possibilities:

1. **Shift from reported-income manipulation to organizational scale distortion.**  
   This is the single most important upgrade in framing. “Manipulating reported income” sounds narrow and accounting-specific. “Distorting organizational growth/scale” is much broader and more important.

2. **Bring the £1 million threshold to the center and demote the £25,000 threshold.**  
   The lower threshold currently weakens the story because placebo round-number bunching looks similar and the reform evidence is inconclusive. The paper should stop pretending the two thresholds are equally informative.

3. **Show why scale distortion matters in real terms.**  
   Even a simple back-of-the-envelope—how many charities appear to remain below the audit threshold, what revenue is foregone, what share of sector resources are affected—would make the contribution feel less like a shape-of-the-density paper and more like economics.

4. **Speak directly to optimal regulatory design.**  
   The paper hints at sliding-scale oversight. If developed more forcefully, the paper could be positioned as evidence on the costs of threshold regulation.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Regulators often use bright-line thresholds to determine who faces more intensive scrutiny, because thresholds are simple and administrable. In the charitable sector, these thresholds are meant to improve accountability.

### Tension
But bright-line rules create cliffs. If crossing a threshold imposes a discrete fixed cost, organizations may avoid growth to remain below it. We know this happens in tax and firm-regulation settings; do mission-driven nonprofits behave similarly?

### Resolution
The paper finds substantial bunching just below the UK’s £1 million audit threshold, much larger than at the lower threshold. This suggests that audit compliance costs distort charities’ reported scale and likely their organizational growth.

### Implications
Oversight is not free. Threshold-based accountability rules may discourage expansion, implying a tradeoff between transparency and scale. Policymakers should consider smoother schedules rather than hard cliffs.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
**Serviceable, but not yet sharp.** The raw materials are there, but the paper currently reads somewhat like a collection of threshold exercises:

- bunching at £25k,
- bunching at £1m,
- reform test,
- heterogeneity,
- placebo thresholds,
- Scotland placebo that could not be done.

That feels more like a results bundle than a tightly controlled story.

### What story should it be telling?
The story should be:

1. **Threshold regulation creates audit cliffs.**
2. **The £1 million audit threshold offers especially clean evidence because the compliance cost is large.**
3. **The key fact is economically large bunching at that threshold.**
4. **The lower threshold is informative mainly as context and as a cautionary tale about generic round-number bunching, not as coequal main evidence.**
5. **The broader implication is about designing oversight systems that preserve accountability without discouraging growth.**

In other words: one big fact, one big idea, one policy implication.

Some would lean in—especially public, labor, IO, and nonprofit economists—because it is an intuitive instance of a familiar and important phenomenon in a new organizational setting. But many would lean in only briefly unless the speaker quickly answers: **why is this more than a niche bunching fact?**

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Probably one of these:
- “Is this real scaling behavior or just accounting/reporting?”
- “How economically important is the distortion?”
- “Why should we think charities are responding to costs rather than just rounding to salient numbers?”
- “What should regulators do instead?”

Those are exactly the questions the paper needs to anticipate in its framing.

### If findings are modest or null in places
The lower-threshold and reform results are weak or ambiguous. To the paper’s credit, it says so. But strategically, those sections currently dilute rather than strengthen the paper. The null/ambiguous reform result is not itself interesting enough to feature prominently. It feels like a failed supporting test, not a central finding.

The paper should therefore stop trying to make the reform evidence carry weight. Treat it briefly and honestly as suggestive but inconclusive.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

At present, the gap is mostly a mix of **framing problem, scope problem, and ambition problem**.

### Framing problem
This is the biggest issue. The paper is framed as an application of a known empirical design to a new setting. That is publishable somewhere, but not enough for AER. The paper needs to be framed as evidence on a broader economic question: **how threshold-based oversight distorts organizational scale.**

### Scope problem
The paper’s strongest evidence is one threshold in one setting. That can still work if the framing and economic interpretation are powerful, but right now the paper spreads attention across weaker elements rather than deepening the strongest one.

### Novelty problem
Moderate. The basic empirical phenomenon—bunching at thresholds—is not novel. The setting is novel-ish, but setting novelty alone is not enough. The author needs to persuade the reader that the setting teaches us something general.

### Ambition problem
Yes. The paper is competent but safe. It reads like “here is bunching in charities” rather than “here is a broader lesson about regulatory design in organizations we do not usually analyze through this lens.”

### Single most impactful advice
**Recenter the paper entirely on the £1 million audit threshold as evidence that hard oversight cliffs distort nonprofit scale, and stop trying to sell the £25,000 threshold/reform evidence as equal pillars of identification.**

That one change would improve the paper’s credibility, clarity, and importance all at once.
