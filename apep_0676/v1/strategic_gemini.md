## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
The paper examines how mandatory oversight thresholds (independent examinations at £25k and full audits at £1M) distort the reported income and growth of UK charities. Using a massive administrative dataset and a "dose-response" bunching design, it shows that charities aggressively stay below these "cliffs" to avoid compliance costs, with the distortion scaling directly with the cost of the audit. 

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates the pitch well but focuses too heavily on the "UK charity" context. To reach a general AER audience, it should pivot from "bunching in the UK" to "the efficiency-accountability tradeoff in organizational regulation." 

**The pitch the paper should have:** "When does the cost of oversight exceed the value of accountability? This paper uses the universe of UK charities to show that discrete regulatory thresholds act as growth ceilings, where a £5,000 audit requirement can induce a 50% drop in organizational density. By comparing thresholds with varying compliance 'prices,' I provide the first evidence that regulatory costs—rather than mere round-number psychology—systematically shrink the scale of the nonprofit sector."

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**Contribution:** The paper provides a direct empirical "dose-response" test of how the magnitude of compliance costs determines the degree of organizational bunching, moving beyond the simple documentation of distortions to identify the economic mechanism.

**Evaluation:** 
- **Differentiation:** It is well-differentiated from *Kleven & Waseem (2013)* by moving from tax to regulation, and from *Garicano et al. (2016)* by exploiting multiple thresholds to isolate cost-responsiveness.
- **Framing:** Currently, it feels like it’s filling a gap in the "charity bunching" literature. To be AER-caliber, it must be framed as a question about the **Deadweight Loss of Oversight**.
- **What would make it bigger?** The paper needs to quantify the "missing" charitable services. If a charity stays at £990k to avoid a £10k audit, what is the welfare loss to the beneficiaries?

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
- **Closest Neighbors:** *Kleven & Waseem (2013)* (Methodology); *Garicano, Lelarge, & Van Reenen (2016)* (Firm-size distortions); *Dharmapala & Khanna (2018)* (Nonprofit regulation).
- **Positioning:** It should "bridge" the public economics bunching literature with the industrial organization of the nonprofit sector. 
- **Missing Conversations:** The paper is silent on the **Theory of the Firm** (specifically nonprofit objectives). Do nonprofits bunch because managers are lazy, or because they want to maximize "mission per dollar" and see audits as pure waste?

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC
- **Setup:** Governments use tiered oversight to ensure nonprofit integrity.
- **Tension:** These tiers create "cliffs." Are these cliffs just statistical artifacts of round numbers, or do they actually prevent organizations from scaling?
- **Resolution:** Bunching is massive at the £1M mark and proportional to cost. The "cliff" is real and economic.
- **Implications:** Regulation is not "free." Current flat-threshold designs may be Pareto-inferior to sliding-scale scrutiny.

**Evaluation:** The arc is currently a bit flat. It reports results but doesn't build "suspense" around the 2022 reform or the Scotland placebo. It needs to lean into the *failure* of the reform test to explain why the dose-response is the superior "smoking gun."

*Current status:* The paper is "lean in" territory, but it needs to answer that follow-up to stay there.
