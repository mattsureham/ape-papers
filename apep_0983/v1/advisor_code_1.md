**Idea Fidelity**  
The manuscript follows the original idea in many respects: it exploits the Swiss institutional quirk that municipalities set separate Steuerfuss rates for natural and legal persons to test for factor-specific sorting. It constructs the corporate-personal wedge, uses the Zurich panel data, and frames the research question within the Tiebout and tax competition literatures, as outlined in the manifest. Two noteworthy deviations are that the empirical analysis is confined to the canton of Zurich rather than the broader Zurich + Basel-Landschaft sample mentioned in the manifest, and the identification materializes as a relatively standard two-way fixed effects model rather than the more explicitly described triple-difference design. The paper would benefit from either incorporating the extra cantons or clarifying why Zurich alone suffices and from making clearer how the within-municipality, across-factor variation functions as a triple-difference (factor × time × municipal) exercise.

**Summary**  
The paper examines whether the factor-specific Steuerfuss wedge in Zurich municipalities induces firms and residents to sort on their respective tax margins, as predicted by Tiebout-style models. Using municipal panel data over 2012–2023, the author finds that neither establishments nor population respond to the wedge, yet municipal tax capacity (Steuerkraft) falls sharply when the corporate rate increases relative to the personal rate. The paper interprets this as evidence that municipal tax competition operates through tax-base mobility/income shifting rather than through physical relocation.

**Essential Points**

1. **Endogeneity of the Wedge** – The identifying assumption is that wedge changes are uncorrelated with unobserved shocks to firm or household location, conditional on municipality and year effects. Yet the same municipal assemblies that adjust corporate versus personal rates also react to fiscal or economic shocks (e.g., local corporate profit declines, labor demand changes) that likely affect establishments, employment, and tax capacity. Contemporaneous responses in the wedge could therefore be endogenous. The paper needs to present stronger evidence that wedge changes are effectively exogenous: for example, event-study graphs showing no pre-trends in outcomes when the wedge changes, or instrumenting the wedge with political/fiscal cycles (e.g., election timing, mandated budget rounds) unrelated to immediate economic conditions. Without this, the zero effects on sorting could reflect the wedge being a response to shocks rather than a cause.

2. **Interpretation of Steuerkraft Response** – The strong negative coefficient of the corporate rate on Steuerkraft is presented as evidence of income shifting, but Steuerkraft is itself constructed from reported tax bases that mechanically depend on firms’ reported profits and, hence, on the corporate rate. The paper must show that the effect is not simply due to the rate entering the tax capacity definition (e.g., through definition of “taxable base” or revenue anchor). Additional tests, such as examining other fiscal outcomes (e.g., actual tax revenue, assessments of taxable income per firm) or decomposing Steuerkraft into its corporate vs. personal components, would bolster the income-shifting interpretation. Otherwise, the conclusion that the wedge affects only the tax base is premature.

3. **Power and Timing for Sorting** – The null on physical relocation could stem from insufficient variation in the wedge, slow response horizons, or measurement choices rather than a genuine absence of sorting. The wedge’s standard deviation is only 1.7 pp, and rate changes occur only in a third of municipality-years. The author should report formal power calculations (or at least implied minimum detectable effects) to demonstrate that the analysis can reject economically meaningful sorting responses. Moreover, relocation decisions may take longer than a one- or two-year lag; while lagged rates are tested, longer horizons or dynamic/specification models (event studies, distributed lag models) would help. Without this, the null could simply reflect low statistical power or timing mismatch.

**Suggestions**

1. **Clarify Identification and Triple-Difference Mechanism** – The introductory sections claim a triple-difference design exploiting within-municipality, within-year, across-factor variation. Make this explicit in the empirical section: spell out the three “differences” (e.g., municipality vs. within-municipality wedge; corporate vs. personal; before vs. after wedge change) and show how equation (1) implements them. If the modern literature on difference-in-difference with staggered treatment is relevant, discuss why standard concerns (e.g., variation in treatment timing, heterogeneous treatment effects) are mitigated. This will strengthen the argument that the estimates legitimately isolate wedge-driven sorting.

2. **Expand Sample or Justify Zurich-Only Focus** – The manifest envisioned exploiting Zurich plus Basel-Landschaft (and possibly others). If data collection for other cantons is incomplete, explain why the additional variation is not used and whether the wedge dynamics there differ. If feasible, add the Basel-Landschaft municipalities—their mention in the policy section suggests they may have wedge variation and adding them would increase power and external validity. Even if not, a short subsection explaining the canton selection (e.g., data availability, comparability issues) would help readers assess generalizability.

3. **Event Study and Pre-Trend Evidence** – To assuage endogeneity concerns, include event-study-style figures showing the trajectory of establishments, population, and Steuerkraft around large wedge changes. These plots can reveal whether outcomes start trending before the policy change (which would threaten identification) and whether any delayed responses exist that the regressions might miss. Similarly, estimate models using leads of the wedge (or corporate/personal rates) to show that future wedge changes do not predict current outcomes. This would reinforce the exogeneity assumption.

4. **Decompose Steuerkraft and Examine Revenue Side** – Tax capacity is a composite measure. Where possible, split Steuerkraft into its corporate and personal components to see whether the corporate rate specifically affects the corporate tax base, and whether the personal rate affects the personal base. Alternatively, add regressions with actual municipal tax revenue (if accessible) or with separate corporate taxable income measures. Doing so would better tie the wedge to the hypothesized income-shifting channel rather than to mechanical definitions.

5. **Test for Alternative Channels (Capitalization, Housing Markets)** – The discussion mentions property capitalization as a possible explanation for the null on sorting. Consider testing this directly: include housing price or building permit data (if available) and examine whether they respond to the wedge. If housing markets do adjust, that would suggest a mechanism whereby price adjustments absorb the incentive to relocate. Even descriptive evidence (e.g., correlation between wedge changes and property tax base) would enhance the discussion.

6. **Power Calculations and Minimum Detectable Effects** – Since the wedge variation is modest and the estimates for establishments and population are small, a short section outlining the detectable effect sizes given the sample variance and clustering would add credibility to the null. A table showing the implied percentage change in establishments/population corresponding to, say, a 5 pp wedge change would let readers judge whether the data can reject economically meaningful responses.

7. **Robustness to Alternative Fixed Effects and Controls** – The current specifications rely on municipality and year fixed effects only. Consider adding municipality-specific time trends, interactions between municipality and demographic controls (e.g., pre-trends in population growth), or canton-wide economic controls (e.g., unemployment rate, GDP growth) to account for differential trends across municipalities. While the wedge variation is internal, these additional controls could help ensure that omitted local economic dynamics are not driving the results.

8. **Presentation of Statistical Significance in Discussion** – The abstract and conclusion present the null finding and the Steuerkraft effect confidently, but the text should carefully frame the null in terms of precision. For example, mention that the confidence intervals rule out responses larger than X%, and that the results are robust across firm-size subgroups. This will guard against misinterpretation by readers who might assume “no effect” means “zero effect” rather than “no statistically distinguishable effect.”

In sum, the paper addresses a compelling and underexplored question. Strengthening the identification narrative, broadening the sample or justifying the scope, detailing the income-shifting channel, and addressing statistical power will substantially enhance the paper’s credibility and contribution.
