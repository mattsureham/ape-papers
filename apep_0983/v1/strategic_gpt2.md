## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple and potentially important question: when local governments can tax firms and residents differently, do those tax wedges actually move firms and people, or do they mainly move reported tax base? Using the rare Swiss setting where municipalities set separate tax multipliers for corporations and natural persons, the paper finds little evidence of physical relocation of firms or residents, but substantial movement in measured tax capacity, suggesting that local tax competition may operate more through paper profits and taxable income than through real activity.

A busy economist should care because this goes to the heart of what local tax competition is actually about. If municipal tax policy mostly reshuffles taxable income rather than jobs, establishments, or households, then a large class of models and policy arguments may be talking about the wrong margin.

### Does the paper articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Not quite. The ingredients are there, but the introduction is too quick to define the contribution as “a direct test of factor-specific sorting” and too slow to state the bigger economic question: what exactly is mobile in response to local taxes—real factors or tax base? Right now the paper sounds like a clean design in search of a large question.

### What the first two paragraphs should say instead

The paper should open with something like this:

> Local tax competition is usually discussed as a contest for real economic activity: firms move to low-tax places, households sort into attractive jurisdictions, and municipalities compete for jobs and residents. But in a modern economy, taxes may move something else more easily than they move people or plants: reported income and profits. The central empirical question is therefore not just whether taxes matter, but which margin they affect—real location decisions or the tax base on paper.
>
> This paper answers that question using a rare institutional setting from Swiss municipalities that separately tax corporations and natural persons. These factor-specific tax instruments should, in a standard Tiebout-style world, generate factor-specific sorting: firms should respond to corporate tax changes and residents to personal tax changes. I find instead that these wedges do not measurably move establishments, employment, or population, but they do move municipal tax capacity. The core implication is that local tax competition may be more about shifting taxable income than relocating real factors.

That is the story. It is larger, cleaner, and more legible than “here is a within-municipality wedge design.”

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### Contribution in one sentence

The paper’s contribution is to show, in a setting with separate local tax rates on firms and residents, that factor-specific tax differences appear to reallocate taxable income rather than real firms or households.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest papers?

Only partly. The paper distinguishes itself from standard tax competition papers by emphasizing separate municipal tax rates for corporations and individuals, which is genuinely unusual. But the introduction does not do enough to explain how this changes what we learn relative to the existing literatures on:

1. household sorting across jurisdictions,
2. firm responses to local/corporate taxes,
3. profit shifting and tax-base mobility.

Right now a smart reader may think: “This is a Swiss municipal FE paper showing null effects on establishments and population, but some response in tax base.” That is more niche and less memorable than what the paper wants to be.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a gap in a literature?

It is mixed, but still too literature-gap-ish. The stronger framing is a world question:

- **Weak framing:** “Existing literature has not directly tested factor-specific sorting with factor-specific tax instruments.”
- **Strong framing:** “When local governments use tax policy to compete, are they competing for real activity or for taxable income?”

The latter is much better and much closer to what the results seem to support.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?

Not crisply enough. They could probably say: “It’s a municipal tax paper from Switzerland using separate corporate and personal rates, finding no sorting.” That is not enough. What you want them to say is: “It overturns the usual story about local tax competition—the tax base moves, but the underlying firms and households do not.”

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Several possibilities, all strategic rather than technical:

1. **Make the tax-base-vs-real-activity distinction the centerpiece.**  
   The current paper spends more effort proving the null on sorting than making the tax-base result economically rich. If the big claim is that local tax competition operates through paper reallocations, the paper needs more direct evidence on that margin.

2. **Bring in outcomes that better separate real from paper responses.**  
   The current “real” outcomes are establishments, employment, and population. Helpful, but limited. The paper would become much bigger with outcomes like:
   - wage bill,
   - payroll or commuting flows,
   - property values or rents,
   - taxable corporate profits versus headcount,
   - composition of tax base by source,
   - incorporation/self-employment responses,
   - headquarters versus production locations.

3. **Sharpen the mechanism.**  
   “Income shifting rather than physical relocation” is plausible, but still somewhat inferred. The paper needs at least one more mechanism-oriented piece of evidence if it wants to sell that interpretation as central rather than speculative.

4. **Reframe away from ‘null plus one significant coefficient.’**  
   The paper is stronger if presented as a decomposition of tax competition into margins of response, not as a failed test of Tiebout plus an auxiliary tax-capacity result.

### How should the paper position itself relative to those neighbors?

Mostly **build on and connect**, not attack.

- Relative to the local sorting literature: “You have shown that local taxes can matter for location; we show that when taxes can be targeted by factor, the dominant response may not be location at all.”
- Relative to the firm tax literature: “Prior work often studies reduced-form responses of local activity to broad tax changes; our setting lets us test whether the specific taxed factor actually moves.”
- Relative to the profit-shifting literature: “That literature shows tax bases are mobile; our contribution is to embed that insight in a local public finance setting where one can directly compare paper and physical margins.”

The paper should not posture as overturning Tiebout theory. That claim is too grand for the evidence. It should instead say that many empirical and policy discussions over-attribute tax responses to physical mobility when financial mobility may be the first-order channel.

### Is the paper currently positioned too narrowly or too broadly?

Paradoxically, both.

- **Too narrowly** in that it leans heavily on a particular Swiss institutional feature and a wedge-specific test.
- **Too broadly** in claims like “the standard one-dimensional tax competition model is not misspecified; it is merely mislabeled,” which reads sloganistic and larger than the evidence.

The right audience is broader than Swiss local public finance but narrower than “all tax competition theory.” The paper should target the intersection of public finance, urban/local economics, and corporate taxation.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of or under-engaged with?

It seems under-engaged with:

- the modern empirical literature distinguishing real activity from reported profits,
- state and local public finance papers on business tax incidence and local outcomes,
- the tax-base composition/capitalization literature,
- possibly fiscal federalism papers on tax competition under multiple instruments.

It should speak more directly to the literature on whether corporate tax cuts buy jobs versus taxable profits.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?

Not fully. The most impactful conversation is not “Can we test factor-specific Tiebout sorting?” It is: **What do local tax cuts actually purchase—real activity or taxable income?** That is a better conversation and a much bigger one.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Municipalities compete using taxes, and economists often interpret this competition through the mobility of firms and households. In Switzerland, municipalities can unusually set different tax rates for corporations and natural persons, creating a natural test of who moves in response to what.

### Tension

If tax competition works through factor mobility, then factor-specific taxes should sort factors: firms should respond to corporate taxes, residents to personal taxes. But there is a competing possibility: taxes mainly move the tax base on paper, not the underlying economic activity.

### Resolution

The paper finds essentially no response of establishments, employment, or population to the tax wedge, but does find a sizable response of municipal tax capacity.

### Implications

Local tax competition may be more about shifting taxable income than attracting real factors. If so, both the welfare analysis and policy rhetoric around place-based tax cuts may need to change.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the outline of one, but at present it reads more like a collection of results with a strong title attached. The narrative is there, but underdeveloped and a bit unstable.

The biggest problem is that the paper wants the null physical-sorting result and the tax-capacity result to jointly tell a profound story. That can work—but only if the paper more deliberately organizes itself around the contrast between **real margins** and **paper margins**. Right now the story emerges late and is somewhat overinterpreted in the conclusion.

### What story should it be telling?

Not “factor-specific tax wedges do not validate Tiebout sorting.”

Rather:

> Local tax competition has two candidate margins: real relocation and tax-base reallocation. This paper uses rare factor-specific local tax instruments to compare them directly, and finds that the paper margin dominates the real margin.

That is the actual story, and it is better.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?

“I have a setting where municipalities separately tax firms and residents. Changing those rates doesn’t move firms or people—but it does move the measured tax base.”

That is a good line. People would lean in, at least initially.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

They would lean in for the first sentence, especially public finance economists. But they will quickly ask whether the tax-base movement is really telling us about profit shifting versus reverse causality or municipal fiscal responses. Since this memo is not about identification, the strategic point is: the paper’s most interesting result is also the one that needs the most conceptual support. So the paper must narratively anticipate that skepticism.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Probably one of these:

1. “So what exactly is moving—reported profits, legal form, headquarters, high-income taxpayers?”
2. “If the real activity doesn’t move, is this just a paper-profit story?”
3. “How general is this beyond Zurich?”
4. “Why should I think this says something broad about local tax competition rather than one peculiar Swiss institution?”

Those are all healthy questions. The introduction and discussion should be written around them.

### If the findings are null or modest, is the null itself interesting?

Yes, potentially. But the paper must make a stronger case that the null is informative because:
- the treatment is economically meaningful,
- the institutional setting is exactly the kind where sorting should show up if it exists,
- the estimates are precise enough to rule out effects that matter for theory or policy.

The paper does some of this, but it still feels a bit like “we didn’t find sorting.” The more powerful version is: “We can rule out the margin that most discussions of local tax competition emphasize, while observing movement on a different margin.”

That turns a null into a substantive result.
