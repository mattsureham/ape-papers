### 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a fundamental question: When jurisdictions change taxes for specific groups (firms vs. residents), do those specific groups actually move, or does just their money? Using a unique institutional setting in Zurich where municipalities set separate tax rates for people and corporations, the author finds that while "physical" factors (people and jobs) are remarkably immobile, the "tax base" (reported income/profit) is highly sensitive. The paper suggests that Tiebout sorting—the bedrock of local public finance—might be a financial shell game rather than a story of moving trucks.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this well in the abstract, but the first two paragraphs of the introduction are a bit "textbook." They lead with a general definition of Tiebout sorting that every AER reader already knows.

**The Pitch the Paper Should Have:**
"Standard models of tax competition assume that targeted tax breaks attract targeted economic activity. By exploiting a rare institutional feature in Zurich—where municipalities set independent tax multipliers for corporations and residents—I provide the first clean test of factor-specific sorting. I find that while these 'wedges' fail to move a single person or firm, they trigger massive shifts in the reported tax base, suggesting that local tax competition is a game of income shifting rather than productive relocation."

---

### 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution:** The paper demonstrates a "decoupling" between physical factor mobility and tax base mobility, proving that the latter drives local fiscal responses even when the former is zero.

*   **Differentiation:** It moves beyond Brülhart et al. (2012) and Schmidheiny (2006), who look at uniform tax changes. By observing *differential* rates within the same town, the author can control for "amenity shocks" (e.g., a new park or school) that usually confound tax studies.
*   **Framing:** It is framed as a question about the **WORLD** (Are we moving people or just ledgers?), which is high-impact.
*   **Clarity:** A smart economist would say: "It’s a paper showing that firms/people are stuck, but their accountants are mobile."
*   **Expansion:** To make it "bigger," the author needs to explore the *who*. Is it the high-income residents who incorporate to shift between the two rates? Adding data on individual-level income composition would turn this from a "where" paper into a "how" paper.

---

### 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

*   **Closest Neighbors:** Tiebout (1956); Kleven et al. (2014) on superstar mobility; Serrato and Zidar (2016) on corporate tax incidence; and the profit-shifting literature (e.g., Tørsløv, Wier, and Zucman).
*   **Positioning:** The paper should **synthesize** the urban/public finance literature (sorting) with the international tax literature (profit shifting). Currently, it sits too much in the "Urban" bucket.
*   **Missing Conversations:** The paper needs to speak to the **Tax Incidence** literature. If firms don't move but the base does, who is actually bearing the burden of these municipal taxes?

---

### 4. NARRATIVE ARC

*   **Setup:** Municipalities compete for taxpayers by lowering rates.
*   **Tension:** We assume this works by attracting "real" activity (jobs/people), but relocating is costly. If the "wedge" between corporate and personal rates changes, we should see specific groups move.
*   **Resolution:** They don't move. Physical factors are "dead" to these changes. However, the tax base (Steuerkraft) reacts violently.
*   **Implications:** Tax competition is a zero-sum accounting game.

**Evaluation:** The arc is very strong. It has a "puzzle and solution" structure that AER readers love.

---

### 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

*   **The Lead Fact:** "When a Swiss town raises its corporate tax relative to its personal tax, it loses 3% of its tax base immediately, but doesn't lose a single employee."
*   **Reaction:** People will lean in. It confirms a cynical suspicion many have about tax incentives.
*   **Follow-up:** "How are they shifting the money?" (This is where the paper needs more work on mechanisms).

---

### 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

*   **Front-loading:** The "Steuerkraft" (Tax Base) results are the most exciting part but feel slightly secondary to the null results in the current layout. They should be elevated.
*   **Appendix:** Table 3 (Heterogeneity) is a bit thin. Unless the author can find a "smoking gun" (e.g., certain types of firms *do* shift more), this could be compressed.
*   **Conclusion:** The conclusion is currently just a summary. It needs to discuss the **Fiscal Federalism** implications. If municipalities are just stealing tax base from each other via accounting tricks, should the Canton restrict their ability to set separate rates?

---

### 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

**The Gap:** Currently, it's a very high-quality "local" paper. To make it AER, it needs to be an "Institutional" paper.

**Single Biggest Advice:** The author must bridge the gap between the null physical result and the large tax base result. **Show us the "Shifters."** If the author can use the "Micro" vs "Small" firm data to show that firms with more "shiftable" income (e.g., service sector vs. manufacturing) are the ones driving the Steuerkraft result, this becomes an undisputed Top 5 paper.
