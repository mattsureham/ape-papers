---

## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
This paper asks whether state-imposed internet shutdowns—a rising tool of information control—cause measurable economic damage. Using a district-year panel from India (the world leader in digital blackouts) and satellite nighttime lights, the author finds that while simple correlations suggest a 4% decline in activity, most of this disappears once state-level conflict trends are controlled for, except in cases of prolonged (50+ day) shutdowns.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates the pitch well in terms of *context* (India's dominance in shutdowns) and *methodology* (nighttime lights), but it fails to lead with the most provocative part of its story: the "tension" between the visible human cost and the difficulty of finding a causal economic footprint using standard remote-sensing proxies.

**The pitch the paper should have:** "Governments increasingly weaponize internet shutdowns to control information, yet their economic toll remains speculative. This paper uses India’s 2,000+ district-level blackouts and high-resolution satellite data to provide the first subnational causal estimate of these costs. I find that the economic 'fingerprint' of digital disruption is surprisingly elusive in annual data, appearing only during extreme, prolonged blackouts—a result that challenges current 'top-down' estimates of the global costs of internet censorship."

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
The paper provides the first district-level causal analysis of internet shutdowns on economic activity using objective satellite data.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It is well-differentiated from cross-country studies (West, 2016) and case studies. It is the first to use the "Black Marble" VIIRS data for this specific policy.
*   **Question vs. Literature:** It currently leans toward "filling a gap" (no causal subnational estimate exists). It would be stronger if framed as a question about the *resilience* of modern economies to digital disconnection.
*   **Clarity:** A smart economist would say: "It’s a paper that tries to find the cost of internet shutdowns in India but finds that the signal is mostly drowned out by the noise of the underlying conflict, except in extreme cases."
*   **Make it bigger:** To make the contribution "AER-sized," the author needs to move beyond the "cost of X" and explain the *mechanism of adaptation*. If a 5-day shutdown doesn't move the needle on annual lights, do firms front-load activity? Do they shift to cash/offline? The paper needs more than just a "near-significant" 3.4% result for the most extreme cases.

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
The paper sits at the intersection of **Development (ICT)**, **Political Economy (Information Control)**, and **Methodology (Nighttime lights)**.

*   **Closest Neighbors:** Jensen (2007) and Aker (2010) on the *gains* from connectivity; Guriev & Treisman (2019) on informational autocrats; and Henderson et al. (2012) on NTL as a proxy.
*   **Positioning:** It should position itself as the **"Symmetry Test"** for the ICT literature. We know what happens when you add the internet; does the exact opposite happen when you take it away? (The paper hints at this "mirror image" framing on page 3—this should be the central pillar).
*   **Missing Conversations:** The paper needs to speak to the **Digital Payments** literature (e.g., UPI in India). India’s economy is uniquely dependent on mobile payments. A shutdown is a liquidity shock. Connecting to the "Mobile Money" literature would raise the stakes.

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC
*   **Setup:** The digital revolution has made the internet a "utility," and India is the "shutdown capital."
*   **Tension:** If the internet is vital, shutdowns should be catastrophic. Yet, identifying this cost is plagued by the fact that shutdowns happen in places already in crisis.
*   **Resolution:** Most "costs" reported in the media are likely confounded by the underlying crisis. However, once a shutdown crosses a "duration threshold" (50+ days), the damage becomes structurally visible.
*   **Implications:** Policy-makers must realize that "short" shutdowns might have high human/political costs but hidden economic resilience, whereas "long" shutdowns are unequivocally destructive.

**Evaluate:** The narrative is currently a bit "defensive" regarding the null results. It should embrace the null for short shutdowns as a finding about economic adaptation.

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
*   **The Fact:** "In India, you need to shut the internet down for 50 days before it clearly shows up in satellite data."
*   **The Reaction:** People would lean in. It's counter-intuitive. We assume a 2-day shutdown is a disaster.
*   **The Follow-up:** "Is that because the data is too coarse, or because people are resilient?" This is the question the paper *must* answer to get into the AER.

---

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
*   **The Exam Strategy:** This is the most "AER-like" part of the paper (the exogenous variation). It is currently buried. It needs to be front-and-center.
*   **Measurement Limitation:** Move the discussion on "Annual vs. Monthly" data from Section 3 to the very front. Own the limitation as a feature: "What survives an annual average?"
*   **Visuals:** We need a map showing the spatial overlap of shutdowns and luminosity changes in a high-intensity state like Rajasthan or J&K.

---

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The paper is currently a very high-quality *Journal of Development Economics* (JDE) or *AEJ: Applied* paper. To reach the AER, it needs **Ambitious Mechanisms**.

**The single most impactful piece of advice:**
Go beyond the "average effect" and use the **Exam-Triggered Shutdowns** (which are short and exogenous) to look at high-frequency outcomes—if not monthly lights, then perhaps digital payment metadata or agricultural market arrivals (mandi prices) from the same districts.

If the author can show *why* the 2-day exam shutdowns don't show up in lights (e.g., because people just shop the day after), they move from "measuring an effect" to "explaining a phenomenon."
