## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper uses federal job-posting data to ask how the 2025 U.S. federal hiring freeze changed the composition of government hiring, not just its level. Its headline claim is that the freeze did not shrink the state uniformly: it hit scientific, regulatory, and administrative agencies much harder than enforcement, military, and clinical functions, implying a selective reshaping of state capacity.

A busy economist should care because the paper is trying to say something bigger than “vacancies fell after a freeze”: it is about how ostensibly neutral administrative tools reallocate capacity across functions of the state. That is a live question in political economy, public economics, and the economics of state capacity.

**Does the paper articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Mostly, but not sharply enough. The current opening is good on immediacy and institutional detail, but it spends too much energy on the chronology of the executive order and not enough on the broader economic question. The key idea — that a hiring freeze can change what kind of state you have — should appear immediately and unmistakably.

**What the first two paragraphs should say instead:**

> Governments often cut personnel through blunt instruments such as hiring freezes, but these policies need not shrink the state uniformly. If politically protected agencies continue hiring while less salient agencies do not, a hiring freeze becomes a mechanism for reallocating state capacity across functions — preserving coercive or frontline services while hollowing out scientific, regulatory, and administrative capabilities.
>
> This paper studies that reallocation in the 2025 federal hiring freeze using universe USAJOBS vacancy data. We show that the aggregate decline in hiring masked a strong compositional pattern: enforcement, military, and clinical functions were relatively protected, while agencies tied to science, regulation, and administration saw dramatic collapses in external hiring. The central message is that a nominally uniform personnel policy changed not just the size of government hiring, but the type of state being staffed.

That is the pitch the paper should have.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution in one sentence:**  
The paper argues that the 2025 federal hiring freeze functioned as a selective reallocation of state capacity, disproportionately suppressing hiring in scientific, regulatory, and administrative agencies relative to more politically salient enforcement and clinical functions.

### Evaluation

**Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?**  
Not yet. The paper says it is the “first systematic, microdata-based estimate” of the freeze, but that is a data contribution, not yet a conceptual one. For AER purposes, “we use better/more current administrative data to study a recent policy shock” is not enough. The differentiator has to be the idea that administrative austerity is compositionally targeted in effect even when formally broad in design.

Right now the contribution is split between:
1. measuring a recent federal policy episode quickly,
2. documenting heterogeneity across departments,
3. proposing the phrase “selective atrophy.”

Of these, only #2 has real potential. #1 is timely but ephemeral. #3 is branding unless tied to a durable conceptual framework economists will reuse.

**Is the contribution framed as a question about the WORLD, or filling a gap in a LITERATURE?**  
It is mixed, but still too literature-gap-ish in the contribution paragraph. The stronger version is a world question:

- **Weak framing:** “There is little microdata evidence on the 2025 hiring freeze.”
- **Strong framing:** “When governments use blunt personnel controls, which state functions are actually protected and which are allowed to erode?”

The latter is much more AER.

**Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain what’s new?**  
Some could, but many would still summarize it as: “It’s a DiD on federal job postings showing the hiring freeze hit some departments more than others.” That is not enough. The introduction needs to make the novelty impossible to miss: the paper is not mainly about a hiring freeze; it is about how political salience shapes the composition of bureaucratic contraction.

**What would make the contribution bigger? Be specific.**  
Three possibilities:

1. **Move from departments to functions/occupations.**  
   The paper itself gestures at this. The big claim is about scientific, regulatory, administrative, coercive, and clinical capacity. But the evidence is still mostly at the department level, which is a crude proxy. An occupation-series decomposition would greatly strengthen the conceptual claim and make it more generalizable.

2. **Link hiring composition to downstream state outputs.**  
   If the paper could connect the hiring contraction to delays in permits, inspections, grants, scientific output, enforcement actions, or administrative backlogs, it would stop being a paper about postings and become a paper about how capacity shocks alter state performance.

3. **Frame as a general political-economy result, not just a 2025 event study.**  
   The paper needs either comparison to past hiring freezes/austerity episodes or a clearer conceptual model of why salient functions are protected. Otherwise it reads as a sharp case study of a single partisan moment.

Relative to vacancy papers, the stance should be instrumental: vacancy data are the measurement tool, not the intellectual home. Right now the labor-demand positioning is a bit too prominent given the actual question.

### Is it positioned too narrowly or too broadly?

Currently, it is oddly both:

- **Too narrowly** as a paper on a specific federal hiring freeze using USAJOBS data.
- **Too broadly** when it jumps to very expansive claims about “the state” and “coercive vs scientific capacity” without enough scaffolding.

The right level is: a paper in political economy/public economics about **how administrative personnel rules reshape the composition of state capacity**, illustrated by the 2025 freeze.

### What literature does it seem unaware of?

It should be in more explicit conversation with:

- **Political economy of selective state action** — how governments preserve some capacities while weakening others.
- **Administrative burden / policy implementation** — not because this is a classic burden paper, but because reduced staffing changes who can access the state and how fast the state acts.
- **Austerity and bureaucratic retrenchment** — comparative public finance/public administration work, including non-U.S. cases.
- Possibly **organizational economics of mission salience** — why some functions receive exemptions and management attention.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?

Not quite. It currently oscillates between:
- a public-sector labor measurement paper,
- a timely policy note on DOGE and the EO,
- and a state-capacity political economy paper.

For impact, it should choose the third. The first two are supporting elements, not the main conversation. The unexpected but fruitful connection is to the literature on **how political incentives shape the composition, not merely level, of government activity**.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Governments often use broad personnel controls to restrain spending or remake the bureaucracy. Observers usually ask whether these tools reduce headcount, but less attention goes to whether they alter the composition of the state.

### Tension
A “uniform” hiring freeze may not be uniform in practice. Agencies differ in political salience, exemption opportunities, mission criticality, and public visibility. So the same formal rule may preserve some state functions and hollow out others.

### Resolution
Using vacancy data, the paper finds that the 2025 federal hiring freeze was associated with much larger hiring collapses in science-, regulation-, and administration-heavy agencies than in enforcement-, military-, and clinical-heavy agencies.

### Implications
The important implication is not merely that hiring slowed. It is that administrative tools can reweight the state toward some functions and away from others, potentially changing what the government is capable of doing even if aggregate staffing later recovers.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the ingredients, but the narrative is not yet fully disciplined. At times it reads like a sequence of empirical tables around a topical policy event, and only intermittently like a paper with a durable idea. The phrase “selective atrophy” helps, but the paper has not yet earned that phrase as a concept with broader resonance.

**What story should it be telling?**  
Not: “Here is what happened after EO 14148.”  
But: “Blunt personnel policies are politically filtered. What looks like administrative austerity is often compositional state-building in reverse.”

That is the story.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

**What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?**  
“A federal hiring freeze didn’t just reduce hiring — it appears to have protected enforcement and clinical agencies while crushing hiring in science, regulation, and administration.”

That is a much better lead than “March 2025 had the lowest vacancies on record.”

**Would people lean in or reach for their phones?**  
They would lean in at first, because the compositional claim is interesting and politically charged in a way that maps onto deep economic questions about state capacity. But they will quickly ask whether this is a deep result or just a timely descriptive pattern from one unusual episode.

**What follow-up question would they ask?**  
Almost certainly: “Does this matter for what the government actually does?”  
Secondarily: “Is this unique to this administration, or a general feature of hiring freezes and administrative retrenchment?”

That tells you exactly where the current paper is short of AER-level excitement. The main follow-up is about consequences and generality.

**If findings are modest/null:**  
Not applicable in the usual sense — the findings are not null. But the pooled estimate is not the source of interest anyway. The heterogeneous pattern is the paper. The authors are right about that. They should fully embrace it and stop apologizing for the pooled effect.

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

### What would make it read better?

1. **Compress the econometric throat-clearing in the introduction.**  
   The introduction spends too much time on coefficient magnitudes, standard errors, and specification variants too early. AER readers should first get the fact pattern and the conceptual claim.

2. **Front-load the heterogeneity result.**  
   The main table/exhibit should effectively appear in the introduction. The paper’s central point is the departmental/functional decomposition, not the pooled average effect.

3. **Shorten or demote the generic methodology section.**  
   For strategic positioning, the empirical strategy is over-weighted relative to the conceptual contribution. The details can stay, but much of the estimator discussion belongs later or in an appendix.

4. **Make “selective atrophy” earn its keep.**  
   Right now it is a nice phrase attached to one episode. The discussion section should either formalize it more clearly as a reusable measurement concept or tone down the branding.

5. **Reorganize results around functions, not specifications.**  
   The results section currently reads as pooled DiD, event study, GS heterogeneity, departmental decomposition. That is a standard empirical paper architecture, but not the best storytelling architecture. Better:
   - First: the freeze sharply reduced hiring overall.
   - Second: this average masks dramatic functional heterogeneity.
   - Third: the heterogeneity aligns with political/mission salience.
   - Fourth: the implications for state capacity.

6. **The GS-grade section feels secondary.**  
   Unless grade heterogeneity is central to the conceptual argument, it can be shortened or moved to appendix. It currently interrupts the paper’s strongest narrative thread.

7. **The conclusion should do more than restate.**  
   It should end by broadening the lesson: personnel controls are a politically mediated instrument for changing the composition of government. Right now the conclusion is decent, but still a bit like a summary.
