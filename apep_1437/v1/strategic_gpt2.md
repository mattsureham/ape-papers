## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper studies the 2025 federal hiring freeze using USAJOBS vacancy postings and argues that the freeze did not reduce hiring uniformly across government. Instead, it claims the contraction was concentrated in scientific, regulatory, and administrative agencies, while enforcement, military, and clinical functions were relatively protected—implying that hiring freezes can reshape the composition of state capacity rather than just shrink government overall.

A busy economist should care because the paper is trying to make a broader point about how seemingly neutral fiscal or personnel rules reallocate bureaucratic capacity across functions of the state. That is potentially interesting and important. But in its current form, the paper feels too tied to one fresh political episode and too descriptive to immediately register as a general-interest AER contribution.

**Does the paper articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Not quite. The current introduction is energetic and politically vivid, but it gets pulled quickly into the specifics of EO 14148, DOGE, and the month-by-month collapse in postings. The broader economic question—how governments selectively preserve some capacities while allowing others to wither under nominally uniform constraints—arrives, but too late and too entangled with the current event.

**What the first two paragraphs should say instead:**

> Governments often use across-the-board hiring freezes as neutral tools to reduce payroll growth. But such freezes need not operate uniformly in practice: when exemptions, managerial discretion, and political attention differ across agencies, a common rule can reallocate state capacity away from some functions and toward others. The key question is not only whether a hiring freeze reduces hiring, but which parts of the state it actually shrinks.
>
> This paper studies that question using the 2025 U.S. federal hiring freeze and universe data on USAJOBS postings. We show that the freeze was associated with a sharp contraction in external hiring, but more importantly that the contraction was highly uneven: scientific, regulatory, and administrative agencies saw much larger declines than enforcement, military-adjacent, and clinical agencies. The main lesson is that uniform personnel policies can produce non-uniform changes in the functional composition of the state.

That is the pitch the paper should have. Start with the general phenomenon, then use the episode as a sharp case study.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution in one sentence:**  
The paper argues that the 2025 federal hiring freeze functioned less as an aggregate hiring slowdown than as a selective reallocation of federal capacity away from scientific, regulatory, and administrative functions.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3-4 papers in the literature?
Only partially. The paper cites broad bureaucratic-politics and state-capacity literatures, but the actual empirical contribution is much narrower: a real-time descriptive account of one federal personnel shock using vacancy data. The introduction says “first systematic microdata-based estimate,” which is true in a narrow sense, but “first” is not enough for AER unless paired with a broader conceptual or substantive payoff. Right now the differentiation is mostly about **data source and timing**, not about a genuinely new economic insight.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or filling a gap in a LITERATURE?
It is trying to be framed as a question about the world—how uniform rules reshape state capacity—but it repeatedly slips back into “we provide the first estimate using these data” and “we connect to literature X.” The world question is stronger; it should dominate.

### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what’s new?
They could probably say: “It’s a DiD paper showing the 2025 hiring freeze hit some agencies much harder than others.”  
That is better than “another DiD paper about X,” but not by much. The risk is that the paper reads as a nicely written event-study/descriptive policy note rather than a major economics paper.

### What would make this contribution bigger?
A few possibilities:

1. **Move from departments to functions/occupations.**  
   The paper’s real idea is about composition of state capacity, but department-level results are a very blunt proxy. If the authors could show effects by occupational series—scientists, engineers, inspectors, economists, procurement staff, attorneys, clinicians, law enforcement—the paper would become much more conceptually precise and much more generalizable.

2. **Link vacancy composition to downstream state outputs.**  
   Right now the paper shows “who stopped posting jobs.” A bigger paper would show what that implies: slower grant processing, delayed inspections, fewer rulemakings, reduced scientific output, procurement delays, backlog accumulation, etc. That would elevate the paper from personnel accounting to economic consequences.

3. **Make the comparative claim more explicit.**  
   The strongest version is not “some departments fell more than others,” but “politically salient frontline functions were protected while diffuse expert-administrative functions were not.” That’s a political economy claim about how governments choose what capacity to preserve. It needs sharper operationalization.

4. **Generalize beyond this one episode.**  
   Compare to prior federal hiring freezes, state-level freezes, foreign civil-service freezes, or other broad personnel austerity episodes. Right now the paper risks reading as a single contemporary case.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Hiring freezes are usually understood as broad instruments to reduce headcount growth or save money. The implicit model is uniformity.

### Tension
In practice, governments do not value all functions equally, and exemptions/discretion may shield some agencies more than others. So a common rule may change the composition of the state, not just its size.

### Resolution
Using vacancy postings during the 2025 freeze, the paper finds large heterogeneity: scientific, regulatory, and administrative agencies saw much steeper declines than enforcement and clinical agencies.

### Implications
Personnel policy can reallocate state capacity toward politically salient coercive/frontline functions and away from diffuse expert functions. That matters for how economists think about austerity, bureaucracy, and institutional capacity.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
Yes, but only in embryo. The ingredients are there. The phrase **“selective atrophy”** is actually strong and memorable. The problem is that the paper keeps oscillating between:
- a big conceptual story about state capacity, and
- a narrow descriptive paper about a very recent federal freeze.

At present it is **not fully a collection of results looking for a story**, but it is close. The heterogeneity table is doing most of the narrative work; the paper’s conceptual architecture is not yet carrying enough weight.

**What story should it be telling?**  
Not “here is what happened after EO 14148.”  
Rather:

> This paper shows that nominally uniform personnel retrenchment can selectively hollow out low-salience expert capacity while preserving high-salience enforcement and clinical capacity.

That is the story. The current event is the setting, not the story itself.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?
“After the 2025 hiring freeze, federal hiring didn’t just fall—it collapsed in agencies like Commerce, Agriculture, Interior, HHS, and NASA, while DOJ, DHS, and VA were much less affected. The freeze seems to have changed what kind of state the government was reproducing.”

That is a decent opening fact.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
Some would lean in, especially economists interested in public finance, political economy, labor, and institutions. But many would quickly ask whether this is just a transient artifact of one chaotic political episode. That is the paper’s core vulnerability.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Almost certainly:  
**“So what happened to actual government output or capacity?”**  
Second question:  
**“Is this general, or just this one administration and this one freeze?”**

Those are exactly the questions the paper currently cannot answer well enough.

### If the findings are modest or partial, is that okay?
Yes—but only if the paper fully owns being a **composition** paper rather than a total-effect paper. The pooled estimate is noisy; the department heterogeneity is the point. That can work. But then the paper should stop trying to sell the pooled effect as if it were the headline and fully embrace “heterogeneous incidence of capacity contraction” as the main result.

The null-ish pooled result is not itself interesting. The interesting part is the composition pattern. The paper basically knows this, but it still spends too much rhetorical energy apologizing for or dressing up the pooled DiD.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

Be honest: in current form, this is **not an AER paper**. It is an interesting, topical, potentially publishable paper somewhere, but the gap is substantial.

### What is the main gap?

Mostly a **scope + framing + ambition** problem.

- **Framing problem:** The paper has a stronger idea than its current packaging. It should be about selective preservation of state capacity under uniform retrenchment, not mainly about the 2025 DOGE freeze.
- **Scope problem:** The evidence is limited to vacancy postings over essentially two post months at the department level. That is too thin for the scale of the claims.
- **Ambition problem:** The paper stops at “who posted fewer jobs,” when the more ambitious question is “what type of state capacity was protected, what type was hollowed out, and with what consequences?”

### Is it a novelty problem?
Somewhat. The descriptive fact is new because the event is new, but novelty driven by recency is weak novelty. AER wants durable novelty.

### What is the single most impactful piece of advice?
**Rebuild the paper around functional composition of state capacity—using occupations, subagencies, or downstream outputs—not around department-level vacancy declines in one recent political episode.**

If they can only change one thing, that is it. The paper needs to move from “timely evidence on a freeze” to “a general result about how governments selectively retrench.”
