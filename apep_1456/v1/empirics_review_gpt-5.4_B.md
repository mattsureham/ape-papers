1. **Idea Fidelity**

The paper broadly follows the manifest’s core idea: use within-EU heterogeneity in GDPR enforcement to separate enforcement from the common legal shock of GDPR itself, and study ICT startup outcomes using Eurostat business demography matched to the Enforcement Tracker. It also adopts the intended staggered-adoption framing and uses a modern staggered DiD estimator (Callaway–Sant’Anna), which is faithful to the proposed identification strategy.

That said, several important elements of the original idea were either dropped or only partially implemented. First, the manifest emphasized enforcement *intensity* (fines per GDP, cases per enterprise, budgets/FTE as potential instruments), but the paper’s main treatment is instead a much coarser binary “first fine” indicator; the continuous intensity specification is relegated to a brief robustness check and appears uninformative. Second, the manifest proposed a richer mechanism analysis (entry quality, compliance investment, sectoral comparisons, possibly 3-year survival), but the paper provides only limited evidence on mechanisms and does not seriously pursue the compliance-investment channel. Third, the manifest’s central claim was that DPA budgets/FTE create plausibly exogenous enforcement variation; the paper invokes this argument institutionally but does not implement the IV or otherwise validate the exogeneity of enforcement timing. In short, the paper pursues the main question, but in a thinner and weaker form than the original design envisioned.

2. **Summary**

This paper studies whether heterogeneous national enforcement of the GDPR affected ICT startup formation and survival within the EU. Using country-level Eurostat business demography data and GDPR fine data in a staggered DiD framework, it finds no effect on ICT birth rates and a positive but imprecise effect on 1-year survival, which the paper interprets as suggestive of selection rather than chilling.

The topic is interesting and potentially important. However, in its current form the paper does not yet make a convincing causal contribution, because the main identifying assumption is directly undermined by the paper’s own pre-trends evidence, and the treatment measure is too coarse and potentially endogenous to support the substantive conclusions drawn.

3. **Essential Points**

1. **The causal design is not credible as currently implemented.** The paper explicitly reports that the formal pre-trends test rejects parallel trends for the main survival outcome at \(p<0.001\). That is not a peripheral concern; it is central. Once the core identifying assumption fails for the main outcome, the positive survival result cannot be presented as evidence “consistent with” a selection mechanism in a causal sense. At minimum, the paper must substantially redesign the empirical strategy or sharply downgrade its claims.

2. **Treatment measurement is poorly aligned with the economic object of interest.** The paper claims to study “enforcement intensity,” but the principal treatment is the year of first fine. That may capture publicity or activation, but it is not intensity, and it is highly sensitive to arbitrary coding choices (“any fine” vs. “significant fine,” country aggregation for Germany, treatment of tiny early fines, and the Enforcement Tracker’s dependence on public disclosure). The paper needs a much more defensible treatment construction and a serious discussion of measurement error and comparability across DPAs.

3. **The conclusions overreach relative to the evidence.** With null effects on entry, an imprecise positive survival estimate, rejected pre-trends, and a null continuous-treatment specification, the paper does not establish that enforcement “raises the compliance bar without deterring entry,” nor that “the binary distinction between active and dormant DPAs matters more than the size of any individual fine.” Those are interesting hypotheses, but the evidence here is too weak to support them.

4. **Suggestions**

The paper asks a worthwhile question, and there is a potentially publishable contribution here if the authors are willing to tighten the design and narrow the claims. My suggestions are therefore aimed at making the paper more credible rather than merely adding more tables.

First, I would strongly encourage the authors to rethink the identification strategy around the fact that the binary “first fine” event is probably not the right treatment. The paper’s own continuous treatment result suggests that fine amounts per GDP do not map cleanly into the outcome, but that does not validate the onset measure. It may simply reflect that both measures are noisy proxies for enforcement. A better approach would be to construct several conceptually distinct enforcement variables and treat the choice among them as a first-order empirical issue: (i) first fine of any size; (ii) first fine above a meaningful threshold; (iii) cumulative fine count per 100,000 firms; (iv) cumulative fines per GDP; (v) DPA staffing or budget per capita; and ideally (vi) complaint-processing or investigation activity if available. The paper should show how countries move under each definition and whether the estimated effects are robust across sensible measures. Right now, too much turns on one coarse classification.

Relatedly, the paper needs a much richer discussion of data quality. The Enforcement Tracker is convenient and replicable, but it is not an official harmonized administrative dataset. It depends on public reporting and legal disclosure practices that may themselves vary across countries. That is especially problematic here because the paper’s identification comes from cross-country differences in observed enforcement. A strong revision would benchmark the tracker against official DPA annual reports or EDPB aggregates for at least a subset of countries and years. If reporting intensity differs systematically—for example, if “active” countries are simply more transparent—then the treatment may partly measure observability rather than true enforcement. This does not kill the project, but it must be addressed explicitly.

Second, I think the paper would benefit from shifting from a broad causal claim to a more modest and more credible empirical contribution unless the design can be materially improved. As written, the paper states both that the pre-trends fail and that the results are informative for harmonization policy. That combination is hard to sustain. One way forward is to recast the paper as evidence on whether *observable enforcement activation* is associated with startup outcomes, while being explicit that causal inference is tentative. Another, stronger path is to redesign the estimation to better handle underlying differential trends. For example, the authors could allow for country-specific linear trends; estimate effects in a narrower window around treatment onset; or match/synthetically reweight early- and late-enforcing countries based on pre-2018 trends and levels in startup outcomes and macro variables. None is a panacea, but each would be more responsive to the documented pre-trend problem than the current strategy.

A particularly promising improvement would be to exploit the sector dimension more directly rather than relying on a single-sector placebo. Since the theory is about data intensity, the strongest version of the design is not simply “early-enforcing countries versus late-enforcing countries in ICT,” but rather “more data-intensive sectors versus less data-intensive sectors within countries, before and after enforcement activation.” In other words, a triple-difference structure could help absorb country-level confounds that affect all sectors. The current construction placebo is useful, but it is a weak test because construction differs from ICT along many dimensions besides GDPR exposure. A more persuasive approach would compare ICT to a set of sectors with graded data intensity, or interact enforcement with sector-specific exposure to personal data processing. That would bring the paper much closer to isolating the relevant mechanism.

Third, the outcome construction and timing should be clarified much more carefully. The paper says the estimation sample is 2014–2021, but the data discussion also states that the business demography outcomes cover 2008–2020, and the table observation counts are not obviously reconciled with 27 countries over 8 years. For survival rates in particular, timing matters: a 1-year survival rate in year \(t\) corresponds to firms born in \(t-1\), and 3-year survival rates embed even longer lags. This means treatment timing relative to cohort birth is critical. The paper should define exactly what a year-\(t\) survival observation refers to and map treatment timing onto the birth cohort’s exposure to enforcement. Right now the interpretation “enforcement onset in year \(t\) affects year-\(t\) survival” is too loose. A cleaner design would explicitly model birth cohorts and their exposure at entry, which is conceptually central to the paper’s selection story.

Fourth, the mechanism claims need to be scaled back or substantiated. The paper presents “selection versus chilling” as if it can distinguish them, but with a null effect on birth, an imprecise survival coefficient, and a negative point estimate for entrant size, it cannot. If the authors want to keep a mechanism section, they should frame it as exploratory and add more direct evidence. The manifest suggested compliance-investment outcomes from ICT usage surveys; that would be valuable. If stricter enforcement increases the share of firms with formal privacy or cybersecurity policies, that would at least support the interpretation that enforcement changes organizational investments. Likewise, if birth rates are unchanged but entrant composition shifts toward larger or more capitalized firms, that would be informative. Without such evidence, the “selection” interpretation feels more like a conjecture than a finding.

Fifth, I would encourage the authors to make fuller use of the rich institutional variation they describe. The paper repeatedly notes that DPA budgets and staffing are set nationally and vary enormously. That is potentially useful, but right now it serves mostly as narrative support. If the authors believe capacity drives enforcement, they should show it empirically: a first-stage relationship between budget/FTE and fines or onset timing, the timing of budget changes relative to outcomes, and whether budget predicts startup trends in the pre-GDPR period. I am not fully convinced the proposed IV would satisfy exclusion—it is easy to imagine state capacity or broader regulatory preferences affecting entrepreneurship directly—but even absent IV estimation, budget/FTE data could help validate the treatment and sharpen the institutional story.

Sixth, the paper should put less interpretive weight on the TWFE-versus-CS sign reversal. In principle, that comparison is useful, but here it is over-read. A sign change is not itself evidence that the CS estimate is credible; it could just as easily reflect noise, small samples, or unstable cohort composition in a dataset with 27 countries and limited post-treatment years. If the paper wants to make a methodological point, it should report cohort-specific effects, influence diagnostics, and the weight structure much more transparently. Which cohorts drive the positive ATT? Is it a few early-enforcing countries such as France or Germany? How sensitive are results to dropping Spain, Ireland, or Germany? With such a small sample, leave-one-country-out analyses are essential.

Seventh, the placebo analysis should be strengthened and more consistently reported. At present the text and table appear to mix TWFE placebo estimates with CS main estimates, which makes comparison awkward. If the preferred estimator is Callaway–Sant’Anna, then the placebo exercises should ideally be shown with the same estimator, or at least the paper should explain why they are not. More importantly, a single construction placebo is not enough. I would suggest adding several lower-data-intensity sectors and, if possible, a placebo outcome that should not react on the hypothesized margin. Also, because the pre-trends problem is the main weakness, “placebos” should include graphical and formal tests not only for fake treatment dates but also for treated versus control countries in the actual pre-period.

Finally, the paper would improve with a more restrained conclusion. The current title, framing, and conclusion imply a fairly strong lesson for EU harmonization. But the empirical record in the paper is: no effect on birth rates, no robust effect using continuous intensity, suggestive but statistically weak positive survival estimates, and a rejected parallel-trends assumption. That is enough for an interesting short paper if framed correctly: the evidence does not support a large chilling effect of observable enforcement activation on ICT startup entry, while any survival benefits remain tentative. That is a useful contribution. It just is not yet the stronger claim that enforcement improves startup quality or that activation matters more than sanction intensity.

In sum, the paper has a good question and a potentially valuable setting, but the current version falls short on causal identification and on the alignment between its concepts, measures, and conclusions. A successful revision would either substantially strengthen the design—especially treatment measurement and handling of differential trends—or narrow the paper’s claims to match what the evidence can currently support.
