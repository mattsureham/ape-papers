## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether a procedural feature of U.S. lawmaking—the Congressional Review Act’s “lookback” window—changes how agencies write regulations when presidential power is about to change hands. Using federal rulemaking data around presidential transitions, it argues that agencies facing a real risk of CRA reversal do not issue fewer rules, but they issue markedly shorter ones, implying that political vulnerability changes the composition of regulation before Congress ever votes.

Why should a busy economist care? Because this is, at root, a paper about how political institutions shape the production technology of policy. If true, the CRA does not just kill a handful of rules ex post; it distorts bureaucratic investment ex ante.

### Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Not quite. The introduction is competent and readable, but the pitch arrives as an institutional-design paper rather than as a broadly important economics question. The first paragraphs emphasize that the CRA has “never been studied as a source of causal variation” and explain the design mechanics quickly. That is useful, but it is not the hook. The hook is that **reversal risk changes what the state produces**.

The introduction should lead less with “there is a neat threshold in the CRA” and more with “what happens to policy production when future political control is uncertain?” That is the economic question. The CRA is the setting, not the contribution.

### The pitch the paper should have

A stronger first two paragraphs would say something like:

> Governments often make policy under the shadow of future political reversal. When today’s policymakers expect tomorrow’s government to undo their actions, do they stop producing policy—or do they change the kind of policy they produce? This question matters far beyond U.S. regulation: it speaks to how political turnover shapes state capacity, investment in policymaking, and the durability of public decisions.
>
> This paper studies that question using the Congressional Review Act, which allows an incoming Congress and president to nullify regulations finalized late in the previous administration. I show that when a cross-party transition makes CRA reversal credible, agencies do not reduce the number of final rules they publish, but they publish substantially shorter rules. The implication is that political vulnerability distorts policy production on the intensive margin: institutions that make policy easier to reverse can shrink regulatory effort before any formal repeal occurs.

That is the AER version of the paper. The current intro is closer to a good field-journal intro.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper’s contribution is to show that credible ex post reversal risk from the Congressional Review Act changes the **intensive margin** of regulation—agencies keep producing rules, but produce less elaborate ones during vulnerable periods.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?

Only partly. The paper says it is the “first causal evidence” on the CRA and distinguishes itself from work on midnight rulemaking, but the differentiation is still a bit mechanical. A reader can tell there is a new institutional setting and a clean design, but it is less clear why this is not just “another paper showing politics affects rulemaking near transitions.”

The paper needs sharper contrasts with neighboring literatures:

1. **Midnight rulemaking papers** document end-of-term surges or timing changes.
2. **Congressional oversight / separation-of-powers papers** study how political control affects agencies.
3. **Policy uncertainty / hold-up style papers** study underinvestment when future appropriation or reversal is possible.
4. **Legal scholarship on the CRA** discusses usage and doctrine, but not behavioral effects on rule production.

Right now, the paper is strongest on (4), middling on (1), and underdeveloped on (2) and (3). For AER, the contribution must be framed less as “first causal paper on CRA” and more as “evidence on how reversibility alters state behavior.”

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a literature gap?

Too much as a literature gap. “The CRA has never been studied causally” is true but not enough. The world question is stronger:

- How does the threat of policy reversal affect bureaucratic investment?
- Do political institutions reduce policy output or just induce simplification?
- What margin of government behavior responds to expected lack of durability?

That is the framing the paper should lean into.

### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what’s new?

They could say: “It’s a paper showing that around CRA cutoffs in cross-party transitions, rules get shorter.” That is intelligible.

But they might also say: “It’s another quasi-experimental paper about rulemaking around presidential transitions.” That is the danger. The paper has novelty in setting, but the intro does not yet elevate that into novelty in concept.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Several specific possibilities:

- **Better outcome concept.** “Page length” is intuitive but narrow. The paper would feel bigger if it could connect to outcomes that economists read as more substantive than document length: legal durability, implementation speed, litigation, later amendment/rescission, compliance burden, or economic significance.
- **Mechanism sharpening.** Is the result about reduced investment in a given rule, or about selection into which rules get finalized? The discussion notes both, but the paper’s strategic value would rise if it could distinguish them more clearly.
- **Broader framing.** Connect the CRA to a general theory of reversible policymaking, not just U.S. administrative law.
- **More meaningful composition measures.** If shorter rules also cite fewer comments, fewer analyses, fewer statutory justifications, or affect narrower regulated constituencies, the “quality/quantity tradeoff” would be more convincing as a broad phenomenon.
- **Comparative benchmark.** The paper would get bigger if it could show whether the CRA effect is economically large relative to other major shocks to administrative production, not just statistically large.

If I had to name the single change that would most enlarge the contribution: **move from “page length” to “bureaucratic investment and policy durability.”** Page length can remain an empirical measure, but the contribution should not sound like it is about pages.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Agencies often rush rulemaking near presidential transitions. The administrative state is producing policy under a known deadline, and some late-term rules may be vulnerable to future political reversal.

### Tension

The CRA creates a sharp increase in reversal risk for rules finalized late in a congressional session, especially when power is about to switch parties. The unresolved question is: when policy becomes vulnerable, do agencies change timing, volume, or the nature of the rules themselves?

### Resolution

The paper finds little evidence of a volume change at the threshold, but a sizable drop in rule length during cross-party transitions. Agencies appear to preserve throughput while simplifying or truncating what they produce.

### Implications

The CRA’s main effect may not be the handful of rules Congress later repeals, but the anticipatory distortion of rule production itself. Institutions of review and reversal can shape state behavior long before they are formally invoked.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the ingredients, but not a fully disciplined arc. Right now it is somewhat a collection of sensible findings orbiting a main result. The story is there, but the paper does not fully commit to it.

The main narrative should be:

1. **Policy under threat of reversal**
2. **A setting where reversal risk sharply changes**
3. **Agencies preserve output but reduce investment**
4. **Therefore political reversibility distorts the production function of the state**

That story is stronger than the current version, which is closer to:

1. Here is the CRA
2. Here is a design
3. Here are some outcomes
4. Here is a quality-quantity interpretation

The latter is serviceable, but not memorable.

### If it is a collection of results looking for a story, what story should it be telling?

It should be telling a story about **how governments respond to fragile property rights over policy**. Agencies “own” policy imperfectly when successors can cheaply undo it. That creates underinvestment or simplification. This is a classic economics story wearing an administrative-law costume.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?

“Rules written under credible CRA threat are much shorter, but there aren’t fewer of them.”

That is the right lead because it contains the puzzle. If agencies fear reversal, why not change timing or volume? Why simplify instead?

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

They would lean in briefly—especially political economists and public economists. The fact is interesting enough. But they will lean in much more if the presenter immediately translates “shorter rules” into “lower policy investment” or “lower durability,” not if they stay at the level of document page counts.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Almost certainly: **“Does shorter mean lower quality, lower ambition, or just different composition?”**

That is the question sitting right behind the paper’s main result. The paper anticipates it in the discussion, but for strategic positioning, that question is central. If readers suspect page length is too noisy or cosmetic, the paper’s punch weakens quickly.

### If the findings are modest: is the modesty itself interesting?

The volume null is actually useful and should be sold more strongly. It makes the paper less obvious and more interesting. “Agencies don’t stop producing—they cheapen what they produce” is a much more compelling fact than “agencies publish fewer rules.”

The null on volume does not feel like a failed experiment; it feels like a key part of the story. The paper should emphasize that more cleanly and earlier.
