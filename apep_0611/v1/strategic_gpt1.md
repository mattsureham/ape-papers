## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether the Congressional Review Act changes how agencies write regulations before any repeal vote ever happens. Using the sharp timing of the CRA lookback window around presidential transitions, it argues that when control of the presidency changes parties, agencies do not issue fewer rules, but they issue markedly shorter ones—suggesting that anticipated political vulnerability changes the composition of the regulatory state.

A busy economist should care because this is, at least in aspiration, a paper about how political institutions distort government production under threat of reversal. The broader question is not really “what does the CRA do?” but “how does anticipated policy instability shape the quality/intensity of state capacity?”

### Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Partially. The current introduction is competent, but it leads with the institution and the design rather than the big economic idea. The first two paragraphs say, in effect, “there is a rule, here is the threshold, I exploit it.” That is serviceable for a field journal. For AER, the pitch has to start at a higher level: political reversibility changes investment incentives, and regulation is one domain where we can see that distortion clearly.

Right now, the paper undersells itself by sounding like an institutional note on midnight rulemaking plus a neat RD. The opening should foreground the world question and only then introduce the CRA as the lever.

### The pitch the paper should have

> Governments often make policy under the shadow of political reversal. When future officeholders can cheaply unwind today’s decisions, bureaucracies may respond not by doing less, but by investing less in the quality and durability of what they produce. This paper studies that force in U.S. federal rulemaking by exploiting the Congressional Review Act, which makes regulations issued late in an outgoing administration especially vulnerable to repeal after a cross-party transition.
>
> Using the CRA’s lookback cutoff across presidential transitions, I show that agencies facing credible repeal risk do not reduce the number of rules they finalize, but they do produce substantially thinner rules. The result suggests that policy instability distorts the composition of state output ex ante: the threat of reversal weakens regulatory production before any political veto is actually exercised.

That is the AER version of the paper. It turns a specialized administrative-law paper into a paper about dynamic political incentives and state capacity.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper’s contribution is to show that credible ex ante repeal risk from the Congressional Review Act changes the composition of federal regulation—reducing the apparent complexity of rules during cross-party transitions without reducing rule volume.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?

Not yet clearly enough. The paper names the right broad neighbors, but the differentiation is still a bit generic:

- from **midnight rulemaking** papers, it differs by focusing on *content/composition* rather than volume/timing;
- from **CRA/legal scholarship**, it differs by studying *behavior before repeal* rather than repeal episodes themselves;
- from **bureaucratic politics/political control** papers, it differs by identifying one concrete institutional channel;
- from **difference-in-discontinuities applications**, it differs mainly in setting, which is not itself a major contribution.

That structure is fine, but the intro currently spends too much space announcing “first causal evidence on the CRA” and not enough space proving why that is economically important. “First causal evidence” is not a contribution category that excites general-interest readers on its own.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or as filling a gap in a LITERATURE?

Too much the latter. The paper often sounds like: “the CRA has not been causally studied” and “this design has not been used here before.” That is a literature gap and a methods gap. The stronger framing is a world question:

- When policies are vulnerable to future reversal, do governments cut quantity, or cut quality?
- Does political instability weaken state capacity through anticipatory underinvestment?
- Are institutions that increase democratic control also making regulation thinner ex ante?

That is the right frame.

### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what’s new?

Right now, maybe, but with some risk of “it’s another quasi-experimental paper on regulatory timing.” The intro gives the ingredients, but the novelty is not distilled sharply enough.

What you want a reader to say is:

> “It’s a paper showing that repeal risk changes how agencies produce regulation before repeal happens—they keep output up, but the rules get thinner. So the CRA affects the regulatory stock indirectly, not just through the handful of rules Congress overturns.”

That is memorable. The paper is close to that but not fully there.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Three possibilities, in descending order of strategic value:

1. **Reframe the outcome as durability/implementation capacity, not just page count.**  
   Page count is a weak flagship outcome for AER. If the paper can connect thinner rules to something economists care about more directly—survival, litigation vulnerability, implementation delays, rescission, judicial challenge, compliance burden, or downstream policy salience—the contribution becomes much bigger.

2. **Develop the mechanism around bureaucratic underinvestment under reversal risk.**  
   The discussion gestures at holdup/underinvestment. That is promising. If the paper can more clearly show that agencies cut analytical effort, legal insulation, or politically costly investments when reversibility rises, it becomes a paper about dynamic incentives in public production, not just document length.

3. **Make the comparison more substantively meaningful.**  
   “Cross-party vs same-party transitions” is useful, but broad. If the paper can distinguish cases where CRA threat is especially credible—e.g., unified government, highly salient agencies, economically important rules, ideologically distant transitions—it would sharpen the underlying mechanism and broaden relevance.

The single biggest “scope” problem is that page length and CFR parts are still too administrative as primary outcomes. For AER, the ideal version of the paper would connect institutional vulnerability to economically meaningful policy output.

### How should the paper position itself relative to those neighbors?

Mostly **build on and redirect**, not attack.

- Relative to **midnight rulemaking**: “That literature showed end-of-term surges; I show that one institution changes the *kind* of regulation produced.”
- Relative to **CRA scholarship**: “That literature studies repeal as an ex post event; I show its ex ante shadow.”
- Relative to **bureaucratic control**: “This is a concrete, measurable margin by which anticipated political oversight changes public production.”
- Relative to **policy uncertainty/hold-up**: “The CRA offers a setting where reversibility risk changes investment in public outputs.”

The paper should not overclaim that it overturns existing accounts. It is adding a sharp mechanism within an established story.

### Is the paper currently positioned too narrowly or too broadly?

Slightly too narrowly in substance and slightly too broadly in ambition.

Too narrow because much of the language is administrative-law specific: Federal Register pages, CRA lookback dates, Senate legislative calendars. That risks confining the audience to regulation specialists.

Too broad because lines like “first causal evidence that CRA exposure alters the composition of the federal regulatory stock” sound grander than the current outcomes warrant. “Composition” here is mostly page length. That is suggestive, not yet sweeping.

The sweet spot is: a focused institutional paper with broad implications for policy reversibility and bureaucratic incentives.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of? What fields should it be speaking to?

It should speak more directly to:

- **State capacity / bureaucratic incentives**
- **Policy uncertainty and reversibility**
- **Dynamic political economy of institutions**
- **Law and economics of regulation**
- Possibly **organizational economics**: when organizations face higher ex post veto risk, do they adapt by changing effort allocation rather than output targets?

Right now it feels more connected to political science and legal scholarship than to mainstream economics conversations. For AER, that is a problem unless the paper translates itself into an economics question very explicitly.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?

Not fully. The paper is currently having the “midnight rulemaking / CRA” conversation. That is necessary but not sufficient.

The more impactful conversation is:

> Political institutions that increase the probability of ex post reversal can reduce ex ante investment in public decisions.

That is the unexpected and richer literature link. If the author can make readers think of hold-up, dynamic incentives, and state capacity rather than just administrative calendars, the paper’s ceiling rises significantly.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Agencies nearing a presidential transition face pressure to complete rules before political control changes. The regulatory state is known to surge at midnight, but we do not know how a specific reversal threat changes what gets produced.

### Tension

The CRA creates credible repeal risk for rules finalized within a narrow window, especially when the incoming administration is from the opposite party. The puzzle is whether agencies respond by changing timing, changing volume, or changing the nature of the rules themselves.

### Resolution

The paper finds that around the CRA threshold in cross-party transitions, rules become substantially shorter, while publication volume does not shift discontinuously.

### Implications

The threat of political reversal seems to distort the composition of state output even if formal repeal is rare. Institutions designed to discipline agencies may therefore weaken regulatory investment ex ante.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the skeleton of one, but not yet a fully compelling arc. The paper currently reads as a good design attached to a set of outcomes. The “story” is there, but the reader has to assemble it.

The main weakness is that the resolution rests too heavily on a proxy. “Rules are 9.6 pages shorter” is a result; it is not yet a full story. The discussion tries to provide three possible interpretations—truncated deliberation, strategic simplification, or underinvestment under repeal risk—but because the paper does not choose among them strongly, the narrative loses force.

### What story should it be telling?

It should tell one disciplined story:

1. **Political reversibility raises the expected return to delay less than the expected return to simplification.**
2. **Agencies therefore preserve output levels but reduce investments that make rules longer, more defended, or more durable.**
3. **The CRA’s main effect may be as a shadow cost on regulatory production, not merely as a repeal device.**

That is coherent and general-interest. If the author cannot fully adjudicate among mechanisms, the introduction and conclusion should still emphasize one central idea: repeal risk changes the *investment margin* of policy production.

### What fact would I lead with?

I would lead with:

> “When a new president from the other party is about to take office, regulations finalized in the CRA-vulnerable window are dramatically thinner—but agencies don’t publish fewer of them.”

That is the fact with the highest chance of engagement.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

Mixed, but leaning in if framed correctly.

If presented as:
- “I study page counts around a Senate-calendar threshold,” phones.

If presented as:
- “I show that the threat of policy reversal causes the state to keep output up while downgrading the intensity of what it produces,” people lean in.

So this is a framing-sensitive paper. The underlying fact is interesting, but only if lifted out of the legal-institutional weeds.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Probably one of these:

1. “Does shorter mean less important, less legally durable, or just written more efficiently?”
2. “Do these thinner rules get overturned or challenged more often?”
3. “Why don’t agencies just move rules earlier rather than make them thinner?”
4. “Is this really about the CRA, or about cross-party transitions generally?”

Those are exactly the strategic questions the paper should anticipate and use to structure the introduction and discussion.

### If the findings are modest: is the null itself interesting?

The null on volume is genuinely useful because it sharpens the composition story. It says agencies do not simply stop producing. That is a nice contrast.

The problem is not that the findings are null or modest; it is that the positive result is on a somewhat modest outcome. The paper makes a decent case that “X doesn’t affect volume” is informative, but the broader interest still depends on whether “page length” is accepted as a meaningful proxy for investment/quality.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

At present, the gap is mainly a **framing problem**, secondarily a **scope problem**.

### Framing problem

The science, at least at the level relevant to this memo, may be perfectly respectable. But the paper is framed as a niche paper on the CRA and midnight rulemaking. That is not enough for AER. It needs to be framed as a paper about how anticipated policy reversal distorts public-sector investment and state capacity.

### Scope problem

Even with better framing, the flagship outcome is thin. AER readers will ask whether shorter rules are actually less consequential, less durable, less analytically grounded, or just shorter. To really excite the top people in the field, the paper likely needs at least one outcome that is closer to economic substance or institutional durability.

### Novelty problem

Moderate. The exact design and setting are novel enough, but the broad intuition—that politics affects agency behavior near transitions—is not new. What is new is the ex ante composition angle. The paper must make that the centerpiece.

### Ambition problem

Yes, somewhat. The paper is careful and competent, but safe. It takes a clever institutional setting and extracts a clean result, but it stops one step short of the bigger question. The bigger question is not “does the CRA shorten rules?” It is “how does political instability deform state output?”

### Single most impactful piece of advice

**Rewrite the paper around a broader claim: the threat of policy reversal causes bureaucracies to preserve output while reducing investment in the quality/durability of policy—and then support that claim with at least one outcome more substantively meaningful than page count.**

If the author can only change one thing, that is the change.
