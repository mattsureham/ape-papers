**Idea Fidelity**

The paper remains faithful to the manifest’s core ambition: it exploits staggered bans on coal-tar sealants to ask whether coal-tar–specific PAH concentrations in waterways decline following policy adoption, using Water Quality Portal data and comparing treated jurisdictions to contemporaneous controls via staggered DiD. That said, key elements promised in the manifest are only partially realized. The identification strategy outlined there envisaged 6+ statewide bans and 30+ municipal bans across 2006–2025, with treated stations being compared to unbanned neighbors, and included spatial RDD and placebo strategies. In contrast, the empirical paper restricts treatment to four statewide/district bans (D.C., Washington State is omitted because of data limits, Minnesota, New York, Maryland) and largely ignores the broader constellation of municipal/county bans. The spatial granularity that would allow “neighboring controls” or boundary-based inference is absent, and the advertised spatial RDD robustness check is never implemented. The paper does test placebos and alternative outcomes, but it never quite realizes the multi-jurisdictional richness suggested by the manifest, nor does it deliver the explicit spatial continuity check. Those omissions should be flagged.

**Summary**

The paper estimates the effect of coal-tar sealant bans on surface-water fluoranthene concentrations using USGS monitoring data, a staggered DiD design, and modern estimators (TWFE and Callaway–Sant’Anna). The baseline TWFE point estimate suggests a large decline, but the estimate is fragile: placebo contaminants respond (lead), a sister PAH (pyrene) does not, pre-trends are worrisome, and excluding D.C. flips the sign. The authors conclude that existing compliance-oriented monitoring networks are too sparse and irregular to credibly identify the ban’s effect.

**Essential Points**

1. **Credibility of Parallel Trends and Control Group Construction.** The paper cannot demonstrate that treated and control stations are comparable pre-treatment. The reported pre-trends in the Callaway–Sant’Anna event study are substantively large and negative, and treated jurisdictions appear to have been on a downward trajectory before treatment. Given the small number of treated jurisdictions and their distinctive monitoring footprints (e.g., D.C.’s urbanized district, Minnesota’s climate conditions), the paper must either show balanced pre-trends via tighter matching or station selection, or otherwise confront selection on unobservables (e.g., differential stormwater investments) more rigorously. As it stands, the identifying assumption is rejected in the event-study diagnostic, which undermines any causal claim.

2. **Treatment Assignment and Sample Definition.** The paper ostensibly evaluates a multi-jurisdictional ban, but the actual sample excludes crucial ban adopters (Austin, Washington State, Virginia, Maine) and includes very few treated stations—D.C.’s 8 stations drive the negative estimate. The treatment variable is not defined at the spatial scale where bans operate; municipal bans within non-treated states are absorbed into the control group, potentially contaminating the comparison. The authors need a transparent map/table showing which jurisdictions are in the sample, why others are excluded, and how municipal bans are treated. Without this, the “population” to which the results apply is unclear, and attenuated treatment effects from contaminated controls cannot be ruled out.

3. **Use of Aggregated Station-Year Means and Measurement Issues.** Collapsing to station-year means may hide systematic variation in sampling frequency, timing, and detection limits, while substituting non-detects with half the detection limit introduces mechanical relationships between treatment and the outcome if sampling intensity changes post-ban. The fact that controlling for percent non-detect dramatically changes the estimate indicates measurement artifacts. The authors must justify (with sensitivity analyses) both the aggregation approach and the non-detect treatment, and ideally show results with alternative constructions (e.g., Tobit models, sample-level regression with fixed effects for sampling dates) to ensure the signal isn’t driven by mechanical averaging or detection-limit censoring.

**Suggestions**

- **Clarify and Expand the Treatment Sample.** The manifest emphasized “6+ statewide bans plus 30+ municipal/county bans.” The paper should revisit this promise by either including those additional bans (e.g., Austin, Washington State, Virginia, Maine, municipal bans) or by clearly explaining why they are excluded (lack of data, insufficient pre-period, data quality issues). If data scarcity prevents their inclusion, the authors should explicitly state that the paper is essentially a four-jurisdiction study, and discuss how generalizable the findings are once the more comprehensive legal timeline is omitted. A table listing all ban dates, available states, reasons for exclusion, and the number of stations per jurisdiction would greatly aid transparency.

- **Improve Pre-Trend Balance via Matching or Synthetic Controls.** The problematic pre-trends suggest treated stations differ systematically from controls. One way forward would be to restrict the control group to stations that resemble treated ones in pre-treatment trends: e.g., match on pre-ban fluoranthene trajectories, watershed characteristics, urbanization, or sampling frequency. Alternatively, constructing synthetic control groups for each treated jurisdiction could provide more credible counterfactuals, especially since there are only a few treated units. Another option is to exploit the spatial aspect of the manifest by focusing on ban boundaries and employing spatial RDD (as initially promised). Even if the data are thin, a boundary design would help isolate local comparators subject to similar seasonal and regional shocks.

- **Address Measurement and Sampling Intensity Concerns Head-On.** The paper notes that sampling frequency and detection limit substitution substantially affect estimates. Rather than simply controlling for these factors, the analysis could model the sampling process explicitly. For instance, an instrumental variables-style strategy could use the presence of a ban to instrument for sampling intensity changes (if bans induce more monitoring), or a Tobit/regression on the raw concentration with censoring at the detection limit (instead of substituting half the limit) could reduce mechanical bias. Alternatively, the authors could estimate the models on a subsample of stations with regular sampling (e.g., at least 5 observations per year over several years) to see whether the results persist. This would demonstrate whether the fragility arises from noise in the monitoring process rather than the absence of a treatment effect.

- **Elaborate on the “Monitoring as Infrastructure” Argument with Comparative Evidence.** The paper concludes that monitoring networks are ill-suited for causal evaluation. It would be helpful to substantiate this claim with more detailed diagnostics: e.g., histograms of stations per jurisdiction, sampling irregularity metrics, and covariates that differ between treated and control networks (e.g., urban vs. rural, regulatory purpose). Showing that stations in treated jurisdictions systematically differ in sampling density or baseline concentration from those in controls would strengthen the critique and provide constructive guidance for future monitoring design (e.g., recommending paired stations or dedicated pre-post sampling protocols).

- **Discuss the Implications of the Placebo Tests More Nuancedly.** The lead placebo is strikingly negative, but the interpretation is currently somewhat cursory. The authors suggest that this means the ban variable captures other environmental improvements. They could leverage this by showing whether those improvements coincide with known policy changes (e.g., combined sewer overflow upgrades, stormwater utilities) during the sample period. Time-varying municipal-level covariates or fixed effects might help separate sealant bans from broader environmental trajectories. If such data are unavailable, the authors should explicitly qualify what confounds are most plausible and how they might be addressed in future work.

- **Expand on Why Pyrene Behavior Diverges.** The null result for pyrene is puzzling. If fluoranthene responds but pyrene does not, the mechanism is unclear. The authors could investigate whether pyrene is less frequently measured or more often below detection limits, which could explain the imprecision. Reporting detection frequencies, sample sizes, and trends for each PAH would clarify whether the null for pyrene reflects data limitations or a real chemical puzzle. If measurements of pyrene are less reliable, that too supports the paper’s broader claim about monitoring inadequacies.

- **Reframe the Contribution Around “Limits of Monitoring-Based Evaluation.”** Even if a clear causal estimate is elusive, the paper offers a valuable diagnostic case study. Emphasize this framing more: treat the null/fragile estimates as evidence about data limitations rather than a failed attempt at causal estimation. This could involve providing a short “roadmap” for how future data collection could change the inference—e.g., specifying the minimum number of stations, regular sampling frequency, or co-located control sites needed to detect a 50% decline. Such a roadmap would make the paper more prescriptive and policy-relevant.

Overall, the paper raises important questions but should tighten its empirical scope, enhance identification, and more systematically diagnose the monitoring limitations it seeks to highlight.
