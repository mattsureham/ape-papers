## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
This paper evaluates whether digitizing a ubiquitous legal friction—the requirement for in-person notarization—stimulates entrepreneurship. Using the staggered adoption of Remote Online Notarization (RON) laws across US states, the author finds a "precise zero" effect on new business filings, suggesting that physical presence requirements are not a binding constraint on US business dynamism. 

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this pitch very clearly. The first two paragraphs effectively set the stage (the "archaic" friction vs. the "natural experiment"). However, to elevate it for the AER, the pitch should lean harder into the *theoretical* tension: why might we have expected this to matter more than, say, broadband access or one-stop registration shops? It needs to frame notarization not just as a "law" but as a proxy for "transactional distance" in a modern economy.

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
The paper’s contribution is establishing that removing a high-frequency administrative friction (physical notarization) has zero impact on the margin of business formation in a developed economy.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It differentiates itself well from the "registration reform" literature (Djankov, Bruhn) by showing that what works in developing/unreformed markets (Mexico) does not necessarily translate to marginal improvements in highly efficient markets (USA).
*   **Question vs. Literature:** It starts with a question about the WORLD (Does digitization matter?), but quickly retreats into being a "well-powered null" paper.
*   **"Another DiD paper":** Currently, a smart economist might dismiss this as "another DiD paper about a niche law." 
*   **Bigger Contribution:** To make this "big," the author needs to explore *where the friction actually went*. Did it shift to the banking sector? Did it affect the *speed* of formation rather than the *volume*? If the volume didn't change, did the *composition* of founders change (e.g., more rural founders)? Without a "where it went" or "who it helped" story, a null result on a single outcome feels small.

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
The paper sits at the intersection of **Regulatory Barriers** (Djankov et al., 2002), **Business Dynamism** (Decker et al., 2014), and **e-Government** (Hjort & Poulsen, 2019).

*   **Relationship:** It builds on the "declining dynamism" literature by testing a specific supply-side explanation and finding it wanting.
*   **Conversation:** It is currently positioned a bit too much as a "methodological win" (using Callaway-Sant'Anna). The AER doesn't care about the estimator unless the estimator changes the conclusion. It should speak more to the **Industrial Organization** of the legal profession or the **Urban/Regional** benefits of removing distance-based costs.
*   **Missing Pieces:** The paper should connect to the literature on "sludge" (Sunstein) or administrative burden. It also misses the "legal tech" disruption conversation.

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC
*   **Setup:** The US has seen declining business dynamism; reformers point to archaic "red tape."
*   **Tension:** Digital advocates claim in-person notarization is a major "last mile" hurdle; yet US infrastructure for notaries is already dense.
*   **Resolution:** Precise zero. RON didn't help.
*   **Implications:** Digitization isn't a silver bullet for dynamism.

**Evaluation:** The arc is serviceable but lacks a "Resolution 2.0." A great AER paper doesn't just say "it didn't work"; it explains *why* it didn't work in a way that teaches us something new about economic behavior. Is it because the cost was already negligible? Or because the real bottleneck is elsewhere in the chain (e.g., occupational licensing)?

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
At a dinner party: "We looked at those laws that let you notarize documents over Zoom. It turns out they had absolutely zero impact on how many people started businesses."
*   **Reaction:** "Oh, I guess that makes sense. I usually just go to the UPS store anyway."
*   **The Follow-up:** "Wait, did it at least make it easier for people in the middle of nowhere?"

**The Rub:** If the "So What" is "the law was pointless," the paper is a policy brief. If the "So What" is "even when you remove physical distance, the administrative 'sludge' of the US government remains a barrier," it's an AER paper.

---

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
*   **Move to Appendix:** Much of the "Standardized Effect Sizes" (Table 5) and the repetitive robustness checks can be slimmed down.
*   **Front-load:** The "Mechanism" discussion in Section 6 is actually the most interesting part. Move the "Why this might not bind" discussion earlier to frame the empirical test.
*   **Addition:** The paper needs a heterogeneity test by **geographic isolation**. If RON doesn't help people in rural areas who are 50 miles from a notary, then the friction truly doesn't matter. If it *only* helps them but they are a small share of the data, the "zero" is misleading.

---

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The gap is **Ambition**. Currently, the paper is a very competent "Null Result" exercise. To be an AER paper, it needs to be an **"Anatomy of a Friction"** paper.

**The Single Most Impactful Advice:** 
Don't just report the null on total applications; prove *why* it didn't bind by testing for heterogeneity in founder distance to the nearest notary and by looking at high-frequency data on the *speed* of business registration (if available) rather than just the *volume*.
