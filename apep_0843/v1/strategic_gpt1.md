## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether eliminating one visible legal friction to entrepreneurship—the need to appear in person before a notary—meaningfully increases new business creation. Using staggered adoption of Remote Online Notarization laws across US states, it finds essentially no effect on business applications, suggesting that this much-discussed “last mile” of government digitization is not a first-order constraint on entrepreneurship.

Does the paper itself articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs? Mostly yes, but the current opening is still too institutional and too small-bore. It starts from notarization rather than from the larger economic question. For AER, the first two paragraphs should lead with the broader claim people make about digitizing government and reducing administrative frictions, then use notarization as a sharp test of that claim.

### The pitch the paper should have

“Economists and policymakers increasingly argue that digitizing government can unlock entrepreneurship by removing small but pervasive administrative frictions. Yet we know surprisingly little about whether eliminating these last-mile legal hassles changes real economic behavior in advanced economies, where formal entry is already relatively easy.

This paper studies one of the clearest such reforms: state adoption of Remote Online Notarization, which removes the requirement that entrepreneurs physically appear before a notary for many business-related documents. Exploiting staggered adoption across US states, I find that eliminating this friction had no detectable effect on new business formation. The result suggests a broader lesson: not every administrative inconvenience is an economically meaningful barrier, even when it is salient, archaic, and politically attractive to reform.”

That is the AER version of the paper. Right now the paper is close to this, but still reads a bit like “here is a neat policy and a clean null” rather than “here is evidence on which administrative frictions matter for entrepreneurship.”

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper’s contribution is to show that removing an in-person legal compliance requirement through remote notarization did not measurably increase business formation in the US, implying that this particular form of regulatory digitization is not a quantitatively important entrepreneurship constraint.

### Is this clearly differentiated from the closest papers?

Partially, but not sharply enough. The paper distinguishes itself from broad entry-regulation papers and from digitization papers, but the differentiation is still somewhat generic: “those papers study bigger reforms; I study this narrower one.” That is true, but it is not yet intellectually crisp.

What would make the differentiation clearer is something like:

- Prior papers show that **large reductions in registration burden** can matter.
- This paper asks whether **marginal digitization of a specific legal step** matters in a high-income setting with otherwise low administrative barriers.
- The substantive contribution is therefore about the **limits** of administrative simplification, not just one more policy estimate.

That is a much more defensible and interesting contribution than “first paper on RON.”

### World question or literature gap?

At its best, the paper is framed as a world question: do small legal frictions bind entrepreneurship? That is the stronger framing. But the introduction drifts into “contributes to three literatures,” which dilutes the force. The paper should be much more unapologetic about the world question and much less eager to catalog literatures.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new?

Right now, a smart economist could say: “It’s a staggered DiD on remote notarization laws and business formation, and they find zero.” That is intelligible, but not yet memorable. The risk is exactly what you flagged: “another DiD paper about X.”

What they should be able to say is: “It’s a paper showing that one prominent class of e-government reform—removing in-person legal formalities—doesn’t move entrepreneurship in the US, so the marginal administrative frictions policymakers obsess over may not be the binding ones.”

That is the version with a claim on attention.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Specific ways to enlarge it:

1. **Use outcomes closer to the friction.**  
   The current outcome is business applications. That is sensible but indirect. A bigger paper would connect more directly to the legal margin supposedly affected:
   - state incorporation/LLC filings,
   - filing completion times,
   - attorney/title/formation service usage,
   - document classes that actually require notarization.

2. **Demonstrate take-up or exposure.**  
   The paper currently infers irrelevance from no effect on outcomes. A more ambitious version would show whether RON was actually used, by whom, and in which document categories. If usage was tiny, then the paper becomes about policy non-salience/implementation failure. If usage was substantial and effects are still zero, then the “friction doesn’t bind” interpretation becomes much stronger.

3. **Exploit heterogeneity where the friction should matter most.**  
   Not just corporate applications, but:
   - rural vs urban states/counties,
   - places with thin notary availability,
   - industries with more notarization-intensive startup processes,
   - founders more likely to face mobility/time constraints.

4. **Reframe toward marginal vs substantial regulatory reform.**  
   A larger contribution would ask: when do administrative simplifications matter? Then RON is one test case in a broader taxonomy. As written, the paper risks being “RON doesn’t matter”; reframed, it could be “small convenience-oriented digital reforms do not substitute for meaningful reductions in entry costs.”

The last of these is likely the most publishable strategic move.

### How should the paper position itself?

Mostly **build on**, not attack.

The right positioning is:

- The entry-regulation literature shows that **major** burdens can matter.
- This paper studies whether a **targeted digitization reform removing one physical-presence requirement** matters in the US.
- The null result refines the literature by separating **big administrative barriers** from **small convenience frictions**.

This is not a paper that should posture as overturning Djankov/Bruhn. It should say those papers identified settings with large wedges; this paper shows that not every procedural reform creates such a wedge.

### Is it positioned too narrowly or too broadly?

Currently both, oddly.

- **Too narrowly** because the object “remote online notarization” is niche and somewhat technocratic.
- **Too broadly** because the paper claims contribution to “three literatures,” including a “credible null result literature,” which reads like compensation for limited substantive reach.

The right audience is narrower but more coherent: entrepreneurship, regulation, and state capacity/e-government. The paper should own that audience, then make a clean general point from it.

### What literature does it seem unaware of?

A few things seem underdeveloped:

1. **State capacity / administrative burden / government digitization**  
   There is a broader political economy / public economics conversation on administrative burdens, take-up frictions, and state modernization. The paper should probably speak to that more explicitly.

2. **Transaction cost / law-and-economics literature**  
   Since notarization is a legal formality, there is likely relevant law-and-economics work on formalities, verification, and document authentication. The paper currently leans more on entrepreneurship than on legal institutions.

3. **Business registration digitization in developed economies**  
   If there are papers on online filing portals, one-stop shops, e-signatures, or digital identity systems, those are closer conceptual comparators than some of the current citations.

4. **Salience / take-up / implementation literatures**  
   If the null is partly because entrepreneurs do not know or use RON, then that is not just a business formation paper; it is also about adoption of enabling legal technologies.

### Is it having the right conversation?

Not quite. The paper is currently in a somewhat awkward conversation between entrepreneurship, digitization, and null-results methodology. The most impactful conversation is:

**Which administrative frictions are truly binding, and when does government digitization translate into real economic activity?**

That is broader and more important than the current “what is the effect of RON laws?” conversation.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

There is a live policy belief that archaic administrative requirements suppress entrepreneurship, and that digitizing these requirements should stimulate firm creation. Remote notarization is a clean case of removing one such old-fashioned in-person formality.

### Tension

We have good evidence that large registration burdens can impede business creation, but much less evidence on whether more targeted, incremental digital reforms matter in advanced economies. Policymakers often assume these reforms are growth-enhancing; that assumption has not really been tested.

### Resolution

The paper finds no detectable effect of RON laws on business formation overall or in the most plausibly exposed business categories.

### Implications

The implication is not merely “RON doesn’t work.” It is that policymakers may overestimate the economic payoff to convenience-oriented digitization when the underlying friction is small, non-salient, or already mitigated by market alternatives. Efforts to boost entrepreneurship may need to target larger costs or different bottlenecks.

### Does the paper have a clear arc?

It has a reasonably clear arc, but it is underpowered narratively because the object is too small and the stakes are not elevated enough. The paper currently has the shape of a good field-journal paper: clear question, clean design, precise null. For AER, it needs to feel like the resolution of a broader economic puzzle.

At moments, it also reads like a collection of careful null-result exercises looking for a larger claim. The story it should be telling is:

**This is a paper about the limits of administrative simplification as entrepreneurship policy. RON is the test case, not the main character.**

That shift would make the narrative much stronger.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?

“I’d lead with: removing the requirement to physically appear before a notary—a reform many people would guess lowers startup frictions—had essentially zero effect on business formation.”

That is a decent dinner-party fact.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

A mixed reaction.

They would lean in initially because the result is counterintuitive enough: this sounds exactly like the kind of annoying friction economists like to say matters. But the follow-up reaction would depend on whether the presenter can immediately answer: “Was notarization ever actually binding?” If not, attention drops quickly.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

The obvious one:

**“But did anyone actually use remote notarization, and are you measuring the margin it should affect?”**

That is the central strategic vulnerability of the paper. Not an identification issue; a storytelling issue. If the answer is “usage was low” or “BFS is indirect,” then the contribution narrows from “this friction doesn’t matter” to “this reform as implemented didn’t move business formation.”

### Is the null itself interesting?

Potentially yes. This is one of the better forms of null result: a policy that is intuitive, plausible, and politically salient appears not to matter. That is useful.

But the paper has to work harder to explain why this null updates beliefs. Right now it sometimes reads as if it is trying to certify “precisely zero” as sufficient in itself. Precision helps, but precision alone does not make a paper important. The null is interesting if the author can persuade the reader that many informed people would have expected a positive effect and that this result meaningfully changes how we think about digitization and entrepreneurship.

At present, it is close, but not fully there.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

Bluntly: in current form, this is not yet an AER paper. It is tidy, competent, and possibly publishable in a solid field journal if the execution holds up. But AER needs either a bigger question, a sharper conceptual payoff, or richer evidence on the mechanism that makes the null genuinely illuminating.

### What is the gap?

Mostly a **scope and ambition problem**, secondarily a **framing problem**.

- **Framing problem:** The paper is about a niche policy lever and does not yet fully elevate itself into a paper about which administrative frictions matter.
- **Scope problem:** The outcome is broad and indirect relative to the policy. Without more direct evidence on exposure, usage, or affected margins, the null feels bounded.
- **Ambition problem:** The paper is content to stop at “no effect.” For AER, it needs to say more about why, and what broader theory of digitization and entrepreneurship this supports.

I do **not** think the main problem is novelty in the sense of “someone already studied RON.” The issue is that the current novelty is too narrow.

### What would excite the top 10 people in this field?

One of two things:

1. **A broader, more general claim with evidence:**  
   A paper showing that a class of small administrative digitization reforms does not move entrepreneurship, and explaining why not.

2. **A much tighter link from reform to actual affected behavior:**  
   Evidence on notarization usage, incorporation filings, firm type, geography, and timing that turns the paper into a decisive statement about the non-bindingness of this friction.

Right now it has neither fully.

### Single most impactful advice

**Reframe the paper from “the effect of RON laws” to “the limits of administrative simplification as entrepreneurship policy,” and support that framing with outcomes or heterogeneity that get closer to the margin actually affected by notarization.**

That is the one change that most improves the paper’s odds. If the author cannot add more direct outcomes, then the framing must become even more disciplined and modest: this is evidence that one prominent convenience-oriented legal digitization reform did not translate into more business formation, not definitive proof that in-person notarization is irrelevant.
