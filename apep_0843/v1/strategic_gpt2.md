## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether a very specific form of government digitization—allowing documents to be notarized remotely rather than in person—meaningfully increases business formation. Using staggered state adoption of Remote Online Notarization laws, it argues that removing this legal formality had essentially no effect on new business creation, suggesting that some highly visible administrative frictions are simply not first-order constraints on entrepreneurship.

A busy economist should care because the paper is really about a broader question: when does reducing bureaucratic hassle matter for real economic behavior, and when is digitization mostly convenience theater? If credible, the answer informs how we think about state capacity, regulatory reform, and the economics of administrative burden.

### Does the paper articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Not quite. The current introduction is competent, but it starts too literally—with notarization as an archaic formality—rather than with the broader stakes. The paper’s best idea is not “here is a policy called RON”; it is “we can test whether a much-discussed, intuitively annoying legal friction actually constrains entrepreneurship.” That should be the opening frame.

Right now the introduction gets to the question fairly quickly, but it still reads like a niche policy paper rather than a paper about the economic importance of administrative frictions. The broader motivation is there, but buried.

### The pitch the paper should have

Here is what the first two paragraphs should say instead:

> Economists and policymakers increasingly argue that digitizing government procedures can unlock economic activity by removing small but pervasive administrative frictions. Yet we know surprisingly little about which frictions actually bind. Many legal requirements appear cumbersome and outdated, but appearance is not the same as economic relevance.
>
> This paper studies one such friction: the requirement that entrepreneurs appear in person before a notary to complete legal documents used in business formation and related transactions. I use the staggered adoption of Remote Online Notarization laws across U.S. states to ask whether eliminating this in-person requirement increases new business creation. The answer is no: despite its intuitive appeal as a barrier-reducing reform, remote notarization had essentially no measurable effect on business applications, suggesting that not all administrative simplifications generate economically meaningful responses.

That gets the paper into the right conversation immediately.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### Contribution in one sentence

The paper’s contribution is to show that eliminating one prominent legal-administrative friction—in-person notarization—did not measurably increase U.S. business formation, implying that this widely discussed form of digitization was not a binding constraint on entry.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?

Only partly. The paper cites classic entry-regulation papers and some digitization work, but the differentiation is still thin. As written, the contribution risks sounding like: “another reduced-form paper on a regulatory reform with a null result.” The introduction says what the paper finds, but it does less well at saying why this specific reform is a particularly revealing test of a broader hypothesis.

The differentiation should be sharper along two dimensions:

1. **Relative to business registration reforms:**  
   The paper should stress that prior work studied large, bundled reforms that reduced multiple procedures, costs, or delays. This paper isolates a narrower friction embedded inside legal process. That is potentially interesting because it helps distinguish “administrative burden in general matters” from “every administratively annoying step matters.”

2. **Relative to e-government/digitization papers:**  
   The paper should emphasize that much of that literature studies digital access, service delivery, or broad digital infrastructure. This paper asks whether converting an offline legal compliance requirement into an online one changes real economic decisions. That is a more precise and, in some ways, more policy-relevant question.

### Is the contribution framed as a question about the world, or as filling a gap in a literature?

Mostly the former, which is good. The core question—does removing in-person notarization increase entrepreneurship?—is a world question. But the paper occasionally slips into “three literatures” mode, especially in the intro, and that weakens it. The paper is stronger when it says: policymakers think administrative frictions matter; here is a test of one; it does not matter much.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the intro?

Yes, but only somewhat cleanly. They could say: “It studies remote online notarization laws and finds no effect on business formation.” That is understandable. The risk is that they would add, “So it’s a DiD on a niche deregulation with a null result.”

To avoid that, the paper needs the reader to instead say: “It’s a paper about which administrative frictions are actually binding. It isolates a reform that seemed plausibly important and shows it wasn’t.”

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Several possibilities:

- **Better outcome closer to the actual friction.**  
  The paper itself admits the biggest limitation: EIN applications are not the exact margin where notarization bites. If the author could connect to state incorporation filings, LLC formations, articles of incorporation, or other filing-level outcomes closer to notarization-intensive acts, the contribution would become much more persuasive and less indirect.

- **Actual uptake or exposure.**  
  If the paper could show whether RON was used at nontrivial rates after authorization, it could distinguish “not a binding friction” from “reform existed on paper but was barely implemented.” That would materially deepen the interpretation.

- **A stronger mechanism/comparison.**  
  The corporate-vs-other application split is the right instinct, but still fairly blunt. A more convincing comparison would isolate entities, industries, or legal actions for which notarization is definitely required versus those for which it is not.

- **A broader conceptual framing.**  
  The paper should market itself as evidence on the limits of “small-friction deregulation” or “marginal digitization,” not just notarization. That makes the question bigger without changing the data.

If the author could only enlarge the paper one way, I would say: get outcomes closer to actual state filing behavior.

2. **State capacity and digital government**  
   Not just digitization as technology, but digitization as institutional modernization.

   A law authorizing a technology is not the same thing as actual usage. The paper should at least nod to the distinction between formal policy adoption and effective operational uptake.

Not fully. Right now it is having a somewhat mechanical conversation with business entry and staggered DiD papers. The more interesting conversation is with the economics of administrative frictions and the uneven payoff to digitization.

The unexpected but more impactful framing is: **governments are digitizing countless touchpoints under the assumption that every removed hassle translates into economic gains; this paper shows that assumption can fail even in a clean, intuitively plausible case.**

That is a better AER-style conversation than “another state-policy business formation paper.”

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

The world before this paper: policymakers and reformers believe that archaic, in-person legal requirements impose unnecessary costs on entrepreneurs, and digitizing those processes should facilitate business creation.

### Tension

We know that big regulatory barriers can deter entry, but we do not know whether narrower, highly visible procedural frictions—like in-person notarization—actually bind in a modern U.S. setting. The puzzle is that notarization feels burdensome and outdated, yet may be too small or too easily worked around to matter.

### Resolution

Remote Online Notarization laws had no detectable effect on business applications, including categories where notarization should matter most.

### Implications

The payoff to digitization depends on whether the targeted friction is truly binding. Policymakers should not assume that every legal modernization reform will stimulate entrepreneurship; some reforms deliver convenience and legal flexibility without materially changing economic outcomes.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

Mostly yes, but it is still more **results-forward** than **story-driven**. The story exists; it just has not been sharpened enough. The current manuscript often reads like: policy background, design, null result, sensitivity checks. That is fine for a field journal, but top general-interest papers need a cleaner conceptual arc.

### What story should it be telling?

It should be telling this story:

> There is a broad enthusiasm for reducing administrative friction through digitization. But reformers often conflate “annoying” with “economically consequential.” Remote notarization is a useful test case because it targets a real, concrete, legally salient, and politically popular friction. The fact that its removal does not move entry tells us something important: the marginal burdens that are easiest to digitize may not be the ones that most constrain economic behavior.

That is the paper’s real narrative.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at dinner?

“I have a paper showing that letting firms notarize documents online instead of in person had basically zero effect on business formation.”

That is actually a decent opening fact because it has a nice contrast: intuitively burdensome reform, no effect.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

Some would lean in—especially those interested in entrepreneurship, regulation, digitization, or public administration—but the reaction depends entirely on framing. If presented as “a null result on notarization,” phones come out. If presented as “evidence that many small-friction digitization reforms may not affect real activity,” people lean in.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Immediately: **“Is that because notarization never mattered, or because the outcome is too far from the actual margin / take-up was low?”**

That is the key question, and the manuscript should confront it more frontally. It does mention the limitation in discussion, but this is not a minor caveat; it is the central interpretive issue.

### Is the null itself interesting?

Yes, potentially. This is one of the better kinds of nulls: it evaluates a reform that many intelligent people would expect to help, and it can rule out effects that are not just statistically but economically meaningful. That is valuable.

But the paper has to work harder to sell the null as **learning**, not merely **non-finding**. The right case is:

- This was a prominent, politically salient modernization reform.
- The mechanism was intuitive.
- The estimates are tight enough to reject effects in the range associated with more substantial deregulation.
- Therefore the null updates our beliefs about which frictions matter.

That argument is present, but it needs to be the organizing frame, not just a paragraph about “credible null result literature.”

The “credible null” paragraph in the intro is, frankly, the weakest part of the framing. It feels defensive. Better to show why the question mattered and let the precision do the work.
