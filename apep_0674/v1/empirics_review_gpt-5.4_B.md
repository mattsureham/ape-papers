## 1. Idea Fidelity

The paper follows the core spirit of the manifest: it studies PBF 2.0 using institution-level IPEDS data, uses staggered adoption with a Callaway-Sant’Anna design, examines completion outcomes, and tries to test unintended consequences via enrollment composition. It also implements the proposed private-institution placebo and discusses treatment-intensity heterogeneity.

That said, the paper delivers only a narrower version of the original idea. Most importantly, it drops several central components that were part of the proposed contribution: the sample is restricted to public four-year institutions rather than the broader public higher-education sector; the “cream-skimming” margin is tested only through minority enrollment share rather than Pell, part-time, or broader at-risk groups; the promised decomposition of the completion pipeline is largely absent; and the field-of-study, credential mix, and faculty-investment mechanisms are not analyzed. The resulting paper is therefore much closer to “PBF and minority enrollment share at public universities” than to the broader paper envisioned in the manifest.

## 2. Summary

This paper studies whether performance-based funding for public universities increased degree production and whether it induced cream-skimming. Using institution-level IPEDS data and a Callaway-Sant’Anna staggered DiD design, the paper finds no detectable effect on bachelor’s completions or graduation rates, but reports a decline in minority enrollment share, which it interprets as evidence of cream-skimming.

The paper addresses an important policy question and uses a more appropriate estimator than older TWFE-only studies. However, the main substantive claim rests on a fairly thin evidentiary base, and several design and interpretation choices need to be strengthened before the paper can support a causal contribution of the kind expected in AER: Insights.

## 3. Essential Points

1. **The key “cream-skimming” conclusion is not yet convincingly established.**  
   The paper’s headline result is a decline in minority enrollment share, but this is only one compositional measure, and it is not clearly tied to the groups most directly implicated by PBF incentives. Minority share can change because of state demographic trends, recruitment shifts, or changes in total enrollment composition unrelated to academic risk. The decomposition table is also not aligned with the main estimator: the paper’s central claim uses CS-DiD, while the Black/Hispanic decomposition is reported only with TWFE and is imprecise. To support the cream-skimming claim, the authors need a fuller set of outcomes—especially Pell share, perhaps part-time share if feasible, and group-specific enrollment levels rather than only shares—and should estimate these using the same heterogeneity-robust framework.

2. **Identification is plausible but under-demonstrated, especially given state-level policy endogeneity.**  
   The paper leans heavily on the assertion that PBF adoption was driven by politics rather than outcome trends, but this is not enough. Many states adopted PBF amid broader higher-education reforms, fiscal pressures, and changing demographic conditions. The private-college placebo is useful, but it is not sufficient to validate parallel trends for public institutions, since public and private sectors may be exposed differently to state budget shocks and policy bundles. The paper needs much stronger event-study evidence, more transparent cohort timing and treatment coding, and a better-developed within-state public-versus-private design as a main corroborating analysis rather than a brief robustness check limited to completions.

3. **The paper’s contribution relative to the literature is currently overstated.**  
   The null effects on completions and graduation are broadly in line with existing work, so the paper’s incremental contribution depends on the unintended-consequences margin. But because that margin is only partially measured and not triangulated across multiple outcomes, the current evidence does not yet justify the stronger framing that PBF “shift[s] who enrolls” or that institutions respond on the “easiest margin available.” A more modest claim would be appropriate unless the authors substantially expand and tighten the evidence on composition and mechanisms.

## 4. Suggestions

This is a promising paper, and I think it can become much stronger with a tighter design and a more disciplined interpretation.

First, I would encourage the authors to **refocus the paper around a narrower and more credible contribution**. Right now the manuscript promises a broad assessment of PBF’s intended and unintended effects, but the actual evidence is strongest on only two margins: null effects on bachelor’s completions and one composition result on minority share. Rather than presenting a sweeping story about gaming, it may be better to frame the paper as: modern staggered-DiD evidence confirms little effect of PBF on university completions, and there is suggestive evidence of enrollment-composition changes that warrant closer scrutiny. That would be a more proportionate match between evidence and claims.

Second, the paper would benefit from **much more transparent treatment coding and sample construction**. There are several small inconsistencies that matter for credibility: 25 treated states in the paper versus about 30 in the manifest; 676 institutions in the abstract versus 727 in the data section; Indiana and Ohio grouped in 2009 despite the institutional background saying Ohio followed in 2013; and use of GR200 while describing six-year/150 percent graduation rates. These may be fixable, but in a short empirical paper the coding decisions need to be exceptionally clean. I would strongly suggest an appendix table listing, for each treated state: legislative year, implementation year, formula intensity, covered sectors, and source. A reader should be able to audit the treatment definition easily.

Third, I would like to see **event-study figures for all main outcomes**, especially completions and minority share, with pre-trend estimates and confidence intervals shown clearly. The text says pre-trends are “centered near zero,” but the reader needs to see that directly. Given the staggered rollout, it would also help to report both overall dynamic effects and cohort-specific dynamics, or at least effects separately for early and late adopters. Since the paper emphasizes heterogeneity-robust methods, the graphical evidence should be central rather than buried in text.

Relatedly, the paper should **take the within-state comparison more seriously**. The manifest correctly highlights private institutions as a useful within-state control. In the current version, this appears mainly as a placebo and a DDD robustness check for log completions only. But the main identifying concern is state-level confounding, and the within-state public-versus-private contrast is one of the strongest tools available to address it. I would encourage the authors to estimate DDD-style models for the main composition outcomes as well, especially minority share and Pell share. Even if private institutions are imperfect controls, showing that the minority-share result survives a within-state public-private comparison would materially strengthen the argument.

On outcomes, the paper should do more to **separate shares from levels**. A decline in minority share does not necessarily imply fewer minority students enrolled; it could reflect faster growth in nonminority enrollment. Reporting effects on total minority enrollment, Black enrollment, Hispanic enrollment, and total enrollment would make the interpretation much sharper. If the true concern is cream-skimming away from at-risk students, levels matter. The same applies on the completion side: if minority completion shares fall but minority completion counts do not, the interpretation is different.

I also recommend expanding the composition analysis to **Pell recipients**, since PBF formulas often explicitly or implicitly create incentives related to economically disadvantaged students. Pell share is much closer to the “at-risk” concept in the paper than minority share alone. If Pell data are noisier or not available for all years, the paper can be explicit about the tradeoff and perhaps use a shorter panel. But without some income-related measure, the cream-skimming interpretation remains incomplete.

The current decomposition in Table 2 is not yet persuasive because it mixes estimators and yields imprecise subgroup results. A better approach would be to **estimate subgroup-specific CS-DiD effects directly** for Black share, Hispanic share, Pell share, and perhaps interactions with baseline selectivity or minority-serving status. If the aggregate minority-share result is really driven by Hispanic enrollment, as the paper suggests, that should be shown using the same estimator as the main result.

I would also encourage the authors to **tone down claims about “precise nulls”** somewhat. For log completions, the estimate is fairly tight, but for graduation rates it is not. More importantly, institutional responses to PBF may operate with long lags, especially for six-year outcomes. Since many treated cohorts are late adopters, a simple average ATT may understate delayed effects. One useful addition would be a dynamic analysis focused on longer-run post-treatment years where available, even if only for earlier cohorts. This would also help address the concern that the design is better suited to near-term enrollment responses than to eventual graduation outcomes.

The discussion of dose response is interesting, but at present it is too thin to support interpretation. The paper should be more explicit that **formula intensity is not randomly assigned** and may proxy for broader reform ambition. If the authors retain this analysis, I would present it cautiously and perhaps show descriptive evidence first: which states are “high dose,” what years they adopt, and how comparable they are to other adopters. Otherwise the monotonicity claim may read as stronger than the evidence allows.

A more general suggestion is to **align the empirical material more tightly with the theory**. The paper invokes multitask principal-agent theory and analogies to K–12 accountability, but the actual evidence on strategic response is limited. This is not necessarily a problem, but then the theoretical framing should be lighter and more disciplined. If the authors want to maintain a stronger theory section, they should bring in outcomes more directly linked to strategic adjustment: certificate versus bachelor’s mix, transfer patterns if available, admissions proxies, or faculty/instructional staffing. Even one or two additional mechanism outcomes would help.

Finally, the manuscript would benefit from **a clearer statement of what is and is not learned causally**. The strongest causal result appears to be: PBF did not materially increase bachelor’s degree production at public four-year institutions. The composition result is potentially important, but should be presented as evidence consistent with cream-skimming rather than definitive proof of it unless the additional analyses above are added. That change in tone would make the paper more credible and, in my view, more persuasive.

Overall, this is a worthwhile topic and the use of modern DiD methods is a real plus. But for the paper to make a genuine contribution to our understanding of causal policy effects, it needs stronger validation of the composition result, better transparency on treatment coding, and a more measured interpretation of what the evidence can support.
