## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
This paper investigates whether tying state funding for public universities to graduation rates (Performance-Based Funding 2.0) actually increases the number of degrees produced. Using modern staggered difference-in-differences, it finds a "precise null" on degree production but uncovers a significant unintended consequence: universities shift their enrollment composition away from minority students—who may be perceived as "riskier" to graduate—to protect their funding.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this pitch reasonably well by the end of the first page, but the opening is a bit "textbook." It leads with a specific case (Tennessee) and then moves to Holmström-Milgrom. To be an AER paper, the pitch should be less about the policy evaluation of PBF and more about the fundamental economic tension of **multitask principal-agent problems in public goods provision.** 

**The pitch it should have:** 
"High-powered incentives in the public sector often lead to 'gaming' where agents optimize on the measured margin while neglecting—or actively harming—unmeasured ones. This paper uses the staggered adoption of performance-based funding in U.S. higher education to show that while billions of dollars in incentives failed to increase degree production (the intensive margin), they successfully induced universities to 'cream-skim' their student bodies (the extensive margin). The results provide a stark cautionary tale for the design of accountability systems in settings where the quality of inputs is heterogeneous and costly to observe."

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**Contribution:** The paper identifies a trade-off in public sector incentive design where high-stakes completion targets fail to improve output but successfully induce exclusionary enrollment shifts (cream-skimming).

- **Differentiation:** It differentiates itself from the education-policy literature (e.g., Hillman, Ortagus) by using modern "heterogeneity-robust" DiD methods (Callaway-Sant’Anna) and by focusing on the *joint* test of production vs. composition.
- **World vs. Literature:** It is currently framed largely as a gap-filling exercise (applying new DiD to an old PBF question). It needs to be framed as a question about the **limits of performance pay** in the public sector.
- **Smart Economist Test:** A smart economist would say, "It’s a DiD paper that shows PBF doesn't work and causes cream-skimming." To get into the AER, they need to say, "It’s the definitive evidence of why multitask incentives fail in higher ed."
- **Bigger Contribution:** To make this bigger, the author needs to explore the **"Why."** Is the cream-skimming driven by selective admissions at "flagship" schools, or by changes in recruitment at less selective ones? The current "differential growth" finding (p. 8) is a start, but it needs more meat.

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
The paper sits at the intersection of Public Economics (Incentives) and Economics of Education.

- **Neighbors:** Holmström & Milgrom (1991) on multitasking; Neal & Schanzenbach (2010) on K-12 gaming; and the recent "DiD revolution" (Goodman-Bacon, CS).
- **Positioning:** It currently "builds" on the neighbors. It should instead **bridge** them. It should position itself as the higher-ed equivalent of the famous K-12 accountability papers.
- **Missing Literatures:** It should speak more to the **Industrial Organization of Higher Education.** Universities are firms with market power; how do they compete for "easy-to-graduate" students? There is a missing conversation here about the "supply side" of social mobility.

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC
- **Setup:** States want more degrees and think money-on-the-line is the answer.
- **Tension:** Theories of multitasking suggest universities might just change *who* they teach rather than *how well* they teach.
- **Resolution:** Degree production is flat, but minority shares drop.
- **Implications:** Policy backfires; the "equity bonuses" meant to prevent this (mentioned on p. 4) are clearly insufficient.

**Evaluation:** The arc is "serviceable" but lacks a climax. The "Dose-Response" section (p. 10) is currently a footnote in the results. If the high-intensity states (Tennessee/Ohio) showed *both* more production and more cream-skimming, that would be a much more "AER-style" tension.

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
**The dinner party fact:** "States spent billions to bribe colleges into graduating more students; the colleges didn't graduate more people, they just started admitting more affluent white kids to hedge their bets."

**Reaction:** People lean in. The follow-up is: "Did the kids they rejected actually go somewhere else, or did they drop out of the system entirely?" The paper currently can't answer this (IPEDS is institution-level, not student-level), but it's the "So what" that determines the paper's ceiling.

---

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
- **Front-load the "Why":** The discussion of *why* the null exists (p. 11) is more interesting than the 10th robustness check. 
- **Decomposition:** Table 3 (Hispanic vs. Black) is weak. Instead of just splitting by race, split by **institutional selectivity.** The cream-skimming mechanism should look very different at a Top 50 public research university vs. a regional "access" college.
- **Eliminate:** The TWFE vs. CS meta-commentary is fine, but don't let the econometrics overshadow the economics.

---

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The gap is **Ambition.** Right now, this is a very high-quality "Policy Evaluation" paper. To be an "AER Paper," it needs to be a "Human Behavior" paper.

**The single most impactful change:** 
The author must move beyond the "average effect" and provide a sharper analysis of the **competitive response.** Do schools in states with higher competition for students engage in more cream-skimming? If the author can show that PBF changes the *competitive landscape* of higher education, this becomes a top-tier paper.
