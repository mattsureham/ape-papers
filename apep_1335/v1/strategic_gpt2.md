## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple, policy-relevant question: when a new cannabis dispensary opens, does it actually stimulate the local economy, or does it mostly reshuffle nearby consumer spending? Using Illinois’s lottery allocation of dispensary licenses, the paper argues that dispensary entry raises food-service employment modestly but does not generate broader employment or earnings gains, suggesting a narrow “foot traffic dividend” rather than neighborhood revitalization.

Does the paper itself articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs? Mostly, but not optimally. The current introduction gets to the design very quickly and frames the contribution partly as a cleaner identification exercise relative to prior work. That is useful, but it is not the strongest AER-facing pitch. The first two paragraphs should lead with the world question and the policy stakes: do highly visible new retail establishments create local multipliers, and should policymakers treat dispensary licensing as economic development policy? Only then should the paper introduce the Illinois lottery as the unusual opportunity to answer that question.

### The pitch the paper should have

“States increasingly justify cannabis dispensary licensing not only as a regulatory choice, but as a place-based development strategy: new stores are supposed to bring jobs, nearby spending, and neighborhood renewal. But do new dispensaries actually expand local economic activity, or do they simply generate narrow consumption spillovers with little broader labor-market effect?

This paper studies that question using Illinois’s lottery allocation of adult-use cannabis dispensary licenses. I show that dispensary entry increases local food-service employment, consistent with a foot-traffic channel, but produces no detectable gains in retail employment, total employment, or earnings. The core implication is that dispensaries look more like localized demand shocks than engines of broad-based economic revitalization.”

That is a much sharper “why care?” than “here is a cleaner DiD setting.”

### One-sentence contribution

The paper’s contribution is to show that new cannabis dispensaries generate narrow, adjacent consumption spillovers—especially to food service—without producing broader local employment or earnings gains, thereby reframing dispensary licensing as a limited retail agglomeration shock rather than a general economic development tool.

Only somewhat. The paper says prior work is mostly state-level and that Burkhardt studies crime rather than employment, which is fine as a start. But right now the differentiation still feels too much like “same topic, different outcome, cleaner design.” That is not enough for AER unless the question is big and the answer is surprising.

The closest challenge is that many readers will initially see this as:

- another staggered DiD paper,
- another “modest local spillover / mostly null” result.

To rise above that, the paper needs much clearer differentiation along one of two dimensions:

1. **Substantive question:** this is not mainly a cannabis paper; it is a paper about whether politically celebrated retail entry creates local multipliers.
2. **Conceptual result:** the key finding is not just “food service up 2.2%.” It is that a salient new retail amenity creates highly localized complementary demand but no broad labor-market expansion.

That second formulation makes the contribution bigger.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or filling a gap in a LITERATURE?

At present it is split, and too often drifts into the literature-gap framing: cleaner than cross-state studies, first to examine employment rather than crime, etc. That is weaker.

The stronger framing is world-facing:
- Policymakers claim dispensaries revitalize neighborhoods.
- That claim has become central to social equity licensing.
- The paper tests whether that claim is true.

That is a much stronger question than “there is surprisingly thin evidence on dispensary-specific effects.”

### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain what’s new?

The memorable version would be: “A randomized retail-entry setting shows that dispensaries bring restaurant spillovers but not economic revitalization.” That is closer to a top-journal identity.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Be specific:
- **Different framing:** Make this a paper on local multipliers from controversial retail entry / place-based consumption shocks, not primarily on cannabis legalization.
- **Different outcomes:** The paper would feel larger with outcomes that map more directly to the revitalization claim—commercial vacancies, business entry/exit, tax base, foot traffic, nearby wages, rents, property values, or municipal revenues.

If they can only keep the current data, the biggest upgrade is not adding more standard robustness; it is making the paper clearly about the limits of retail-led neighborhood development claims.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

States and cities increasingly sell cannabis dispensary licensing—especially social equity licensing—as a tool for local economic renewal. The implicit premise is that a new dispensary does more than create a single business; it sparks broader neighborhood economic activity.

### Tension

We do not actually know whether individual dispensary openings create broad local multipliers or just highly localized spending spillovers. Existing legalization studies bundle many policy changes together, so they cannot isolate the effect of retail entry itself.

### Resolution

In Illinois, lottery-based dispensary allocation provides quasi-randomized variation in entry. The paper finds a modest increase in food-service employment but no detectable effects on retail employment, total employment, or earnings.

### Implications

Dispensaries may generate complementary demand for nearby restaurants and bars, but they do not appear to be engines of broad-based labor-market improvement. Policymakers should therefore recalibrate claims about social equity licensing as neighborhood development policy.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the ingredients, but the arc is only **partially formed**. Right now the paper is a bit too much:
1. policy context,
2. design,
3. findings,
4. three literatures.

That is serviceable, but not fully compelling.

The story it should be telling is:

**Policymakers claim dispensaries revitalize neighborhoods. That claim rests on an untested multiplier logic. Illinois’s lottery lets us test that logic. The answer is mostly no: dispensaries create adjacent consumption spillovers, not broad local development.**

That is a clean setup-tension-resolution-implications arc. The paper currently gets there by page 3, but it should get there in paragraph 1.

There is also some “collection of results looking for a story” risk because the outcome set is broad but the findings are mostly null. The paper solves that by naming the positive result (“foot traffic dividend”), but it needs to work harder to make the nulls and the positive result part of the same conceptual story: **complementarity without multiplier.**

That phrase, or something like it, is the paper’s actual narrative core.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?

“Illinois dispensary openings appear to raise restaurant employment nearby, but they do not raise retail employment, total employment, or earnings—so dispensaries create foot traffic, not broad economic revitalization.”

That is the dinner-party fact.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

Some would lean in, but only if presented correctly. If pitched as “a lottery DiD on cannabis dispensaries,” many will drift. If pitched as “a test of whether a heavily politicized retail-entry policy actually creates local multipliers,” more will care.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Most likely:
- “Is that because the effects are hyper-local and your geography is too coarse?”
- Then: “So are dispensaries just stealing spending from elsewhere?”
- And for policy-minded economists: “If not jobs, do these licenses create wealth for owners or tax revenue for municipalities?”

Those are exactly the questions the paper should anticipate and partly incorporate into its framing.

### If the findings are modest or null, is the null interesting?

Yes—potentially. But the paper needs to make the case more forcefully.

Right now the nulls are described as precise and informative, which is good. But the paper should lean harder into why learning “dispensaries do not produce broad employment gains” matters:
- because the policy rhetoric says they should,
- because local development claims are politically consequential,
- because social equity programs are being justified on those grounds,
- and because many retail-entry narratives overstate multiplier effects.

So the nulls can be interesting, but only if framed as falsifying an important policy belief, not merely failing to detect an effect.

At present, the paper is halfway there.
