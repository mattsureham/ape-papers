## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple, policy-relevant question: when a new cannabis dispensary opens, does it generate meaningful local economic spillovers, or just reshuffle consumer spending? Using Illinois’s lottery allocation of dispensary licenses, the paper argues that dispensary entry boosts food-service employment modestly, but does not produce broader gains in retail employment, total employment, or earnings.

Why should a busy economist care? Because cannabis legalization has been sold not just as criminal justice reform or tax policy, but as local economic development. If the real effect is a narrow “foot traffic dividend” rather than neighborhood revitalization, that matters for how economists think about place-based development, retail spillovers, and the promises attached to social-equity licensing.

### Does the paper articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Not quite. The current introduction is competent, but it leads with legalization growth and then pivots quickly to design. It tells me “here is a nice setting” before fully persuading me “here is the big economic question.” The paper is strongest when framed as a paper about **what kind of local economic shock a dispensary actually is**—not as a paper that happens to have a lottery.

### The pitch the paper should have

“Cannabis dispensaries are increasingly justified as engines of neighborhood revitalization, especially under social-equity licensing programs. But do they actually create broad local employment gains, or do they merely attract consumer foot traffic that benefits a narrow set of nearby businesses? Illinois’s random allocation of dispensary licenses provides a rare opportunity to isolate the local economic effects of individual store openings within a common statewide policy regime. I show that dispensaries generate a modest increase in food-service employment, but no detectable gains in broader retail employment, total employment, or earnings—suggesting that dispensary entry creates a narrow consumption spillover rather than broad-based economic renewal.”

That is the version that belongs in the first two paragraphs.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper’s contribution is to show, using lottery-driven within-state variation in dispensary entry, that new cannabis dispensaries create a narrow local consumption spillover to food service rather than broad employment or earnings gains.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest papers?

Only partially. The paper distinguishes itself from cross-state legalization studies and from Burkhardt’s Washington lottery paper on crime, but the differentiation still feels too method-centered: “I have cleaner variation” and “I study employment instead of crime.” That is not yet enough for AER-level novelty.

The closest neighbors seem to be:
- **Hansen, Miller, and Weber** on labor-market and broader effects of cannabis legalization
- **Dragone et al.** on legalization outcomes
- **Sabia et al.** on employment/health/crime-related consequences of cannabis policy
- **Burkhardt et al.** on dispensary lotteries and crime in Washington
- On the retail spillover side, **Basker (Walmart)** and **Neumark et al.** on local retail effects

Relative to those, the real differentiation should be:
1. this is about **store entry rather than legal regime change**;
2. it identifies **the type of local spillover** a new controversial retail establishment creates;
3. it speaks to **economic development claims**, not just cannabis policy per se.

Right now that third point is underdeveloped.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or filling a gap in a LITERATURE?

At present, too much as filling a literature gap. The introduction says in effect: existing studies are state-level; here is a cleaner within-state setting. That is useful, but second-order.

The stronger framing is a world question:
- When policymakers introduce a new high-demand retail category, what local spillovers actually materialize?
- Are “job creation” claims about dispensary licensing substantively right, or are they rhetoric built around a narrower consumer-demand channel?

That is much stronger than “there are few papers on dispensary-specific effects.”

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the intro?

They could get there, but not cleanly enough. Right now a smart economist might say: “It’s a staggered DiD on Illinois dispensaries and local employment, with a small restaurant effect.” That is not the reaction the paper wants.

The ideal reaction is: “Interesting—the paper shows dispensaries are not local development engines; they mainly create a small consumption spillover to nearby food service.”

That is a meaningful idea. The current draft does not yet force the reader to say it.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Specific ways to enlarge it:

1. **Sharper economic object**  
   Move from “employment effects of dispensaries” to “what kind of local economic shock a dispensary is.” This is bigger and more conceptual.

2. **Better outcome hierarchy**  
   If the main finding is about foot traffic, then outcomes tied more directly to foot traffic would make the contribution feel much larger:
   - restaurant sales or taxable sales
   - cellphone visitation
   - merchant-level spending or card transactions
   - nearby commercial vacancy or rents  
   Right now food-service employment is only an indirect proxy for the story the paper wants to tell.

3. **Spatial tightening**  
   The county level makes the paper feel diluted. Even without changing the design, reframing this as a lower-bound test of local multiplier claims could help. But if the authors had tract/ZIP/place-level outcomes, the contribution would jump.

4. **Connect to social-equity policy more concretely**  
   The paper hints that advocates overpromised neighborhood revitalization. To make that claim bigger, it needs to be shown as a direct test of the core policy rationale, not an afterthought.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Cannabis dispensaries are often promoted as tools for local economic development, especially in social-equity frameworks. The world before this paper is one in which legalization advocates and policymakers make expansive claims about jobs and neighborhood revitalization, while the evidence mostly comes from broad state-level policy changes.

### Tension

Those broad claims are plausible but untested at the establishment level. A dispensary could be:
- a genuine local growth engine,
- a narrow traffic generator,
- or mostly a reallocation of spending across nearby businesses.

The key tension is that the paper’s empirical setting allows one to separate **store-entry effects** from **statewide legalization effects**, but the current narrative does not fully exploit that distinction.

### Resolution

The paper finds that dispensary entry is not associated with broad employment or earnings gains, but is associated with a modest rise in food-service employment.

### Implications

The implication is not “dispensaries do nothing.” It is “dispensaries do something narrower than advocates claim.” They may raise local foot traffic and complementary consumption without generating broad labor-market multipliers.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the ingredients, but the arc is still somewhat underpowered. At moments it reads like a collection of sensible estimates: retail null, total null, earnings null, food service positive, manufacturing placebo. That is orderly, but not yet a memorable story.

### What story should it be telling?

The story should be:

**Policymakers treat dispensaries as economic-development investments. But economically, dispensaries behave more like niche retail amenities: they create complementary consumption nearby, not broad-based renewal.**

Everything in the paper should serve that story:
- the lottery matters because it lets the author isolate store-entry effects;
- the food-service result matters because it reveals the actual spillover channel;
- the nulls matter because they discipline overbroad claims.

Right now the “foot traffic dividend” phrase is helpful, but the paper needs to make that the central organizing idea rather than one result among several.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?

“Illinois cannabis dispensaries appear to raise restaurant employment a bit, but they do not create broader county-level job growth. So the local effect seems to be a foot-traffic spillover, not economic revitalization.”

That is the best single fact in the paper.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

Some would lean in—but mostly if it is framed correctly. If presented as “a DiD on Illinois dispensaries,” phones come out. If presented as “a test of whether dispensaries are development engines or just traffic generators,” it gets attention.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Almost certainly:
- “How local is local—can you get closer than counties?”
- And second: “Is this about spending reallocation rather than net new activity?”

Those are exactly the questions the current paper raises but cannot fully answer. That is fine if the paper owns them and frames itself as a first clean answer, not the definitive one.

### If findings are modest, is the modesty itself interesting?

Yes, potentially very much so. The paper’s most interesting implication is that **the absence of broad gains is informative**, because the political rhetoric around these programs often implies much more. But the paper needs to lean harder into why a “narrow positive plus broad nulls” result is a substantive contribution rather than a partially successful hunt for significance.

At present, the nulls are described competently, but the authors need to make the interpretive point more forcefully:
- This is a test of an important policy claim.
- The claim fails on the broad outcomes.
- The surviving effect reveals the specific channel that actually operates.

That is a meaningful contribution if argued crisply.

A version of this paper that could more decisively answer:
