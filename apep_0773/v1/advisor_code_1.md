**Idea Fidelity**  
The paper largely follows the original idea. It focuses on the Medicaid unwinding’s administrative ripple effects on SNAP, uses ASAP state-level participation data, exploits variation in integrated versus separate eligibility systems, and attempts to isolate the administrative channel from the SNAP emergency allotment (EA) termination. That said, the manifest emphasized a triple-difference (DDD) identification—with SNAP enrollment before/after April 2023, integrated vs. separate systems, and high vs. low Medicaid procedural termination intensity. The paper’s main specification is a difference-in-differences (DiD) comparing integrated versus separate states, supplemented by a continuous treatment (procedural termination rate) and an underpowered triple interaction. The promised DDD is therefore present only in a limited and statistically weak form, which should be acknowledged more explicitly as a deviation from the original manifest.

**Summary**  
This paper studies whether the enormous administrative surge from the 2023–2024 Medicaid unwinding crowded out SNAP processing in states with shared eligibility systems. Using state-level SNAP participation rates from the ACS and variation in administrative integration and procedural termination rates, the author finds suggestive negative effects: integrated states display larger post-unwinding declines in SNAP participation (−0.30 pp, not statistically significant) and states with higher procedural termination rates see larger drops (−2.71 per unit, $p=0.10$). These findings are interpreted as the first causal evidence of cross-program administrative spillovers in the U.S. safety net.

**Essential Points**  
1. **Credibility of the DiD comparison.** The core identifying assumption is that SNAP participation trends would have evolved similarly in integrated and separate states absent the unwinding—after accounting for EA termination. The paper presents only a single placebo test using a fake post-period. With only 51 macro-units and pre-treatment differences (integrated states have higher SNAP rates and different economic profiles), we need stronger evidence of parallel trends, e.g., an event-study or state-specific linear trends. Without it, the DiD may simply pick up compositional time-varying shocks correlated with integration (e.g., states with integrated systems might also have different pandemic recovery patterns or SNAP outreach). Please estimate event-study coefficients or exploit additional pre-treatment periods to show parallel trends.  
2. **Measurement and data concerns.** The paper’s monthly SNAP participation series comes from annual ACS estimates interpolated to months, a decision that introduces considerable attenuation and may misalign with the timing of the Medicaid unwinding. ACS 1-year data is not designed for monthly analysis, and the paper does not justify why it was chosen over publicly available monthly USDA administrative SNAP counts (FNS, SNAP applications, benefits disbursed). Using ACS also restricts the post-treatment sample to 2023 only, which limits the ability to observe the unwinding’s dynamics and undermines power. Please justify this choice or, preferably, re-estimate using higher-frequency administrative SNAP data that directly captures monthly enrollment and feeding into the treatment timing.  
3. **Endogeneity of the continuous treatment and classification.** Procedural termination rates are outcomes of the Medicaid unwinding itself and likely reflect state administrative capacity, which may correlate with parallel trends in SNAP outside of the proposed channel. Similarly, the integrated/separate classification could proxy for broader administrative organization, state policy preferences, or funding levels. The paper needs to provide stronger evidence that procedural termination is not simultaneously driven by SNAP changes—for example, is the procedural termination rate pre-determined (e.g., from planning metrics) or only observable post-treatment? If the latter, the continuous specification risks reverse causality. Consider instrumenting procedural burden with exogenous variation (e.g., redetermination caseload relative to staff) or, at minimum, clarifying the timing of the procedural rate measure relative to SNAP outcomes.

**Suggestions**  
1. **Strengthen the main identification strategy.**  
   - Include an event-study plot (pre-post coefficients) to demonstrate parallel trends between integrated and separate states. If pre-trends exist, consider allowing for state-specific time trends or using synthetic controls for a subset of states.  
   - Expand the placebo exercises beyond the fake post-period. For instance, randomly assign “treatment” to states and check if similar point estimates arise; or run the DiD for unrelated outcomes (e.g., TANF participation) to see if “integration” predicts changes unrelated to SNAP.  
   - Explore matching or weighting approaches so integrated and separate states are more comparable on observables (economic conditions, poverty rate, political control). If integration is correlated with these factors, this can mitigate omitted-variable bias.

2. **Revisit the data choice for SNAP participation.**  
   - The ACS-derived monthly series is novel, but the large measurement error from repeating annual estimates across months raises concerns. USDA FNS publishes monthly SNAP caseloads and benefit issuance since the 1980s (e.g., Table A-1). These data would allow a richer time series (monthly from 2019 to 2024) and more precise alignment with the unwinding. If administrative data is unavailable, at least compare the ACS series to the FNS series to ensure similar trends—the appendix could show that the ACS-based rate tracks monthly administrative counts well.  
   - Consider augmenting the analysis with additional outcomes mentioned in the manifest: SNAP benefit issuance or application/approval rates from state portals, as these might be more sensitive to administrative capacity and provide alternative tests of the mechanism.  
   - If ACS must be used, acknowledge the attenuation bias more explicitly and consider aggregating to annual data to reduce noise, then implement a DiD at the annual level with more pre-treatment years and two post-treatment years (2023 and the upcoming 2024 release when available).

3. **Clarify the timing and exogeneity of the procedural termination rate.**  
   - The specification uses the cumulative procedural termination rate through December 2023 as a state-level intensity. Since this rate is observed only after the unwinding is well underway, it may itself be endogenous to the very administrative chaos that also affects SNAP. Explain whether a state’s high procedural rate is pre-determined (e.g., based on past measures of procedural churn) or whether it could be affected by SNAP disruptions (e.g., similar staff shortages).  
   - If causality is a concern, consider lagging the procedural termination measure (e.g., use the rate observed during the first quarter of the unwinding to predict later SNAP outcomes) or instrumenting it with an exogenous predictor such as the share of Medicaid enrollees requiring redeterminations relative to staffing levels prior to the unwinding (information that might be available from CMS or state reports).  
   - Alternatively, define “high procedural burden” using characteristics that were determined before April 2023—such as the ratio of Medicaid eligibility staff to medicaid enrollees or the share of Medicaid cases overdue for renewal in 2022—and interact that with the DiD. This would preserve the DDD structure hinted at in the manifest while reducing endogeneity.

4. **Assess the policy channel with auxiliary evidence.**  
   - The paper argues that SNAP losses are procedural (rather than benefit reductions) due to administrative crowding out. To bolster this claim, provide direct evidence that SNAP cases in integrated states were more likely to be closed for procedural reasons post-unwinding (if data exist for SNAP procedural denials or late recertifications).  
   - Use the Household Pulse Survey or other microdata with SNAP participation mentions to see whether the drop in SNAP participation is concentrated among households that also experienced Medicaid churn (e.g., newly disenrolled from Medicaid). Showing micro-level co-occurrence would strengthen the cross-program spillover story.  
   - Examine whether processing times or caseload backlogs spiked more in integrated states. Even state-level administrative metrics (e.g., average SNAP processing time) would make the capacity story more tangible.

5. **Address standard errors and statistical power explicitly.**  
   - With 51 clusters and a treatment affecting 24 states simultaneously, the statistical power is limited but not insurmountable. The paper should report the implied minimum detectable effect size (MDE) to contextualize the $p=0.25$ estimate. It would also be helpful to present wild-cluster bootstrap $p$-values or other inference methods suitable for small cluster counts.  
   - Consider aggregating to quarterly or yearly frequency to increase the number of post-treatment periods—this can improve power for estimating dynamic effects, even though it reduces the total number of observations; the trade-off may be worthwhile if it stabilizes the estimates.

6. **Tighten the narrative around the “dark side of integration.”**  
   - The policy implications would be clearer if the paper distinguished between short-run shocks (like the unwinding) and normal operations where integration may be beneficial. Discuss how staffing surges or temporary segregation of tasks could mitigate spillovers during future redeterminations.  
   - If integrated states also performed better during “normal” times (higher SNAP take-up), consider analyzing whether the temporary losses post-unwinding fully negate those gains or just momentarily suppress them. This would help policymakers weigh integration’s benefits against its vulnerabilities.

By addressing these points—especially strengthening the identification and data choices—the paper would offer more compelling causal evidence on an important administrative-burden channel affecting the U.S. safety net.
