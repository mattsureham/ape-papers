## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple, policy-relevant question: when England sharply cut its community police workforce—Police Community Support Officers (PCSOs), civilian neighborhood patrol staff—did crime rise? Using large cross-force variation in those cuts over 2008–2024, the paper’s answer is essentially no: it finds little evidence that removing this visible but non-enforcement police tier changed recorded crime.

A busy economist should care because the paper speaks to a broader question than “do PCSOs matter?” It asks whether the crime-reducing effect of policing comes from visible presence and community contact, or from the coercive authority of sworn officers. That is a first-order question for public-sector production, workforce design, and the economics of deterrence.

### Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Not quite. The current opening is punchy, but still too institutional and too narrow. “England cut more than half its community police officers in a decade, and nobody noticed” is memorable, but it risks sounding glib and small-bore. The introduction gets to the bigger question—whether police effectiveness comes from presence versus powers—but only after some throat-clearing.

The first two paragraphs should do three things immediately:
1. Start with the broad question: what exactly about police reduces crime?
2. Explain why PCSOs are a clean test of that question.
3. Give the headline result early: large cuts, no detectable crime response.

### The pitch the paper should have

“Economists know surprisingly little about which margin of policing reduces crime. Is it the visible presence of uniformed personnel in neighborhoods, or the enforcement powers of sworn officers? This paper studies that question using England’s large post-2010 cuts to Police Community Support Officers (PCSOs)—civilian neighborhood patrol staff who are visible on the street but lack arrest powers.

Between 2010 and 2024, English police forces cut PCSOs by roughly 60 percent, with some forces eliminating them entirely and others largely preserving them. Exploiting that variation, I find that forces that cut PCSOs more did not experience meaningfully different crime trends. The result suggests that the crime effects of policing may depend much more on enforcement capacity than on non-coercive uniformed presence alone.”

That is the AER version of the paper. Right now the paper is close to this, but not disciplined enough in putting the big question first.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### Contribution in one sentence

The paper’s contribution is to provide national-scale evidence that large reductions in civilian community-policing staff in England had little detectable effect on recorded crime, suggesting that visible police presence without enforcement powers may do far less for crime reduction than the literature on sworn officers would imply.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?

Somewhat, but not sharply enough.

The paper distinguishes itself from:
- the large literature showing sworn police reduce crime,
- community policing/procedural justice work that emphasizes trust and legitimacy,
- English austerity papers on police budgets or officer cuts.

That said, the introduction currently reads too much like “the police literature studied officers; I study PCSOs.” That is a staffing-category distinction, not yet a major intellectual distinction. To make this feel bigger, the paper has to insist that PCSOs are not just another worker type—they are a test of a mechanism: **presence without powers**.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a gap in a literature?

It is mixed, and that is a problem.

The stronger framing is the world question: **What component of policing actually deters crime?**  
The weaker framing is the literature-gap version: **No one has estimated PCSOs at scale.**

Right now the paper uses both, but leans too often on the second. “First national-scale causal estimate” is nice, but AER does not publish categories of workers because nobody got around to them. It publishes papers that resolve a substantive uncertainty about how the world works.

### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what’s new?

At present, maybe, but with some risk they would summarize it as: “It’s a DiD/TWFE paper on English community police cuts and crime, and the effect is zero.”

That is not enough. The colleague should instead say: “It uses PCSOs as a test of whether police reduce crime through visible presence/community engagement or through coercive authority, and the evidence points strongly toward the latter.”

If readers do not come away with that sentence, the paper has lost the plot.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Most of all: **reframe the paper around the production function of policing rather than the fate of PCSOs.**

Beyond framing, the contribution would become materially bigger if the paper did one of the following:

- **Shift the main outcome from total recorded crime alone to a more conceptually central set of margins.** The natural one is crime types most plausibly affected by neighborhood visibility, but that is already partly done. More powerful would be outcomes tied to the theorized channels: calls for service, clearance rates, public cooperation, reporting, anti-social behavior, fear of crime, trust, victimization surveys, neighborhood disorder.
- **Lean harder into substitution and workforce composition.** If the real question is whether forces can substitute sworn officers for civilian neighborhood staff, then the paper should be framed as evidence on the marginal product of different policing inputs.
- **Connect more directly to economic theory.** This could be Becker/deterrence versus legitimacy/information production. Right now the mechanism discussion is there, but too diffuse.
- **Use a sharper comparison:** not just “PCSOs versus nothing,” but “PCSOs versus sworn officers per budget dollar” or “community presence versus enforcement capacity.”

The biggest upgrade would be to stop treating the paper as a null on one English institution and start treating it as evidence on the **technology of policing**.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

### Closest neighbors

The closest neighbors appear to be:

1. **Levitt (1997)** on police and crime.
2. **Draca, Machin, and Witt (2011)** on police deployment and crime in London.
3. **Chalfin and McCrary (2018/2022 review)** on the police-crime relationship.
4. **Mello (2019)** on COPS hiring grants / more police.
5. **Bell, Fasani, and Machin (2016)** or related UK workforce-composition work on police inputs and crime.
6. **Deste et al. (2024)** or recent austerity/police spending papers on UK crime effects.
7. On the criminology side, **MacDonald, Fagan, and Geller (2016)** and the hot spots / foot patrol literature.
8. On procedural justice/community policing, **Tyler**, **Bradford**, and related work.

### How should the paper position itself relative to those neighbors?

Mostly **build on**, not attack.

- Relative to the sworn-police literature: “That literature identifies the effect of enforcement-capable police. I study a margin that isolates visible presence and community engagement from coercive authority.”
- Relative to community policing / procedural justice: “Those literatures emphasize trust, legitimacy, and local intelligence; I ask whether those channels show up in aggregate crime outcomes.”
- Relative to austerity papers: “Those papers show overall police resources matter; I isolate which component did not.”
- Relative to foot patrol and hot spots work: “Short-run, localized patrol interventions may work, especially with sworn officers and high-intensity deployment; my results suggest that a broad civilian neighborhood tier does not generate large force-level crime effects.”

The paper should not posture as disproving community policing writ large. That would be too aggressive and probably not credible from this design and this outcome.

### Is it currently positioned too narrowly or too broadly?

Oddly, both.

- **Too narrowly** in institutional focus: too much “PCSOs in England,” not enough “what inputs in policing matter?”
- **Too broadly** in some claims: it occasionally drifts into “challenging a foundational premise of neighborhood policing” when the actual evidence is on force-level recorded crime.

The ideal target is narrower than “community policing everywhere is wrong” but broader than “one staffing category in England seems unimportant.”

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of? What fields should it be speaking to?

The paper should speak more to:

- **Public economics / public-sector production**: what is the marginal product of different worker types inside a public agency?
- **Personnel economics / task allocation**: when should organizations use lower-powered, lower-cost workers versus high-authority professionals?
- **State capacity / organizational economics**: what components of bureaucratic capacity matter for deterrence versus legitimacy?
- **Political economy of austerity**: not just crime effects of budget cuts, but how agencies choose which tasks/workers to cut.
- **Measurement/reporting literature**: if PCSOs affect reporting, then recorded crime is a complicated welfare object.

This broader conversation would make the paper more interesting to AER readers who are not policing specialists.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?

Mostly, but it is spending too much time in the “economics of policing” silo and not enough in “how public organizations produce outcomes with different classes of workers.” The unexpected and potentially high-impact framing is: **What can civilianized state capacity accomplish without coercive authority?**

That is a bigger and better conversation.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

We know from prior economics work that more sworn police tend to reduce crime. Meanwhile, many real-world police systems invest heavily in community-facing, lower-power personnel on the theory that visibility, trust, and information also prevent crime.

### Tension

But those two ideas are not the same. Existing evidence does not tell us whether crime falls because police are present, because they can punish, or because they gather information through community relationships. England’s dramatic cuts to PCSOs create an opportunity to test whether non-enforcement community policing mattered for crime.

### Resolution

The paper finds little detectable effect of large PCSO cuts on recorded crime, overall or by offense category.

### Implications

The crime-reducing effects of policing may come mainly from enforcement authority rather than civilian neighborhood presence. For policymakers, the result shifts the workforce-composition discussion: if the goal is crime reduction, civilian community policing may be a weak margin relative to sworn officers, though it may still matter for non-crime outcomes.

### Does this paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has one, but it is not yet fully under control.

The paper does have a real story. That is a major plus. This is not a random results dump. But the story is diluted by:
- too much emphasis on econometric reassurance in the introduction,
- too many repeated “the null is robust” paragraphs before the reader has fully absorbed why the null matters,
- some overstatement in the discussion and conclusion.

The paper should be telling one clean story:
> “PCSOs are a policy-relevant test of whether policing works through visible community presence rather than enforcement powers. England cut them massively. Crime did not move much. Therefore, at least on crime, presence-without-powers appears to have limited effect.”

That is the story. Everything else should serve it.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?

“England cut its civilian neighborhood police workforce by roughly 60 percent over a decade, with some forces eliminating it entirely—and recorded crime doesn’t seem to have responded much.”

That is a good opening fact. People will lean in.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

Lean in, initially. The combination of a huge policy change and a null effect is intrinsically interesting.

But the second sentence matters. If the speaker says only, “So PCSOs don’t matter,” people may lose interest or object. If instead the speaker says, “It looks like visible police presence without arrest power contributes much less to crime reduction than the sworn-officer literature would suggest,” that gets attention.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Probably one of these:
1. “Maybe PCSOs affect trust/reporting/fear, not crime?”
2. “Maybe force-level data are too aggregated and the effects are hyper-local?”
3. “Were PCSOs replaced by sworn officers?”
4. “So is this about community policing failing, or just this specific institutional form?”

Those are exactly the questions the paper should anticipate and use to sharpen its contribution. Importantly, they are not fatal questions—they are evidence that the topic is interesting.

### If the findings are null or modest: is the null itself interesting?

Yes, potentially quite interesting. But the paper needs to earn the null.

At present, it partly does. The paper tries to show the estimate is precise enough to rule out large crime effects, which is the right instinct. But the current manuscript still reads a little too defensively, as if trying to prove it did not simply fail to find something.

The right framing is:
- England’s cuts were large and policy-relevant.
- PCSOs are an economically meaningful input.
- The design rules out large aggregate crime effects.
- That is informative because many policy arguments for community policing implicitly claim such effects.

The null is interesting if framed as **disciplining inflated claims about what community-facing civilian police can do for crime**. It is less interesting if framed as “we estimated zero and here are 12 ways zero remains zero.”

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

### What would make it read better?

A lot of improvement is editorial and structural, not substantive.

#### 1. Shorten the introduction by 25–35%.
It currently front-loads too many details: specific coefficients, p-values, inference variants, and lengthy literature signposting. The introduction should give:
- the question,
- why it matters,
- the institutional setting,
- the core finding,
- the big implication.

Most of the “wild cluster bootstrap / RI / jackknife” material belongs later.

#### 2. Move much of the “threats to validity” prose out of the main text or shorten heavily.
This is referee-facing prose, not reader-facing prose. For editorial positioning, it clogs the narrative. AER introductions and early sections work best when they prioritize the idea over a preemptive defense brief.

#### 3. Bring the conceptual comparison to sworn officers earlier.
The single best line in the paper is not that PCSOs fell 60 percent; it is that PCSOs let you test **presence versus powers**. That should appear on page 1, not later.

#### 4. Streamline institutional background.
The background is competent but too long. There are four subsections, and some material is repetitive. The reader does not need a mini-report on English police administration. Keep:
- what PCSOs are,
- why they are different from officers,
- how austerity generated variation,
- what mechanisms they might affect.

Cut the rest or move it to appendix/background footnotes.

#### 5. Put the main result earlier and more starkly.
The paper does this somewhat, but it can be sharper:
- one headline coefficient,
- one economic translation,
- one sentence on what effect sizes are ruled out.

Do not make the reader wait through multiple specifications before the punchline lands.

#### 6. The decomposition by crime type is probably more central than some of the inferential machinery.
If the theory is about visible patrol, disorder, local information, etc., then offense-specific results are substantively important. Those should remain in the main text. By contrast, some inferential material can be compressed.

#### 7. Conclusion should do less summary and more interpretation.
The current conclusion repeats the findings, then gets somewhat provocative. Better would be a shorter conclusion with three crisp takeaways:
- what we learned about policing inputs,
- what we did not learn,
- what policy margin this speaks to.

### Is the paper front-loaded with the good stuff?

Partly. The headline null appears early, which is good. But the best **conceptual** stuff is not front-loaded enough. The paper front-loads methods reassurance more than intellectual stakes.

### Are there results buried in robustness that should be in main results?

Not really; if anything, there is too much robustness in the main narrative. The crime-type heterogeneity is important and belongs in the main text. The bootstrap/RI/jackknife could be summarized more tersely.

### Is the conclusion adding value or just summarizing?

Some value, but also some overreach. The line about “the deterrence value of police may reside not in their presence but in their power” is strong and memorable; it should survive. But claims that the paper challenges a “foundational premise” of neighborhood policing are a bit much given the outcome and level of aggregation.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

### What is the gap between current form and a paper that would excite the top 10 people in this field?

Primarily, this is a **framing and ambition** problem, with some scope concerns.

#### Framing problem
The science may be perfectly competent, but the current manuscript too often presents itself as:
- an estimate of one staffing category in England,
- a careful null result,
- an addition to the policing literature.

That is not enough for AER. The paper needs to be unmistakably about a bigger question:
**Which dimensions of state presence produce deterrence?**  
Or more concretely:  
**How much of the output of policing comes from coercive authority versus community-facing visibility?**

#### Scope problem
The paper is vulnerable to the response: “Maybe PCSOs matter for everything except recorded crime.” That does not kill the paper, but it limits its reach. In current form, the paper likely belongs more comfortably in a strong field journal unless the framing is elevated and the implications are disciplined.

#### Novelty problem
The null itself is novel enough if properly framed, but the empirical template is familiar. Without a sharp conceptual positioning, readers may indeed say “another DiD/TWFE policing paper.”

#### Ambition problem
The manuscript is competent but a bit safe in the wrong places and over-bold in the wrong places. Safe: lots of inferential reassurance. Over-bold: sweeping claims about community policing. The ambitious version would do the opposite—be bolder about the central question and more disciplined about the claim.

### Single most impactful advice

**Rewrite the paper around the question “Does policing reduce crime through presence or through powers?” and treat PCSOs as the empirical test of that question, not as the object of interest in themselves.**

That one change would materially improve the paper’s odds. If the authors do only one thing, it should be that.
