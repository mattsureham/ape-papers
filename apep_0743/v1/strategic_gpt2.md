## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether a particularly stark form of occupational licensing regulation—state laws that require families to hire a licensed funeral director in order to handle basic post-death arrangements—actually restricts supply in local death-care markets. Using county pairs on either side of state borders, the paper finds little evidence that these mandates reduce funeral-home density, employment, or other observable measures of market structure, suggesting that this regulation may not operate mainly through entry restriction.

A busy economist should care because the paper speaks to a broad and important claim in economics and policy: that occupational licensing raises prices by choking off supply. If even this emotionally charged, highly regulated service industry does not show the predicted market-structure distortions, that is potentially informative about when licensing bites and when it does not.

**Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Reasonably, but not optimally. The current opening is competent and vivid, but it gets pulled too quickly into “licensing theory predicts X” and the industry details before landing the broader economic question. The better pitch is not “here is another setting where licensing might matter”; it is “here is an unusually clean and substantively important test of whether licensing works through the canonical supply-restriction channel.”

**What the first two paragraphs should say instead:**

> Occupational licensing is often criticized for raising prices and protecting incumbents by restricting entry. But direct evidence on the mechanism is thin: in many settings, we do not know whether licensing actually changes local market structure or instead affects prices and consumer choice through other channels.  
>  
> This paper studies one of the clearest and most consequential licensing mandates in the U.S. economy: in nine states, families cannot legally complete basic death-care arrangements without hiring a licensed funeral director. Comparing adjacent counties on either side of mandate and non-mandate state borders, I find no meaningful discontinuity in funeral-home entry, employment, firm size, or payroll. The main implication is that even a highly salient licensing rule in a captive local market may fail to generate the standard entry-barrier patterns that dominate the licensing debate.

That version leads with the general question, tells the reader why this setting is unusually revealing, and states the punchline faster.

Mixed, but too often as the latter. The stronger framing is about the world: **Do licensing mandates actually alter supply in local service markets, or are economists and policy advocates overgeneralizing from price effects to entry effects?** The current paper sometimes gets there, but it falls back into “first causal estimate for death care.”

**Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what's new?**  
Yes, but probably as: “It’s a border design on funeral-director mandates and they find null effects on market structure.” That is intelligible, but it still risks sounding like “another DiD/border null paper in a quirky setting.” The introduction does not yet arm the reader with a larger claim.

**What would make this contribution bigger?**  
Most importantly, the paper needs to shift from “licensing in funeral homes” to “a test of the mechanism behind licensing effects.” Specific ways to enlarge it:

1. **Center the mechanism question.**  
   The main contribution should be: many arguments for licensing reform presume supply restriction; this paper tests that mechanism directly and finds little support in a setting where it should be easiest to detect.

2. **Bring in consumer-choice outcomes if possible.**  
   The paper itself repeatedly says the real action may be in consumer choice, especially home funerals/direct cremation. If the authors can show effects on cremation take-up, family-directed burial activity, or the presence of nontraditional providers, the paper becomes much more consequential. 3. **Connect more directly to prices or pass-through.**

Right now the story is “no market-structure distortion, maybe prices still rise.” That is interesting but incomplete. A bigger paper would say whether mandates distort prices without changing supply, or whether they do little at all.

4. **Sharpen the comparative claim.**

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### What is the setup?
Occupational licensing is widespread, politically contested, and usually justified or criticized in terms of prices, quality, and market access. Funeral director mandates are a stark and emotionally salient form of licensing in a local service market where one might strongly expect supply restriction.

### What is the tension?
Critics claim these laws are anti-competitive and burden grieving families, but we do not actually know whether they visibly reshape local market structure. If licensing is really functioning as an entry barrier, this should show up in fewer firms, larger incumbents, or other observable border discontinuities.

### What is the resolution?
The paper finds no large discontinuities in funeral-home density, employment, average firm size, or payroll at mandate borders.

### What are the implications?
The implication is not simply “funeral regulation is harmless.” It is that the canonical mechanism in the licensing debate—supply restriction—may not be the relevant margin here. Any welfare costs may instead operate through prices, choice sets, or the suppression of nontraditional forms of care.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
**Serviceable, but not strong.**  
The ingredients are all there, but the paper often reads like a competent empirical note with multiple plausible interpretations attached after the fact. The biggest weakness is that the tension and implication are not fully aligned with the evidence. The paper says “the debate is about anti-competitive effects,” but then measures only a subset of those effects. That creates narrative slippage.

### If it is a collection of results looking for a story, what story should it tell?
The story should be:

1. **Canonical claim:** licensing raises prices by restricting supply.
2. **Clean test:** funeral-director mandates are a strong setting to test that mechanism.
3. **Result:** no detectable supply-side distortion in observed market structure.
4. **Implication:** policy arguments against these mandates should not presume the mechanism; the welfare debate is more likely about consumer autonomy and nontraditional alternatives than standard incumbent protection.

That is a coherent story. The current paper gets close but has not fully committed to it.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Almost certainly: **“Okay, but do these mandates raise prices or reduce consumer choice?”**

And that is exactly the problem. The paper’s main result naturally invites a more important question that the paper can currently only speculate about. That does not make the paper uninteresting, but it does cap the excitement.

### If the findings are null or modest: is the null itself interesting?
Yes, but only if framed correctly. The null is interesting because:

- the regulation is unusually stark;
- the market is local and demand is captive;
- this is where a supply-distortion story should be easiest to see.

The paper does make part of that case. But it needs to sharpen why this is an informative null rather than a failure to find something. The authors should explicitly state: **this is a strong-likelihood setting for the canonical mechanism, so absence of evidence here meaningfully updates prior beliefs.**

At the same time, the paper should be more disciplined about what the null does **not** show. It does not show no welfare effects, no price effects, or no anti-competitive behavior in a broader sense.
