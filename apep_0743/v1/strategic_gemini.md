## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
This paper asks whether a specific, heavy-handed occupational regulation—the legal mandate to hire a funeral director for all body dispositions—distorts market structure. By exploiting state-border discontinuities, the author finds a precisely estimated null: these mandates do not significantly change the density of funeral homes, employment, or payroll. It challenges the standard "licensing-as-entry-barrier" narrative in a multi-billion dollar industry that touches every household.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this well in the first two paragraphs. However, it leads with the institutional "what" rather than the economic "why."
**Revised Pitch:** *Occupational licensing is often framed as a classic entry barrier that restricts supply to inflate rents. This paper tests this theory in the death care industry, where nine states mandate the use of a professional funeral director—a "licensing tax" on grieving families. Using a border-discontinuity design, I find that these mandates have no detectable impact on market density or firm size, suggesting that either the regulation is non-binding for most consumers or that the market has reached a long-run equilibrium where traditional barriers to entry are irrelevant.*

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**Contribution:** The paper provides the first causal, border-discontinuity evidence that mandatory professional involvement in the funeral industry does not alter the intensive or extensive margins of market supply.

- **Differentiation:** It moves beyond the "licensing vs. no licensing" debate (Kleiner) to "mandated use vs. optional use." It is cleaner than Harrington & Krynski (2002) because it uses a border-pair FE rather than state-level variation.
- **Question vs. Literature:** It currently sits between the two. It is framed as a test of "theory," but it’s most powerful when framed as a question about whether "Consumer Protection" laws actually protect incumbents.
- **Bigger Contribution:** To make this an AER-level contribution, the author needs to bridge the gap between "Market Structure" and "Welfare." If the number of firms doesn't change, but prices (anecdotally) do, where is the money going? A focus on **consumer price data** (even if self-collected from General Price Lists) would turn a "null result paper" into a "rent-seeking paper."

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
- **Closest Neighbors:** Kleiner & Soltas (2023) on labor fluidity; Chevalier & Scott Morton (2008) on caskets; Dube et al. (2010) on border designs.
- **Positioning:** The paper is currently too humble. It should "Attack" the industry's self-serving narrative that these regulations are necessary for public health, and "Synthesize" the licensing literature by suggesting that mandates might only matter for *niche* entrants rather than the "median" firm.
- **Missing Conversations:** The paper should speak more to the **Industrial Organization of "Captive" Markets.** This is an industry with perfectly inelastic demand (everyone dies) and high search costs (grief). The lack of market response to regulation is a profound IO puzzle that goes beyond simple licensing.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC
- **Setup:** A $23B industry with unique "mandatory" regulations in 9 states.
- **Tension:** Consumer advocates scream "monopoly," but standard theory suggests we should see entry barriers in the data.
- **Resolution:** We don't. The "barrier" is invisible in establishment data.
- **Implications:** Regulation might be "symbolic" or "rent-extracting" through price rather than quantity.

**Evaluation:** The arc is "serviceable" but lacks a "Resolution" that satisfies an economist's curiosity. A null result needs a "smoking gun" explanation. Is it because the 41 "free" states have culturally "captured" consumers who hire directors anyway?

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
- **The Fact:** "In states where you are *forced* by law to hire a funeral home, there aren't actually any more funeral homes than in the states where you can do it yourself."
- **Reaction:** Initial interest, followed by: "Wait, so are the funeral homes just richer in those states?"
- **The Follow-up:** "If the quantity didn't change, did the price?" This is the paper's Achilles' heel. Without price data, the "So What?" feels incomplete.

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
- **Move to Appendix:** The heterogeneity by individual border segment (Table 4) is noisy and distracting. It suggests the "null" might actually be a series of offsetting local shocks.
- **Expand:** Section 6 (Discussion) is the most interesting part. It needs to be more than 3 paragraphs. This is where the AER-level "thinking" happens.
- **Front-load:** Move the "Minimum Detectable Effect" logic earlier. In a null-result paper, the power calculation *is* the result.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The gap is **Ambition.** Right now, it’s a very competent "Note" on a specific regulation. 

**Single Biggest Improvement:** The author needs to collect or scrape **Price Data** (General Price Lists/GPLs) for a subset of these border counties. If you can show that mandates don't change the number of firms but *do* change the price of a "Direct Cremation," you have a definitive paper on how incumbents use the law to extract rents without needing to restrict entry.
