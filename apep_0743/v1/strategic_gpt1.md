## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether laws in nine U.S. states that require families to use a licensed funeral director actually restrict competition in local death-care markets. Using county pairs straddling state borders, it finds little evidence that these mandates reduce funeral-home entry, employment, or other establishment-level measures of market structure, suggesting that this form of licensing may not operate through the standard entry-barrier channel.

A busy economist should care because occupational licensing is often justified and criticized in sweeping terms, yet we still know too little about which specific regulations actually distort markets and through what margins. A clean “important regulation, surprisingly little market-structure effect” result could matter—if framed as a broader lesson about licensing, not just a quirky paper about funerals.

### Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Reasonably, but not sharply enough. The current introduction leads with institutional detail and then quickly drops into “licensing theory predicts X.” It does not fully cash out why this particular setting is an informative test of a broader economic question. Right now the paper sounds like: “here is an unusual regulation; I run a border design; I get a null.” For AER-level positioning, it needs to sound like: “here is a strong test of a central claim in the licensing debate, in a setting where the regulation is unusually direct and the economic margin unusually clear.”

### The pitch the paper should have

“Occupational licensing is widely argued to raise prices by restricting entry, but evidence on the mechanism remains thin because most regulations are bundled, gradual, and hard to interpret. This paper studies one of the starkest licensing mandates in the U.S.—state laws that require families to use a licensed funeral director even for the most basic disposition tasks—and asks whether that mandate changes local market structure at state borders. Despite the regulation’s apparent bite, I find no meaningful discontinuity in funeral-home density, employment, or firm size, suggesting that some licensing rules may affect consumers through channels other than incumbent protection and entry restriction.”

That is the version that belongs in a top-field conversation.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### Contribution in one sentence

The paper’s contribution is to show that a highly salient and seemingly restrictive occupational licensing mandate in the funeral industry does not generate detectable border discontinuities in local provider market structure.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?

Only partially. The paper cites the occupational licensing literature and a few funeral-industry papers, but the differentiation is still too mechanical: “first causal estimate in death care,” “uses border discontinuity,” “studies this specific mandate rather than other regulations.” That is not yet enough. “First in this niche” is rarely a compelling top-journal contribution by itself.

The stronger differentiation is conceptual and should be stated more explicitly:

- most licensing papers document average wage/price effects, not mechanism;
- most cannot isolate a regulation that directly forces consumers into the licensed sector;
- this setting is unusually well suited to test the core entry-restriction story because the law literally prevents legal bypass of the profession.

If the paper says that cleanly, it stops sounding like “another DiD/border paper about a weird industry.”

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or as filling a gap in a LITERATURE?

Mostly literature-gap framed, with some world framing. It should lean much harder into the world question:

**When governments legally require households to transact with a licensed professional, does market structure actually change?**

That is much stronger than:
**There is no causal estimate yet for funeral director mandates.**

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the intro?

At present, they would probably say: “It’s a border-discontinuity paper on funeral-director mandates and it finds no effect on establishment outcomes.” That is understandable, but not memorable. The paper needs the reader to say instead:

> “It’s a paper showing that even a very direct, mandatory-use licensing rule may not distort market structure in the classic way, so the usual licensing story may be incomplete.”

That is a contribution; the current draft is too close to a method-and-setting summary.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Most importantly: **move from market structure alone to economic incidence or consumer margins.** The paper repeatedly admits that price effects could still exist and that the real welfare issue may be consumer choice. That is exactly the problem strategically: it means the paper is testing only one margin of a broader claim.

Specific ways to make it bigger:

1. **Outcome expansion toward prices or consumer expenditure**
   - Even imperfect local price data, direct cremation prices, itemized funeral charges, or FTC Funeral Rule disclosures would matter enormously.
   - If the central result is “no entry effect, but price effect remains,” that becomes a much richer paper.

2. **Consumer-choice outcomes**
   - Cremation share, home funeral incidence, direct disposition use, or timing/logistics choices.
   - The paper hints that the main effect may be on niche alternatives; if so, measure that margin.

3. **Mechanism by alternative-provider suppression**
   - If traditional funeral homes are unaffected but nontraditional providers are missing, that is substantively interesting.
   - Right now this is speculation in the discussion.

4. **Broader framing**
   - Recast from “funeral regulation” to “mandatory intermediation by licensed professionals.”
   - The funeral case then becomes an especially stark test case, not merely the object of study.

The current paper is competent but narrow because its main measured outcomes are one step removed from the welfare and mechanism claims it wants to speak to.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Occupational licensing is pervasive and often criticized as anti-competitive. Funeral director mandates are a particularly stark version: in some states, families cannot legally handle basic death-related tasks without hiring a licensed professional.

### Tension

If licensing works by restricting entry and protecting incumbents, this is exactly the kind of rule where we should see local market distortions. Yet it is also possible that the law is largely inframarginal because most families would hire a funeral director anyway, or that effects show up in price/choice rather than provider counts.

### Resolution

At mandate/non-mandate borders, the paper finds no meaningful discontinuity in establishment density, employment, firm size, or payroll per worker.

### Implications

The standard anti-competitive story for licensing may not travel cleanly to all settings; in this context, if the regulation is harmful, the harm likely operates through consumer choice, niche alternatives, or prices rather than incumbent supply restriction.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

Serviceable, but not yet strong. It currently has the bones of a narrative, but it also reads like a collection of sensible empirical exercises around a null. The discussion section actually contains the most interesting story—non-binding mandates, alternative-provider suppression, consumer choice—but that story arrives too late and too tentatively.

### What story should it be telling?

Not “I studied funeral laws and found no effect.”

It should be:

1. Licensing critics often claim entry restriction.
2. This setting offers a strong test because the law literally compels use of the licensed occupation.
3. Yet market structure does not change.
4. Therefore, the economics of licensing here are not about standard firm-count distortion; they are about whether regulation constrains alternative modes of provision or acts through consumer-side frictions.

That is a coherent arc. Right now the paper presents the null before fully teaching the reader why the null is surprising in a deep way.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?

“I looked at states where families are legally required to hire a funeral director, and despite that stark mandate, funeral-home density and employment look basically the same right across the border.”

That is the best fact in the paper.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

They would lean in at first because the setting is vivid and slightly macabre. Then they would quickly ask whether the paper has prices or consumer outcomes. If the answer is no, attention will fade. The setting gets you 30 seconds of interest; the broader mechanism and welfare margin are what keep the room.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Almost certainly:
- “Okay, but do prices differ?”
- Or: “Does it kill off home funerals / direct cremation providers?”
- Or: “If there’s no supply effect, what margin is the regulation biting on?”

That is revealing. The most natural economist reaction is not about your current outcomes; it is about the outcomes you do not have.

### Is the null itself interesting?

Yes, but only conditionally. A null can be interesting when:
1. theory strongly predicts otherwise,
2. the test is sharp,
3. the confidence intervals rule out important magnitudes,
4. the null changes how we think about the mechanism.

The paper gets part of the way there, but weakens itself by candidly admitting that it cannot rule out moderate effects and that the main welfare channels may be elsewhere. That honesty is good science, but strategically it means the paper has not yet earned a large-importance null. Right now it is closer to “useful, careful, but partial null.”

To make the null feel important, the paper must really sell:
- why this is an especially strong test of the canonical mechanism, and
- why failing to find market-structure effects is itself a meaningful update.
