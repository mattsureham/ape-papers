### 1. Idea Fidelity

The paper pursues the core identification strategy outlined in the manifest—exploiting state-border discontinuities in mandatory funeral director laws—but deviates significantly from the proposed data construction. The manifest emphasized a **25-year panel (1998–2022)** with "stacked regression" exploiting temporal variation alongside border-pair fixed effects. The delivered paper instead **averages 2017–2022 into a cross-section**, sacrificing the panel dimension entirely. This is a material departure: the manifest promised temporal variation to bolster credibility ("stable for decades, eliminating concerns about reverse causality"), while the paper relies solely on cross-sectional contrasts. The paper also expands from the manifest’s 22 border segments to 26, which is minor, but omits analysis of the price data (\$2,600 vs. \$2,100 cremation premiums) that featured prominently in the original research design. The shift from a dynamic event-study approach to a static border discontinuity is not justified, and the severe truncation of the time series (5 years vs. 25) substantially weakens the empirical foundation.

### 2. Summary

This paper employs a border-discontinuity design to test whether mandatory funeral director laws distort local death care markets. Comparing 800 adjacent county pairs across 26 state borders separating mandate from non-mandate states, the author finds precisely estimated null effects on funeral home density, employment, firm size, and payroll per worker. The estimates rule out market structure effects larger than 43% of the mean, challenging the hypothesis that these regulations function as classical entry barriers.
