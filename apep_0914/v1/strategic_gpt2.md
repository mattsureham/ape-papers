## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a sharp historical question: when Black farm workers were pushed out of the cotton South in the 1930s, did that displacement scar them economically, or did it help propel them into better occupations through migration? Using linked census records that follow the same workers from 1930 to 1950, the paper argues that Black workers from more cotton-dependent counties experienced faster occupational upgrading, especially in the 1930s and especially when they migrated.

A busy economist should care because this is potentially a reversal of a canonical story: a policy/environmental shock usually understood as harmful to a deeply disadvantaged population may have accelerated exit from a trapped low-status equilibrium. If true, that speaks to migration, structural transformation, racial convergence, and the economics of displacement more broadly.

### Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Not quite. The opening is vivid, but the paper gets too quickly tangled in the proxy/treatment apparatus—cotton dependence, agricultural intensity, AAA exposure, mechanization, displacement, migration—all before the reader knows the core claim. The first two paragraphs should do less scene-setting about specifications and more conceptual work: what is the old view, what is the new claim, and why does it matter beyond this episode?

### The pitch the paper should have

A stronger opening would say something like:

> The displacement of Black sharecroppers from the cotton South is usually told as a story of economic harm: New Deal acreage restrictions and subsequent mechanization broke an exploitative but stable labor arrangement and deepened Black insecurity. But an alternative possibility is that destroying the bottom rung of the Southern agricultural labor market accelerated movement into better jobs elsewhere, especially through the Great Migration.
>
> This paper asks which of these views better describes what happened to the workers themselves. Using linked census records that follow more than two million Southern farm workers from 1930 to 1950, I show that Black workers from more cotton-dependent counties experienced faster occupational upgrading relative to whites, with gains concentrated in the 1930s and strongly associated with migration. The broader claim is not just about the AAA; it is that displacement from a highly segmented labor market can increase mobility when exit options exist.

That is the AER version of the story: not “here is my DDD,” but “here is a surprising fact about structural change and racial mobility.”

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### Contribution in one sentence

The paper’s contribution is to argue, using linked individual census data, that Black farm workers originating in more cotton-dependent Southern counties experienced faster occupational convergence rather than lasting scarring, apparently because displacement accelerated exit from low-status agricultural work through migration.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?

Only partially. The introduction gestures at aggregate Great Migration papers, AAA displacement papers, and occupational convergence papers, but the novelty remains blurry because the paper oscillates between three claims:

1. a Great Migration paper,
2. an AAA/New Deal displacement paper,
3. a broader paper about “displacement as liberation.”

Right now it wants all three, and that dilutes differentiation. A smart reader may say: “So this is a linked-census DiD about Black mobility in the cotton South.” That undersells the paper, but the paper itself invites that reaction.

The real differentiator is not just the design or the data. It is the conceptual inversion:
- the standard view: displacement harmed Black farm workers;
- this paper: for many, displacement accelerated occupational upgrading via exit.

That contrast needs to be made cleaner and more forcefully.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a gap in a literature?

It starts with a world question, which is good. But it quickly slides into literature-gap language: “first individual-level causal estimates,” “prior work uses aggregates,” etc. That is second-order. AER introductions should lead with the world question and only then explain why existing evidence could not answer it.

The stronger frame is:
- What happens when a trapped labor market breaks apart?
- Do disadvantaged workers get scarred, or do they move up by moving out?

That is a world question with broad relevance.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the intro?

Not crisply enough. They would probably say:
> “It’s a linked-census paper showing Black farm workers in highly agricultural counties had slightly better occupational trajectories, maybe because they migrated.”

That is not yet memorable. What they should be able to say is:
> “It shows that one of the classic shocks thought to hurt Black Southern farm labor may actually have sped up occupational convergence by forcing exit from the bottom of the labor market.”

That is much better.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Several possibilities, but one is especially important:

- **Reframe the outcome around economic transformation, not just occupational score.** Occupational score is serviceable, but not inherently headline-worthy. If the paper can show movement out of farm labor, into urban/nonfarm sectors, into Northern labor markets, or into specific occupational ladders, the story becomes much more concrete and consequential.
- **Lean into heterogeneity that speaks to theory.** Where should “displacement as liberation” be strongest? Counties with strong migration networks? Places with worse initial racial stratification? Workers at the very bottom of the occupational distribution? Those comparisons would make the contribution feel like a claim about mechanisms in the world rather than a coefficient.
- **Clarify whether this is about cotton/AAA specifically or about structural transformation more generally.** Right now the paper tries to have both. Making this choice would enlarge the contribution.
- **Exploit the mover/stayer framing more effectively.** The most naturally interesting comparison in the paper is not the DDD coefficient; it is that migrants from high-cotton counties moved much farther up the occupational ladder. That should be central.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

### Closest neighbors

The nearest literatures and likely papers include:

1. **Great Migration / Black migration and labor-market outcomes**
   - Collins and Wanamaker / Collins on African American migration and economic progress
   - Boustan’s work on Black migration and labor market competition
   - Stuart and coauthors on migration networks

2. **Southern agricultural change / mechanization / displacement**
   - Alston and Ferrie on paternalism and Southern labor institutions
   - Whatley on agricultural labor and mechanization
   - Hornbeck and Naidu-type work on agricultural shocks and migration/reallocation
   - Lee on AAA and Black tenants

3. **Occupational upgrading / racial convergence / structural transformation**
   - Goldin on occupational change
   - Donohue and Heckman-era work on Black economic progress
   - More recent work on barriers, mobility, and reallocation

### How should the paper position itself relative to them?

Mostly **build on** and partially **revise**.

- Build on the Great Migration literature by saying: aggregate migration flows are well understood, but we know less about the medium-run occupational trajectories of the actual workers displaced from the rural South.
- Revise the AAA displacement literature by arguing: the short-run harm narrative may miss the medium-run reallocation margin.
- Connect to structural transformation and labor-market segmentation: exit from a trapped sector can improve mobility for marginalized workers.

It should **not** “attack” the prior literature too aggressively. The current language occasionally overstates the reversal—“contrary to the conventional narrative” is fine, but “net effect was positive” is too broad for the evidence and too likely to provoke resistance. Better to say the paper changes the margin under discussion: prior work emphasized contemporaneous harm; this paper highlights medium-run occupational reallocation.

### Is the paper currently positioned too narrowly or too broadly?

Paradoxically, both.

- **Too narrowly** in the empirical implementation: it spends too much time on cotton-belt counties, farm share, linked male farm workers, and exact specifications.
- **Too broadly** in its interpretive ambition: it wants to speak to AAA, mechanization, migration, occupational convergence, and displacement theory all at once.

The better strategy is to choose one central conversation and make the others supporting literatures.

My recommendation: position it primarily as a **Great Migration / structural transformation / racial mobility** paper, with the AAA/cotton episode as the historical shock that sharpens the test.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?

It should engage more explicitly with:
- broader work on **reallocation after sectoral decline**;
- labor economics on **displacement effects and worker upgrading/downgrading**;
- economic history on **Black urbanization and occupational sorting**, not just migration volumes;
- possibly development-style work on **push shocks, migration, and mobility**.

There is a potentially richer conversation here with papers on how negative local shocks can generate positive long-run individual outcomes when mobility constraints are relaxed.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?

Not yet fully. The most impactful conversation is not “does farm share proxy AAA exposure?” It is:

> When does destruction of a low-wage, exploitative employment system create upward mobility for workers trapped inside it?

That is a much bigger and more modern conversation. It connects economic history to labor, development, and inequality.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Before this paper, the default understanding is that Black Southern farm workers were trapped at the bottom of a highly stratified labor market, and that policies/shocks that displaced them from tenancy and sharecropping likely made them worse off, at least initially.

### Tension

The puzzle is that the same shock that destroyed a source of subsistence may also have broken a binding constraint. If the bottom rung disappears and migration opportunities exist, do workers suffer lasting damage, or do they move into better matches?

### Resolution

The paper’s main answer is that Black workers from more cotton-dependent counties experienced faster relative occupational improvement, with effects concentrated in the 1930s and associated with migration.

### Implications

The implication is that displacement in a segmented labor market need not only scar; it can induce upward reallocation. That matters for how we interpret the Great Migration, the legacy of Southern agricultural transformation, and more generally the economics of forced exit from bad jobs.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the ingredients, but not the discipline. Right now it is partly a story and partly a running regression commentary. The arc gets muddied by two unresolved tensions:

1. **Is the protagonist the AAA, cotton dependence, or migration?**
   The paper keeps switching.
2. **Is the claim farm-worker-specific displacement, or county-level Black mobility in transforming local economies?**
   The placebo discussion suggests the latter may actually be the stronger story, but the paper still narrates the former.

That is a major strategic issue. The paper cannot be both “AAA displaced farm workers and that helped them” and “the same pattern appears for nonfarm workers because county-level spillovers mattered” without choosing which story is primary.

### What story should it be telling?

The cleanest story is:

> Cotton-dependent local economies were the places where the old Southern racial labor order broke down most sharply. Black workers from those places experienced faster occupational upgrading, largely through migration and exit from farm work. The contribution is to show that local dependence on cotton agriculture predicted not just out-migration, but medium-run occupational convergence among the workers who started at the bottom.

That story can accommodate both farm and nonfarm evidence. It moves away from an overly literal AAA story and toward a stronger structural transformation story.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?

I would lead with this:

> Among Black Southern farm workers, those from the most cotton-dependent counties moved up the occupational ladder faster than comparable whites, not slower—and the difference is concentrated in the decade when agricultural displacement and migration surged.

Even better, in oral form:

> The workers we usually think of as being devastated by the collapse of cotton tenancy appear, in medium run occupational terms, to have been pushed into better jobs rather than trapped in worse ones.

That gets attention.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

They would lean in at first, because the claim cuts against a familiar narrative. But they will lean back quickly if the paper cannot say exactly what the object is:
- Is this an AAA paper?
- a cotton dependence paper?
- a migration paper?
- a general equilibrium county-transformation paper?

Right now the paper earns interest, then squanders some of it by being strategically imprecise.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Probably:
> “Is this really about the workers who were displaced from farming, or is it a broader county-level migration/network story?”

That is the right question, and the paper needs a cleaner answer. Right now its own evidence points toward the broader county-level story.

### If the findings are modest, is that a problem?

The magnitude is not huge in the headline coefficient, which means the paper must sell **conceptual importance**, not coefficient size. The author partly does that, but then undercuts it by emphasizing statistical detail and standardized effect sizes. No AER reader cares that the main estimate is 0.01 SD in the abstract sense. They care whether the paper changes how we think about Black mobility, the Great Migration, or displacement.

So yes, modest magnitudes are fine **if** the paper confidently argues that the important result is a sign reversal of the conventional narrative, not a large treatment effect.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The gap is mainly a **framing-plus-ambition problem**, with some **scope** issues.

### Framing problem

The science may be competent, but the story is not yet strategically optimized. The paper still reads too much like a historical reduced-form exercise and not enough like a paper with a broadly important idea.

### Scope problem

The current manuscript is too narrow in outcome space and too uncertain about mechanism. Occupational score alone may be enough for a field journal if the story is very clean; for AER, the paper likely needs either:
- richer evidence on sectoral exit / migration destination / occupational ladder transitions, or
- a stronger conceptual framework tying the historical episode to a general question about displacement and mobility.

### Novelty problem

The question is not wholly new, and the empirical style is familiar. Therefore the paper cannot win on technique alone. It has to win on a sharp reversal, a memorable conceptual claim, and cleaner differentiation from adjacent Great Migration papers.

### Ambition problem

The paper is currently competent but safe. The most ambitious idea in it—negative shocks can improve outcomes for trapped workers by breaking immobile, exploitative labor arrangements—is present but not fully developed. That is the part that could interest the top people in the field.

### Single most impactful advice

**Choose one paper: either an AAA-specific displacement paper or a broader paper about how the collapse of the cotton labor regime accelerated Black occupational mobility through migration—and if you want AER, choose the broader second framing and reorganize the entire paper around that question.**
