## 1. Idea Fidelity

The paper broadly pursues the original manifest’s central question: whether loss of SNAP-authorized retailers reduces SNAP participation through a supply-side access channel. It uses the intended core data sources—ACS tract-level SNAP receipt and the USDA SNAP retailer historical file—and it implements the two proposed classes of instruments: corporate chain shocks and pre-2018 small-format exposure interacted with the post-2018 period.

That said, several key elements of the manifest are either missing or materially altered. First, the manifest proposed a two-instrument LIML design with an overidentification test as a central validation step; the paper instead estimates 2SLS-style IV and does not report any overidentification statistics. Second, the secondary outcomes in the manifest (food insecurity, vehicle access, etc.) are not meaningfully developed beyond heterogeneity/placebo exercises. Third, the empirical design in the paper leans overwhelmingly on the stocking-rule instrument in the tract-FE specification, whereas the manifest framed the contribution as resting on triangulation across distinct shocks. So the paper is faithful to the idea at a high level, but it misses some of the most important validation pieces that were supposed to make the design persuasive.

## 2. Summary

This paper asks an important and potentially novel question: whether SNAP participation depends not only on eligibility and administrative frictions, but also on the local availability of authorized redemption outlets. Using tract-level panel data and instruments based on chain closures and the 2018 stocking-rule shock, the paper concludes that losing one SNAP retailer substantially reduces tract SNAP receipt, especially in low-vehicle-access and rural places.

The topic is policy-relevant and the “redemption desert” framing is appealing. However, in its current form the paper does not convincingly identify a causal effect of retailer loss on SNAP take-up, because the outcome measurement, IV validity, and interpretation of the estimated magnitude all remain too fragile.

## 3. Essential Points

1. **The outcome data are not well matched to the annual treatment design.**  
   The paper uses ACS tract-level 5-year estimates for 2013–2022 but analyzes them as if they were annual tract outcomes responding to tract-year retailer exits. This is a major problem. The ACS 5-year estimates are rolling averages with substantial overlap across adjacent years, so the left-hand-side variable is mechanically smoothed and serially dependent. A “2019” tract SNAP rate reflects information from multiple surrounding years, including pre- and post-treatment periods, which makes the timing of the corporate shocks and 2018 rule difficult to interpret causally. This issue is first-order and must be addressed directly. At minimum, the paper needs a clear discussion of what variation is actually identified with rolling-window ACS data, and ideally it should move to a genuinely annual outcome measure or reformulate the design around low-frequency changes.

2. **The exclusion restrictions are not credible enough in the current specification, and the paper’s own placebo casts serious doubt on the causal interpretation.**  
   The most damaging result in the paper is the significant “placebo” effect on poverty. If the instruments shift poverty, then they may be affecting SNAP receipt through local economic conditions, labor-market changes, or neighborhood decline rather than redemption access alone. That is especially problematic for store closures, which plausibly affect employment, retail activity, and amenity value. The paper acknowledges this but still maintains a strong causal interpretation centered on access. In my view, the placebo result substantially weakens that interpretation. The authors need either a much more convincing argument that these are reduced-form local-access shocks rather than broader local economic shocks, or they need to narrow and soften the claims.

3. **The tract-FE IV result appears to be driven almost entirely by one shift-share instrument whose exogeneity is not demonstrated.**  
   In the preferred tract-FE specification, the corporate-shock first stage weakens dramatically or behaves inconsistently, while the stock-rule instrument becomes the main source of identifying variation. But pre-2018 small-format retailer share is exactly the sort of tract characteristic likely correlated with differential post-2018 trends in poverty, urban form, transportation access, and neighborhood retail change. Under tract FE, identification comes from whether tracts with different baseline shares evolve differently after 2018. That requires a much stronger parallel-trends-type justification than the paper provides. As written, the headline estimate looks less like triangulation across multiple quasi-experiments and more like a single shift-share design with unresolved differential-trend concerns.

## 4. Suggestions

The paper addresses an interesting question and could become a useful short paper if the empirical strategy is tightened and the claims are disciplined. My suggestions below are intended in that spirit.

First, **rebuild the data section around the timing problem**. The paper needs to explain exactly what ACS table B22003 provides at the tract-year level and what it does not. If these are 5-year rolling estimates, then the treatment should not be interpreted as affecting a sharp annual outcome. A simple and transparent table showing the mapping between ACS release year and underlying survey years would help. If tract-level annual outcomes are unavailable, one workable path is to redesign the paper around longer differences—e.g., pre/post changes over multi-year windows aligned with the ACS construction—rather than annual event timing. That would also reduce the risk that the estimated sign reversal is driven by overlapping-window artifacts rather than true within-tract responses.

Second, **clarify the unit of economic interpretation of the coefficient**. A 5.9 percentage point drop in household SNAP receipt from the loss of one retailer is very large relative to the mean tract SNAP rate, and in many tracts there are only a few retailers. The paper should report treatment intensity more carefully: how often tracts lose a retailer, what fraction of tracts have only one authorized store, and what the effect looks like when scaling exits by baseline retailer count or tract population. A tract losing 1 of 1 retailer is conceptually very different from losing 1 of 10, yet the current specification imposes a constant marginal effect. I would strongly encourage specifications using: (i) an indicator for losing the last retailer, (ii) percent change in retailer count, and (iii) event studies around first/last major exit.

Third, **separate store closure from SNAP deauthorization as clearly as possible**. The paper’s mechanism is about loss of redemption infrastructure, but the treatment variable is “net exits,” which combines deauthorizations and entry/exit dynamics of many kinds. Some deauthorizations reflect rule changes; some reflect ordinary store closure; some “new authorizations” may offset access loss. If possible, decompose the treatment into: (a) chain-closure exits, (b) non-chain deauthorizations, and (c) rule-related losses among small-format retailers. This would help connect the empirical estimates to the proposed mechanism and policy claims.

Fourth, **the IV strategy needs much deeper validation, especially for the shift-share component**. Because the preferred estimate seems to rely mainly on pre-2018 small-format share × post-2018, the paper should provide a standard battery of diagnostics:  
- pre-trend/event-study plots by bins of pre-2018 small-format share;  
- balance tests showing whether high-share and low-share tracts had different pre-2018 trends in SNAP receipt, poverty, population, and retailer counts;  
- interactions of baseline tract covariates with time trends or post indicators;  
- state-by-year or county-by-year fixed effects if feasible, to absorb broader policy changes and local shocks.  
Without these exercises, it is hard to rule out that the stock-rule IV is proxying for differential changes in disadvantaged neighborhoods after 2018.

Fifth, **the corporate-shock instruments should be treated more cautiously**. The paper currently asserts that corporate decisions were made “in boardrooms” and are therefore plausibly exogenous. That is too quick. Store closures within chains are rarely random across neighborhoods; they reflect store-level profitability and local market conditions. You can still use these shocks, but then the identifying variation should come from exposure to nationally driven chain contractions, not from assuming local closure incidence is unrelated to local trends. A better approach would be to construct Bartik-style exposure using predetermined chain presence and national chain contraction rates, and then show that exposed and unexposed tracts had similar pre-trends. Also, because Walmart and Family Dollar serve systematically different markets, separate reduced-form results by chain would be informative.

Sixth, **report reduced-form estimates and not only 2SLS coefficients**. Given the concerns about exclusion and scaling, reduced forms may actually be more credible and more policy relevant here. For each instrument set, show the effect on SNAP participation and on retailer exits. If the reduced-form effects are small or unstable across instruments, that would be informative. If they are consistent, that would strengthen the paper.

Seventh, **soften the interpretation of the “overidentification” logic**. The introduction says that similar estimates across instrument sets would provide “direct evidence” that the exclusion restriction holds. That overstates what overidentification can do, especially when instruments may violate exclusion in similar directions. Moreover, the paper does not actually report the overidentification test. I would revise the language substantially and present any over-ID statistic, if only as a limited diagnostic rather than a validation theorem.

Eighth, **the placebo and mechanism sections should be reworked**. The poverty placebo is not a minor caveat; it is one of the paper’s most important findings. It suggests the instruments may move broader neighborhood conditions. Rather than relegating this to a robustness subsection, I would elevate it and reframe the paper’s contribution more modestly: retailer losses may reduce SNAP receipt through a combination of access and local economic disruption. Then, to isolate the access channel more convincingly, try outcomes less likely to move through general equilibrium local shocks—for example, outcomes stratified by vehicle access, distance to nearest alternative store if that can be constructed, or effects concentrated where the last/nearest retailer disappears.

Ninth, **construct spatial access measures if at all possible**. The current treatment is tract-level count of net exits, but the mechanism is distance/travel cost. The geocoded retailer data are a major asset. A much more convincing design would use: distance to nearest SNAP retailer, count within 1/3/5 miles, or loss of the nearest store. These measures would align much more closely with the “redemption desert” concept. Heterogeneity by no-vehicle rate and rural status is suggestive, but direct access metrics would make the story coherent.

Tenth, **the presentation of the tables needs careful cleanup**. Table 4 appears multiple times with conflicting first-stage F-statistics, including impossible values. This is more than cosmetic; it undermines confidence in the reported results. The final manuscript must contain one correct version of each table, with clearly labeled first-stage statistics corresponding to the preferred estimator and fixed-effects structure. Given how central instrument strength is to the paper, this needs especially careful verification.

Eleventh, **the paper should discuss contemporaneous policy changes more seriously**. The 2013–2022 period includes substantial changes in SNAP policy, labor markets, and the pandemic. Even with year fixed effects, tracts with high baseline exposure to small-format retail or distressed chains may have evolved differently along many dimensions. State-by-year fixed effects would be a useful minimum check. If the estimates are unstable once these are included, that would be important to know.

Finally, **I would narrow the policy claims in the conclusion**. The paper currently jumps from a fragile tract-FE IV estimate to strong statements about welfare costs of retailer standards and future SNAP reforms. That is premature. A more defensible conclusion would be: there is suggestive evidence that contraction of the SNAP retail network may reduce measured tract-level SNAP receipt, particularly in lower-mobility places, but more work is needed to separate redemption-access effects from broader local economic shocks and to measure the magnitude with better-aligned outcome data.

In short, the paper asks a worthwhile question and has a potentially interesting dataset, but the current evidence does not yet support the strong causal claims. The most productive revision path is to tighten the outcome measurement, refocus the identification on a better-validated source of variation, and present the contribution more modestly.
