## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
This paper examines how federal student-aid policy acts as an inadvertent "place-based shock" to local economies. By exploiting a 2012 tightening of credit standards for Parent PLUS loans—which disproportionately hit Historically Black Colleges and Universities (HBCUs)—the author shows that institutional revenue shocks lead to significant, persistent declines in total county employment. It argues that the "anchor institution" effect of universities is as potent (and fragile) as the manufacturing plants often studied in the regional economics literature.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this well, though it spends a bit too much time on the "how" (DiD, IPEDS, QWI) in the second paragraph rather than the "why it matters." 

**The pitch the paper should have:** 
"When the federal government changes student-aid eligibility, it doesn't just affect students; it redistributes economic activity across American geography. This paper shows that an administrative change in credit standards triggered an 11% enrollment collapse at HBCUs, leading to a persistent 2.7% decline in total employment in host counties. These findings reveal that colleges serve as vital but vulnerable 'anchor institutions,' where federal policy can inadvertently extract wealth from historically marginalized communities."

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**Contribution:** The paper identifies the local labor market multiplier of a higher-education institution using a plausibly exogenous federal policy shock.

- **Differentiation:** It sits between the "anchor institution" literature (usually descriptive/urban planning), the "local multipliers" literature (Moretti/Greenstone), and the "student aid" literature (usually focused on individual attainment). It is clearly differentiated by looking at the *spatial* spillover of a *financial aid* policy.
- **Question vs. Literature:** It answers a question about the **WORLD** (How do federal rules affect community stability?).
- **Clarity:** A smart economist would definitely get it. It’s "Moretti (2010) but for colleges instead of factories."
- **Bigger Contribution:** To make this truly "AER-big," the author needs to bridge the gap between the short-run multiplier and the long-run human capital effect. Does the loss of an "anchor" lead to a permanent "poverty trap" for these counties?

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
**Closest Neighbors:** 
1.  **Moretti (2010)** & **Greenstone et al. (2010)**: The gold standards for local multipliers and plant openings/closings.
2.  **Autor et al. (2013)**: For the "hysteresis" and "persistence of shocks" framing.
3.  **Bound and Turner (2007)**: On how higher-ed supply affects local attainment.

**Strategy:** The paper correctly builds on the local multiplier literature. It should **attack** the current "fiscal-only" evaluation of student loan policy. The authors should frame the "Credit Check Tax" as a critique of how the CBO or Department of Ed "scores" policy—ignoring the massive spatial externalities.

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC
- **Setup:** Universities (especially HBCUs) are "too big to fail" in rural Southern counties.
- **Tension:** A sudden, unannounced federal bureaucratic change (the 2012 tightening) creates a massive revenue hole for these specific institutions.
- **Resolution:** County-wide employment drops, and—critically—it doesn't come back even when the policy is partially reversed.
- **Implications:** Place-based policy is happening whether we like it or not; student aid *is* place-based policy.

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong. The "reversal test" (Section 5.3) is the most compelling part of the narrative—it proves the "stickiness" of the damage.

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
At a dinner party: "Did you know that when Obama’s Dept of Ed tightened credit checks for parents in 2012, it accidentally nuked the economy of 74 Black-majority counties in the South?"

- **Reaction:** People would lean in. It connects technical administrative law to racial equity and regional decline.
- **Follow-up:** "Why didn't the jobs come back when they fixed the rule in 2014?" This is the core economic puzzle.

---

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
- **Sectoral Results:** Table 3 is weak. The results are "directionally consistent" but imprecise. The author should move the noisy sector results to the appendix and instead focus the main text on the **aggregate** multiplier and the **hysteresis** (Table 4).
- **Weighting:** The employment-weighted result (-4.8%) is much larger and more interesting than the baseline. This suggests the effect is *larger* where the institution is more central. This should be moved earlier.

---

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The paper is currently a very high-quality "Applied Micro" paper. To be an **AER** paper, it needs more **Ambition**. 

Specifically: The author frames this as a study of HBCUs. While that is the identification strategy, the paper should be framed as a general theory of **"Institutional Fragility to Federal Rulemaking."** 

**Single Biggest Improvement:** Explicitly model or estimate the *asymmetry* of the shock. Why does the "tax" hurt more than the "subsidy" (the 2014 reversal) helps? If the author can explain *why* the recovery failed (e.g., firm exit, permanent migration), it moves from a measurement paper to a "Big Ideas" paper.
