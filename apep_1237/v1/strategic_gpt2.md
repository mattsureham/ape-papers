## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether federal student-aid policy can have economically important spillovers on local labor markets by destabilizing colleges that function as anchor institutions. Using the 2012 tightening of Parent PLUS credit standards, the paper argues that HBCU enrollment fell sharply and that counties more exposed to HBCUs subsequently experienced meaningful employment declines, especially in majority-Black Southern places with few alternative engines of growth.

Why should a busy economist care? Because the paper tries to connect three things that are usually studied separately—student finance, higher education, and local labor markets—and says that an ostensibly narrow credit-rule change operated like a place-based shock.

**Does the paper itself articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Pretty well, but not optimally. The current opening starts with the generic large-employer/plant-closing framing and only then gets to the genuinely interesting part: a federal student-credit rule unexpectedly hurting Black communities through HBCUs. That is the hook. The paper should lead with that immediately.

### The pitch the paper should have

> Federal student-aid rules are usually evaluated for their effects on college access and repayment. But when colleges are major local employers, those rules may also reshape local labor markets. This paper studies the 2012 tightening of Parent PLUS loan credit standards and shows that, because HBCUs depended disproportionately on PLUS financing, the policy reduced HBCU enrollment and was followed by persistent employment losses in the counties that host them.
>
> The broader point is that student-finance policy can function as place-based economic policy. In communities where HBCUs are anchor institutions—many of them majority-Black counties in the South—a change in federal credit eligibility did not just affect students; it contracted the local economy.

That version foregrounds the world question, the population, and the broader economic relevance faster.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution in one sentence:**  
The paper’s core contribution is to argue that a federal student-loan eligibility change generated persistent local employment losses by hitting HBCUs that serve as anchor institutions in economically vulnerable counties.

1. Prior Parent PLUS papers study student-level or institution-level effects, not local labor market spillovers.
2. Prior local-labor-market shock papers mostly study manufacturing, trade, or large firms, not colleges.
3. Prior higher-ed local impact papers tend to focus on openings, expansions, or broad college presence—not a clean federal financing shock to a specific class of colleges.

Right now, the paper states these links, but not forcefully enough.

The world framing is there, but it should dominate much more.

That is a coherent bridge between literatures.

The more impactful conversation is:

That connects student-aid policy to a much larger economics conversation about spatial incidence and the unintended geography of federal policy.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Colleges, especially HBCUs in the South, often function as anchor institutions in communities with limited diversification. Federal student-aid policy affects enrollment and institutional revenue, but economists typically do not think of such policy as a local labor-market shock.

### Tension
The 2012 Parent PLUS tightening sharply reduced access to financing at HBCUs. If those institutions are true local anchors, the consequences should extend beyond students and campus budgets into county employment. But we do not know whether such spillovers are economically meaningful or persistent.

### Resolution
The paper finds that counties with greater pre-shock exposure to HBCUs experienced employment declines after the tightening, with patterns consistent with gradual propagation and persistence even after partial policy reversal.

### Implications
Student-finance policy may have unmeasured place-based consequences, especially for majority-Black communities reliant on HBCUs. The incidence of federal credit rules thus extends beyond borrowers to local workers and firms.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
Mostly yes. This is one of the stronger features of the manuscript. There is a recognizable setup-tension-resolution structure.

But the paper still slides at times into **a collection of confirmatory exercises**:
- main result,
- binary variant,
- earnings,
- event study,
- sector cuts,
- reversal,
- jackknife.

The results are lined up sensibly, but the story is not maximally disciplined. The paper should tell one story:

> A federal credit shock hit HBCUs; HBCUs are local anchors; therefore the shock propagated into local labor markets; and the striking fact is not merely that employment fell, but that it stayed down.

That means the **reversal/persistence result should move much closer to center stage.** It is the tension-resolving twist that makes the paper more than “another local shock paper.”

Also, “credit check tax” is rhetorically punchy but risks sounding editorialized rather than analytical. It may help in a policy brief; in AER framing it may come off as advocacy unless used sparingly.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party?
I’d say:

> “A change in federal Parent PLUS credit rules didn’t just reduce HBCU enrollment—it appears to have reduced employment in the counties around those schools, and those losses persisted even after the policy was partially reversed.”

That is the interesting fact.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
Economists would **lean in**, but with conditions. They will care because it reframes student-aid policy as a local-shock paper and because the racial/spatial incidence is salient. But they will immediately ask whether the paper is really teaching a general lesson or just documenting an unusual episode affecting a small set of counties.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Likely one of these:
1. “Is this a story about HBCUs specifically, or about colleges as anchor institutions generally?”
2. “How big are the local multipliers relative to the direct campus contraction?”
3. “Why didn’t the local economy recover when the loan rules loosened?”
4. “Does this imply student-aid policy should be scored partly as regional policy?”

Those are good follow-up questions. The paper should anticipate them in the introduction.

### If findings are modest, is the modesty interesting?
The magnitudes are not modest in the paper’s own telling, but they risk *feeling* modest because the presentation is abstract. Again, the coefficient is not the right headline. If translated into county-level job losses or community-level incidence, the result becomes much more vivid.

The persistence result is what rescues the paper from feeling like a narrow one-off shock. Without that, it could feel like a competent but bounded application.

1. stronger mechanism evidence, or
2. broader implications, or

Yes. The paper is competent but somewhat safe. It reports a local-employment effect of a federal loan-policy shock. For AER, the paper needs to more boldly answer:
- What class of policies does this teach us about?
- Why were economists systematically missing these spillovers?
- How should policy analysis change?

**Reframe the paper around the broader claim that federal student-credit rules have spatial incidence through anchor institutions, and make persistence after partial reversal—not the average DiD coefficient—the centerpiece of the paper.**

That one change would improve the introduction, the contribution, the literature positioning, and the narrative arc all at once.
