## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple, important question: when local governments cut business taxes to compete for firms, do they actually end up providing fewer public goods? Using Swiss municipalities that frequently change local tax multipliers, the paper argues that the canonical “race to the bottom” is largely absent: tax cuts neither attract firms nor reduce municipal spending.

Busy economists should care because a large normative case for tax harmonization rests on the claim that tax competition erodes public goods. A paper showing that this mechanism does not operate even in a highly decentralized, high-variation setting is potentially interesting.

**Does the paper itself articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Mostly, but not sharply enough. The current introduction is competent and literate, but it is still written too much as “the literature has studied X and not Y.” The strongest version of this paper is not “here is a clean municipal test of a classic theory,” but rather: **the core welfare logic of tax competition may fail in a setting where it should be easiest to see.** That should be the front-door pitch.

### The pitch the paper should have

> Economists have long argued that tax competition forces governments into a race to the bottom: cut taxes to retain mobile capital, lose revenue, and cut public goods. This paper asks whether that mechanism actually operates in practice.  
>  
> I study Zurich municipalities, where local governments frequently change tax multipliers and have meaningful spending responsibilities. Despite visible tax competition, I find no evidence that lower tax rates attract firms or reduce municipal spending. In this setting, the race is real in tax-setting behavior but phantom in its economic consequences.

That is cleaner, world-facing, and easier to repeat.

2. papers on **firm/capital mobility responses**, and

Right now a smart economist might summarize it as: “It’s another subnational tax competition paper, except the outcome is expenditure.” That is not enough for AER-level strategic positioning.

- Do local tax cuts actually erode public goods?
- Is the race to the bottom empirically real or just a theoretical benchmark?

That is stronger than “direct evidence on spending is thin.”

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the intro?
Yes, but not crisply enough. They would probably say:  
“It's a municipality panel from Zurich showing no effect of corporate tax changes on spending or firms.”

That is decent, but still sounds like a competent field-journal paper rather than a must-read. What they should be able to say is:  
“Surprisingly, one of the canonical welfare predictions of tax competition seems to fail in a place where local competition is intense.”

### What would make this contribution bigger?
Specific ways to enlarge it:

1. **Reframe around the failure of the canonical mechanism, not just the spending null.**  
   The paper already has two nulls—no spending response, no firm response. That joint failure is the real contribution. Lean into the chain breaking at multiple points.

2. **Exploit the “lockstep” result much more aggressively.**

3. **Change the comparison class.**

4. **Add composition/margin language, even if not new estimation.**

5. **Be more precise about the external object.**

Because corporate/personal rates move together, the paper is really about a broader local fiscal package, and acknowledging that directly could make the paper more conceptually honest and more interesting.

### How should the paper position itself relative to those neighbors?
Mostly **build on and redirect**, not attack.

- Relative to the strategic interaction papers: “Those papers show that jurisdictions mimic each other in tax setting; I ask whether that strategic behavior actually produces the welfare-relevant consequence.”
- Relative to base-response papers: “Those papers ask whether firms or capital move; I show that in this setting even the base-response channel appears weak.”
- Relative to theory: “This is not a rejection of the general theory, but evidence that one canonical mechanism is not empirically important in an institutional environment often treated as a textbook case of local tax competition.”

The paper should not overclaim that it “challenges” Pillar Two. That leap feels opportunistic and invites skepticism. Municipal Swiss tax multipliers are not the same as multinational profit shifting and global minimum taxes. The better move is: “This speaks to one pillar of the normative case for coordination, not the entire case.”

### Is the paper currently positioned too narrowly or too broadly?
Oddly, both.

- **Too narrowly** in the empirical conversation: it reads like a very specific canton-municipality panel paper.
- **Too broadly** in the policy rhetoric: it jumps from Zurich municipalities to OECD Pillar Two.

That combination is unstable. The paper needs a middle register: broad enough to matter, disciplined enough to remain credible.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of or under-engaged with?
A few underdeveloped conversations:

1. **Yardstick competition / political economy of local taxation.**  
   The “lockstep” fact screams political benchmarking, ideology, or administrative simplicity, not pure tax-base competition. The paper mentions this, but only late and lightly.

2. **Fiscal smoothing and local public finance institutions.**  
   If spending does not respond to tax changes, institutional insulation is part of the story. The paper needs to speak more to literatures on intergovernmental transfers, budget smoothing, rainy-day funds, and mandated spending.

3. **Public finance theory on margins of adjustment.**  
   If the spending margin is muted, the relevant question becomes: where does adjustment happen? Transfers, fees, debt, reserves, or future taxes? That literature would help the paper explain its null rather than merely report it.

4. **Broader literature on null effects in economics.**  
   Not as a separate literature review, but strategically: the paper needs to persuade the reader that this null is informative because the setting is theoretically favorable, the treatment is meaningful, and the MDEs are substantive.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?
Not fully. The most impactful conversation may not be “another Swiss tax competition paper,” but rather:

> “What if the canonical race-to-the-bottom mechanism is empirically much less operative because local governments smooth, bundle taxes, and face limited base mobility?”

That connects tax competition, political economy, and fiscal institutions. That is a more interesting conversation.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Standard public finance theory says jurisdictions competing for mobile capital cut taxes too much and therefore underprovide public goods. This logic has shaped how economists and policymakers think about tax coordination.

### Tension
There is abundant evidence that governments react to each other’s tax rates, and some evidence that tax bases respond, but much less evidence on the key welfare-relevant outcome: do public goods actually fall? Moreover, in real local public finance systems, transfers, mandates, and bundled tax policy may sever the theory’s simple chain.

### Resolution
In Zurich municipalities, there is substantial tax-rate movement, but these changes do not measurably affect municipal expenditure or firm location. The visible tax competition appears to have little real consequence along the classic channels.

### Implications
The race-to-the-bottom mechanism may be weaker, more conditional, or institutionally mediated than the canonical model suggests. Policy arguments for harmonization should not rest too heavily on the simple claim that tax competition mechanically erodes public goods.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
It has one, but it is not fully disciplined. At times it reads like a collection of sensible empirical exercises—main spending regressions, firm outcomes, placebo, heterogeneity, event study, lagged revenue—rather than a story advancing one central claim.

### What story should it be telling?
The story should be:

1. **Canonical mechanism:** tax competition should cut taxes, move firms/capital, erode revenue, reduce spending.
2. **Empirical test in a favorable setting:** decentralized municipalities with frequent tax changes.
3. **Result:** neither the intermediate mechanism nor the final welfare outcome shows up.
4. **Interpretation:** observed “tax competition” may often be political benchmarking or bundled fiscal posture, while institutions buffer spending.
5. **Implication:** the standard race-to-the-bottom narrative is not a reliable reduced-form description of local public finance.

That is stronger than “I test whether spending responds and find a null.”

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?
“I looked at Swiss municipalities that keep changing business tax multipliers, and despite all that apparent tax competition, I can’t find evidence that they either attract firms or cut public spending.”

That is a decent lead. Better still:
“Even in Switzerland, where local tax competition is supposed to be textbook, the race to the bottom looks mostly phantom.”

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
Some would lean in—especially public finance economists—because the question targets a classic theory. But many would immediately ask whether this is just a special Swiss-institutional null, and whether the paper is overinterpreting a local result. So the interest is there, but the framing needs to manage that skepticism fast.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Almost certainly one of these:
- “If spending doesn’t adjust, what does?”
- “Are municipalities really competing for firms, or just moving all tax rates together?”
- “Is this about corporate tax competition at all?”
- “Why should I update my beliefs about broader tax harmonization from Zurich municipalities?”

Those are exactly the questions the paper should anticipate more directly in the introduction and discussion.

### If the findings are null or modest, is the null itself interesting?
Yes—but only because the null concerns a central prediction in a canonical model and comes from a setting where the effect should plausibly be visible. The paper is on the right track with minimum detectable effects, but it still needs to sell the null as **theoretical falsification of a common reduced-form story**, not merely “we estimated something and it was imprecise.”

At present it is not yet fully making that case. It says “precise null,” but it needs to say more clearly why this is not a failed hunt for significance. The strongest point is the **double null**:
- tax cuts do not attract firms,
- and they do not reduce spending.

That matters more than either null alone.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

At present, this is **not yet an AER paper**. The issue is less technical than strategic.

### What is the gap?
Primarily:

- **Framing problem:** yes.
- **Scope problem:** yes.
- **Ambition problem:** also yes.
- **Novelty problem:** somewhat, but less fatal than the others.

The paper has a potentially interesting idea—a canonical mechanism appears absent in a favorable setting—but it currently presents itself as a careful, bounded municipal-panel null-result paper. That is too safe for AER.

### What would excite the top 10 people in this field?
A version that convincingly argued:

> We have overlearned from tax-setting interactions. Jurisdictions may mimic each other’s tax rates without generating the underlying mobility and public-goods effects that make tax competition normatively important.

That is a much bigger idea. To get there, the paper needs to be less about “a direct test in Zurich” and more about “why strategic tax competition is not the same thing as welfare-relevant tax competition.”

### Is this a scope problem?
Yes. The paper currently tests one outcome family in one canton over six years. That can still work if the conceptual claim is sharp enough, but then the interpretation has to carry more weight. Right now the paper stops one level too early: it documents the absence of spending effects without fully explaining what that implies about the underlying model of local competition.

### Is it a novelty problem?
Moderately. “Null effect of local tax changes on spending” by itself is not AER-level novel. “One of the core welfare claims in the tax competition canon fails in a textbook setting, because observed tax competition is bundled politics rather than mobile-capital competition” is much closer.

### Single most impactful advice
**Reframe the paper around the failure of the canonical tax-competition mechanism—especially the disconnect between strategic tax setting and real economic consequences—rather than around a narrow municipal spending null.**

That is the one change that would most improve its odds.
