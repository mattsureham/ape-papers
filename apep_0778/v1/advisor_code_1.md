**Idea Fidelity**

The paper follows the original manifest’s intent: it studies the staggered introduction of transitional SNAP benefits across 24 adopting states between 2001 and 2016, and it employs a Callaway-Sant’Anna estimator with never-treated states as controls to examine SNAP participation. However, it deviates in the operational details. The manifest emphasized using FNS/ACF administrative data and the SNAP-to-TANF participation ratio as the key outcome, whereas the submitted paper relies on Census ACS data and analyzes the aggregate state-level SNAP participation rate (and logged SNAP households). This change is consequential: the ACS route introduces measurement attenuation (reporting bias, only annual snapshots, and household counts rather than precise caseloads), while the key mechanism described in the manifest—the direct effect on the SNAP-to-TANF ratio—has been abandoned. Thus, the paper partly fulfills the idea but misses the most promising data and outcome that directly capture the identification strategy’s logic.

**Summary**

The authors leverage staggered state adoption of transitional SNAP benefits to estimate their impact on SNAP participation, using ACS state-year data from 2005–2023 and the Callaway-Sant’Anna DiD estimator alongside TWFE and randomization-inference checks. The point estimates are uniformly positive (about a 0.5 percentage point increase in the SNAP rate) but statistically indistinguishable from zero, and the event study shows no pre-trends. The paper concludes that either the TANF-to-SNAP cliff is smaller than expected or aggregate ACS outcomes are too noisy to detect the policy’s effect, recommending future studies use linked administrative data.

**Essential Points**

1. **Data choice undermines the identification strategy.** The manifest promised administrative SNAP/TANF records and a treatment-outcome ratio that directly reflects the targeted bureaucratic gap. Relying on ACS SNAP participation rates greatly attenuates the signal because TANF-exiting families are a tiny share of the SNAP population, and ACS suffers from underreporting and only annual frequency. The authors acknowledge this attenuation but proceed without a better outcome. To credibly link transitional benefits to food assistance continuity, the paper must either (a) obtain administrative data (as envisioned) or (b) construct a proxy that closely tracks the TANF-to-SNAP transition (e.g., ACS-based SNAP-to-TANF ratios if TANF counts are available). Otherwise, the identification strategy is too imprecise to answer the research question.

2. **Parallel trends and confounding policies require tighter controls.** The paper leans heavily on the Callaway-Sant’Anna estimator but does not sufficiently rule out that adoption correlates with broader SNAP modernization efforts or state-specific trends in poverty/TANF caseloads. The event study shows noisy pre-trends, but the confidence intervals are wide, and no pre-period joint test is offered. Additionally, the text asserts that adoption timing reflected “administrative capacity and political considerations,” but there is no empirical exploration (e.g., covariate balance, state-specific time trends, or synthetic controls). Without tighter diagnostics or robustness to contemporaneous policy changes (BCE, SNAP simplification, QE expansions), the credibility of the DiD assumption remains fragile.

3. **Insufficient power and interpretation risk misconstruing the policy’s impact.** The paper repeatedly emphasizes the unremarkable precision of its main coefficient, which is positive but far from significant. While a null result can be valuable, the comparison to a 0.5 percentage point effect on an aggregate rate risks overstating policy irrelevance without quantifying the detectable minimum effect. There is no formal power/noise calculation, nor is there an attempt to translate the estimate into the implied number of TANF exiters retained. As a result, readers cannot distinguish whether the null is due to policymaking insignificance or severe attenuation from the outcome measure. The analysis should either demonstrate sufficient power to reject economically meaningful effects or avoid drawing policy conclusions.

**Suggestions**

1. **Revisit the outcome variable and data source.** The manifest’s original idea—using administrative SNAP and TANF records and focusing on the SNAP-to-TANF participation ratio—is still the most direct way to test the intended mechanism. If possible, acquire FNS/ACF administrative data (or state-level monthly counts already mentioned in the manifest) to construct the exact ratio or the number of SNAP households with a TANF link. If administrative data are not feasible, justify the use of ACS data in detail: for example, show that state-level ACS SNAP rates are highly correlated with the administrative totals over time and that measurement error is unlikely to be differential around adoption. Even better, supplement with TANF caseload data (available from ACF) to compute the SNAP/TANF ratio or at least contextualize how much of the SNAP population could plausibly be affected.

2. **Quantify the expected magnitude and power.** Given the small share of SNAP households that transition from TANF, estimate the maximum attainable effect (e.g., assume 100% retention of exiting TANF households for five months) and compare it to your standard error. This calculation will clarify whether the study is underpowered by construction. Coupling this with a formal power analysis (perhaps via simulated treatment given observed variation) would explain why the point estimate is noisy and whether the study can reject, say, a 1 pp effect. Without this, readers cannot tell whether the null result is substantive.

3. **Strengthen the parallel-trends diagnostics.** Provide and discuss the joint pre-trend tests (e.g., F-tests for event-time coefficients being zero) and show more granular plots (with confidence intervals) so readers can gauge any systematic biases. Incorporating state-specific linear trends or time-varying controls (poverty rate, unemployment) could further protect against confounding policies or macroeconomic shocks. Also, consider a synthetic control or matching version for early adopters to check that the never-treated group is indeed plausible; if not, explore alternative comparison groups (e.g., late adopters before treatment) and discuss any discrepancies.

4. **Reinterpret heterogeneity carefully.** The heterogeneity table reports some negative coefficients for mid-2000s cohorts, which contradicts the main narrative of uniformly positive but insignificant effects. These estimates might be artifacts of noise or small sample splits. Rather than interpreting them substantively, acknowledge their imprecision explicitly and clarify that they should not be overinterpreted. If possible, aggregate cohorts to preserve power and focus on pre-specified dimensions (e.g., high vs. low TANF caseload, region) with a clear rationale grounded in policy mechanism.

5. **Clarify policy implications without overstretching.** The discussion currently frames the null as either evidence that the gap is small or that aggregate data are too noisy. To avoid ambiguous takeaways, structure this section around what the current design can (and cannot) tell us. Emphasize that the ACS-based analysis is informative about aggregate SNAP participation but not about individual TANF exits. Suggest concrete next steps—such as partnering with states for linked administrative data or exploiting the SNAP/TANF ratio if available—to directly measure the desired outcome. Avoid strong policy prescriptions given the data limitations.

6. **Document data processing and treatment coding in the appendix.** While the paper describes the ACS and policy sources, more detail on treatment timing coding (especially for states whose adoption year predates the ACS window) and any imputation decisions would improve reproducibility. Provide a table listing adopters, adoption years, and sample windows to let readers assess how each state contributes to the estimation. Also, clarify how the ACS survey year aligns with policy implementation dates (e.g., if a state adopts mid-year, which ACS year is treated?).

7. **Address potential reporting bias in ACS SNAP data.** The ACS is known to undercount SNAP recipients. Although the paper briefly cites Meyer et al., explicitly discuss whether this underreporting could vary systematically around adoption (e.g., if administrative staff publicize transitional benefits, households might be more willing to report SNAP receipt). If there is concern, consider validating the ACS trend against administrative SNAP totals from FNS to show that there is no sudden change in reporting concurrent with adoption.

By aligning the empirical strategy more closely with the manifest’s focus on the SNAP-TANF interface, tightening the identification diagnostics, and better communicating the limits of the data, the paper can make a more convincing case about whether transitional SNAP benefits close the welfare cliff.
