## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple and policy-relevant question: when states promise that marijuana tax revenue will go to schools, do schools actually get more money, or do governments just relabel existing spending? Using staggered legalization across U.S. states, the paper argues that education spending rises more in states that earmark marijuana revenue for education, potentially suggesting that earmarks operate less as mechanical fiscal transfers and more as political commitment devices.

Why should a busy economist care? Because this is not really a marijuana paper. It is a paper about fiscal fungibility, earmarking, and whether public-budget labels constrain real allocations—an old public finance question with a fresh and highly salient policy setting.

**Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Not quite. The opening is decent, but the paper gets pulled too quickly into the fungibility literature and then into design details before fully stating the broader world question. The first two paragraphs should make clearer that the paper is about whether visible earmarks change actual government behavior, with marijuana taxes as the unusually clean contemporary setting.

### The pitch the paper should have

“States increasingly sell politically controversial taxes by promising voters that the money will fund popular goods like schools. But are these promises real, or are earmarked revenues largely fungible within government budgets? This paper studies that question in the context of recreational marijuana legalization, where many states explicitly told voters that cannabis taxes would support education. I show that legalization alone does not robustly increase school spending, but states that earmark marijuana revenue for education spend substantially more on education than states that do not. The central implication is that earmarks may matter not because dollars are mechanically non-fungible, but because they create politically salient commitments that alter budget choices.”

That is the AER version of the pitch. Right now the paper is still pitched as “first causal estimate of marijuana earmark fungibility,” which is narrower and less interesting than the underlying question.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:**  
The paper claims that earmarked marijuana tax revenue is associated with higher education spending—possibly by more than the revenue itself—suggesting that earmarks can act as political commitment devices rather than merely labeled fiscal transfers.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest papers?
Only partially. The paper names the lottery-earmarking literature and says marijuana offers a cleaner setting, but the differentiation is still too mechanical: “first causal estimate in this context.” That is not enough. A top-journal contribution cannot just be “same question, newer policy, better DiD.” It needs to be: **the setting changes the conceptual stakes**. Marijuana legalization combines salience, ballot-politics, and moral controversy in a way that may make earmarks especially politically binding. That is the differentiator.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a gap in a literature?
It is currently too often framed as filling a literature gap. “First causal estimate of fiscal fungibility for marijuana tax revenue” is a literature-gap sentence. The stronger version is a world question: **When governments make salient earmarking promises to voters, do those promises meaningfully constrain budgets?**

### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what’s new?
At present, maybe, but not crisply. They would probably say: “It’s a DiD paper on marijuana legalization and education spending, with a distinction between earmark and non-earmark states.” That is not good enough. You want them to say: **“It shows that politically salient earmarks may increase spending by more than the earmarked revenue, which challenges the usual fungibility view.”**

### What would make this contribution bigger?
Several possibilities:

1. **Lean much harder into the political-commitment mechanism.**  
   Right now that mechanism is intriguing but underdeveloped. The paper’s most interesting claim is not that marijuana taxes raise school spending; it is that **salient earmarks may discipline appropriations**. The paper should revolve around that.

2. **Expand the outcomes beyond education spending alone.**  
   If the claim is “earmarks work because they create commitment,” then contrast outcomes that should move under different mechanisms:
   - Education spending
   - Other state spending categories
   - General fund appropriations to education
   - Capital vs current spending
   - Local tax effort or district revenue composition  
   These are not robustness checks in the referee sense; they are strategic scope expansions that would tell a bigger story.

3. **Use the marijuana setting as a test of when fungibility breaks down.**  
   The real contribution could be: fungibility is weaker when revenue is politically salient, voter-facing, and explicitly promised. That is broader than cannabis and worth economists’ attention.

4. **Sharpen the comparison to lotteries.**  
   The most natural “bigger” version is not merely “marijuana earmarks matter.” It is “compared with the classic lottery case, marijuana earmarks appear different because they are more salient and politically contested.”

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Governments often promise that earmarked revenues from controversial or new taxes will fund popular services, especially education. Standard public finance says such earmarks should mostly be irrelevant because budgets are fungible.

### Tension
Yet some earmarks are much more politically salient than others. Marijuana legalization campaigns explicitly sold legalization as “money for schools,” raising the possibility that these promises bind politicians in a way ordinary earmarks do not.

### Resolution
The paper finds weak and somewhat sensitive aggregate effects of legalization overall, but larger education-spending increases in states that earmark marijuana revenues for education. The estimated increase exceeds the revenue itself.

### Implications
If taken seriously, this suggests that earmarks may matter through political commitment rather than pure accounting. That would qualify the standard fungibility view and matter for how economists think about hypothecated taxes.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
It has the ingredients, but the arc is not yet disciplined. Right now it feels like **two papers awkwardly fused**:

1. A general DiD paper on marijuana legalization and education spending.
2. A more interesting paper on earmarks as political commitment devices.

The first is not especially exciting. The second could be. The paper should choose the second and make everything else subordinate to it.

### What story should it be telling?
Not: “Does marijuana legalization increase education spending?”  
But: **“When politicians make highly salient earmarking promises, does fungibility break down?”**  
Then marijuana is the setting, earmarking heterogeneity is the core test, and the aggregate legalization effect becomes background context rather than the main event.
