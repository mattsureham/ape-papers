## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
The paper examines whether earmarking "sin taxes" (marijuana) for education actually increases school spending or if it is simply offset by reductions in general fund appropriations. Using a staggered difference-in-differences design, it finds that earmarking states actually see spending increases that *exceed* the new revenue by a factor of five, suggesting that earmarks act as a "political commitment device" rather than a mere accounting label.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this well, but it buries the "Puzzle" (the 5.2x passthrough) too deep in the intro. To be an AER paper, the pitch needs to move away from "marijuana policy" and toward "the political economy of fiscal commitment."

**The Pitch the paper should have:** 
"Does fiscal earmarking actually constrain legislative behavior, or is money perfectly fungible? This paper shows that in the case of marijuana legalization, earmarks do more than prevent crowds-out; they act as a catalyst for broader spending increases that far exceed the tax revenue itself. This suggests that the 'flypaper effect' is not just a failure of accounting, but a result of earmarks lowering the political cost of expansionary fiscal policy."

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**Contribution:** Earmarks for education don't just "stick"—they leverage additional spending by functioning as a visible political commitment that changes the equilibrium of the appropriations process.

- **Differentiation:** Most literature (Evans & Zhang) finds partial fungibility (50-70 cents on the dollar). This paper finds a "super-flypaper effect" (>100 cents). This is a stark departure from the 30-year-old consensus.
- **World vs. Literature:** It currently sits between the two. It needs to frame itself as a study of **the efficacy of ballot-box budgeting** (the World) rather than just an update to the lottery literature.
- **What would make it bigger?** To be AER-level, it needs a "Mechanism" section that uses more than just state-level aggregates. It needs to show *why* the 5x multiplier exists. Is it because of the salience of the ballot initiative? The author mentions this but doesn't test it.

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
The paper sits in the intersection of Public Finance (Hines & Thaler 1995) and Political Economy (Peltzman 1992).

- **Closest Neighbors:** *Evans & Zhang (2007)* on lotteries; *Knight (2002)* on federal grants; *Hansen et al. (2020)* on marijuana economics.
- **Positioning:** It should **attack** the lottery analogy. The paper's best move is to argue that marijuana is fundamentally different from lotteries because it is a high-salience social reform, whereas lotteries are "background noise" in the budget.
- **Unaware of:** It should connect to the "Salience and Taxation" literature (e.g., Chetty, Looney, and Kroft 2009). If voters see the marijuana tax on their receipt every day, does that make the "money for schools" promise more enforceable?

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC
- **Setup:** The "Fungibility Myth"—the idea that earmarks are a shell game played by clever politicians.
- **Tension:** Marijuana legalization was sold on a specific fiscal promise. If legislatures just cut other funding, they are breaking a very public contract. 
- **Resolution:** Not only is the money not fungible, but the earmark seems to "shame" or "embolden" the legislature into spending even more. 
- **Implications:** Earmarks are not just accounting labels; they are strategic tools for institutional change.

**Evaluation:** The arc is currently "Serviceable." It feels a bit like a "marijuana paper" first and a "theory paper" second. It needs to flip that.

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
**The Party Fact:** "When a state promises to spend its marijuana tax on schools, they don't just spend the tax—they end up spending $5 on schools for every $1 they collect in weed tax."

**Reaction:** Lean in. That is a counter-intuitive and "big" number. 
**Follow-up:** "Wait, where does the other $4 come from? Are they cannibalizing the police budget? Or did they just happen to legalize right before a tech boom?" (The paper struggles to answer this, which is its main weakness).

---

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
- **The "Alaska" problem:** Section 5.2 is too defensive. Move the "Excluding Alaska" results to the front. In a 20-unit sample, one outlier is a "nuisance parameter" that shouldn't clog the main narrative.
- **Front-loading:** The "Puzzle" of the 5.2x passthrough (Table 4) is the most interesting thing in the paper. It should be the center of the Introduction.
- **Eliminate:** The Federal Placebo is "clean" but boring. Keep it as a one-sentence footnote or a tiny table. Don't let it take up space in the results narrative.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The paper has a "competence" problem—it is a very well-executed DiD, but AER reviewers will find a 20-unit state-level DiD "thin."

**The Gap:** It lacks a "Smoking Gun" for the mechanism. To move from "Good" to "AER," the author needs to show *why* the spending exceeds the revenue. 
- **The advice:** Perform a "Salience Analysis." Code the ballot initiatives or news coverage in those 7 earmarking states. If the states with the "loudest" campaigns saw the biggest "over-spending," you have a world-class story about political salience and fiscal commitment.
