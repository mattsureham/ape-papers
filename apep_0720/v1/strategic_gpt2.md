## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple policy question: when states legalize online sports betting, do they actually raise new tax revenue, or do they mostly divert spending away from existing gambling products and other taxed activities? Using staggered legalization across U.S. states after *Murphy v. NCAA*, the paper argues that gambling-related tax revenue rises after legalization and finds no evidence of offsetting declines in pari-mutuel tax revenue or in broader placebo tax categories.

Why should a busy economist care? Because states justified legalization on fiscal grounds, and the core policy question is not whether sports betting generates gross revenue, but whether it generates **net new public revenue**. That is a first-order public finance question about product expansion versus substitution in sin-tax markets.

### Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Not quite. The opening is energetic, but the paper overpromises relative to what it can actually show. It sets up a strong “lottery cannibalization” question, then immediately pivots to outcomes that do **not** directly observe lottery revenue. A reader quickly realizes the paper is really about whether broad gambling tax collections rose after legalization, with a limited test for substitution using pari-mutuel taxes. That is a narrower and less decisive claim than the title and opening suggest.

### The pitch the paper should have

Here is the pitch the paper **should** use:

> States legalized online sports betting claiming it would generate new tax revenue, but that claim depends on whether sports betting expands the gambling market or merely displaces existing taxed gambling products. Using the staggered post-*Murphy* legalization wave and state tax collection data, this paper asks whether legalization increased total gambling-related state tax revenue and whether it reduced other observable gambling tax bases, especially pari-mutuel taxation. I find that total gambling-related tax revenue rises after legalization, with no detectable decline in pari-mutuel tax revenue or in placebo tax categories, suggesting short-run fiscal expansion rather than obvious cross-product cannibalization.

That pitch is narrower, but honest. It tells the reader what the paper can actually establish.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### Contribution in one sentence

The paper’s contribution is to provide a national, state-panel estimate of how sports betting legalization affects **aggregate state gambling tax revenue**, with a limited test of cross-market substitution using pari-mutuel taxes.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?

Only partially. The paper says prior work is mostly single-state and this is the first national staggered DiD. That is a valid contribution, but as currently written it sounds methodological and incremental: “the same question, just in a broader panel.” It does not sharply distinguish whether it is overturning prior findings, reconciling conflicting single-state evidence, or answering a substantively different question.

More importantly, the paper does **not** clearly differentiate between:
1. “Does sports betting raise gross gambling tax revenue?” and
2. “Does sports betting cannibalize lottery and other legacy gambling tax bases?”

Those are not the same contribution. Right now the paper tries to claim the second while mainly delivering the first.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or filling a gap in a LITERATURE?

It starts as a world question, which is good: do states get genuinely new revenue from sports betting legalization? But it drifts into “first national-scale staggered DiD estimate,” which is a literature-gap framing. The world question is much stronger. The paper should lean much harder into: **Are gambling markets fiscally zero-sum or expanding?** That is a real economic question.

### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what’s new?

At present, probably not cleanly. They would likely say: “It’s a DiD paper on sports betting legalization and tax revenue using Census data.” That is not enough for AER.

The reason is not just framing. It is that the current main outcome is a tax category that mechanically includes the new product being legalized. So the immediate reaction is: “Of course the gambling tax bucket went up when a new taxed gambling product was added.” The introduction acknowledges this, but too late and too softly.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Specific ways to make it bigger:

1. **Directly observe the legacy gambling bases that matter most.**  
   The obvious missing outcome is lottery revenue. The title leads with lottery. The paper needs lottery. If the unified national panel is imperfect, then build it anyway from NASPL or state lottery reports, even if the sample is smaller or less balanced. Without that, the cannibalization claim remains strategically weak.

2. **Reframe around net fiscal incidence across gambling products.**  
   A stronger contribution is not “sports betting raises T11,” but “states did or did not offset new sports betting revenue with declines in lottery/casino/racing revenue.” That requires product-level fiscal decomposition.

3. **Exploit heterogeneity that speaks to mechanism.**  
   For example: does cannibalization differ by preexisting lottery dependence, casino presence, mobile vs retail-only legalization, or tax rate structure? That would move the paper from a generic average effect to a substantive market-design paper.

4. **Connect spending substitution to tax efficiency.**  
   The big public finance angle is not merely expansion; it is whether sports betting is a lower-yield substitute for lottery revenue per gambling dollar. If the paper could compare effective tax extraction across gambling products, it would say something larger about optimal sin-tax design.

5. **Use timing to distinguish novelty versus persistent expansion.**  
   If the key story is “short-run expansion, not long-run substitution,” event-time dynamics should be central to the narrative. That would make the paper more than a static post dummy exercise.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

### Closest neighbors

Based on the paper’s own citations and field, the closest neighbors appear to be:

- Coughlin et al. on Pennsylvania VLT displacement / gambling substitution
- Matheson on New York lottery revenue after sports betting launch

- More broadly, papers on sin taxes and product substitution, e.g. Allcott et al. on regressive sin taxes

If I were editing this for placement, I would also expect some engagement with:
- The state lottery public finance literature
- Casino legalization papers
- Broader product-substitution work in taxed vice markets: cigarettes/e-cigarettes, alcohol/cannabis, sugary drinks, etc.

### How should it position itself relative to those neighbors?

It should **build on and reconcile** them, not attack them. The right move is:

- Single-state papers identify mixed or context-specific cannibalization patterns.
- This paper asks whether there is a **systematic national fiscal pattern** in the legalization wave.
- The contribution is to distinguish idiosyncratic state stories from an average equilibrium pattern.

That is a sensible positioning. But it must also concede that its evidence on substitution is partial because it does not directly observe the largest legacy product, lottery.

### Is the paper currently positioned too narrowly or too broadly?

Oddly, both.

- **Too broadly** in title and rhetoric: it implies a definitive answer to lottery cannibalization.
- **Too narrowly** in actual empirical execution: it studies a handful of tax categories and mainly shows the treated gambling bucket rises.

So the paper is mismatched. It markets itself as a broad fiscal cannibalization paper but is operationally a narrower tax-category paper.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?

It seems underconnected to at least four literatures:

1. **State lottery finance and public budgeting**  
   This is the most obvious missing conversation.

2. **Casino legalization / gambling market expansion**  
   There is older work on whether new gambling products expand participation or substitute away from existing products.

3. **Optimal taxation of sin goods with internal and external substitution**  
   This could elevate the paper beyond a niche gambling audience.

4. **Industrial organization / product introduction in addictive or vice markets**  
   Sports betting is a new product introduction in a regulated market with endogenous platform adoption.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?

Not yet. The current conversation is “Did legalization raise some tax categories?” That is too small. The better conversation is:

> What happens to public revenue when governments introduce a new low-friction, app-based vice product into an existing portfolio of taxed gambling goods?

That connects public finance, industrial organization, and regulation. It is a more interesting AER-type conversation than a narrow “sports betting tax collections paper.”

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

States legalized sports betting after *Murphy*, often selling it politically as a budget-friendly new revenue source.

### Tension

That fiscal promise only holds if sports betting expands taxable gambling rather than cannibalizing existing products like lottery tickets and horse racing, possibly at different effective tax rates.

### Resolution

The paper finds that aggregate gambling-related tax revenue rises after legalization and pari-mutuel tax revenue does not fall.

### Implications

The short-run fiscal case for legalization may be stronger than critics claimed; gambling markets may expand rather than merely reshuffle.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the bones of one, but it is not fully coherent because the paper’s evidence does not cleanly resolve the tension it sets up.

The tension is explicitly about **lottery cannibalization** and whether sports betting is “new money.” But the resolution is mainly: “the broad gambling tax bucket went up, and pari-mutuel did not fall.” That is not the same thing. So the story is somewhat a collection of results looking for a stronger claim.

### What story should it be telling instead?

The paper should tell the following story:

1. States introduced a new digital gambling product under the banner of fiscal gain.
2. The core uncertainty is whether new gambling products expand taxable participation or cannibalize legacy gambling tax bases.
3. Using national tax collection data, the paper can credibly speak to **total gambling-related fiscal impact** and one observable legacy product, pari-mutuel wagering.
4. It finds no sign of short-run fiscal offset in the observable categories it studies.
5. Therefore, the evidence supports short-run fiscal expansion, while leaving open unresolved substitution within the lottery/casino components of the broad gambling category.

That is a tighter, more credible arc. Less dramatic, but more publishable.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?

I would lead with:

> After states legalized online sports betting, total state gambling-related tax revenue rose substantially, and the one clearly separate legacy gambling tax base I can observe—pari-mutuel taxes—did not decline.

That is the cleanest defensible finding.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

At a dinner party of economists, this gets **some** lean-in, especially from public finance and applied micro people, because the topic is timely and salient. But the next question comes immediately, and it is a dangerous one:

> “Fine, but what happened to lottery revenue?”

If the answer is “I don’t directly observe it in the main data,” the oxygen leaves the room. That is the strategic problem.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Almost certainly one of these:

- “Does it cannibalize lottery revenue?”
- “Is this just mechanical because the tax bucket includes sports betting itself?”
- “Is the effect just New York/New Jersey?”
- “Is online betting different from retail betting?”
- “Do states with existing casinos or lotteries see less expansion?”

The first two questions strike directly at the paper’s current vulnerability.

### If findings are modest or null, is that interesting?

The pari-mutuel null is potentially interesting, but only if the paper leans into why horse racing is the right test market. As written, it feels like a secondary result carrying too much interpretive weight. “No decline in pari-mutuel taxes” is not enough to establish absence of meaningful cannibalization across gambling markets writ large.

So the null is only interesting if framed as: sports betting did **not** obviously displace one of the most traditional gambling products, contrary to a simple fixed-budget substitution view. But that still leaves the main lottery question unanswered.

That is a real AER-type result: economically meaningful, policy-relevant, and conceptually richer.
