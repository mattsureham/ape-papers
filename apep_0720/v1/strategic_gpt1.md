## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple policy question: when states legalize online sports betting, do they generate genuinely new gambling tax revenue, or do they mostly shift spending away from existing taxed gambling products such as lotteries and horse racing? Busy economists should care because the fiscal case for gambling legalization depends not on gross betting volume, but on whether legalization expands the tax base or merely reshuffles it.

The paper does articulate something close to this pitch in the first two paragraphs, and the opening is stronger than in many field papers. But it is still not sharp enough about the central limitation: the main revenue measure mechanically includes the new sports-betting tax itself, so the reader immediately wonders whether the paper can actually answer the cannibalization question it poses. Right now the intro oversells a broad fiscal test and only later concedes that the main outcome cannot separate new revenue from substitution within the gambling category.

### The pitch the paper should have

After *Murphy v. NCAA*, states justified sports-betting legalization as a fiscal free lunch: a new tax base with little cost to existing gambling revenues. This paper asks whether that claim is true at the state level. Using the staggered rollout of sports betting across U.S. states, I show that total gambling-related tax revenue rises after legalization and that there is no evidence of offsetting declines in pari-mutuel revenue, suggesting that—at least in the short run—legalization expands gambling tax collections rather than simply displacing all existing taxed gambling activity.

That pitch is honest, policy-relevant, and narrower than the current one. It avoids claiming more than the data can support.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper provides national evidence that state sports-betting legalization increased total gambling-related tax revenue and did not produce detectable declines in pari-mutuel tax revenue in the short run.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest papers?

Only partially. The paper does a reasonable job distinguishing itself from single-state case studies, but the differentiation is mostly about scale (“national” rather than “West Virginia” or “Pennsylvania”), not about conceptual advance. That is usually not enough for the AER unless the national design answers a broader question that smaller papers cannot.

The trouble is that the contribution is narrower than the rhetoric suggests. The paper says it tests “fiscal cannibalization,” but its main aggregate gambling tax measure includes the new sports-betting taxes by construction. So relative to the closest papers, the true novelty is not “the first convincing test of whether sports betting cannibalizes existing state gambling revenue”; it is “the first national evidence on whether total state gambling tax collections rise after legalization, with a narrow non-result for pari-mutuel taxes.” That is a materially smaller contribution.

### WORLD question or LITERATURE gap?

The paper is framed mostly as a question about the world, which is good: do new gambling products expand or reshuffle the tax base? That is the right instinct. But the execution drifts toward a literature-gap framing: “single-state papers exist; here is a national staggered DiD.” The world question is stronger and should dominate.

### Could a smart economist explain what is new after reading the intro?

Not cleanly. Right now, a smart economist might say: “It’s a DiD paper on sports-betting legalization and state gambling taxes.” That is not enough. They are unlikely to say, “This paper fundamentally changes how we think about gambling-market substitution,” because the paper does not isolate substitution among the largest incumbent products.

### What would make the contribution bigger?

Most importantly: **bring in disaggregated lottery revenue.**  
That is the obvious margin everyone actually cares about. The intro itself highlights lottery cannibalization, but the paper cannot really test it. If the author could match in lottery revenues from NASPL or state lottery reports—even for a somewhat smaller or less balanced sample—the paper would become much more consequential.

Other ways to make it bigger:

- **Separate online/mobile from retail sports betting.** The economic question is really about low-friction digital access. “Sports betting legalization” is too coarse.
- **Exploit heterogeneity in preexisting gambling portfolios.** Cannibalization should be strongest in lottery-heavy states or states with weak casino sectors. That would turn the paper from “average effect” into “when does substitution happen?”
- **Connect to incidence / public finance.** The deeper issue is whether states are creating new sin-tax revenue or reallocating a regressive tax base across products. That is bigger than gambling per se.
- **Measure net fiscal value, not just tax-category revenue.** If sports betting expands T11 but at much lower effective tax rates than lottery play, fiscal gains may be smaller than gross category growth suggests.

As written, the contribution is competent but modest.

It should **build on** the state case studies, not “attack” them. More importantly, it should position itself as a paper at the intersection of:

- public finance of sin taxes,
- demand substitution across regulated vice goods,

That conversation is much bigger than “sports betting studies.”

Oddly, both.

- **Too narrow** in data and measured outcomes: the actual evidence is basically one broad gambling category plus pari-mutuel.

So the paper is strategically miscalibrated. It needs either broader evidence or narrower claims.

### What literature does it seem unaware of?

It seems underconnected to:

- **sin tax substitution** more generally, including cigarettes/alcohol/cannabis-type product substitution frameworks;
- **tax-base competition and fiscal externalities** across regulated goods;
- **industrial organization of gambling products**, especially product differentiation and market expansion versus business stealing;

The unexpected but potentially high-impact framing is not “another gambling paper,” but “what happens when states introduce a new, digitally delivered sin product into an existing taxed vice portfolio?”

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

States legalized sports betting after *Murphy* and sold it politically as a revenue windfall.

### Tension

That fiscal argument may be wrong if sports betting merely displaces existing taxable gambling, especially lotteries and racing.

### Resolution

Total gambling-related tax revenue rises after legalization, and pari-mutuel revenue does not fall.

### Implications

The short-run fiscal case for legalization may be stronger than critics suggest; at minimum, sports betting does not appear to be pure revenue reshuffling.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

Yes, but it is weaker than it should be because the resolution does not fully answer the tension. The paper sets up a high-stakes substitution question—especially lotteries—but resolves it with evidence on a broad category that mechanically includes the new product and on pari-mutuel taxes, which are probably not the main concern for most economists or policymakers.

So this is not quite “a collection of results looking for a story.” There is a real story. But it is currently the wrong-sized story for the evidence.

### What story should it be telling?

Not: “We test whether sports betting cannibalizes state gambling revenue.”

Better:  
“Using national fiscal data, we show that the introduction of sports betting increases total state gambling tax collections and does not depress one observable incumbent gambling category, pari-mutuel racing, in the short run. This suggests market expansion is quantitatively important, though the key unresolved margin remains lottery substitution.”

That story is more modest, but coherent and credible.

They are somewhat interesting, but not yet AER-interesting. If the paper remains unable to measure lottery substitution, it needs to recast its main finding as one about **aggregate fiscal expansion** rather than a definitive rejection of cannibalization.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

At present, the main gap is a combination of **scope problem** and **framing problem**, with some **novelty problem** underneath.

- **Framing problem:** The paper claims to answer a stronger question than the data allow.
- **Scope problem:** The observed outcomes are too limited for the policy question being posed.
- **Novelty problem:** “National DiD on legalization and tax revenue” is not enough by itself for the AER, especially when the key margin—lottery displacement—is not directly measured.

This is not primarily an econometrics problem. It is a strategic problem about what question the paper can honestly own.

### What is the gap to an AER paper?

An AER paper here would likely do one of the following:

1. **Directly measure substitution across the main incumbent products**—lottery, casino, pari-mutuel, maybe neighboring-state spillovers.
2. **Show a broader conceptual result** about product-line expansion in vice markets and tax-base design.
3. **Use heterogeneity to uncover mechanism**—e.g., why some states see expansion while others see displacement.
4. **Tie the findings to optimal tax policy** in a way that changes how economists think about sin-tax portfolios.

This paper does not yet do any of those at a top-field level.

### Single most impactful piece of advice

**If the author can change only one thing, it should be to bring in direct lottery revenue data and make lottery substitution the core empirical test.**

That is the make-or-break issue. Without that, the paper is a respectable short paper on aggregate gambling tax expansion; with it, the paper could become a serious contribution to public finance and regulation.
