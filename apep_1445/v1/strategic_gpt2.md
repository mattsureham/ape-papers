## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether a public regulatory downgrade does more than reveal poor quality—whether it itself pushes marginal care homes out of the market. Using the sharp threshold that moves English care homes from “Requires Improvement” to “Inadequate,” the paper argues that the label and associated escalation substantially increase the probability of closure, implying that quality disclosure can reduce supply in already thin long-term-care markets.

A busy economist should care because this is not really about care homes per se; it is about a broad policy tradeoff at the heart of regulation: public information and enforcement may improve accountability, but they may also destroy capacity. If true, that matters for health care, education, finance, environmental regulation, and any market where “bad” public labels can trigger exit.

### Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Reasonably, but not sharply enough. The current introduction is competent and intelligible, but it still reads like a clean applied micro paper entering a known literature rather than a paper making a large claim about regulation. It gets to the institutional setting quickly, but the first paragraphs should do more to foreground the general economic question and the stakes.

Right now the intro says, in effect: “care home closures are bad; here is an English threshold; I estimate an effect.” What it should say is: “regulators increasingly use public ratings and categorical sanctions; we know these policies reveal information, but we know much less about whether the labels themselves destroy supply; this paper shows that crossing a regulatory cliff causes exit.”

### The pitch the paper should have

Regulators often sort firms into public categories—pass/fail, investment grade/junk, good school/failing school, adequate/inadequate—and these labels can trigger both consumer responses and regulatory escalation. The central economic question is whether such labels simply identify low-quality providers or whether they themselves cause market exit, especially in sectors where supply is already scarce. Using the sharp threshold that places English care homes into “Special Measures,” this paper shows that barely crossing into the “Inadequate” category sharply raises closure risk, implying that categorical regulatory disclosure can contract supply as well as inform consumers.

That is the first-paragraph story. The second paragraph should then say: why this setting is unusually powerful, what the main result is, and why it changes how we think about disclosure design.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### Contribution in one sentence

The paper’s contribution is to provide causal evidence that crossing a public regulatory quality threshold increases provider exit, showing that categorical disclosure and enforcement can have supply-destroying effects beyond underlying quality differences.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?

Only partially. The paper names adjacent literatures, but the differentiation is still mostly “those papers are observational / different setting / not this exact threshold.” That is not enough for AER-level positioning. The paper needs a cleaner statement of what the field currently believes and how this paper updates that belief.

At present, the reader could reasonably file this as:
- another ratings/disclosure paper,
- another reduced-form paper on long-term-care markets,
- another threshold design around a regulatory rule.

The contribution becomes stronger if differentiated as:
1. prior disclosure papers focus mainly on demand reallocation, prices, quality, or market shares;
2. prior long-term-care regulation papers relate quality to exit but do not isolate the causal effect of the label/regime itself;
3. this paper identifies an overlooked margin: disclosure plus regulatory escalation can eliminate providers, not just discipline them.

That is a world question, not just a gap claim.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a literature gap?

Mixed, leaning too much toward literature-gap framing. The strongest parts are world-facing: “do public labels cause closure?” The weaker parts lapse into literature language: “first causal evidence,” “adds to three literatures,” “uniquely sharp cliff.” For AER, the paper should more consistently frame the issue as a general fact about how regulation operates in capacity-constrained service markets.

### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain what’s new?

Right now they could say: “It’s an RD paper showing that getting the worst care-home rating in England raises closure risk.” That is clear, but still sounds narrow.

What you want them to say is: “It shows that categorical regulatory labels can themselves shut down providers, so disclosure rules have supply-side effects, not just information benefits.” That is much bigger.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Specific ways to enlarge it:

1. **Mechanisms.** The current paper gestures at demand, compliance costs, and strategic exit but cannot distinguish them. Even one credible mechanism margin would materially upgrade the paper. Occupancy, admissions, staffing, finances, enforcement actions, or time-to-exit would all help.

2. **Incidence and welfare framing.** Not just “closures rise,” but who bears the cost? Residents? local markets? thin-supply areas? If closures are concentrated where alternatives are scarce, the paper becomes much more important.

3. **Market-level outcomes.** AER-level payoff would be stronger if the paper showed local bed supply, prices, distance to nearest alternative, or spillovers to neighboring homes. “Labels destroy supply” is bigger than “some homes close.”

4. **General framing beyond long-term care.** The paper needs to make this a paper about categorical regulation. The care-home setting is the vehicle, not the destination.

5. **Dynamic consequences.** If the paper could show persistence—e.g., capacity is not replaced—then the policy stakes rise a lot.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Regulators rate providers publicly, and those ratings affect both reputation and enforcement. In long-term care, poor ratings may be especially consequential because residents are vulnerable and supply is limited.

### Tension

A bad label may help consumers avoid low-quality providers, but it may also force out marginal providers and worsen capacity shortages. We typically cannot tell whether subsequent closures are caused by the underlying poor quality or by the label and escalation itself.

### Resolution

Using the threshold that triggers “Inadequate” status and Special Measures in England, the paper finds that crossing the line substantially raises closure risk.

### Implications

Regulatory disclosure is not just information policy; it is also market-structure policy. Designing thresholds and escalation regimes requires weighing consumer information benefits against the possibility of capacity destruction.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

Yes, but only in a serviceable way. The ingredients are all there; the execution is not yet fully shaped.

The main weakness is that the paper’s narrative arc peaks too early and then flattens. After the result, the paper does not deepen into a more consequential payoff. The “so what” is asserted—closures displace vulnerable residents and reduce supply—but not developed enough. The paper ends where an AER paper would want to begin the final act.

### If it is a collection of results looking for a story, what story should it be telling?

Not “the Inadequate label affects closure.”

Instead:

- **Setup:** modern regulation relies on public categorical labels;
- **Tension:** these labels can improve information but may trigger nonlinear sanctions and consumer flight;
- **Resolution:** in long-term care, the cliff causes borderline providers to exit;
- **Implications:** regulatory categories can reduce service capacity in scarce markets, so disclosure design changes equilibrium supply.

That is a much stronger story.
