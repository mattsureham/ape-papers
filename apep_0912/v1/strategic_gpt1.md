## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple, policy-relevant question: when a dominant exporter suddenly shuts off supply, how much of that shock shows up in consumer prices around the world? Using India’s 2023 non-basmati rice export ban and retail price data from 1,530 markets in 59 countries, the paper argues that price pass-through was sizable and sharply increasing in countries’ pre-ban dependence on Indian rice.

A busy economist should care because this is really about the incidence of trade fragmentation: whether concentration in global supply chains turns a national policy into foreign consumer inflation. The paper’s current ingredients are strong, but the pitch is still narrower than it should be.

**Does the paper articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Partly. The opening has urgency and scale, which is good. But the second paragraph slips too quickly into “there is no market-level evidence; this paper fills that gap,” which is a literature-gap frame rather than a world-question frame. For AER, the paper should open with the broader economic question first, then present India as an unusually clean test case.

**What the first two paragraphs should say instead:**

> Global food markets are increasingly exposed to unilateral export restrictions by large suppliers. Yet we still know surprisingly little about how shocks to concentrated trade relationships transmit to the prices households actually pay for staple foods—and whether import diversification meaningfully insulates consumers from these shocks.
>
> This paper studies that question using India’s sudden July 2023 ban on non-basmati white rice exports, which removed roughly 40 percent of global rice trade overnight. Combining retail food prices from 1,530 markets in 59 countries with countries’ pre-ban dependence on Indian rice, I show that the ban passed through strongly to local consumer prices in highly exposed countries, but had essentially no effect in diversified importers. The core message is that global supply concentration translates directly into local cost-of-living risk.

That is the pitch the paper should own.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution in one sentence:**  
The paper shows that a sudden export ban by a dominant supplier can generate substantial retail-price pass-through abroad, and that this pass-through is strongly mediated by pre-existing bilateral import dependence.

### Evaluation

**Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?**  
Not clearly enough. The paper names some relevant work, but the differentiation is still mostly “I use more granular data” and “I estimate retail rather than aggregate prices.” That is real, but not by itself a top-journal contribution. The paper needs to distinguish itself conceptually, not just in level of aggregation.

The sharper differentiation is:

- prior work mainly studies export restrictions through national or global price indices;
- this paper studies **cross-country heterogeneity in consumer incidence** from one large, discrete shock;
- the new object is not merely “a price effect,” but **how trade concentration maps into retail vulnerability**.

That is a stronger, more memorable contribution.

**Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a literature gap?**  
Too much the latter. The paper repeatedly says no one has done this at market level. That is not what will make people care. The stronger framing is about the world: countries have become exposed to concentrated supply chains, and we need to know whether diversification actually buys insurance.

**Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain what is new?**  
At the moment, they might say: “It’s a DiD paper on India’s rice export ban using WFP prices.” That is not enough. They should instead be able to say: “It shows that global supply concentration creates local inflation exposure, and quantifies how bilateral dependence governs pass-through to consumers.” The current introduction does not force that takeaway hard enough.

**What would make this contribution bigger?**  
Specific possibilities:

1. **Tie prices to household exposure.**  
   Right now the endpoint is retail rice prices. Bigger would be: what happened to the cost of the staple basket, caloric purchasing power, or food affordability in highly exposed countries? AER readers will care more about consumer welfare incidence than about one commodity’s price alone.

2. **Say more about substitution and insulation.**  
   If the paper can show whether consumers substitute to other staples, or whether those prices also move in exposed places, the contribution becomes about broader food-market adjustment, not just rice pass-through.

3. **Exploit the “within-rice” margin.**  
   The institutional setup mentions exempted varieties like basmati/parboiled. If the data can separate varieties even imperfectly, that would make the story much bigger: it becomes a cleaner test of product-level trade shock transmission, not just rice vs. other staples.

4. **Generalize from India to a broader object.**  
   The paper should explicitly argue that this episode is a window into the economics of export restrictions, trade fragmentation, and concentration risk more generally—not just an interesting case study about rice.

At present, the contribution is solid but still too easy to summarize as “granular evidence on a known phenomenon.”

This is not a revisionist paper showing the prior literature is wrong. It is better cast as taking a large, well-understood macro policy issue and showing where incidence appears on the ground: in retail markets, heterogeneously by bilateral dependence.

The right line is not “aggregate studies mask enormous heterogeneity” in a vaguely adversarial way. It is: “existing work established that export restrictions matter at aggregate level; this paper shows how that effect is transmitted to consumers and disciplined by trade concentration.”

Right now it is **too narrowly positioned in the food-security/export-ban niche**, while also gesturing too broadly at “trade fragmentation” without really cashing that out. It needs a clearer audience.

The best audience is not just agricultural economists or food-policy economists. It is economists interested in:
- international trade and fragmentation,
- inflation transmission,

1. **Trade concentration and resilience / supply-chain fragility.**  
   The paper invokes fragmentation, but lightly. It should connect more directly to recent work on concentration, resilience, and geopolitical trade risk.

2. **Inflation incidence / cost of living.**  
   If this is about consumer prices in staple markets, there is a strong bridge to inflation and cost-of-living measurement in developing countries.

3. **Commodity-market transmission and consumer welfare.**  
   There is likely useful adjacent work in development and agricultural economics on staple-food substitution, caloric intake, and welfare pass-through that is not yet part of the conversation.

4. **Sanctions / embargo / large supplier shock literatures.**

Not quite. The current conversation is “export bans affect food prices.” True, but familiar.  
The more consequential conversation is: **When global supply chains are concentrated, how exposed are local consumers to unilateral policy shocks, and how much insurance does diversification provide?**

That is the conversation with AER potential.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Global food trade is concentrated in a few large suppliers, and governments periodically impose export restrictions to stabilize domestic prices. Policymakers talk about food security and diversification, but there is limited evidence on how these shocks reach retail markets abroad.

### Tension
We know export restrictions can move aggregate prices, but we do not know enough about consumer-level pass-through or how exposure depends on bilateral trade concentration. Does a shock like India’s rice ban show up materially in what households pay, or is it absorbed upstream?

### Resolution
The paper finds that local retail rice prices rise meaningfully after the ban in countries that were more dependent on Indian rice, with little effect in low-dependence countries.

### Implications
Import diversification appears to provide insurance against foreign export restrictions; concentrated sourcing creates direct cost-of-living vulnerability. This matters for trade policy, food security strategy, and how economists think about the incidence of fragmentation.

### Does the paper currently have a clear narrative arc?

**Serviceable, but not yet sharp enough.** The paper has the raw materials for a strong narrative, but it still reads somewhat like a well-executed empirical note: event, data, estimate, robustness, implication. The story is present, but the paper has not fully elevated it from “one export ban caused price increases” to “this episode reveals a general economic principle about concentration and pass-through.”

At moments, it also feels like a collection of regression outputs looking for a larger claim. The strongest story available is:

> The world has built concentrated staple-food supply chains. India’s rice ban was an unexpected stress test. The paper measures how much of that global shock reached local consumers and shows that bilateral diversification is the key margin of resilience.

That story should organize the entire paper.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at dinner?
“India shut off roughly 40 percent of global rice trade overnight, and retail rice prices jumped by about 10 percent in the countries most dependent on Indian imports—while diversified importers saw essentially nothing.”

That is a good lead fact. People will lean in.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
They would lean in initially, because the shock is big and intuitive. But the second sentence matters. If you then say “I estimate this in a triple-difference design with market-by-time fixed effects,” you lose them. If you say “this is evidence that concentrated trade relationships create direct local inflation risk,” you keep them.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Likely one of these:
- Did households actually become worse off, or did they substitute away from rice?
- Why didn’t other exporters fill the gap more quickly?
- Is this about rice, or about concentrated supply chains more generally?
- What should governments do—diversify suppliers, stockpile, or subsidize consumers?

These are revealing. The paper currently answers only the first-order price question, not the broader welfare or adjustment questions that naturally arise.

### If findings are modest, is that a problem?
The magnitude here is not trivial. A 10 percent retail staple price increase is meaningful. The issue is not that the effect is too small; it is that the paper has not yet fully translated the estimate into an economically big statement. It should do more to convey why a 7–10 percent change in a staple matters for household budgets and food security.
