## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple, important question: when a dominant exporter suddenly shuts off supply, how much of that shock reaches consumers in other countries? Using India’s 2023 ban on non-basmati rice exports and market-level retail prices from 59 countries, the paper argues that countries more dependent on Indian rice saw meaningfully larger increases in local rice prices, while diversified importers were largely insulated.

Busy economists should care because this is not just a paper about rice. It is about how trade policy shocks propagate through consumer prices, how concentrated global supply chains create vulnerability, and whether import diversification actually protects households from foreign policy risk.

**Does the paper articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Not quite. The current opening is vivid and energetic, but it leans too hard into dramatic language (“supply amputation”) and too quickly pivots to a literature gap. The core idea is stronger than the current framing: this is an opportunity to learn about the transmission of geopolitical/trade disruptions into household cost of living. That should be front and center.

**What the first two paragraphs should say instead:**

> In July 2023, India—by far the world’s largest rice exporter—abruptly banned exports of non-basmati white rice, removing a large share of globally traded rice from world markets overnight. For many low- and middle-income countries, rice is a core consumption good, so the ban created an unusually sharp test of a first-order economic question: when a major exporter restricts trade, how much of that shock is passed through to the retail prices consumers actually pay?
>
> This paper measures that pass-through using retail food prices from more than 1,500 markets in 59 countries. I show that the retail price response was strongly increasing in countries’ pre-ban dependence on Indian rice imports: highly dependent countries experienced sizable rice price increases relative to other staples, while more diversified importers saw little to no effect. The broader lesson is that exposure to concentrated foreign suppliers is a consumer-price vulnerability, and diversification of import sources can materially insulate households from external policy shocks.

That is the pitch the paper should have.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution in one sentence:**  
The paper provides cross-country, market-level evidence that the retail price pass-through of a major export ban depends sharply on pre-existing import dependence, implying that supply-chain concentration translates into consumer-price vulnerability.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest papers?
Only partly. The paper says prior work uses aggregate national price indices or smaller wholesale samples, and this paper uses many retail markets. That is a real distinction, but currently it risks sounding like a data upgrade rather than a conceptual contribution. The differentiation needs to be sharper along two dimensions:

1. **Unit of observation:** retail markets rather than national indices or wholesale prices.  
2. **Economic object of interest:** heterogeneity in pass-through by bilateral import dependence, not just average price effects.

Right now the introduction gestures at both, but it does not forcefully say: *existing work asks whether export restrictions raise prices on average; this paper asks who gets hit, through what exposure, and whether diversification insures consumers.*

### World question or literature-gap question?
Too much “fills a gap,” not enough “answers a big question about the world.” The stronger version is:

- The world question: *How vulnerable are local consumer prices to concentrated foreign supply?*
- The paper’s answer: *Vulnerability is highly elastic to supplier dependence.*

That is much better than “no study has traced pass-through from a single export ban to retail prices across thousands of markets.”

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?
At present, they might say: “It’s a DiD on India’s rice export ban using WFP market prices.” That is not enough.  
You want them to say: “It shows that a single exporter’s policy can move retail staple prices around the world, but only where countries are exposed through concentrated import dependence; diversification looks like insurance.”

That second version is memorable. The current intro does not yet make it sufficiently memorable.

### What would make the contribution bigger?
Several options:

- **Stronger welfare or household angle:** connect price changes to food expenditure shares, caloric dependence on rice, or a back-of-the-envelope real-income effect. Right now “meaningful reduction in real purchasing power” is asserted, not demonstrated.
- **Comparison to other shocks:** show whether this pass-through is large relative to exchange-rate pass-through, shipping shocks, or other food commodity shocks. That would help economists calibrate importance.
- **Mechanism via substitution or quality margins:** do consumers substitute into other staples, and do those prices move too? That would elevate the paper from “rice prices went up” to “food systems transmit shocks through substitution and expenditure reallocation.”
- **Broader framing around supply-chain concentration:** the paper should connect this event to a general economic phenomenon, not just one ban in one commodity.

The biggest way to make it feel larger is to treat the India ban as a natural stress test of global food-system concentration, not as a one-off export-ban case study.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Global food trade is highly concentrated in a few suppliers. India dominates global rice exports, and many countries rely heavily on it for a staple consumed by billions.

### Tension
We know export restrictions can move world prices, but it is much less clear how much these shocks reach local retail markets, how quickly, and which countries are most exposed. Aggregate evidence misses the consumer-facing incidence and the role of dependence.

### Resolution
The paper finds that retail rice prices rose more in countries more dependent on Indian rice after the ban, with especially large effects in highly dependent importers and little effect in diversified ones.

### Implications
Import dependence on a dominant foreign supplier is not just a trade pattern; it is a source of consumer-price risk. Diversification may serve as insurance against foreign policy shocks in essential goods.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
**Serviceable, but not yet strong.**  
There is a story here, but the manuscript still reads too much like a competent event-study paper with lots of estimates, not like a paper built around a single big economic idea.

The missing ingredient is a sharper statement of the paper’s main claim:

> The policy relevance is not that India banned rice; it is that concentrated import dependence determines how foreign trade-policy shocks hit domestic consumers.

Once that is the spine, the rest of the paper can line up behind it:
- event: India ban
- exposure: import dependence
- outcome: retail staple prices
- implication: diversification as price-risk management

Right now the paper sometimes drifts into “collection of results” mode: main effect, event study, heterogeneity, robustness, attenuation after reversal. Those are ingredients, not the story.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?
“India cut off a huge chunk of globally traded rice overnight, and in countries that depended heavily on Indian imports, retail rice prices jumped by about 10 percent relative to other staples.”

That is a good opening fact.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
They would **lean in initially**, because the event is dramatic and intuitive. But the follow-through depends on whether the presenter can quickly answer: “Why does this teach us something general?”

If the answer is merely “because I have more granular data than previous papers,” attention will fade.  
If the answer is “because it quantifies how supply concentration abroad becomes consumer-price risk at home,” attention will hold.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Probably one of these:
- “Is this just rice, or a general lesson about concentrated trade relationships?”
- “How much does this matter for household welfare?”
- “Did consumers substitute into other staples?”
- “Was the effect mostly international pass-through or local market power/distribution?”
- “Would this replicate for wheat, palm oil, fertilizer, etc.?”

Those are exactly the questions the paper should anticipate in the framing, even if it cannot answer all of them empirically.

### If findings are modest: is the modesty itself interesting?
The effect size is not tiny, but it is also not explosively large given the dramatic event. That creates a subtle communication problem: the event sounds massive, yet the headline estimate is “around 10 percent in highly dependent countries.” The paper needs to help readers interpret that. Is that a lot? For staple-food budgets in poor countries, yes. Relative to commodity shocks, perhaps yes. Relative to removing 40% of global trade, maybe surprisingly incomplete.

That incompleteness is itself interesting. It hints at adjustment through inventories, substitution across exporters, and imperfect retail pass-through. The paper should lean into that. Otherwise some readers may think: “For such a huge shock, only 10%?”  
That is a strategic mistake. The right message is: *even a shock this large passes through incompletely on average, but very unequally—and dependence is the key margin.*

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The gap is mostly **framing plus ambition**, with some **scope**.

This is not obviously a bad paper. It is a plausible, clean, topical empirical study. But in current form it feels like a solid field-journal paper on a salient event, not yet an AER paper.

### What is the main problem?

#### 1. Framing problem
The science may be adequate, but the story is still too close to:
“Here is a policy shock, and here is a DiD estimate.”

For AER, it needs to become:
“This paper shows how concentrated international supply relationships map into consumer-price exposure, using the India rice ban as a rare clean stress test.”

That reframing makes it about a general economic phenomenon.

#### 2. Scope problem
The current paper has one outcome: retail rice prices relative to controls. That is not quite enough to carry a top-general-interest paper unless the broader implications are made much more concrete. It needs either:
- stronger welfare interpretation,
- stronger evidence on substitution/spillovers,
- or stronger comparative perspective showing why this event is unusually informative.

#### 3. Ambition problem
The paper is competent but safe. It documents pass-through. Fine. But top-field readers will ask: what belief changes? The answer should be something like:
- exposure to dominant-supplier risk matters a lot for household prices,
- and diversification substantially insures consumers.

Right now that claim is present, but not developed enough to feel like a major contribution.

### Is it a novelty problem?
Not exactly. The event is novel enough, and the data are interesting. The risk is not “already done,” but “interesting case study without enough conceptual reach.”

### Single most impactful advice
**Reframe the paper around supply-chain concentration and consumer-price exposure—not around being the first market-level study of one export ban.**

That one change would improve the introduction, literature positioning, narrative arc, and significance all at once.
