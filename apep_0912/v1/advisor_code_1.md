**Summary**  
Using 329,030 market-commodity-month observations from the WFP Food Price Monitoring system, the paper studies how India’s July 2023 ban on non-basmati rice exports propagated to retail rice prices across 59 countries. A within-market, across-commodity difference-in-differences design with continuous treatment intensity (pre-ban Indian import share) shows that higher import dependence predicts a significant differential rice price increase relative to other staples (13.1 percentage points per standard deviation in India share). The results are robust to clustering, trimming, trends, and leave-one-country-out checks and are concentrated in highly dependent countries.

**Essential Points**

1. **Clarify the estimating equation and the role of lower-order interactions.** Equation (1) reports only the triple interaction, yet the empirical tables show specifications with Rice×Post and (implicitly) its interaction with India share. For interpretation and to reassure readers about collinearity / partialling issues, the paper should clearly state all covariates that are included. In particular, explain whether Rice×Post and IndiaShare main effects (and the Rice×IndiaShare interaction) are identified given the fixed effects, and why the triple interaction alone identifies the treatment effect. If some of these terms are subsumed by the fixed effects, say so explicitly; if not, report their coefficients or note that they are absorbed. Without this clarity, it is hard to assess whether the reported 0.131 estimate reflects the marginal effect of the ban conditional on the treatment intensity or an omitted-variable bias.

2. **Strengthen the evidence that the control commodities are unaffected and that the remaining variation identifies the rice-specific shock.** The identifying assumption is that, conditional on market×time shocks, rice would have tracked the control staples absent the ban. However, several of the controls (e.g., wheat, maize) are themselves traded commodities whose prices could respond to global shocks contemporaneous with the ban, particularly in the same regions. The paper should present more direct evidence on this point—e.g., show event-study plots for selected control commodities, or re-estimate the treatment using an alternative set of controls (e.g., entirely domestically produced roots). This would make it more convincing that the estimated differential price movement indeed isolates the Indian-ban shock rather than, say, a regional weather event or demand shift that affected cereals generally.

3. **Exploit the promised within-rice and reversal/placebo tests.** The manifest mentions within-rice placebos (basmati vs non-basmati) and the partial lifting (October 2024) as falsification devices. Neither appears in the empirical tables. Without them, the paper relies solely on the rice vs non-rice contrast and the dynamic event study for identification. Adding the within-rice placebo would further isolate the effect on the banned variety; adding an analysis of the post-October 2024 reversal, even if descriptive (e.g., do rice prices moderate after the partial lifting in high-dependence markets?), would solidify the causal narrative and demonstrate the mechanism. At minimum, the authors should report additional robustness checks applying the same DiD logic to basmati (as a negative treatment) and the reversal period.

**Suggestions**

1. **Enrich the data description and summary statistics.** Table 1 reports means and standard deviations but omits counts of rice vs control observations, the time span, and the distribution of India import shares. A panel that separates rice and control prices by region and dependence decile would help readers understand sample balance. Providing a figure that maps the treatment intensity (India import share) across countries would also make the dose-response story more concrete.

2. **Report more results from the event study and heterogeneity sections.** The event-study table currently displays quarterly estimates with an oddly formatted joint F-test row whose statistic and p-value look inconsistent. Consider plotting the event study for rice vs control separately for high- and low-dependence countries to illustrate the heterogeneity in timing. In the heterogeneity table, the difference column is helpful, but adding a formal test for a monotonic gradient (e.g., polynomial in India share or bins) would further substantiate the dose-response claim.

3. **Highlight the role (or absence) of substitution effects.** If rice prices rose relative to other staples, households may have switched consumption to those other staples, thereby muting welfare losses. While the paper rightly limits itself to price pass-through, it could briefly document whether there is any suggestive evidence of substitution in the WFP data (e.g., persistent price changes in some control commodities in high-dependence markets) or argue why substitution is unlikely over this short window. This would contextualize the magnitude of the price shock.

4. **Address potential measurement error in the treatment intensity.** Using 2020–2022 FAO trade averages may introduce attenuation if import patterns changed right before the ban. The authors might check the robustness of the key result to weighting the treatment with 2023 import data (if available) or using an instrument for dependence (e.g., pre-ban historical ties). At the very least, discuss whether import shares were stable over time and, if not, whether that would bias the estimate toward zero.

5. **Discuss the external validity and policy implications with nuance.** The concluding paragraphs rightly emphasize import diversification, but the paper could elaborate on whether the estimated price pass-through is likely to generalize to other commodities or policy interventions. For example, would similar effects arise from an exporter’s subsidy or from export restrictions with longer notice? Briefly situating the findings within the broader literature on food security policy would help practitioners translate the results.

6. **Ensure reproducibility.** The acknowledgements note that the paper was autonomously generated. To align with best practices, consider releasing (or citing) the code used for data processing and estimation, or at least providing sufficient detail (e.g., variable construction, sample selection criteria) so that another researcher could replicate the results. This is especially important given the novel combination of WFP and FAO data.

7. **Clarify the interpretation of the coefficients.** The text translates the 0.131 estimate into a 13.1% rise going from zero to full dependence, but the regression interacts with a continuous share and presumably normalizes to a standard deviation. Including a short derivation or an example (as in the paper’s paragraph on the “average highly-dependent country”) is helpful, but to avoid confusion, explicitly state the underlying scaling—does the coefficient represent the change per unit share, per SD, or something else? Consistency across tables and text will aid comprehension.

Overall, the paper addresses a timely question with novel data and a transparent design. Addressing the issues above would strengthen the identification, clarify the causal interpretation, and enhance the paper’s contribution to the literature on food security and trade shocks.
