## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a fundamental question about the unintended consequences of government intervention: Can the act of labeling a neighborhood as "distressed" to provide aid actually make it less valuable? Using a sharp spatial RDD at the boundaries of France’s *Quartiers Prioritaires*, the author finds that properties inside the zones sell for 13–15% less than those just meters away, despite those properties receiving massive tax breaks and urban renewal subsidies.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this pitch excellently. The first two paragraphs of the introduction move from the global policy tradeoff (targeting vs. stigma) to the specific French setting. It is one of the clearer "problem-to-setting" transitions I have seen recently. 

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution:** The paper provides the first quasi-experimental evidence that the informational stigma of a "poverty" label can more than offset substantial fiscal subsidies in the housing market.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It is well-differentiated. While Mayer et al. (2017) look at firms/employment in this context, the capitalization into land values is the "sufficient statistic" for welfare that this literature has lacked.
*   **World vs. Literature:** It frames the question as one about the **World** (the design of place-based policy).
*   **Clarity:** A smart economist would immediately understand the "Stigma > Subsidy" takeaway. 
*   **What would make it bigger?** To reach the AER’s highest tier, the paper needs to move beyond "net effect." It needs to decompose *why* the discount is 15%. Is it because of the label itself (stigma), or because the label triggers anticipation of future bad outcomes (crime, school decline)? A mechanism test (e.g., looking at search data or buyer characteristics) would make this a "big" paper.

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

The paper sits at the intersection of Urban Economics and Public Finance.

*   **Closest Neighbors:** Oates (1969) on capitalization; Cellini et al. (2010) on school bonds/spatial RDD; Galster (2017) on neighborhood stigma; Mayer et al. (2017) on French enterprise zones.
*   **Positioning:** Currently, it builds on them. It should arguably "attack" the standard Tiebout/Oates model more aggressively by highlighting how information externalities (stigma) are a missing primitive in those models.
*   **Missing Lit:** It should speak more to the "Social Signalling" literature in behavioral economics. If I buy a house in a "priority zone," what does that signal to my peers? The housing market is a status market.

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

*   **Setup:** Governments want to help poor areas with cash.
*   **Tension:** To give cash, you must point at them. Pointing at them tells the market they are "bad."
*   **Resolution:** The "pointing" (stigma) hurts the price more than the "cash" (subsidy) helps it.
*   **Implications:** Don't use visible geographic boundaries; use person-based or "stealth" subsidies.

**Evaluation:** The narrative arc is very strong. It is not a collection of results; it is a "whodunnit" where Stigma is the killer.

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

*   **Lead Fact:** "France gave these neighborhoods a 30% property tax cut and prices *fell* 15%."
*   **Reaction:** People will lean in. It's a counter-intuitive result that violates the "no free lunch" intuition of tax capitalization.
*   **Follow-up:** "Is it just because the houses are uglier?" (The RDD handles this, but the dinner party will ask it).

---

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

*   **Front-loading:** The paper is well-structured.
*   **Appendix:** The bandwidth sensitivity (Table 4) is important but could be summarized in one figure to save space for more mechanism discussion.
*   **Mechanism Section:** This is the weakest link structurally. The "Discussion" (Section 6) needs to be a "Mechanisms" section with more data. Do we see a drop in listing views? Do we see a change in the type of people moving in?

---

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The gap is **Mechanism**. 

Right now, the paper proves the "stigma" channel exists by process of elimination (the fiscal channel is positive, the net is negative, so something else must be very negative). To be a "slam dunk" AER paper, the author needs to find a way to measure the "something else" directly.

**Single biggest piece of advice:** Use the 2024 boundary revision mentioned on page 4. Even if it’s too early for full price data, can you show that when a neighborhood *loses* the label, its prices recover faster than the loss of the subsidy would suggest? A "Difference-in-Discontinuities" around the 2024 change would elevate this from a great empirical note to a landmark paper.
