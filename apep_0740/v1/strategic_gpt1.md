## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple, policy-relevant question: when governments label a neighborhood as disadvantaged and attach benefits to that label, do markets value the subsidies or punish the stigma? Using sharp boundaries around France’s priority neighborhoods, the paper argues that homes just inside designated areas sell for substantially less than homes just outside, suggesting that the negative signal from official designation dominates the positive value of the policy bundle.

A busy economist should care because this is a broad question about place-based policy design, not just about French urban geography: can targeting aid to places backfire by making disadvantage more salient?

### Does the paper articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Mostly yes, but not optimally. The current introduction gets close, yet it muddies the most important distinction in the paper: is this about **stigma from official labeling**, or about the **net capitalization of a bundled place-based policy**? Right now it wants both, and that weakens the pitch. The paper should decide which is the headline and which is the interpretation.

### The pitch the paper should have

> Governments increasingly target struggling neighborhoods with tax breaks, subsidies, and public investment. But targeting requires publicly labeling places as poor, and that label may itself depress demand. This paper studies that tradeoff in France’s priority neighborhood program and shows that, despite substantial fiscal benefits, properties just inside designated neighborhoods sell for markedly less than properties just outside.
>
> The broader implication is that place-based policy can impose a “designation cost”: the public signal of disadvantage may offset or even overwhelm the value of the resources attached to the designation. That is a first-order issue for how governments design and communicate spatially targeted programs.

That is the AER-facing version. It is about the world: governments target places, labels carry information, markets capitalize both.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper’s contribution is to show that official disadvantaged-neighborhood designation in France appears to reduce nearby housing values on net, implying that the informational or stigma costs of designation can dominate the fiscal benefits of place-based aid.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest papers?

Not clearly enough. The introduction says “first spatial RDD estimate of QPV designation effects on property values,” which is technically a contribution, but not an AER contribution. “First RDD on this French program” is a field-journal claim unless it opens a broader economic question. The real contribution is not the estimator; it is the claim that **visible place-based targeting can lower asset values despite subsidies**.

The paper needs to differentiate itself from at least three adjacent classes of work:

1. **French place-based policy evaluations** studying firm creation, employment, or urban renewal.
2. **Capitalization papers** on local public goods and tax incentives.
3. **Neighborhood stigma / labeling papers** in urban economics, sociology, and public policy.

At present, it gestures at all three but does not sharply explain how it changes what we know relative to each.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a gap in a literature?

It starts with a world question, which is good, but then retreats into “first spatial RDD estimate of QPV.” That is a literature-gap frame, and a small one. The world question is much stronger: **when governments visibly target distressed places, what gets capitalized—the subsidy or the stigma?**

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the intro?

Right now, maybe not cleanly. They would probably say: “It’s a spatial RD on French priority neighborhoods showing lower housing prices inside the boundary.” That is competent, but it still sounds like “another RD paper about a place-based policy.”

What they should be able to say is: “This paper shows that the public designation of neighborhood disadvantage can reduce property values even when the designation comes with sizable tax and investment benefits.” That is memorable.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Several possibilities:

- **Stronger mechanism distinction.** The paper currently wants to call the result a “designation discount,” which implies labeling/stigma, but the actual treatment is a bundle of taxes, VAT, business incentives, renewal eligibility, and label. If the paper wants a big contribution, it needs to more carefully frame the finding as a **net capitalization result** and treat stigma as an interpretation, not as the result itself. Alternatively, if it wants to claim stigma, it needs evidence that isolates the signaling channel.
- **Exploit the 2024 boundary revision.** Even suggestive evidence here would enlarge the paper a lot. A before/after boundary-change design would move the paper closer to the question people really care about: what happens when the label changes?
- **Use outcomes that speak directly to who is valuing what.** Heterogeneity by new construction, investor purchases, owner-occupier segments, or local market tightness would make the result more economically interpretable.
- **Compare to areas just outside that receive some overlapping benefits.** The VAT spillover within 300 meters is mentioned but underused. That could be part of a more nuanced story: when some fiscal benefits spill across the line but the label does not, what remains is especially informative.
- **Widen the implication beyond France.** The introduction should connect to enterprise zones, Opportunity Zones, school accountability labels, environmental hazard designation, “distressed” municipality status, etc. The underlying question is about public categorization and market response.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Governments use place-based programs to help distressed neighborhoods. These programs often bundle subsidies, tax relief, and public investment.

### Tension

To target places, governments must identify them publicly. That identification can reveal or amplify negative information about the place. So a basic tension arises: the same designation that delivers benefits may also reduce demand.

### Resolution

In France’s QPV system, housing prices just inside designated zones are lower than just outside, despite substantial benefits attached to designation. The market appears to value the signal negatively on net.

### Implications

If this interpretation is right, policymakers should worry not just about how much aid to send to neighborhoods, but how the targeting architecture itself affects neighborhood reputation, investment, and resident wealth.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

Serviceable, but not fully coherent. The central narrative is there, yet the paper keeps slipping between three possible stories:

1. **A property-value paper about QPV.**
2. **A place-based policy paper about net capitalization.**
3. **A stigma paper about official labeling.**

These are related, but they are not identical. Right now the paper presents a collection of results under all three banners, and that creates slippage.

### What story should it be telling?

The cleanest story is:

> QPV designation is a bundled intervention that both transfers resources and conveys information. House prices reveal the market’s valuation of that bundle. The surprising fact is that the net valuation is strongly negative, which implies that the negative components of designation—stigma, expectations, reputation, or regulatory concerns—outweigh the subsidies.

That story is disciplined. It does not oversell mechanism, but it still has a punchline.

If the author insists on “stigma,” then the paper needs to become a mechanism paper. In current form, it is better as a **net capitalization of visible place-targeting** paper.
