**Idea Fidelity**

The submitted paper adheres closely to the manifest idea. It studies the 2015 QPV boundaries, exploits the spatial RDD comparing transactions just inside versus just outside, uses the DVF geolocalized data, and focuses on the capitalization (discount) implied by the policy bundle versus stigma. Key elements from the proposal—sharp algorithmic boundaries, large sample within 500 meters, and the novel application to property prices—are all pursued. The promise to consider future rezoning as a source of temporal variation is only mentioned in passing (Section 3) but not empirically exploited; expanding on that in future work would further align with the original research agenda.

**Summary**

The paper uses a spatial regression discontinuity at the 1,362 QPV boundaries to estimate the capitalization of France’s place-based policy bundle into property prices. It finds that properties just inside the boundary transact for roughly 13–15 percent less per square meter than those just outside, suggesting that the negative stigma effect overwhelms the fiscal incentives embedded in the policy. The result is robust across bandwidths, property types, and specifications with boundary and time fixed effects.

**Essential Points**

1. **Covariate discontinuities remain large and raise identification concerns.** Table 3 reports statistically significant jumps in surface area, number of rooms, apartment share, and transaction price at the boundary. While the paper frames these as “expected” because boundaries are drawn on income, the size of these jumps implies that the treated and control units are not exchangeable without richer controls. The nonparametric estimate (−15.4 percent) cannot be interpreted as the causal effect if these covariates proxy for omitted amenities or building quality. The fixed-effects specification that controls for a handful of observable characteristics still yields a −12.7 percent effect, but this is not sufficient to rule out remaining confounders, especially if unobserved factors (e.g., building age, maintenance, proximity to nuisances) also vary systematically at the cutoff. I therefore urge the authors to bring in more detailed controls or use alternative strategies (e.g., boundary fixed effects with fine spatial trends, high-resolution building data) to convince readers that the remaining discontinuity is causal.

2. **The claim that “stigma dominates subsidies” is not empirically grounded.** The paper demonstrates a net negative effect but does not isolate the channels. The policy bundle contains fiscal incentives (TFPB reduction, VAT subsidy, ANRU investment) that should raise prices; the observed discount may be due to residual compositional differences, low expectation of investment, or unobserved deterioration rather than stigma per se. As written, the interpretation leap—especially the policy implications—outpaces the evidence. To substantiate a stigma channel, consider using proxies for information (e.g., media mentions, police reputation data) or showing that the discount persists in areas where development is slow despite subsidies. Alternatively, tone down the language about stigma and present the result as reduced-form evidence that designation coincides with a price penalty, without attributing the mechanism.

3. **The identification strategy could be strengthened by exploiting temporal variation or alternative margins.** The paper relies entirely on the cross-sectional boundary comparison with post-2020 data. Because QPV boundaries are based on income, there may be pre-existing spatial trends in price gradients that are not fully accounted for by the RDD. The 2024 rezoning offers a compelling opportunity for a difference-in-differences (or event-study) exercise exploiting boundaries that moved in or out of treatment; without it, the RDD assumes that all running-variable dependence is smooth at the boundary. Please either (a) explicitly test for pre-existing gradients (e.g., using 2014 transaction data even if coarser, or proxy pre-2015 price data) or (b) incorporate the rezoning to provide a within-boundary counterfactual. At minimum, the paper should discuss the absence of pre-2015 geolocated data as a limitation for validating continuity and clarify why the RDD assumptions are credible given the income-based boundary drawing.

**Suggestions**

- **Augment the control set with richer property and neighborhood characteristics.** DVF contains more than just price, surface, and rooms; it includes building age, cadastral category, and transaction type that can be used as additional covariates in the parametric specification or as balance checks. Furthermore, linking to external datasets (e.g., cadastral data on building age or type, INSEE variables on local amenities) could help rule out alternative explanations. Even if these controls do not change the coefficient, reporting those results would increase confidence in the identifying assumption.

- **Explore the 2024 rezoning for temporal variation.** The manifest noted that 2024 boundary revisions modified 960 boundaries, added new QPVs, and removed others. This provides a quasi-experimental setting where a location crosses from just-outside to inside (or vice versa), permitting a DiD or event-study that compares price trends before and after the rezoning for “switchers” relative to never-treated neighbors. If data availability allows, such an exercise would bolster causal claims and potentially isolate the effect of being newly labeled. At the very least, outline a future roadmap for exploiting these boundary changes and, if feasible, include preliminary results.

- **Strengthen the discussion of mechanism and policy implications with additional evidence.** To move beyond the reduced-form observation that designation coincides with a discount, consider (a) checking whether the effect is larger for properties with higher visibility (e.g., ground-floor vs. top-floor apartments) or in communes with more media coverage of QPVs (which could proxy for awareness and thus stigma); (b) comparing buyer demand measures (e.g., time-on-market if available) inside versus outside the boundary; or (c) looking at rental prices if data exist. These additional outcomes would help differentiate between valuation due to stigma, expected municipal spending, or expected taxes. Even narrative evidence (e.g., surveys about perceptions of QPVs) could help substantiate the stigma interpretation.

- **Clarify or revisit the placebo tests.** Panel C in Table 3 reports placebo cutoffs at ±250m and ±500m, but those cutoffs are spatially proximate to the true boundary and may still be contaminated by the same underlying gradients. Consider more distant false cutoffs (e.g., several kilometers away) or using “pseudo-boundaries” generated by randomly shifting the actual boundary. Additionally, the “placebo cutoff at −250m” yields a statistically significant negative estimate, suggesting the running variable is not fully smooth even away from the treatment. This should be addressed—either by explaining why those placebo results are acceptable or by refining the estimation to remove such trends (e.g., using higher-order polynomial controls or local randomization within very narrow windows).

- **Report more about the clustering and inference choices.** Clustering standard errors at the commune level is appropriate, but given the spatial nature of the RDD, consider two-way clustering (commune and boundary segment) or spatially correlated errors. Alternatively, report results using wild bootstrap or other robust inference to reassure readers that the standard errors are not downward biased due to many observations per cluster.

- **Provide a clearer link between theoretical expectations and empirical findings.** The discussion references capitalization theory (e.g., Oates, Brueckner). To make the comparison sharper, compute the implied capitalization of each policy component (property tax savings, VAT subsidy, ANRU spending) and juxtapose it with the observed discount. This can help clarify whether the negative effect is surprising, given the magnitude of the incentives, and quantify how much stigma (or other negative factors) would need to offset the positive components.

- **Improve transparency on data cleaning and sample selection.** The paper refers to excluding “outliers” but does not report the exact thresholds (it does, but more detail on the frequency lost). Provide a table that breaks down the sample construction (e.g., number of transactions dropped due to missing coordinates, extreme prices, non-arm’s-length transactions). This would reassure readers that the results are not driven by an idiosyncratic subset.

- **Consider heterogeneity by proximity to major amenities or public investment.** Since the policy bundle includes public infrastructure investment (ANRU), the effect might depend on whether a particular QPV was slated for renewal. Interacting the treatment with indicators for ANRU investment intensity could reveal whether neighborhoods that received more visible improvements experienced smaller discounts (supporting the stigma interpretation) or larger ones (supporting fiscal capitalization).

- **Clarify the role of new construction versus existing stock.** Because VAT reductions target new construction, it would be informative to analyze whether the treatment effect differs for very recent buildings versus older ones. This would help separate the subsidy (which affects future development) from the stigma (which affects existing stock). If DVF records construction year, use it to stratify estimates or include interactions.

Overall, the paper addresses an important question with rich data and a plausible spatial RDD framework. Addressing the points above—particularly those concerning covariate discontinuities and the interpretation of the “designation discount”—will significantly strengthen the contribution and make the policy implications more compelling.
