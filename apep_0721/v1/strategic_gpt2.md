## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether Britain’s large post-2016 minimum wage increases affected only workers at the wage floor or reshaped the broader lower half of the wage distribution. Using cross-area variation in how binding the National Living Wage was before its introduction, it argues that the biggest wage gains appeared around the 25th percentile, not the 10th, implying substantial spillovers and lower-tail compression.

A busy economist should care because the paper speaks to a first-order question about minimum wages: are they just a floor, or do they reorganize pay-setting more broadly? If the latter, then much of the standard way economists summarize minimum wage incidence, inequality effects, and even the relevant theoretical benchmark is incomplete.

**Does the paper articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Not quite. The current opening is energetic, but it is too self-congratulatory (“most ambitious sustained minimum wage experiment”) and too quick to announce the answer before sharpening the question. It also spends too much rhetorical capital on the UK policy rollout and not enough on the economic stakes.

**What the first two paragraphs should say instead:**

> Minimum wage debates usually focus on employment and on wages right at the statutory floor. But for inequality and firm wage-setting, the more important question may be whether large minimum wage increases spill over well above the minimum, compressing the broader lower tail of the wage distribution. We still know surprisingly little about how far those spillovers travel in modern developed labor markets.
>
> This paper studies Britain’s National Living Wage, a large and sustained national minimum wage increase whose bite varied sharply across local labor markets because pre-policy wage levels differed. I show that areas where the policy was more binding experienced not only larger gains at the bottom, but even larger gains around the 25th percentile and meaningful effects through the median. The central fact is simple: the NLW appears to have compressed the lower half of the wage distribution, not merely lifted the floor.

That is the pitch. Cleaner, less boastful, more world-question-driven.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Before this paper, the standard mental model is that minimum wages primarily raise wages at or just above the floor, with uncertain but often limited spillovers further up. We know minimum wages matter for lower-tail inequality, but we do not have a settled view on how broad those effects are when increases are large and sustained.

### Tension
Britain’s NLW was unusually large and persistent, which could plausibly force firms to restructure wage ladders rather than merely bump up the lowest-paid workers. But existing evidence leaves ambiguous whether spillovers are narrow and mechanical or broad and organizational.

### Resolution
The paper finds that higher-bite areas saw larger wage gains throughout the lower half of the distribution, with the largest effects at the 25th percentile rather than the 10th. It interprets that as evidence of broad compression and pay-scale restructuring.

### Implications
If true, economists should rethink minimum wage incidence as broader than “floor effects,” and policymakers should expect larger inequality effects—and potentially different firm adjustment margins—than models focused only on directly bound workers imply.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
Serviceable, but not fully disciplined. There is a story in there, but the paper also feels like a collection of percentile regressions and robustness checks wrapped in strong prose. The central narrative should be:

1. Large sustained minimum wage increases are different from marginal tweaks.
2. The key empirical question is how far spillovers travel up the distribution.
3. The UK provides a good test because bite varied sharply across places.
4. The central fact is not “positive coefficients everywhere”; it is the **shape** of the response, especially p25 > p10.
5. That shape is informative about wage-setting and inequality.

Right now the paper spends too much time reasserting that the result is important and not enough time helping the reader see why the shape of the gradient is the whole ballgame.

Also, the heterogeneity section seems narratively undercooked and may even confuse the main story. The split-sample result as written does not naturally strengthen the headline; it raises interpretive questions. If a result complicates the narrative, the paper either needs to incorporate that complication honestly or demote the section.
