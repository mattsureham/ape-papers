**Summary**

The paper documents that counties with stronger social ties to SVB’s California footprint experienced disproportionately large deposit outflows during the March 2023 panic, even after conditioning on geographic proximity, county demographics, and pre-crisis deposit trends. The identification relies on an SCI-weighted SVB exposure and several falsification checks including pre-pandemic placebos and a placebo “epicenter” using JPMorgan Chase. The results support the interpretation that private social networks constitute a distinct channel of financial contagion beyond public information or balance-sheet similarity.

**Essential Points**

1. **Causal interpretation of the SCI shift-share requires stronger justification.** The shift-share construction has SVB shares only in nine California counties—but the shift is constant across all counties. The only cross-county variation comes from the “share” (SCI) itself. In this setup, pinning down that SCI variation reflects network transmission rather than other unobserved county-level factors correlated with SCI (e.g., business sophistication, unemployment spillovers, media markets) is crucial. The pre-trend and placebo tests help, but it remains possible that SCI captures some unobserved propensity of counties to participate in high-tech-related tightening that happened contemporaneously with SVB’s failure (for example, counties with strong ties to Silicon Valley might have also seen venture-backed firms reduce deposits for reasons unrelated to panic). The authors should more fully argue why, conditional on controls, SCI is exogenous.

2. **County deposit changes may reflect heterogeneous exposure to other banks or aggregate shocks, not solely depositor panic tied to social networks.** The outcome aggregates deposits across all banks in a county. Without direct linkage to whether these deposits came from institutions with SVB-like risk profiles or uninsured deposit concentrations, the estimated effect could pick up general bank flight from aggregate factors correlated with SCI. The current controls (distance, tech share, income, population, pre-trend) may be insufficient to isolate network-form coordination as the channel. There should be a more direct connection from the social network exposure to the behavior of specific banks or depositors to justify interpreting the effect as a “networked bank run.”

3. **State-level clustering and inference need clarification given the spatial clustering of SCI and deposit movements.** Clustering at the state level may be insufficient if there is cross-state spatial correlation driven by SCI (which crosses state lines). Spatial dependence could lead to standard errors that are too small, especially because the treatment is a continuous cross-county measure derived from a limited number of epicenter counties. It would help to show that results are robust to alternative clustering (e.g., Conley) or to explicitly assess whether residuals exhibit spatial autocorrelation.

If these fundamental issues cannot be convincingly addressed, the paper may require rejection because the causal claim about private networks remains under-identified.

**Suggestions**

- **Clarify the role of social connectedness versus other spillovers.** One way to strengthen the exogeneity argument is to show that counties with high SCI exposure do not systematically differ from others along additional dimensions that might affect deposit changes. Appendix tables with balance checks (e.g., pre-2022 deposit growth, bank concentration, unemployment, venture capital activity) would help. The authors could also control for county-level media coverage intensity (e.g., Google searches, local newspaper hits about “banking crisis”) if data are available. Alternatively, they could use an IV approach where only the components of SCI that are orthogonal to measured economic linkages are used (e.g., residualized SCI after regressing on GDP, tech employment, and geography).

- **Anchor the deposit outcome to the hypothesized mechanism.** The paper argues that deposits drained because panic traveled through private networks. Providing evidence that the outflows occurred at banks where deposit shares are concentrated among uninsured (tech, venture-funded) clients would bolster this narrative. The authors could, for instance, compute deposit flows by bank type (e.g., banks with high uninsured deposit shares) or exploit the branch-level nature of the SOD data to focus on branches of banks similar to SVB (e.g., high cash holdings, high venture capital client base). If data permit, one could show that the SCI effect is strongest in deposits at banks that had more uninsured deposits, consistent with depositors running when trust erodes.

- **Assess the sensitivity of results to exposure definition and functional form.** The current SCI exposure aggregates over the nine epicenter counties. Testing robustness to alternative weighting schemes (e.g., using SCI quintiles, log-SCI, or excluding Santa Clara to focus on more peripheral Bay Area counties) would demonstrate that the effect is not driven by a small set of high-SCI connections. Moreover, since the SCI is extremely skewed (per summary stats), winsorizing or transforming it might mitigate the influence of outliers. Showing that the effect persists across such specifications would increase confidence in the findings.

- **Deepen the discussion of potential mechanisms.** The heterogeneity results point toward high-deposit counties being most affected, but the link between social networks and depositor coordination remains abstract. The paper could elaborate on how private ties transmit panic—e.g., through venture capitalists “calling” portfolio companies, through industry associations, or through inter-firm supply chains. If possible, the authors could correlate SCI exposure with measures of venture capital intensity or tech firm density to show that the network effect aligns with channels where private communication is plausible.

- **Complement state fixed effects with more granular controls.** Since SCI crosses states and counties, state FEs may absorb only broad shocks, leaving room for omitted variable bias. Including, for example, county banking market structure (Herfindahl of deposits), the presence of large regional banks, or local regulatory variations could make the identification tighter. If those data are unavailable, a robustness check using “synthetic control” at the county level (matching on pre-trend) might help to rule out confounding.

- **Address inference concerns explicitly.** Explore clustering strategies beyond states—e.g., Conley standard errors with 500km cutoff or double-clustering by state and SCI-based macro-region—to ensure the statistical significance is not driven by underestimated variability. Reporting p-values for these alternative inference methods would instill greater confidence.

- **Discuss the time horizon of the outcome.** The move from June 2022 to June 2023 aggregates outflows before and after March 2023. While the authors use pre-period placebos, the main outcome still encompasses periods where other shocks (e.g., interest rate hikes) may have influenced deposits. If feasible, narrow the outcome window to closer to the panic (e.g., March–June 2023 or quarterly deposits from Q4 2022 to Q1 2023) to better isolate the panic period. If data limitations prevent this, justify more explicitly why June–June is appropriate and demonstrate that excluding later months does not change the story.

- **Explicate policy implications carefully.** The policy discussion argues for broader deposit insurance because private networks spread panic. It would be helpful to contrast the magnitude of the estimated network effect with known channels (e.g., mention how it compares to the effect of uninsured deposit share or bank fundamentals from related literature) to gauge practical relevance. Also, clarify whether the findings imply that communication channels should be monitored or that structural reforms (deposit insurance) are the only feasible response.

- **Provide additional visual evidence.** A map or scatter plot showing the relationship between SCI exposure and deposit changes (with a fitted line and local polynomial smoother) would aid readers in understanding the spatial distribution. Similarly, plotting the distribution of SCI exposure could highlight whether a few counties drive the effect.

In sum, the paper raises an intriguing hypothesis and provides suggestive evidence, but reinforcing the causal story through additional robustness and mechanism-focused analysis would significantly strengthen the contribution.
