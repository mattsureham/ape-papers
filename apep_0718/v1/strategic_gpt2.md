## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether natural disasters permanently shrink the stock of low-cost housing by changing what gets rebuilt after destruction. Using tornado paths, it argues that severe storms leave behind persistent vacancy and possibly trigger redevelopment away from manufactured housing, raising the broader possibility that disasters can act as a force of housing-market reallocation rather than temporary damage alone.

Why should a busy economist care? Because if disasters do not just destroy housing but also alter the composition of local housing supply, then climate risk, land markets, and affordability are much more tightly linked than the usual recovery narrative suggests.

### Does the paper articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Not quite. The current opening is vivid and readable, but it promises a paper about the “permanent loss of manufactured housing” and “the replacement problem,” while the actual results support a narrower claim: persistent post-tornado vacancy, with only suggestive evidence on housing upgrading and no clear tract-level decline in manufactured housing share. The intro is selling a sharper, more definitive story than the evidence presently delivers.

### The pitch the paper should have

Here is the pitch the paper should give in the first two paragraphs:

> Severe natural disasters may do more than cause temporary housing damage: they may change what kinds of housing exist afterward. This matters especially for manufactured housing, which supplies a large share of unsubsidized affordable housing in disaster-prone areas and may be less likely to be replaced after destruction.
>
> This paper studies whether tornado destruction leaves a long-run housing-market scar. Exploiting sharp tornado path boundaries, we show that locations hit by severe tornadoes exhibit persistently higher vacancy rates many years later, with suggestive evidence of subsequent upgrading in local housing markets. The core message is that disaster recovery may involve prolonged non-rebuilding and land-use transition, with important implications for affordable housing supply.

That is more defensible and, strategically, better. It aligns the headline with the actual strongest result.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper’s contribution is to argue that severe tornadoes generate long-run changes in local housing markets—most clearly persistent vacancy, and possibly affordable-housing loss—rather than merely short-run physical destruction.

### Is this clearly differentiated from the closest papers?

Only partially. Right now the paper sounds like a mash-up of:
1. long-run disaster effects,
2. affordable housing/manufactured housing,
3. a spatial RDD using tornado paths.

What is not yet clear is which of these is the true contribution. If the contribution is substantive, it should be “disasters change housing composition and create durable affordability losses.” If the contribution is methodological, it is “tornado path boundaries create quasi-experimental variation.” If the contribution is descriptive, it is “manufactured housing is especially exposed and weakly replaced.” At present it gestures at all three and lands cleanly on none.

The biggest problem: the most novel substantive claim—permanent loss of manufactured housing—is not actually what the main estimates establish. A smart economist may finish the introduction thinking, “Interesting, they show tornadoes eliminate mobile home stock and it gets replaced by higher-value development,” but that is not what the main table delivers.

### World question or literature gap?

The paper is strongest when framed as a question about the world: **What happens to low-cost housing after disasters?** That is much better than “there is little work on manufactured housing in the disaster literature.” The current draft begins in the world, which is good, but then slides into a literature-gap framing and a method-first framing.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the intro?

Not confidently. They might say:
- “It’s a tornado-path RDD paper on post-disaster housing outcomes,” or
- “It’s another disaster paper with some manufactured housing angle.”

That is not enough. They should be able to say one crisp thing:
- “It shows disasters may create persistent vacancy scars and reduce low-cost housing replacement, not just temporary damage.”

Right now the introduction overstates the mobile-home-replacement result and understates the vacancy result.

### What would make the contribution bigger?

Specific ways to make it bigger:

1. **Make vacancy the centerpiece, not the side result.**  
   The vacancy result is the actual finding. Build the paper around “disaster-induced non-rebuilding” or “vacancy scars,” then bring manufactured housing in as the motivating setting.

2. **Show the transition, not just the scar.**  
   If the paper wants to claim redevelopment/upgrading, it needs an outcome more directly tied to redevelopment: building permits, parcel transitions, land use, structure type, satellite-based rebuilding, FEMA individual assistance, assessor data, or at least later-period recovery patterns. “Persistent vacancy” is interesting; “vacancy that later becomes higher-end redevelopment” is bigger.

3. **Measure affordable-housing loss more directly.**  
   Tract mobile-home share is too coarse for the claim being made. A stronger outcome would be actual counts of manufactured housing communities, pads, parcels, or units.

4. **Lean into heterogeneity that speaks to the mechanism.**  
   High pre-storm manufactured housing intensity, land value pressure, zoning restrictiveness, urban fringe vs rural, investor ownership. Those comparisons would turn a generic disaster paper into a housing-market paper.

5. **Potentially broaden beyond manufactured housing.**  
   Ironically, the paper may be more important if it is less exclusively about mobile homes. The larger claim is that disasters can trigger housing stock reallocation away from lower-cost uses. Manufactured housing is the sharpest case, but not the only audience.

- Disaster papers mostly study income, prices, population, and aggregate recovery.
- Affordable-housing papers mostly study policy, supply constraints, and regulation.
- This paper sits at the intersection: disasters may permanently reshape the composition of low-cost housing supply.

That is the conversation the paper should own.

The right scale is: a disaster-and-housing paper with manufactured housing as the salient setting and vacancy as the core empirical fact.

That is a first-order housing-market question with broad relevance under climate change.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Manufactured housing is a large, fragile, and understudied component of affordable housing, disproportionately located in disaster-prone places.

### Tension

Disaster research usually treats housing destruction as temporary and recovery as eventual rebuilding. But for low-cost housing on valuable land, destruction may instead create an opportunity not to rebuild the old use.

### Resolution

The paper finds persistent post-tornado vacancy in affected tracts and some suggestive signs of upgrading, but it does not convincingly show tract-level permanent loss of manufactured housing.

### Implications

Disasters may produce long-run housing-market scars and alter the composition of affordable housing supply; recovery policy may therefore need to focus on replacement, not just cleanup and aid.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

Only partially. At the moment, it is **a stronger story than the evidence supports**.

The declared story is:
1. tornado destroys mobile home park,
2. affordable housing does not return,
3. land gets redeveloped upward.

The demonstrated story is:
1. tornado hits,
2. vacancy remains elevated for a long time,
3. maybe some upgrading, maybe not,
4. manufactured-housing loss is not measured cleanly enough to show.

That mismatch is the main narrative problem. It makes the paper feel like a collection of suggestive pieces assembled around a preferred conclusion.

### What story should it be telling?

It should tell this story:

- **Setup:** Affordable housing is physically vulnerable, and recovery may not restore it.
- **Tension:** We know little about whether disaster-damaged low-cost housing gets rebuilt, especially in land markets with redevelopment pressure.
- **Resolution:** Severe tornado exposure leads to durable vacancy scars; these scars are larger where manufactured housing was initially more prevalent, suggesting slow or incomplete replacement of low-cost housing.
- **Implication:** Disaster recovery can become a mechanism of housing-market restructuring.

That story is cleaner, truer to the results, and potentially high-impact.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?

“I’ve got a paper showing that areas hit by severe tornadoes still have significantly elevated vacancy rates 8 to 18 years later. Recovery isn’t just slow—it may never restore the prior low-cost housing stock.”

That is the fact to lead with.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

They would lean in—*if* that is the opening. Persistent vacancy many years after disaster is interesting, surprising, and policy-relevant.

They would reach for their phones if the opening is “mobile homes account for many tornado deaths,” because that is tragic but not new to economists, or if the paper leads with a methodological description of path-boundary RDD.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Probably one of these:
1. “Is this really about affordable housing disappearing, or just slow rebuilding?”
2. “What eventually gets built there?”
3. “Is this specific to mobile homes, or a broader disaster-redevelopment phenomenon?”
4. “Where is the mechanism—developer conversion, zoning, insurance, or abandonment?”

Those are exactly the questions the paper needs to anticipate.

### If findings are modest/null, is the null interesting?

The null on mobile-home share is only interesting if the paper explicitly re-centers around the stronger finding and says:  
**At tract level, we cannot see the exact housing-type replacement, but we can clearly see long-lived non-rebuilding.**

That is useful. But as written, the null feels like a failed attempt to prove the headline claim. The paper needs to stop treating the null as a minor inconvenience and instead reconceptualize the contribution around the vacancy scar.
