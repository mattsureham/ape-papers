## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether severe tornadoes permanently reduce the supply of low-cost housing by destroying manufactured housing and changing what gets rebuilt afterward. Using the sharp spatial boundary of tornado damage, it argues that places hit by EF2+ tornadoes exhibit persistently higher vacancy and possibly housing-market upgrading, suggesting that disasters may not just damage housing temporarily but alter local housing composition for years.

Why should a busy economist care? Because the paper sits at the intersection of climate/disaster economics, housing supply, and distribution: if disasters permanently eliminate unsubsidized affordable housing, then climate risk is also a mechanism of housing scarcity and inequality.

### Does the paper articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Not really. The first paragraph is strong and vivid, but the paper immediately overcommits to a claim it does not actually establish cleanly: that mobile home parks “rarely come back” and are “converted to higher-value uses.” The actual headline result is persistent vacancy, not permanent replacement of manufactured housing by higher-value development. The introduction is currently selling a stronger and more specific paper than the results support.

### What should the first two paragraphs say instead?

The paper should lead with the broader and truer question: **Do disasters permanently shrink affordable housing supply, or does the housing market rebuild what is lost?** Manufactured housing is the motivating case, but the empirical payoff is about long-run post-disaster housing dynamics.

A better first-two-paragraph pitch would be something like:

> Manufactured housing provides one of the largest sources of unsubsidized affordable housing in the United States, yet it is disproportionately exposed to tornado risk. This raises a broader question: when severe storms destroy low-cost housing, is that housing rebuilt, or do disasters leave a lasting scar on local housing supply?
>
> This paper studies the long-run neighborhood consequences of tornado destruction using the sharp geographic boundary of EF2+ tornado paths. Comparing census tracts just inside versus just outside the damage swath, we find persistent increases in vacancy 8–18 years after a tornado, with suggestive evidence of housing-market upgrading in areas with greater manufactured-housing exposure. The central message is that disaster recovery may be much less complete than standard recovery narratives imply, especially for low-cost housing.

That is a paper the current evidence can carry. The “replacement problem” can remain as a mechanism or interpretation, but it should not be the title claim unless the authors can show actual replacement.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Manufactured housing is a major source of unsubsidized affordable housing and is disproportionately exposed to tornado risk. Disaster recovery is often implicitly treated as temporary disruption followed by rebuilding.

### Tension

What if recovery is incomplete or selective? In particular, what if low-cost housing is not rebuilt after destruction, so disasters permanently reallocate land away from affordability?

### Resolution

The paper finds persistent increases in vacancy inside severe tornado paths, with some suggestive but not definitive evidence of upgrading. It does **not** convincingly show the headline mechanism of manufactured-housing replacement.

### Implications

Disasters may have long-run distributional consequences through housing-market scarring, and recovery policy may need to care about preserving affordable housing stock rather than simply clearing debris and financing generic recovery.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

Only partially. It currently reads like a paper that wants to tell one story but proves another.

- The story it wants to tell: tornadoes destroy mobile home parks, which are then redeveloped into higher-value uses.
- The story it actually tells: tornadoes create persistent vacancy scars, and these scars may be especially relevant in manufactured-housing areas.

That mismatch is the central strategic problem.

### If it is a collection of results looking for a story, what story should it tell?

The story should be:

> Severe tornadoes leave behind durable housing-market scars rather than full local recovery; these scars are especially consequential where low-cost manufactured housing is exposed, implying that climate disasters may erode affordable housing supply even when aggregate recovery metrics look benign.

That is coherent, defensible, and connected to the actual estimates. “Replacement problem” can remain as a motivating hypothesis and longer-run interpretation, but not as the main resolved claim unless the evidence gets much stronger.
