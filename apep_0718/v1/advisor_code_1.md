**Summary**

The paper introduces a novel empirical strategy that exploits the near-random geographic boundary of EF2+ tornado paths as a spatial regression discontinuity to estimate long-run neighborhood impacts of tornado destruction. Comparing census tracts whose centroids fall just inside versus just outside the tornado swath, the authors find a persistent increase in vacancy rates—and suggestive movement in housing values and incomes—consistent with a land-use transition away from affordable manufactured housing. The study contributes to the disaster-economics literature by highlighting how physical destruction can induce a redistribution of the housing stock rather than mere aggregate recovery.

**Essential Points**

1. **Mismatch between research question and empirical unit of analysis.** The manifest’s causal claim centers on manufactured housing communities being destroyed and replaced by higher-value uses. The empirical strategy, however, operates at the census-tract level, with the treatment defined by tract centroids relative to tornado paths. Tracts are large and heterogeneous—many tracts may contain few or no mobile home parks, and the treatment is not tied to actual destruction of manufactured housing units. Without evidence linking the tract-level discontinuity to the destruction of manufactured housing, the mechanism (“replacement problem”) is under-identified. The authors need either (a) to restrict the sample to tracts with confirmed manufactured housing parks intersecting the path (as sketched in the manifest), or (b) to demonstrate that tract-level discontinuities are driven by park-level destruction (e.g., via matching tract-level treatment to HUD/ATTOM park locations and showing that treated tracts have an MHC within the path). Otherwise the core claim—tornadoes accelerate loss of manufactured housing—is not credibly established.

2. **Threats to the continuity assumption need stronger diagnostics.** The key RDD assumption is that outcomes vary smoothly at the path boundary absent treatment. The paper reports McCrary’s density test (with a rejection that is dismissed as geometric), but no formal evidence on pre-treatment trends or spatial heterogeneity in tornado exposure that might violate continuity. Given that disaster paths might correlate with land cover, tract size, or local geography that also affect long-run outcomes, the authors should present multiple falsification checks (e.g., placebo discontinuities at shifted boundaries, tests on pre-treatment changes, or white noise outcomes) and provide more convincing arguments that tract-level covariates are balanced at the threshold.

3. **The identification of the “replacement mechanism” remains suggestive.** The only statistically significant result is an increase in vacancy rates, yet the core mechanism is that destroyed manufactured housing is not rebuilt but redeveloped into higher-value uses. The vacancy result could reflect temporary disruption or measurement noise, and the paper’s only supporting evidence is a non-significant point estimate for mobile home share. To make the mechanism credible, the authors should (a) identify and use more direct outcomes (e.g., counts of manufactured homes, parcel-level land-use codes, RE transactions, FEMA HMGP buyouts) that capture what replaces the destroyed housing; (b) demonstrate that land parcels within the path show increases in higher-value development or sales; or (c) show that vacancy increases persist alongside decreases in mobile home stock or increases in housing value in the treated area. As currently presented, the “replacement problem” narrative rests on a single vacancy coefficient, which is insufficient to support the broader policy claims about the permanent loss of affordable housing.

**Suggestions**

1. **Refine the treatment to manufactured housing communities.** The manifest outlines a solid plan: geocode MHCs from HUD/EPA/ATTOM, overlay them on tornado path polygons, and use proximity to path edges as the running variable. Implementing this would align the empirical strategy directly with the research question. If park-level data proves infeasible, report direct evidence on the overlap between tornado paths and concentrations of manufactured housing (e.g., share of affordable units within treated tracts). In addition, consider constructing a binary indicator for tracts that contain an MHC centroid within the path versus those without, to limit the analysis to the population actually at risk.

2. **Include additional robustness/falsification analyses.** Beyond the placebo tests reported, consider (i) estimating the RDD separately for pre-treatment periods (e.g., 1990–2000 vs. 2002–2006) to confirm the absence of pre-existing discontinuities, (ii) conducting “donut” RDDs that exclude tracts whose centroid is extremely close to the boundary to mitigate measurement error, (iii) checking for spatial trends by including smooth functions of latitude/longitude or controlling for tract-level landcover or elevation, and (iv) using alternative running variables (e.g., distance from tornado center) or alternative bandwidth choices with transparency about sensitivity.

3. **Better connect the empirical estimates to the policy narrative.** The policy discussion emphasizes FEMA programs and tenant protections, but the empirical analysis does not engage with state-level heterogeneity in these policies. Empirically test whether the vacancy/land-use effects differ by state policies (right-to-purchase laws, tenant protections, FEMA participation) or by the presence of HMGP buyouts. At minimum, provide descriptive statistics showing how many tornadoes occurred in states with stronger protections versus weaker ones, and whether the outcomes differ systematically.

4. **Investigate the time dynamics more explicitly.** The paper uses a single pre- and post-period (2006–2010 vs. 2018–2022), which conflates events with different timing. Consider exploiting staggered treatment timing by constructing a panel of tract outcomes over multiple ACS waves and running an event-study-type specification around the tornado year. This would allow the reader to see how outcomes evolve over time and strengthen claims of persistence (e.g., showing that the vacancy spike emerges only after the tornado and remains elevated). Alternatively, for the RDD, report whether the effects differ with the time since the tornado (e.g., 5-year post vs. 10-year post).

5. **Clarify and justify the bandwidth/sample construction.** The working sample includes tracts within 5 miles of path edges, but the RDD estimates use varying optimal bandwidths. Provide a clearer discussion of how tracts are selected (e.g., do paths overlap with multiple tracts?), how distance is measured when a tract overlaps the path, and how multiple tornadoes affecting the same tract are handled. For tracts that straddle the path, centroid assignment might misclassify treatment status; consider using area-weighted treatment or restricting to tracts with centroids at least some distance from the boundary.

6. **Strengthen the presentation of the main results.** Column titles in Table 2 (RD estimates) are somewhat inconsistent; clarify units (e.g., percentage points) and note whether the running variable is miles or meters. For Table 3 (OLS), the negative point estimate on $\Delta$ mobile home share conflicts with the RDD point estimate—discuss why the signs differ and what that implies about weighting and specification choices. Additionally, include a figure showing the RD plot (outcome vs. distance) with binned means to visually assess the discontinuity.

7. **Discuss mechanistic alternatives explicitly.** Beyond redevelopment, increased vacancy could result from residents being displaced and choosing not to return, or from insurance-driven delays. Consider whether the observed effects could reflect temporary dislocations, and, if possible, link to data on rebuilding permits or insurance payouts. If no such data are available, acknowledge these alternative interpretations and clarify how your preferred mechanism remains more consistent with the full set of facts (e.g., persistent vacancy plus suggestive increases in housing values imply holding land for higher-value uses).

Overall, the paper addresses an important empirical question with a creative design. To make the argument compelling for publication, tie the RDD more directly to manufactured housing destruction, strengthen validity diagnostics, and provide more mechanistic evidence for the replacement narrative.
