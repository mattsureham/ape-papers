## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether state drug-price transparency laws changed how pharmaceutical firms raise prices. Using nationwide drug-level price data, it documents that large brand-drug price increases virtually disappeared after these laws spread, and argues that the change looks less like firms gaming reporting thresholds and more like broad restraint under political and reputational scrutiny.

A busy economist should care because this is potentially a paper about something much bigger than pharmacy pricing: whether low-powered disclosure regulation can discipline firms in politically salient markets even without hard price controls.

### Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Not quite. The current introduction gets to the facts quickly, which is good, but it undersells the real stakes and muddies the core question. It oscillates between:
1. a health-policy paper on state drug transparency laws,
2. a bunching paper about thresholds, and
3. a broader paper about disclosure as regulation.

Those are not the same paper. Right now the introduction reads like a competent applied micro paper with an interesting result, not like an AER paper with a big idea.

### The pitch the paper should have

Here is the version the first two paragraphs should convey:

> For years, U.S. brand-drug manufacturers routinely imposed large price increases despite intense public concern and little direct federal price regulation. Then, quite suddenly, the upper tail of drug price increases collapsed. This paper asks whether the spread of state transparency laws helped cause that shift, and more broadly whether disclosure rules can restrain firm behavior not by creating mechanical threshold avoidance, but by making politically costly actions visible.
>
> Using the universe of CMS drug acquisition cost data for brand drugs from 2013 to 2025, I show that large price increases became rare after transparency laws spread. The key empirical fact is not bunching just below reporting thresholds, but compression of the entire upper tail of the price-change distribution. That pattern suggests these laws may work as “spotlight” regulation: disclosure changes behavior because firms want to avoid public and political scrutiny, not just paperwork.

That is the big-paper version. It starts with a world fact, not a statute. It makes the reader care before getting into thresholds.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### Contribution in one sentence

The paper’s contribution is to document that the spread of state drug-price transparency laws coincided with a dramatic compression of the upper tail of brand-drug price increases, suggesting that disclosure regulation may deter conspicuous pricing behavior through reputational and political channels rather than simple threshold bunching.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest papers?

Only partially. The paper differentiates itself from scarce direct work on drug transparency laws, but not sharply enough from two broader literatures:
- pharma pricing policy papers that ask whether transparency affects spending or prices, and
- disclosure/regulation papers showing that public reporting changes behavior.

The novelty is **not** merely “first using drug-level data” or “first bunching test.” That is too methodological and too incremental. The sharper novelty is:
- this paper studies **how the whole distribution of firm behavior changes under disclosure**, and
- in a nationally priced market, **state laws may matter through salience and political exposure, not just through formal compliance incentives**.

That distinction is present but not yet crisp.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or filling a gap in a LITERATURE?

Too much as a literature gap. Phrases like “first systematic evidence,” “most comprehensive empirical test,” and “aggregate analyses miss this” are serviceable, but second-tier. AER introductions need to lead with a world question:

- Can disclosure rules discipline firms in markets with no direct price caps?
- Did the era of routine double-digit brand-drug price hikes end because firms came under a transparency spotlight?
- When does transparency act like information and when does it act like regulation?

That is much stronger than “there are only two prior studies.”

### Could a smart economist explain to a colleague what’s new?

Right now, maybe, but not confidently. The likely summary today is: “It’s a paper on drug transparency laws using bunching and pre/post changes in price increases.” That is not enough.

What you want them to say is: “Interesting paper — it shows that once drug pricing came under public-reporting regimes, the whole upper tail of price increases disappeared; the point is that disclosure may discipline firms through political visibility, not just threshold gaming.”

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Three concrete ways:

1. **Shift the headline outcome from threshold exceedance to distributional regime change.**  
   The big fact is not just “above 10% fell.” It is “extreme price hikes as a business practice largely disappeared.” Lead with that.

2. **Connect more directly to the mechanism that matters in economics: salience/political cost.**  
   The current “spotlight” idea is the best part of the paper. It should be promoted from discussion material to central framing.

3. **Broaden the implications beyond pharmaceuticals.**  
   The paper should be speaking to disclosure mandates in healthcare, ESG, executive pay, hospital prices, school report cards, environmental releases, etc. Right now that connection appears too late and too abstractly.

If the author wanted to make the paper materially bigger, the most promising route is not another specification. It is a broader and more convincing account of **why disclosure sometimes changes conduct rather than merely reveals it**.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Before this paper: U.S. drug prices are widely viewed as hard to regulate, large brand-drug price increases were common, and policymakers turned to transparency laws as a low-powered alternative to direct controls.

### Tension

The tension should be: transparency laws seem weak on paper. Firms only have to report and justify increases; they are not prohibited from raising prices. So why would such laws matter in a nationally priced market? If they matter at all, do they merely induce gaming around thresholds, or do they meaningfully change pricing conduct?

### Resolution

The paper’s empirical resolution is that large price increases almost disappear and the upper tail compresses, with little evidence of simple bunching below the trigger. That points to broad deterrence rather than pure threshold avoidance.

### Implications

The implication is that disclosure can operate as a form of regulation when publicity itself is costly. More broadly, state-level transparency policy may influence national firm behavior even absent direct state-by-state price setting.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the ingredients of a good arc, but in its current form it still feels somewhat like a collection of empirical patterns looking for the biggest story they can support.

The strongest story is there:
- **setup:** drug makers could raise prices with little direct constraint,
- **tension:** transparency laws look weak and should at most induce threshold gaming,
- **resolution:** instead, conspicuous price hikes broadly disappear,
- **implications:** disclosure may work by creating political accountability.

That should be the overt structure of the paper. Right now the introduction moves too quickly into methods and not enough into the puzzle.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?

I would lead with:
> “One in four brand drugs used to have semiannual price increases above 10%; after state transparency laws spread, that fell to about 1%, and the whole upper tail vanished rather than bunching just below the threshold.”

That is a strong fact.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

They would lean in — initially. The fact is genuinely striking.

But the immediate follow-up would be:
> “Is that really the laws, or is it broader political pressure and industry-wide norm change after the pricing scandals?”

That is exactly why the paper’s strategic positioning matters so much. Since the causal interpretation is not airtight from the paper as currently framed, the paper should not oversell “the laws caused this” as the only possible reading. The durable value is the empirical fact plus the mechanism-oriented interpretation.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Most likely:
- “Why didn’t firms just bunch below the threshold?”
- Or: “How can state laws matter when pricing is national?”
- Or: “Is this about transparency per se or about being publicly labeled a bad actor in a highly salient sector?”

Those are good follow-up questions. The introduction should anticipate them.

### If the findings are modest or null

Not applicable here; the main descriptive finding is not modest. The paper has a big fact. Its problem is not weak findings; it is making sure the findings are attached to the right story.
