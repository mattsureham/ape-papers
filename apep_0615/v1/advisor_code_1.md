**Idea Fidelity**

The paper largely tracks the manifest. It studies the NADAC price series for brand drugs, focuses on state-level transparency laws with differing thresholds, and proposes to test for strategic threshold avoidance through bunching (and related DiD/dose-response ideas). However, the implementation diverges from the manifest’s central identification strategy. While the manifest promised a cross-threshold/bunching design that exploits the staggered timing and heterogeneous thresholds of 21 states, the submitted paper collapses those rules into a single pre/post comparison centered on the 10 % threshold and Oregon’s adoption. **Summary**

The paper documents a dramatic post-2018 decline in large semi-annual brand drug price increases in the NADAC data and interprets this as evidence that state drug price transparency laws compressed the entire upper tail of price change distributions. It reports bunching estimates that turn sharply negative after Oregon’s 10 % threshold becomes binding and presents a dose-response regression linking the count of active transparency laws to probabilities of exceeding 10 % price increases. The author concludes that transparency laws operate as a broad reputational deterrent rather than merely creating strategic threshold avoidance.

---

**Essential Points**

1. **Identification strategy is too coarse to isolate the law’s causal effect.** The paper fails to exploit the cross-state, cross-threshold variation that makes the question compelling. All bunching and dose-response analyses rely on aggregate pre/post comparisons around the 10 % mark, effectively treating 2018+ as a single “transparency regime.” This ignores that only a subset of states had law in place at any given time, with different thresholds. Without a proper comparison group (e.g., drugs that are not yet subject to a binding state threshold or only affected by higher thresholds) and without accounting for the staggered timing, the large pre/post differences could reflect national trends, market-wide shocks, or changes in NADAC coverage rather than transparency laws. The manifest promised a “bunching estimator applied to the universe of weekly NADAC prices” with “cross-threshold identification” and a CS-DiD, but the paper never implements that fuller strategy.

2. **Outcome measure and thresholds do not correspond.** Transparency laws are triggered by manufacturer WAC increases, whereas NADAC reflects net acquisition costs at pharmacies. These series can diverge substantially (rebates, distribution allowances, lagged updates, etc.). The paper does not justify why NADAC semi-annual percentage changes are valid proxies for the WAC changes that trigger reporting obligations. If the reporting thresholds do not align with the observed outcome, then the bunching exercise around 10 % loses its meaning. Moreover, state reporting obligations refer to the same increase in every state (since the WAC is national), yet the empirical work does not show how each state’s threshold would bind for a given drug at a given time, so it is unclear which threshold the bunching should be tested against.

3. **Interpretation of bunching results is unconvincing.** The reported drop in excess mass around the 10 % threshold is interpreted as “compression of the upper tail,” but the methodology does not convincingly establish that. First, standard bunching inference would require estimating the counterfactual density conditional on the law being absent, ideally using drugs or time periods unaffected by any threshold. The paper’s counterfactual simply comes from a high-order polynomial fit over the entire sample and interprets changes after 2018 mechanically as law effects. Second, the paper lacks formal statistical tests that exploit variation in which thresholds were binding when (e.g., compare 10 % bundling post-Oregon vs. 16 % effects when only California’s law existed). Without such structure, the negative excess mass could equally reflect natural shifts in price increase behavior or measurement choices (e.g., the semi-annual window). Third, the policy interpretation (reputational deterrence) requires showing that the compression is not driven by threshold avoidance, but the evidence presented (negative bunching at 10 % plus placebo thresholds) is insufficient because there is no direct link between the political costs and the observed aggregate shift absent a more granular identification.

**Suggestions**

1. **Exploit the promised staggered-threshold variation.** To credibly attribute the observed distributional changes to transparency laws, the analysis must distinguish drugs/times that are subject to a binding threshold from those that are not. Consider constructing a state-period panel where each drug is mapped to the lowest active threshold in states covering a large enough share of its sales (or simply to the national binding threshold). Use the staggered adoption dates to implement event studies or a Donut DiD: compare price change densities before and after a state imposes a threshold while controlling for national trends via unaffected drugs or periods. This should also allow you to test whether bunching shifts from 16 % to 10 % when Oregon joins, as promised in the manifest. The current pre/post split around 2018 is too blunt and may conflate the policy with other simultaneous reforms.

2. **Align the outcome with the reporting metric and document the mapping.** Transparency laws trigger on WAC increase percentages. Provide evidence that NADAC semi-annual changes move in tandem with those WAC increases (e.g., correlate NADAC changes with known list-price changes for select NDCs, or use a subset of drugs where WAC data are publicly available). Alternatively, try to obtain the actual WAC series (some commercial sources or regulatory filings may provide it). If NADAC is truly the best available proxy, discuss the expected attenuation bias and why it does not undermine the bunching signature. Without this, it is hard to interpret what “bunching around 10 % in NADAC” actually means for the policy.

3. **Strengthen the bunching analysis.** Standard practice (e.g., Kleven 2016) is to estimate the counterfactual density using observations believed to be unaffected by the policy and to provide graphical evidence of the density before/after for the same support. Present the raw density plots of semi-annual changes for pre- and post-periods to visually demonstrate the “compression.” When computing excess mass, condition on subsamples that differ in exposure to the law (e.g., prices of drugs that spent time under different thresholds) to show that the negative mass is not just due to a generalized reduction in volatility. Including more formal tests (e.g., interacting treatment with threshold exposure) would help justify the mechanism. Also, report how sensitive the negative excess mass is to the choice of bin width, polynomial order, and bandwidth, beyond just stating in the appendix that the direction is stable. Presenting a figure comparing the raw histogram to the fitted polynomial would make the phenomenon concrete.

4. **Address other confounding policies and market changes.** The post-2017 period saw biosimilar competition, heightened scrutiny of drug pricing, federal initiatives (Trump Administration, Inflation Reduction Act), and PBM-related changes. The current dose-response regression simply regresses the probability of a >10 % increase on the count of active states without controlling for time fixed effects, drug fixed effects, or other policy variables. This likely captures the same national secular decline as the raw means. Consider adding time fixed effects (semi-annual or annual), drug fixed effects, and, if available, controls for competitors entering (biosimilars) or other regulatory milestones. Alternatively, use an event-study specification around each state’s adoption to flexibly control for trends and to show parallel pre-trends.

5. **Clarify the policy framing in light of the evidence.** The conclusion that transparency works through reputational and political deterrence hinges on showing broad compressions beyond threshold regions. If possible, examine other parts of the distribution (e.g., the effect on changes between 5–8 %, 8–10 %, etc.) to document the “entire upper tail” claim. You could also analyze whether the frequency of media mentions or Congressional inquiries correlates with the intensity of reporting, to substantiate the reputational channel. Without such evidence, the claims remain speculative. Explicitly discuss alternative explanations (e.g., manufacturers choosing to increase prices more frequently but by smaller amounts for operational reasons) and, if feasible, test them.

6. **Revisit data construction choices.** The paper aggregates weekly NADAC data into semi-annual changes by taking the last observed price in each half-year. This could obscure short-lived price dynamics and introduce measurement noise if the last observation is an outlier. Consider using more frequent changes (quarterly or monthly) and averaging over the period, or at least showing that the patterns hold when using alternative aggregation rules. Additionally, the trimming choices (–30 % to +60 %, base price ≥ $1) should be justified with reference to the risk of misreporting. Provide a robustness check showing that results are not driven by these trimming thresholds.

7. **Expand the heterogeneity analysis.** The current high/low price split is informative, but additional heterogeneity could strengthen the mechanism. For example, examine whether drugs with larger national sales (proxy via NADAC weighted by utilization) react more strongly, or whether drugs with multiple state markets respond differently. If data on manufacturer characteristics (size, therapeutic area) are available, explore whether those with more exposure to the spotlight effect show stronger compression. This would help differentiate reputational channels from aggregate policy effects.

By addressing these points, the paper can better align its empirical strategy with the compelling manifest and provide a more credible causal story linking transparency laws to pharmaceutical pricing behavior.
