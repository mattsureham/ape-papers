## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether state-level transparency laws—which require pharmaceutical companies to justify price hikes above a certain percentage—actually curb rising drug costs. It finds that these laws didn’t just lead to "gaming" at the threshold; they essentially ended the era of double-digit price increases for brand-name drugs, shifting the entire industry toward a model of reputational deterrence. 

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this well, though it leans heavily on the "Daraprim/Shkreli" anecdote for color. The pitch is clear by the second paragraph: this isn't just about price levels, it’s about a structural collapse in the *distribution* of price changes. 

**The Pitch the Paper Should Have:**
"Can the mere requirement to *explain* a price increase prevent it from happening? This paper uses the staggered adoption of state drug-transparency laws to show that when 'reporting thresholds' (e.g., 10%) are enacted, the share of drugs exceeding those thresholds collapses from 25% to under 1%. Crucially, I show this isn't simple bunching below a notch, but a wholesale compression of the price distribution driven by the political and reputational costs of being 'named and shamed' on public lists."

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**The Contribution:** The paper demonstrates that information-based regulation can act as a "spotlight" that reshapes an entire market's pricing distribution through political deterrence, rather than acting as a standard regulatory "notch" that induces strategic bunching.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It is well-differentiated from the existing health econ literature (Sood et al., 2021) which looks at aggregate spending. By looking at NDC-level distributions, this paper finds the "signal" that aggregate data masks.
*   **Question vs. Literature:** It frames itself as answering a question about the world (Do transparency laws work?), but the *value-add* for the AER is the theoretical pivot from "Bunching" (Saez/Chetty) to "Deterrence" (Stigler/Jin).
*   **The "Smart Economist" Test:** A reader would explain this as: "It turns out drug companies are terrified of being on a state's 'bad actor' list, so they stopped doing big price hikes altogether rather than just aiming for 9.9%."
*   **Growth Potential:** To make this bigger, the paper needs to prove the "Spotlight" mechanism. Is the effect stronger for firms with higher consumer brand equity? Do we see mentions of these "transparency reports" in earnings calls or local media? Linking the pricing behavior to actual *reputational* proxies would move this from "interesting empirical fact" to "fundamental insight on regulation."

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

*   **Closest Neighbors:** Kleven & Waseem (2013) on notches; Jin & Leslie (2003) on disclosure; Lakdawalla (2018) on pharma economics.
*   **Positioning:** It currently positions itself as an "answer" to the scarcity of evidence in drug transparency. It should instead position itself as a **challenge** to the standard bunching literature. The fact that the authors *don't* find traditional bunching is the most interesting part.
*   **Missing Conversations:** The paper needs to speak to the **Political Economy** literature. This is a story about firms' "Non-Market Strategy." They are pricing to avoid legislative retaliation (the "precursor to price controls" mentioned on page 4). 
*   **The "Right" Conversation:** It should lean harder into the "Regulatory Threat" literature. The price restraint isn't just about the law; it's about the *shadow* of future, harsher laws.

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

*   **Setup:** The 2016 "Shkreli era" of unbridled price hikes.
*   **Tension:** States pass "transparency" laws that have no real teeth—they just require a form to be filled out. Economists would expect firms to either ignore them or price just below the line (9.9% increases).
*   **Resolution:** Firms did neither. They retreated from high price increases entirely. The "upper tail" vanished.
*   **Implications:** Information is a more powerful regulatory tool than we thought, provided the information is politically "radioactive."

**Evaluation:** The narrative is strong, but the "Tension" section needs more work on *why* the null of bunching is the standard economic expectation. It makes the "compression" result feel more surprising.

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

*   **The Lead Fact:** "After Oregon set a 10% reporting threshold, the number of brand drugs with 10%+ price hikes didn't just drop—it effectively hit zero."
*   **The Reaction:** People will lean in because drug pricing is a high-salience "villain" story.
*   **Follow-up:** "Did they just raise prices more frequently at lower percentages (e.g., 4% twice a year) to compensate?" The paper touches on semi-annual frequency, but a more robust "total annual price increase" check is the first thing a dinner guest (or referee) will ask.

---

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

*   **Front-loading:** The paper is well-structured. Table 1 and Table 2 are the stars.
*   **Robustness:** Move the "high-price vs. low-price" heterogeneity (Table 4) to the main results and expand it. If the "spotlight" theory is true, high-profile drugs should show more restraint. This is a mechanism test, not just a robustness check.
*   **Appendix:** The 2026 data points in Table 3 feel like a typo or a projection (given the paper is dated March 2026). If this is a "future-dated" simulation or autonomous project, the "real-world" validity needs to be clarified.

---

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The gap between this being a good *Journal of Health Economics* paper and a great *AER* paper is the **Mechanism**. 

Right now, the paper says: "The distribution changed, and it looks like deterrence." To be AER, it needs to show the *cost* of that deterrence. Do we see pharmaceutical stocks reacting to the passage of these laws? Do we see a reduction in R&D or a change in "launch prices" for new drugs (to bake in future increases)? 

**Single most impactful piece of advice:** Focus less on the "transparency" and more on the **"Political Economy of Anticipatory Restraint."** Prove that firms are trading off short-term profits for long-term political survival.
