## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether state drug price transparency laws changed how pharmaceutical companies raise prices. Using product-level national drug price data, it argues that once states began requiring manufacturers to report and justify large increases, very large price hikes largely disappeared—not because firms bunched just below reporting thresholds, but because the whole upper tail of price increases compressed.

A busy economist should care because this is, in principle, a broad question about whether disclosure regulation changes firm behavior in politically salient markets. If true, the paper speaks not just to pharmaceutical pricing, but to a larger class of “transparency as regulation” policies.

**Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Not quite. The current introduction gets to the basic result quickly, which is good, but it leads with Daraprim and then jumps almost immediately into the descriptive collapse in large price increases. What is missing is a sharper statement of the big question: **Can non-binding disclosure mandates discipline pricing in a market where states cannot directly regulate national list prices?** That is the AER-level hook. Right now the paper reads more like “here is a striking pattern in drug price data around transparency laws.”

**What the first two paragraphs should say instead:**

> Prescription drug prices in the United States are set nationally, while direct price regulation remains limited. Yet over the last decade, many states adopted “transparency” laws that do not cap prices, but require manufacturers to report and justify unusually large increases. The central question is whether such disclosure mandates actually restrain pricing behavior, or merely generate paperwork while firms continue to raise prices much as before.
>
> This paper shows that the spread of state transparency laws coincided with a dramatic change in the distribution of brand-drug price increases. Using the universe of CMS NADAC prices for brand drugs, I find that once low-threshold reporting laws became binding, double-digit price increases largely vanished. Importantly, manufacturers do not appear to simply bunch just below the reporting cutoff; instead, the entire upper tail of price changes compresses, consistent with transparency working as political and reputational deterrence rather than as a notch that firms mechanically game.

That is the pitch the paper should have.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution in one sentence:**  
The paper’s core contribution is to argue that drug price transparency laws changed the *distribution* of brand-drug price increases—compressing the upper tail rather than inducing narrow bunching below reporting thresholds.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest papers?
Only partially. The introduction does some differentiation, but it leans too much on “there are few studies” and “aggregate state-level analyses miss distributional effects.” That is a reasonable start, but it is not yet crisp enough. The paper needs to distinguish itself more explicitly from at least three neighboring literatures:

1. **Drug pricing / pharmaceutical regulation papers** that study list prices, rebates, and market structure.
2. **Transparency / disclosure regulation papers** that ask whether disclosure changes firm conduct.
3. **Bunching / notches papers** where thresholds generate avoidance behavior.

Right now the paper signals all three, but doesn’t convincingly claim the intersection as its own terrain.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a gap in a literature?
It drifts between both, and too often toward the weaker version: “prior work is scarce” and “aggregate data miss this.” The stronger framing is about the world:

- **Can disclosure mandates restrain behavior even without direct enforcement power?**
- **When does transparency work as deterrence rather than as a reporting notch?**
- **Can state policy discipline nationally set prices?**

Those are live economic questions. “This is the first comprehensive study” is not.

### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what’s new?
They could probably say: “It’s a paper showing that after state transparency laws, large drug price increases went away, and the pattern looks like broad deterrence rather than bunching.” That’s not bad.

But there is still a risk that many readers would reduce it to: “another policy timing paper on drug prices.” The mechanism distinction—**compression versus bunching**—is the part that rescues it from that fate, and the paper should elevate that much more aggressively.

### What would make this contribution bigger?
Several possibilities:

- **Broader outcome framing:** Not just “probability of exceeding 10%,” but a more explicit characterization of how pricing norms changed. The bigger question is whether transparency changed *pricing strategies*, not just threshold exceedance.
- **Stronger mechanism framing:** The paper’s best idea is that disclosure can work through reputational/political deterrence rather than threshold gaming. That should be the centerpiece, not a secondary interpretation.
- **Cross-market relevance:** The paper should more directly connect this to hospital transparency, ESG disclosure, emissions reporting, pay ratio disclosure, etc. The generalizable insight is the payoff.
- **Comparison that enlarges the question:** If possible, compare drug pricing to another nationally priced product market facing state disclosure but not direct regulation. Even a conceptual comparison in the introduction would help.
- **Clearer distinction between “information policy” and “price regulation”:** The paper becomes bigger if it is presented as evidence that information policy can substitute for more direct intervention in politically salient markets.

If the authors can only enlarge one thing, it should be the **general mechanism claim about disclosure regulation under salience**.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Before this paper, the world looked like this: pharmaceutical manufacturers could set national prices with limited direct price regulation, and states turned to transparency laws as a politically feasible substitute for hard controls.

### Tension
The puzzle is whether those laws matter at all. Skeptics would say disclosure requirements are symbolic, easy to game, or too weak to affect national pricing. Even if they matter, one might expect simple threshold avoidance rather than meaningful restraint.

### Resolution
The paper claims that after transparency laws became binding, large price increases largely disappeared, and the change looks like broad upper-tail compression rather than bunching just below the threshold.

### Implications
If that pattern is real and attributable to the policy environment, then disclosure can work as deterrence in politically salient markets. That matters for drug policy and for a broader class of information-based regulations.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
It has the pieces, but the arc is not yet fully disciplined. Right now it is somewhere between:

- a paper about **drug price transparency laws**, and
- a paper about **how disclosure regulation changes firm conduct**.

The first is field-interesting. The second has a better shot at AER relevance.

At moments the paper also feels like a collection of empirical patterns—pre/post collapse, threshold shift, dose response, placebo thresholds, heterogeneity—without a single dominant storyline organizing them. The author should choose one narrative and make all results serve it.

### What story should it be telling?
The paper should tell this story:

1. States cannot directly regulate national drug prices, so they tried transparency.
2. The obvious prediction is local gaming around thresholds.
3. But the surprising fact is not bunching—it is the disappearance of the upper tail.
4. Therefore, transparency in this setting appears to work as a political/reputational sanction, not a mechanical reporting notch.
5. That changes how economists should think about disclosure mandates in salient markets.

That is a real narrative. The current draft is close, but not yet fully committed to it.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?
“After states started requiring firms to report and justify large drug price increases, double-digit brand-drug price hikes almost disappeared—and not because firms just moved to 9.9%.”

That is a good lead.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
Initially, they would lean in. The empirical fact is striking. It has the right kind of surprise.

But the immediate next thought for many economists would be: “Is this really the law, or is it the whole post-2016 political environment?” Since this is an editorial memo and not a referee report, I’ll put it strategically: the paper’s challenge is that the *fact* is much more compelling than the current *claim discipline*. The more grandly the paper states “transparency laws did this,” the faster skeptical readers will disengage.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Almost certainly:  
**“Why should a few state reporting laws affect a nationally set price so dramatically?”**

That is the right question, and the paper should embrace it. The answer is not “because the coefficient is large”; the answer is the mechanism story about salience, multi-state exposure, and reputational politics.

### If findings are modest or null
Not applicable here—the finding is not modest. In fact the opposite problem arises: the result is so dramatic that it demands especially careful strategic framing. The paper should not oversell certainty; it should sell the importance of the pattern and the mechanism interpretation.

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

### What would make it read better?

#### 1. Rewrite the introduction around one big question
The intro currently moves quickly into institutional detail and then into empirical strategy. It should instead be organized around:

- Why transparency is used when direct regulation is infeasible
- What standard theory predicts: threshold avoidance
- What the paper finds instead: broad deterrence
- Why that matters beyond pharmaceuticals

#### 2. Move some institutional threshold detail later
The California 16% versus Oregon 10% detail is useful, but too much of it arrives too early. It clutters the opening before the reader fully understands the larger question.

#### 3. Front-load the single most interesting result
The reader should learn in the first page:
- large price increases disappeared;
- the key surprise is *not* bunching;
- the implication is that disclosure may work through political deterrence.

That is the “good stuff.” The paper mostly does this, but could do it with more force and less hedging.

#### 4. Tighten the contribution paragraph
The “contribution is threefold” paragraph is generic. Top-field intros often weaken when they enumerate three contributions. Better to state one main contribution and one secondary contribution.

#### 5. Reduce time spent on niche estimation exposition in the main text
The bunching methodology section is more detailed than needed for a top-journal reader in the main body. Keep the intuition in text; move implementation detail to an appendix.

#### 6. Promote the conceptual mechanism discussion
The mechanism section in institutional background is actually important. It should be expanded slightly and used as the conceptual spine of the paper, because that is where the general-interest value sits.

#### 7. Shorten the conclusion
The conclusion currently restates the paper capably, but the leap to other domains feels somewhat essayistic. A better conclusion would do less analogy and more precise statement of what economists should update on:
- disclosure can discipline firms without direct caps,
- the effect may be nonlocal in the action distribution,
- salience and political exposure matter.

### Are there results buried that should be in the main text?
The paper’s broader “distribution compression, not threshold bunching” result is the main event. If there is a simple figure showing the full distribution pre/post, that should be central—more central than some of the tabulated bunching estimates. This feels like a paper where one or two distribution plots could do a huge amount of narrative work.

### Is the conclusion adding value?
Some, but not enough. It is strongest when discussing “when does requiring disclosure change the thing being disclosed?” That line is good and points toward the broader contribution. The rest can be sharper and less diffuse.
