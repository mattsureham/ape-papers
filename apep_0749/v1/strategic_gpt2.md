## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Before this paper: states are rapidly legalizing online sports betting; most discussion emphasizes tax revenue, consumer surplus, problem gambling, and household financial harm. Economists know alcohol affects crash mortality, but less is known about whether new digital entertainment markets can indirectly increase alcohol-related externalities.

### Tension
Online sports betting is a particularly plausible source of harm because it is synchronized with live sports viewing, often in alcohol-serving social environments. But it is not obvious whether legalization actually changes fatal crash risk, and if it does, whether the mechanism is specific enough to distinguish from generic omitted-policy stories.

### Resolution
The paper finds that legalization raises alcohol-involved fatal crashes and that the increase is concentrated on NFL game days, with no corresponding effect for non-alcohol crashes.

### Implications
If this pattern is real, then online sports betting is not merely a private vice or a revenue source: it creates a socially costly, predictable, calendar-based externality. That has implications for gambling regulation, taxation, disclosure, and targeted DUI enforcement.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
It has the bones of one, but it is not fully disciplined. The paper has a promising story, yet it sometimes slides into being a **collection of supportive findings**: main effect, placebo, DDD, welfare calculation, enforcement discussion. The strongest story is there, but it needs tighter sequencing.

**What story should it be telling?**

1. **Online betting changed the nature of sports consumption.**
