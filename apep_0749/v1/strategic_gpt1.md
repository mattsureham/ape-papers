## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether legalizing online sports betting creates an off-platform social cost: more alcohol-involved fatal car crashes. Using staggered legalization across states, it argues that crashes rise after legalization and that the increase is concentrated on NFL game days, suggesting that mobile betting and drinking are behavioral complements in game-watching environments.

A busy economist should care because the paper is not really about gambling per se; it is about whether digital access to a new “sin good” changes complementary behaviors in ways that generate large, unpriced externalities. If true, that is a first-order policy point for the design of gambling markets and for how economists think about bundled consumption and regulatory spillovers.

**Does the paper itself articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Not quite. The opening is vivid, but a bit journalistic and too close to a descriptive “New York launched, people bet in bars” story. The paper gets to the question quickly enough, but the broader economic stake is underdeveloped. It needs to lead less with scene-setting and more with the conceptual point: legalization of a digitally delivered product may change where people go, what they consume jointly, and what external harms result.

**What the first two paragraphs should say instead:**

> States have rapidly legalized online sports betting, typically evaluating the policy in terms of tax revenue, consumer surplus, and problem gambling. But online betting may also create externalities outside the gambling market if it changes complementary behaviors around live sports consumption—especially drinking in bars and restaurants while games are being watched. Whether this new form of digital gambling increases alcohol-related traffic deaths is an important and largely unanswered policy question.
>
> This paper studies that question using staggered legalization of online sports betting across U.S. states. I show that legalization increases alcohol-involved fatal crashes, and that the increase is concentrated on NFL game days rather than on other days or in non-alcohol crashes. I interpret this pattern as evidence of a game-day externality: mobile sports betting raises the value of live, social game watching in drinking environments, with downstream traffic-safety costs.

That is the pitch. It is cleaner, more general-interest, and more obviously economic.
