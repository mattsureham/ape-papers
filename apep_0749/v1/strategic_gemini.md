### 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
This paper examines the unintended public health consequences of the rapid legalization of online sports betting in the U.S. It asks whether the "mobile revolution" in gambling creates a behavioral complementarity with alcohol consumption that leads to more fatal car crashes, specifically on high-stakes game days.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this well, though the first paragraph is a bit "journalistic." The pitch it *should* have (and nearly has) is: 
> "While sports betting legalization is often debated in terms of tax revenue and addiction, it also fundamentally alters the social context of sports consumption. This paper documents a 'game-day externality' where online betting increases alcohol-involved fatal crashes by 15%, an effect that triples on NFL game days, suggesting a dangerous synergy between digital wagering and the American 'sports bar' ritual."

### 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
The paper identifies a significant, time-varying negative externality of online sports betting (fatalities) that outweighs the associated state tax revenues.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It is well-differentiated from Baker et al. (2023) (household finance) and Swanson (2023) (health behaviors) by focusing on a lethal, high-frequency externality.
*   **Question vs. Gap:** It successfully frames this as a question about the world (the hidden cost of a policy trend) rather than a gap in the literature.
*   **"Another DiD?":** A smart economist would see this as a clever "mechanism-led" DiD. The triple-difference on NFL days elevates it above a standard staggered adoption paper.
*   **Bigger Contribution:** To make it truly AER-level, the author needs to bridge the gap from "this happens" to "why this happens" using more than just inference. Even a small amount of data on bar foot traffic (SafeGraph/Cell phone data) or ride-share surges on game days would turn this from a "crash paper" into a "behavioral bundling" paper.

### 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
The paper sits at the intersection of Sin Taxes/Externalities and the Social Costs of Gambling.
*   **Neighbors:** Carpenter & Dobkin (2011) on drinking/mortality; DeAngelo & Hansen (2014) on enforcement; and the emerging "sports betting" cluster (Baker et al. 2023).
*   **Strategy:** It should **synthesize** the "sin goods" literature with the "digital platform" literature. It's not just about gambling; it's about how *mobile access* to gambling changes physical-world risks.
*   **Missing Conversations:** The paper is light on the "Attention Economy" or "Leisure Utility" literatures. Why does betting make the bar more attractive? Is it "skin in the game" increasing the marginal utility of a drink?

### 4. NARRATIVE ARC
*   **Setup:** The 2018 PASPA ruling triggered a gold rush for sports betting apps.
*   **Tension:** States see dollar signs (tax revenue), but ignore the "bundled" risks of how these apps are actually used (in bars, during games).
*   **Resolution:** Legalization leads to a 14.6% spike in drunk driving deaths, almost entirely concentrated on the days when the apps are most "useful" (NFL days).
*   **Implications:** The externality cost ($6.9B) dwarfs the tax revenue ($1.8B).
*   **Evaluation:** The arc is exceptionally strong. It moves from a policy shift to a specific behavioral hypothesis to a welfare-relevant conclusion.

### 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
*   **The Lead Fact:** "Legalizing FanDuel and DraftKings effectively killed 600 people a year because they stayed at the bar for the fourth quarter."
*   **The Reaction:** People lean in. This is a "water cooler" result that affects how people view a visible social change.
*   **Follow-up:** "Does this happen for the NBA too?" or "Can we fix this with more cops on Sundays?" (The paper already anticipates the latter).

### 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
*   **Front-loading:** The paper is well-structured, but the "Welfare" section (5.4) is the "AER section." It needs to be more robust. Move the math on the $6.9B vs $1.8B closer to the front or expand it.
*   **Appendix:** Move the TWFE vs. CS-DiD comparisons to the appendix once the point is made. We know TWFE is biased in staggered designs; we don't need a sermon on it in the main text.
*   **FARS Detail:** The "non-alcohol crash" placebo is the strongest part of the paper—ensure the definition of "alcohol-involved" in FARS is defended against reporting bias (e.g., do police test more on game days?).

### 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The paper is currently a "very good" applied micro paper (think *AEJ: Policy* or *JPubE*). To make it **AER**, it needs more **Ambition** regarding the behavioral mechanism.

**The single most impactful piece of advice:**
Supplement the crash data with a secondary data source that proves the "bar attendance" or "time-at-venue" mechanism—for example, showing that ride-share prices or bar-sector credit card transactions spike specifically in treated states on game days—to prove the "bundling" of betting and drinking.
