### 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
The paper examines the 2015–2017 Mercury and Air Toxics Standards (MATS), which mandated the largest reduction in hazardous air pollutants in US history, yet finds a precisely estimated zero effect on infant health (low birth weight) in affected counties. The "paradox" is that any potential health gains from cleaner air appear to be neutralized by the economic dislocation (plant closures and job losses) triggered by the high costs of regulatory compliance.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this pitch excellently in the second and fifth paragraphs. It moves quickly from the "massive regulatory shock" to the "surprising null" and provides a compelling "why" (economic trade-offs). No rewrite is needed for the intro; it is punchy and AER-caliber.

---

### 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**Contribution:** The paper provides the first national-scale causal evaluation of MATS on infant health, identifying a "compliance paradox" where economic shocks from environmental regulation offset its biological health benefits.

*   **Differentiation:** It differentiates itself from the "heroic" era of environmental econ (Isen et al. 2017) where every regulation is a win-win, and from narrow case studies (Casey et al. 2018) by using a nationwide staggered design.
*   **Framing:** It is framed as a question about the **WORLD**—specifically the trade-off between health and wealth in the energy transition—rather than a gap in the literature.
*   **Clarity:** A smart economist would summarize this as: "Pollution went down, but health didn't improve because the local economies were gutted by the compliance costs."
*   **Bigger Contribution:** To make this even bigger, the author needs to move beyond "suggestive" evidence on the economic channel. Specifically, adding outcomes like local employment or SNAP enrollment for the same counties would turn the "suggestive" mechanism into a definitive "general equilibrium" story.

---

### 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
The paper sits at the intersection of Environmental Economics (Currie/Greenstone) and the "Deaths of Despair" literature (Case & Deaton/Autor).

*   **Closest Neighbors:** Isen et al. (2017) on CAA benefits; Shapiro (2022) on pollution/employment trade-offs; and Hollingsworth et al. (2017) on coal decline.
*   **Strategy:** The paper should **synthesize** these. It builds on the health-pollution link but uses the "economic restructuring" literature to explain why the expected health gains vanished.
*   **Unexpected Connection:** Connecting this to the "Just Transition" literature (Green Industrial Policy) would broaden the audience from health economists to macro/labor/policy economists.

---

### 4. NARRATIVE ARC
*   **Setup:** EPA implements MATS, slashing mercury by 86%.
*   **Tension:** Regulatory impact analyses predicted $37-90B in health benefits. Why don't we see them in the data?
*   **Resolution:** A precisely estimated null result across three waves of compliance.
*   **Implications:** Environmental policy is not a vacuum; in "brown" communities, the economic cost of cleaning up may be a health hazard of its own.

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong. It isn't just a list of coefficients; it's a "whodunnit" where the culprit is economic dislocation.

---

### 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
At a dinner party, you lead with: "The EPA forced coal plants to cut mercury by nearly 90%, but it didn't make babies any healthier because the plant workers lost their jobs and health insurance."

*   **Reaction:** Lean in. It challenges the "free lunch" narrative of environmental regulation.
*   **Follow-up:** "Does this mean the EPA was wrong, or just that we need a better social safety net for coal towns?"

---

### 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
*   **Front-loading:** The paper is well-structured. However, the triple-diff (Table 2, Col 4) is actually the "money" result. It should be elevated earlier and perhaps given its own visualization.
*   **Data Caution:** The use of "County Health Rankings" (3-year rolling windows) is a major structural weakness for a staggered DiD. The author admits this in "Limitations." I would strongly suggest the author re-run the core analysis with annual CDC WONDER data before sending to referees; otherwise, the "null" might be dismissed as "measurement noise from smoothing."

---

### 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The gap is **Mechanism Proof.** Currently, the economic dislocation story is a "suggestive" interpretation of a capacity gradient. 

**Single Biggest Advice:** Prove the economic channel directly. Show me that the counties with the "worst" health trends under MATS are the ones that saw the sharpest drops in local GDP or employment. If you can show a 1:1 trade-off between "tons of mercury removed" and "dollars of local payroll lost," this becomes a landmark paper on the political economy of the Green Transition.
