## 1. Idea Fidelity

The paper does pursue the core manifest idea: it centers on the sharp bunching of standard-review approvals at the 300-day PDUFA deadline and asks whether deadline-induced timing distortions worsen post-market safety. It uses the FDA NME compilation and openFDA/FAERS-linked outcomes, documents a density spike at day 300, and attempts both an RD-style comparison and a bunching-based treatment definition.

That said, the executed design departs from the original promise in important ways. The manifest emphasized a local causal design around the deadline, including donut-RD and a comparison of manipulated vs. nearby counterfactual approvals. In the paper, the main estimates are not really RD estimates but controlled OLS comparisons of “bunched” drugs in a fairly wide \([295,310)\) window against drugs in \([250,400]\), which is much more observational and less plausibly quasi-experimental. The bunching analysis is also not used to recover a causal estimand for “marginal” drugs in the way the manifest suggested. So the paper follows the broad idea, but the final empirical strategy falls short of the original identification ambition.

## 2. Summary

This paper documents substantial bunching of FDA standard-review drug approvals at the 300-day PDUFA deadline and asks whether drugs approved in this deadline window have worse post-market safety outcomes. The main finding is that bunched drugs have more adverse-event reports in raw comparisons, but these differences disappear after controlling for therapeutic class, approval year, and time on market; the paper concludes that PDUFA deadlines distort timing but not safety.

## 3. Essential Points

1. **The causal identification argument is not currently convincing.** The paper acknowledges manipulation of the running variable, but then treats the setting as if standard RD logic still applies. That is not enough here, especially because the paper’s own balance table shows discontinuities in predetermined covariates (orphan and fast-track status), directly undermining continuity at the cutoff. Given the tiny number of observations just below 300 days, the RD evidence is essentially uninformative, and the paper’s headline conclusion rests on controlled comparisons that are observational rather than quasi-experimental.

2. **The outcome data and sample construction need much more validation.** Only 312 of 538 drugs link to FAERS, and the main comparison sample falls to 175. This is a major source of potential selection bias, especially if linkage or market presence is correlated with review timing or approval era. More fundamentally, cumulative FAERS counts are an extremely noisy measure of drug safety because they reflect time on market, utilization, indication size, publicity, and reporting intensity. The current controls are not sufficient to justify strong conclusions about safety.

3. **There are serious internal inconsistencies that must be resolved before the results can be evaluated.** Most notably, the paper states that predetermined covariates are smooth at the cutoff, but Table 4 reports highly significant jumps in orphan and fast-track status. Several tables and notes also appear inconsistent or erroneous (for example, p-values that do not match estimates and standard errors; text saying “none significant” when the table shows otherwise; sample sizes shifting across sections without a clear accounting). In its current form, the empirical narrative is not fully coherent.

## 4. Suggestions

My overall view is that this is an interesting paper with a potentially publishable descriptive fact, but not yet a persuasive causal paper. The strongest path forward is to narrow the claim: the paper can make a useful contribution by documenting deadline bunching and showing that simple correlations between deadline approval and post-market safety are highly sensitive to composition and exposure controls. That is already informative. But the current title, framing, and conclusion overstate what the evidence supports.

A first priority is to **rebuild the identification section around what the data can actually sustain**. Right now the paper oscillates between three designs: a formal RD, a bunching design, and a controlled treated-vs-control comparison. These are not interchangeable. Given the severe asymmetry around the cutoff and the manipulation of the running variable, the RD should probably be demoted from “main design” to “suggestive local diagnostic.” If the authors want to preserve a causal interpretation, they need to explain much more clearly why manipulated timing around day 300 approximates random assignment for marginal applications. At minimum, I would want to see a serious treatment of the bunching framework: define the excess mass, characterize the likely origins of the bunchers, and estimate bounds or local average effects for the inframarginal/marginal units implied by the bunching model. As written, the bunching exercise is mostly descriptive and does not deliver a causal parameter.

Relatedly, the paper should **confront rather than downplay the covariate discontinuities**. Table 4 appears to show meaningful sorting in orphan and fast-track status at the cutoff. That directly contradicts the continuity claim. The authors should verify whether these are coding errors, artifacts of the very small local sample, or genuine evidence of compositional change. If genuine, they must either (i) abandon the RD interpretation, or (ii) present a design that conditions on these dimensions more carefully, perhaps within a narrower and more homogeneous sample. A natural step would be to stratify by approval era and therapeutic area, and possibly exclude years or divisions where the administrative process changed substantially.

The paper also needs a **much more transparent sample flow and data audit**. Readers need to know: of the 538 standard-review approvals, how many are NMEs vs BLAs, how many are marketed, how many have valid NDA/BLA identifiers usable in openFDA, how many link to any FAERS report, and how many enter each regression. A single CONSORT-style sample figure would help enormously. The statement that drugs without linkage are “typically older drugs approved before electronic reporting systems were established or drugs that were never marketed” is plausible, but too vague for a paper whose conclusions depend heavily on observed post-market safety counts. The authors should show whether linkage success is related to review duration, approval year, therapeutic class, or other observables.

On outcome measurement, I strongly recommend **moving away from raw cumulative FAERS counts as the primary outcome** unless exposure is handled much better. Even with years-on-market controls or offsets, adverse-event counts mainly reflect how many patients used the drug, not just its risk profile. This is especially problematic if deadline-bunched drugs differ in market size or indication prevalence. More credible safety outcomes would include: withdrawals, major safety labeling changes, boxed-warning additions with timing, REMS impositions, safety-related Dear Doctor letters, or post-market study requirements. These are rarer but more policy-relevant and less contaminated by utilization. If the authors want to keep FAERS, they should ideally construct rates using some proxy for exposure—prescription volume, sales, or claims-based utilization. Without that, the interpretation should be explicitly limited to “reported adverse event volume,” not safety per se.

A closely related suggestion is to **use outcome timing rather than cumulative totals where possible**. For example, the probability of a boxed warning within 3, 5, or 10 years of approval is more interpretable than whether the current label has a boxed warning in 2024. Likewise, recall/withdrawal hazards or event-study style survival outcomes would map more naturally to the question of whether rushed approval produces earlier post-market safety problems. This would also help with the severe confounding by approval cohort and time on market.

The paper would also benefit from **a more credible comparison set**. The current \([250,400]\) window for the OLS comparison is very broad, and the authors’ own descriptive tables suggest substantial heterogeneity across that span. I would encourage a design closer to the original manifest: compare drugs approved just before the deadline to those just after, possibly excluding the exact bunching mass (“donut” approach), and then present the wider-window OLS only as a robustness exercise. Even if power is limited, that would align the paper’s implementation more closely with its conceptual claim. If sample size remains too small, that is an important finding in itself: the paper may simply not be able to support precise causal estimates.

There are also several **basic presentation and consistency issues** that should be fixed. Table notes and text repeatedly say that balance tests show no significant discontinuities, but the displayed p-values say otherwise. Some p-values in the bandwidth table do not appear compatible with the estimates and standard errors reported. The summary statistics table mixes a “full sample” of 312 with an “analysis window” of 175, while the text often refers to 538 drugs; the transitions between these samples are not cleanly explained. I would urge the authors to systematically reconcile every sample count and every inferential statement in the text with the underlying tables. For a short paper, credibility depends heavily on this sort of internal consistency.

Substantively, the conclusion should be **reframed more modestly**. “The FDA appears to manage deadline pressure without sacrificing review thoroughness” goes beyond the evidence. A better statement would be something like: “We find strong evidence that PDUFA induces approval bunching at the 300-day deadline. In our data, raw post-market safety differences between deadline-approved and nearby drugs are largely explained by observable composition and exposure differences, and the remaining evidence for a causal safety penalty is imprecise.” That would still be a meaningful contribution and would be much easier to defend.

Finally, I think the paper could make a stronger contribution by emphasizing its **descriptive institutional insight**. The bunching itself is striking and worth documenting carefully over time: did it intensify after particular PDUFA reauthorizations? Does it differ across review divisions, therapeutic classes, or political administrations? Is there a similar pattern at the 180-day priority deadline? Does the secondary 360–370 day spike reflect clock stops or complete response behavior? A short AER: Insights paper does not need to solve everything, but it does need one clean, credible takeaway. Right now the paper is closest to delivering that on the timing-distortion margin, not on the causal safety margin.

In short: the project is promising, but the paper should either substantially strengthen the causal design or scale back its causal claims and reposition itself as a careful descriptive/diagnostic paper about how deadline-driven regulatory timing relates to observed post-market safety measures.
