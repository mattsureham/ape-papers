## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
The paper uses the institutional "spike" in FDA drug approvals occurring exactly at the 300-day PDUFA deadline to test whether regulatory haste compromises drug safety. While there is a massive raw correlation between deadline-bunched drugs and adverse events, the paper argues this is entirely a product of selection—specifically therapeutic class and time-on-market—rather than a causal "deadline effect" on review quality.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this clearly. The first two paragraphs effectively bridge the gap between a specific regulatory quirk (the PDUFA clock) and a broader economic question (the trade-off between speed and quality in bureaucracy). The pitch is solid.

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
The paper’s contribution is providing the first quasi-experimental "null" result on the safety costs of PDUFA deadlines by using a bunching/RD design to debunk a widely cited correlational alarm.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It differentiates itself from Carpenter (2008) and Downing (2017) by moving from correlation to a bunching/discontinuity framework.
*   **Question:** It frames the question about the **world** (Is the FDA killing people with deadlines?) which is much stronger than filling a gap in the literature.
*   **The "DiD" trap:** A smart economist would see this as a "bunching" paper. The risk is that it feels like a "forensic" correction of a medical journal finding rather than a foundational economic insight.
*   **Bigger Contribution:** To make this "AER big," the paper needs to move beyond "the FDA is doing okay" to a broader theory of **optimal bureaucratic bunching**. Why do they bunch? Is there a model where bunching is the *efficient* response to a performance metric?

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
The paper sits at the intersection of Health Econ, Industrial Organization (Regulation), and Public Finance (Bunching methods).

*   **Closest Neighbors:** Saez (2010)/Kleven (2016) for method; Carpenter (2010) and Budish et al. (2015) for context.
*   **Positioning:** It currently positions itself as an ombudsman for the FDA. It should pivot to build on the **Economics of Bureaucracy**. It needs to speak to the literature on "gaming" performance metrics (e.g., Holmstrom & Milgrom).
*   **Unexpected Connection:** It should connect to the literature on "Time-Inconsistency" or "Procrastination" in public agencies. Is this bunching evidence of procrastination or efficient batch processing?

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC
*   **Setup:** Regulatory agencies face strict deadlines to speed up innovation.
*   **Tension:** Critics say these deadlines force "Friday afternoon" approvals of dangerous drugs; raw data shows bunched drugs are indeed more dangerous.
*   **Resolution:** The danger is an optical illusion. Once you control for the fact that certain (riskier) classes of drugs are more likely to be pushed to the deadline, the safety gap vanishes. 
*   **Implications:** Bureaucratic deadlines may distort *timing* without distorting *quality*.

**Evaluation:** The arc is very clean. It’s a "detective story" where the primary suspect (the deadline) is exonerated by a clever use of institutional detail.

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
**The Lead Fact:** "The 10-fold spike in drug approvals at the PDUFA deadline is a pure timing game that doesn't actually cost lives."
**The Reaction:** Economists will lean in because they love "debunking" papers, especially those that use sophisticated tools (RD/Bunching) to overturn a scary headline. 
**The Follow-up:** "If it doesn't affect quality, why is the FDA bunching so hard? What are they delaying/accelerating specifically?"

---

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
*   **The RD is weak:** The author admits only 11 observations are below the cutoff. The RD section (Table 4) feels like "Econometric theater." I would move the formal RD to the appendix and focus the main paper on the **Bunching + Controlled OLS** comparison. 
*   **Mechanism Section:** Expand Section 5.4. This is the heart of the paper. We need to see more on *why* these specific drugs bunch. Are they more complex? Are they from smaller firms?
*   **Front-loading:** The contrast in Table 3 (Unconditional vs. Conditional) is the money shot. It should be discussed even more prominently in the intro.

---

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The gap between "Solid Health Econ" and "AER" here is **Ambition**. Currently, it is a very high-quality "note" that corrects a JAMA-style correlation.

**The Single Most Impactful Advice:** Move from a "Safety Null" to a "Selection Theory." Develop a simple model or more empirical evidence on the **selection mechanism**. If the FDA "saves" certain drugs for the deadline, why *those* drugs? Is it because they are the marginal "tough calls" or because they are administrative laggards? If you can explain the *logic* of the bunching, this becomes a paper about how agents respond to deadlines, which is a core AER-level interest.
