## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether South Korea’s headline 2018 cut in the legal maximum workweek—from 68 to 52 hours—actually reduced hours worked, and whether enforcement capacity determined where the law bit. Using industry-level variation in pre-reform overtime intensity, it argues that the reform did reduce hours, with the largest adjustments appearing when medium-sized firms came under the law, suggesting that labor standards matter only where the state can credibly enforce them.

A busy economist should care because the broader question is important: when governments tighten labor standards, do firms actually comply, and which parts of the economy change behavior? South Korea is a potentially powerful setting because the reform was large, salient, and politically consequential in one of the OECD’s longest-hours economies.

**Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Not quite. The opening is vivid and informative, but it is too descriptive and too Korea-specific before telling the reader the larger economic question. The current introduction spends more time on institutional detail and social context than on the paper’s economic stake. The paper needs to get to the general-interest question immediately: do statutory hours caps change actual labor-market behavior, and how does enforcement shape compliance?

**What the first two paragraphs should say instead:**

> Governments around the world regulate hours, wages, and working conditions, but the central question is not what the law says—it is whether firms comply. This is especially true for hours regulation: statutory caps are common, yet we know surprisingly little about when they reduce actual hours worked and when they remain largely symbolic.  
>  
> This paper studies one of the most ambitious recent labor-regulation reforms in an OECD country: South Korea’s 2018 reduction in the legal maximum workweek from 68 to 52 hours. I show that industries with greater pre-reform exposure to overtime reduced hours more after the reform, and that the timing of the response aligns with the staggered extension of the law to medium-sized firms. The broader lesson is that the effect of labor standards depends not just on legal design, but on enforcement reach.

That is the pitch the paper should have. Start with the general question, then the setting, then the main finding, then the implication.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution in one sentence:**  
The paper claims to show that South Korea’s 52-hour workweek reform reduced actual hours worked in more overtime-intensive industries, with the pattern of adjustment suggesting that enforcement capacity determines where labor standards bind.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?
Only partially. The paper does a reasonable job saying “this is not France, this is Korea,” but it does not yet clearly establish what is conceptually new relative to prior work on working-time regulations or labor-law compliance. Right now the novelty is a mix of:
1. new country,
2. new reform episode,
3. suggestive enforcement story.

That is not enough unless one of those is made much sharper. “First English-language causal estimate” is not an AER contribution. It is a dissemination fact, not an economic contribution.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a gap in the literature?
Too much as a literature gap, not enough as a world question. “First English-language causal estimate” and “novel setting” are weak forms of contribution. The stronger framing is: **What determines whether labor regulation changes real outcomes?** If that is the true contribution, the introduction should revolve around that.

### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what’s new here?
At present, they would probably say: “It’s a DiD paper on Korea’s hours reform, with some suggestive evidence that medium-sized firms are where compliance happens.” That is not yet memorable enough. The memorable version would be: “This paper says labor standards have a compliance frontier: the law bites most in firms large enough to be visible but not large enough to have already adapted.”

### What would make this contribution bigger?
Several possibilities:

- **Make enforcement the actual object, not an interpretation.** Right now “compliance cascade” is a label attached to timing patterns. If the paper wants to be about enforcement, it needs direct evidence on firm-size composition, inspections, penalties, exemptions, or sectoral exposure to state capacity.
- **Move beyond mean hours.** Average weekly hours is a thin outcome. A bigger paper would speak to the distribution of hours, incidence of extreme overtime, employment, wages, worker well-being, or substitution margins.
- **Exploit the staggered firm-size thresholds more directly.** The most interesting feature of the reform is not just that Korea changed the law; it is that implementation varied by firm size. A micro design around that would be much more ambitious and much more persuasive as a paper about compliance.
- **Connect to broader labor-regulation questions.** Is this about hours caps specifically, or about the enforcement of labor law more generally? The latter is bigger.

The biggest upgrade would be to reframe the paper from “Did Korea’s law reduce hours?” to “Where is labor law enforceable, and what part of the firm distribution constitutes the compliance margin?”

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Before this paper, we know governments impose hours limits, but actual compliance is uncertain. South Korea presents an extreme setting: very long hours, major reform, staggered rollout, high political salience.

### Tension
A legal reform can look dramatic on paper while doing little in practice. Aggregate hours fell in Korea, but that could reflect secular trends, composition, or COVID. More fundamentally, even if laws matter on average, we do not know where compliance occurs across the economy.

### Resolution
The paper finds larger post-reform declines in hours in industries with greater pre-reform overtime exposure, with the strongest timing aligned with the extension to medium-sized firms.

### Implications
The paper wants the implication to be: labor standards work only where enforcement is credible, and medium-sized firms may be the key compliance margin.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
It has the beginnings of one, but not a fully disciplined one. The narrative is currently split between:
- “Korea had very long hours and passed a big reform,”
- “I estimate a modest differential decline,”
- “This implies a compliance cascade.”

The last step is too fast. The paper has results; it does not yet fully have the story that matches them. Specifically, “compliance cascade” sounds like the paper’s real contribution, but the evidence for that mechanism is explicitly exploratory and indirect. That creates a mismatch between the paper’s title, rhetoric, and actual design.

### If it is a collection of results looking for a story, what story should it be telling?
The right story is:

1. **Big, visible labor reforms are not self-executing.**
2. **South Korea is an ideal setting to study this because the reform was large and staggered.**
3. **Industries more exposed to the cap reduced hours more.**
4. **The timing points to a specific compliance margin: the law mattered most when it reached firms that were both numerous and enforceable.**
5. **Therefore, the effect of labor standards depends on enforcement reach, not just statutory ambition.**

But the paper should present the enforcement point as a disciplined inference, not a triumphant slogan. Right now the title oversells what the data can actually establish.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?
I would say: “When Korea cut the legal maximum workweek from 68 to 52 hours, the actual reduction in hours appears to have happened mostly when the law reached medium-sized firms—not when it first hit the largest firms.”

That is a potentially good fact.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
Some would lean in, but only if you immediately connect it to a bigger point about compliance and enforcement. If you present it as “an industry-level DiD on Korean working hours,” many will check out.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Almost certainly: **“Do you actually observe firm size or enforcement?”**  
And then: **“Did firms cut hours by hiring more workers, cutting output, lowering wages, or relabeling hours?”**

Those are exactly the questions that reveal the current paper’s limits.

### If findings are modest: is the modest result itself interesting?
Potentially yes. A modest average effect can still matter if the paper’s point is that dramatic labor laws often generate only partial compliance. But then the paper must own that lesson. It should not simultaneously market the reform as historic and the effect as transformative while showing relatively modest industry-average changes.

The null/modest aspect is interesting if framed as: **even a sweeping reform with criminal penalties only partly changed actual work patterns, and only in the enforceable middle of the firm distribution.** That is a real insight. But the paper needs to make that case explicitly.
