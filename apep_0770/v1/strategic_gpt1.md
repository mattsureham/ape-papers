## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a politically salient question: when the state withdraws a visible local public service, do voters punish the political system by turning to the far right? Using maternity ward closures across France, it argues that one of the most vivid symbols of “state abandonment” does not produce a detectable increase in Rassemblement National vote share.

A busy economist should care because the paper takes a broad and influential claim in political economy and regional economics—public-service retreat breeds populist backlash—and tests it in a concrete, emotionally charged setting. If the result is credible and well framed, it is not “a paper about maternity wards”; it is a paper about the limits of one of the dominant narratives explaining the geography of discontent.

**Does the paper articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Not quite. The opening is reasonably vivid, but it slips too quickly into France-specific scene-setting and then into a literature-gap formulation. The paper’s real hook is stronger than that: it is a direct test of a widely repeated causal story. The introduction should state this upfront and more sharply.

**What the first two paragraphs should say instead:**  
For the last decade, a central explanation for the rise of populism has been that voters in peripheral places react against the retreat of the state. As schools, hospitals, rail lines, and other public services disappear, citizens infer abandonment and reward anti-system parties. This narrative is enormously influential in policy debate and academic work, but there is surprisingly little causal evidence on whether the loss of a specific local public service actually changes voting behavior.

This paper tests that claim using the closure of maternity wards across France. Maternity units are among the most visible and symbolically charged public services the state provides, especially in rural communities. Yet I find that increases in distance to maternity care caused by ward closures do not generate a detectable rise in far-right vote share. The result suggests that the correlation between local service deprivation and populist voting may reflect broader social and economic decline rather than a direct electoral response to individual service losses.

That is the pitch. It is cleaner, more ambitious, and more obviously of general interest.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:**  
The paper provides a quasi-experimental test of whether the local withdrawal of a salient public healthcare service causes greater far-right support, and finds little evidence that it does.

### Evaluation

**Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?**  
Partially, but not yet sharply enough. The paper says prior work is mostly correlational and that quasi-experimental evidence has focused on austerity or commercial decline rather than specific public services. That is directionally correct, but the differentiation remains generic. Right now, the contribution reads as “I do DiD on a new service-loss setting,” which is not enough for AER-level positioning. It needs to say more explicitly:

- existing “geography of discontent” papers mostly establish **where** populism rises, not **which mechanisms** cause it;
- existing causal papers typically study **income shocks, austerity, trade, immigration, or commercial decline**, not **symbolically salient local state withdrawal**;
- this setting is a hard test because maternity wards are unusually visible, politically resonant, and emotionally meaningful.

That last point matters a lot. The contribution is bigger if the paper is framed as a **most-likely test** of the abandonment hypothesis: if even this kind of service closure does not move far-right voting, then the simple version of the hypothesis is weaker than commonly assumed.

**Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or as filling a gap in a literature?**  
It is mixed, but too much of the introduction is still framed as “the causal evidence remains thin.” That is a literature-gap framing. The stronger framing is: **Do people politically punish the state when it withdraws local public services?** That is a world question.

**Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what's new?**  
Somewhat, but many would still summarize it as “a DiD paper on service closures and RN voting.” That is a warning sign. The newness has to be memorable: “It tests one of the canonical explanations for populism and finds the emblematic service-closure channel may not be doing much.”

**What would make this contribution bigger?**  
Most importantly, the paper needs to move from a narrow healthcare-closure study to a broader test of political interpretation. Specifically:

1. **Broaden the outcome beyond FN/RN vote share alone.**

Trust, anti-incumbent voting, protest, abstention, ballot fragmentation, local mobilization, or issue salience. 3. **Compare to other forms of state retreat.**  
   The paper would be bigger if it positioned maternity closures as one node in a broader map of state withdrawal. Even a conceptual comparison—maternities versus post offices, schools, rail, emergency rooms—would help clarify whether the paper is testing a general mechanism or only one narrow channel.

4. **Make the contribution about revising a big narrative.**

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
A dominant account of recent European populism says peripheral places feel abandoned as public services disappear, and this abandonment fuels support for anti-system parties.

### Tension
This claim is highly influential but causally under-tested. Observed correlations between poor service access and populist voting may reflect long-run decline rather than voters responding to specific withdrawals of the state.

### Resolution
Using maternity ward closures in France, the paper finds little evidence that increases in distance to maternity care raise far-right vote share.

### Implications
The political consequences of territorial decline may not operate through marginal losses of individual public services. Scholars and policymakers may need to distinguish between diffuse, cumulative decline and the electoral effect of specific closures.

### Evaluation of narrative arc

The paper **does have the ingredients of a narrative arc**, but it does not fully exploit them. Too much of the paper reads like a competent empirical note with results and robustness, rather than a paper with a strong conceptual story.

The main weakness is that the paper has not decided what kind of paper it is:

- a France paper about maternity services?
- a null-results paper on one policy shock?
- a general test of the abandonment hypothesis?
- a methodological caution against reading causality off deprivation maps?

It gestures at all four. That dilutes the story.

**What story should it be telling?**  
The best story is:

1. A powerful explanation of populism holds that local state retreat produces anti-system voting.
2. Maternity ward closures are a highly salient and emotionally potent form of state retreat, making this a strong setting to test that explanation.
3. Yet the paper finds no detectable electoral response in far-right voting.
4. Therefore, the common “service closure → populist backlash” narrative is at best incomplete and may be attributing too much to visible local cuts and too little to slower-moving structural transformations.

That is a coherent setup-tension-resolution-implications sequence. The current draft only partially gets there.

### Is the null result itself interesting?

Yes—but only if the paper embraces what makes the null informative.

A null can be interesting when:
- it tests a widely believed causal claim;
- it studies a setting where the theory should work especially well;
- it narrows or revises a big narrative.

This paper has the raw material for that. But at present it is too defensive and too local. It often sounds like “we didn’t find much.” That makes it feel like a failed attempt rather than a substantive lesson.

The paper needs to argue more forcefully that this is **not a disappointing null in a random context**. It is a null in a setting where many people would have predicted a positive effect. That is exactly why it matters.

Yes, strongly. The paper’s strongest asset is that it tests a big, fashionable, policy-relevant causal story and finds a boundary condition or contradiction. But it still presents itself too much like a tidy application paper on a French healthcare setting.

### Is it a scope problem?
Also yes. For AER, one narrow electoral outcome in one service domain is probably not enough unless the paper is framed as decisively revising a broad theory. The author likely needs either:
