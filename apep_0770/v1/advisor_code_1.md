**Idea Fidelity**

The paper is broadly faithful to the original idea manifest. It uses the DREES maternity ward registry for closure timing, commune-level election returns from data.gouv.fr, and exploits the staggered rationalization of public maternity services to study RN voting as prescribed. However, several key elements in the manifest are either missing or have been substantially altered. Most notably, the paper abandons the superior design of using French presidential and legislative elections (manifest specified both); instead it relies on presidential and European Parliament outcomes, which have different political dynamics and may be less sensitive to localized service shocks. The manifest’s emphasis on commune-specific event-study DiD (including Callaway-Sant’Anna ATT and pre-trend validation) is present, but the paper’s realization of these elements is internally inconsistent (e.g., the main results table reports coefficients that contradict the text discussion and the ATT numbers are incoherent). Finally, the manifest mentions a fuzzy RD based on the 300-delivery threshold and a mechanism placebo controlling for other public services; neither appears in the paper. These departures weaken the paper’s fidelity to the original design.

**Summary**

This paper studies whether nearby maternity ward closures, a visible form of state withdrawal, causally influence far-right support in France. Using a panel of 34,791 metropolitan communes across nine national elections and constructing commune-level distance shocks to nearby maternity services, the author runs TWFE and Callaway-Sant’Anna specifications and reports null effects of increased distance on FN/RN vote share, turnout, and left-populist voting. The paper concludes that the “state abandonment” narrative is not supported by causal evidence from healthcare service rationalization.

**Essential Points**

1. **Identification is weak because the paper acknowledges failed parallel trends but does not resolve them.** The pre-trend test rejects at \(p=0.0003\). The TWFE coefficients therefore cannot be interpreted causally, especially given the staggered nature of treatment and the documented differential dynamics (the event-study coefficients fluctuate substantially before treatment). Without a convincing correction—e.g., conditioning on pre-trend slopes, implementing synthetic controls, or exploiting the 300-delivery threshold via an RD—the claimed causal inference is unjustified. Either re-specify the empirical strategy to credibly isolate the closure shock or pivot to a more descriptive analysis that does not rely on the parallel trends assumption.

2. **The empirical tables and reported results are internally inconsistent and raise concerns about estimation validity.** Table 2’s coefficients (e.g., column 1 reports \(0.0097\) with SE \(0.0351\)) differ from the narrative stating \(-0.03\). Column 4 reports an ATT of \(1.7507\) (SE \(1.4907\)) while the text claims \(-0.28\), and the event-study table includes implausible values with incomplete SEs (“NA”) and wildly varying coefficients. These inconsistencies make it impossible to trust the estimates. The authors must clarify the exact specifications, provide accurate and consistent tables, and explain how the Callaway-Sant’Anna ATT was computed. Without transparent reporting, readers cannot assess the empirical evidence.

3. **Threats from endogenous closures and omitted variables remain unaddressed.** Closures respond to declining birth volumes, which correlate with rural decline, aging populations, and political realignment. Commune fixed effects and time dummies are insufficient if the treatment groups are on different trends. The manifest’s proposed fuzzy RD around the 300-delivery threshold is absent, yet that design is one of the few ways to isolate quasi-random closures. Without exploiting that mechanism or providing convincing instruments, the paper cannot disentangle the causal effect of the closure itself from the socio-economic trajectory that prompted it. The authors must either implement the RD/instrument or confess the limitations and perhaps reframe the study as correlational with sensitivity analyses.

Given these fundamental issues, the paper cannot be recommended for publication in its current form.

**Suggestions**

- **Reconcile the data and tables with the textual claims.** Ensure that the TWFE coefficients reported in Table 2 (and elsewhere) match the values discussed in the text. If the main specification yields \(-0.03\) as reported, that number should appear in the table with the correct standard error and \(p\)-value. Provide full-event-study graphs with confidence bands to visualize the pre- and post-trends. For the Callaway-Sant’Anna results, describe the cohort definitions, the reference period, and how the overall ATT is aggregated; provide both the raw cohort-specific ATTs and the aggregate statistic. Cleaning up these inconsistencies is essential for credibility.

- **Address the failed parallel trends via alternative strategies.** Since the pre-trend test rejects, consider incorporating commune-specific linear or quadratic time trends, or use a matching-on-trends approach to pair treated and control communes with similar pre-treatment trajectories. Better yet, follow the manifest’s suggestion to exploit the 300-delivery minimum volume rule as a fuzzy RD or regression kink design: localidades near the threshold are quasi-exogenously more likely to close, which would allow a comparison of voting outcomes across communes that are similar in observable characteristics but differ in their probability of experiencing a closure. At minimum, show event studies for matched subsets where pre-trends align.

- **Clarify the treatment construction and its timing.** The manifest envisioned a binary indicator for losing the last local maternity ward as well as a continuous distance treatment; the paper focuses only on distance changes. Why use Haversine distance instead of actual travel times or the count of closures within a catchment? How sensitive are results to alternative distance thresholds (e.g., 20 km)? Furthermore, describe how you distinguish between a commune whose own maternity closes versus one affected indirectly because the nearest facility shut down. Are commuters reassigning to adjacent departments? Adding a sensitivity analysis using travel times from actual road networks (or bounding via accessibility measures) would strengthen the interpretation.

- **Re-evaluate the placebo tests and heterogeneity results.** The negative coefficient on the proximity indicator raises the possibility that treatment is correlated with other shifting forces (e.g., urbanization). Provide stratified analyses (e.g., stratify by demographic decline, income change, or labor market shocks) to show whether the null persists. Discuss why LFI/Mélenchon votes are chosen as the placebo—would we expect the same mechanisms to operate? Consider additional outcomes such as abstention or smaller parties to triangulate the political response. The event-study tables currently have missing standard errors (NA); fill these gaps and interpret whether the wide uncertainty is due to small treated samples or noisy data.

- **Improve discussion of mechanisms and policy implications.** The conclusion draws broad statements about the state-abandonment hypothesis without fully substantiating the null result. Acknowledge that maternity ward closures may not be salient to the general electorate and discuss whether the effect may differ for specific subgroups (young families, pregnant women, local clinicians). If the estimated effect truly is zero, calculate the power: what effect size could you detect with your sample? This would situate the null result in context. Lastly, consider linking your findings to other public services (e.g., schools or post offices) and explaining whether you expect different mechanisms for those closures.

- **Supplement with stronger documentation of the closure process.** The claim that the ARS makes closures independent of politics should be supported with institutional detail or citations. Do local politicians sometimes succeed in keeping a ward open for political reasons? If so, this might introduce endogeneity. Incorporate qualitative evidence or descriptive statistics showing that closures are indeed driven by delivery volumes rather than electoral variables.

- **Document data-processing steps in an appendix.** Since you merge multiple large administrative datasets, provide clear replication materials: how were facility closures coded, how were distances computed, what imputation (if any) was used for missing data? This will aid future researchers and improve transparency.

Implementing these suggestions will go a long way toward making the manuscript publishable. A reworked version that resolves the identification and reporting issues could contribute meaningfully to the literature on state withdrawal and populism.
