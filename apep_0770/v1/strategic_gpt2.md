## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a sharp political-economy question: when the state withdraws a highly salient local public service, do voters turn to the far right? Using maternity ward closures across French communes, it argues that one emblematic form of “state abandonment” does **not** produce a detectable increase in Rassemblement National vote share, challenging a widely repeated story about the political consequences of territorial neglect.

A busy economist should care because the “geography of discontent” narrative has become one of the dominant explanations for populist voting in Europe, but much of that literature is correlational and bundles many forms of decline together. A paper that cleanly tests one concrete mechanism could matter.

### Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Reasonably, but not optimally. The current opening is vivid, but it spends too much time setting up the broad “peripheral France” motif before making the sharper claim: this is not a paper about French politics generally; it is a paper about whether a **specific, symbolically potent public-service closure** changes voting. The introduction also reveals the null too quickly and too mechanically, before the reader is fully convinced why this particular service should have been a strong test case.

### What the first two paragraphs should say instead

The first two paragraphs should say something like:

> A central claim in contemporary political economy is that when the state retreats from lagging places, voters respond by backing populist parties. But most evidence for this “state abandonment” hypothesis is cross-sectional: places with fewer services vote differently, yet those places also differ in many deeper ways. What is missing is evidence on whether the loss of a concrete, salient public service itself changes political behavior.
>
> This paper studies the closure of maternity wards in France as a hard test of that mechanism. Maternity wards are among the most visible and emotionally charged local public services: their closure is publicly contested, symbolically resonant, and disproportionately concentrated in the kinds of peripheral areas often invoked in the geography-of-discontent narrative. I show that, despite this salience, communes that lose nearby maternity access do not exhibit a detectable increase in far-right voting. The result suggests that the political geography of RN support is driven less by marginal service withdrawal than by broader structural or identity-based forces.

That is the pitch the paper should have.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper offers a quasi-experimental test of whether the local withdrawal of a salient public healthcare service—maternity wards—causally increases far-right voting in France, and finds no detectable effect.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?

Only partially. The paper distinguishes itself from broad correlational “geography of discontent” work, but the differentiation is not yet crisp enough. Right now the contribution reads as:

- “Here is one more DiD on populism and public services.”
  
It needs to read as:

- “Most of the literature infers political backlash from broad spatial deprivation; this paper isolates one of the most symbolically powerful and politically discussed forms of state retreat and finds that the canonical mechanism does not show up.”

That is a sharper intellectual move.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a gap in a literature?

It starts with a world question, which is good: does state withdrawal breed populism? But it drifts into “this literature is mostly correlational and I add causal evidence,” which is a weaker framing. AER papers generally win when they say: “People believe X about how politics works. Here is evidence that X is wrong / conditional / overstated.”

This paper should be framed less as “filling the causal gap” and more as “testing a central factual claim in contemporary political economy.”

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?

At present: maybe, but not confidently. They would probably say, “It’s a DiD paper on maternity ward closures and RN voting in France, with a null.” That is not enough.

You want them to say: “It’s a paper showing that one of the most salient forms of local state retrenchment doesn’t seem to move far-right voting, which weakens a popular mechanism behind the geography-of-discontent story.”

That is closer to an AER-grade takeaway.

### What would make the contribution bigger?

Be specific:

1. **Broaden the political outcome beyond RN vote share alone.**  
   If the paper wants to challenge the state-abandonment hypothesis, it should show where the political response does and does not go: trust, anti-incumbent voting, protest abstention, municipal outcomes, candidate entry, or support for anti-system parties more broadly. Right now “no effect on RN” is narrower than “no political backlash.”

2. **Leverage maternity wards as a “most likely case.”**  
   The paper should argue more forcefully that if any service closure should matter politically, this one should. That means emphasizing symbolic salience, media attention, local protest, and community identity—not just kilometers.

3. **Connect to a broader bundle of service withdrawal or to comparative categories of closure.**  
   As currently framed, the paper tests one narrow service. To make the contribution bigger, it could situate maternity closures against other closures—schools, post offices, emergency care, rail stations, tax offices. Even if only maternity is estimated here, the framing should make clear why this is informative about a broader theory.

4. **Make the mechanism argument more conceptual.**  
   The interesting question is not just whether closures matter, but whether populism responds to **marginal local shocks** or to **cumulative narratives of decline**. That contrast is lurking in the discussion and should be central.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

A common view in European political economy is that peripheral places lose public services, feel abandoned, and respond by voting for the far right.

### Tension

But the evidence for that mechanism is mostly spatial and correlational. We do not know whether the withdrawal of a salient local public service itself causes political backlash, or whether service loss simply tags places already on distinct trajectories.

### Resolution

This paper studies maternity ward closures in France—a setting where symbolism and salience should be unusually strong—and finds no detectable increase in RN vote share.

### Implications

The implication is not merely “this treatment has no effect.” The implication is that the standard state-abandonment story may be overstated, too localist, or conceptually misspecified: voters may respond to broad narratives of cumulative territorial decline rather than to individual service closures.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the ingredients, but not the discipline. At present it feels somewhat like a collection of estimates looking for a story. The null is reported clearly, but the paper has not fully decided what kind of paper it is:

- a France paper,
- a healthcare-access paper,
- a populism paper,
- or a methodology-to-correct-correlational-narratives paper.

The best version is the third and fourth combined: **a paper about testing a canonical mechanism in the populism literature using a particularly salient public-service shock**.

### What story should it be telling?

Not “maternity closures don’t matter.”

Rather:

> “One of the most plausible and symbolically potent examples of local state retreat does not measurably increase far-right voting. That weakens a widely invoked causal mechanism behind the geography-of-discontent narrative and shifts attention toward broader structural and identity-based explanations.”

That is a real story.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?

“If closing a local maternity ward in France—a politically explosive symbol of state retreat—doesn’t move far-right voting, then the usual story that local service withdrawal mechanically breeds populism is probably too simple.”

That is the dinner-party line.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

Among political economists and applied micro people, some would lean in—**if** that is the line. If the line is just “null effect of maternity closures on RN voting,” they reach for their phones.

The paper’s salience depends almost entirely on whether it convincingly presents itself as a test of a major mechanism, not a narrow French null.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Probably one of these:

1. “So if not service closures, then what *does* explain the geography-of-discontent pattern?”
2. “Is maternity too narrow or too infrequent to matter?”
3. “Does the effect show up in anti-incumbent voting, turnout, protest, or trust rather than RN support?”
4. “Is this telling us something about cumulative decline versus discrete shocks?”

Those are good follow-up questions. The paper should anticipate them in the introduction, not only in the discussion.

### Is the null itself interesting?

Potentially yes—but only if sold correctly.

A null is interesting here if:
- the treatment is plausibly salient,
- the mechanism is central in public and academic debate,
- and the paper makes clear that this was a **most-likely case** for finding an effect.

At present, the paper does some of this, but not enough. It still risks feeling like a failed search for significance rather than a meaningful negative result. The authors need to earn the null by making the reader believe this should have mattered if the standard story were right.
