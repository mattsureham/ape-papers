## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper tests the "state-abandonment" theory of populism by examining whether the closure of local maternity wards—a highly salient loss of public infrastructure—drives voters toward the far-right Rassemblement National in France. Leveraging a 1998 policy decree that shuttered low-volume wards, the author uses a micro-geographic staggered difference-in-differences design to find that these closures have essentially zero effect on voting behavior. This result suggests that the "geography of discontent" is driven by slow-moving structural declines rather than discrete, localized policy shocks.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this well in the first paragraph, but it leans too heavily on the "France périphérique" trope initially. The pitch the paper *should* have is: "While the 'revenge of the places that don't matter' is a dominant narrative in political economy, we lack causal evidence on whether the withdrawal of specific, vital public services actually triggers backlash. By exploiting exogenous thresholds for maternity ward closures, this paper shows that even the most emotionally charged form of state retreat fails to move the needle on populist voting, forcing a re-evaluation of the mechanisms behind regional political polarization."

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution:** The paper provides the first micro-level causal "null" test of the state-abandonment hypothesis using specific service-provision shocks rather than broad austerity measures or correlational indices.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It is well-differentiated from Fetzer (2019) and Dal Bó et al. (2023). Most literature finds *positive* effects of austerity; finding a zero on a specific, high-stakes service (childbirth) is a provocative counter-point.
*   **World vs. Literature:** It currently frames itself as a "test of a hypothesis" (literature-focused). It would be stronger if framed as: "Why doesn't the closing of a local hospital turn people against the state?"
*   **Clarity:** A smart economist would immediately grasp the "what's new": it's a "No" to a question everyone assumed the answer to was "Yes."
*   **Making it bigger:** To make this an AER-level contribution, the author needs to explore **spillovers**. Does a closure in Commune A affect voting in the whole *canton* or *département*? If the effect is symbolic/discursive rather than personal, the commune-level fixity might be the wrong unit of analysis for the "story."

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

*   **Closest Neighbors:** Fetzer (2019) on Brexit/Austerity; Algan et al. (2019) on French populism; Rodríguez-Pose (2018) on the geography of discontent.
*   **Positioning:** It should position itself as a **skeptical corrective**. It is not "building on" the literature; it is challenging the micro-foundations of the "discontent" narrative.
*   **Missing Conversations:** The paper needs to speak to the **Salience** literature in behavioral economics (e.g., Bordalo et al.). Why is a hospital closure—which happens once in a decade—less salient than a carbon tax (Douenne & Fabre, 2022)?
*   **Framing:** It is currently a "France paper." It needs to be a "Theory of the State" paper.

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

*   **Setup:** The state is retreating from rural areas; the far-right is rising.
*   **Tension:** We assume A causes B, but areas losing services are already in decline. Is it the closure itself, or the 20 years of decline that preceded it?
*   **Resolution:** Closures don't matter. The "shock" of state withdrawal doesn't change votes.
*   **Implications:** Populism is a "slow burn" of deindustrialization, not a "flash fire" triggered by policy rationalization.

**Evaluation:** The narrative is "Serviceable" but currently feels a bit like a "failed experiment" report. To move to "Strong," the author needs to lean into the *paradox*: Why do people protest these closures in the streets (as noted in Section 6) but not at the ballot box?

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

**The Dinner Party Lead:** "In France, closing the local maternity ward—forcing women to drive an hour to give birth—has zero impact on whether that village votes for Le Pen."

**The Reaction:** People will lean in. The follow-up will be: "Then what *does* drive it?" or "Maybe they already reached a ceiling of RN support?"

**Null Result Evaluation:** This is a "High-Value Null." It closes off a very popular "common sense" explanation in political economy.

---

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

*   **Front-loading:** Move the "Discussion" of why this might be a null (Section 6) much earlier, perhaps into a "Conceptual Framework" section.
*   **Appendix:** The Callaway-Sant’Anna and TWFE comparison is standard now; keep the tables lean and move the nine different specifications to an appendix to focus on the most robust "zero."
*   **Mechanism:** The paper needs a table on "Protest Activity." If there is data on local protests against these closures, showing that people protest but don't change their vote would be the "smoking gun" for an AER paper.

---

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The gap is **Ambition**. A "competent null" is often a "Journal of Public Economics" or "REStat" paper. To be AER, it needs to solve the puzzle it creates. 

**The Single Most Impactful Advice:** Shift the focus from "Does X cause Y?" to "Why does a highly salient loss of a vital public service *fail* to move the political needle?" This requires the author to look at **identity**. If people in these communes already identify as "abandoned," the actual closure is just a confirmation of a sunk cost, not a new piece of information. Adding data on "RN support pre-closure" as a moderator would be key.
