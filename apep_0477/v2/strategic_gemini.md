## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
Using 7 million English property transactions, this paper investigates whether the coarse categorical ratings on Energy Performance Certificates (EPCs) actually influence market prices. By exploiting a regulatory "cliff" that bans the rental of properties below an 'E' rating alongside purely informational boundaries (e.g., C vs. D), the paper finds a precise null: while the market prices energy efficiency smoothly, it ignores the discrete labels themselves, even during the historic 2021-2023 energy crisis.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this reasonably well, but the first paragraph is a bit heavy on institutional detail (MEES). It needs to lead with the broader economic puzzle of the "Energy Efficiency Gap" and the failure of information salience.

**The pitch the paper should have:**
"Does the way we label information matter for market outcomes? We use the English housing market as a laboratory to test whether discrete categorical energy labels—ranging from A to G—generate price discontinuities, particularly when those labels trigger binding rental regulations. Despite a massive dataset and the 'perfect storm' of a tripling in energy prices, we find that market participants price energy efficiency as a continuous variable, rendering the design of categorical disclosure systems irrelevant for capitalization."

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
The paper identifies a "precisely estimated null" showing that discrete energy labels do not generate price jumps, suggesting that existing "EPC premia" in the literature are likely smooth efficiency effects or omitted variable bias rather than responses to the labels themselves.

**Evaluation:**
- **Differentiation:** It is strongly differentiated from Fuerst et al. (2015) and Aydin et al. (2020) by moving from hedonic "level" comparisons to an RDD "marginal" approach.
- **Framing:** It is currently framed as filling a gap in the energy/housing literature. To get into the AER, it needs to be framed as a question about **Information Design and Salience**—i.e., when does a coarse label fail to improve on a continuous signal?
- **Clarity:** A smart economist would say, "It's an RDD that shows the letter grade doesn't matter, only the underlying efficiency score does."
- **Bigger Contribution:** The paper would be more significant if it could show *who* is ignoring the labels. Are professional landlords more responsive than owner-occupiers? Is the null driven by a lack of trust in the auditors (as hinted at by the McCrary bunching)?

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
The paper sits at the intersection of Environmental Economics, Urban Economics, and Behavioral Economics.

- **Neighbors:** Allcott & Greenstone (2014) on the energy gap; Chetty et al. (2009) on salience; Best & Kleven (2018) on regulatory notches.
- **Positioning:** It should **attack** the naive interpretation of the "5-10% EPC premium" found in earlier hedonic studies. It should **synthesize** with the behavioral literature to explain why a legal mandate fails to create salience.
- **Unexpected Connection:** The paper should speak more to the **Audit/Certification** literature. The McCrary bunching at the E/F boundary is a "smoking gun" for assessor manipulation. The story might not be "labels don't matter," but rather "labels are corrupted when they become regulation."

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC
- **Setup:** Governments use labels (A-G) to solve information asymmetries in energy efficiency.
- **Tension:** Some of these labels carry heavy regulatory weight (the "cliff"), yet we don't know if the market actually prices the "letter" or just the "utility bill."
- **Resolution:** The labels themselves are a "nothingburger." No jumps at the cliff, no jumps at the colors, even when energy bills tripled.
- **Implications:** Policy shouldn't rely on "nudge" labels or categorical thresholds to move markets; they are either ignored by buyers or gamed by sellers/assessors.

**Evaluation:** The arc is currently "results-first." It needs to build the tension of the Energy Crisis more—if a tripling of bills doesn't make the 'D' vs 'E' label matter, nothing will.

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
**The Lead Fact:** "In a sample of 7 million homes, during a crisis where energy bills tripled, moving from an 'unlettable' F-rating to a 'lettable' E-rating resulted in a 0% price change."

**The Reaction:** People will lean in because it contradicts the common "green premium" narrative. The follow-up question: "If the price didn't move, did the landlords just bribe the assessors to change the score?" (The paper answers: Yes, look at the McCrary test).
