# Literature Review: Energy Labels and Housing Markets

## Key Papers

1. **Fuerst et al. (2015)** - EPC ratings and transaction prices in England. Hedonic regressions find 5% premia for A/B vs D. No causal identification.
2. **Aydin et al. (2020)** - Dutch housing market, 2.2% per EPC label step. Hedonic approach, same identification concern.
3. **Eichholtz et al. (2010)** - AER. Green office buildings, 3% premium. Energy Star certification, not EPC bands.
4. **Brounen et al. (2011)** - Dutch market, 3.6% per label step. Cross-sectional hedonic.
5. **Chegut et al. (2016)** - Affordable housing, energy efficiency value. Similar hedonic approach.
6. **Best & Kleven (2018)** - RDD at stamp duty thresholds in UK housing. Methodological template for housing RDD.
7. **Chetty et al. (2009)** - Salience theory. Information visibility affects market responses.
8. **Allcott & Greenstone (2014)** - Energy efficiency gap. Information alone may be insufficient.
9. **Gerarden et al. (2017)** - Comprehensive review of energy efficiency gap explanations.
10. **Ghosh et al. (2024)** - SSRN working paper on EPC information shocks. Most closely related to our identification.
11. **Romani et al. (2026)** - Energy crisis effects on real estate. Contemporary evidence.
12. **Cattaneo et al. (2016)** - Multi-cutoff RDD framework. Key methodological reference.

## Gap

No study uses RDD at EPC band boundaries to test for discrete label effects. The hedonic literature cannot distinguish label effects from omitted variables. The multi-cutoff design with 5 boundaries and the MEES regulatory variation is novel.

## Novelty Assessment

This paper is the first to:
- Apply multi-cutoff RDD to all five EPC boundaries simultaneously
- Exploit the MEES regulatory cliff at E/F for information-vs-regulation decomposition
- Test energy label salience during an acute energy crisis
- Document a well-powered null at all EPC boundaries using 7.1M transactions
