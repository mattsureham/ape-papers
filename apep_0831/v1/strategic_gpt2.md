## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple, policy-relevant question: when industrial policy boosts manufacturing demand, does it reduce racial inequality, and if so on which margin? Using county exposure to the 2018 Section 232 steel and aluminum tariffs, the paper argues that protection increased Black manufacturing hiring relative to White hiring in more exposed places, but did not reduce the Black-White earnings gap.

A busy economist should care because this is not really a paper about one Trump tariff episode; it is about the distributional incidence of industrial policy. If governments are going to spend political and fiscal capital on “bringing back manufacturing,” the first-order question is whether those policies change who gets jobs, who gets wage gains, or both.

### Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Not quite. The current opening is reasonably written, but it starts with a stylized steel-mill vignette and only gradually reveals the broader stakes. The best version of this paper is not “a tariff paper with race heterogeneity”; it is “a paper on whether manufacturing demand shocks reduce racial inequality through jobs or wages.” The introduction should get to that immediately.

### The pitch the paper should have

Here is what the first two paragraphs should say instead:

> Industrial policy is back. From tariffs to subsidies to reshoring initiatives, policymakers increasingly justify support for manufacturing not only on efficiency or national security grounds, but also on equity grounds: the idea that expanding domestic manufacturing will create good jobs for disadvantaged workers and help narrow long-standing racial gaps in the labor market. But those are distinct claims. A manufacturing boom may expand access to jobs without changing the wage-setting institutions that sustain racial pay gaps.
>
> This paper studies that distinction using the 2018 Section 232 steel and aluminum tariffs. Exploiting cross-county variation in pre-tariff metals exposure and comparing Black and White workers within county labor markets, I find that tariff-exposed counties saw substantially larger increases in Black manufacturing hiring relative to White hiring, but no corresponding reduction in the Black-White earnings gap. The central lesson is that manufacturing protection can widen access on the employment margin without delivering wage convergence.

That is the AER-facing pitch.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### Contribution in one sentence

The paper’s contribution is to show that a positive manufacturing demand shock from trade protection appears to have increased Black workers’ relative entry into manufacturing without reducing racial earnings inequality within manufacturing.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?

Only partially. The paper does distinguish itself from the China-shock literature and from Flaaen et al. on Section 232, but the differentiation still feels too much like “same empirical strategy, new subgroup.” Right now the introduction risks sounding like: “the trade literature studied average labor-market effects; I study racial heterogeneity.” That is publishable in a field journal if executed well, but it is not yet an AER contribution.

The sharper differentiation is conceptual, not just empirical:

- China-shock papers are about negative trade shocks and average local labor-market adjustment.
- Section 232 papers are about average upstream/downstream employment effects.
- This paper is about whether industrial policy changes racial inequality through the extensive margin or the intensive margin.

That distinction is stronger and should be front and center.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or filling a gap in a LITERATURE?

It is currently mixed, leaning too often toward literature-gap framing (“Flaaen et al. do not examine racial heterogeneity”). The stronger framing is a world question: **when governments revive manufacturing, do disadvantaged workers gain better jobs, better pay, or merely greater access to existing job ladders?** That is a real question about the world; “first to examine race in Section 232” is not enough.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?

A smart economist could probably say: “It’s a DDD paper on Section 232 showing Black hiring rose in exposed counties but wages didn’t.” That is decent. But they might also say, a bit dismissively, “another reduced-form trade paper with subgroup heterogeneity.”

That is the danger. The novelty is there, but it is not yet elevated into a general insight.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Most importantly: **make the paper about the decomposition of distributional effects into access versus pay.** That is already the best result; the paper should lean harder into it.

Specific ways the contribution could become bigger:

1. **Different framing:**  
   Recast from “trade protection and the racial wage gap” to “industrial policy and the margins of racial inequality.” The current title already gestures toward this; the paper should fully follow through.

2. **Stronger mechanism-oriented outcomes:**  
   The current distinction between hires and earnings is good. But the contribution would feel larger if the paper could say more about *where* Black workers entered:
   - entrants vs incumbents,
   - age or tenure margins,
   - job-to-job vs nonemployment-to-employment hires,
   - establishment size or industry segment.
   
   Even one of these would make the “opened doors but not pay ladders” story much more vivid.

3. **Better comparison class:**  
   The paper should more explicitly compare this episode to other labor-demand shocks, not just trade shocks. If the general result is that racial inequality is more elastic on hiring than on wages, then Section 232 is one instance of a broader phenomenon.

4. **More explicit industrial-policy connection:**  
   The paper references CHIPS/IRA, but mostly in passing. If that is the intended audience, the paper needs to convince the reader that Section 232 is informative about today’s industrial-policy toolkit, despite being a tariff rather than a subsidy.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

### Closest neighbors

The closest papers are roughly:

1. **Autor, Dorn, and Hanson (2013)** — China shock and local labor markets.
2. **Pierce and Schott (2016)** — trade liberalization and manufacturing employment.
3. **Flaaen, Hortaçsu, and Tintelnot (2020)** — effects of Section 232 tariffs, especially upstream/downstream employment consequences.
4. **Dix-Carneiro and Kovak (2017)** or related trade-adjustment papers — persistent labor-market impacts of trade shocks.
5. On racial wage gaps, likely **Derenoncourt and Montialoux (2021/2022)** and **Card, Cardoso, and Kline (2016)** on firms and inequality/bargaining-related wage-setting channels.

If the author wants a broader labor-market framing, one could also bring in work on demand shocks and racial inequality outside trade.

### How should it position itself relative to those neighbors?

Mostly **build on and redirect**, not attack.

- Build on the trade-shock literature by asking a different incidence question: not “what is the average local effect?” but “who captures the gains within local labor markets?”
- Build on racial-inequality papers by emphasizing that labor-demand shocks and wage-setting institutions affect different margins.
- Build on the Section 232 literature by saying: aggregate employment accounting misses an important distributional fact about access.

The paper should not overclaim that it overturns the China-shock literature or industrial-policy debates. It is adding a distributional layer, not replacing the core results.

### Is it positioned too narrowly or too broadly?

Currently, oddly, both.

- **Too narrowly** because it is anchored to the minutiae of Section 232 and NAICS metals exposure.
- **Too broadly** in occasional gestures toward “industrial policy” without fully earning that generalization.

The right level is: **Section 232 as a clean, salient test case for a broader industrial-policy question.**

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?

It seems somewhat underconnected to:

- **Labor-demand shocks and inequality** beyond trade.
- **Within-firm inequality / internal labor markets / job ladders** literature, if that is the intended mechanism.
- **Race and cyclical labor-market sensitivity**: there is a longstanding literature on whether Black workers are more marginal in expansions and contractions. This paper should connect to that more directly. The finding may be less “trade-specific” than “tight labor markets change hiring margins first for marginalized workers.”

That last point may actually be the most fruitful unexpected literature connection.

### Is it having the right conversation?

Almost, but not quite. It is currently having a conversation with the trade literature plus a separate race-gap literature. The more interesting conversation may be:

**How do labor-demand shocks affect racial inequality differently at entry versus within-job pay?**

That connects trade, labor, racial inequality, and industrial policy in a way that is more synthetic and more impactful.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Manufacturing has reemerged as a policy target, and many policymakers implicitly assume that supporting manufacturing will deliver broad-based gains, including for historically disadvantaged groups. At the same time, racial wage gaps in the labor market remain persistent and may be governed by institutions different from those determining employment.

### Tension

A positive manufacturing shock could reduce racial inequality in two very different ways:
- by expanding hiring access for Black workers, and/or
- by raising relative wages for Black workers already in the sector.

Those are not the same thing, and theory does not tell us they should move together.

### Resolution

The paper’s core result is that tariff exposure is associated with more relative Black hiring into manufacturing, but not with a narrower Black-White earnings gap.

### Implications

Industrial policy may broaden access without altering the wage structure. If policymakers care about racial pay convergence, demand-side manufacturing support alone may be insufficient.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

Yes, more than most papers of this type. The extensive-margin/intensive-margin split is a genuine narrative spine. That is the paper’s best asset.

But the arc is still somewhat obscured by the paper’s current organization and emphasis. The introduction says too much about the specific tariff episode and too little about the deeper conceptual point. Then the discussion offers mechanisms somewhat speculatively. The result is a paper that has a good story but occasionally sounds like a collection of standard tables surrounding one nice contrast.

### If it is a collection of results looking for a story, what story should it tell?

The story should be:

> Industrial policy changes the queue for manufacturing jobs more readily than it changes the wage-setting rules inside manufacturing.

That is memorable, general, and policy-relevant. Everything in the paper should serve that line.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?

“I’ve got a paper suggesting that the 2018 metals tariffs increased Black manufacturing hiring in more exposed counties, but didn’t narrow the Black-White earnings gap at all.”

That is a reasonably strong opening fact.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

Some would lean in. This is not an automatic blockbuster fact, but it is more interesting than the average trade paper because it cuts across trade, race, and industrial policy. The hook is the asymmetry: **jobs moved, wages didn’t.**

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Probably one of these:

- “So are Black workers getting in at the bottom of the ladder?”
- “Is this about labor-market tightness generally rather than tariffs specifically?”
- “How much should I generalize from Section 232 to CHIPS/IRA-style industrial policy?”
- “Are these new jobs in upstream metals or elsewhere in manufacturing?”

Those are exactly the questions the framing should anticipate.

### If the findings are modest or null, is the null itself interesting?

Yes, but only because it is paired with the positive hiring result. A stand-alone null on the racial earnings gap would not carry enough weight. What makes the paper interesting is the divergence. The paper understands this, to its credit.

That said, the author should be careful not to oversell the null as “precisely estimated” if the real intellectual contribution is the contrast between margins, not the precision rhetoric. The paper is strongest when saying “industrial policy changed access but not pay,” not when saying “I reject earnings effects above X log points.”

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

At present, the gap is mostly **framing and ambition**, with some **scope** concerns.

### Framing problem?

Yes. The science may be competent, but the current framing undersells the paper by presenting it too much as a tariff heterogeneity exercise. The real paper is about what industrial policy can and cannot do for racial inequality.

### Scope problem?

Somewhat. The existing outcomes are enough for a solid paper, but for AER the reader wants either:
- a more general conceptual payoff, or
- richer evidence on the mechanism behind “hiring dividend but no wage convergence.”

Without that, the paper risks feeling like one striking reduced-form contrast rather than a field-defining result.

### Novelty problem?

Moderately. The novelty is not the setting per se. Section 232 is studied, local trade shocks are studied, racial disparities are studied. The novelty is in the **specific decomposition of distributional effects into hiring versus earnings**. That is real, but it must be sharpened and generalized.

### Ambition problem?

Yes. The paper is competent but safe. It observes a well-identified policy shock, estimates standard reduced-form specifications, and reports a clean contrast. For AER, it needs to ask a bigger question and more visibly change how people think.

### Single most impactful piece of advice

**Reframe the paper around a broader claim: industrial policy can reduce racial inequality at the point of entry into manufacturing without changing the wage-setting structure inside manufacturing.**

If the author changes only one thing, it should be that. Everything else follows.
