### 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

The paper examines the 2018 Trump Administration tariffs on steel and aluminum (Section 232) to determine if trade protectionism can reduce racial economic disparities. It finds that while the resulting demand shock significantly increased the hiring of Black workers relative to White workers (the "hiring dividend"), it had zero effect on the Black-White earnings gap, suggesting that increased job access does not automatically translate into pay equity.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this well, though the first paragraph is a bit "stylized" with the Gary, Indiana anecdote. 
*   **The pitch the paper should have:** "While economists have long studied the devastating local effects of trade *liberalization*, we know much less about the distributional consequences of trade *protection*. Exploiting the 2018 Section 232 tariffs, this paper shows that protectionist shocks create a 'hiring dividend' for Black workers by drawing them in at the extensive margin, yet fail to move the needle on the racial wage gap due to rigid internal pay structures."

---

### 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution:** The paper identifies an asymmetry in the trade-labor nexus: protectionism recovers jobs for marginalized groups faster than it recovers their relative pay.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It successfully flips the "China Shock" (Autor et al. 2013) literature on its head to look at the *positive* shock of tariffs. It also differentiates itself from the minimum wage literature (Derenoncourt & Montialoux 2021) by showing that demand-side shocks operate differently than price-floor shocks.
*   **World vs. Literature:** It frames itself as answering a question about the world—specifically the effectiveness of modern industrial policy (CHIPS, IRA) in helping disadvantaged workers. This is a major plus for AER.
*   **AER-worthy?** A smart economist would see this as "the mirror image of the China shock with a racial twist." To make it bigger, the author needs to explore *why* the hiring happened but the wages didn't. Is it purely mechanical (new hires start at the bottom) or structural (unions/contracts)?

---

### 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

*   **Closest Neighbors:** Autor, Dorn, & Hanson (2013); Pierce & Schott (2016); Derenoncourt & Montialoux (2021); Flaaen et al. (2020).
*   **Positioning Strategy:** The paper should lean harder into "Industrial Policy." The connection to the CHIPS Act and the Inflation Reduction Act in the introduction is the "hook" that moves this from a narrow trade paper to a general-interest economics paper.
*   **Missing Conversations:** The paper needs to engage more with the **Personnel Economics** literature on internal labor markets (e.g., Doeringer and Piore). If firms hire more Black workers but don't raise their relative wages, is it because of "sticky" internal ladders?

---

### 4. NARRATIVE ARC

*   **Setup:** Trade shocks are usually studied as "shocks of despair" (factory closures).
*   **Tension:** Protectionism is sold as a way to "bring back" good jobs for the working class, but does it actually help those at the bottom of the racial hierarchy?
*   **Resolution:** Yes, they get hired (hiring dividend), but no, they don't catch up in pay.
*   **Implications:** Demand-side industrial policy is a partial tool; without wage-setting interventions, the "manufacturing renaissance" won't close racial gaps.

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong. It's a "Yes, but..." story, which is often more satisfying than a "Yes, and..." story.

---

### 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

*   **The Lead Fact:** "Trump’s tariffs increased Black hiring by 17%, but the racial wage gap didn't move a cent."
*   **The Reaction:** People will lean in. It's a counter-intuitive finding that challenges both the "tariffs help no one" and "tariffs help everyone" camps.
*   **Follow-up:** "Why? Are they just hiring Black workers for the lowest-paid roles?" This is the question the author must answer more convincingly.

---

### 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

*   **Front-loading:** The paper is well-structured. Table 3 (the divergence between hiring and earnings) is the "money" table and should be moved even earlier if possible.
*   **The Event Study (Table 4):** The author admits to pre-trends in 2015. While they argue it’s the "shale boom," this is a red flag for AER. They need to address this more aggressively, perhaps by controlling for local oil/gas exposure or showing the results are robust to the start date of the pre-period.
*   **Discussion:** Section 6 needs to move from "speculating" about mechanisms to "testing" them. If they can’t get occupation data, can they look at unionization rates at the county level to see if the "wage rigidity" story holds?

---

### 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

**The Gap:** Currently, it's a very high-quality "Applied Micro" paper. To be an AER paper, it needs more **Economic Theory/Mechanisms**. 

**Single Most Impactful Advice:** The author needs to decompose the "null" wage result. Is the wage gap flat because (a) individuals' wages aren't changing, or (b) the *composition* is changing (hiring low-wage Black workers drags down the average)? If they can't distinguish between these two, the paper remains a "reduced form" black box.
