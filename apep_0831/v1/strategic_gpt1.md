## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether a large manufacturing demand shock created by the 2018 Section 232 steel and aluminum tariffs reduced racial inequality in manufacturing labor markets. Using county variation in exposure to metals production, it finds that tariff-exposed places hired relatively more Black manufacturing workers, but did not reduce the Black-white earnings gap.

A busy economist should care because this is not really a tariff paper; it is a paper about whether industrial policy changes who gets access to good jobs and whether that access translates into pay equality. That is a live question well beyond Section 232.

### Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Not quite. The current opening is readable and not bad, but it starts with a vignette and then moves quickly into the policy episode. What is still underdeveloped is the bigger question: when manufacturing gets a positive shock, does inequality narrow through labor demand, or are hiring margins and wage-setting margins fundamentally distinct?

Right now the paper is framed as “I study Section 232 and estimate a triple-difference by race.” For AER purposes, it should be framed as “I use Section 232 to answer a broader question about the distributional incidence of industrial policy.”

### The pitch the paper should have

“Can industrial policy reduce racial inequality in labor markets, or does it mainly create jobs without changing pay structures? This paper uses the 2018 steel and aluminum tariffs as a large, plausibly concentrated shock to manufacturing demand and shows that tariff-exposed counties increased Black manufacturing hiring relative to White hiring, but saw no corresponding narrowing of the Black-white earnings gap. The broader lesson is that demand-side manufacturing policy can expand access to jobs without altering the wage-setting institutions that sustain pay inequality.”

That is the paper’s best version.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### Contribution in one sentence

The paper’s contribution is to show that a positive trade-protection shock increased Black workers’ relative entry into manufacturing without reducing the Black-white earnings gap, implying that industrial policy can change access to jobs without changing relative pay.

### Is this clearly differentiated from the closest papers?

Only partially. The paper distinguishes itself from China-shock papers and from work on the aggregate employment effects of Section 232, but the differentiation is still too mechanical:

- “They study import competition; I study import protection.”
- “They study employment; I study racial heterogeneity.”

That is not enough on its own. The paper needs to make clearer that the novelty is conceptual, not just topical: **it separates the access margin from the pay margin in the incidence of industrial policy**, and does so through the lens of racial inequality.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a gap in a literature?

It oscillates, but too often it sounds like gap-filling. The strongest version is a world question:

- Weak: “Flaaen et al. do not examine racial heterogeneity.”
- Strong: “A central policy question is whether manufacturing-oriented protection and subsidy regimes reduce inequality by changing who gets hired, or whether they leave wage hierarchies intact.”

The paper should do much more of the latter.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the intro?

At present, maybe, but not cleanly. They would probably say: “It’s a DiD/triple-diff paper showing Section 232 increased Black hiring in exposed counties but didn’t move relative wages.”

That is not terrible, but it still sounds like “another quasi-experimental paper about a Trump-era policy.” The introduction needs to help the reader say something bigger: “It shows industrial policy can broaden access to manufacturing jobs without compressing racial pay gaps.”

### What would make the contribution bigger?

Specific ways to enlarge it:

1. **Reframe around industrial policy generally, not Section 232 specifically.**  
   The current paper is about one tariff episode. The bigger question is about the equity effects of demand-side manufacturing policy.

2. **Elevate the access-versus-pay distinction into the main contribution.**  
   This should not be one result table; it should be the paper’s organizing idea.

3. **Tie the finding to a clearer mechanism class.**  
   Not by adding a dozen speculative mechanisms, but by making the conceptual claim sharper: labor demand shocks operate first on entry/hiring margins, while wage inequality is governed by occupational assignment and firm pay-setting.

4. **If possible, show whether the hiring response is concentrated in better jobs or just any manufacturing jobs.**  
   Strategically, the biggest hole in the story is that “more Black hires” is interesting, but economists will immediately ask: hired into what? If the paper had occupation, establishment type, earnings distribution, or worker-flow evidence, it would become materially bigger. Even if not possible here, the paper should acknowledge this as the key boundary of interpretation.

5. **Compare the result explicitly to other tools that do move racial wages.**  
   The Derenoncourt framing is promising. The bigger claim is not merely “tariffs do not change wages,” but “product-market interventions and labor-market institutions affect different margins of inequality.”

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Industrial policy and trade protection are often justified partly on equity grounds: revive manufacturing, create good jobs, and disadvantaged workers will benefit.

### Tension

But it is not obvious that a positive manufacturing demand shock changes racial inequality in the way policymakers imply. It may increase labor demand and hiring while leaving wage-setting institutions, occupational sorting, and within-firm hierarchies intact.

### Resolution

The paper finds precisely that split: Black manufacturing hiring rises in more tariff-exposed counties after Section 232, but Black relative earnings do not.

### Implications

The implication is that industrial policy may improve access to jobs without reducing pay inequality; if policymakers care about wage convergence, demand-side manufacturing policy is not enough on its own.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the bones of one, but not yet a fully disciplined narrative. At present it still feels somewhat like a collection of outcomes—earnings, employment, hires—organized around one statistically strong result and one null.

The story it should tell is simpler and stronger:

- **Question:** Does industrial policy reduce racial inequality?
- **Key distinction:** Access versus pay.
- **Evidence:** Section 232 improved access, not pay.
- **Implication:** Equity claims about manufacturing policy need to distinguish labor demand from wage-setting.

That is a clean arc. The paper is close to it, but still too table-driven.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?

“I’d lead with: the steel tariffs increased Black manufacturing hiring in exposed counties, but they did not narrow the Black-white earnings gap.”

That is a good dinner-party fact.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

Some would lean in, especially labor and trade economists, because the split between hiring and earnings is genuinely interesting. But many would immediately wonder whether this is a narrow Section 232 curiosity rather than a general lesson.

So the reaction depends entirely on framing. If presented as “a tariff paper,” phones. If presented as “industrial policy changes employment margins but not wage inequality,” more leaning in.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Almost certainly:

- “What kind of jobs were those hires?”
- “Why no wage effect?”
- “Is this about firms hiring Black workers into lower-paying roles?”
- “Does this generalize beyond one weird Trump tariff episode?”

Those are exactly the questions the paper needs to anticipate in its framing.

### If findings are null or modest, is the null interesting?

Yes, the earnings null is potentially interesting, but only if the paper insists that this is not a failed search for a wage effect. The paper does some of this, but it should do more. The null matters because policy rhetoric often conflates job creation with equitable distribution of the gains from job creation.

The paper should say, more explicitly: **learning that product-market protection does not compress racial pay gaps is substantively important, not merely statistically disappointing.**
