**Idea Fidelity**

The paper largely stays faithful to the manifest, but with a few departures the authors should acknowledge. The original idea promised a comprehensive, nationwide spatial RDD across the 806 wilderness areas, exploiting Landsat/Hansen data and multiple secondary outcomes. The submitted draft restricts attention to 359 western locations, uses 500K sampled pixels, and focuses on one primary outcome (tree cover loss) without incorporating the additional datasets (NDVI, MTBS, FACTS, VIIRS) mentioned in the manifest. The promised heterogeneity explorations (USFS vs. BLM, pre/post-2000 designations) and the broader “wilderness versus timber market” framing are not pursued. That said, the central identification strategy—a pixel-level spatial RDD at wilderness boundaries—is preserved, so the divergence is one of scope rather than substance. The authors should clarify these scope choices and, if possible, justify why certain datasets or regional samples were prioritized.

**Summary**

The paper exploits spatial regression discontinuities at federal wilderness boundaries in the U.S. Pacific Northwest to estimate the effect of statutory wilderness protections on forest loss. Using randomly sampled 30-meter pixels from Hansen’s tree cover loss dataset, the authors find a roughly 1.1 percentage point (≈5.5%) reduction in tree cover loss just inside versus just outside the boundary. The effect is robust to bandwidth choices and placebo boundaries, suggesting a “legal fortress” effect of the Wilderness Act in a high-rule-of-law setting.

**Essential Points**

1. **Covariate discontinuity threatens identification.** Table 4 reveals a statistically significant discontinuity in baseline canopy cover (≈–1.5 percentage points) at the boundary. Since the treatment is supposed to be the only feature changing at the cutoff, this discontinuity raises the specter that boundaries systematically cut near forest edges, which could directly affect tree cover loss independent of legal protection. Simply controlling for baseline cover in one specification is insufficient; the authors should either (a) demonstrate that the discontinuity disappears when measuring baseline cover at a more granular level (e.g., pre-treatment average) or (b) implement a RDD that explicitly conditions on baseline tree cover in the running variable (e.g., by controlling for it in both sides’ trends) and shows the main effect remains. Otherwise, the continuity assumption is questionable.

2. **Density manipulation test fails.** The Cattaneo–Jansson–Ma density test rejects continuity ($p=0.002$). While the authors attribute this to geometry, they do not show empirically that the sampling design (or county boundaries, road networks) explains the discontinuity. The rejection could reflect endogenous selection (e.g., wilderness boundaries coinciding with ridgelines where forest density—and thus pixel availability—differs). The authors should probe this further: plot density on both sides, rerun the test after subsetting to areas where the buffer geometry is symmetric, or use alternative sampling (regular grid within buffer) to ensure the density discontinuity is not an artifact influencing the treatment effect.

3. **Interpretation conflates harvest with all forest loss.** The outcome aggregates all causes of tree cover loss (fire, pests, blowdown) over 2001–2023. Given that wildfires and insects may be influenced by suppression policies and not necessarily halted by legal protection, the estimated discontinuity could reflect spatial differences in such disturbances, not just timber harvest. The paper argues these effects work against finding an effect, but that argument is speculative. The authors should either: (a) restrict the outcome to losses attributable to harvesting (e.g., use USFS FACTS or other harvest-permitting shapefiles to match logging operations), (b) difference out known fire years using MTBS severity in a triple-difference/interaction framework, or (c) provide evidence that the boundary discontinuity persists when excluding high-fire years or areas. Without that, it is difficult to claim the effect captures the “legal prohibition on harvesting” rather than other forms of disturbance.

**Suggestions**

- **Clarify regional generalizability.** The idea manifest envisaged a national analysis using all wilderness boundaries, but the paper focuses on the Pacific Northwest. Please explain the rationale for this geographic restriction (e.g., data availability, greatest counterfactual harvest pressure, computational limits) and assess whether the estimated effect might differ in other regions (e.g., Appalachians, Rocky Mountain forests). If feasible, expand the sample to additional wildernesses or at least provide summary statistics that show the chosen region is representative of the broader NWPS in terms of baseline deforestation pressure and institutional context.

- **Leverage additional data sources for triangulation.** The manifest mentioned NDVI, MTBS fire perimeters/severity, FACTS harvest data, and VIIRS nightlights. Incorporating at least one of these could strengthen the mechanistic interpretation. For example, using MTBS to classify loss into fire versus non-fire events would help isolate the effect on harvest. Similarly, USFS FACTS could provide a direct analogue (presence/absence of harvest units) to compare with the satellite-based measure. Even if these datasets are not used in the main specification, a supplementary table showing consistency across multiple indicators would bolster the claim that the boundary discontinuity reflects timber harvest.

- **Report estimated effects in per-hectare terms or through event timing.** Presenting the result solely as a percentage-point reduction in a binary indicator over 23 years obscures the economic magnitude. Consider converting the effect into area preserved per kilometer of boundary per year, or translate it into avoided board feet using average harvest intensities. Additionally, because the running variable is purely spatial, the temporal aggregation (2001–2023) washes out dynamics—are more recent years driving the effect? Estimating the RDD separately for sub-periods (e.g., 2001–2010, 2011–2023) could reveal whether post-2000 logging trends differ across the boundary, which would strengthen the story about active harvest pressure.

- **Revisit the bandwidth selection and sample inclusion.** The paper uses MSE-optimal bandwidths (~1.14 km) but samples up to 5 km for heterogeneity and placebo tests. It would be helpful to show plots of the running variable (bin scatter) and to justify why forested pixels beyond 2 km should be comparable. The significant density discontinuity might partly stem from the buffer construction; using uniform grids or adjusting for per-boundary length could mitigate this. Also, while elevation is controlled for, slope, aspect, and accessibility (distance to roads) could likewise influence harvest decisions. Including these covariates—or at least demonstrating their continuity—would bolster the design.

- **Enhance the placebo/validity section.** In addition to false boundaries, consider using other nearby legal boundaries with no harvest discontinuity (e.g., USFS roadless areas where harvest restrictions are similar on both sides) as negative controls. Presenting graphical evidence (e.g., outcome means by distance bins) would also help readers assess whether the discontinuity is localized and whether the functional form is adequate.

- **Address the “marginal significance” head-on.** The main estimate is only marginally significant ($p=0.063$). Rather than just noting it in the discussion, the authors could compute confidence intervals relative to the mean and interpret the practical significance, or use combined data (e.g., stacking multiple outcomes) to increase power. Alternatively, Bayesian inference or moment-based methods tailored to spatial RDDs might yield sharper estimates. Including such analysis would make the cautious tone throughout (“suggestive evidence”) more convincing.

- **Discuss institutional mechanisms more concretely.** The “legal fortress” language is evocative, but the institutional story could be enriched. For example, show how enforcement (e.g., timber sale plans, patrols) operates differently inside/outside, or cite evidence that wilderness boundaries are indeed enforced with near-perfect compliance, reducing the likelihood of illegal logging. Doing so would help readers evaluate whether the legal discontinuity plausibly translates into differential behavior.

In sum, the paper addresses an important gap by applying a spatial RDD to U.S. wilderness boundaries, but the authors should more fully exploit the available data, fortify the identification checks (especially given the density test rejection and baseline discontinuity), and clarify the scope relative to the original idea. These additions would substantially strengthen the contribution.
