## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
This paper asks whether legal land protections actually work in a high-rule-of-law setting with strong commercial incentives for extraction. By applying a spatial RDD to U.S. federal wilderness boundaries, the author finds that wilderness designation reduces the probability of tree cover loss by 5.5% relative to adjacent managed forest land. It is the first causal study of its kind in a developed-country context, providing a "clean" estimate of the marginal value of legal status over and above geographic remoteness.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this pitch excellently. The first two paragraphs of the introduction move quickly from the global context of protected areas (the "why") to the specific institutional discontinuity of the 1964 Wilderness Act (the "how"). No rewrite is needed.

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**Contribution:** The paper provides the first causal estimate of the "legal fortress" effect of U.S. wilderness areas, demonstrating that even in a highly regulated developed country, strict legal prohibition provides a marginal conservation gain of ~1.1 percentage points at the boundary.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It is clearly differentiated from the "remoteness" literature (Joppa and Pfaff, 2009) and the tropical conservation literature (Andam et al., 2008). 
*   **Framing:** It is framed as a question about the world: "Do legal boundaries protect forests?" This is a strong, fundamental question.
*   **Clarity:** A smart economist would immediately understand the "Fortress" concept. It’s not just "another DiD/RDD"; it’s an RDD on a legally binding line that was drawn by political, not ecological, compromise.
*   **Bigger Contribution:** To move from "interesting" to "essential," the author should investigate **leakage**. Does the 1.1% reduction inside the line cause a 1.1% increase elsewhere in the same National Forest? Without a leakage analysis, we don't know if the policy "protects" or just "shifts" harvest.

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
The paper sits at the intersection of Environmental Economics and Applied Micro (Spatial Metrics).

*   **Closest Neighbors:** Joppa & Pfaff (2009) on selection bias; Andam et al. (2008) and Ferraro et al. (2011) on tropical protection; Araya-Ajoy et al. (2024) in the *AER*.
*   **Positioning:** It builds on the tropical literature by testing if those findings hold in a "high-rule-of-law" setting. This is a smart pivot. 
*   **Missing Conversations:** The paper needs to speak to the **Political Economy of Land Use**. Since the boundaries were "congressionally drawn," there is a missed opportunity to discuss why certain lines were drawn where they were. If the line was a "compromise," is the 5.5% effect the result of that compromise, or despite it?
*   **Breadth:** It is currently a bit narrow (Pacific Northwest focus). To broaden the audience, it should frame the results as a test of the **"Institutional Strength"** of federal land agencies.

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC
*   **Setup:** We know protected areas exist, but we think they are only "protected" because they are too far away/high up to log anyway.
*   **Tension:** In the US, we have strict laws but also high timber demand. Does the law "bite" when the terrain is actually loggable?
*   **Resolution:** Yes, but only modestly (5.5%). The law matters most on "marginal" lands (25-50% canopy) rather than the most valuable old-growth.
*   **Implications:** Simply "declaring" wilderness isn't a silver bullet, but it provides a "fortress" effect that other regulations (spotted owl, etc.) don't fully cover.

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong. However, the "Resolution" is slightly weakened by the p=0.063 result. The paper needs to decide if it's telling a story of "success" or "modest impact."

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
*   **Dinner Party Fact:** "Congress drew a line in the woods in 1964, and today, that line is visible from space as a 5% difference in tree loss, even when the dirt on both sides is identical."
*   **Reaction:** Lean in. It’s a classic "invisible hand vs. iron fist" story.
*   **Follow-up:** "Does that mean we just log more on the other side of the line?" (This brings us back to the leakage problem).
*   **Null Results:** The lack of effect in dense forests (75-100% cover) is actually quite interesting—it suggests that for high-value old growth, either the law is redundant or the timber is too hard to get anyway.

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
*   **Front-loading:** The paper is well-structured. The summary statistics (Table 1) and RDD plots (which are missing from this draft but implied) are the stars.
*   **Density Test:** Table 3, Panel B shows a failure of the density test (p=0.002). In an RDD paper, this is a red flag. The author dismisses it as "geometry of the sampling," but this needs a much more robust explanation or a different sampling strategy to ensure it isn't "sorting."
*   **Robustness:** The placebo tests are excellent and should remain prominent.

---

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The paper is currently a "very good" field journal paper (e.g., *JAERE* or *Journal of Environmental Economics and Management*). To make it an *AER* paper, it needs **Ambition and Scope.**

1.  **Leakage/General Equilibrium:** Does wilderness designation reduce *total* logging or just move it 5km down the road?
2.  **Mechanisms:** Can the author use Forest Service timber sale data to prove the mechanism is indeed "commercial harvest" and not just "road-building for recreation"?
3.  **National Scale:** Why only 359 boundaries in the PNW? The US has 800+ wilderness areas. To be the definitive paper on US Wilderness, it should attempt a national census or explain why the PNW is the only valid laboratory.

**Single Biggest Piece of Advice:** Address the "Leakage" question. If you show that wilderness protects the forest *without* increasing deforestation 5km outside the boundary, this becomes a major statement on the efficacy of environmental institutions.
