## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether formula-driven fiscal windfalls to Brazilian municipalities reduce violence against women by expanding female employment in health and education, the two sectors where municipal spending is concentrated and women are the overwhelming majority of workers. Using population thresholds in Brazil’s main intergovernmental transfer system, the paper’s substantive conclusion is essentially that these windfalls do not produce detectable reductions in female homicide, suggesting that indirect, public-employment-based improvements in women’s outside options may be too weak or diffuse to move extreme violence.

A busy economist should care because this is, in principle, a nice bridge between three important conversations: fiscal federalism, gender and labor-market bargaining, and violence against women. The core attraction is not “another RD on transfers,” but a broader world question: can the gender composition of public spending generate meaningful downstream social effects?

The paper does **not** articulate this pitch clearly enough in the first two paragraphs. The introduction currently tries to do too much at once: domestic violence prevalence, FPM institutional detail, female employment shares, household bargaining, and identification. Worse, it oversells the result before the reader understands the question, and it is internally inconsistent about what the main outcome even is. The first two paragraphs should instead say something like:

> Municipal governments spend much of their budgets on health and education, sectors that employ disproportionately many women. This raises a broader question: when governments receive exogenous fiscal windfalls, can the gender composition of public spending improve women’s economic autonomy enough to reduce violence against them?
>
> This paper studies that question in Brazil using sharp population thresholds in the country’s largest intergovernmental transfer program. Those thresholds create quasi-experimental increases in municipal revenue that plausibly expand employment in female-intensive public sectors. I find little evidence that these fiscal windfalls reduce female homicide, implying that indirect improvements in women’s labor-market opportunities through municipal hiring may not be sufficient to affect extreme forms of violence.

That is the pitch. It is cleaner, world-facing, and honest about the result.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:**  
The paper uses quasi-experimental variation in municipal fiscal transfers in Brazil to test whether transfer-funded expansion in female-intensive public employment reduces violence against women, and finds little evidence that it does.

That contribution is only **partially** differentiated from the nearest literature. I can see the intended distinction:

1. FPM papers study fiscal transfers, corruption, education, and public employment.
2. IPV papers study women’s wages, labor demand shocks, unemployment, and cash transfers.
3. This paper wants to sit at the intersection: fiscal windfalls as a *supply-side* source of female employment.

That is potentially interesting. But right now the differentiation is not sharp enough, for three reasons.

### Is it clearly distinct from the closest papers?
Only somewhat.

The nearest papers seem to be:
- **Brollo, Nannicini, Perotti, and Tabellini (2013)** on federal transfers and local politics/corruption in Brazil.
- **Litschig and Morrison (2013)** on the impact of intergovernmental transfers on education in Brazil.
- **Corbi, Papaioannou, and Surico (2019)** or similar FPM-based work on fiscal multipliers/local labor markets.
- **Aizer (2010)** on relative wages and domestic violence.
- **Erten and Keskin (2018)** on female employment shocks and IPV.
- Possibly **Bobonis, González-Brenes, and Castro (2013)** / **Hidrobo and Fernald / Hidrobo et al.** on cash transfers and IPV.

The paper says it is “the first to connect fiscal federalism and intimate partner violence.” Fine. But “first intersection paper” is rarely enough for AER. The paper must be clearer that the novelty is not merely crossing two literatures, but answering a broader question: **Do indirect, government-mediated improvements in women’s employment opportunities change household violence in the same way as direct wage or transfer shocks?** That is a real scientific question.

### World question or literature gap?
At present, the contribution is framed too much as a literature gap: “no study has connected X and Y.” That is weak. The stronger framing is a world question:

- Governments routinely receive fiscal windfalls.
- Much of municipal spending goes to female-intensive sectors.
- Does that kind of gendered public spending change women’s bargaining position enough to reduce violence?

That is much better than “this literature has not examined gendered consequences.”

### Could a smart economist explain what is new?
Right now, not reliably. Too many readers would summarize this as: **“It’s an RD paper using Brazil’s transfer thresholds to look at violence against women, and the results are null.”** That is not enough.

To get beyond “another quasi-experimental paper about X,” the introduction must explain why this is a distinct conceptual test:
- direct vs indirect effects on women’s outside options,
- private labor-market shocks vs public-sector hiring,
- transfer targeting to women vs general municipal revenue shocks.

That comparison is the intellectual payload. It should be the centerpiece.

### What would make the contribution bigger?
Several possibilities, ordered by likely payoff:

1. **Make the comparison sharper:**  
   Frame the paper explicitly as testing whether *indirect* improvements in women’s economic position via municipal public employment resemble the effects found for *direct* shocks to women’s wages or transfers. That would immediately elevate the contribution.

2. **Measure the channel directly:**  
   The biggest current weakness in positioning is that the paper’s central mechanism—female public employment expansion—is asserted, not shown. Even without going full referee-mode, strategically this is a major limitation. If the authors could show that FPM threshold crossings increase female public employment specifically, the paper’s contribution becomes much more coherent.

3. **Use a violence outcome closer to the proposed mechanism:**  
   Female homicide is clean but extremely distal. If the paper’s real story is about bargaining, dependence, and exit options, then non-lethal IPV or feminicide-specific measures would fit much better. Right now the paper may be testing too far downstream from the hypothesized mechanism.

4. **Exploit heterogeneity where the theory bites:**  
   The contribution would feel larger if the effects were strongest where municipal spending is most female-intensive, where baseline female formal employment is low, or where public hiring is especially important for women’s labor market opportunities.

If they only do one thing, it should be the mechanism. Without evidence that the transfer actually expands women’s employment, the paper’s conceptual novelty remains mostly rhetorical.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

### Closest neighbors
The obvious neighbors are:

- **Brollo et al. (2013)** on Brazilian federal transfers and local political outcomes.
- **Litschig and Morrison (2013)** on education effects of FPM transfers.
- **Corbi et al. (2019)** or adjacent work on local labor market effects/fiscal multipliers from FPM.
- **Aizer (2010)** on relative wages and domestic violence.
- **Erten and Keskin (2018)** on female employment shocks and domestic violence.
- Also likely relevant: **Anderberg et al. (2016)** on unemployment and domestic violence; **Bobonis et al. (2013)** and **Hidrobo et al. (2016)** on cash transfers and IPV.

### How should it position itself?
Mostly **build on and connect**, not attack.

This paper should not posture as overturning the IPV literature. The more persuasive position is:
- existing work shows violence can respond to women’s wages, labor-demand shocks, or transfers;
- existing fiscal federalism work shows transfers affect local spending and employment;
- this paper asks whether those two findings combine to imply an effect of general fiscal windfalls through female-intensive public employment;
- answer: apparently not much, at least for female homicide.

That is a useful synthesis. The paper is strongest when presented as a disciplined test of whether mechanisms identified elsewhere travel to a different institutional margin.

### Too narrow or too broad?
Currently it is oddly both.

- **Too narrow** in the sense that it overemphasizes the institutional trivia of the FPM formula and the claim of being the first to combine literatures.
- **Too broad** in the sense that it gestures toward “violence against women,” “domestic violence,” “female homicide,” “bureaucratic employment,” and “fiscal federalism” without deciding what precise conversation it wants to lead.

This needs tightening. The natural audience is economists interested in:
1. public finance/fiscal federalism,
2. development and local public goods,
3. gender, labor, and household bargaining.

The paper should be written for that combined audience, not as an institutional-note plus a violence application.

### What literature does it seem unaware of?
A few gaps or underdeveloped connections stand out.

1. **Targeting vs incidence literature in public finance/development.**  
   The paper’s conclusion is really about the difference between resources spent in female-intensive sectors and resources directly controlled by women. That is close to the literature on who receives and controls resources, not just on violence.

2. **State capacity / local public employment literature.**  
   If the channel is public hiring, then the paper should speak more directly to how municipal expansions translate into actual jobs, service delivery, and bureaucratic composition.

3. **Gendered effects of public sector employment.**  
   There is a broader conversation on the public sector as a source of “good jobs” for women, especially in developing countries. This paper should hook into that more explicitly.

4. **Crime and public spending.**  
   If the outcome is homicide, there is also a relevant conversation about whether municipal fiscal shocks affect crime or mortality more broadly. The placebos touch this, but the framing does not.

### Is it having the right conversation?
Not quite. The most impactful framing is probably **not** “fiscal federalism meets IPV.” It is:

> “When public budgets expand in ways that disproportionately employ women, does that change women’s effective outside options enough to reduce violence?”

That connects to household bargaining, public employment, and the incidence of public spending. It is a better conversation than the current “first paper to examine gendered consequences of FPM.”

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Municipal fiscal windfalls are common and often flow into health and education, sectors dominated by female workers. Prior research suggests women’s economic autonomy can reduce domestic violence.

### Tension
But it is not obvious whether indirect public-sector employment expansion generated by untargeted municipal transfers is strong enough to affect violence. Existing evidence mostly concerns direct wage shocks, labor-demand shocks, or transfers to women, not general fiscal windfalls.

### Resolution
Using Brazil’s transfer thresholds, the paper finds little to no detectable reduction in female homicide.

### Implications
Indirect improvements in women’s labor-market opportunities through municipal budgets may be too diffuse, weak, or poorly targeted to move extreme violence; this matters for how we think about the gendered incidence of public spending and the external effects of intergovernmental transfers.

That is a perfectly viable narrative arc. The problem is that the paper does **not** execute it cleanly.

At the moment, the paper feels partly like a collection of ingredients looking for a settled story:
- transfer thresholds,
- female-intensive sectors,
- household bargaining,
- homicide vs domestic violence,
- placebos,
- null result.

The biggest narrative problem is **outcome confusion**. The paper repeatedly toggles among domestic violence notifications, female homicide, “violence against women,” and even tables whose labels do not match the surrounding text. The introduction says the main finding is lower female homicide; the abstract says insignificant; the results table seems mislabeled; the discussion pivots back to nulls. This is fatal to narrative credibility, even before any referee gets involved.

So what story should it be telling?

A simple one:

1. **Fiscal windfalls to municipalities disproportionately fund female-intensive public employment.**
2. **This creates a clean test of whether indirect gains in women’s employment opportunities reduce violence.**
3. **For severe violence, the answer appears to be no detectable effect.**
4. **Therefore, the channel identified in wage/cash-transfer studies may not generalize to untargeted fiscal expansions.**

That story is coherent, disciplined, and potentially publishable in a strong field journal. It could also support a top-journal submission if the mechanism were directly demonstrated and the outcome strategy were better aligned with the theory.

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

### What fact would I lead with?
I would lead with this:

> “In Brazil, crossing a population threshold mechanically raises municipal transfers and funds sectors where most workers are women—but these windfalls do not appear to reduce female homicide.”

That is the most interesting version of the result because it creates tension with the prior household bargaining literature.

### Would people lean in?
Some would lean in, but only briefly. The topic is important, and the design is familiar and credible. But unless the presenter quickly explains **why this null is informative**, people may disengage. A top-field audience will immediately ask: “Why should we have expected public transfer windfalls to work like wage shocks or transfers to women?”

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Almost certainly:

**“Do the transfers actually increase female public employment?”**

That is the central question hanging over the paper. The second likely question is:

**“Why female homicide rather than a closer measure of IPV?”**

Those are not technical objections here; they are strategic ones. They go to whether the paper’s story cashes out.

### Is the null interesting?
Potentially yes. But the paper has not yet earned the right to make the null important.

For a null result to matter in AER territory, the paper must convince readers that:
1. there was a strong prior reason to expect an effect,
2. the treatment really moves the mechanism,
3. the outcome is the right place to look,
4. ruling out even moderate effects updates beliefs.

The paper partially does (1), weakly does (4), and does not yet do enough on (2) or (3). So at present the null reads less like “important discipline on a prominent mechanism” and more like “a plausible but unsuccessful extension.”

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

### 1. Clean up the outcome definition immediately
This is the first order issue. The paper is badly muddled on what the main outcome is:
- abstract emphasizes female homicide,
- table notes refer to domestic violence notifications,
- columns are mislabeled,
- text around results does not align with the table,
- summary statistics mention 2015–2019 in one place and 2009–2022 elsewhere.

This is not a stylistic quibble. It destroys strategic clarity. Decide:
- What is the primary outcome?
- What is secondary?
- What is placebo?
Then rewrite the introduction, abstract, results, and tables around that single hierarchy.

### 2. Front-load the conceptual contribution, not the institution
The reader should not have to wade through institutional detail before hearing the question. Start with:
- female-intensive public spending,
- indirect vs direct bargaining channels,
- why Brazil’s FPM offers a clean test.

Only then give the formula details.

### 3. Shorten the institutional section
The FPM background can be tighter. Right now it reads like an applied micro template. For strategic positioning, the institution is a vehicle, not the paper’s soul.

### 4. Expand the motivation for why the null matters
The discussion section is actually closer to the right framing than parts of the introduction. Some of that should move up. In particular:
- public employment vs private wage shocks,
- targeted vs untargeted resources,
- extreme violence vs broader IPV.

These are the reasons the paper teaches us something even with null estimates.

### 5. Move some validation/robustness material out of the main narrative
This is not a referee report, but as an editor reading for story, the paper feels cluttered with design-validation prose too early and too repetitively. The introduction should not spend so much energy on donut RD, polynomial choices, and placebo cutoffs. That material can be compressed and partly shifted back.

### 6. Put the strongest comparative insight in the conclusion
The conclusion currently mainly insists the identification is good. That is not where a top-journal conclusion should end. It should end with the substantive takeaway:
- untargeted fiscal windfalls are not the same as resources controlled by women;
- the incidence of public spending matters;
- gendered spending composition may not be sufficient to alter severe violence.

That is a real idea. End there.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

At the moment, the main gap is a mix of **framing problem, scope problem, and ambition problem**.

### Framing problem
This is the biggest one. The current version is not yet telling the highest-value story. It is too easy to read it as “first paper to apply FPM RD to violence against women.” That is not enough for AER. The paper has to become a paper about the **limits of indirect, untargeted public spending as a tool for shifting women’s bargaining power and reducing violence**.

### Scope problem
The paper’s theory is about female employment and bargaining, but the empirical presentation does not convincingly trace that arc. The mechanism is asserted, not shown, and the outcome may be too distal. That makes the scope feel narrower than the title promises.

### Novelty problem
Moderate. The ingredients are familiar: FPM thresholds, violence outcomes, bargaining theory. The novelty lies in the synthesis and the substantive comparison between kinds of shocks. That novelty is there, but underexploited.

### Ambition problem
Also moderate. The paper is competent and cautious, but safe. It is set up as a neat reduced-form exercise rather than as a bigger statement about the incidence of public spending, women’s economic autonomy, and the transportability of findings from wage/transfer shocks to public finance settings.

### Single most impactful advice
If the author changes only one thing, it should be this:

**Rebuild the paper around a sharper conceptual question—whether untargeted fiscal windfalls that disproportionately expand female public employment affect violence the way direct shocks to women’s resources do—and organize every section around answering that question cleanly and consistently.**

If I were allowed a close second, it would be: **show the female-employment first stage directly.**
