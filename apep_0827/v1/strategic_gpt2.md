## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple and policy-relevant question: when the Netherlands legalized the cannabis supply chain to coffeeshops—closing the famous gap between tolerated retail and illegal wholesale—did crime fall? Using the Dutch policy experiment and municipality-level crime data, the paper’s headline result is that, in the first 18 months, legalizing supply produced no detectable change in drug crime, violence, or total crime.

A busy economist should care because this is one of the cleanest real-world tests we have of a core claim in the economics of prohibition: if black markets generate crime, then replacing illicit supply with legal regulated supply should reduce it. The Dutch setting is unusual precisely because retail was already tolerated, so the policy targets supply-chain criminality rather than consumer access.

### Does the paper articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Not quite. The ingredients are there, but the introduction leads with institutional detail before fully crystallizing the world-level question. The current opening says “this is the world’s first experiment” and “I estimate the effect on crime,” but the sharper pitch is not yet doing enough work: this is not mainly a paper about a Dutch policy pilot; it is a paper about whether legalizing supply, holding retail access broadly fixed, reduces the crime associated with prohibition.

The first two paragraphs should say something more like:

> For decades, economists and policymakers have argued that prohibition creates crime not only by criminalizing users, but by forcing production and distribution into illegal markets. Yet most evidence on cannabis legalization confounds multiple channels at once: when jurisdictions liberalize cannabis, they typically change retail access, possession rules, and supply conditions simultaneously. As a result, we still do not know whether legalizing the supply chain itself reduces crime.
>
> The Netherlands’ 2024 cannabis supply-chain experiment offers a rare test of that question. Dutch coffeeshops have long been allowed to sell cannabis legally while sourcing it illegally through the “back door.” In 10 municipalities, the government replaced that illegal supply chain with licensed growers and regulated distribution, while 10 matched municipalities remained under the old regime. Using municipality-level crime data, this paper shows that closing the back door produced no detectable short-run change in drug crime, violence, or total crime.

That is the pitch the paper should have.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### Contribution in one sentence

The paper provides early causal evidence that legalizing the upstream cannabis supply chain—without newly legalizing retail sales—did not measurably change crime in the first phase of the Netherlands’ controlled supply experiment.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest papers?

Partly, but not sharply enough. The introduction usefully distinguishes the paper from broad cannabis-legalization studies, but the differentiation still reads a bit like “there is no paper on this exact Dutch reform, so here is one.” That is too literal and too local for AER positioning.

The real differentiation is stronger than that: prior work mostly studies reforms that jointly affect possession, retail access, policing, and market scale; this paper isolates a narrower and conceptually important margin—legal supply replacing illicit wholesale in a market where retail tolerance already existed. That distinction should be the centerpiece, not an afterthought.

### Is the contribution framed as a question about the world or as filling a gap in a literature?

Mixed, but too much of the latter. The better framing is world-facing:

- Do black-market supply chains themselves generate observable crime?
- If you formalize upstream production while leaving retail institutions largely intact, does crime fall?

That is much stronger than “the first paper on the Dutch experiment.”

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?

Right now, they might say: “It’s a DiD on the Dutch cannabis experiment and finds no crime effect.” That is competent, but not memorable.

You want them to say: “It isolates the supply-chain channel in legalization and finds that replacing illegal wholesale with regulated production doesn’t seem to reduce crime, at least in the short run.” That is a real idea.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Several possibilities:

1. **Stronger emphasis on the supply-chain margin.**  
   This is the cheapest and most important gain. The paper should frame itself as identifying which part of prohibition matters: retail criminalization versus upstream market illegality.

2. **A more direct organized-crime outcome.**  
   If the theory is about illegal supply chains and criminal networks, municipality-level registered drug crime is only an indirect proxy. The paper would feel bigger with outcomes closer to the mechanism: cannabis cultivation busts, trafficking offenses, seizures, weapons incidents tied to drug markets, property damage from illegal grows, extortion, or organized-crime investigations.

3. **A cleaner distinction between short-run transition and full treatment.**  
   Right now the paper wisely notes that illegal and legal supply coexist in the transition. But that also means the current result is “no detectable effect during partial replacement of illegal supply,” not yet “supply legalization doesn’t reduce crime.” The paper could become much bigger if it leaned into this as an early reading on treatment intensity rather than a broad verdict.

4. **Market-level outcomes beyond crime.**  
   If the reform also changed quality, prices, product composition, enforcement, or shop behavior, connecting crime results to those margins would broaden the paper from “crime null” to “what supply-chain legalization does and does not change.”

5. **Comparative framing with other legalization episodes.**  
   The paper could be more ambitious in arguing that prior crime reductions in legalization contexts likely came from different channels—cartel displacement, border substitution, policing changes, or retail liberalization—not from regulated supply per se.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

For decades, the Netherlands tolerated cannabis sales at retail while leaving production and wholesale illegal. More broadly, economists have long argued that black-market crime is generated by prohibition, but empirical evidence typically comes from reforms that change many margins at once.

### Tension

If illegal supply chains are a major source of crime, then replacing them with licensed, tracked, regulated production should reduce crime. But this has rarely been tested in a setting where retail access is already in place, so we do not know whether “the back door” itself is the real problem.

### Resolution

The paper finds no detectable short-run effect of the Dutch experiment on drug crime, violent crime, or total crime.

### Implications

Either the illegal supply chain contributes less to observable local crime than commonly presumed, or partial/transition-stage legalization is too weak to disrupt entrenched criminal networks. In either case, a central argument for supply legalization appears weaker in the short run than advocates often claim.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the outline of one, but it still feels somewhat like **a collection of estimates searching for the strongest story**. The introduction has many correct elements, yet the narrative energy dissipates because the paper alternates among several framings:

- first evidence on the Dutch experiment,
- cannabis legalization and crime,
- broader prohibition/black-market theory,
- a null-result policy note.

Those are related but not identical. The paper needs to choose one spine.

### What story should it be telling?

The best story is:

**Economists think crime arises from illicit supply under prohibition. The Dutch reform uniquely targets that supply channel while keeping retail tolerance intact. Early evidence shows no detectable crime reduction, suggesting either that illicit supply is not the main local crime margin or that partial legal replacement is insufficient to displace criminal markets.**

That is a coherent AER-style narrative. Right now the paper under-sells it by spending too much time on procedural detail and too little on the conceptual puzzle.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?

“Here’s a neat one: the Netherlands finally legalized the cannabis back door—the supply chain to coffeeshops—and in the short run crime didn’t budge.”

That is the line.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

They would **lean in initially**, because the Dutch institutional setting is famous and the policy is genuinely interesting. But the second reaction will be: “Interesting, but is that because the treatment is still partial?” If the paper cannot quickly answer or reframe that, interest will fade.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Almost certainly:
- “Was the illegal market actually displaced yet?”
- Then: “What crime margin should have moved if the theory were right?”
- And: “Is this telling us supply legalization doesn’t matter, or only that 18 months is too soon?”

These are exactly the questions the paper should anticipate and turn into strengths of the framing.

### Is the null result itself interesting?

Yes—but only if framed correctly. A null in a high-profile, theoretically consequential, unusually clean institutional setting can be very interesting. But the paper has to argue that this is informative about a first-order mechanism, not merely that “we estimated something and didn’t find significance.”

The current draft does some of this, especially by emphasizing that large effects are ruled out. But it still risks reading like an early inconclusive evaluation. The authors need to be more explicit:

- Why many people expected crime to fall.
- Why even a null in the transition phase is informative.
- What classes of theories survive or fail after seeing this null.

Without that, it can feel like a premature progress report rather than a field-shaping result.
