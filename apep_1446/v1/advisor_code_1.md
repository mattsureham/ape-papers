**Summary**

The paper uses Medicaid T-MSIS data to document that most buprenorphine prescribers who entered the market after the January 2023 X-waiver elimination located in counties that already had providers, rather than in treatment deserts. A county-month difference-in-differences shows a statistically and economically significant negative desert × post coefficient, suggesting deregulation primarily thickened already-served markets. Robustness checks (alternative desert thresholds, placebo drug test, permutation inference) and descriptive survival analyses reinforce the conclusion that deregulation reduced the credential barrier without altering the geography of access.

**Essential Points**

1. **Parallel-trends logic is tenuous given the pre-treatment mechanics of the outcome.** Desert counties mechanically have zero new entrants before treatment, while non-desert counties occasionally record positive counts. The DiD coefficient therefore captures a post-treatment difference in entry “gaps,” but the usual parallel trends intuition—i.e., that the two groups would have evolved similarly absent treatment—is effectively untestable. The paper needs to more thoroughly justify that this comparison isolates policy effects rather than simply reflecting the mathematical impossibility of desert entry prior to 2023. At minimum, the authors should present additional placebo tests (e.g., random “fake” reforms in earlier years) or structural modeling showing that the difference-in-differences estimate is not driven by differential secular trends in county observable characteristics or by mean reversion.

2. **The desert definition and treatment indicator may conflate long-standing supply shortfalls with post-treatment heterogeneity.** Counties labeled as deserts had zero buprenorphine NPIs in 2022, but many of those counties also differ systematically from non-deserts (population size, Medicaid eligibility changes, broadband, hospital closures). Without controls or reweighting, the difference may be capturing differential responsiveness to any national policy, not just geographically specific entry dynamics. The authors should demonstrate that the desert coefficient remains when conditioning on county-level covariates (e.g., population, urban/rural indicators, prior opioid mortality, Medicaid expansion status) or using matching to create more comparable treated and control groups.

3. **Provider location from NPPES may misrepresent local service delivery, particularly given growth in telehealth and facility-based billing.** If many new entrants operate through telemedicine based in urban areas (but serving rural patients), they would appear to “cluster” in already-served counties even if their patients are in deserts. The paper acknowledges this issue in passing, but the magnitude of the concern is large enough to warrant empirical investigation—e.g., by comparing practice ZIP codes to county-level telehealth utilization, examining facility taxonomy (hospital vs. office-based), or restricting analysis to providers with multiple practice locations. Without addressing this, the geographic concentration result may overstate the failure to reach deserts.

**Suggestions**

1. **Bolster the identification strategy with alternative specifications and visual diagnostics.** The current event study is deterministic for new entrants because of the zero pretreatment values in deserts. Consider adding a “lead-lag” table for active NPIs or beneficiary counts where there is more pre-treatment variation, which would allow standard parallel-trends diagnostics. Alternatively, apply a synthetic control or regression discontinuity approach at county-level thresholds (e.g., comparing counties with 0 versus 1 prior provider) to provide a robustness check that does not rely on zero pre-treatment events. Presenting a placebo test using earlier years (e.g., treating January 2021 as the reform date) would also help build confidence that the estimated desert deficit is specific to the X-waiver elimination.

2. **Introduce richer covariate controls or weighting to rule out confounding country trends.** Desert counties differ dramatically from non-deserts (population density, socioeconomic status, Medicaid policy). Including time-varying covariates such as county population, urban/rural classification, Medicaid enrollment trends, opioid overdose rates, and telehealth policy changes would help ensure the desert × post coefficient is not capturing compositional shifts. You might also explore an event-study-like reweighting (e.g., entropy balancing) to align the support of non-desert counties with desert counties, making the comparison more credible. While the fixed effects absorb time-invariant heterogeneity, differential time trends remain a concern given the small number of non-desert counties (101) and potential idiosyncratic shocks.

3. **Address potential mismeasurement in provider geography using supplementary data.** The NPPES practice ZIP is a proxy for service location, but many physicians maintain administrative addresses that differ from where they see Medicaid patients. Use the Taxpayer Identification Number (TIN) linkage or facility identifiers (e.g., whether the provider is affiliated with a federally qualified health center) to triangulate actual service sites. Alternatively, compare the NPPES address to T-MSIS servicing ZIP codes (if available) to assess how often the “practice” location matches the billing location. If systematic measurement error exists, consider restricting to providers with match flags (e.g., same county for billing and servicing NPIs, or providers whose taxonomy indicates “office-based” rather than “telehealth service”). These strategies would clarify whether the concentration in non-desert counties reflects practice location or just registration addresses.

4. **Explore heterogeneity in provider characteristics and market structure.** The manifest emphasizes whether new entrants reached deserts, but it would be helpful to understand which types of providers entered (specialty, NP/PA vs. physician, practice type). Are the new entrants in deserts more likely to be part of community health centers or the Indian Health Service? Does the telehealth reimbursement environment correlate with entry patterns? Incorporating HRSA facility data or NPPES taxonomy codes into heterogeneous treatment effect analyses could reveal whether certain segments were more responsive to deregulation. Similarly, examine whether deserts that did receive entrants share common features (e.g., proximity to a nearby urban center, presence of a rural health clinic) that might suggest policy levers.

5. **Clarify the interpretation of the beneficiary null result.** Column (3) of Table 2 shows a null effect on beneficiary counts even in non-desert counties. This is consistent with market thickening, but the standard error is large relative to the coefficient, and enrollment declines from the Medicaid unwinding could be confounding. Consider reporting per-capita beneficiary changes or analyzing beneficiary counts normalized by county population to give a clearer sense of whether the null effect is due to noise or genuine stagnation. You might also explore whether beneficiary volumes increased for the top counties that absorbed many new providers, to ascertain whether thickening occurred through existing patient pools rather than new access.

6. **Deepen the policy discussion around telehealth and integration with demand-side interventions.** The credential-gap fallacy framing is persuasive, but the policy takeaways would be richer if you discussed how deregulation could interact with other reforms. For instance, how might targeted telehealth reimbursement, in-person support hubs, or loan repayment programs complement the credential elimination to reach deserts? Are there promising models (e.g., hub-and-spoke telemedicine) that already bridge this provider willingness gap? Including a short subsection linking your findings to specific ongoing policy proposals (such as CMS rural emergency hospital rules or methadone dispensing reforms) would broaden the paper’s relevance.

7. **Report power calculations or effect sizes with context.** Table A1 reports standardized effect sizes, but it would help to contextualize those magnitudes for readers unfamiliar with entry rates of 0.088 per month. For example, how many additional entrants per county-year does that imply? Is the coefficient economically meaningful relative to the total number of providers? Including a brief calculation in the main text (e.g., “the negative coefficient corresponds to roughly one fewer entrant every nine years per desert county”) would aid interpretation.

Overall, the paper makes an important contribution, but strengthening the identification arguments, addressing measurement concerns, and enriching the exploration of heterogeneity would greatly improve its credibility and policy relevance.
