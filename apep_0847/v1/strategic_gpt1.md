This is an interesting paper with a potentially publishable core idea, but in its current form it reads more like a well-executed field paper with a clever label than like an AER paper with a field-defining question. The main strategic issue is not that the paper lacks findings; it is that the findings are framed too locally around English stop smoking services, and not yet elevated into a broader economic question that top readers will immediately recognize as important.

## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether public-service capacity disappears as quickly as budgets do. Using cuts to local public health grants in England, it argues that stop-smoking services exhibited substantial persistence: places with larger initial public health investment kept producing more successful quits for several years after austerity began, implying that public health systems embody a stock of organizational capital that survives temporary funding cuts but can be destroyed by operational shutdowns.

Why should a busy economist care? Because the paper is really about the depreciation rate of public-sector capacity. If true, that has implications far beyond tobacco policy: it changes how we think about government investment, austerity, dynamic public production, and the measurement of policy costs.

### Does the paper articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Partly, but not fully. The current opening is competent and policy-relevant, but it starts too narrow and too descriptively. It opens with institutional detail and service collapse, then only later tells the reader the bigger conceptual point. The strongest idea in the paper is not “England cut stop smoking services.” It is “public spending creates organizational capital, and its depreciation path matters.” That should be paragraph one, not paragraph five.

The term “cessation capital” is memorable, but also a little too paper-specific and a bit slogan-y. The paper should lead with “organizational capital in public services” or “public-sector capacity” and then use smoking cessation as the setting.

### What the first two paragraphs should say instead

A stronger version would say something like:

> Governments often evaluate spending cuts as if service capacity moves one-for-one with annual budgets. But many public services rely on organizational capital—trained staff, referral networks, routines, and community relationships—that may persist after funding is reduced. How quickly that capital depreciates is a first-order question for public economics, yet we know surprisingly little about it.
>
> This paper studies that question in the context of England’s stop-smoking services after large cuts to local public health grants. I show that areas with greater pre-austerity investment maintained substantially higher rates of professionally supported smoking cessation for four to five years after budget cuts began, even though service spending fell sharply nationwide. The results suggest that public health investment creates durable capacity that erodes slowly under fiscal pressure but can be destroyed quickly by operational shutdowns, as happened during COVID.

That is the pitch. It tells the reader the world question, the setting, the result, and the broader implication in two paragraphs.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper’s contribution is to show that local public health spending builds organizational capacity that persists for several years after budgets are cut, using English stop-smoking services as a case study.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest papers?

Not yet sharply enough. The introduction cites broad austerity-health papers, smoking cessation effectiveness papers, and a very broad public-capital analogy literature, but the differentiation is still somewhat diffuse. Right now the reader may think: “So this is a DiD on public-health austerity with quit rates instead of prevalence.” The paper needs to work harder to say why that is a distinct contribution rather than just a cleaner outcome variable in an existing conversation.

The real differentiator is not just the outcome. It is the dynamic margin: the paper claims to recover a depreciation path of public-service capacity. That needs to be foregrounded much more aggressively. “We identify persistence in organizational capital” is bigger than “we use quit rates because prevalence is confounded by convergence.”

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a gap in a literature?

At present it oscillates, but leans too much toward literature-gap framing. The strongest framing is clearly about the world: when governments cut budgets, what actually happens to service capacity over time? That is a real question about state capacity and public production. The paper should stay there.

The weaker framing is: “prior work on austerity has identification problems, and we provide cleaner evidence in one context.” That is respectable, but not AER-level by itself.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the intro?

In current form, only partially. A smart economist would probably say: “It’s a paper on English public-health austerity showing that stop-smoking quit rates fell less in initially better-funded places, and that prevalence effects are confounded by convergence.” That is accurate, but not exciting enough.

What you want them to say is: “It shows that public services have organizational capital with slow depreciation, so annual budget cuts do not map one-for-one into immediate service collapse.” That is a paper.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Three possibilities, in order of value:

1. **Reframe around public-sector organizational capital, not smoking per se.**  
   This is the single biggest gain. The authors already have the bones of this. The title, intro, and discussion should all push harder on the general object of interest.

2. **Show the phenomenon is not unique to smoking.**  
   Right now the paper uses sexual health as a placebo, which is useful defensively but does not enlarge the contribution. A much stronger paper would include one or two additional service lines where capacity plausibly operates similarly or differently. That would let the paper speak to depreciation across services rather than just one niche domain.

3. **Clarify the economic mechanism of persistence.**  
   Not in an econometric sense, but in a conceptual-production-function sense. Is persistence driven by staff retention? Referral networks? embedded primary-care routines? local public-health management quality? The paper names these ingredients but does not really discipline them. A cleaner conceptual framework would make the contribution feel less descriptive.

A more ambitious outcome variable would also help. “Successful quits” is close to a service-output measure, which is good, but it is still somewhat narrow. If the paper could link persistent service capacity to downstream health utilization, labor supply, or inequality in a convincing narrative, the stakes would rise. Right now the most policy-relevant downstream outcomes are explicitly null or contaminated, which leaves the paper with a narrower payoff.

In other words: not “attack” the closest neighbors, but pivot away from their narrowest framing. The paper should say, in effect: prior austerity work asked whether spending cuts worsen health; we ask a prior question—how quickly does service capacity depreciate after spending falls?

Even a small amount of engagement with those literatures could dramatically improve the paper’s stature. Right now the move from “quit rates persisted” to “organizational capital depreciates slowly” feels more asserted than situated.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Governments fund public services annually, but many services depend on accumulated capacity—people, routines, trust, and networks—not just current-year spending. England’s post-2015 public-health cuts create a setting to observe what happens when funding is withdrawn.

### Tension

If service capacity moves one-for-one with spending, output should collapse immediately. But if public investment creates durable organizational capital, output may persist even after budgets shrink. Existing outcome measures like smoking prevalence are noisy and confounded, so it is hard to see the underlying capacity dynamics.

### Resolution

Using professionally validated quit rates, the paper finds that areas with higher initial grant levels maintained more cessation output for several years after austerity began. This persistence disappears during COVID-era shutdowns, suggesting gradual depreciation under cuts but rapid destruction under closure.

### Implications

Public spending should be viewed partly as building a stock of organizational capital, not just purchasing contemporaneous flow output. This changes how we think about austerity, cost-benefit analysis, and the timing of policy effects.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the ingredients, but the arc is not yet clean. Right now the paper alternates among three stories:

1. a paper about austerity and smoking outcomes;
2. a paper about quit rates being cleaner than prevalence;
3. a paper about “cessation capital.”

These are related but not fully integrated. The best story is number 3, recast in more general language. The quit-rate-versus-prevalence contrast should support that story, not become a coequal storyline.

At times the paper feels like a collection of findings looking for the dominant frame: the prevalence effect disappears, the quit rate persists, the placebo is null, COVID reverses the result. The story tying them together should be: **service output reveals the stock of organizational capacity, and its depreciation is slow until a true operational break occurs.**

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?

“I’d lead with: after England cut local public-health grants, places with greater pre-existing public-health investment continued generating substantially more verified smoking quits for about five years. The budget was cut, but the service capacity did not disappear immediately.”

That is a good dinner-party fact.

### Would people lean in?

Some would, but not all—not if presented as a smoking-services paper. They would lean in much more if presented as a paper about the depreciation of government capacity. The difference in reaction is large.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Almost certainly: “Is this specific to smoking cessation, or does it tell us something broader about public services?” That is the key question the paper needs to anticipate and answer better.

Other likely questions:
- “What exactly is persisting—staff, management, referral networks, or demand?”
- “Does this persistence show up in other services?”
- “If health outcomes don’t move cleanly, how policy-relevant is the service-output result?”

### Are the findings interesting if modest or null on downstream outcomes?

Yes, but only if the paper makes peace with that and leans into it. The null on smoking prevalence can be interesting as a cautionary tale about what aggregate health outcomes can and cannot reveal. But that cannot be the centerpiece. The paper has to persuade readers that learning about service capacity itself is valuable, even absent clean downstream health effects in this setting.

That argument is available: governments make budgeting decisions on service lines long before ultimate health outcomes show up. Knowing that capacity persists—and then can collapse nonlinearly—is itself highly policy-relevant.

But the paper currently still sounds slightly apologetic about the null. It should sound more strategic: “The reason to study quit rates is that they are the correct output of the public production function we care about.”

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The gap is mostly **framing plus ambition**, with some **scope** concerns.

### Is it a framing problem?

Yes, strongly. The science, as presented, supports a larger question than the paper currently claims. The paper has a real idea—public spending builds durable organizational capacity—but it keeps retreating into the safer language of English smoking services and austerity.

### Is it a scope problem?

Also yes. A single service line in one institutional setting makes the paper vulnerable to the “interesting, but niche” reaction. To feel AER-level, the paper either needs:
- a stronger general framework and much better framing, or
- broader evidence that the same mechanism appears across multiple public services.

Right now the paper does not quite do enough to establish external relevance beyond this application.

### Is it a novelty problem?

Not fatal, but present. There are many papers on austerity and many papers on smoking policy. The novelty lies in the **dynamic persistence of service capacity**. If that is not made unmistakable, the paper will feel incremental.

### Is it an ambition problem?

Yes. The paper is careful, competent, and sensible—but a bit safe. It lands on a moderate claim in a narrow context when it could be asking a much more ambitious question. Top-field readers want to leave with a changed way of thinking about public-sector production. The paper is close to that, but not yet there.

### Single most impactful advice

If the author changes only one thing, it should be this:

**Rewrite the paper around the general question “how fast does public-sector organizational capital depreciate after funding cuts?” and present smoking cessation as the ideal empirical setting for answering that question, not as the question itself.**

That one change would improve the title, introduction, literature positioning, narrative arc, and the paper’s odds of feeling AER-relevant.
