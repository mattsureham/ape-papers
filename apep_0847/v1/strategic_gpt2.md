## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether cuts to public health budgets immediately destroy service capacity, or whether some of that capacity persists because local governments accumulate organizational know-how that outlives annual appropriations. Using variation in baseline public health grants across English local authorities during post-2015 austerity, the paper argues that stop-smoking services were more resilient than budgets imply: places with greater pre-cut investment sustained more successful quits for several years, even as spending fell.

A busy economist should care because the paper is trying to say something broader than smoking: public spending may create durable organizational capital, so the effects of austerity depend not just on current expenditure but on the depreciation rate of public-sector capacity.

### Does the paper itself articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Reasonably well, but not sharply enough. The current introduction leads with institutional detail and then moves to “cessation capital,” which is catchy but slightly inward-looking. The broader point—that this is a paper about the durability of public service capacity under fiscal retrenchment—should come earlier and more plainly. Right now the paper risks sounding like a sector-specific evaluation of English stop-smoking services rather than a general-interest economics paper about public capital, depreciation, and austerity.

### What the first two paragraphs should say instead

The introduction should open with the big question: when governments cut budgets, how fast does public capacity actually disappear? Annual appropriations are flows, but effective public services depend on stocks—trained workers, referral networks, managerial routines, and trust—that may persist after money is withdrawn. This matters for how economists think about austerity, state capacity, and the dynamic effects of public spending.

Then the paper should say that England’s post-2015 cuts to local public health grants provide a useful setting to study this question. The paper shows that local authorities with higher pre-austerity public health investment sustained substantially more professionally supported smoking quits for several years after cuts began, even though spending on stop-smoking services fell sharply nationwide. The key interpretation is that public health investment built organizational capital that depreciated slowly under fiscal pressure but could be rapidly destroyed by operational shutdowns during COVID.

That is the pitch the paper should have.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### Contribution in one sentence

The paper’s contribution is to show that pre-existing public health investment created durable local organizational capacity in smoking cessation services, so austerity reduced annual budgets much faster than it reduced service output.

### Is this clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?

Partially, but not yet convincingly enough for AER-level positioning.

The introduction says: prior austerity papers study aggregate health outcomes and face identification problems; this paper studies service output and finds persistence. That is useful, but the differentiation is still a bit muddy because the paper is straddling several literatures without fully owning one. Is the contribution:

1. a better austerity design?
2. a paper on smoking cessation services?
3. a paper on organizational/public capital depreciation?
4. a cautionary paper about convergence bias in needs-based funding designs?

All four are present. That is intellectually interesting, but strategically dangerous. Readers may struggle to identify the main novelty.

The best version is #3, with #1 and #4 as supporting elements. If positioned that way, the paper becomes: “Here is rare evidence on the depreciation of public organizational capital under austerity.” That is more memorable than “another local-austerity DiD.”

### WORLD question or LITERATURE gap?

The paper does some of both, but it should lean more toward the world question. Right now the introduction contains too much “this contributes to three literatures” language. A stronger framing is:

- World question: When governments disinvest, how long does service capacity survive?
- Empirical setting: English stop-smoking services under austerity.
- Broader implication: state capacity is a stock, not just a flow.

That is much stronger than “there is a gap in the literature on smoking cessation under fiscal pressure.”

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the intro?

At present: maybe, but with effort. A smart economist would probably say, “It’s a DiD on English public health grants showing that quit rates held up longer than expected in high-grant places.” That is not bad, but it is not yet distinctive enough.

What you want them to say is: “Interesting paper—the main point is that public health spending builds organizational capital that survives budget cuts for years, so austerity affects service capacity with long lags.”

That requires stronger conceptual framing and less emphasis on the mechanics of the grant formula in the opening pages.

### What would make the contribution bigger?

Specific ways to enlarge it:

1. **Make depreciation the centerpiece, not a metaphor.**  
   The paper repeatedly uses “cessation capital” and “depreciates slowly,” but it does not really operationalize a depreciation framework. If the authors can more explicitly estimate or characterize the dynamic persistence of service capacity, the paper becomes much more than a policy note.

2. **Show broader consequences of persistence, not just service output.**  
   The paper says smoking prevalence is not cleanly identified. Fine. But then the broader stakes remain somewhat abstract. A more compelling set of downstream outcomes—service take-up, pharmacotherapy prescribing, referrals, or other direct markers of cessation system activity—would make the story feel more complete.

3. **Compare organizational capital across service lines.**  
   The placebo on chlamydia screening is nice, but not enough. If the paper could compare services that are more vs. less organization-intensive, it could say something broader about which kinds of public programs retain capacity under austerity.

4. **Frame as a paper on public-sector capacity under retrenchment.**  
   The current title and framing are very sector-specific. The contribution becomes bigger if smoking cessation is presented as a clean laboratory for a more general phenomenon.

### How should the paper position itself relative to them?

Mostly **build on and redirect**, not attack.

- Relative to the austerity-health literature: “Existing work asks whether cuts worsen health; we ask how fast service capacity depreciates, and show that direct service outputs are more informative than downstream prevalence measures.”
- Relative to smoking cessation papers: “The literature establishes effectiveness of treatment; we study the durability of delivery capacity when funding contracts.”
- Relative to public capital literature: “This is evidence that some public investments create intangible, organization-based capital whose depreciation is nonlinear and path dependent.”

The paper should not overclaim that it “overturns” assumptions or fundamentally resolves the austerity-health literature. It has a cleaner setting for one service line, not a universal refutation.

### Too narrow or too broad?

At the moment, oddly both.

- **Too narrow** in data and institutional detail: this can feel like a specialized UK public health paper.
- **Too broad** in its contribution claims: austerity, smoking, organizational capital, public capital, and convergence bias all appear as equal themes.

The fix is to choose a single core conversation: **durability of public service capacity under budget cuts**. Then the others become sub-conversations.

### What literature does it seem unaware of?

It should engage more seriously with:

1. **State capacity / public administration / organizational capital**  
   There is a broader literature in economics and political economy on bureaucratic capacity, implementation, and institutional persistence. That is the right conceptual home for the “cessation capital” idea.

2. **Human capital specificity and organizational knowledge**  
   The paper’s mechanism—trained counselors, referral networks, local trust—is really an organizational economics mechanism. It should talk to that literature rather than only to public health and macro public capital citations.

3. **Dynamic effects of spending cuts / hysteresis in public services**  
   Even if not a formal literature, the authors need neighbors on persistence and irreversibility in public provision.

4. **Health systems and provider capacity**  
   There is likely work on closures, care networks, and capacity destruction in hospitals/clinics that would give this paper better conceptual peers than Barro and Pritchett.

### Is it having the right conversation?

Not quite yet. The most impactful conversation is not “another austerity and health paper,” nor “a smoking cessation policy paper.” It is a paper about **how public organizations retain or lose productive capacity when budgets fall**. That conversation could attract public finance, health, macro-public capital, organizational economics, and political economy readers.

That is the unexpected but better literature bridge.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

England devolved public health responsibilities to local authorities and then cut grants substantially. Stop-smoking services suffered large spending declines, creating a natural question about whether service collapse followed immediately.

### Tension

Budgets are flows, but service delivery may depend on stocks of organizational capacity. So observed spending cuts may overstate short-run damage if infrastructure persists—or understate long-run damage if capacity eventually collapses.

### Resolution

Areas with higher baseline public health investment maintained higher quit-service output for several years after austerity began, suggesting persistence of local cessation capacity. But that advantage vanished during COVID-era shutdowns, implying that capacity is resilient to gradual fiscal retrenchment yet fragile to operational disruption.

### Implications

The effects of public spending cuts depend on the depreciation path of organizational capital. Policymakers should not assume service output moves one-for-one with annual budgets, and researchers should distinguish service-output measures from slower-moving downstream health outcomes.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

Yes, more than many papers. This is one of its strengths. There is a real story here. But the paper weakens its own arc by overloading the reader with side missions:

- proving smoking prevalence is confounded by convergence,
- hinting at COPD effects,
- offering a placebo,
- talking about public capital in macro terms.

These are supporting pieces, not the narrative spine.

### Is it a collection of results looking for a story?

No, but it occasionally reads that way because the paper has not fully committed to its best story. The true story is not “here are four outcomes from a DiD.” The story is:

1. Austerity cut budgets.
2. Service capacity did not collapse immediately.
3. That persistence reveals organizational capital.
4. COVID shutdowns then reveal the fragility of that capital.
5. Therefore the economics of austerity must account for depreciation of public capacity.

That should be the dominant arc from title through conclusion.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?

“I’d lead with this: places that had built more public health capacity before austerity kept delivering substantially more successful smoking quits for four to five years after budgets were cut, suggesting that public service capacity survives disinvestment longer than spending does.”

That is the dinner-party fact.

### Would people lean in?

Some would. Especially economists interested in public finance, health, or state capacity. But many would only lean in if the paper makes the broader point quickly. If presented as “a paper on English stop-smoking services,” a large share of economists will reach for their phones. If presented as “evidence that public organizational capital depreciates slowly under budget cuts and then collapses under shutdown,” they will lean in.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Probably one of these:

- “Is this really about organizational capital, or just about richer/high-need places protecting smoking services differently?”
- “Does this persistence show up in any downstream outcomes?”
- “How general is this beyond smoking cessation?”
- “Can you distinguish durable human capital from political protection or demand?”

Those are exactly the questions the framing needs to anticipate.

### If findings are modest or null

The null on smoking prevalence is actually potentially interesting, but only as a **disciplinary point**: downstream health outcomes are often too contaminated by convergence and secular trends to identify service-capacity effects. The paper does make this case somewhat well. Still, the null should not be oversold as a main contribution; it is a cautionary lesson, not the headline.

That one change would improve the title, introduction, contribution, literature positioning, and perceived ambition all at once.
