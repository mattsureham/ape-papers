## 1. Idea Fidelity

The paper only partially pursues the original idea. The manifest proposed studying whether the two-quarter ASP update lag causally affects **physician substitution between brand and generic Part B drugs**, using generic entry dates, ASP reimbursement, and some measure of market price to isolate a temporary profit shock. The submitted paper instead documents a **mechanical pattern in Medicare payment limits** around inferred generic entry and then converts that pattern into an estimated fiscal cost. That is a different and substantially narrower question.

Three departures are especially important. First, the paper does not study prescribing substitution or utilization behavior at all, despite the title and motivation repeatedly invoking physician incentives. Second, the key identification in the manifest relied on actual generic entry timing and heterogeneity in the reimbursement-minus-market-price “windfall”; the paper instead infers entry from a large payment drop in the ASP data itself, which makes the main result close to a restatement of the reimbursement rule. Third, although the manifest listed Orange Book dates and spending/claims data as central ingredients, those sources are only lightly used here and not integrated into the identification strategy in a serious way. In short, the paper preserves the institutional setting and broad motivation, but it does not deliver the original causal question.

## 2. Summary

This paper documents that Medicare Part B payment limits fall sharply roughly when generic competition is incorporated into the ASP formula, and interprets the two preceding quarters as a “lag windfall” generated by the statutory two-quarter update delay. Using CMS ASP files, the paper estimates that this lag implies roughly \$169 million per year in temporary overpayment.

The institutional point is potentially useful, but in its current form the paper does not provide convincing evidence on the causal effect of the policy on provider behavior, and even the fiscal-cost exercise rests on event definitions and assumptions that need much stronger validation.

## 3. Essential Points

1. **The paper does not identify a causal effect of the policy on economically meaningful behavior.**  
   The current evidence shows that payment limits decline when the formula updates; that is largely mechanical. But the paper’s motivation, title, and policy significance hinge on the claim that the lag “subsidizes brand drugs” and may delay generic adoption. No evidence on brand/generic prescribing shares, claims composition, or utilization response is presented. As written, the paper should either (i) be reframed transparently as a descriptive/accounting paper about delayed price pass-through in administered prices, or (ii) add direct evidence on provider behavior using claims or drug-level brand/generic utilization data.

2. **The event definition is endogenous and risks circularity.**  
   Generic entry is defined as the first quarter with a payment-limit drop greater than 20 percent, and the main finding is then that payment limits are elevated just before that drop and fall at event time 0. Because the event is detected from the outcome series itself, the event study does not by itself establish that the pre-period corresponds to post-entry/pre-adjustment quarters rather than other pricing dynamics, code changes, shortages, rebasing, or product mix shifts. The paper needs serious validation against independent entry dates (Orange Book, biosimilar launch data, manufacturer announcements, HCPCS coding changes), and it should report match rates, timing distributions, and false-positive analyses.

3. **The aggregate cost estimate is too weakly founded for an AER: Insights-style causal claim.**  
   The \$169 million figure uses average annual units, assumes the event truly corresponds to generic entry plus two quarters, and compares lag-window payments to a post-adjustment “equilibrium” that may itself reflect evolving market shares, code consolidation, or dose-unit changes. The paper needs a much more transparent decomposition, quarter-specific utilization where possible, sensitivity to alternative equilibrium windows, and careful exclusion/handling of HCPCS code changes and biosimilars. Without that, the headline cost estimate feels too fragile.

## 4. Suggestions

The paper has a potentially publishable core, but it needs to become much sharper about what is identified and what is not.

First, I strongly recommend **reframing the contribution** unless the authors can add behavior. Right now the introduction and title promise more than the evidence supports. “Subsidizes brand drugs” suggests an effect on brand prescribing relative to generics; the paper instead shows delayed adjustment in administered reimbursement. A more accurate framing would be that Medicare’s ASP update lag delays the pass-through of generic price declines into reimbursement limits and creates temporary overpayment. That is still interesting, especially because the lag is rule-based and actionable, but it is a different contribution from identifying distorted prescribing.

Second, the paper would benefit from a **clean institutional mapping of event time to real generic entry time**. The manifest’s original idea was strongest precisely because the lag is statutory and exact. But to exploit that feature, the paper needs actual entry dates, not just inferred breakpoints from the reimbursement series. At minimum, the authors should:
- Match the detected events to Orange Book generic approvals for small molecules.
- For biologics/biosimilars, use FDA Purple Book or biosimilar launch data rather than Orange Book.
- Show a histogram of the distance between approval/launch dates and the inferred event quarter.
- Report how many of the 235 events are validated, how many are ambiguous, and how many are clearly false positives.

If validated timing is noisy but usable, the paper could estimate an event study centered on actual entry date and show that payment limits do not move for two quarters and then fall when the ASP formula catches up. That would be much more compelling than the current design.

Third, I encourage the authors to **separate small-molecule generics from biosimilars** throughout. These markets differ in pricing dynamics, coding practices, provider substitution, and launch timing. A pooled analysis may obscure very different mechanisms. Biosimilars often have slower uptake, distinct HCPCS coding issues, and smaller initial discounts; small-molecule injectables may exhibit more immediate price competition. A clean heterogeneity analysis by product type could both validate the mechanism and improve policy relevance.

Fourth, there is substantial room to improve the **data section and sample construction**, which currently raises red flags. For example:
- The number of HCPCS codes per quarter, total drug-quarter observations, and event counts should be reconciled carefully.
- The sample period is 26 quarters, not the 40 quarters in the original manifest; this affects the plausibility of 235 “entry events.”
- Some reported summary statistics look extreme (mean payment limit \$1,353, median \$15.6, SD \$22,196), suggesting the influence of very high-cost codes or unit-definition issues. The paper should explain dosage units, multi-source codes, and any winsorization/trimming decisions.
- The placebo table reports \(N=0\) for placebo observations, which is plainly an error and undermines confidence in execution.
- The ordering of quartiles in the heterogeneity table (Q4, Q3, Q1, Q2) also looks careless.

For a short empirical paper, these details matter disproportionately because the identification is already narrow.

Fifth, the paper should do more to rule out **alternative reasons for large payment-limit changes**. Potential confounds include HCPCS code splits/mergers, introduction of separate billing codes for generics or biosimilars, manufacturer reporting changes, shortages, or other administrative changes. I would like to see:
- Exclusion of quarters surrounding HCPCS code changes.
- Manual validation of the top 20 highest-cost events.
- Event-study graphs for a few canonical drugs with known generic entry dates.
- A table categorizing detected events by likely mechanism: generic entry, biosimilar entry, code change, unknown.

Sixth, the authors should consider whether the more informative outcome is not the normalized payment limit but the **implied reimbursement spread** relative to a benchmark price. The manifest proposed constructing a treatment intensity measure like ASP payment minus market price. The paper currently never observes market price, so the “windfall” is proxied by reimbursement before versus after adjustment. That is understandable, but weaker. If the authors can incorporate a pricing benchmark—NADAC, wholesale acquisition data, SSR Health, Red Book, or even product-specific post-entry transaction prices—they could estimate a much closer analogue to provider margin. Even a limited sample with high-quality benchmarks would materially strengthen the argument.

Seventh, if the authors wish to keep the paper behavioral in spirit, they need at least **some evidence on utilization or substitution**. Several possibilities:
- Medicare Part B drug spending files may permit code-level beneficiaries/claims before and after entry, though not always brand/generic shares.
- For some drugs, brand and generic or reference product and biosimilar have distinct HCPCS codes; then the authors could study shifts in claims shares.
- A narrower sample with separable codes may be better than a broad but purely mechanical exercise.
- Alternatively, use 100% claims or other utilization datasets if available, even for a smaller validation sample.

Even one convincing figure showing slower-than-expected switching during the lag window would dramatically change the paper’s contribution.

Eighth, the aggregate-cost exercise should be **more explicit and more conservative**. I suggest:
- Report both lower-bound and upper-bound estimates.
- Use quarter-specific units if possible rather than 2019–2023 averages.
- Show sensitivity to the choice of post-adjustment comparison window (e.g., quarters 1–2, 2–4, 0–3).
- Clarify whether utilization is for the same HCPCS code before and after entry.
- Distinguish “delayed pass-through savings” from “overpayment relative to contemporaneous acquisition cost,” since the latter requires stronger assumptions.

Ninth, the event-study specification itself could be improved. Because the event is staggered and the panel is short, I would prefer:
- A clear stacked event-study design.
- Quarter fixed effects, unless there is a reason not to include them.
- Presentation of raw mean paths, not just regression coefficients.
- More pre-period coefficients and a direct test for pre-trends.
- A discussion of why event quarter \(-3\) is the omitted category when the normalization already uses \(-4\) to \(-1\).

As written, the coefficients are somewhat difficult to interpret because the outcome is normalized by a pre-event mean that already includes the alleged lag window.

Finally, the paper would benefit from a **more modest discussion of policy implications**. If the evidence remains mechanical, the takeaway should be that faster ASP updating would accelerate the pass-through of generic price declines into Medicare reimbursement, not that the paper has shown physicians are strategically exploiting margins or that generic adoption is delayed. Those are plausible hypotheses, but not established here.

Overall, I think there is an interesting short paper somewhere in this project, but the current draft overclaims relative to what the evidence can support. The path forward is either to narrow the claims and sharpen the accounting exercise, or to add direct evidence on entry timing and provider substitution so the paper can genuinely speak to causal effects of reimbursement design on behavior.
