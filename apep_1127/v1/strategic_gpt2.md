## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple, policy-relevant question: when Oklahoma regulators forced wastewater injection wells to cut volumes during the state’s induced-earthquake crisis, did regulation actually stop the earthquakes, or did the earthquakes fade only because oil prices crashed and drilling slowed? A busy economist should care because the answer speaks to whether direct environmental regulation can successfully shut down a rapidly emerging externality in a major industry—and because similar risks now matter for the Permian Basin and carbon storage.

The paper does articulate a recognizable pitch in the first two paragraphs, and in fact the basic ingredients are there: a dramatic fact, a clear question, and an important policy distinction. But the current opening is too “inside the paper”: it leans quickly into geoscience detail and then into the oil-price confound, rather than foregrounding the broader economics question. It also overstates certainty and starts signaling methods before fully establishing why this is an economics problem of first-order importance.

### What the first two paragraphs should say instead

Oklahoma’s earthquake surge created a rare natural experiment in environmental regulation. In just a few years, wastewater injection from oil and gas production turned a low-seismicity state into the most earthquake-active part of the continental U.S.; regulators then imposed well-specific volume limits in stages across affected areas. The central question is whether those directives actually caused the subsequent collapse in seismicity, or whether the crisis would have faded anyway as oil prices fell and drilling slowed.

That distinction matters well beyond Oklahoma. If market conditions alone solved the problem, then direct regulation played little role and future seismicity risks in places like the Permian Basin or at carbon storage sites may disappear on their own when activity ebbs. If, instead, targeted volume restrictions were decisive, then this is strong evidence that a regulator can rapidly contain a technologically generated environmental externality when the physical mechanism is understood.

That is the pitch the paper should have. Then the third paragraph can say: “I answer this using staggered county exposure to Oklahoma Corporation Commission directives.”

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:**  
The paper claims to provide the first causal econometric evidence that targeted limits on wastewater injection volumes sharply reduced induced seismicity in Oklahoma, implying that direct regulation—not just the oil price collapse—ended the earthquake crisis.

That is a real contribution. The problem is that the paper does not consistently decide what its main contribution is.

At different moments, it presents itself as:
1. the first evaluation of induced-seismicity regulation;
2. a demonstration of staggered-DiD bias;
3. a paper about “regulatory ratchets” in environmental policy;
4. a policy template for CCS and the Permian.

Those are related, but not the same. Right now the introduction stacks them rather than prioritizing them.

### Is it clearly differentiated from the closest papers?

Only partially.

The paper does distinguish itself from geoscience papers that document mechanisms and descriptive declines in seismicity. That differentiation is potentially strong: “the geophysics literature shows injection causes earthquakes; this paper asks whether regulation reduced them.” Good.

But relative to economics papers, the differentiation is much fuzzier. The paper invokes the staggered-DiD literature and generic environmental-regulation papers, but it does not crisply show why this is more than “an application of modern DiD to a new environmental setting.” If I read only the introduction, I could imagine a colleague summarizing it as: “It’s a DiD paper on injection-well regulation and earthquakes.” That is not enough for AER positioning.

### World question or literature-gap question?

Mostly the former, which is good. The strongest framing is: **Can a regulator stop an environmental crisis generated by energy production when market forces are moving at the same time?** That is a world question.

But the paper periodically retreats into weaker literature-gap framing: “first econometric estimate,” “applied example of TWFE bias,” “framework for emerging injection-related seismicity.” Those are supporting claims, not the main event.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new?

Yes, but only if they are charitable. The best version is:

> “It shows that Oklahoma’s injection-well directives, not just the oil-price collapse, appear to have caused the collapse in induced earthquakes, which matters for how we think about direct environmental regulation of industrial externalities.”

The weaker likely summary is:

> “Another staggered-DiD paper, but on earthquakes.”

The paper is too close to triggering the second reaction.

### What would make the contribution bigger?

Most importantly: **make the paper about regulation of industrial externalities under known physical mechanisms**, not about an exotic Oklahoma episode.

Concretely, the contribution would feel bigger if the paper:
- tied earthquake reductions more directly to an economically meaningful welfare margin: felt earthquakes, housing risk, insurance exposure, infrastructure risk, or emergency response, rather than raw counts alone;
- more clearly connected regulatory design to mechanism: not just “regulation worked,” but “well-specific quantity restrictions worked because they directly constrained the state variable generating harm”;
- framed Oklahoma as a prototype for a broader class of environmental policies where the regulator targets the physical input causing damage, rather than as a standalone fossil-fuel case;
- downplayed the methodological “gotcha” about TWFE sign flips unless the paper truly wants to be a methods-application paper.

If they could add one substantive dimension, I would want **salience or welfare-relevant seismic outcomes**: felt quakes, higher magnitude thresholds, property damage proxies, or population exposure. “Regulation reduced earthquake counts” is interesting; “regulation sharply reduced socially salient earthquake risk” is more AER.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Before this paper, we knew that wastewater injection caused Oklahoma’s earthquake surge, and we knew that earthquakes later fell dramatically. But it was unclear whether that decline reflected regulation or merely the collapse in oil prices and associated drilling activity.

### Tension
The same period featured both regulatory intervention and a giant commodity shock. So the puzzle is whether government action deserves credit, or whether the regulator arrived after the market had already begun solving the problem.

### Resolution
The paper’s headline claim is that staggered injection-well directives materially reduced earthquake activity, with effects growing over time and persisting after oil prices recovered.

### Implications
If true, the implication is that direct, mechanism-targeted environmental regulation can rapidly suppress a technologically generated externality—and that similar regulatory design may be useful in other injection-risk settings.

That is a solid narrative arc. The issue is not absence of a story; it is that the paper muddies its own story by interleaving multiple competing arcs.

Right now there are at least three stories:
1. **Policy story:** regulation ended the earthquake crisis.
2. **Methods story:** TWFE can reverse sign in staggered adoption.
3. **Concept story:** regulation can create a ratchet that outlasts market conditions.

The paper needs one primary story and maybe one secondary story. For AER purposes, the primary story should be the policy story. The methods point is a useful subplot, not the movie.

Also, the phrase “ended Oklahoma’s earthquake crisis” in the title is punchy but risky. It sounds triumphalist, almost advocacy-like, and invites skeptical pushback. A title slightly less certain and more analytical would help the paper feel more serious.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party?
I would say: “Oklahoma’s induced-earthquake crisis did not just disappear when oil prices fell—the paper argues that targeted injection limits by regulators were the main reason seismicity collapsed.”

That is a good dinner-party fact. Economists will lean in, because it combines energy, regulation, natural hazards, and a clean policy question.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
Lean in, at least initially. The topic is unusual and vivid. Earthquakes are intrinsically attention-grabbing, and the market-versus-regulation distinction is economically meaningful.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Probably one of these:
- “How do you separate regulation from the oil-price crash?”
- “Did the policy reduce socially meaningful earthquake risk, or just low-magnitude counts?”
- “Is this a special case because the regulator directly controlled the physical source of harm?”

That last question is the one the paper should embrace. It is the bridge to broader economics.

### If the findings are modest or null?
The paper is not selling a null. It is selling a large effect. The issue is instead whether the effect is framed in a way that feels economically important rather than technically estimated. Right now the emphasis on IHS units and standardized effects is not helping. Nobody at dinner cares about “-1.29 sigma in IHS units.” They care that a regulator may have shut down a major environmental hazard.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

This is not primarily an identification problem in editorial terms; it is mainly a **framing and ambition** problem.

### What is the gap?

**1. Framing problem.**  
The science is potentially interesting, but the paper has not yet decided whether it is:
- an environmental regulation paper,
- an energy externalities paper,
- a methods-application paper,
- or a geophysics-adjacent policy evaluation.

For AER, it needs to be clearly the first two.

**2. Scope problem.**  
The outcome is currently narrow: earthquake counts. That is an important first-order measure, but by itself it still feels a bit specialized. The paper would feel much bigger if it connected the seismicity result to exposure, salience, or damages.

**3. Novelty problem, but only partially.**  
The setting is novel to economics, but novelty of setting alone is not enough. The broader conceptual takeaway must be sharper: when does command-and-control regulation work unusually well?

**4. Ambition problem.**  
The paper is competent and energetic, but somewhat safe in intellectual ambition. It shows a policy worked in one setting. AER wants a clearer general lesson about policy design, externalities, and the conditions under which regulation outperforms market adjustment.

### Single most impactful advice

**Reframe the paper around a broader economics question: when can direct, mechanism-targeted regulation rapidly eliminate an industrial environmental externality, beyond what markets would do on their own?**

Everything else should serve that. The Oklahoma earthquake episode is the evidence; it should not be the entirety of the contribution.

If the author can make only one change, it should be to **rewrite the introduction and discussion so the paper is unmistakably about the economics of regulation under a known physical mechanism, not about a clever DiD applied to a quirky natural-hazard setting.**
