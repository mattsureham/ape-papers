**Idea Fidelity**

The paper follows much of the original manifest. It exploits the 2016 Integration Act’s district-level variation in the suspension of the Vorrangprüfung and applies a difference-in-differences strategy using Eurostat’s NUTS-3 data, as promised. It also discusses the institutional details (Bavaria’s unemployment threshold, NRW and MV exceptions) and considers threats such as spatial sorting via the Wohnsitzauflage. However, several elements from the manifest are missing or underdeveloped: there is no explicit RDD around Bavaria’s 3.6% unemployment cutoff, no county-level refugee employment data from BA Statistik or IAB-BAMF-SOEP to examine direct refugee outcomes, and the placebo leveraging recognized refugees is not implemented. **Summary**

The paper studies Germany’s 2016 suspension of the Vorrangprüfung (labor market priority check) and finds, using a TWFE DiD on 400 NUTS-3 regions from 2012–2021, that lifting the check in 133 districts had essentially zero effect on regional employment or GDP. Event studies, robustness checks, and placebo tests all support the null, leading the author to conclude that the policy was a “compliance illusion” with administrative costs but no labor-market impact.

**Essential Points**

1. **Identification relies heavily on between-region DiD with heterogeneous treatment assignment.** The control group (23 districts) is highly selective—Bavarian high-unemployment districts, Ruhr-area cities, and all of MV—so DiD comparisons with the 133 treated districts are subject to serious concerns about differential trends and cross-region heterogeneity. The paper presents limited evidence beyond the aggregate pre-trend table to defend parallel trends. With a small control group concentrated in structurally different regions, the baseline estimate may absorb omitted factors (e.g., western vs. eastern growth dynamics, city vs. countryside), making the null result ambiguous. The authors must either strengthen the empirical strategy (e.g., more rigorous matching, synthetic controls, or weighted DiD) or demonstrate convincingly that these control regions indeed provide a credible counterfactual.

2. **Granular outcomes and treatment variation are underused.** The manifest emphasizes BA Statistik data (monthly employment, refugee-specific employment) and the within-Bavaria RDD at the 3.6% unemployment cutoff to isolate causal effects. The paper instead relies entirely on aggregate NUTS-3 employment and GDP, with no direct refugee outcome or RD evidence. Without leveraging refugee-specific data or the quasi-experimental cutoff, the study cannot claim to identify the mechanism most plausibly affected. The authors should either incorporate these richer data (or explain why they are infeasible) or acknowledge that the current analysis primarily captures average regional effects and not the intended treatment-on-the-treated cohort.

3. **Interpretation of the null lacks nuance.** A zero effect on aggregate regional employment could arise from offsetting increases in refugee employment and declines in native employment, or from the policy simply not binding due to other constraints (labor demand, skill mismatch). The paper briefly mentions these alternatives but does not explore them empirically. In particular, the manifest’s built-in placebo—recognized refugees who were exempt from the check—would help test whether outcomes for the directly affected group differed from an unaffected group within the same district. Without such checks, the conclusion that the priority check was a compliance illusion remains speculative.

**Suggestions**

- **Improve the control group construction.** Given the small number of maintained districts, consider refining the comparison set via propensity score weighting, entropy balancing, or matching on pre-treatment trends and structural characteristics (population density, industry mix, wage levels). If the policy variation occurs at the Agenturbezirk level, could the analysis be rerun at that level (156 units) rather than aggregating to NUTS-3? This would reduce heterogeneity between treated and control units and allow a more precise mapping of treatment status. Alternatively, exclude Bavaria and use NRW/MV only, or focus solely on the within-Bavaria discontinuity where treatment is mechanically determined by unemployment. If external validity permits, present separate results for each subgroup rather than pooling.

- **Leverage more targeted outcomes.** The manifest mentions BA Statistik’s monthly data and refugee employment measures, which offer two advantages: (1) they track the refugee group directly affected by the policy, and (2) they provide higher-frequency variation for robustness. Even if restricted access prevents the use of microdata, aggregate series (e.g., employment subject to social insurance by nationality from BA Statistik) should be feasible. At a minimum, show that native and refugee employment trends move together; ideally, estimate effects on refugee employment or labor market participation. Similarly, the within-Bavaria RDD around the 3.6% unemployment threshold can isolate causal effects within one state. Presenting RDD estimates (with proper bandwidth selection, covariate balance, and sensitivity to functional form) would bolster the credibility of the null result by relying on quasi-random variation.

- **Expand placebo and heterogeneity analyses.** To test the mechanism, implement the suggested placebo using recognized refugees: they were not subject to the priority check but could relocate, so there should be no treatment effect on this group even if general employment shifted. Similarly, explore heterogeneity by industry (e.g., sectors where refugees typically entered—hospitality, construction) if data allow, since the policy is most relevant for sectors with lower qualification requirements. Examine whether job vacancy rates, welfare receipt, or social insurance contributions changed in treated districts; these intermediate outcomes can help demonstrate that the policy removal had no economic ripple effects rather than being offset by other forces. Also, consider dynamic effects with more granular timing—if the check mainly affected early integration, there may be lags that annual data smooth over; monthly BA data would be helpful here.

- **Clarify the interpretation of null results.** The current conclusion—that the priority check was merely a compliance cost—is plausible but needs stronger backing. Discuss alternative explanations more explicitly and, where possible, rule them out empirically. For example, if refugee employment increased but displaced natives, there would likely be observable changes in wage growth or unemployment by nationality, which could be tested. If the check was already ineffective because employers bypassed it, provide anecdotal or administrative evidence (e.g., processing time data, caseworker reports). Without such evidence, avoid overclaiming that the policy had “no effect” on refugees; instead, frame the result as “no detectable aggregate effect” and note the remaining uncertainty about distributional impacts.

- **Provide more transparency on data and implementation.** Since the paper relies on publicly available Eurostat data, consider including key mapping tables (Agenturbezirk to NUTS-3) in the appendix. Clarify how jobs in counties overlapping multiple districts are assigned, how standard errors are computed (e.g., are there few treated units within each cluster?), and whether any regions with missing data were dropped. These details help readers assess sample composition and robustness.

- **Reference concurrent literature more precisely.** The manifest refers to studies on residence obligations and other integration policies. In the paper, cite specific findings (e.g., “Brücker et al. (2016) found no displacement effects in Germany”) and explain how this study complements them. If the null finding contrasts with other policy changes (like access to work permits in Sweden), explicitly discuss why the context might differ (administrative check vs. outright work restriction). This situates the paper within the broader literature and strengthens the argument for policy relevance.

Overall, the paper tackles an important question with an interesting natural experiment, but it needs a more focused empirical strategy (preferably exploiting the within-state discontinuity and refugee-specific outcomes) and clearer interpretation of the null result before it can be considered for publication.
