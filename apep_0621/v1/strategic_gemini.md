## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
This paper uses 9.6 million linked census records to evaluate the population-level impact of America’s first cash-transfer program, Mothers’ Pensions (1911–1919), on intergenerational mobility. While previous research shows large gains for the narrow subset of children who actually received benefits, this paper finds that the program was too small (reaching <2% of families) to move the needle on state-level occupational outcomes. It is a "precise null" that provides a sobering boundary condition for the aggregate power of social safety nets.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this clearly. It effectively sets up a "clash of the titans" framing between a known micro-level success (Aizer et al., 2016) and a newly documented macro-level null. The first two paragraphs successfully establish the high stakes: the origins of the American welfare state.

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
The contribution is a precisely estimated zero population-level ITT for a landmark social policy, reconciling large micro-effects with aggregate insignificance via low program take-up.

- **Differentiation:** It is well-differentiated. While Aizer et al. (2016) is the shadow this paper stands in, the shift from *recipient* outcomes to *population* outcomes is a distinct and meaningful step.
- **Framing:** It is framed as a question about the WORLD (Does the welfare state actually drive geographic variation in mobility?). This is much stronger than "I add a control variable to a previous study."
- **Clarity:** A smart economist would immediately understand the "Reach vs. Impact" story.
- **Making it bigger:** To make this even bigger, the author should lean harder into the "Chetty-style" geography. If Mothers' Pensions didn't cause the variation in mobility across the US, the paper should more aggressively test what *did* (e.g., were the "progressive" traits of early-adopters actually the real drivers of the Great Gatsby Curve in 1940?).

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
The paper sits at the intersection of Economic History (Skocpol; Moehling), Labor/Mobility (Chetty; Abramitzky), and Applied Micro/Safety Net (Hoynes; Aizer).

- **Strategy:** The paper currently "respectfully reconciles" with Aizer et al. This is the right move. An "attack" would feel petty given the different estimands; a "reconciliation" feels like scientific progress.
- **Narrow vs. Broad:** The audience is currently a bit narrow (history buffs). To broaden it, it needs to speak to modern "scaling" literatures—the idea that many "proven" interventions fail at the population level because of general equilibrium effects or, as here, simple lack of scale.
- **Missing Conversations:** The paper should connect to the "Scaling" literature (e.g., Al-Ubaydli, List, et al.) regarding why RCT/LATE results don't always translate to policy success.

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC
- **Setup:** The Progressive Era birthed the first cash transfers to save children from orphanages.
- **Tension:** We know these transfers helped the kids who got them, but did they actually change the "opportunity map" of America?
- **Resolution:** No. The raw data suggests they did, but this is a "Progressive State" illusion. Once you control for the fact that early-adopting states were already "better," the effect vanishes.
- **Implications:** Policy design matters as much as policy existence. A 2% reach is effectively a 0% impact on the social fabric.

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong. It’s a "detective story" where the initial suspect (Mothers' Pensions) is exonerated by a 1910 baseline F-test.

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
At a dinner party, you lead with: *"We’ve been crediting the early welfare state for lifting children out of poverty, but it actually reached so few people that it didn't change the economic trajectory of a single US state."*

People lean in because it challenges the "triumph of the welfare state" narrative. The follow-up is: *"So what actually made the difference?"* The paper needs to be ready with a slightly better answer to that follow-up to be a true AER blockbuster.

---

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
- **Front-loading:** The "Spurious Results" section (Section 5.1/5.2) is the heart of the paper. It should be even more prominent. 
- **The "Mechanical" DiD:** The discussion of Table 2 (child labor) is a bit clunky because the "working" variable changes definition as kids age. This needs a cleaner presentation or a move to the appendix to avoid distracting from the much cleaner SEI results in Table 3.
- **The Reconciliation:** The back-of-the-envelope math in Section 6 is actually the most important part of the paper. Consider moving a summary of that math earlier to show that the null isn't "bad luck," but "mathematical inevitability."

---

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The gap is **Ambition**. Currently, it feels like a very high-quality "Comment" on Aizer et al. (2016). To be a standalone AER article, it needs to broaden its scope from "This one program didn't work at scale" to "What the 1920-1940 census links tell us about the *true* drivers of the birth of American mobility."

**The single most impactful piece of advice:**
Don't just kill the "Mothers' Pension" hypothesis; use your 9.6 million observations to crown the winner. If it wasn't pensions, was it the 30% difference in child labor laws? Was it the school attendance rates? Transform this from a "Null result about a policy" into a "Foundational result about what actually created the American geography of opportunity."
