**Idea Fidelity**

The paper largely follows the manifest idea: it leverages the staggered state adoption of mothers’ pension laws (1911–1919) together with IPUMS MLP linked census panels to estimate population-level ITT effects. The data sources and the core research question—whether state-level expansion of the first welfare program influenced children’s long-run occupational attainment—match the manifest. However, the empirical implementation diverges in important ways. The manifest emphasized individual-level Callaway‐Sant’Anna-style DiD with individual controls, extensive robustness (e.g., migration controls, generosity heterogeneity), and mechanisms (schooling, child labor) drawn from panel-level variation. The paper presented here instead analyzes state-aggregated outcomes using simple cross-sectional OLS and two-period (1910–1920) DiDs, without exploiting individual-level panel variation beyond reporting aggregated summary statistics. It also omits the promised migration controls, heterogeneity by benefit generosity, and more refined identification checks based on the extensive linked panels. Thus, while the high-level idea is pursued, several promised elements of the identification strategy and empirical richness are missing.

**Summary**

The author uses IPUMS MLP data on 9.6 million children to compare adult outcomes in states that adopted mothers’ pension laws early versus late/never, finding that the large raw correlation between early adoption and higher 1940 SEI disappears once baseline state characteristics are controlled for. A short-run 1910–1920 DiD on child labor and school attendance likewise vanishes once Southern states are excluded, leading to the conclusion that the population-level ITT of mothers’ pensions on intergenerational mobility is effectively zero, even though prior work found a substantial LATE for recipients.

**Essential Points**

1. **Identification is fundamentally undermined by aggregation and seasonal omission of individual-level variation.** The core idea was to exploit individual-level linked panels and staggered adoption to identify a DiD estimate. Instead, the paper regresses state-level means on a single exposure variable (years of exposure) across 49 states in 1940, with only four baseline controls. This cross-sectional design cannot credibly separate the effect of mothers’ pensions from unobserved, time-varying state-specific shocks or persistent omitted variables (e.g., industrialization, policy bundles, Great Migration). Without a panel structure (state × cohort) or more granular variation, the claim that controlling for share white/male/age/schooling “kills” the effect confounds the treatment with the very pre-trends the research intends to isolate. The short-run DiD collapses to a placebo once the South is excluded, but the DiD itself is flawed because the “treated by 1919” binary is collinear with a host of other reforms, and the pre-trend test (placebo on age 15–18) simply shows the design is invalid. This identification strategy is not credible.

2. **The empirical specification does not match the research question.** The manuscript promises to estimate the ITT of mothers’ pension adoption using staggered timing across states, with multiple cohorts and individual-level controls, but ultimately reports only one cross-sectional regression (SEI on MP exposure) and a short-run two-period DiD on state aggregates. There is no use of panel differencing (e.g., child birth cohorts before/after adoption within states) nor treatment timing variation beyond 1911–1919. The paper does not model exposure as dynamic (e.g., by birth year relative to adoption), nor does it exploit the linked panel to control for family-level characteristics, track movers, or study heterogeneity. As a result, it cannot credibly estimate the ITT it claims to target.

3. **Controls are insufficient to address the key confounders, and placebos do not rescue the design.** The paper argues that adding four state-level controls “kills” the effect, but these controls themselves are economically endogenous to the same underlying progressiveness factor driving both adoption and outcomes. Without richer controls (industrial composition, urbanization, state GDP, education expenditure, child labor laws) or more credible quasi-experimental variation (e.g., synthetic controls, event-study across cohorts), the zero estimate is driven by over-controlling for proxies of the very selection it tries to address. In addition, the stated robustness tests (non-South sample, placebo age groups) mostly demonstrate the unreliability of the aggregate design rather than supporting causal inference. Given these issues, the paper should not proceed without a fundamentally different empirical strategy.

**Suggestions**

- **Re-establish the identification strategy using individual-level panel variation.** Instead of cross-sectional averages, construct a panel of state × birth cohort (e.g., birth years 1911–1920) or state × age in 1940, and exploit the staggered adoption with an event-study or Callaway–Sant’Anna-style DiD at the individual level. Use the linked MLP data to assign children to birth cohorts relative to adoption and to include covariates (child sex, race, nativity, parents’ SEI, urban/rural status). This will also allow you to control flexibly for state and cohort fixed effects and to study dynamics (do cohorts born right after adoption experience different effects than those born much later?). These richer specifications would better align with the manifest and the research question.

- **Address selection by leveraging variation in exposure intensity and by controlling for contemporaneous policies.** The raw OLS of SEI on years of MP exposure is confounded by everything that made Northern/Progressive states early adopters. Add controls for state industrial structure, urbanization, percent with compulsory schooling laws, child labor law stringency, public school spending, and the progressiveness of other social policies. Better yet, include state-specific trends or, even more transparently, restrict to a subsample of states with similar pre-trends/use synthetic controls. Including a falsification test using outcomes plausibly unaffected by MP laws (e.g., male/female employment in professions not reliant on parental support) would help highlight whether identification holds.

- **Re-examine the short-run DiD with appropriate pre-trend checks and a clear treatment definition.** The current DiD uses a “treatment by 1919” indicator, but the policy takes effect at different times across states. Instead, define treatment as exposure for a child depending on whether he/she lived in the state after adoption (with age-specific exposure). Estimate difference-in-differences with multiple periods or state-by-year fixed effects, and plot event studies to visually inspect pre-treatment trends. Control for time-varying confounders, include linear county-level controls if possible, and incorporate migration status to address misclassification. Without these improvements, the DiD cannot establish a causal effect—it merely restates the selection problem.

- **Leverage heterogeneity and mechanism tests promised in the manifest.** The idea paper mentioned analyses by benefit generosity, race, sex, marital status, and migration, as well as mechanisms like schooling and child labor. Implementing these would not only add depth but also help assess whether the null effect persists across subgroups and channels. For example, compare SEI effects in states with higher generosity or stronger enforcement; examine whether children of single mothers (if identifiable) benefited differently; and link the MLP panel to 1940 education/income data to test intermediate outcomes. These exercises can both strengthen identification (if heterogeneity aligns with plausibility) and clarify why the population effect differs from the applicant-level LATE.

- **Clarify the connection to Aizer et al. (2016) with consistent notation and more nuanced interpretation.** The paper positions itself as complementary but would benefit from more precise translation between the LATE and ITT. Rather than just noting that 2% coverage implies a tiny average effect, consider using the paper’s estimates to simulate the expected aggregate effect under the Aizer estimates: e.g., if recipients' SEI increases by X and take-up is Y, what ITT would you predict? Does the observed 95% CI cover that predicted range? This exercise would strengthen the reconciliation and show that the null is not just a failure to detect something but is quantitatively consistent with limited reach.

- **Reword the narrative around “null result” and discuss statistical power / precision.** The paper repeatedly frames the null as a precise zero effect, but the current specification is arguably underpowered due to 49 states. Instead, report confidence intervals, discuss minimum detectable effects, and consider whether the design can actually rule out small but policy-relevant effects. This is especially important if the policy goal is to evaluate population-level impacts—being explicit about the magnitude of effects that can be excluded will help readers understand the policy relevance of the null.

- **Augment robustness with richer placebo and falsification exercises.** The current placebo (age 15–18) mostly shows the DiD design is invalid. Instead, construct placebo treatments with fake adoption years or apply the same DiD to outcomes that should not respond to mothers’ pensions (e.g., male adult SEI in 1910). Also, consider permutation tests where adoption years are shuffled across states to see how the estimated distribution behaves under the null. These diagnostics can help reassure readers that, once the design is properly specified, the null is not a mechanical artifact.

- **Be transparent about data linkage limitations and address potential biases.** The paper mentions differential linkage rates but does not quantify them. Provide linkage rates by state and discuss whether differential attrition could bias state means (e.g., if Northern states’ linkage success is higher, SEI might be upward biased). If possible, apply sample weights or inverse-probability weighting to adjust for differential linkages, or at least show that aggregated outcomes are robust to reasonable assumptions about linkage heterogeneity.

- **Consider converting the manuscript into a longer-format paper** if incorporation of these suggestions makes the project more extensive. The current AER: Insights-type length might be insufficient to thoroughly address the identified gaps, especially given the complexity of the historical context and data. Even if the eventual goal remains a short format, ensure each section—data, identification, results, mechanisms—has enough detail to convince skeptical referees.

By re-centering the empirical strategy on individual-level linked panels, fleshing out the promised heterogeneity/mechanism tests, and bolstering the identification with richer controls and falsifications, the paper can more convincingly estimate the population-level ITT of mothers’ pensions.
