## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper studies whether housing markets price anticipated climate regulation. Using France’s 2021 reform of energy performance certificates—which turned a disclosure label into a legally consequential rating tied to future rental bans—the paper asks whether poorly rated homes became “stranded assets” before the bans actually took effect. A busy economist should care because this is a concrete, household-balance-sheet version of transition risk: not fossil fuel reserves, but ordinary homes.

Does the paper itself articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs? Not quite. The current opening is informed and serious, but it takes too long to decide whether the paper is about housing green premia, stranded assets, decomposition of information vs regulation, or French institutional detail. The first two paragraphs should lead with the big question—do markets capitalize anticipated regulation into household wealth?—and only then explain why France is unusually useful.

**The pitch the paper should have:**

> Climate policy can destroy asset values before any regulation is enforced. This paper asks whether housing markets capitalize anticipated energy-efficiency regulation into house prices, turning inefficient homes into stranded household assets.  
>  
> I study France’s 2021 reform of energy performance certificates, which converted a familiar disclosure label into a legally binding rating tied to progressive rental bans for the worst-performing homes. Using nearly 815,000 property transactions linked to energy certificates, I show that homes exposed to the new regime sold at a discount after the reform and that assessments bunch just below key regulatory thresholds, consistent with strategic gaming once labels acquire regulatory bite.

That is the paper’s best story. The introduction currently dilutes it by promising a decomposition it does not convincingly deliver.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:**  
The paper shows that when an energy-efficiency label becomes a regulatory trigger rather than mere information, housing markets discount affected properties and market participants manipulate ratings around the threshold.

This is a better contribution statement than the paper’s current “decompose informational and regulatory channels” framing, because the decomposition is ultimately inconclusive. Right now the paper oversells the clean separation and then repeatedly walks it back. That creates disappointment rather than momentum.

### Is the contribution clearly differentiated from the closest papers?
Only partially. The paper knows the EPC capitalization literature and the newer stranded-assets/climate-risk literature, but the differentiation is not yet crisp enough. A reader could still summarize it as “another housing DiD around an energy-label reform.” The paper needs to insist more clearly on what is unusual here:

1. **A label-to-regulation transition within the same market**  
   Most of the literature studies labels as information or regulation as already embedded. This setting speaks to the change in meaning of a label.

2. **Household-level stranded assets**  
   That is broader and potentially more important than “French DPE effects.”

3. **Gaming induced by regulatory salience**  
   The manipulation result may be the most original fact in the paper.

### World question or literature-gap question?
The paper is at its strongest when framed as a **world question**:  
**What happens to asset prices and measurement behavior when governments convert disclosure into enforceable climate regulation?**

It is weaker when framed as:  
“the literature has not cleanly separated information and regulation in one market.”

That second statement is true, but it sounds incremental and method-driven. AER papers usually win by changing how economists think about an economic phenomenon, not by filling a missing cell in a design matrix.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the intro?
At present: not cleanly. They might say, “It studies France’s EPC reform and finds a modest brown discount, but the mechanism is mixed.” That is not enough.

What you want them to say is:  
**“It shows that climate transition risk gets capitalized into ordinary residential property once labels become legal constraints—and that regulation also changes the measurement system because people game the threshold.”**

That version is memorable.

### What would make the contribution bigger?
Most importantly: **make the manipulation result central, not ancillary.** Right now the paper treats bunching as both a nuisance and a side result. Strategically, it may be the paper’s sharpest and most generalizable finding.

Other ways to make it bigger:
- **Emphasize distributional implications** more credibly: who owns these assets, where are they located, and why does this matter for household wealth and political economy?
- **Show market-level reallocation**, not just price levels: do affected properties take longer to sell, shift toward cash buyers/investors, or concentrate in certain geographies? Even simple descriptive reallocations would enlarge the paper’s scope.
- **Lean into the institutional lesson**: disclosure and regulation are not separate policies; turning one into the other changes both prices and the integrity of the measurement regime.
- If feasible, **tie the paper more explicitly to the EU-wide policy rollout**, making France a leading indicator rather than a one-country curiosity.

- not distant climate exposure,
- but highly salient transition risk in household wealth.

Relative to the manipulation literature, the paper should **import that insight into environmental/housing economics**:

Paradoxically, both.

- **Too broadly** in claiming to settle information vs regulation decomposition.

The right audience is broader than French housing economists but narrower than “all of environmental economics.” The best positioning is at the intersection of:
1. climate transition risk,
2. household finance / wealth,

It could do more with:
- **household finance / wealth effects** from regulation
- **political economy of climate policy** and distributional incidence

### Is it having the right conversation?
Mostly, but not optimally. The paper is currently having a conversation with the EPC/green premium literature. That is fine, but it caps the ambition. The more powerful conversation is:

**How does climate transition policy get capitalized into household asset values, and how does making a metric legally consequential distort the metric itself?**

That is a more interesting AER conversation.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Before this paper, we know that greener buildings often sell for more. But we know less about whether markets price the regulatory consequences attached to energy labels, as opposed to the information labels convey about costs and quality.

### Tension
France changed the meaning of the label: same general object, new legal consequences. This creates an opportunity to ask whether the market responds when disclosure becomes regulation. But the same reform also changed the measurement methodology, so the clean decomposition the paper wants is hard.

### Resolution
The paper finds a modest post-reform discount for the worst-rated homes and strong bunching below the key threshold after the reform, suggesting that the reform affected both prices and reporting behavior. The attempts to isolate the pure regulatory channel are suggestive but not decisive.

### Implications
Climate regulation can create residential stranded assets before formal enforcement, and threshold-based regulation may induce gaming of the very labels policymakers rely on.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
It has the raw ingredients, but the narrative is currently unstable because it promises one story and delivers another.

The paper **says** the story is:
- “I decompose information from regulation.”

But the paper **actually delivers**:
- “When labels acquire regulatory bite, prices move somewhat and measurement gets gamed.”

That second story is coherent and interesting. The first story is not resolved by the evidence presented.

So yes, at present it feels somewhat like **a collection of results looking for a story**:
- a DiD showing a modest discount,
- an inconclusive decomposition,
- a strong manipulation result,
- heterogeneity that cuts against the pure-regulation story.

The story it should be telling is:

> France turned an informational label into a regulatory boundary. Housing markets responded in two ways: affected homes became less valuable, and assessments became strategically manipulated. The key lesson is that climate transition policy works through both capitalization and gaming.

That narrative unifies the price results and the density results instead of treating one as “main” and the other as an awkward sidebar.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?
I would not lead with “2.0% discount” alone. That sounds modest and paper-specific.

I would lead with:

**“When France turned home energy labels into legally binding rental restrictions, the worst-rated homes were discounted and energy assessments immediately bunched just below the regulatory cutoff.”**

That gets attention because it contains both market pricing and behavioral distortion.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
With the current framing, some would reach for their phones: “another EPC capitalization paper, 2% effect, inconclusive mechanism.”  
With the better framing, many would lean in: “ah, this is about transition risk in household wealth and gaming of regulatory thresholds.”

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Likely:
- “Is the discount really regulation, or just energy-cost salience / methodology changes?”
- “Who bears the incidence—small landlords, owner-occupiers, rural households?”
- “Does this induce renovation or just relabeling?”
- “Will this generalize across Europe?”

Those are productive questions. But note that the first one is the danger point: the paper currently invites it too directly and cannot fully answer it.

### If findings are modest, is that okay?
Yes, but only if the paper makes the right case. A 2% price effect on a massive household asset base is economically meaningful, but **2% is not by itself an AER-level headline**. The paper needs either:
- a stronger conceptual point,
- a broader implication,
- or a second, sharper fact.

The manipulation result can serve as that sharper fact. Without it, the paper risks feeling like a competent but modest policy evaluation.

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

5. **Tighten the conceptual framework.**

   The paper is already unusually candid about its limitations. That is admirable, but from an editorial perspective the main text currently spends too much energy on why its own designs are imperfect. Let referees probe that. The main text should foreground the facts.

   The conclusion now largely summarizes. It should instead crystallize the paper’s core lesson:
   - transition risk affects household assets,
   - regulation changes behavior at thresholds,
   - climate policy design must account for gaming and incidence.

Not enough. The reader has to traverse a lot of setup before realizing that the paper’s strongest differentiator may be the bunching/manipulation evidence.

### Are there buried results that should be in the main results?
Yes: the **manipulation evidence** is not buried exactly, but it is underexploited. It belongs conceptually alongside the main price result as a primary outcome of regulation.

### Is the conclusion adding value?
Some, but not enough. It needs to stop sounding like a thorough dissertation wrap-up and start sounding like a paper with one or two lasting messages.

Mostly a **framing problem**, with some **ambition/scope problem**.

- **Scope problem:** there is not yet enough around incidence, reallocation, or policy design to make the paper feel field-defining.

### What is the gap between current form and something that would excite the top 10 people in this field?
Top people in environmental/urban/public economics would likely say:

- a cleaner and more surprising central fact,
- a clearer general lesson beyond France,

That pair of claims is bigger than “there is a 2% brown discount in France.”

**Reframe the paper around the transformation of labels into regulatory thresholds—and make the dual response of price capitalization and threshold gaming the central contribution, while dropping the claim of cleanly decomposing informational from regulatory channels.**

That one change would make the paper more honest, more coherent, and more interesting.
