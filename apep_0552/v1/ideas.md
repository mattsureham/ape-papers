# Research Ideas

## Idea 1: Who Captures a Tax Cut? Property Price Capitalization of France's Taxe d'Habitation Abolition
**Policy:** France's abolition of the taxe d'habitation (TH) on primary residences — the country's largest local tax reform in decades, worth approximately EUR 23 billion annually. The reform was phased: 80% of households (by income) received a 30% reduction in 2018, 65% in 2019, and full exemption in 2020. The remaining 20% received 30%/65%/100% reductions in 2021/2022/2023. Secondary residences remain subject to TH throughout.
**Outcome:** Transaction-level property prices from DVF (Demandes de Valeurs Foncieres), covering the universe of property sales in France from 2014 onward. Approximately 3-4 million transactions per year, with exact sale price, date, commune, property type, and surface area.
**Identification:** Continuous-treatment DiD exploiting cross-commune variation in pre-reform TH rates. Before abolition, communes set TH rates independently, creating enormous variation (ranging from roughly 10% to over 50% of the cadastral rental value). Communes with higher pre-reform TH rates experienced larger effective tax cuts per euro of property value — a natural dose-response design. The staggered 80/20 income phasing provides additional temporal variation. The built-in placebo: secondary/vacation homes, which remained subject to TH throughout, should show no differential price response by pre-reform TH rate. Triple-difference specification: (high- vs low-TH communes) x (primary vs secondary residences) x (pre vs post reform). Clustering at the commune level (35,000+ communes). Modern staggered DiD estimators (Callaway-Sant'Anna or de Chaisemartin-D'Haultfoeuille) with continuous treatment dose.
**Why it's novel:** The TH abolition is one of the largest natural experiments in local tax incidence in any OECD country. Unlike US property tax studies that examine marginal rate changes, France eliminated an entire tax category — providing a unique opportunity to estimate full capitalization. The occupant-based tax (paid by residents, not owners) creates a distinctive incidence question: does eliminating a tax on occupants flow to property owners via higher prices? The primary-vs-secondary-residence placebo is clean because the policy explicitly maintained TH on secondary homes. Transaction-level DVF data avoids the aggregation bias that plagues commune-level price indices.
**Feasibility check:** Confirmed: DVF is open data on data.gouv.fr (bulk CSV, 2014-present). Commune-level TH rates are published by DGFiP in the REI (Recensement des Elements d'Imposition) files, available on data.gouv.fr. The 80/20 income split is observed at the departement level in aggregate tax statistics. Secondary residences are flagged in DVF via the "nature de mutation" and property-type fields. 35,000+ communes provide massive cross-sectional variation. Pre-period (2014-2017) gives 4 years before treatment onset.

## Idea 2: Stranded by the Label? Energy Performance Bans and Property Values in France
**Policy:** France's 2021 DPE (Diagnostic de Performance Energetique) reform made energy performance certificates legally binding and announced progressive rental bans: G-rated properties banned from new leases starting January 2025, F-rated from 2028, E-rated from 2034. The reform also changed the DPE calculation methodology, reclassifying many properties. Prior to 2021, DPE was purely informational.
**Outcome:** Transaction-level property prices from DVF, linked to the ADEME DPE database (publicly available on data.gouv.fr) containing millions of energy performance certificates with ratings (A through G), energy consumption estimates, and property addresses.
**Identification:** Two complementary strategies. (1) DiD around the July 2021 reform date: compare price trends of properties near the G/F boundary vs. properties near safer boundaries (C/D, B/C), before and after the reform announcement. Properties near the G/F boundary face imminent rental prohibition; those at C/D face no regulatory threat. (2) RDD at the DPE letter-grade boundaries: exploit the sharp energy-consumption thresholds that determine letter grades (e.g., G if >450 kWh/m2/year before 2021, >420 after). Placebo: owner-occupied properties face no rental ban and should show smaller (information-only) effects at the same thresholds.
**Why it's novel:** Distinct from the UK EPC literature (including top-ranked APEP paper apep_0477) because France provides a unique before/after on the REGULATORY channel. UK EPCs always combined information with regulation (MEES). France's 2021 reform holds the informational channel approximately constant while adding a regulatory rental ban — directly decomposing information vs. regulation effects. The progressive ban timeline (G→F→E) creates a natural gradient of regulatory urgency.
**Feasibility check:** DVF is open data. The ADEME DPE database is publicly available on data.gouv.fr with property addresses, enabling linkage to DVF via geocoding or address matching. The match rate may be imperfect (not all transacted properties have a DPE certificate), but millions of matches should be feasible. The 2021 reform date is sharp and well-documented. Risk: address matching quality between DVF and DPE databases may introduce noise. Mitigation: restrict to apartment transactions in large cities where geocoding is more precise.

## Idea 3: Does Mandatory Social Housing Change Neighborhoods? Evidence from France's Loi SRU
**Policy:** France's Loi SRU (Solidarite et Renouvellement Urbain, 2000) requires communes above 3,500 inhabitants (1,500 in Ile-de-France) to maintain at least 20% social housing stock (raised to 25% in 2013 by the Duflot law). Non-complying communes face annual financial penalties proportional to their shortfall. As of 2020, approximately 1,100 communes were subject to the law, with about 700 falling short of the quota.
**Outcome:** Transaction-level property prices from DVF for private-market (non-social) housing. Secondary outcomes: transaction volume, property-type composition (apartments vs houses), buyer profiles.
**Identification:** RDD around the population threshold: communes just above 3,500 (or 1,500 in IDF) are subject to the SRU obligation; those just below are exempt. The treatment is the social housing construction mandate and associated financial penalties. The running variable (commune population) is measured in the census and determines obligation status mechanically. Supplement with a dose-response DiD: among obligated communes, exploit cross-sectional variation in distance-to-target (communes at 5% social housing face much stronger pressure than those at 18%). The 2013 increase from 20% to 25% provides additional temporal variation.
**Why it's novel:** The Loi SRU is one of the most aggressive social-mixing policies in any OECD country, yet rigorous causal estimates of its effect on private-market property values are scarce. The population threshold creates a clean identification opportunity rarely available in housing policy. The question addresses a first-order policy debate: does mandating social housing in affluent suburbs reduce nearby private property values (the "NIMBY" concern) or increase them through improved public services and diversity?
**Feasibility check:** DVF provides transaction-level prices with commune identifiers. Commune populations are available from INSEE census data. SRU compliance reports (listing obligated communes and their social housing percentages) are published triennially by the Ministry of Housing. The 3,500-inhabitant threshold is institutional and non-manipulable (census-based). Potential concern: communes near the threshold may be small with few transactions. Mitigation: pool transactions over multiple years and restrict to communes within a reasonable bandwidth of the threshold. IDF-specific 1,500 threshold provides a second sample.
