## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper investigates how the transition of energy efficiency labels from purely "informational" to "regulatory" (triggering rental bans) affects property values. Using the 2021 French reform as a natural experiment, it asks whether markets capitalize the risk of "stranded residential assets" before regulations take effect. This matters because it provides a rare empirical test of how policy-induced obsolescence redistributes wealth in the world's largest asset class: residential real estate.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this well in paragraph 2, specifically identifying the "stranded assets" concept. However, the first two paragraphs are a bit heavy on French institutional context. 
**The pitch it should have:** 
"Does the threat of future regulatory bans on inefficient assets cause immediate wealth losses for owners? This paper uses a landmark French reform—which converted energy labels from buyer information into a legal basis for rental prohibitions—to identify the capitalization of regulatory risk. I find that the mere anticipation of these bans created a 2% price discount and triggered immediate strategic manipulation of energy assessments, suggesting that 'regulatory' salience exerts a pressure that 'informational' salience does not."

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution:** The paper identifies the specific price impact of *anticipatory regulatory risk* in the housing market, separate from the informational value of energy efficiency.

- **Differentiated?** Yes. It moves beyond the "green premium" literature (Eichholtz et al.; Brounen & Kok) by exploiting a regime shift from information-only to regulation-linked labels.
- **World vs. Literature:** It starts with a world problem (decarbonizing buildings via bans) but pivots quickly to a literature gap (the identification challenge of bundling info and regulation).
- **Explanation to a Colleague:** A smart economist would say: "It's the paper that shows people start dumping or 'gaming' energy-inefficient houses the moment the government sets a date to ban them from the rental market."
- **Bigger Contribution:** The contribution would be massive if the author could definitively prove the "why." Currently, the triple-diff and RDD results are "imprecise" or "suggestive." Strengthening the link between the *rental share* of a neighborhood and the *magnitude* of the discount is the key to moving this from a "maybe" to a "must-read."

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

- **Neighbors:** *Sallee (2024)* on stranded assets; *Allcott & Greenstone (2012)* on the efficiency gap; *Giglio et al. (2021)* on climate risk pricing; and *Garel & Cheng (2025)* (the direct competitor).
- **Positioning:** The paper needs to be more aggressive against *Garel & Cheng (2025)*. It should position itself as the "mechanism" paper that uses threshold and spatial variation to decompose what they only see as an aggregate trend.
- **Narrow vs. Broad:** Currently a bit niche (French housing). It needs to speak louder to the **Climate Finance** literature. This isn't just a housing paper; it’s a paper about how "policy risk" is priced into long-lived physical assets.
- **Missing Conversations:** It should connect to the literature on **"Gaming and Incentives"** (e.g., Figlio; Jacob & Levitt). The McCrary test result showing bunching *only* after the reform is actually one of the most "AER-worthy" facts here—it proves that regulation changed the data-generating process itself.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

- **Setup:** Energy labels exist, but we don't know if they affect prices because of "bills" or "laws."
- **Tension:** France suddenly "switched on" the laws. If it's just about bills, nothing should change. If it's about laws, the "G" properties should crash. 
- **Resolution:** Prices fell by 2% and—critically—the ratings became "gamed" (bunching).
- **Implications:** Policy dates create stranded assets and "diagnostic fraud" long before the first inspector knocks on a door.

**Evaluate:** The arc is slightly broken because the "Resolution" is fuzzy. The author admits the triple-diff (the "smoking gun" for the rental ban mechanism) fails to find a significant result. This leaves the paper as a "collection of results" (DiD, RDD, McCrary, Heterogeneity) that don't quite point to the same culprit.

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

- **Lead Fact:** "The moment France announced a future rental ban on 'G' rated homes, the market didn't just discount them; people started bribing or bullying inspectors to rate them as 'F' instead."
- **Reaction:** Lean in. The bunching result (Figure 4) is the hook. The 2% price drop is modest, but the *behavioral* response to the threshold is fascinating.
- **Follow-up:** "If the discount is only 2%, but the renovation cost is 10%, why aren't prices falling more? Is the market under-reacting or are subsidies over-compensating?"

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

- **Front-load the McCrary:** The manipulation result is cleaner and more novel than the "imprecise" RDD price results. Move it earlier to establish that the market *definitely* cares about the regulatory threshold.
- **Appendix:** The "Multi-cutoff RDD" and "DiDisc" (Tables 5 & 6) are currently yielding nulls/insignificance. They take up too much mental real estate in the main text. Move the "failed" decompositions to the appendix and focus the main narrative on the **robust DiD** and the **robust Manipulation**.

---

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The paper is currently a "very good" field journal paper (e.g., *Journal of Urban Economics*). To make it AER, it needs to solve the **Ambition Problem.**

The author finds a 2% discount but then shows that the discount is *larger* for houses/rural areas (where rental bans matter *less*). This contradicts the "rental ban" story. To get into the AER, the author needs to reconcile this: Is it because rural owners are more liquidity-constrained? Is it because the "salience" of the reform was higher than the "legality" of the ban? 

**Single Most Impactful Advice:** Focus the paper on the **strategic response to regulatory thresholds.** The fact that the *data itself* became gamed (the bunching) is a high-level contribution to the economics of regulation. Use the price results as a secondary, supporting fact rather than the primary (and slightly shaky) pillar.
