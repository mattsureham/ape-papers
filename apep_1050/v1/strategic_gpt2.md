## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether annual ownership-tax incentives—rather than the much-studied one-time purchase subsidies—meaningfully increase electric-vehicle adoption. Using variation across Swiss cantons, it argues that modest tax discounts do essentially nothing, while full tax exemptions increase EV adoption, implying that for this policy instrument the relevant margin may be a behavioral threshold rather than a smooth price response.

A busy economist should care because the paper is trying to say something broader than “Swiss cantons did X”: namely, that recurring taxes may affect durable-goods adoption differently from upfront subsidies, and that policy design may be sharply nonlinear.

**Does the paper articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Pretty well, but not optimally. The current opening gets to the punchline fast, which is good, but it frames the contribution as a Swiss policy puzzle before fully establishing the larger economic question. The first two paragraphs should make clearer that the paper is about **how consumers respond to recurring ownership costs versus upfront incentives**, with EVs as the application.

**The pitch the paper should have:**

> Governments spend billions subsidizing electric vehicles, but almost all evidence focuses on one-time purchase incentives. This paper asks whether recurring annual ownership taxes matter for EV adoption—and whether consumers respond smoothly to such incentives or only when the tax is eliminated altogether.  
>  
> I study Swiss cantons, which adopted EV-specific annual motor-vehicle tax exemptions of different intensities at different times. The key finding is stark: partial discounts have little detectable effect, while full exemptions increase EV adoption. The broader lesson is that for salient recurring charges, policy effects may be highly nonlinear: reducing a tax is not the same as eliminating it.

That is a much stronger AER-style opening because it starts with the economic question, not the institutional setting.

Those are world questions.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Governments use fiscal tools to speed EV adoption. Most evidence concerns purchase subsidies, but many policies instead alter recurring ownership costs, such as annual taxes and registration fees.

### Tension
It is unclear whether consumers respond meaningfully to these recurring incentives, and whether they respond proportionally to incentive size or only when the tax bill disappears entirely.

### Resolution
In Swiss cantons, average effects of “any incentive” are small, but decomposing by intensity suggests a sharp threshold: full exemptions raise EV adoption; partial discounts do not.

### Implications
Policy design may be nonlinear. If true, governments cannot assume that a half-sized tax break produces half the effect of a full one. More broadly, recurring taxes may operate through salience and categorical “zero versus nonzero” perceptions.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
**Serviceable, but not fully coherent.**

The paper has the ingredients of a strong narrative, but it currently feels like **two partially competing stories**:

1. A null-average-effect paper on annual tax incentives.
2. A threshold-effect paper where full exemptions matter.

The second is clearly the better story. The first is just setup. The paper should stop pretending the null average effect is coequal with the threshold result. The actual paper is about **masked heterogeneity and policy thresholds**.

There is also an unresolved narrative issue: the triple-difference results are presented as “strongly significant,” yet the main reduced-form average effects are null and the intensity decomposition is the central result. This creates storyline clutter. Even if technically reconcilable, strategically it muddies the message. For editorial positioning, that is costly.

**The story it should be telling:**  
“Annual EV tax incentives do not work on average because most such incentives are too small to matter. Only complete tax elimination changes behavior. The lesson is that recurring ownership-tax incentives may display threshold effects that standard average-treatment summaries conceal.”

That is clean and memorable.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?
“Swiss cantons that merely discounted annual EV taxes saw no detectable adoption gains; cantons that eliminated the tax entirely did.”

That is the fact.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
Some would lean in—especially environmental economists, public-finance economists, and behavioral economists—because “full exemption works, partial doesn’t” is a neat and portable result.

But the current version risks phone-reaching because the result is still one step too application-bound. “Swiss municipality-level EV registrations” by itself is not enough. The audience leans in only if they believe the paper is about **nonlinear responses to recurring costs**.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Immediately:  
**“Why?”**

That is both the paper’s strength and its problem. The result is provocative, but the mechanism remains speculative. In AER terms, the paper can survive without fully nailing mechanism if the reduced-form fact is important enough. But then the framing has to elevate the fact into a broader class of economic behavior. Right now it only partly does that.

A second follow-up would be:  
**“Is this really about salience/zero-price effects, or just that small annual tax changes are economically trivial?”**

That is exactly the conceptual challenge the paper needs to confront in framing, even if referees later judge the empirical support.

### If the findings are modest, is the modesty itself interesting?
Yes, but only if framed correctly. “Average effect is near zero” is not interesting on its own. What is interesting is:
- modest policy tweaks often fail,
- aggregation can hide threshold effects,
- recurring incentives may not behave like scaled versions of upfront subsidies.

That is not a failed experiment; it is potentially a useful policy-design lesson.
