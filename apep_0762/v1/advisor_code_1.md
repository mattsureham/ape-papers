**Summary**

This paper evaluates whether Dark Sky Community certifications—which require enforceable outdoor lighting ordinances—affect residential property values. Using Zillow ZHVI data for treated zip codes and matched controls, the author applies the Callaway–Sant’Anna estimator for staggered adoption and finds a roughly 4–7% decline in home values following certification, with heterogeneity across cohorts (e.g., Flagstaff positive, most others negative). The paper interprets this “missing amenity premium” as evidence that the compliance costs of darkness regulation outweigh any amenity value homebuyers currently place on reduced night lighting.

**Essential Points**

1. **Interpretation of Identification and Controls:** The paper relies on never-treated controls and tight matching on pre-treatment levels, but the core identifying assumption is parallel trends conditional on observables. The event study shows mostly flat pre-trends, but the late negative pre-trend at $t=-3$ and the sharp drop at $t=0$ call for a more thorough discussion of timing concerns. In particular, the certification process itself may unfold over several years, so contemporaneous timing (year of certification) may not cleanly capture when ordinances begin affecting home values. Clarify when ordinances take effect relative to certification and explore models that allow treatment to begin with ordinance adoption rather than certification, or instrument via certification status with a first stage on ordinance enforcement. Without this, attributing the observed post-period decline to the amenity value of darkness versus compliance costs remains speculative.

2. **Selection into Treatment and Control Validity:** Matching solely on levels of ZHVI risks omitting other confounders correlated with both adoption timing and housing trends (e.g., tourism dependence, local policy priorities, demographic change). The paper reports excluding tourism hubs and using not-yet-treated controls, but there is little discussion of other possible confounders (e.g., pre-existing environmental branding, economic shocks) nor of whether the propensity score or outcome adjustments include time-varying covariates. More substantively, the skipped first stage (the manifest promised VIIRS radiance reductions) weakens the case that certification meaningfully reduces light pollution. If certification is merely symbolic or if enforcement is lax, the observed housing decline could reflect regulatory uncertainty rather than light pollution mitigation. Provide additional checks on the validity of the treatment (supply-side costs, measured reductions) or argue why certification should affect housing even without measurable light reductions.

3. **Statistical Precision and General Magnitude:** The paper emphasizes a statistically marginal overall effect (p≈0.10 TWFE, 0.541 randomization inference p-value), yet frames the finding as a robust negative capitalization story. This misalignment between statistical and substantive claims risks overstatement. Either tighten the inference (e.g., more robust standard errors, better clustering) or temper the interpretation, emphasizing that while the point estimate is consistently negative, the precision is limited and may not allow strong policy conclusions about negative capitalization. Otherwise readers may doubt whether the “missing amenity premium” is an empirical regularity rather than an artifact of noise.

**Suggestions**

1. **Clarify Treatment Timing and Mechanism:** Provide detailed information on when specific ordinance provisions take effect relative to certification (e.g., is the ordinance passed a year before, does it allow 5–10 years for retrofit?). This affects how treatment should be coded and interpreted. If compliance costs arise gradually, consider modeling treatment as a distributed effect that ramps up post-certification, or include dynamic compliance covariates (numbers of fixtures replaced, municipal spending, etc.) if available. If the certification is largely symbolic until enforcement, this should be acknowledged and, if possible, linked to observed compliance behavior.

2. **Strengthen the Control Group Strategy:** Because never-treated zip codes may systematically differ from treated ones (e.g., in urbanization, tourism, or environmental branding), consider implementing synthetic control or entropy balancing approaches that incorporate additional covariates beyond log home values (population density, median income, tourism employment share, housing stock age, etc.) at baseline. The manifest mentioned augmented SCM for early adopters; incorporating that analysis—even if only for a subset (Flagstaff, Borrego Springs)—would reinforce the credibility of the staggered DiD and help persuade readers that the average negative effect is not driven by unobserved heterogeneity.

3. **Reintroduce or Proxy the Light Pollution Reduction Outcome:** The original idea included a first stage on VIIRS radiance reduction, which also strengthens the interpretation that certification meaningfully changes the amenity being valued. If direct VIIRS measures are not feasible, consider alternative proxies (municipal lighting budgets, fixture inventories, energy consumption) or qualitative information (audit findings, compliance reports) to show the ordinance is not merely a regulatory tax. Coupling the treatment with a measurable reduction in radiance would also allow for an instrumental variables approach (certification → reduced radiance → home values) that could better isolate the amenity channel.

4. **Deeper Discussion of Heterogeneity and External Validity:** The heterogeneity table is intriguing but underexplored. For example, middle cohorts (2012–2017) show very large negative effects—these may coincide with municipalities that adopted ordinances during or after the Great Recession, when housing markets were depressed. Consider regressions of cohort-level ATT on observables (tourism share, baseline brightness, economic trends). Also, discuss whether the negative effects persist in fast-growing vs. stagnating communities or whether they vary by urban-rural classification. This would help readers understand whether the missing amenity premium is a general feature of darkness regulation or a context-specific phenomenon.

5. **Refine Robustness and Inference Interpretation:** The randomization inference p-value of 0.541 suggests the point estimate is within the distribution of placebos; this should be interpreted carefully. Instead of presenting it as confirming the effect, frame it as evidence that precision is limited and that the effect should be interpreted with caution. Additionally, consider reporting confidence intervals for aggregated ATTs (aggregate effect ± CI) and using recently recommended standard-error adjustments for staggered DiD with few clusters (e.g., wild cluster bootstrap, permutation-based inference). Providing more transparent uncertainty measures will align interpretation with the data’s limits.

6. **Address Alternative Explanations:** The negative effect could stem from regulation-related uncertainty, increased municipal taxes (to fund audits), or the elimination of certain amenities (nighttime walkability). Incorporate placebo tests (e.g., other ordinances enacted without light restrictions) or alternative dependent variables (e.g., sales volumes, new construction permits) to distinguish between income effects and preference shifts. If possible, use qualitative data (municipal minutes, local news) to substantiate mechanisms: are residents opposing ordinances because they reduce safety, or are the costs concentrated on commercial properties?

7. **Consider Welfare Implications More Nuanced:** The conclusion asserts that darkness lacks amenity value and thus regulation must be justified on non-market grounds. Yet even if housing markets do not capture value, residents might still derive non-market benefits (sleep, ecology). Discuss whether the measured negative capitalization might understate welfare benefits (due to misperception) or reflect distributional issues (existing homeowners vs. newcomers). A short section connecting the housing market finding to broader welfare (e.g., linking compliance costs to energy savings or health outcomes) would help readers interpret policy relevance without overgeneralizing from the housing price effect.

8. **Provide Additional Transparency in Appendix:** The appendices are helpful but could include more methodological detail—specify the propensity score model (covariates, functional form), smoothing choices for ZHVI, and the exact matching algorithm. Include figures of the event study (with confidence bands) and balance tables for covariates to help readers assess pre-treatment similarities. If some control zip codes are matched to multiple treated zips, note how clustering or weighting is handled in estimation.

By addressing these points, the paper would strengthen its identification claims, clarify the substantive interpretation of the housing effect, and better situate its contribution within the environmental amenity literature.
