**Summary**

The paper evaluates whether the 2016 benefit cap reduction increased local-authority temporary accommodation (TA) burdens, exploiting variation in cap intensity (capped households per 1,000 residents) across 278 English LAs in a continuous-treatment TWFE framework. The naïve specification shows a positive but statistically weak effect, yet an event study and placebo tests document strong, monotonic pre-trends: high-intensity areas were already experiencing faster TA growth well before the reform, undermining the parallel-trends assumption. The authors interpret this as evidence that cross-area cap exposure simply proxies for pre-existing housing-market pressures, cautioning that aggregate designs cannot credibly attribute increased homelessness to the cap.

**Essential Points**

1. **Time-Variant Treatment Intensity and Measurement Date:** The treatment intensity (cap exposure) is measured only once (May 2017) and treated as time-invariant. Yet the reform was rolled out across jobcentres between November 2016 and January 2017, and the number of capped households likely evolved during the pre- and post-years (e.g., as households moved, employment status changed, or UC rollout progressed). This raises concerns that the “CapIntensity” measure may be endogenous to the TA trend it is trying to explain, and that the timing of the measurement could be capturing post-reform adjustments rather than exogenous exposure. The authors need to justify why a single post-reform snapshot accurately proxies pre-reform cap distributions, or—preferably—construct a dynamic exposure measure (e.g., using earlier DWP caseloads or housing benefit data) that predates the reform.

2. **Parallel Trends Failure Without Remedial Strategies:** The central conclusion is that parallel trends fail, but the paper stops at rejection without attempting remedies. Given the richness of the data (multiple pre-periods, regional controls, placebo regressions), stronger identification could still be pursued—e.g., by conditioning on trends in housing market pressure (rent inflation, social-housing stock, supply/demand proxies), employing synthetic control/matching on pre-trends, or leveraging the discrete rollout (Nov 2016–Jan 2017) to instrument for exposure. Without such strategies, the paper risks being read solely as a cautionary tale, rather than providing actionable evidence on whether, how much, and under what conditions the cap affected homelessness. The authors should more explicitly explore alternative identification approaches before concluding that the question “cannot be answered.”

3. **Scale of Outcome and Mechanism Linkage:** The paper’s primary outcome is TA households per 1,000 population, which mixes periods before and after the reform, but the intervention occurs close to the end of the 2016/17 financial year. The lack of finer temporal resolution (monthly/quarterly data) makes it difficult to detect short-run spikes induced by the cap, especially if CA placements respond within months. Additionally, the proposed mechanism—capped households being displaced into homelessness—does not appear to be validated by any direct welfare or housing-payment data (e.g., rent shortfalls, eviction notices, claims on Discretionary Housing Payments). The authors should consider dissecting the mechanism using richer TA or Housing Benefit micro-data, or at least acknowledging the limitations of using aggregated stock counts measured annually.

**Suggestions**

1. **Construct Pre-Treatment Exposure Variation:** To solidify the identification, endeavor to recover earlier measures of cap exposure. The manifest mentions the DWP’s quarterly ODS downloads and benefit cap caseloads; these could be used to construct pre-reform snapshots (e.g., 2015/16) of capped households per LA. If those data are unavailable, consider proxies such as the share of households with Housing Benefit above certain thresholds or the distribution of LHA vs. actual rents. Having time-varying intensity would allow for leads/lags analysis without relying on a single 2017 measurement.

2. **Model Pre-Trends Directly:** The event study demonstrates a monotonic pre-trend, but it does not tell us whether the differential trend is linear, nonlinear, or driven by outliers (e.g., London boroughs). Consider estimating models that include LA-specific linear or quadratic time trends, interacted with observable characteristics (housing cost indices, population growth). Alternatively, implement the “pre-trend adjustment” approach (e.g., via the method in Borusyak and Jaravel, 2017), or use flexible splines in time to soak up long-run housing market pressure. Showing that the main result is stable to these adjustments would strengthen the claim.

3. **Explore Heterogeneity and Mechanism Indicators:** The manifest envisions testing fiscal displacement (DWP savings vs. LA costs). While the paper concludes no aggregate effect, it could still investigate whether certain subgroups (high rent areas, metropolitan districts, or LAs with worsening affordability) exhibit different patterns. Similarly, incorporate alternative outcomes—such as TA entries, homelessness applications, or rent-arrears statistics—to see if the benefit cap affected transitions rather than stocks. If only aggregate TA stocks are available, consider decomposing them into “hostels versus nightly-paid accommodation,” which might respond differently to sudden shocks.

4. **Clarify the Causal Parameter and Interpretation:** Currently, the paper highlights the failure of parallel trends, but it could do more to articulate what the coefficient would have meant had the assumption held. For example, present the implied effect on the average LA if the coefficient were causal, or compare the magnitude to the overall increase in TA. Then explain why that is implausible given the pre-trends and other evidence. This would help readers understand the policy relevance even in the absence of a clean causal estimate.

5. **Engage More with Alternative Approaches in Discussion:** The Discussion suggests that individual-level matched designs are preferable. To guide future research, the authors could be more explicit about how such an individual-level design could be operationalized for the housing outcome: what data would be needed, what comparison groups would be credible, and what assumptions would be required. This would turn the paper into a constructive roadmap rather than a warning.

6. **Transparency on Data and Extent of Variation:** Provide more descriptive statistics on TA trends by cap intensity deciles, including visualizations (e.g., plots of TA trajectories for high vs. low exposure LAs). This would help readers see the pre-trend issue concretely and evaluate whether the monotonic pattern is driven by a few LAs with extreme intensities. Also clarify how much of the variation comes from London versus the rest of England—is the exposure distribution bimodal? Could the event study be dominated by London boroughs? Reporting such heterogeneity would contextualize the results.

7. **Consider Alternative Identification Leveraging Policy Heterogeneity:** The 2016 reform had a differential cap for London (£23,000) versus the rest of England (£20,000). While the paper excludes London in one robustness check, it could exploit this discontinuity more formally (placing London boroughs as a higher threshold group) or compare adjacent LAs straddling the boundary to better separate housing market confounding from policy effects. Alternatively, exploit the rollout timing (November 2016–January 2017) by comparing LAs that were implemented earlier versus later if that information exists.

By addressing these points—especially constructing a time-varying treatment, modeling the pre-trends more flexibly, and exploring complementary outcomes—the paper can transform its cautionary conclusion into a more robust empirical assessment of the benefit cap’s housing implications.
