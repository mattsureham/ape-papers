## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether delays in implementing EU directives into national law have real economic costs. Its core claim is that when countries miss transposition deadlines, firms face a period of regulatory uncertainty that materially suppresses new business formation, especially in heavily regulated sectors.

A busy economist should care because this takes something usually treated as bureaucratic noncompliance in EU governance and reframes it as a distortion to market entry. If true, the paper turns “transposition deficits” from an administrative scorecard issue into a first-order economic consequence of incomplete integration.

**Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Mostly yes. The introduction is better than average: it gets quickly to the institutional setting, defines “regulatory limbo,” and links it to the uncertainty literature. But it still sounds a bit like “here is a new DiD setting for policy uncertainty” rather than “here is a hidden friction in European integration with broad implications for entry and market formation.” The first two paragraphs should lead more with the world question and less with the literature pedigree.

**What the first two paragraphs should say instead:**  
> The EU often legislates centrally but implements locally. When member states miss directive transposition deadlines, firms know that the regulatory regime is about to change but do not yet know the domestic rules they will actually face. This paper asks whether that implementation gap has real economic consequences by deterring firms from entering affected markets.  
>
> I show that it does. Linking directive-level transposition delays across EU member states to sector-level business demography data, I find that firm entry falls sharply during these periods of regulatory limbo, with the largest effects in sectors where compliance details matter most. The broader point is that incomplete implementation is not just a political or legal problem: it is an economic friction that weakens the gains from market integration.

That is the pitch the paper should have.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:**  
The paper’s contribution is to argue that delayed national implementation of EU directives creates a distinct form of regulatory uncertainty that reduces firm entry in affected sectors.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest papers?
Only partially. The paper distinguishes itself from:
1. the policy uncertainty literature,
2. the EU transposition/compliance literature,
3. the entrepreneurship/entry-regulation literature.

That triangulation is sensible, but the differentiation is still not sharp enough. Right now the contribution reads as: “apply uncertainty logic to a novel EU setting.” That is not yet the same as showing why this setting teaches us something new that those literatures could not already infer.

The paper needs to be much crisper on what is distinctive here:
- this is **implementation uncertainty rather than policy uncertainty**;
- the uncertainty arises **after legislation is known but before domestic legal form is fixed**;
- it is a friction in **multilevel governance**, not simply bad institutions or burdensome regulation;
- it directly speaks to the economic consequences of **incomplete integration**.

Those are stronger and more original claims than “another paper showing uncertainty reduces activity.”

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a literature gap?
It starts with a world question, which is good, but slips back into literature-gap language (“first causal evidence,” “contribute to three literatures”). The stronger framing is absolutely the world framing: **What happens to market entry when supranational law exists but domestic implementation lags?** That is an AER-style question. “No one has estimated this before” is not.

### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain what’s new?
A smart economist could probably say: “It’s a paper on EU directive delays causing entry suppression through regulatory uncertainty.” That is better than “another DiD paper about X,” but not by much. The paper currently risks being summarized as: **another reduced-form paper showing uncertainty lowers firm creation**.

To escape that, the introduction needs to insist that the object of study is not generic uncertainty but a very specific institutional wedge: **the implementation gap between central legislation and national legal force**. That wedge is potentially much more conceptually interesting.

### What would make this contribution bigger?
Three ways:

1. **Frame the paper as one about the economics of incomplete state capacity in integrated markets.**  
   Not “EU bureaucracy matters,” but “market integration depends not just on common rules, but on timely domestic implementation.”

2. **Push harder on market integration implications rather than just firm births.**  
   If there is any way to say something about cross-country convergence, sectoral dynamism, or whether delays blunt Single Market gains, that would substantially raise the ambition.

3. **Make the mechanism more conceptually distinctive.**  
   The most valuable version is not “uncertainty discourages entry,” but “uncertainty over domestic legal translation of supranational rules is a hidden tax on entry.” That is a larger idea.

A different outcome variable could also help. Firm births are fine, but if the paper could tie this to **market structure, concentration, cross-border entry, or post-entry performance**, the contribution would look much bigger.

That is the conversation that gives the paper a larger audience.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
The EU creates common rules through directives, but those rules only bite domestically when member states transpose them. Delays are common and usually treated as a political, legal, or governance problem.

### Tension
If firms care about the rules they will actually face, delayed implementation may create a period in which legislation exists but operative domestic law does not. That could deter entry—but the existing literature mostly studies why states delay, not what those delays do economically.

### Resolution
The paper finds that periods of delayed transposition are associated with substantially lower firm entry, especially in more regulated sectors.

### Implications
Implementation is economically consequential. Delayed transposition is not just administrative slippage; it is a real friction that may dilute the benefits of the Single Market and distort entrepreneurship.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
It has the ingredients of one, but the arc is not fully disciplined. Right now the paper partly reads like:
- interesting institutional setup,
- some reduced-form results,
- some heterogeneity,
- then a general discussion of uncertainty.

The story it **should** be telling is tighter:

1. **Europe legislates centrally but implements locally.**
2. **That creates a predictable implementation gap.**
3. **The gap is a form of legal uncertainty for firms.**
4. **That uncertainty suppresses entry.**
5. **Therefore, incomplete implementation is an internal market friction.**

That final step is the key. Without it, the paper is a collection of results around a nice setting. With it, the paper has a real narrative identity.

One thing that weakens the arc is that the paper itself flags substantial tension between empirical approaches deep in the robustness section. I will avoid methodological evaluation, but strategically this is deadly because it causes the reader to stop believing the narrative just when the story should be consolidating. If that issue remains, it needs to be reframed transparently and much earlier, or the paper will feel like a result in search of a stable interpretation.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party?
“EU directives that miss domestic implementation deadlines appear to reduce new firm formation in affected sectors by about one-fifth.”

That is a good opening fact. It is concrete and somewhat surprising.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
They would lean in initially. The phrase “regulatory limbo” is intuitive, and economists like hearing that something dismissed as bureaucratic delay may matter for real activity.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Almost certainly:  
**“Is this really about uncertainty from delayed implementation, or are late-transposing countries just places where entry is weak for other reasons?”**

That is the natural follow-up because the finding is large and the setting is politically loaded. Again, referees can adjudicate strategy; from an editorial perspective, the point is that the paper must preempt this question in its framing and not overstate certainty.

### If findings are modest or null
They are not modest. The issue is actually the reverse: the effect is large enough that readers will ask whether the paper is discovering a profound friction or overinterpreting a narrow setup.

The paper should therefore make the “so what” less about the exact 21.5 percent and more about the broader fact that **implementation delays have real economic incidence**. The current draft leans too hard on the headline magnitude. That invites skepticism. The more durable claim is conceptual: delayed implementation is economically costly.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

At present, the gap is mostly a **framing and ambition problem**, with a secondary **scope problem**.

- **Framing problem:** The paper has a good setting, but it undersells the deeper question. It should not primarily be “uncertainty lowers entry in a novel context.” It should be “in integrated markets, delayed implementation creates hidden barriers to entry.”
- **Scope problem:** The paper is narrow in outcome space and sector coverage. That makes it harder to see this as a general insight rather than a specialized fact.
- **Novelty problem:** Moderate. The setting is fresh; the mechanism is familiar. To feel new at AER level, the setting must teach us something larger than the familiar mechanism.
- **Ambition problem:** Yes. The paper is careful and competent, but strategically safe. It does not yet fully claim the bigger intellectual territory available to it.

If I ask whether this would excite the top 10 people in the relevant fields, the answer is: **not yet**. They would say the setting is clever and the question is plausible, but they would still want to know what general lesson travels beyond EU directive timing.

### Single most impactful advice
**Reframe the paper as one about the economic costs of incomplete legal integration—not as a niche EU-compliance application of the uncertainty literature.**

That single change would force improvements everywhere else: introduction, literature positioning, implications, and perhaps even outcome selection.
