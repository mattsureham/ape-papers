## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether delayed implementation of EU directives has real economic costs before any formal noncompliance sanctions bite. Its core claim is that when national governments miss transposition deadlines, firms face a period of regulatory limbo, and that this uncertainty materially suppresses new firm entry, especially in heavily regulated sectors. A busy economist should care because the paper tries to convert what is usually treated as a bureaucratic governance metric into a first-order question about investment, entrepreneurship, and the economic costs of state capacity failures.

Does the paper articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs? Mostly yes. The introduction is better than average: it opens with a concrete institutional fact, states the mechanism, and gives the headline result quickly. But it still leads a bit too much with the EU-internal framing and not enough with the broader economic question. Right now the paper sounds like “an application of uncertainty to EU transposition.” For AER, it should sound like “a clean setting for measuring the economic cost of incomplete policy implementation.”

The first two paragraphs should say something more like:

> Governments often announce policy before they actually implement it. In that interval, firms know that rules are changing, but do not yet know the legally operative details they will face. This paper studies whether that implementation gap itself deters economic activity.
>
> I examine EU directives, which create a sharp and recurring form of delayed implementation: the policy is fixed at the EU level, but national governments often miss the deadline to transpose it into domestic law. Using cross-country differences in transposition timing for the same directive, I show that these periods of regulatory limbo substantially reduce firm entry, with the largest effects in sectors where compliance details matter most. The broader message is that policy uncertainty is generated not only by shocks and political conflict, but also by ordinary implementation delays in multilevel governance.

That is the pitch the paper should have.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper’s contribution is to show that delays in implementing already-announced policy—measured through late transposition of EU directives—reduce firm entry by creating regulatory uncertainty during the implementation gap.

### Is it clearly differentiated from the closest papers?

Only partially. The paper distinguishes itself from:
- the policy uncertainty literature, by emphasizing directly observed implementation delays rather than newspaper/text indices;
- the EU compliance literature, by shifting from why states delay to what delay does economically;
- the entrepreneurship/regulation literature, by focusing on uncertainty about regulation rather than the level of regulation.

Those are real distinctions. But the introduction still does not sharply define the nearest neighbors well enough. The reader can infer the difference, but the paper does not forcefully say: “Existing work studies uncertainty from elections, trade policy, or aggregate policy volatility; this paper isolates uncertainty generated by incomplete implementation of already-enacted policy.” That is the crisp novelty.

### World question or literature gap?

It is framed mostly as a world question, which is good: what happens to firms when law exists politically but not yet operationally? That is stronger than “the literature has not studied transposition outcomes.” The paper should lean even harder into that world question and relegate the “three literatures” contribution paragraph to later or compress it.

### Can a smart economist explain what’s new?

Yes, but not as cleanly as one would want. Right now a smart economist would probably say: “It’s a paper showing that late EU directive transposition lowers firm entry through uncertainty.” That is decent. But they might also say: “It’s another reduced-form paper on policy uncertainty, just in an EU setting.” The introduction needs to make the object of interest the implementation gap itself, not just one more policy uncertainty setting.

### What would make the contribution bigger?

Three ways:

1. **Broader framing around implementation, not just EU directives.**  
   The biggest upgrade is conceptual: this is a paper about the economic cost of delayed implementation in multilevel governance. EU directives are the laboratory, not the audience.

2. **Sharper mechanism outcomes.**  
   If the paper could show that delay affects margins tied specifically to waiting under legal uncertainty—timing of entry, legal form, sector reallocation, licensing-heavy businesses, or post-transposition rebound—it would feel more like a substantive contribution about behavior rather than a single reduced-form result on firm births.

3. **More ambitious comparison.**  
   The paper would be bigger if it could compare the cost of delay to the cost of the regulation itself, or distinguish uncertainty about *how* a rule is implemented from the eventual effect of the rule once in force. That would elevate it from “delay matters” to “the implementation margin is economically distinct from the policy-content margin.”

Possibly also adjacent:

### How should it position itself?

It should **build on** the policy uncertainty literature, **pivot away from** the narrow political science transposition literature, and **connect more explicitly to state capacity and implementation**. It does not need to “attack” neighboring papers. The right positioning is:

- Existing uncertainty papers show firms delay under uncertain future policy.
- Existing EU compliance papers explain why implementation is late.
- This paper connects the two and asks whether incomplete implementation itself is an economically meaningful source of uncertainty.

That is a bridge contribution, not a frontal challenge.

### Too narrow or too broad?

Currently, it is positioned **too narrowly in institutional setting and too broadly in claim**.

- Too narrow because much of the introduction sounds like a paper for scholars of EU governance.
- Too broad because it occasionally implies a sweeping claim about “policy uncertainty” writ large without fully earning that broader generality.

The sweet spot is: **a general paper about implementation delays, with EU directives as an unusually clean empirical setting.**

### What literature does it seem unaware of?

The biggest missing conversation is not another EU paper; it is the literature on:
- **state capacity and bureaucratic quality,**
- **policy implementation versus policy adoption,**
- **administrative burdens and compliance frictions,**
- perhaps **incomplete contracts / legal uncertainty / rule-of-law predictability**.

This paper should be speaking not just to EU economists and uncertainty people, but also to economists interested in how the state’s ability to operationalize law affects economic activity.

### Is it having the right conversation?

Not quite. It is currently having the conversation: “Political scientists study why transposition is delayed; I study an economic outcome.” That is a respectable but not top-journal conversation.

The more impactful conversation is: **Economists often treat policy as a binary enacted/not enacted object. But in the real world, implementation is a separate margin, and delays at that margin can have large allocative consequences.** That is a bigger and more interesting conversation.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Policy uncertainty affects firm behavior, but most evidence comes from aggregate shocks, elections, or trade-policy environments. Meanwhile, modern governance often separates policy adoption from policy implementation, creating periods in which the direction of policy is known but operational legal rules are not.

### Tension

We know governments frequently implement policy late, especially in the EU, but we do not know whether these implementation gaps matter economically. Are missed transposition deadlines just administrative noise, or do they create real uncertainty that changes entry decisions?

### Resolution

The paper finds that late transposition is associated with materially lower firm entry, with especially large effects in regulated sectors and little evidence of impact on incumbent firm stocks.

### Implications

Implementation delays are not merely legal or political failures; they can function as a meaningful source of economic uncertainty. That matters for EU institutional design, but also more broadly for how economists think about state capacity, timing of regulation, and the costs of partial policy execution.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has a **serviceable** narrative arc, but it is not yet maximally disciplined. The story is there, but the paper keeps slipping between three different versions of itself:

1. a policy uncertainty paper,
2. an EU governance paper,
3. an entrepreneurship paper.

At the moment, none fully dominates. The right story is not “here is another setting where uncertainty matters,” nor “here is the first paper on transposition and firm births.” The right story is:

> Implementation is economically consequential. The paper identifies one concrete and recurring implementation failure—late legal transposition—and shows that the gap between policy announcement and legal force deters entry.

That should organize the whole paper.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?

“I’d lead with: when governments miss the deadline to implement EU directives, new firm formation drops sharply in affected sectors—even though the policy itself is already known.”

That is a dinner-party fact with some bite.

### Would people lean in?

Some would. The hook is intelligible and somewhat surprising. But the reaction depends heavily on how it is framed.

- If framed as “EU transposition deficits suppress firm births,” many economists will drift.
- If framed as “the lag between policy adoption and implementation has real economic costs,” people lean in.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Probably: **“Is this really about uncertainty over implementation details, or just about weak states / bad business environments?”**

That follow-up is telling. Even though I am not writing a referee report, strategically the paper must anticipate that reaction in its framing. The paper should not overclaim a universal uncertainty mechanism unless it can persuade the reader that implementation delay is not just a proxy for generally dysfunctional governance.

### If findings are modest or null?

The paper’s findings are not null; they are large. That helps with salience. But because the estimates are large, readers will ask whether the result is plausible and general. Strategically, that means the paper must work extra hard to explain why this setting could generate economically meaningful waiting behavior. The “option value of waiting” story is right, but it needs more texture and institutional specificity.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The gap is mainly a **framing-plus-ambition problem**, with some **scope** issues.

- **Not primarily a science problem** in editorial terms; the paper has a plausible and interesting empirical setting.
- **Primarily a framing problem** because it is still written like a solid field paper on EU regulation rather than a broader paper on implementation and economic behavior.
- **Secondarily an ambition problem** because the paper settles for one main reduced-form effect and a familiar mechanism story instead of pushing toward a broader conceptual payoff.
- **Also some scope problem** because the outcome space is narrow and the application remains confined to a few sectors, making the paper feel smaller than the title and claims suggest.

If this paper is to excite the top people in this area, it needs to make them think: “I should rethink how I model policy timing and implementation,” not just “interesting EU institutional application.”

### Single most impactful advice

**Reframe the paper around the economics of policy implementation gaps—not EU transposition per se—and organize every section around the claim that delayed implementation is a distinct and economically important source of uncertainty.**

That is the one change that would most improve its odds. If the author does only one thing, do that.
