## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper investigates how state funding cuts to public universities reshape the socioeconomic profile of their students. While the standard narrative suggests these cuts "price out" the poor, the author finds a "composition squeeze" where wealthy students—who have more private or out-of-state options—exit first, leaving behind a student body that is more Pell-dependent but attending more resource-depleted institutions.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this reasonably well by the second paragraph, but it wastes space on data descriptions too early. 

**The pitch the paper *should* have:**
"Does state disinvestment in higher education actually displace low-income students? While the conventional wisdom assumes tuition hikes drive out the poor, I show that fiscal austerity triggers a 'composition squeeze' where high-income students with greater outside options exit the public system first. This process leaves low-income students anchored in increasingly underfunded institutions, fundamentally altering the distributional incidence of state budget cuts."

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution:** The paper identifies a counter-intuitive "composition squeeze" where state funding cuts increase the share of low-income students due to the selective exit of wealthy peers rather than the displacement of the poor.

*   **Differentiation:** It moves beyond the "tuition passthrough" literature (Webber, 2017) and the "attainment" literature (Bound & Turner, 2007) to focus on *compositional* shifts.
*   **Framing:** It is currently framed as filling a gap in the literature about "who bears the burden." This is strong, but it could be stronger if framed as a "Quality vs. Access" trade-off in public goods.
*   **Clarity:** A smart economist would get it: it’s not that the poor are coming in, it’s that the rich are leaving.
*   **What would make it bigger?** To be a "Big AER" paper, it needs to quantify the *quality* loss for those left behind. If the Pell share goes up but the value of the degree goes down because the chemistry lab is closed, the "equity" gain is a mirage. The paper needs to link composition to institutional quality measures (instructional spend, student-faculty ratios).

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

*   **Closest Neighbors:** Webber (2017) on tuition; Bound, Braga, Khanna, & Turner (2019) on the supply side; Deming & Walters (2017) on the returns to funding.
*   **Positioning:** It builds on these by looking at the *sorting* mechanism.
*   **Niche vs. Broad:** It risks being a "Higher Ed" niche paper. To broaden it, it should speak to the **Public Economics** literature on the flight from public goods (like the "private school v. public school" literature or "white flight" in urban econ).
*   **Missing Conversation:** It is remarkably silent on the **Sorting/Peer Effects** literature. If the composition changes, the peer-group value of the degree changes. That is a first-order AER-level question.

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

*   **Setup:** States are cutting funding; we assume this hurts the poor.
*   **Tension:** Actually, the data shows the share of Pell students *increases* when funding is cut. Why?
*   **Resolution:** Wealthy students are more mobile. They "vote with their feet" and move to the private sector.
*   **Implications:** Austerity doesn't just reduce the *quantity* of education; it changes the *social contract* of the public university.

**Evaluation:** The arc is there, but it's buried under a "failed IV" narrative. The author spends too much time apologizing for a weak Bartik instrument. In the AER, if the IV is weak, you pivot the narrative to the *political economy* of why it's weak (as the author suggests in the discussion) or you find a better shock.

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

**Dinner Party Fact:** "When states cut university budgets, the student body actually gets *poorer* on average, not because the poor find it easier to get in, but because the rich kids flee to private schools."

**Reaction:** Lean in. It's a "freakonomics" style reversal of intuition.
**Follow-up:** "Does that mean the university is actually more equitable now?" (The answer is almost certainly "no," which is where the paper needs to dig deeper).

---

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

*   **Front-loading:** Move the heterogeneity by "Research vs. Non-Research" much earlier. It is the most interesting part of the results (the $0.10 vs. $0.00 passthrough).
*   **The IV:** If the first-stage F is < 4, stop trying to make the IV happen in the main tables. Move the IV to an appendix as a "transparency check" and lead with the OLS/State-Trends as the best available evidence, or find a different instrument (e.g., specific state tax-law changes).
*   **The Appendix:** The current "Standardized Effect Sizes" table is an eyesore. It belongs in a footnote or a brief sentence in the text.

---

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

**The Gap:** Currently, this is a "very good" Education Finance paper (think *Journal of Human Resources* or *Economics of Education Review*). To be an **AER** paper, it needs to move from "describing a trend" to "explaining a market shift."

**Ambition Problem:** The paper is currently a "Reduced Form" exercise. It tells us *what* happened. An AER paper needs to tell us the *welfare* implications.

**Single Most Impactful Advice:**
Stop focusing on the Pell share as a success metric and start focusing on the **sorting equilibrium**. Show where those wealthy students went (did they go to out-of-state flagships or private colleges?) and quantify the resulting "resource dilution" for the low-income students who remained.
