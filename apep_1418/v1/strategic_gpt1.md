## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks how cuts in state funding to public universities change not just tuition, but the makeup of the students who remain enrolled. Using institution-level panel data, it argues that disinvestment does less to reduce overall attendance than to re-sort students across institutions, with richer students more likely to peel away and low-income students left behind in less well-funded campuses.

A busy economist should care because this is, in principle, a first-order question about the distributional incidence of public disinvestment in higher education: when states cut support, do public universities become less accessible to the poor, or simply poorer places in which the poor are concentrated?

**Does the paper articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Mostly yes on topic, but not on the actual punchline. The introduction opens with a familiar “state cuts lead to tuition increases” frame and then pivots to composition, but the reader does not immediately know what the striking fact is. More importantly, the paper wants to be about **who bears the burden of austerity**, but the current opening still sounds like a standard higher-ed finance paper with some distributional add-ons.

**What the first two paragraphs should say instead:**

> States have sharply reduced per-student funding for public universities over the last two decades. The standard question is whether universities replace those lost dollars with higher tuition. A more consequential question is who is left holding the bag: when public funding falls, are low-income students pushed out, or do public institutions instead become increasingly concentrated with lower-income students as higher-income students exercise outside options?
>
> This paper studies how state disinvestment reshapes the student body at public four-year institutions. Using IPEDS data from 2004–2022, I show that funding cuts are associated less with large average tuition responses than with changes in enrollment composition: Pell-recipient shares rise when appropriations fall, and tuition passthrough is concentrated at research universities. The central implication is that austerity in public higher education may work less by excluding disadvantaged students outright than by leaving them increasingly concentrated in underfunded institutions.

That is the pitch the paper wants. Whether the evidence fully carries it is a separate matter for referees; strategically, this is the story.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:**  
The paper argues that state disinvestment in public higher education primarily changes the socioeconomic composition of enrolled students—especially by increasing Pell-recipient shares—rather than simply increasing tuition or reducing access overall, with tuition passthrough concentrated at research universities.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?
Not yet. The paper cites some broad higher-ed finance papers, but it does not sharply distinguish itself from work on state funding cuts, tuition passthrough, crowd-out, and stratification in higher education. Right now, the contribution reads as:

- “another paper on state appropriations and tuition,” plus
- “a composition outcome using Pell share.”

That is not enough unless the paper is explicit about why **composition** is the underexplored margin and why existing papers cannot answer that question.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a gap in a literature?
It is closer to the world-question than many papers, which is good. The best version is: **What does public austerity do to the distribution of students across public higher education?** That is a real-world question.  
But the introduction periodically slips into “my contribution is to show…” language and literature-gap framing. The paper would be stronger if it led less with “the literature has studied tuition more than composition” and more with “the policy stakes are about who ends up where.”

### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what’s new?
Not quite cleanly. Right now the colleague would probably say:  
“It's a panel paper on state appropriations and public universities; there’s some evidence Pell shares rise when funding falls, and research universities pass cuts into tuition more.”

That is too mushy for AER positioning. The novelty needs to be stated in a way that survives paraphrase:
- “Disinvestment reallocates disadvantage across institutions more than it reduces aggregate attendance.”
- “The incidence of funding cuts operates through compositional sorting, not just price.”
- “The key margin is institutional stratification within the public sector.”

### What would make this contribution bigger?
Very specifically:

1. **Move from Pell share to a broader stratification claim.**  
   As written, the whole paper rides on one composition proxy. That is too narrow. A bigger paper would show re-sorting along multiple dimensions:
   - in-state vs out-of-state students,
   - income distribution proxies beyond Pell,
   - selectivity/academic preparation,
   - full-time/part-time mix,
   - transfer patterns,
   - retention or completion composition.

2. **Reframe away from “does Pell share rise?” toward “where do students go when states cut funding?”**  
   The current outcome is an institutional composition statistic. The bigger question is sectoral and allocative: do students move to private colleges, community colleges, flagship campuses, nearby states, or out of higher education?

3. **Center heterogeneity as the main contribution, not an auxiliary table.**  
   The research/non-research split is actually more interesting than the average effect. If cuts have different consequences across public higher-ed tiers, then the paper becomes about **how austerity amplifies stratification inside the public university system**. That is much more AER-like than a pooled average with a small Pell coefficient.

4. **Connect to mobility and inequality more directly.**  
   If the claim is “low-income students are left behind in resource-starved institutions,” then the paper should frame itself as speaking to intergenerational mobility and the equalizing role of public higher education—not just university finance.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

### Closest neighbors
Based on the citations and field, the closest neighbors seem to be:

2. **Deming and Walters (2017)** on the returns to public postsecondary spending.

Also likely relevant, though not cited strongly enough:

- literature on **college choice under financial constraints**,
- literature on **higher education as an engine of mobility** (e.g., Chetty et al.),
- literature on **public sector austerity and incidence** more broadly.

### How should the paper position itself relative to those neighbors?
**Build on them, but pivot the conversation.**  
Not “prior work looked at tuition; I add Pell.” Too incremental.  
Instead:

- Prior work shows disinvestment affects price, quality, and completion.
- This paper asks **who remains in the public sector when disinvestment occurs**.
- The point is not merely another consequence of cuts, but that the **incidence of cuts is distributional and compositional**, potentially increasing inequality even without dramatic changes in aggregate enrollment.

That is a meaningful shift in the conversation.

### Is the paper positioned too narrowly or too broadly?
At present, oddly both.

- **Too narrowly** in empirical execution: one sector, one main compositional proxy, one institutional panel.
- **Too broadly** in rhetoric: “consequences extend far beyond campus budgets,” “upward mobility,” “distributional incidence of austerity.”

The rhetoric promises a paper about access, inequality, mobility, and public goods. The evidence, as currently presented, is a narrower institutional-composition paper. AER papers can be narrow in data and broad in implications, but only if the bridge is persuasive. Right now the bridge is shaky.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?
It feels under-engaged with at least four conversations:

1. **Stratification within higher education**  
   Not just funding and tuition, but how institutions sort students by income and preparation.

2. **College demand and application behavior**  
   If the paper’s story is selective exit/entry, it should speak to literature on student college choice under price and quality changes.

3. **Public finance incidence / austerity incidence**  
   The broader contribution could be that cuts to public subsidies change the composition of users, not merely the average price paid.

4. **Mobility and equal opportunity**  
   If public universities are mobility engines, then composition changes under austerity matter because they may alter where low-income students enroll and what resources they encounter.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?
Not quite. It is currently having the conversation:  
“state appropriations → tuition/Pell/minority share.”

The better conversation is:  
“what does disinvestment do to the equalizing function of public higher education?”

That is the conversation top journals care about. The current framing is still too much inside-baseball higher-ed finance. The unexpected but more impactful connection is to **inequality, institutional stratification, and the incidence of state retrenchment**.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Public universities have experienced a long decline in state support. The standard narrative is cost shifting: states cut appropriations, universities raise tuition.

### Tension
That standard narrative is incomplete because it focuses on average prices, not on who gets sorted into which institutions. If higher-income students have better outside options, austerity may not simply price out the poor; it may leave disadvantaged students concentrated in weaker institutions.

### Resolution
The paper reports that appropriations cuts are associated with higher Pell shares and that research universities respond very differently from non-research institutions in tuition setting. The implied story is selective exit by higher-income students and a growing concentration of lower-income students in underfunded public institutions.

### Implications
State disinvestment may worsen inequality less by shrinking enrollment mechanically than by changing the distribution of educational environments: lower-income students remain, but in institutions with fewer resources.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
**Serviceable, but incomplete.** The paper does have a discernible story. But it is not as tight as it needs to be because the narrative outruns the results. In particular:

- The title promises “student body recomposition.”
- The introduction promises a challenge to the “displacement narrative.”
- The evidence delivered is one modest Pell-share result, some null/minimal average tuition effects, and one strong heterogeneity result for research universities.

That is not yet a fully resolved narrative. It still feels somewhat like a collection of regressions:
- pooled tuition,
- pooled Pell,
- minority share,
- reduced form,
- state trends,
- research interactions,
- weak-IV discussion.

### What story should it be telling?
The paper should tell **one** story:

> State disinvestment does not hit all public universities or all students the same way. Flagship/research campuses can offset cuts through pricing and market power; non-research institutions cannot. The result is a more stratified public sector in which disadvantaged students are increasingly concentrated in institutions with the least fiscal capacity.

That is the real story latent in the paper. The current version instead splits attention between:
- a weak-IV methodological detour,
- average effects,
- a Pell-share finding,
- a tuition heterogeneity finding.

For narrative purposes, the **tiered-system stratification story** is much stronger than the average-treatment story.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?
Probably this:

> “When states cut funding to public universities, the biggest response may not be average tuition—it may be that the public sector becomes more socioeconomically stratified, with richer students peeling off and lower-income students increasingly concentrated in underfunded campuses.”

That is the most interesting version.

A second, more concrete fact:
> “Research universities appear able to pass state cuts into tuition, but non-research public colleges largely cannot.”

Frankly, that may get more traction than the Pell result because it is cleaner and more intuitive.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
If presented as “a DiD-ish paper showing Pell share goes up by 0.08 percentage points when appropriations fall,” they will reach for their phones. The magnitude sounds tiny, the finding sounds fragile, and the interpretation sounds one step removed from what the data directly observe.

If presented as “state retrenchment increases stratification within the public university system,” they will lean in—because that speaks to inequality, public finance, and higher-ed structure.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Immediately:
- “Where do the higher-income students go?”
- “Is this really sorting across institutions, or just changing Pell eligibility in recessions?”
- “Does this happen mainly at regional publics versus flagships?”
- “What does this imply for completion, quality, or mobility?”

These questions reveal the current paper’s strategic weakness: the interpretation is more ambitious than the outcomes can support.

### If the findings are modest: is the modest result itself interesting?
Only if the paper makes a more disciplined case that:
1. average tuition passthrough is not the right margin, and
2. even modest changes in composition imply meaningful redistribution of educational quality across student types.

Right now the paper does not quite make that case. The main composition estimate feels modest and specification-sensitive, so unless it is embedded in a larger conceptual point about stratification, it risks reading as a failed attempt to find large average effects.

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

### What would make it read better?

1. **Shorten the methodological throat-clearing in the introduction.**  
   The weak-Bartik discussion comes too early and too prominently. It is admirable transparency, but strategically it hijacks the introduction. The introduction should sell the question and the finding, not foreground an instrument failure.

2. **Put the strongest substantive result earlier.**  
   If the research/non-research heterogeneity is the most vivid result, bring it up in the introduction as a central finding, not a later wrinkle.

3. **Demote the IV material in the narrative hierarchy.**  
   Since the paper itself says the IV is weak and the main conclusions rest elsewhere, the text should not keep returning to it as though it is a coequal contribution. Mention it once, briefly, and move on.

4. **Cut or shrink the minority-share analysis unless it matters to the main story.**  
   Right now it appears as a standard additional outcome with little narrative role. It distracts from the core question about low-income composition.

5. **Make the institutional stratification angle the organizing principle of the results section.**  
   Suggested order:
   - The traditional margin: tuition.
   - Why average tuition misses the action.
   - Composition margin.
   - Heterogeneity by institutional tier.
   - Interpretation: disinvestment stratifies the public system.

6. **Tighten the conclusion.**  
   The conclusion currently overstates what has been shown. “Higher-income students exercise their outside options; lower-income students stay” is too definitive relative to the evidence. The conclusion should emphasize *consistent with* or *suggests a stratification channel* unless the paper can show actual flows.

7. **Move some caveats out of the main results and into a concise interpretation subsection.**  
   The reader currently gets the result, then immediately gets reasons not to believe the interpretation. This is intellectually honest but narratively deflationary. Separate:
   - what the reduced-form patterns are,
   - what mechanisms are consistent with them,
   - what the paper cannot distinguish.

### Is the paper front-loaded with the good stuff?
Moderately, but not enough. The best idea is in the introduction. The best empirical result may be the research-university heterogeneity, and that comes too late.

### Are there results buried that should be in the main text?
Yes: the research vs non-research divergence is more important than the pooled tuition null. It should be elevated from “heterogeneity” to part of the main result.

### Is the conclusion adding value?
Some, but it veers into stronger causal storytelling than the paper has earned. It should conclude with the broader implication—public retrenchment may stratify institutions—rather than a crisp behavioral mechanism it cannot observe directly.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The gap is mostly a **framing + scope + ambition** problem.

- **Framing problem:** The paper’s best idea is stronger than its current packaging. It should be about how state retrenchment alters the equalizing function of public higher education by stratifying students across institutional tiers.
- **Scope problem:** One composition proxy at the institution-year level is too thin to support the full “who is left behind” claim.
- **Ambition problem:** The paper is competent but currently safe and narrow. It asks a big question but answers it with a modest institutional panel exercise.

I do **not** think the core issue is primarily novelty in the abstract. The question is important and plausibly publishable at a high level. The problem is that the paper has not yet assembled a sufficiently big empirical and conceptual package around it.

### What is the single most impactful piece of advice?
**Rebuild the paper around stratification across institutional tiers—show that state disinvestment changes where different students are educated within the public system, not just a small change in Pell share at the average institution.**

If they can only change one thing, that is it. Everything else follows from that:
- different framing,
- different literature conversation,
- stronger “so what,”
- more ambitious outcomes/heterogeneity,
- less emphasis on average effects and weak instruments.
