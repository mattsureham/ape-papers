**Summary**

The paper investigates how declines in state appropriations for public four-year universities impact tuition and the socioeconomic composition of enrolled students. Using IPEDS data from 2004–2022 and a Bartik-style instrument based on initial state higher-education spending exposure and national unemployment, the author shows that the instrument is weak, so the preferred results stem from OLS with institution, year, and state-trend fixed effects. The main empirical finding is an increase in the Pell-grant share (higher representation of low-income students) following appropriation cuts, together with heterogeneity where research universities respond with stronger tuition passthrough than non-research institutions.

**Essential Points**

1. **Credibility of Causal Identification:** The paper’s causal claims rely heavily on OLS with institution, year, and state-specific linear trends because the Bartik IV is essentially weak (\(F<4\)). If the first stage cannot be salvaged, the key causal interpretations (composition effects and tuition passthrough differences) are left unsupported. The author needs to provide stronger evidence that the OLS estimates are not confounded by omitted time-varying state-level shocks correlated with both appropriations and enrollment/tuitions. In particular, how do we know that the increasing Pell share is not driven by wider labor-market changes that simultaneously pressure appropriations and drive more students into public universities? The paper must either strengthen the instrument (e.g., use alternative national shocks or incorporate state-level interactive dynamics) or transparently articulate why the OLS estimates can be interpreted causally, perhaps by exploiting additional quasi-experimental variation (e.g., differential timing of recession exposure or abrupt policy shifts).

2. **Interpretation of Composition Effects:** The paper’s main substantive claim is that appropriation cuts cause higher-income students to exit, increasing Pell share, yet the empirical specification only captures cross-sectional Pell share changes at the institution level. There is no direct evidence distinguishing entry versus exit, nor is there an exploration of alternative mechanisms like changes in applicant pools, admission standards, or institutional capacity. The authors must flesh out how the observed increase in Pell share specifically reflects “selective exit” of wealthier students rather than other processes (e.g., expansions in Pell-funded programs, recruitment shifts, changes in student persistence). Without that, the narrative exceeds what the data can support.

3. **Measurement and Modeling of Passthrough Heterogeneity:** Table 4 shows a large positive coefficient for the research-institution interaction, but the baseline coefficient on state appropriations is essentially zero and insignificant. The interpretation that research universities raise tuition \$0.10 per dollar and non-research institutions do not is based on an interaction in a pooled regression, yet there is no robustness to alternative specifications (e.g., separate regressions by institution type, controls for tuition-setting regimes, or testing for parallel trends). The authors need to demonstrate that this heterogeneity is robust and not driven by unbalanced samples or non-linearities, and should consider modeling tuition responses with a more nuanced functional form (e.g., logs or proportionate changes) to make the policy interpretation sensible.

Given these identification and interpretation concerns, the paper should be revised substantially before being considered for publication.

**Suggestions**

1. **Revisit the Instrument Strategy or Exploit Alternative Quasi-Experiments:** The attempted Bartik instrument is commendable, but the current formulation—initial appropriation share × national unemployment—loses power once year fixed effects absorb the common component. Consider enriching the instrument with variation that survives time-demeaning: for example, interact the initial budget share with *state-level* deviations from the national unemployment rate (i.e., use relative unemployment shocks) or with national GDP growth deviations. Another route is to exploit staggered fiscal shocks (e.g., the timing of automatic stabilizer impacts across states) or incorporate data on state rainy day fund balances to better capture exogenous fiscal pressure. If a credible instrument remains elusive, consider a difference-in-differences design focusing on specific policy changes (e.g., states that implemented tuition freezes or large appropriation cuts tied to identifiable events) or instrument for appropriations with exogenous shocks to state tax bases unrelated to higher-education demand.

2. **Strengthen the Narrative on Mechanisms:** To substantiate the interpretation that higher-income students exit the system, include additional outcomes or analyses. For example, examine changes in application volumes (IPEDS Applicant survey or FAFSA data) if available, or track graduation rates/persistence by income proxies to infer whether the Pell share increase stems from differential attrition rather than more low-income entrants. If individual-level data are unavailable, use cohort-level proxies such as the ratio of transfers, shifts in in-state vs. out-of-state enrollment, or changes in the share of students with prior college credit to triangulate on the mechanism. At a minimum, explicitly acknowledge and bound alternative explanations in the discussion.

3. **Robustness Checks for Composition Effect:** The Pell share result becomes significant only after including state-specific linear trends, suggesting sensitivity to specification. Explore alternative ways to flexibly control for time-varying confounders (e.g., state-year controls for unemployment, poverty, or education policy changes) and compare models with higher-order trends or interaction-weighted trends. Report event-study plots or lead/lag specifications to assess pre-trends and ensure that the observed increase in Pell share does not precede appropriation cuts. In addition, consider interacting appropriations with institution-level characteristics (e.g., share of underrepresented minorities in baseline years, institution size, or selectivity) to see whether the effect is concentrated among institutions with limited pricing power. This would strengthen the claim that the composition squeeze is selective.

4. **Clarify the Empirical Strategy Presentation:** The current specification tables are hard to interpret due to formatting issues (e.g., columns labeled “IV” with tiny standard errors). Provide clearer notation (e.g., denominators per \$1,000 change) and explain the economic magnitude with denominator-adjusted effects. Supply first-stage statistics explicitly (coefficients, \(F\)-stat) in the main text or appendix, and report the reduced-form coefficient/lower bound interpretation if IV is weak. An explicit discussion of the exclusion restriction and potential violations would help readers assess the plausibility of the IV approach.

5. **Contextualize Policy Implications with Precision:** The discussion makes strong claims about policy (e.g., implications for free college proposals), but these rely on tenuous empirical support. Tone down speculative statements or support them with additional analysis. For instance, if institution-level heterogeneity in tuition responses is central to policy discourse, report summary statistics showing how funding shocks vary across institution types and whether they correlate with tuition-setting autonomy. Alternatively, frame these policy implications as hypotheses for future work that could test these mechanisms more directly.

6. **Address Data Limitations Transparently:** The paper notes that Pell share is an imperfect proxy for low-income enrollment. Expand this discussion by, if possible, auditing how the composition of Pell recipients changes over time (e.g., by parental education or asset thresholds) and whether this undermines comparability. If no better proxy exists, consider using multiple proxies (e.g., share of students receiving institutional aid, share of undergraduates receiving federal loan aid) to test the robustness of the composition effect.

By strengthening the identification, clarifying the mechanisms, and refining the robustness exercises, the paper could make a valuable contribution to debates about higher-education funding and access.
