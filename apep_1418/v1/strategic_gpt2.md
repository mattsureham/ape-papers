## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks: when states cut funding to public universities, do schools simply raise tuition, or do they also change who ends up enrolled? Using panel data on public four-year institutions, the paper argues that state disinvestment has little average effect on tuition in the pooled sample but shifts student composition, increasing the enrolled share receiving Pell grants, with research universities uniquely able to offset cuts through tuition increases.

Why should a busy economist care? Because the first-order policy question is not just whether public disinvestment makes college more expensive, but whether it changes the distributional role of public higher education as a mobility engine.

### Does the paper articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Not quite. The opening is decent, but the paper’s real pitch is muddied by three things introduced too early or too diffusely:  
1. it opens with the standard tuition-cost-shift frame,  
2. it only gradually gets to the central “composition” question, and  
3. it overstates mechanism (“higher-income students leave first”) that the data do not actually establish.

The first two paragraphs should be much sharper and more world-oriented.

### The pitch the paper should have

“Over the past two decades, U.S. states have sharply reduced per-student support for public universities. The usual concern is that colleges respond by raising tuition, but an equally important and understudied question is whether disinvestment changes who remains enrolled at public institutions. Using IPEDS panel data on public four-year universities from 2004–2022, this paper shows that funding cuts are associated less with broad enrollment collapse than with student-body recomposition: institutions exposed to larger funding declines end up with more Pell recipients in the enrolled population, while research universities are much more able than non-research institutions to replace lost state support through tuition.”

Then a second paragraph should say why that matters:

“This matters because public universities are a central channel for upward mobility. If state disinvestment does not simply shrink access but instead sorts students across institutions—leaving lower-income students concentrated in campuses with fewer resources—then the incidence of austerity is fundamentally distributional, not just financial.”

That is the story. Everything else should serve it.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper’s contribution is to argue that state disinvestment in public higher education changes the socioeconomic composition of enrolled students—measured by Pell recipiency—more than it changes average tuition or aggregate enrollment, with much stronger tuition passthrough at research universities than elsewhere.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?

Only partially. The paper cites adjacent work, but the differentiation is still fuzzy. As currently framed, a reader could easily summarize it as “another panel paper on state appropriations and public universities.” The novelty is supposed to be composition rather than price or completion, but the paper does not clearly establish that this is a large conceptual departure from prior work on funding, tuition, access, and stratification.

The closest neighbors are likely papers on:
- state appropriations and tuition passthrough,
- public disinvestment and completion/resource dilution,
- higher-ed stratification and student sorting,
- distributional incidence of higher-ed funding changes.

The introduction should explicitly say: prior work studies tuition, completion, or institutional resources; this paper studies **who is enrolled after disinvestment**.

### Is the contribution framed as a question about the world, or about filling a literature gap?

It is mostly framed as a question about the world, which is good. The strongest line in the introduction is essentially: “who bears the burden?” That is much better than “the literature has overlooked Pell share.” The paper should lean even harder into the world question and avoid the “my contribution is to add Pell share to the list of outcomes” vibe.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the intro?

Not confidently. They would probably say: “It’s a paper on state cuts to public universities; apparently the main result is that Pell share rises and research universities raise tuition more.” That is not nothing, but it is not yet crisp enough to sound like a big new fact.

The problem is that the paper currently claims too much mechanism from too little evidence. “Higher-income students leave first” is a strong behavioral statement; the actual result is a change in Pell share among those enrolled. Those are not the same. That gap weakens the clarity of the contribution.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Several concrete possibilities:

- **Move from composition of enrollees to student sorting across sectors/institutions.** The big question is not whether Pell share at a campus changes by 0.08 percentage points; it is whether state cuts reallocate students across public/nonprofit/private sectors, across flagships and regionals, or across in-state and out-of-state options.
- **Add outcomes tied to institutional mission and mobility.** Completion, persistence, transfer, borrowing, or net price by income would make the story materially bigger.
- **Show redistribution within the public system.** If research universities raise tuition and preserve resources while non-research institutions cannot, the real contribution may be about stratification **within** public higher education.
- **Replace or supplement Pell share with richer measures of composition.** Pell is coarse and policy-sensitive. First-generation, SAT/ACT distribution, income bins, in-state/out-of-state mix, or transfer share would strengthen the story.
- **Frame around unequal institutional capacity to absorb austerity.** The tuition heterogeneity by research status may actually be the more publishable fact if developed into a broader story about asymmetric adaptation.

If the author could make the paper about how disinvestment **restructures the public university sector**, rather than nudging one composition proxy, it would become much bigger.

Depending on the exact field mapping, the paper may also need to engage:

- literature on **student demand and sectoral substitution**,
- literature on **income-based enrollment responses to costs/aid**,
- literature on **state politics of higher-ed finance**.

### How should it position itself relative to those neighbors?

Mostly **build on and redirect**, not attack.

The right move is:
- prior work shows that funding cuts affect resources, tuition, and completion;
- this paper asks who remains enrolled and which institutions can financially adapt;
- therefore it shifts the conversation from average price/resource effects to distributional and stratification effects.

That is a legitimate extension. But the paper should not oversell itself as overturning the prior narrative. It is not really disproving the tuition story; it is saying the tuition story is incomplete and heterogeneous.

### Is it positioned too narrowly or too broadly?

Right now it is oddly both.

- **Too narrowly** in measurement: Pell share at institution-year level is a pretty specific proxy.
- **Too broadly** in claims: the paper gestures at mobility, political economy, “who bears the burden,” and even the failure of standard fiscal-exposure models.

This combination creates strategic slippage. The audience is unclear: is this a higher-ed finance paper, a public finance paper, a mobility paper, or a political economy paper? It currently reads like a higher-ed empirical note trying to sound like a broad public economics paper.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?

It seems under-connected to:
- **student sorting and mismatch/stratification** literature,
- **college demand and net-price incidence** literature,
- **sectoral substitution** between public, private nonprofit, for-profit, and non-enrollment margins,
- **public finance of state budgeting** beyond generic references to higher education as a shock absorber,
- **mobility/opportunity-insights style literature** on institutional quality and heterogeneous student returns.

If the paper wants to say austerity leaves low-income students in worse-resourced institutions, it should talk to the literature on college quality, institutional spending, and intergenerational mobility more directly.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?

Not quite. The most promising conversation is not “here is one more estimate of the effect of appropriations on tuition/Pell share.” It is:

**State disinvestment may increase stratification within public higher education because institutions differ sharply in their ability to replace public funds.**

That connects higher-ed finance, inequality, and industrial organization of college markets. That is a better AER-adjacent conversation than the current one.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

States have pulled back from funding public universities. The standard understanding is that this raises tuition and strains institutional resources.

### Tension

But average tuition effects may miss the core distributional issue: if different institutions and different students respond differently, state disinvestment may re-sort who attends which public colleges rather than simply reducing attendance overall.

### Resolution

The paper finds modest average tuition responses, an increase in Pell share following appropriation declines, and strong heterogeneity: research universities can replace cuts through tuition, while non-research institutions largely cannot.

### Implications

The burden of disinvestment may be borne through sectoral/institutional sorting and compositional change, not just sticker-price increases. This implies that austerity can deepen inequality within public higher education by leaving more disadvantaged students in institutions with weaker fiscal capacity.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

Serviceable, but not fully coherent. At present it feels like two papers awkwardly fused:

1. a paper on composition effects of state disinvestment, and  
2. a paper on heterogeneity in tuition passthrough by institution type.

Those can fit together, but the paper has not fully made them part of one story. The current title points to composition, yet the sharpest result appears to be the research-vs-non-research tuition heterogeneity. Meanwhile the most provocative claim—selective exit of high-income students—is not directly shown.

### What story should it be telling?

The cleaner story is:

- Public disinvestment does not hit all public universities the same way.
- High-capacity institutions adapt by raising tuition and altering recruitment.
- Lower-capacity institutions cannot easily backfill lost funding.
- As a result, state cuts reshape the distribution of students across an increasingly unequal public sector.

That story integrates the tuition heterogeneity and the composition result. It is stronger than “Pell share goes up a little.”

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at an economist dinner party?

“State funding cuts to public universities may not mostly price out low-income students from the public sector; instead, they seem to change who remains enrolled, and research universities are far better able than regional publics to replace lost state money through tuition.”

More pointedly, if I wanted people to lean in:
“Public disinvestment may increase inequality within the public university system rather than just making college uniformly more expensive.”

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

They would lean in at first, because the question is important and the broad claim is potentially interesting. But the next question would come very quickly:

**“Do you actually show student sorting, or just a small change in Pell share?”**

And that is where enthusiasm would cool. The result is intriguing, but the current evidence base is too thin relative to the conceptual claim.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Probably one of these:
- “Is this really selective exit of higher-income students, or just recession-induced changes in Pell eligibility?”
- “Does this happen within systems—do flagships and regionals diverge?”
- “Where do the displaced students go?”
- “Are you measuring access, sorting, or just changing composition among continuing enrollees?”

Those are exactly the questions the current framing invites but does not answer.

### If the findings are modest: is the modesty itself interesting?

Only partly. A null average tuition response is not by itself interesting enough for AER. The interesting part is the asymmetry across institutions and the possibility of hidden distributional incidence. The paper does not yet make a sufficiently forceful case that learning “the average effect on tuition is small, but composition changes modestly” is a major fact about the world.

As written, there is some risk it reads like a failed attempt to estimate one effect that pivoted into a suggestive alternative story. The author needs to make the final story feel chosen, not residual.

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

### Without rewriting the paper, what would make it read better?

#### 1. Put the core contribution on page 1, and stop advertising the weak IV as a contribution
The paper gives too much valuable early real estate to the Bartik instrument and its weakness. That is not the reason to read the paper. It belongs later and should be treated as a limitation, not as a quasi-finding.

#### 2. Reorder the introduction around the main fact pattern
Suggested structure:
- para 1: state disinvestment is large and consequential;
- para 2: the missing question is distributional/compositional incidence;
- para 3: preview the main findings, with heterogeneity by institution type;
- para 4: why this matters for inequality and public-sector stratification;
- then literature.

#### 3. Integrate the heterogeneity result much earlier
Right now the most compelling result—research universities can pass through cuts into tuition while others cannot—arrives too late. That should appear in the introduction’s main findings paragraph. It may be the paper’s best hook.

#### 4. Tone down mechanism claims not directly observed
Phrases like “higher-income students leave first” and “selective exit, not differential entry” are too assertive. Those should be rephrased as interpretations or hypotheses. Overclaiming hurts credibility and strategic positioning.

#### 5. Shrink the discussion of the instrument
AER introductions should not spend this much time on the failure mode of one design unless that failure itself is conceptually profound. Here it is not. It is mostly a design limitation. Move much of that discussion to the empirical strategy or appendix.

#### 6. Shorten institutional background
The background section is competent but generic. Much of it could be condensed. Readers in this area do not need several paragraphs on the Great Recession and Pell’s declining purchasing power unless those details become central to the empirical argument.

#### 7. Strengthen the conclusion
The conclusion currently overreaches rhetorically relative to the evidence. It should do less moralizing and more synthesis: what did we learn about institutional adaptation, student composition, and stratification within public higher education?

### Are good results buried?

Yes. The research/non-research heterogeneity is partly buried. It should be elevated in the introduction and perhaps in the title/subtitle logic.

### Is the paper front-loaded with the good stuff?

Not enough. The reader has to parse too much setup and caveat before getting a clean statement of what is new.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

Honestly, the gap is substantial.

### What is the main gap?

Primarily a **scope/ambition problem**, with a secondary **framing problem**.

- **Not mainly a framing-only issue:** better framing would improve the paper, but it would not by itself make the contribution top-tier.
- **Scope problem:** the paper asks a big question—how austerity changes who public universities serve—but answers it with a limited outcome measure and speculative mechanism.
- **Ambition problem:** the design and outcome set are too modest relative to the breadth of the claims.
- **Novelty problem:** as it stands, the paper does not yet establish a sufficiently new fact beyond “Pell share moves somewhat and passthrough is heterogeneous.”

### What would excite the top 10 people in this field?

A paper that could show one of the following would be much closer:
1. **State disinvestment systematically re-sorts students across tiers of the public system**, increasing stratification in resources and student composition.
2. **Institutional fiscal capacity mediates the incidence of austerity**, with clear consequences for access, completion, or mobility.
3. **Where students go instead**—public flagship to regional, public to private, in-state to out-of-state, or out of college altogether.
4. **How cuts affect both composition and outcomes**, not just composition among enrollees.

In short: the paper needs to move from “composition proxy at the institution level” to “reallocation and stratification in the higher-ed system.”

### Single most impactful advice

If the author could change only one thing, it should be this:

**Reframe and expand the paper around stratification within public higher education—using the research/non-research heterogeneity as the spine—and treat Pell share as one piece of evidence rather than the paper’s entire contribution.**

That one move would improve the title, introduction, literature framing, and narrative coherence at once. It would also align the evidence more closely with a genuinely important question about the world.
