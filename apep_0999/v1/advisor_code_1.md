**Idea Fidelity**

The paper builds on the manifest’s central research question—whether the private-sector IR35 extension induced client firms to manipulate headcounts around the 50-employee small company exemption. However, it departs from key aspects of the original plan. The manifest emphasized firm-level data from Companies House XBRL filings, contractor-intensive SIC codes, and a bunching-difference design operating on actual employee counts to infer compliance costs from the demand side. The submitted paper instead relies solely on sector-level NOMIS Business Counts, uses a narrower set of SIC codes (62, 70, 71, 74) versus the manifest’s emphasis on contractor-heavy codes plus relevant comparisons, and defines the outcome as a bunching *ratio* rather than firm-level bunching. This shifts the empirical approach from the intended firm-level manipulation story to an aggregate sector-level comparison, omitting the Companies House microdata that would allow for mechanism tests (e.g., headcount inflation through contractor-to-employee conversions). The identification strategy in the manuscript is therefore not fully faithful to the original manifest (particularly the data sources and the contractor-specific micro-level variation), though the broad research question remains aligned.

**Summary**

The paper documents that, contrary to standard “bunching” expectations, contractor-intensive sectors in the UK saw a *decline* in the ratio of firms with 20–49 employees to firms with 50–99 employees after the April 2021 private-sector IR35 reform, relative to wholesale/retail controls. Using NOMIS employment-size data, it interprets this as evidence of a “compliance trap” whereby firms convert contractors to employees, inflating headcounts and pushing themselves past the 50-employee exemption threshold. The key empirical strategy is a difference-in-bunching design supplemented by triple differences, event studies, sector heterogeneity, and several robustness tests.

**Essential Points**

1. **Control Group Validity and Sector-Specific Trends:** The treatment–control comparison rests on two wholesale/retail SIC codes (46/47) that differ fundamentally from the contractor-intensive sectors in terms of firm dynamics, technology adoption, and post-COVID growth trajectories. Even with SIC fixed effects, aggregate sector-level time trends (e.g., differing recovery paths, outsourcing practices, or sectoral demand shocks) could drive the observed divergence in bunching ratios rather than IR35-induced behavior. Without broader control groups or placebo sectors with similar contractor reliance but no policy exposure, the difference-in-bunching estimate may capture compositional shifts rather than treatment effects. The authors need to demonstrate that the control sectors are valid counterfactuals—ideally by showing parallel pre-trends not just in the bunching ratio but in underlying growth rates or other firm size distributions—and by considering alternative control sets (e.g., other services sectors with low contractor use).

2. **Ambiguity of the Mechanism and Outcome Interpretation:** The paper interprets a falling bunching ratio as evidence of firms being “pushed past” the threshold through contractor-to-employee conversion. Yet the aggregate ratio conflates multiple forces: faster organic growth, differential entry/exit, mergers, or even measurement artifacts in the NOMIS counts. The significant placebo at the 20-employee threshold (Table 7 Panel B) suggests a broader rightward drift in firm sizes in contractor sectors, not necessarily tied to the 50-employee IR35 cliff. Without firm-level evidence of increases in reported employees matched to reductions in contractors, or at least evidence that the ratio changes are localized precisely at the policy threshold, the putative mechanism remains speculative. The authors should either provide additional data (e.g., Companies House filings, account categories, or contractor counts) to link the bunching ratio change to contractor conversion, or clearly acknowledge the limits of what the ratio can reveal about the underlying behavior.

3. **Aggregation and Composition Effects:** The entire analysis relies on employment sizebands aggregated at the SIC-year level, which obscures within-band heterogeneity and makes it impossible to separate changes in firm-level behavior from differential entry/exit or reporting shocks. For instance, if new firms entering contractor-intensive sectors tend to be larger post-2021 (because smaller ones are dissuaded by compliance risk), this would reduce the bunching ratio without any single firm “crossing” from 49 to 51 employees. Similarly, the NOMIS counts do not adjust for corporate restructuring (e.g., mergers, demergers, acquisitions) or for shifts in SIC classifications. The authors need to address whether differential entry/exit or SIC reclassification across the threshold could explain the result, perhaps by using Companies House data as originally intended or by bounding the effect using additional moments (e.g., stability of firm counts below 20 employees).

If these issues cannot be convincingly addressed, the paper’s causal claim that IR35 induced a compliance trap is undermined, and rejection would be warranted.

**Suggestions**

1. **Broaden and Test Controls.** Expand the control group beyond wholesale/retail. Consider services sectors that are close in contractor intensity but were not affected by IR35 (e.g., certain finance or health-related SICs). Alternatively, construct a synthetic control combining multiple sectors to match pre-trend levels and slopes of the bunching ratio. Present balance tests showing similar pre-2021 trends in aggregate firm counts, turnover, or employee shares to bolster the parallel trends assumption. If control-sector selection remains limited, justify it with evidence (e.g., citations showing that wholesale/retail firm-size dynamics closely tracked contractor sectors before 2021).

2. **Leverage Firm-Level Data for Mechanism Tests.** Incorporate Companies House BasicCompanyData (as outlined in the manifest) to move beyond aggregate ratios. For example, track the number of firms filing as MICRO/SMALL/MEDIUM around the threshold, examine year-on-year changes in reported employee counts, or exploit longitudinal data to see whether firms with large contractor shares increased their headcount post-2021. If contractor counts are unavailable, use proxies such as the share of “full-time equivalents” reported or the style of account filings (e.g., more firms switching from “ACCOUNTING DOCUMENTS USING FRS 102” to fuller reporting). These firm-level checks would strengthen the argument that headcount inflation, rather than sector-wide growth, drives the observed pattern.

3. **Disentangle Entry/Exit from Size Manipulation.** Analyze whether the decline in the 20–49 band stems from firms exiting/entering rather than existing firms crossing the threshold. You can do this by comparing the *growth rates* of the 20–49 and 50–99 bands within SIC codes, or by focusing on cohort analyses that follow the same sizeband over time. Incorporating “sizeband share” regressions (e.g., share of firms in each band each year) may help isolate reclassification. Additionally, examine whether the total number of firms in contractor sectors changed differently than in controls, which could signal entry/exit effects. If entry is the culprit, the policy implication differs from what is claimed.

4. **Clarify the Economic Interpretation of the Bunching Ratio.** Since the bunching ratio (20–49 / 50–99) is not a standard measure of bunching, explain more fully why a decline reflects firms crossing threshold versus other dynamics. Consider supplementing with a classic density-bunching exercise using local polynomial methods to estimate excess mass just below 50 employees. Alternatively, compute the proportion of firms in the 45–49 versus 50–54 bands to directly capture movement around the threshold. This would make the interpretation more intuitive and align better with the bunching literature.

5. **Address the Placebo Findings Transparently.** The significant 20-employee placebo suggests broader structural shifts; instead of dismissing it as consistent with the mechanism, discuss whether such shifts could bias the main estimate upward. If the 20-employee decline is driven by the same forces (e.g., contractor conversion at lower sizes), clarify why this doesn’t invalidate the 50-employee result. Another idea is to use the placebo to “difference out” general trends by including it as a control outcome in a stacked regression, ensuring that only the IR35-specific response remains.

6. **Improve Inference Given Limited Clusters.** The dataset has only six SIC codes, so clustered standard errors are infeasible. Consider implementing wild bootstrap inference across SIC-year observations or use permutation tests (e.g., randomly assigning “contractor” labels to SICs) to assess how unusual the estimated effect is. This would add credibility to the statistical significance claims despite the small effective sample.

7. **Quantify the Magnitude of Mechanism-Consistent Behavior.** The back-of-envelope compliance cost estimate is helpful but could be made more precise by translating the estimated number of additional firms in the 50–99 band into employee counts. For instance, estimate how many firms would need to jump from 49 to 51 employees to produce a 0.31 decline in the ratio, assuming stable denominator growth. This would allow you to compare the implied number of contractor conversions to HMRC compliance cost estimates more concretely.

8. **Discuss Alternative Explanations in More Depth.** Consider other mechanisms that could produce similar empirical patterns, such as firms responding to IR35 by splitting into subsidiaries (which might show up as multiple firms with different SICs) or shifting contractors to outsourced “delivery partners” whose headcounts are counted elsewhere. Even if data limitations prevent fully testing these alternatives, acknowledging them and explaining why they are unlikely (or suggesting how future work could test them) would strengthen the narrative.

9. **Clarify the Role of COVID and Delay.** The paper references the 2020 delay as a built-in placebo, but the event study shows little pre-trend; yet COVID itself had dramatic effects on services sectors. Providing more detail on how the policy interacted with pandemic-induced changes (e.g., whether contractor use fell overall, affecting both treated and controls) would help reassure readers that the estimated effect is indeed due to IR35 rather than differential pandemic response.

10. **Enhance Discussion of External Validity.** The conclusion draws lessons for the EU Platform Work Directive. Strengthen this by explaining which aspects of IR35 (e.g., small company exemption tied to the number of employees) are shared by the Directive, and whether the same data patterns could feasibly arise in platform contexts. This would help policymakers understand when a “compliance trap” might emerge elsewhere.

Implementing even some of these suggestions would bolster the credibility of the identification strategy, contextualize the mechanism, and reinforce the paper’s contribution to the regulation and bunching literatures.
