## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple, policy-relevant question: when a country grants legal work status to a very large population of already-present undocumented migrants, does that disrupt local labor markets? Using Colombia’s 2021 regularization of Venezuelan migrants, the paper argues that the answer is no at the aggregate level: legalizing migrants who are already working mainly changes their legal status, not the total labor supply facing natives.

A busy economist should care because the political debate around regularization is intense and global, yet most economic evidence is about immigrant inflows, not legalization of people already there. If the margins are different, then the policy conclusions from the classic immigration literature do not transport automatically.

### Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Reasonably clearly, but not as sharply as it should. The current opening is competent and topical, but it still reads somewhat like a literature-preface rather than a high-stakes empirical question. It spends too much energy distinguishing “arrival” from “regularization” in conceptual terms before fully crystallizing the central fact: this is a huge policy shock to legal status, not to headcount.

The first two paragraphs should say, more directly:

> Governments fear that legalizing undocumented migrants will harm native workers by pushing migrants into formal labor markets. But that fear has rarely been tested, because most research studies immigration inflows rather than regularization of workers already present.  
>  
> This paper studies Colombia’s 2021 regularization of roughly 1.8 million Venezuelans, one of the largest legalization programs in the world. I show that despite its scale, the policy had no detectable effect on aggregate employment, unemployment, or labor force participation across Colombian departments. The key implication is that in an economy with a large informal sector, regularization may change workers’ legal status without changing the aggregate labor-market equilibrium.

That is the pitch. It is cleaner, world-facing, and gives the reader the result fast.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper’s contribution is to show that mass legalization of already-present migrants can have essentially no aggregate labor-market effects, especially in an economy where informality has already absorbed the labor supply shock.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3-4 papers?

Partly, but not fully. The paper does distinguish regularization from arrival, which is the right axis. But it still risks sounding like “another immigration shock paper in Colombia, except the coefficient is null.” The closest-neighbor differentiation needs to be sharper and more disciplined.

The paper should more cleanly separate itself from:
1. Venezuelan arrival papers in Colombia and neighboring countries.
2. IRCA/amnesty papers on migrant outcomes.
3. General immigration-incidence papers in formal labor markets.
4. Informality papers that imply a mechanism but do not test regularization directly.

Right now, the introduction cites these literatures, but the “what is new here?” message is still a bit diffuse. The paper has one core novelty: **it studies legalization rather than inflow, and does so in a high-informality economy at very large scale**. That should be the entire contribution section in essence.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or filling a gap in a LITERATURE?

It is mostly framed as a world question, which is good: do legalization programs disrupt labor markets? But at times it slips into gap-filling mode: “the literature studies arrival rather than regularization.” That is useful as support, but not enough as a primary frame.

The stronger frame is: **Policymakers fear one thing; this setting lets us test whether that fear is warranted.**

### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what’s new?

Probably yes, but with some hesitation. The best version is: “It’s about legalization, not immigration inflow, and in Colombia the aggregate labor market doesn’t move.” The weaker version, which the current draft still invites, is: “It’s a DiD on Venezuelan migration in Colombia with a null.” That is the risk.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Several possibilities:

- **Move from aggregate rates to formal/informal composition.** This is the single clearest way to enlarge the paper. The current story all but demands evidence on formality, social security enrollment, contracts, sectoral reallocation, or native displacement into informality. Without that, the proposed mechanism remains mostly interpretive.
- **Focus the framing on regularization as a distinct policy margin.** The paper should be less about “null labor-market effects” and more about “legal status shocks differ from labor-supply shocks.”
- **Show who changed, not just whether the aggregate changed.** If migrants formalized while natives did not lose out, that is a major result. If migrants formalized and natives were displaced from formal into informal jobs, that is also major. Right now the paper can only speculate.
- **Broaden the implications beyond Colombia.** The paper already gestures at the U.S., EU, Turkey. To make that credible, it needs a more explicit framework for when the result should and should not generalize: large informal sector, already-present workers, weak enforcement baseline, gradual permit uptake.

As written, the paper’s contribution is interesting but still somewhat narrower than AER-level ambition.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

### Closest neighbors

The closest conversations appear to be:

- **Card (1990)** and the immigration-incidence tradition more broadly, as the canonical benchmark the paper wants to depart from.
- **Ottaviano and Peri (2012)** / **Peri and Sparber (2009)** as examples of arrival-focused adjustment mechanisms.
- **Clemens and Hunt / Clemens-related immigration work** on aggregate labor market effects and limits of standard incidence claims.
- **Kossoudji and Cobb-Clark (2002)** and other IRCA/amnesty papers on legalization effects for migrants.
- **Recent Venezuelan migration papers in Colombia**, especially those on informality and local labor-market adjustment.
- **Ulyssea (2020)** and related informality papers as the mechanism backdrop.

### How should the paper position itself relative to those neighbors?

It should **build on** the arrival literature, not attack it. The right message is not “the classic literature is asking the wrong question,” but “it studies a different policy margin.” That is a much more persuasive stance.

It should position itself as:
- complementary to arrival papers,
- extending legalization papers to a much larger and more informal setting,
- and connecting immigration economics to the economics of informality.

That last bridge is potentially the most interesting one.

### Is the paper currently positioned too narrowly or too broadly?

A bit of both, oddly.

- **Too narrowly** in the empirical object: department-level aggregate rates in one country.
- **Too broadly** in the rhetorical claims: “the regularization illusion” and policy extrapolations to the U.S., EU, and Turkey.

The current framing reaches for a general concept, but the empirical design supports a more conditional claim: in Colombia, and plausibly in similar high-informality settings, legalization need not disrupt aggregate labor markets.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of or under-engaged with?

The paper should speak more directly to:
- the **economics of legal status** and documentation,
- **informality and enforcement**,
- **state capacity / labor regulation**,
- possibly the literature on **refugees / temporary protection** rather than only classic labor immigration,
- and the broader **political economy of regularization**.

It also needs a more serious conversation with papers on **formalization margins**. If the core mechanism is that regularization changes legal status but not the underlying employment equilibrium, the relevant comparison is not just immigration incidence papers but formalization papers.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?

Not quite yet. It currently presents itself mainly as an immigration-labor paper with a null result. The more interesting conversation is: **what happens when the state changes the legality of work without changing the number of workers?** That is not just an immigration question; it is a labor-market-institutions question.

That reframing would substantially improve its strategic positioning.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Unauthorized migrants are already working in host economies, and governments debate whether granting legal status will hurt natives by moving migrants into formal labor markets.

### Tension

We have lots of evidence on what happens when immigrants arrive, but far less on what happens when already-present migrants are legalized. The two margins could have very different effects, especially where informality is large.

### Resolution

In Colombia’s massive Venezuelan regularization, aggregate labor-market outcomes did not move.

### Implications

Legalization may be much less disruptive than political rhetoric suggests, because the labor market has already adjusted before legality changes. The real effects may be compositional or welfare-related rather than aggregate.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the ingredients of one, but the arc is not fully disciplined. At times it reads like a collection of standard empirical sections surrounding a null result. The mechanism story comes in late and remains under-evidenced. The coined phrase “regularization illusion” is an attempt to create narrative unity, but at the moment it feels slightly more like branding than a fully earned conceptual contribution.

### If it is a collection of results looking for a story, what story should it tell?

The story should be:

1. **Policy fear:** Legalization is politically toxic because people think it creates labor-market competition.
2. **Conceptual distinction:** But legalization is not immigration inflow; it changes legality, not bodies.
3. **Contextual insight:** In a high-informality economy, much of the adjustment may already have happened.
4. **Evidence:** Colombia’s enormous program produced no aggregate disruption.
5. **Interpretation:** The relevant effects of legalization may be on formality, worker protections, and distribution within the labor market, not aggregate employment totals.

That is a coherent AER-style story. The paper currently tells pieces of it, but not with enough economy or force.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?

“Colombia legalized roughly 1.8 million Venezuelan migrants—about 3.6 percent of the population—and aggregate employment and unemployment didn’t budge.”

That is the dinner-party line.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

Initially, they would lean in. The scale and policy relevance are genuinely attention-grabbing. But the next question comes immediately: **“Okay, but what happened to formality?”** If the answer is “we can’t observe that here,” interest drops sharply.

That is the central strategic issue for the paper. The aggregate null is interesting enough to start a conversation, but not enough to finish one at the AER level.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Almost certainly one of these:
- Did migrants formalize?
- Did natives get displaced from formal to informal jobs?
- Did wages move in the formal sector even if employment didn’t?
- Is this a Colombia/informality result or a general regularization result?
- Was uptake too slow for aggregate effects to appear yet?

These are exactly the questions the paper raises but cannot fully answer.

### If the findings are null or modest: is the null itself interesting?

Yes, but only conditionally. Nulls are publishable when they adjudicate a first-order policy fear in a high-stakes setting and when the paper can say what margin did adjust instead. This paper is halfway there. It has the high-stakes setting. It does not yet fully have the alternative adjustment margin.

So the null is **potentially** interesting, but it currently risks feeling like “we checked aggregate rates and nothing happened” rather than “we learned something decisive about how legalization works.”

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The gap is mainly a **scope-and-ambition problem**, with some **framing problem** layered on top.

This is not primarily a “bad story” paper. The story is there. Nor is it mainly a novelty problem; legalization at this scale in this context is genuinely interesting. The issue is that the current paper stops one step too early. It shows no aggregate labor-market disruption, then infers an informality-based mechanism it cannot directly demonstrate.

For AER, the top people in the field will want one of two things:

1. **Direct evidence on the adjustment margin** — especially formality, contracts, social security enrollment, sector switching, or native-vs-migrant compositional effects; or  
2. **A much stronger conceptual synthesis** that turns this from a local null into a general framework for thinking about legalization in informal economies.

Right now, it has neither fully.

### Is it a framing problem?

Partly. The paper should frame itself less as “a null labor-market paper” and more as “a paper about the economics of legal status shocks.”

### Is it a scope problem?

Yes, strongly. The outcomes are too coarse for the mechanism the paper wants to claim.

### Is it a novelty problem?

Not mainly. The setting is novel enough.

### Is it an ambition problem?

Yes. The paper is competent but a bit safe. It takes the most accessible aggregate outcomes and stops there, while openly acknowledging richer individual-level data that would speak to the central mechanism. That is exactly where an AER paper would push further.

### Single most impactful advice

**Add direct evidence on formalization and compositional adjustment; without that, the paper is an interesting aggregate null, but not yet a decisive paper about regularization.**
