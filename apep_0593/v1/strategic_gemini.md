## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
This paper evaluates the real-economy impact of the EU’s "Roam Like at Home" (RLAH) regulation, which abolished mobile roaming surcharges for 450 million citizens. Using a difference-in-differences design comparing EU border regions to interior regions, the author finds that despite a massive 490% surge in data usage, the policy had no detectable effect on foreign tourist nights. This suggests that while digital frictions are salient, they are inframarginal to the physical decision to cross a border, which remains governed by "deeper" cultural and linguistic barriers.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this pitch exceptionally well. The first two paragraphs of the Introduction are a masterclass in AER-style framing: they lead with a relatable micro-example (the German tourist in Strasbourg), quantify the "first stage" (490% data surge), state the simple question, and deliver the clear, provocative answer. No rewrite is needed.

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**The Contribution:** The paper provides the first empirical evidence that removing salient digital transaction costs does not translate into increased physical mobility in a high-friction international environment.

**Evaluation:**
- **Differentiation:** It is well-differentiated. While the "roaming" literature (Quinn et al. 2024) focuses on telecom welfare, this paper pivots to the real economy. 
- **World vs. Literature:** It frames the question about the WORLD (Does the Digital Single Market actually integrate Europe?). This is a significant strength.
- **Explainability:** A smart economist could explain this in one sentence: "The EU ended roaming, data use exploded, but nobody actually traveled more because language and culture matter more than a €30 phone bill."
- **Bigger Contribution:** To make the contribution "bigger," the author needs to address the **day-trip** margin. If the "accommodation nights" result is a null, but day-trips (where the €30 fee is a much larger % of the trip cost) surged, the story changes from "digital barriers don't matter" to "digital barriers only matter for low-stakes mobility."

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
- **Closest Neighbors:** McCallum (1995) on border puzzles; Chetty et al. (2009) on salience; and the recent "Market Access" literature (Donaldson & Hornbeck 2016).
- **Positioning:** The paper correctly builds on the "Border Puzzle" literature. It acts as a necessary "reality check" for the infrastructure literature by showing that digital infrastructure does not share the same multiplier effects as physical infrastructure (rail/roads).
- **Missing Literature:** The paper could benefit from connecting to the **"Economic Geography of Tourism"** (e.g., Faber and Gaubert). It currently treats tourism as a generic proxy for mobility, but tourism has its own idiosyncratic cost structures.
- **Right Conversation?** Yes. It connects a very specific policy (RLAH) to the broad, fundamental question of why borders still matter in a globalized world.

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC
- **Setup:** The EU creates a "Digital Single Market" to erase borders.
- **Tension:** Mobile data usage skyrockets (the policy is highly salient), yet foreign overnight stays remain flat.
- **Resolution:** Roaming charges are "nuisances" but not "barriers." They affect *how* you travel, not *if* you travel.
- **Implications:** Integration policy should stop chasing "low-hanging fruit" (digital fees) if the goal is true mobility; it must instead tackle "deep" frictions (language/institutions).

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong. It isn't just a collection of results; it's a coherent argument about the hierarchy of trade frictions.

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
At a dinner party: "The EU's most popular policy caused a 500% increase in data usage but didn't actually encourage a single extra person to stay the night across the border." 
**Reaction:** People lean in. It challenges the "common sense" notion that making things easier/cheaper always increases volume. The follow-up question is always: "What about day trips?" (This is the paper's Achilles' heel).

---

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
- **Front-loading:** The paper is well-structured.
- **Section 6 (Interpretations):** This is the "meat" for an AER reader. It could be expanded with more formal "back-of-the-envelope" (BOTE) modeling to show exactly how high the price elasticity of travel would have to be for the null to be surprising.
- **Appendix:** The "Standardized Effect Sizes" (Appendix F) is a bit unusual/unnecessary for the main text; it feels like the author is trying too hard to justify a null. The results in Table 2 speak for themselves.

---

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The paper is currently a "very good" empirical note. To be a "great" AER paper, it needs to solve the **Scope Problem**.

The primary outcome (accommodation nights) is a noisy proxy for the behavior the author actually wants to measure (cross-border mobility). Because accommodation is expensive, roaming fees are a tiny fraction of the cost. The author admits this.

**Single Biggest Improvement:** Secure a second data source for **low-cost mobility**—ideally cross-border train bookings or road sensor data at borders. If the author can show that *even day-trips* didn't increase, the "inframarginal" argument becomes an ironclad law of integration. If day-trips *did* increase, the paper becomes a fascinating study on the elasticity of mobility relative to trip stakes.

- **Current framing quality:** Compelling
- **Contribution clarity:** Crystal clear
- **Literature positioning:** Well-positioned
- **Narrative arc:** Strong

**Decision:** Do not reject. This has high potential. The "clean" null on such a high-profile policy is exactly the kind of "curiosity-driven" economics the AER should publish, provided the author can prove the null isn't just an artifact of the specific outcome variable chosen.
