## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether eliminating EU mobile roaming charges in 2017 increased cross-border tourism. Using the idea that border regions should be most exposed to roaming frictions, it finds that although mobile data use exploded after the reform, foreign overnight stays did not—suggesting that digital convenience improved for existing travelers but did not materially change whether people travel.

A busy economist should care because this is, at least in aspiration, a test of a broader question: when does removing a salient digital transaction cost translate into real economic integration? That is a potentially important question for trade, public economics, industrial organization, and political economy of integration.

**Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Partly. The opening anecdote is vivid and the question is stated cleanly. But the paper still undersells the bigger idea. It currently reads like a policy evaluation of one EU reform plus a careful null result. For AER positioning, the first two paragraphs should make clearer that the paper is about the relationship between **digital frictions and real cross-border activity**, not just “did roaming abolition affect tourism nights?”

### The pitch the paper should have

Here is the version the paper should be leading with:

> Policymakers increasingly remove digital frictions—roaming charges, payment barriers, geo-blocking, platform restrictions—in the hope of deepening economic integration. But do these reforms change real behavior, or do they mainly make existing cross-border activity more convenient?  
> 
> This paper studies the EU’s 2017 abolition of retail roaming charges, one of the largest and most salient reductions in consumer cross-border digital costs in recent decades. I show that while the reform caused a massive increase in roaming data use, it did not produce a detectable increase in foreign tourist overnight stays in border regions. The central implication is that removing a highly salient digital cost can generate large intensive-margin responses without moving the extensive margin of cross-border mobility.

That framing is stronger because it answers a question about the world: **when do digital integration policies affect real integration?**

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:**  
The paper’s contribution is to show that abolishing EU roaming charges sharply increased mobile usage but did not measurably increase cross-border overnight tourism, implying that this salient digital friction was not a first-order barrier to physical mobility.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?
Moderately, but not sharply enough.

The paper does distinguish itself from telecom papers on roaming usage, pricing, and welfare by saying, in effect, “they study telecom outcomes; I study real-economy spillovers.” That is the right instinct. But the differentiation is still too categorical and a bit mechanical. The reader needs a crisper “why this is a different question” line:

- Existing roaming papers ask whether consumers used phones differently.
- This paper asks whether consumers **moved differently**.

That distinction is genuinely meaningful. The introduction should hammer it.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or filling a gap in the LITERATURE?
Mostly both, but too often it slips into “no paper has tested…” framing. That is weaker. “No paper has studied X” is rarely enough for AER. The stronger framing is:

- Governments believe digital market integration promotes real integration.
- This paper tests whether that belief is true in an important setting.

That is a world question.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?
A decent economist could probably say: “It studies the EU roaming reform and finds no effect on border-region tourist nights.” That is clear enough.

But they might also say: “It’s a DiD on tourism after an EU policy.” That is the danger. Right now, the paper is a bit too close to “another clean design around an EU reform” unless the author leans much harder into the broader conceptual contribution.

### What would make this contribution bigger?
Several specific possibilities:

1. **A better mobility outcome.**  
   The paper itself admits the obvious issue: roaming charges should matter most for day trips and short, spontaneous trips, while the outcome is overnight accommodation nights. That is a strategic vulnerability, not just a measurement caveat. If the author could get:
   - border crossings,
   - mobile location mobility flows,
   - same-day trips,
   - short-duration visits,
   - or spending by foreign cards near borders,
   the paper becomes much bigger immediately.

2. **A direct link between digital and physical margins.**  
   The most interesting fact in the paper is the wedge:
   - huge effect on roaming usage,
   - no effect on overnight travel.
   
   That wedge should be elevated from supporting color to the main intellectual contribution. Ideally the author would show more systematically that the reform altered the behavior of existing travelers rather than the number of travelers.

3. **A stronger mechanism comparison.**  
   The “inframarginal vs. adaptation vs. deeper frictions” discussion is plausible, but currently speculative. A bigger paper would distinguish among these channels, not merely list them.

4. **A broader framing beyond tourism.**  
   Tourism is a convenient application, but the real contribution is about integration frictions. The paper would feel more important if it were framed as evidence on the limits of digital integration policies as tools for real economic integration.

The highest-value conversation is probably:
**“When do reductions in salient digital transaction costs change real economic behavior?”**

That gives the paper a cleaner home and broader appeal.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Before this paper: the EU abolished roaming charges in a highly visible reform; people clearly changed mobile use; policymakers touted it as a major integration success.

### Tension
A salient drop in cross-border communication costs should, in principle, make international travel easier—especially for short border trips. But it is unclear whether such digital frictions actually deter travel, or merely inconvenience people who were going to travel anyway.

### Resolution
Roaming abolition massively changed mobile behavior but had no detectable effect on foreign accommodation nights in border regions.

### Implications
The policy improved convenience and consumer surplus for existing travelers, but did not materially deepen physical cross-border integration. More broadly, removing a salient digital friction may not move extensive-margin real activity when deeper frictions remain.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
Yes, more than many papers do. The best element is the “big first stage, no downstream effect” contrast. That is a real narrative.

But the arc gets diluted because the paper tries to do too many things after the main result:
- reassure on every possible empirical concern,
- offer three mechanisms,
- draw broad lessons for EU integration,
- and position against multiple literatures.

The result is that the paper sometimes feels like a careful null trying to prove its worth from several angles rather than one tight story.

### What story should it be telling?
The core story should be:

> Roaming abolition was the perfect test of whether reducing a highly salient cross-border digital cost changes real mobility. It clearly changed phone use, but not travel. Therefore, the marginal digital friction was not the binding constraint on border-crossing behavior.

Everything should serve that story. In particular, the paper should resist turning into a general essay on all barriers to European integration. It has one elegant idea; it should protect it.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?
“EU roaming abolition caused data use to explode, but it didn’t increase cross-border overnight tourism.”

That is the line.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
Some would lean in, because the contrast is intrinsically interesting. It is a neat “behavioral response vs. real response” fact. But they will lean in only briefly unless the presenter quickly explains why this says something general about digital frictions and real economic activity.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Almost certainly:

**“But are overnight stays the wrong margin? Wouldn’t this affect day trips, navigation, and short border crossings more than hotel nights?”**

That is the central strategic challenge. It is the first question because the paper itself teaches the reader to ask it. And it is not a referee-level nitpick; it goes to whether the story is actually about the world or about a mismatched proxy.

### Is the null itself interesting?
Potentially yes. This is not a boring null if framed correctly. The null is interesting because:
- the policy was huge and salient,
- the first-stage behavioral response was enormous,
- and policymakers interpreted that salience as real integration.

That combination gives the null bite.

But the paper still needs to work harder to show that this is a **meaningful non-effect**, not simply “we looked in a place where the effect was unlikely to show up.” Right now it is on the border between those two interpretations.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

At present, this is a competent and thoughtful paper with a nice null. But in current form it is **not yet an AER paper**.

### What is the gap?

#### Mostly a scope problem, secondarily a framing problem.
- **Scope problem:** The outcome is too narrow and probably not the most policy-relevant margin. If the likely effect is on day trips or short-duration border movement, then hotel nights are an imperfect place to look.
- **Framing problem:** The paper has the seeds of a broader idea but still reads too much like a careful evaluation of one EU regulation.
- **Ambition problem:** The paper is intellectually sensible but safe. It stops at “overnight tourism didn’t move.” For AER, it needs either a better outcome, sharper mechanism evidence, or a broader integrative insight that feels definitive.

### What would excite the top 10 people in this field?
One of two things:

1. **A better measure of cross-border mobility** showing that the reform did or did not change actual border crossing behavior on the relevant margin; or
2. **A more persuasive general result** that salient digital-cost reductions often affect intensive use of existing cross-border activity rather than extensive-margin mobility.

Right now the paper hints at both but nails neither.

### Single most impactful advice
**Get data on actual border crossings or same-day/short-duration trips; if you can’t, radically reframe the paper as evidence about the limits of using digital-use responses as proxies for real integration, and stop implying that overnight stays fully test the integration claim.**

That is the one change that most raises the paper’s ceiling.
