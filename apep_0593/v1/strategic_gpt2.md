## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether abolishing EU mobile roaming charges in 2017 increased cross-border tourism. Its headline claim is that although mobile data usage exploded after “Roam Like at Home,” foreign overnight stays in border regions did not, suggesting that digital frictions were not a first-order barrier to physical mobility within Europe.

A busy economist should care because this is potentially a clean test of a broader question: when policymakers remove a highly salient, consumer-facing transaction cost, does behavior change on the margin that actually matters? More broadly, the paper speaks to whether digital-market integration translates into real economic integration.

**Does the paper articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Pretty well, but not optimally. The opening anecdote is vivid, and the second paragraph gets to the question quickly. But the current introduction still sounds a bit like “here is a policy and here is my estimate,” rather than “here is a general question in economics, and this policy delivers an unusually revealing answer.” The first two paragraphs should move faster from roaming to the larger stakes: how small, salient frictions map into real behavior, and whether digital integration can erode border effects.

**The pitch the paper should have:**

> The EU’s 2017 abolition of mobile roaming charges was one of the most visible reductions in cross-border consumer costs in recent decades, and it generated an immediate surge in phone use abroad. This paper asks whether removing that digital friction also increased actual cross-border travel.  
>  
> Using variation between border and interior European regions, I find that it did not: roaming abolition sharply changed how existing travelers used their phones, but not whether people took overnight trips across borders. The broader lesson is that highly salient digital costs can matter a lot for usage behavior while remaining largely irrelevant for deeper mobility and integration outcomes.

That is the AER version of the pitch: not “tourism paper about roaming,” but “a paper about which frictions matter for economic integration.”

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution in one sentence:**  
The paper’s contribution is to show that eliminating a salient cross-border digital charge changed communications behavior but did not measurably increase overnight cross-border tourism, implying that roaming costs were not a binding barrier to this form of mobility.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?
Somewhat, but not sharply enough. The paper distinguishes itself from telecom papers on roaming usage, switching, and market structure by studying a real-economy spillover. That is a real distinction. But it does less well differentiating itself from the vast literature on border frictions, salience, and transaction costs. Right now, the reader can see “this is the first tourism spillover paper on RLAH,” but may not yet see why that is an important intellectual advance rather than an application.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a gap in a literature?
It is mixed. The stronger parts frame the question as: **what kinds of barriers actually limit cross-border movement?** The weaker parts slip into: **no paper has tested this spillover yet.** The former is much stronger. “No one has studied this” is rarely enough for AER unless the answer is especially revealing.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?
Yes, but with some risk. A good reader could say: “It’s a paper showing that EU roaming abolition increased data use but not tourism, so digital frictions weren’t the binding constraint.” That is good. But another smart reader could also say: “It’s another DiD around a policy shock with a null on tourism.” That is the danger.

### What would make this contribution bigger?
Several possibilities:

1. **Shift the central estimand from tourism to mobility more broadly.**  
   Overnight accommodation is a narrow margin and, by the paper’s own admission, maybe not the one most likely to move. If the paper could bring in border crossings, day trips, mobile location flows, card transactions, rail/bus travel, or even destination searches, the claim would become much larger: not just “no hotel effect,” but “no mobility effect.”

2. **Lean harder into the wedge between intermediate and final outcomes.**  
   The 490% increase in data usage is not just context; it is the whole conceptual hook. The paper should be explicitly about why policies can dramatically move an intermediate margin without moving the final object of policy. That is bigger than tourism.

3. **Make the null economically interpretable, not just statistically null.**  
   The paper begins to do this with the “inframarginal” framing. It should push further: what class of models of salience, hassle costs, or border frictions would predict a large effect, and how does this result discipline those beliefs?

4. **Speak to integration policy design.**  
   The bigger contribution is not “RLAH had no spillover.” It is “consumer-visible digital integration is politically salient but may be economically shallow.” That is a stronger claim and one with broader policy interest.

If the paper could only enlarge itself along one dimension, it should be **from tourism to the economics of which frictions actually matter for cross-border mobility.**

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Before this paper: EU policymakers and the public treated roaming abolition as a landmark integration reform. It clearly reduced a salient cost of crossing borders and generated a massive increase in cross-border phone use.

### Tension
But it is unclear whether this was merely a convenience improvement for people who were already traveling, or whether it actually reduced a meaningful barrier to cross-border activity. The key puzzle is the wedge between a huge first-stage behavioral response and the possibility of little or no effect on the underlying real decision.

### Resolution
The paper finds no meaningful increase in foreign overnight stays in border regions after roaming abolition.

### Implications
Digital frictions may matter a lot for conditional usage behavior but little for the extensive-margin decision to travel. This suggests that the remaining border frictions inside Europe are deeper than telecom pricing and that policymakers may overread highly visible consumer reforms as drivers of integration.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
Yes, mostly. This is one of the paper’s strengths. It has a recognizable story: salient policy, huge telecom response, no tourism response, interpretation about deeper frictions.

But there is also some drift. Around the middle, the paper becomes a collection of supporting results and caveats. And the “Interpretations” section, while readable, is more essayistic than integrated into the core narrative.

### If it is a collection of results looking for a story, what story should it be telling?
The story should be:

1. **Policy removed a friction everyone could see.**
2. **People immediately changed phone behavior.**
3. **But they did not change travel behavior.**
4. **Therefore, visible digital frictions are not necessarily the frictions that govern real mobility.**

That is the whole paper. Everything else should serve that arc. Some current material feels like it wants to be a broad essay on European identity, cultural integration, and infrastructure gaps. That is plausible as implication, but not the central story.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?
“EU roaming abolition increased roaming data traffic by roughly fivefold, but apparently did not increase cross-border overnight tourism.”

That is a good line. It has contrast, surprise, and policy relevance.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
They would lean in initially. The contrast is interesting. The follow-up depends on how quickly the presenter can elevate it beyond “nice null in a regional tourism panel.”

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Probably one of these:
- “Maybe the effect is on day trips, not overnight stays?”
- “So does this mean salience matters for usage but not for travel decisions?”
- “What does this say about which barriers actually matter for EU integration?”

Those are healthy follow-up questions. They point to the paper’s opportunity: the first is a limitation, the latter two are the bigger contribution.

### If the findings are null or modest: is the null itself interesting?
Yes, **conditionally**. The null is interesting because the policy was salient, universal, and produced an enormous behavioral response on an adjacent margin. This is not a boring absence of effect from a weak intervention. It is a sharp non-result after a politically important, highly visible price change.

But the paper must keep making that case. A null result is not interesting because it is null; it is interesting because it rules out a plausible story that many policymakers and casual observers would have believed. The author should emphasize that more explicitly: **if even this policy did not move cross-border overnight travel, then not all friction removal is integration-relevant.**

Right now the paper makes this argument, but it sometimes sounds defensive rather than ambitious.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

At present, the main gap is **not basic competence**. The paper is organized, articulate, and knows what it found. The gap is mostly one of **framing and ambition**, with some **scope**.

### What is the main problem?

**Primarily a framing problem.**  
The science appears to support a potentially interesting claim, but the paper is still packaged as a narrow policy evaluation of EU roaming. AER wants the bigger economic question up front: what kinds of frictions matter for real integration, and what can we learn from the disconnect between obvious consumer response and absent mobility response?

**Secondarily a scope problem.**  
The outcome is narrow relative to the paper’s ambition. If the author wants to claim something broad about cross-border mobility or integration, overnight accommodation nights are a limiting proxy. The paper knows this and admits it. That self-awareness is good, but it also reveals the constraint.

**Less of a novelty problem than it might seem.**  
The policy setting is novel enough. The issue is not that the question has been answered too many times; it is that the current package risks feeling like “another credible policy null” instead of “an answer to an important economic question.”

**Also an ambition problem.**  
The current paper is careful and sensible. It is also somewhat safe. It does not fully exploit the conceptual opportunity created by the striking wedge between a giant intermediate response and zero final outcome response.

### The single most impactful piece of advice
**Reframe the paper around the disconnect between large responses in salient intermediate outcomes and no response in the real behavior policymakers care about, rather than around tourism per se.**

If the author can support that framing with even one better mobility margin—day trips, crossings, transaction data, location flows—the paper becomes much more ambitious. But even absent new data, the introduction, title, and discussion should make that the core contribution.

A title closer to the true contribution would help. The current title sounds like a niche tourism paper. Something like “Digital Frictions and Physical Mobility: Evidence from EU Roaming Abolition” would signal the larger stakes immediately.
