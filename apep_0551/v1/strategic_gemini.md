## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
The paper examines a fundamental measurement paradox in regulation: the administrative data used to evaluate safety (accident reports) are themselves generated by the enforcement process. Using a massive French industrial accident database, the author shows that a major regulatory expansion appears to increase accidents, but a severity decomposition reveals this is a mirage of better reporting (detection) rather than a failure of safety (deterrence).

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this well in the abstract, but the first two paragraphs of the introduction spend a bit too much time on the specifics of the 2001 Toulouse explosion. While the narrative hook is strong, the "General Interest" hook (the measurement problem) should be more prominent.

**The pitch the paper should have:** "Economists frequently evaluate regulatory effectiveness using administrative data—crime reports, environmental violations, or workplace injuries—that are endogenously determined by enforcement effort. This paper identifies a diagnostic for this 'detection-deterrence' conflation by exploiting a gradient of detection elasticity: while minor incidents are easily hidden or found by inspectors, severe and fatal accidents are 'detection-inelastic' and must be reported regardless of oversight. Applying this to a major expansion of the French industrial inspectorate, I show that what appears to be a rise in accidents is entirely driven by minor reports, while fatal outcomes remain flat, providing a structural template for interpreting regulatory data."

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**Contribution:** The paper identifies and demonstrates a "severity-gradient diagnostic" to separate the detection of violations from the deterrence of underlying harms in administrative data.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It is well-differentiated from Greenstone (2002) or Duflo et al. (2013) by shifting focus from "does this policy work?" to "how does the data itself betray the measurement of policy?"
*   **Question about the WORLD vs. LITERATURE:** Currently, it leans heavily on being a "measurement insight." It is framed more as a cautionary tale for researchers (Literature) than a definitive statement on French safety (World). 
*   **Clarity:** A smart economist would immediately grasp the "Minor vs. Fatal" logic. It avoids being "just another DiD" because the failure of parallel trends is used as a feature, not hidden as a bug.
*   **Scaling Up:** To make the contribution "AER-sized," the author needs to prove this isn't just a French ARIA database quirk. A "Comparison" section applying this logic to a second domain (e.g., OSHA data in the US or tax audits) would transform this from a specific case study into a universal law of regulatory economics.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
The paper sits at the intersection of public economics (enforcement) and econometrics (endogenous data).

*   **Closest Neighbors:** Gray & Scholz (1998) on OSHA; Shimshack & Ward (2007) on environmental deterrence; Chalfin & McCrary (2018) on crime reporting.
*   **Strategy:** The paper "synthesizes" these by providing the formal "severity gradient" framework that they often hint at but rarely formalize as a diagnostic tool.
*   **Missing Conversations:** The paper should speak more to the **Economics of Information**. There is a deep theoretical literature on "signal jamming" and "selective reporting" (e.g., Holmstrom) that would elevate the conceptual framework.
*   **Framing:** It is currently positioned slightly too narrowly as "Industrial Safety." It should be positioned as "The Political Economy of Administrative Records."

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC
*   **Setup:** Regulatory agencies use incident counts to justify budgets and measure success.
*   **Tension:** If more inspectors lead to more reports, a "successful" agency looks like a failure on paper.
*   **Resolution:** By splitting data into "hard" (fatal) and "soft" (near-miss) signals, we see that the "failure" is actually a sign of increased surveillance.
*   **Implications:** Policy evaluations that ignore the detection-elasticity of their outcome variables are likely biased toward finding null or negative effects.

**Evaluation:** The arc is exceptionally clear. The author is very honest about the "failure" of the causal DiD, which actually strengthens the narrative by pivoting the paper toward a deeper structural insight about data.
