## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a broad and important question: when regulators use administrative incident data to evaluate whether enforcement improved safety, are they measuring real safety or just improved detection? Using French industrial accident records around a major post-disaster enforcement expansion, the paper argues that increases in reported incidents are concentrated in minor events while severe and fatal accidents do not move, suggesting that enforcement-generated data can mechanically rise because reporting capacity improves.

A busy economist should care because this is not really a France paper; it is a paper about how much of empirical regulation work may be using outcomes that are endogenous to the enforcement process itself. If the argument is right, it travels well beyond industrial safety to environmental enforcement, tax compliance, workplace safety, policing, and any domain where “the government sees more” is confounded with “there is more to see.”

### Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Fairly well, but not optimally. The current introduction opens with Toulouse/AZF, which is vivid, but it delays the main intellectual hook. The paper’s real selling point is not the French reform; it is the general measurement problem in enforcement-generated data and a practical diagnostic based on severity. Right now the introduction still reads partly like a policy-evaluation paper that later retreats into “actually this is a measurement paper.” That creates whiplash.

### What should the first two paragraphs say instead?

The paper should lead with the general problem, then use France as the laboratory.

**Pitch the paper should have:**

> Economists routinely evaluate regulation using administrative counts of detected violations, incidents, or accidents. But these outcomes are often generated by the enforcement process itself: more inspectors, better reporting systems, or stronger compliance obligations can raise recorded incidents even when underlying harm falls. This creates a fundamental measurement problem in empirical work on deterrence.
>
> This paper shows how to diagnose that problem using a simple but portable idea: compare outcomes that are detection-elastic to outcomes that are detection-inelastic. Using France’s industrial accident database around a large post-AZF enforcement expansion, I show that increases in reported incidents are concentrated entirely in minor events that are easy to miss and newly likely to be recorded, while severe and fatal accidents do not differentially change. The contribution is not a causal estimate of the French reform; it is a general diagnostic for when enforcement-generated administrative data are measuring detection rather than safety.

That is a much stronger AER-facing opening than beginning as a quasi-case study of French industrial regulation.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Researchers and policymakers use recorded incidents to assess whether enforcement improves safety.

### Tension

But recorded incidents are endogenous to enforcement: more monitoring can increase what is observed even if true harm falls. So the standard outcome variable may be contaminated in exactly the settings where we most want to study deterrence.

### Resolution

In French industrial accident data, the increase in reported incidents appears only for minor events, not for severe or fatal ones. This pattern suggests that improved reporting/detection, not worsening safety, is driving the increase in observed incidents.

### Implications

Empirical evaluations based on enforcement-generated counts need to distinguish detection-elastic from detection-inelastic outcomes. More broadly, outcome choice in regulation papers is not a housekeeping issue; it determines whether the paper is measuring surveillance or safety.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has one, but it is fighting with another narrative.

There are two competing stories in the manuscript:

1. **Story A:** Did France’s post-AZF regulatory expansion improve industrial safety?
2. **Story B:** Enforcement-generated data pose a measurement problem, and severity can diagnose it.

Story B is stronger and more portable. Story A is the bait, but the paper eventually tells the reader that it cannot really answer Story A cleanly. That weakens momentum.

The solution is not to hide the French institutional context; it is to subordinate it. France should be the setting that reveals the more general point, not the main event.

The paper should be telling this story:
- We often cannot trust reported incident counts in regulated settings.
- Here is a simple conceptual reason why.
- Here is an empirical diagnostic.
- Here is a clean illustration in an important regulatory setting.
- Here is how this changes practice in applied work.

Right now the paper still reads too much like a causal policy-evaluation paper that has sensibly downgraded its claims. For AER positioning, it should instead read like a measurement paper with a strong empirical demonstration.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?

“I have a paper showing that when regulation expands, the government records more industrial accidents—but only the minor ones. The severe and fatal ones don’t move. So the increase in the headline count mostly looks like better detection, not worse safety.”

That is a real hook.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

Many would lean in, especially empirical IO, public, labor, environmental, and development economists. The core idea is intuitive and generalizable. But they will lean in only if it is presented as a broad measurement insight, not as a niche France case.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Probably one of these:
- “How general is this beyond industrial safety?”
- “Can severity really be treated as detection-inelastic?”
- “Do you have another setting or validation showing the same pattern?”
- “What should empirical papers do differently after reading this?”

Those are good questions; they indicate the paper has an idea, not just a result.

### If the findings are modest or partially null, is the null interesting?

Yes. Here the null is not “nothing happened.” The interesting object is the divergence between measured minor incidents and severe outcomes. The paper does make this case, and that is a strength.

But it should lean even harder into why this is valuable knowledge. The insight is not “the policy didn’t work.” It is “your data may be designed to overstate failure or activity because surveillance changed what enters the dataset.” That is useful even if the headline causal effect is unresolved.

This is one of those cases where the null is interesting if and only if it is embedded in a bigger framework. Standing alone, “no effect on severe accidents” is modest. As part of a diagnosis of endogenous measurement, it is much more interesting.
