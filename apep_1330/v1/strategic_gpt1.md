## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper studies the largest emergency transfer ever made to U.S. colleges: the $76 billion Higher Education Emergency Relief Fund during COVID. Using the formula that allocated funds based largely on pre-pandemic Pell exposure, it asks whether institutions that received more aid used it to lower prices and preserve student enrollment, and reports a striking pattern: little pass-through to tuition or net price, and larger enrollment declines after the funds expired.

A busy economist should care because this is, in principle, a broad question about temporary public subsidies to distressed institutions: do emergency transfers stabilize socially valuable activity, or merely postpone adjustment?

**Does the paper itself articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Only partly. The current opening is energetic, but it gets to the interesting economic question only indirectly, and too quickly slides into the paper's preferred interpretation (“windfall trap”) before earning it. The first paragraphs should lead with the general issue—how temporary aid affects institutions and beneficiaries when the aid is formula-driven and short-lived—not with the rhetorical juxtaposition of aid and later enrollment decline.

### The pitch the paper should have

> Governments often respond to crises by sending large temporary transfers to institutions rather than directly to final beneficiaries. Whether such transfers stabilize activity or simply delay adjustment is a first-order question for public finance, education policy, and crisis design.  
>   
> This paper studies that question in the context of the $76 billion Higher Education Emergency Relief Fund, the largest emergency subsidy in the history of U.S. higher education. Exploiting the pre-pandemic Pell-based allocation formula, I show that colleges receiving more relief did not meaningfully reduce tuition or net price, and experienced sharper enrollment declines once the aid ended. The central implication is that temporary institutional relief may cushion budgets without building durable student demand.

That is the AER version of the story. It elevates the paper from “COVID college policy paper” to “what temporary institutional subsidies do.”

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:**  
The paper claims to show that a massive, formula-driven emergency subsidy to colleges was largely not passed through to students and did not durably stabilize enrollment, with the strongest enrollment losses appearing after the subsidy expired.

### Is this clearly differentiated from the closest papers?
Not enough. Right now the introduction reads like a blend of three familiar literatures—Bennett hypothesis/incidence of higher-ed aid, flypaper effect, and COVID higher-ed disruption—without clearly showing what is new relative to each.

The contribution is *potentially* differentiated in two ways:
1. **Scale and policy context:** this is the largest emergency aid episode in higher education history.
2. **Dynamic claim:** temporary institutional aid may create a post-subsidy cliff rather than durable stabilization.

The paper does not yet sharply separate itself from existing work on subsidy incidence. “Aid did not lower tuition much” is not new. The genuinely new claim is the **dynamic non-persistence / post-expiration decline**. That should be the centerpiece.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a gap in a literature?
Mixed, but too often it slips into literature-gap framing. The stronger world question is:

- When governments send temporary emergency money to institutions, does it protect beneficiaries in a lasting way?

That is much better than:
- There is no causal evidence on HEERF.

The latter is true but not top-journal interesting by itself.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?
Right now, maybe not cleanly. They might say: “It’s another quasi-experimental paper on higher-ed aid; it finds little tuition pass-through and some negative enrollment effect.” That is not enough.

You want them to say:
- “It studies whether temporary institutional relief actually stabilizes student outcomes after the crisis. In this case, it seems not—it may even have postponed decline.”

### What would make this contribution bigger?
Very specifically:

1. **Make the outcome of interest more central and more fundamental.**  
   Enrollment is fine, but the bigger contribution would be stronger with:
   - persistence/retention,
   - first-time enrollment,
   - student composition,
   - transfer-out behavior,
   - closure/program cuts,

   - institutional finances after aid expiration.

2. **Show the mechanism directly, not rhetorically.**  
   The “windfall trap” language is vivid, but currently it is mostly interpretation. To make the contribution feel bigger, the paper needs evidence that high-exposure schools:
   - used funds for revenue replacement rather than student-facing investments,
   - expanded spending categories that did not build long-run demand,
   - delayed adjustment in staffing/program mix/online capacity,
   - saw no improvement in net price or student support measures.

3. **Frame the comparison more powerfully.**  
   The key comparison is not “high-HEERF vs low-HEERF institutions” per se. It is:
   - **temporary transfers to institutions vs durable reductions in student cost or improved resilience.**

4. **Reduce emphasis on tuition if it is weak.**

### How should the paper position itself relative to those neighbors?
**Build on them, not attack them.** The right message is:

- Relative to the higher-ed incidence literature, this paper studies an unusually large, temporary, formula-driven institutional subsidy rather than marginal changes in aid availability.
- Relative to flypaper-effect papers, it adds a dynamic beneficiary outcome: not just where money sticks, but whether temporary institutional aid creates durable protection for final users.
- Relative to COVID higher-ed papers, it brings a policy-design lens rather than simply documenting pandemic disruption.

That is a coherent conversation.

### Is the paper currently positioned too narrowly or too broadly?
Paradoxically, both.

- **Too narrowly** in the sense that it is very tied to HEERF-specific institutional detail.
- **Too broadly** in the sense that it gestures at three literatures without deeply claiming a home in any one of them.

The best positioning is: **a public finance paper about temporary institutional subsidies, using higher education as the setting.**

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?
It needs to speak more to:
- crisis-response transfers to institutions,
- dynamic effects of temporary subsidies,
- organizational adjustment under temporary fiscal relief,
- public finance work on grants with earmarks that are functionally fungible,
- possibly education finance work outside higher education.

Right now the paper’s conversation is a bit too confined to higher-ed subsidy incidence plus a generic flypaper citation set.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?
Not quite. The most impactful framing is probably **not** “this is more evidence for the Bennett hypothesis.” That is too small and too familiar.

The more interesting conversation is:
- **What do temporary institutional bailouts accomplish?**
- **Do earmarked emergency funds protect intended beneficiaries, or mostly smooth institutional budgets?**
- **Can temporary relief worsen the eventual adjustment cliff?**

That is the conversation AER readers might care about.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
During COVID, the federal government sent an extraordinary amount of temporary aid to colleges, with the explicit goal of stabilizing institutions and helping students.

### Tension
It is unclear whether such aid actually benefits students in a lasting way, especially when it is routed through institutions and expires quickly. The puzzle is whether temporary institutional relief builds resilience or simply masks underlying weakness.

### Resolution
The paper reports little evidence of price pass-through and finds that higher-exposure institutions experienced larger enrollment declines after the aid period ended.

### Implications
Temporary, formula-based institutional aid may keep institutions afloat in the short run without durably protecting the students the program was intended to serve.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
There is a **serviceable arc**, but the paper currently overreaches in the resolution. “Windfall trap” is a strong narrative label, but the evidence presented is thinner than the label suggests. The paper has a pattern; it does not yet fully have a mechanism-backed story.

At the moment it feels somewhat like **a collection of results seeking a big interpretation**:
- small tuition result,
- enrollment decline,
- completion decline,
- event study concentrated in 2022,
- heterogeneity by sector.

The story it *should* be telling is more disciplined:

> HEERF was intended to stabilize students through institutions. The key facts are that colleges did not substantially lower student prices, and any apparent stabilization did not persist past the subsidy window. The contribution is less “aid caused dependency” than “temporary institutional aid did not translate into durable student protection.”

That is cleaner, more defensible, and more important.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?
I would say:

> “The $76 billion COVID relief program for colleges appears to have done almost nothing to lower tuition or net price, and the institutions that got more of it saw the sharpest enrollment drop once the money ran out.”

That is a good opening fact. People would at least look up.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
They would **lean in initially**, because the setting is huge and the headline is counterintuitive. But the next question comes immediately:

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Almost certainly:
- “Why?”
Then:
- “Is that because schools used the money to patch budgets instead of helping students?”
- “Was the effect really at large institutions only?”
- “Is this about levels rather than proportional decline?”
- “Does this tell us about HEERF specifically, or temporary bailouts generally?”

And that is where the paper currently weakens. It has a provocative fact but not yet a fully persuasive strategic answer to the obvious follow-up.

### If findings are modest or null
The tuition result is modest, and by itself not interesting enough. But the paper’s value does not depend on a large tuition effect. The interesting null is:
- even with a huge transfer and student-aid earmarks, student prices barely moved.

That *is* valuable, if framed as evidence on institutional intermediation and fungibility under emergency aid.

The paper should make clearer that the null is informative:
- The policy was explicitly justified in part as helping students.
- If the student-facing price measures barely moved, that is not a failed paper; it is a substantive policy result.
