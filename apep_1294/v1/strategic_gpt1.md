## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether giving India’s Scheduled Tribes more political representation changes the balance between economic development and forest conservation. Using the 2008 redrawing of electoral boundaries, it argues that areas with greater tribal representation experienced slower growth in economic activity, as proxied by night lights, but less forest loss—a “guardian effect” in which representation protects ecologically valuable land.

A busy economist should care because this is, in principle, a big question about what political representation does in the world: not just who gets transfers, but whether representation changes the objective function of the state, trading off growth against environmental preservation.

### Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

No. The current opening starts with a broad, plausible topic, but the second paragraph immediately pivots to “the challenge is identification.” That is too early and too methodological. The introduction should first establish the substantive question and the stakes: representation, indigenous communities, and the development-conservation frontier. Right now, the paper sounds like it is leading with an empirical design in search of an application.

Worse, the actual empirical contribution is more contingent and more modest than the abstract suggests. The paper’s true claim is not “tribal representation protects forests” full stop; it is something closer to: “after delimitation, high-ST places stopped converging as fast in night lights, and at broader geographic aggregation forest loss declined.” That can still be interesting, but it needs to be framed with more discipline.

### What the first two paragraphs should say instead

Something like:

> Across the developing world, governments face a recurring tradeoff between economic expansion and conservation in regions inhabited by indigenous communities. A central unresolved question is whether giving those communities formal political representation changes policy choices on that frontier: do elected representatives from historically marginalized, forest-dependent populations push the state toward faster local development, greater environmental protection, or some combination of the two?
>
> This paper studies that question in India, where Scheduled Tribes are heavily concentrated in the country’s forested regions and where electoral institutions explicitly reserve some legislative seats for tribal candidates. I show that the 2008 delimitation, which mechanically reshuffled reserved constituencies, was followed by slower growth in economic activity in more tribal districts but lower forest loss in more tribal states. The findings suggest that tribal political representation can shift policy toward conservation, revealing a development-conservation tradeoff embedded in democratic representation.

That is the paper’s best version of itself. World question first; institutional setting second; headline finding third.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper claims that expanding Scheduled Tribe political representation in India shifted local policy priorities toward forest conservation and away from rapid economic expansion, revealing a development-conservation tradeoff created by descriptive representation.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?

Only partially. The introduction cites some natural neighbors, but the differentiation is not yet sharp enough. As currently written, the contribution risks sounding like a familiar combination of: representation affects policy priorities; indigenous communities care about forests; here is a quasi-experiment from India. That is not enough for AER unless the paper is crystal clear about what is new.

The paper needs to distinguish itself from at least four adjacent contributions:

1. **Political reservation changes policy allocation**  
   e.g. Pande (2003), Chattopadhyay and Duflo (2004), Besley et al.  
   What is new here is not “representation matters,” but “representation can change the growth/conservation margin.”

2. **Political incentives shape deforestation**  
   e.g. Burgess et al. (Indonesia), and broader environmental political economy work.  
   What is new here should be: the political actor is not rent-seeking politicians or decentralization alone, but representatives of forest-dependent minorities.

3. **Indigenous rights / community governance / conservation**  
   There is a large literature, not all in mainstream economics, on indigenous land rights and forest outcomes. The paper cannot act as though the idea that politically empowered forest communities conserve more is new to the world. It needs to say what is new in this setting: electoral representation rather than land titling alone; a national institutional shock; explicit tradeoff with development indicators.

4. **India delimitation papers**  
   If delimitation has already been used to study political outcomes, then the differentiator must be the question and consequence studied—not the design itself.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or filling a gap in a LITERATURE?

It tries to do both, but often slips into literature-gap mode and design mode. The strongest version is clearly a world question:

- When politically empowered, do indigenous communities redirect the state toward conservation even at the expense of measured economic growth?

That is stronger than:
- “The effect of ST reservation on forests is unknown.”

The latter is a literature gap. The former is a first-order political economy question.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?

Not confidently. Right now, a smart economist might say: “It’s a DiD/trend-break paper on India delimitation showing tribal districts had slower night-light convergence and maybe less deforestation.” That is not yet a memorable contribution.

The introduction spends too much effort explaining why the naive DiD is wrong and too little effort making the substantive novelty vivid. The “guardian effect” label is helpful rhetorically, but it risks overstating the evidence unless more carefully defined.

### What would make the contribution bigger?

Most importantly: **tighten the object of interest**. The paper currently bundles together three ideas:

- representation,
- conservation,
- slower development.

For a top-journal contribution, it needs to persuade the reader that these belong to one coherent phenomenon, not just two parallel reduced-form findings.

Specific ways to make it bigger:

1. **Sharper mechanism outcomes**  
   If the paper’s story is that tribal representation shifts policy toward forest protection, then outcomes closer to the margin of state choice would help conceptually: mining permits, land conversion, road expansion through forest land, FRA claim processing, protected area enforcement, or local public goods composition. Even if referees assess identification later, from an editorial standpoint the current mechanism is too inferential.

2. **A cleaner “policy bundle” framing**  
   Rather than “night lights down, deforestation down,” the paper could ask whether representation changes the composition of development. For example: less extractive growth, more rights-based governance. That is a bigger and more original story.

3. **More direct treatment variation**  
   The paper itself admits the district ST share is a proxy for actual reservation changes. That is a major strategic weakness, because it blurs whether the paper is about political representation or simply about tribal places. Editorially, this matters enormously for contribution clarity.

4. **A stronger comparison class**  
   The SC contrast is useful, but the framing could be bigger if the paper explicitly asked: why does representation for one marginalized group preserve environmental assets while representation for another does not? That comparative angle is interesting.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

### Closest neighbors

Likely closest neighbors include:

- **Pande (2003)** on political reservation and redistribution in India.
- **Chattopadhyay and Duflo (2004)** on reservation and policy priorities.
- **Besley, Pande, Rao, and others** on political selection/representation and public goods.
- **Burgess et al. (2012)** on the political economy of deforestation in Indonesia.
- **Papers on India delimitation**, perhaps Iyer et al. or related work on redistricting/reservation effects.
- Potentially **Gulzar and coauthors** if there is work on the Forest Rights Act or local political institutions in India.
- Outside core economics, there is likely a large literature on **indigenous land rights and conservation** in development/environmental economics and political science.

### How should the paper position itself relative to those neighbors?

Mostly **build on and bridge**, not attack.

The right move is:
- Build on reservation papers by showing that representation can alter policy objectives, not just transfer distribution.
- Build on deforestation papers by showing that who holds political power matters for environmental outcomes.
- Bridge to indigenous-rights and environmental-governance literatures by emphasizing the joint role of representation and legal rights.

The wrong move would be to imply that prior work has ignored the possibility that marginalized groups conserve resources. That would feel uninformed.

### Is the paper positioned too narrowly or too broadly?

At present it is oddly both.

- **Too narrowly** in the sense that it is anchored to a very specific Indian institutional event and a specific coding of ST share × post-2008.
- **Too broadly** in the abstract rhetoric, where it sounds like it has established a general theorem about tribal representation and conservation.

The paper needs a more disciplined middle ground:
- “In a nationally important institutional setting, electoral representation for indigenous communities appears to shift local outcomes toward conservation.”

That is important without pretending to settle everything.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?

It seems under-engaged with at least three areas:

1. **Indigenous rights and conservation**  
   Including economics, political science, environmental governance, and interdisciplinary work.

2. **Resource politics / extraction / land-use change**  
   If forest conservation is the mechanism, the paper should sit in the literature on extraction, infrastructure, mining, and state capacity in frontier regions.

3. **Representation as changing policy objectives rather than competence or rents**  
   There is a larger political economy literature on descriptive representation, citizen-candidate models, and identity-based policy preferences. The paper could draw more on that conceptual frame.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?

Not fully. Right now it is having a somewhat generic “representation affects outcomes” conversation. The more interesting conversation is:

- **What happens when democratic inclusion empowers groups whose welfare is poorly measured by standard development proxies?**

That is a much richer AER-type framing. It ties together representation, measurement, indigenous welfare, and environmental tradeoffs.

In other words, the unexpected but fruitful connection is between:
- political representation,
- environmental political economy,
- and the economics of measurement/welfare.

Night lights are not welfare. The paper almost gets to that point in the discussion, but only defensively. It should be front and center.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

India’s Scheduled Tribes are concentrated in forest-rich areas. The state reserves some legislative seats for ST candidates. These regions sit on a classic frontier between development and conservation.

### Tension

We do not know whether giving tribal communities political voice leads the state to deliver more conventional development, to protect forests, or to reallocate policy toward a different objective altogether. The 2008 delimitation provides institutional movement in representation, but standard comparisons are contaminated by strong convergence patterns in tribal areas.

### Resolution

The paper finds that after delimitation, high-ST areas experienced slower night-light convergence and, in broader forest analyses, lower forest loss. It interprets this as evidence that tribal representation acts as a “guardian,” shifting policy toward conservation.

### Implications

Representation may do more than redistribute spending: it may change the state’s objective function. Democratic inclusion of indigenous communities can reshape the development-conservation frontier, with implications for redistricting, land rights, and how economists measure welfare.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the ingredients, but not the discipline. At present it reads partly as:
- a paper about invalidating naive DiD,
- a paper about a trend-break,
- a paper about forest conservation,
- a paper about the FRA,
- a paper about SC placebo contrasts.

That is too many semi-stories.

### Is it a collection of results looking for a story?

Somewhat, yes.

The paper should choose one central story and subordinate everything else to it. The right story is:

> Political representation for indigenous communities can shift state choices toward conservation, and standard development proxies may therefore understate welfare gains from representation.

Everything else should support that:
- Delimitation = institutional shock.
- Night lights = evidence of slower conventional growth.
- Forest loss = evidence of conservation.
- SC placebo = group-specific rather than generic reservation effect.
- FRA = plausible institutional complement.

That is coherent. Right now the paper sometimes sounds as though the main contribution is the econometric correction from DiD to trend-break. That is not an AER story.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?

I would say:

> “In India, when tribal political representation expanded, tribal areas appear to have grown more slowly in night lights but lost less forest—suggesting democratic inclusion can move policy toward conservation rather than conventional growth.”

That is the arresting fact.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

They would lean in—briefly—because the underlying question is excellent. But the follow-up would come immediately:

- “Do you really know it’s representation, as opposed to just tribal areas trending differently?”
- “Is night lights the right welfare metric here?”
- “Can you actually observe the reservation changes?”
- “Is this about the Forest Rights Act rather than delimitation?”

Those are not referee-style technical quibbles; they are strategic questions about whether the paper has really isolated the idea it wants to sell. Right now, the paper does not fully preempt them in framing terms.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Most likely:

> “What exactly changed on the ground—less mining, fewer roads, more FRA claims, different spending priorities?”

That is the missing middle of the paper. The current mechanism is suggestive but too abstract.

### If findings are modest: is the modesty itself interesting?

The forest result is potentially interesting even if modest; the night-lights result is trickier. “Slower convergence” is not naturally exciting on its own. It becomes interesting only if the reader believes it captures a real policy tradeoff rather than a measurement artifact or a generic slowdown.

So the paper must make the case that learning “representation changes the composition of development, not necessarily its pace” is valuable. Otherwise it will feel like a paper whose main result is “the obvious DiD was wrong, and the corrected result is smaller and harder to interpret.”

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

### What would make it read better?

2. **Cut the extended discussion of the naive DiD from the introduction**

4. **Front-load the paired outcome structure**

5. **Be much more disciplined with the “guardian effect” label**
   It is memorable, but it currently overstates certainty. Use it as a suggestive organizing phrase, not as though it were already an accepted causal object.

6. **Shorten the limitations in the discussion but take the biggest one more seriously earlier**
   The most consequential issue is not a minor caveat—it is that the paper uses district ST share as a proxy for reservation changes. That should be acknowledged in the framing much earlier because it defines what the paper can honestly claim.

7. **Conclusion should do more than summarize**
   The current discussion/conclusion is partly summary plus caveats. A stronger ending would say:
   - this changes how we think about descriptive representation,
   - this cautions against using standard growth metrics as sufficient welfare measures in indigenous regions,
   - and this matters for redistricting and rights-based governance more broadly.

### Are good results buried?

Yes. The truly interesting result is the juxtaposition of slower measured development and lower deforestation. That should be presented as the headline architecture of the paper. Instead, the reader first gets pulled into the convergence problem and trend-break specification.

### Is the paper front-loaded with the good stuff?

Not enough. The good stuff is there, but it is buried under identification and specification language.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

### What is the gap between current form and an AER-level paper?

Mainly three things.

#### 1. Framing problem
The science may or may not survive review, but editorially the story is not yet being told at the right level. The paper should be about how representation changes policy objectives and how standard development metrics miss part of the welfare calculus in indigenous regions. Right now it is too much about a particular Indian redistricting event and a corrected DiD.

#### 2. Scope problem
The outcomes are still too thin to support the ambitious interpretation. Night lights plus forest loss is an intriguing start, but not enough to make the broader “guardian effect” feel fully convincing as a major field-defining contribution. The paper needs at least one more layer of outcomes closer to policy choice or mechanism.

#### 3. Novelty/precision problem
The paper’s own discussion admits that it does not observe the treatment in the ideal way: actual constituency-level switches are not the main empirical object. That weakens the contribution strategically because the title and framing promise a paper about political representation, while the current design is closer to a paper about high-ST places around a national institutional change.

### Is it framing, scope, novelty, or ambition?

Mostly **framing plus scope**, with some **novelty risk**.

The question is ambitious enough. The problem is that the evidence, as framed, supports a more modest claim than the title and abstract want to make. That mismatch is dangerous at AER.

### Single most impactful piece of advice

**Reframe the paper around a bigger and sharper question—whether democratic inclusion changes the objective function of the state in indigenous regions—and then reorganize the evidence to show a coherent policy tradeoff rather than leading with a repaired DiD design.**

If I were allowed a corollary, it would be: get as close as possible to actual reservation changes or policy-margin outcomes, because without that the paper will keep sounding one step removed from its own central claim.
