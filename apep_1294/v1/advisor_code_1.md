**Idea Fidelity**

The paper’s main thrust — estimating whether mechanical increases in Scheduled Tribe (ST) constituency reservation slowed economic convergence in favor of forest conservation — does align broadly with the original idea’s motivation. However, the implementation diverges in several consequential ways. The original manifest emphasized a constituency-level difference-in-differences exploiting the 2008 rotation of ST reservation status (four groups: newly reserved, de-reserved, always reserved, never reserved) using the SHRUG constituency crosswalk and high-resolution Hansen forest data aggregated to those constituencies. The submitted paper instead works at the district level, uses the pre-determined ST population share as the “treatment intensity,” and focuses on trend-breaking convergence in nightlights, interpreting the Delimitation as a deceleration of convergence rather than a direct change in representation status. While Hansen data appear in the paper, they are aggregated to state-level ST shares rather than the original constituency-level variation, and the direct DiD identifying variation from reservation “switchers” is absent. Consequently, although the guardian effect narrative is in spirit similar, the empirical setup has departed enough from the manifest’s promised identification strategy and data design to require reassessment.

**Summary**

The paper studies India’s 2008 Delimitation to argue that expanding ST political representation slowed economic convergence (measured with district-level DMSP nightlights) while conserving forests (measured with Hansen Global Forest Change). A trend-break specification captures a deceleration in convergence following the delimitation, which the author interprets as the “guardian effect,” supported by forest-loss reductions in high-ST states and an SC placebo showing different patterns. The claim is that ST representatives, empowered by the delimitation and the contemporaneous Forest Rights Act, prioritized forest preservation over rapid measurable economic activity, creating a development-conservation trade-off.

---

**Essential Points**

1. **Lack of the promised constituency-level quasi-experiment:** The paper does not implement the constituency-level DiD outlined in the manifest, instead relying on district-level ST shares and a linear trend-break approach. Because the identifying variation now comes from continuous differences in ST share rather than discrete reservation switches, the causal interpretation is much weaker. The key institutional leverage from constituencies that switched reservation status — and thus the opportunity to compare newly reserved versus de-reserved versus always/never reserved districts — is not exploited. Without that, the paper risks confounding ST share with other characteristics and the mechanical nature of the 2008 reservation reassignment is underutilized.

2. **Credibility of trend-break identification:** The preferred specification assumes that the pre-2008 convergence trend would have continued linearly absent the delimitation. This is a strong assumption when the event coincides with multiple national programs (MGNREGA, FRA, infrastructure spending, global financial crisis) and when the delimitation itself also changed constituency boundaries (which may influence development via population or infrastructure reallocation). The author needs to provide stronger evidence that no other shocks coincided with 2008 differentially across ST concentration, for example by showing parallel trends in relevant covariates or by exploiting falsification exercises (e.g., placebo break years outside 2008) that extend beyond the 2004 test already reported. Without a clear demonstration that the trend break reflects the delimitation rather than other contemporaneous initiatives or boundary effects, the guardian effect interpretation remains speculative.

3. **Measurement mismatch and policy mechanism clarity:** The ST share is aggregated at the district level while reservation occurs at the assembly constituency level. Districts typically encompass multiple constituencies with heterogeneous reservation status; some 2008 delimitation effects may cancel out when aggregated. This ecological aggregation likely attenuates the treatment but also raises concerns about what exactly is being estimated. If possible, the authors should construct actual constituency-level panel data (as promised in the manifest) and reconcile it with district-level outcomes, or, at minimum, provide evidence that district-level ST share is a valid proxy for the number of ST-reserved constituencies gained or lost in each district.

If these essential issues cannot be substantially addressed, the paper does not yet deliver the credible empirical foundation required for acceptance.

**Suggestions**

1. **Implement the constituency-level DiD that the manifest promises.** Construct panel data for assembly constituencies (using the SHRUG constituency crosswalk or direct Election Commission data) and classify constituencies into the four groups: newly ST-reserved, de-reserved, always reserved, and never reserved. Re-estimate the key outcomes (nightlights and forest loss) at that level, so the identifying variation hinges directly on reservation rotation rather than district-level ST share. This would simultaneously recover the original policy variation, reduce measurement error, and align the empirical strategy with the manifest’s substantive claim. If district-level outcomes are still preferred, use a weighted average of constituency-level treatment statuses within each district to better capture the actual reservation shock.

2. **Bolster the trend-break assumption with falsification and heterogeneity checks.** Beyond the 2004 placebo, explore alternative “fake” break years (e.g., 2003, 2005) to demonstrate that only 2008 produces the observed deceleration, and show that districts with similar histories but less exposure to ST representation do not experience the break. Additionally, consider including time-varying controls (or interacting state-specific linear trends with pre-2008 ST share) to account for other national programs like MGNREGA, Power for All, or increases in Forest Rights Act enforcement that could correlate with ST concentration. Presenting supporting evidence that the trend would have continued absent 2008 (e.g., by extrapolating pre-2008 trends for subgroups) would make the guardian effect interpretation more compelling.

3. **Clarify and strengthen the mechanism linking ST representation to forest preservation.** The paper invokes the Forest Rights Act (FRA) but does not directly show that ST legislators were instrumental in its enforcement. Incorporate data on FRA claims or approvals, forest office staffing, or mining approvals (if available) to illustrate that ST-reserved constituencies indeed shifted enforcement behavior. If such data are unavailable, at least show heterogeneity: did districts/states with stronger FRA machinery or larger forest shares experience larger guardian effects? This would help establish that the deceleration in nightlight convergence indeed stems from conscious conservation choices rather than some other unobserved channel.

4. **Tighten the SC placebo interpretation.** The SC placebo is used to argue that delimitation effects are not generic, yet the SC results show a negative coefficient while ST is positive. However, this comparison may conflate differential mobility or program targeting between SC and ST areas. Consider presenting the opposite: compare newly SC-reserved versus de-reserved constituencies (if using constituency-level data) to see if similar convergence shifts occur, and test whether the ST effect persists after controlling for concurrent SC-driven trends. Alternatively, leverage triple differences (ST × Post × Forest dependency) to isolate the conservation channel more cleanly.

5. **Extend outcomes and time frame where feasible.** The nightlights panel ends in 2013, leaving only a few years of post-delimitation data. If more recent VIIRS nightlights (2012–present) are available, extend the analysis to capture longer-term effects, especially given that forest outcomes continue through 2023. Likewise, consider adding other proxies for economic or environmental change (e.g., land cover transition, mining approvals, electrification measures) to assess whether the guardian effect appears across multiple dimensions.

6. **Explicitly model the conservation-development trade-off narrative.** The current presentation suggests that forest preservation necessarily slows nightlight convergence, but it would strengthen the argument to quantify this trade-off, e.g., by showing that areas with larger forest loss declines also show larger convergence slowdowns. Presenting a mediation analysis or interaction between ST representation and baseline forest cover would corroborate that the guardian effect is indeed operating through conservation, not through unrelated policy shifts.

7. **Address standard errors and clustering concerns.** District-level clustering may understate the true uncertainty given that treatment variation stems from reservation rotation shared across multiple districts within states. Consider clustering at the level of treatment assignment (e.g., assembly constituency or district groupings) or using wild cluster bootstrapping, especially for the forest regressions where the sample is smaller (449 districts). Reporting such robustness checks will reassure readers that the key coefficients remain statistically significant under alternative inference assumptions.

8. **Improve exposition of the trend-break model.** The current Equation (1) and its interpretation would benefit from a clearer explanation of how the differential convergence rate is identified (for readers unfamiliar with trend-break DiD). A figure plotting the fitted pre-2008 trend along with the post-2008 realized convergence for high- and low-ST districts could make the intuition transparent. Additionally, discussing why a level break specification fails due to pre-trends—perhaps with a visual comparison between the naive DiD and the trend-break fit—would clarify the necessity of the preferred model.

By implementing these suggestions, the paper would better align with its original manifest, sharpen its causal claim, and more convincingly demonstrate the guardian effect.
