**Idea Fidelity**  
The paper diverges substantially from the manifest. The original proposal centered on an IV-DiD strategy that exploited cross-force variation in pre-2010 council-tax precept shares interacted with the post-2010 austerity period to isolate plausibly exogenous shocks to officer strength. The submitted paper instead estimates within-force two-way FE models over 2014–2021 (and longer panels for some specifications) with no instrument, no use of pre-2010 precept shares, and no explicit linkage to the variation highlighted in the manifest. As a result, the key identification strategy, instrument, and argument about “precept insulation” from austerity are entirely absent, meaning the paper does not pursue the original idea it set out to implement.

**Summary**  
This paper documents that, across 42 police forces in England and Wales, reductions in officer strength during the austerity years are associated with lower charge rates for recorded crimes. The author interprets within-force variation in officer FTE as causal and reports large, robust point estimates—especially for investigation-intensive offenses—suggesting that austerity degraded the criminal justice system’s capacity to pursue offenders. Extensions include disaggregating by offense type and showing robustness to various subsamples and controls.

**Essential Points**  
1. **Endogeneity of Officer Variation.** The identifying assumption relies entirely on within-force FE, which is unlikely to deliver causal estimates without stronger justification. Officer cuts may correlate with contemporaneous shocks to crime, resources, or political priorities (e.g., local PCC decisions, central directives) that simultaneously affect charge rates. The manifest’s precept-based IV was designed to address exactly this concern. Without it, the FE estimates may simply capture omitted-variable bias, and the paper lacks a credible source of exogenous variation in staffing.
   
2. **Inconsistent Treatment Definition.** The paper mainly measures treatment as contemporaneous log(FTE) variation and interprets coefficients as the effect of officer reductions, yet the descriptive tables characterize forces by their 2010–2015 cuts. There is no formal linkage between this treatment intensity and the identifying variation, nor is there any use of pre-2010 funding structure (precept share) as in the manifest. It is not clear whether the within-force “shock” is driven by austerity, endogenous manpower decisions, or other contemporaneous factors. The authors should either implement the precept-based IV or clearly motivate why the FE estimates can be construed as causal.

3. **Parallel Trends and Mechanism Interpretation Need Strengthening.** The paper claims parallel trends based on pre-2014 data, but the samples used for the main results contain only 2014–2021 observations and no explicit event study. Moreover, it is difficult to disentangle whether officer reductions affect charge rates because of investigational capacity versus recording practices or demand-side changes. A more explicit discussion of timing (when cuts occurred relative to outcome changes) and alternative mechanisms (e.g., reclassification of outcomes, reduced crime reporting) is needed.

**Suggestions**  
1. **Implement the Precept-Based IV Strategy.** Return to the manifest’s original identification idea. Construct an instrument using the pre-2010 council tax precept share (or the share of funding that could be raised locally) interacted with a post-austerity dummy. First-stage evidence should demonstrate that forces with greater precept reliance experienced smaller officer cuts, and we should see local projection/DiD estimates that benchmark the reduced-form impact on charge rates. This will align the empirical strategy with the stated research question about austerity-driven officer shortfalls and will deliver a more credible causal estimate.

2. **Clarify the Treatment and Timing.** If the goal remains to estimate effects using within-force variation, describe the timing of cuts explicitly. For example, are the officer reductions concentrated between 2010 and 2015, after which staffing stabilized? Present event-study graphs (e.g., coefficients from interactions of “treatment intensity” with year indicators) to show whether charge rates move contemporaneously with cuts. This will help readers assess whether changes are plausibly driven by staffing shocks rather than other time-varying confounders.

3. **Strengthen Threats and Robustness Sections.** (a) Consider controlling for additional force-level covariates that could evolve over time (e.g., local crime rates recorded, socioeconomic indicators, PCC leadership changes) to show the FE results are robust. (b) Expand on the concern that smaller forces (which may have both larger cuts and different case mixes) drive the results. Provide heterogeneity analysis by pre-2010 force characteristics (size, crime composition, urbanization). (c) Provide placebo outcomes (e.g., outcomes in the Home Office dataset that should not be affected by officer counts) or falsification tests (e.g., crime types with little investigative demand) to bolster causal interpretation.

4. **Improve Mechanism Evidence.** The heterogeneity by offense type is a good start. To make the claim that agency capacity was degraded stronger, consider linking officer reductions to downstream outcomes—e.g., court throughput, prosecutions, conviction rates, victim re-engagement—if data are available. Alternatively, show that a) the decline in charge rates is not driven by changes in the denominator (e.g., recording of outcomes with “evidential difficulties”), and b) that forces with greater cuts shifted resources across investigation units (maybe via public reports or budgets). This would reinforce the argument that the observed effect is due to investigative capacity constraints rather than other channels.

5. **Discuss External Validity Carefully.** The paper makes policy claims about national re-staffing, but the 2014–2021 panel is relatively short and coincides with both austerity and broader criminal justice reforms. Clarify whether the estimated elasticity is expected to generalize to upward staffing changes (e.g., Police Uplift Programme) and how the results relate to deterrence literature. Consider comparing the magnitude of the charge-rate effect to those found in the US or other UK studies to contextualize the policy implications.

6. **Address Missing Data/Measurement Issues.** The extended panel uses headcount/charge counts to avoid the recording break; explain how this is comparable to the main specification. Describe whether any forces have inconsistent reporting or definitional changes in outcome categories. Transparency about data merging (how outcomes are aggregated, how force boundaries changed) will strengthen credibility.

Overall, the paper tackles an important question about austerity’s impact on criminal justice quality. However, it must align its empirical strategy with the originally proposed IV, clarify the treatment variation, and bolster the credibility of the causal interpretation before the claims can be sustained.
