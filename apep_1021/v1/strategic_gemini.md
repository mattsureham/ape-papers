### 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
This paper examines the real economic consequences of aggressive anti-money laundering (AML) enforcement by studying a 2018 Latvian ban on shell-company bank accounts. It asks whether these firms are "mere legal fictions" or if they sustain a broader domestic ecosystem of service providers and intermediaries. The author finds that the enforcement shock wiped out nearly 30% of firms in the capital, Riga, including legitimate service providers, revealing a "laundering premium" where illicit finance indirectly subsidizes formal economic activity.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this pitch very well in the first three paragraphs. It sets up a clear "natural experiment" and immediately poses a compelling trade-off between eliminating illicit activity and preserving domestic economic health. It doesn't need a rewrite, though it could benefit from emphasizing the *scale* of the banking sector involved (40% of deposits) even earlier.

---

### 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**The Contribution:** The paper provides the first micro-level causal evidence that AML enforcement imposes a "laundering premium"—a real-sector adjustment cost where legitimate domestic firms (accountants, lawyers, etc.) fail alongside the shell companies they served.

- **Differentiation:** It is well-differentiated. While Collin et al. (2020) look at SWIFT payment flows, and the banking literature (Chodorow-Reich, Huber) looks at credit supply, this paper focuses on the **client-type prohibition** as a unique mechanism for firm death.
- **Framing:** It is framed as a question about the WORLD: What happens to a domestic economy when it "goes clean"? This is much stronger than just filling a gap in the AML literature.
- **Bigger Contribution:** To make this even bigger, the author needs to quantify the *welfare* or *employment* implications. While firm counts are a great proxy, the "So What?" gets louder if we know if these were 1-person shell-service shops or 50-person law firms.

---

### 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
The paper sits at the intersection of **Law & Economics (AML)** and **Banking/Firm Dynamics.**

- **Closest Neighbors:** *Findley et al. (2015)* on anonymous incorporation; *Collin et al. (2020)* on AML and cross-border flows; *Chodorow-Reich (2014)* on bank shocks.
- **Strategy:** It builds on the AML literature by moving from "process" (compliance) to "outcomes" (real-sector impact). It synthesizes the firm dynamics literature with international finance.
- **Missing Conversations:** The paper should speak more to the **"Dutch Disease" or "Resource Curse"** literature. In many ways, financial opacity is a "natural resource" for small states; the paper shows the "extraction" of that resource (opacity) leads to a sectoral collapse.

---

### 4. NARRATIVE ARC
- **Setup:** Latvia as a post-Soviet financial hub where 40% of deposits are non-resident.
- **Tension:** US (FinCEN) pressure forces a sudden, exogenous shutdown of this model. Are these firms "ghosts" or "engines"?
- **Resolution:** They were engines for a specific subset of the domestic economy. 24,000 firms vanished, and sole proprietors (the service providers) were caught in the "blast radius."
- **Implications:** "Cleaning up" a financial system isn't a Pareto improvement in the short run; it has concentrated domestic losers.

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong and linear. It reads like a detective story where the "laundering premium" is the motive discovered at the end.

---

### 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
At a dinner party, I’d lead with: *"Did you know that when Latvia banned shell companies, nearly 30% of the businesses in its capital city disappeared within three years?"*

That is a "lean-in" fact. The follow-up question is: *"Was the economy better off for it?"* The paper currently answers the "cost" side well, but it needs to touch on the "benefit" (e.g., did reputable FDI increase later?) to satisfy a top-tier audience.

---

### 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
- **Front-loading:** The summary statistics in Table 1 are actually quite stunning. The "Active Firm" count for SIA/Riga dropping from ~90k to ~65k tells the whole story.
- **Appendix:** The "Standardized Effect Sizes" (Table 5) could be moved to an appendix; they feel a bit like "math-iness" for a result that is already visually and economically obvious.
- **NACE Codes:** The author admits in Footnote 1 that they lack NACE sector codes. This is the paper's Achilles' heel. Finding a way to link even a subsample of these firms to sectors would move this from "very good" to "AER-territory."

---

### 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The gap is **Scope and Ambition.** Currently, it is a very clean, very professional evaluation of one policy in one small country. To be AER, it needs to argue that this is a **General Theory of the Transition Costs of Transparency.**

**The single most impactful piece of advice:**
Expand the "Discussion" into a more formal conceptual framework or model of "The Laundering Premium"—specifically, model the spillover from "Illicit Shell" to "Legitimate Service Provider"—to show that this isn't just a Latvian story, but a fundamental feature of how offshore financial centers function.
