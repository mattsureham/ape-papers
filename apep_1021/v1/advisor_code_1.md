**Summary**

The paper examines Latvia’s 2018 AML enforcement shock (FinCEN designation of ABLV and the subsequent ban on shell-company accounts) using the full Enterprise Register. By comparing shell-likely limited-liability companies (SIAs) in Riga to control groups defined by geography and firm type, the author documents a large increase in dissolution rates and a 28% drop in active shells, suggesting a measurable domestic cost of eliminating the shell ecosystem. Mechanism checks—heterogeneity by firm type, age cohort, and service-provider spillovers—support the interpretation that the effect operates through the shuttering of non-resident banking-dependent shells.

**Essential Points**

1. **Credibility of Parallel Trends / Control Group Choice:** The paper’s two-by-two DiD collapses geography and firm type into just four aggregate cells. Given the large treatment effect, reader confidence hinges on whether the counterfactual (SIA/Riga) would have followed the average of the three control groups absent the shock. While the paper surmises the FinCEN designation was exogenous, it does not show pre-treatment trends explicitly for the treated and control cells. A conventional event-study plot or formal pre-trend test is essential; without it, we cannot rule out pre-existing divergences driven by the unique evolution of Riga’s shell ecosystem (e.g., policy changes, local administrative practices, or sectoral demand shocks) that could confound the treatment effect. Please include graphical evidence of the dynamics for each group and/or pre-trend coefficients for the treated cell using leads of the treatment indicator.

2. **Spillovers between Treated and Control Groups:** Since the treated group is defined by firm type (SIA) and geography (Riga), but control groups share either one dimension, the DiD implicitly assumes no spillovers from the treatment to the control groups. However, closing Riga-based shells could have affected SIA firms elsewhere (through service providers, bank-level policies, or nationwide compliance costs) or non-SIA firms in Riga (through general demand changes). The paper mentions some elevated dissolution outside Riga and among sole proprietors, but the main specification assumes away these spillovers. Please discuss and, if possible, estimate the extent of these spillovers—perhaps by expanding the model to include interactions capturing the other dimensions (e.g., Riga × non-SIA or SIA × non-Riga treated indicators) or by presenting results excluding firms close to Riga. If spillovers are large, the estimated “laundering premium” may partly reflect broader economy-wide adjustments, not just idiosyncratic shell exits.

3. **Omitted Dynamics and Treatment Timing:** The treatment is coded from February 2018 onward, but the law’s enforcement intensified over several months (FinCEN proposal, ECB declaration, May 2018 legal change). The paper does not exploit the timing variation nor does it address the possibility of anticipatory behavior or staggered compliance dates across banks or firms. This raises concern that the estimated β conflates the immediate FinCEN shock with broader macro developments (e.g., general tightening of AML scrutiny) between February and later months. A more nuanced timing analysis—event study around February–May 2018, possibly using the precise termination dates for bank accounts if available—would strengthen the claim that the dissolution spike is driven specifically by the shell-account ban rather than contemporaneous macro shocks. At a minimum, include a sensitivity check with the post-period starting in May 2018 and discuss whether the effects are driven by the immediate aftermath of FinCEN or the formal law enforcement.

**Suggestions**

1. **Event Study Visualization and Dynamic Effects:** Graphs showing the monthly dissolution (and registration) rates for each group, ideally normalized to the pre-treatment mean, would bolster the narrative. An event-study plot of Treated × Post leads and lags (using group × month interactions) would reveal whether the treatment effect ramps up sharply only after the AML shock and whether any pre-trends exist. This would also help readers assess whether the dissolution hike was persistent or temporary. If the data permit, complement the main DiD with an event-study regression of the form \(Y_{gt} = \alpha_g + \gamma_t + \sum_{k\neq -1} \beta_k \cdot \text{Treated}_g \times \mathbf{1}\{t-k\} + \varepsilon_{gt}\) to illustrate the dynamics.

2. **Refinement of Control Group:** Rather than collapsing all non-treated cells into one average, consider weighting the control groups or estimating the treatment effect against each separately to test robustness. For example, compare Riga SIAs to Riga non-SIAs (within geography) and to non-Riga SIAs (within firm type) in separate regressions. Presenting these decompositions will reveal whether one dimension (geography or firm type) drives the findings and whether the main estimate is overly influenced by one control group with unusual behavior.

3. **Mechanism Quantification:** The paper’s mechanism tests are compelling qualitatively, but readers would benefit from more explicit quantification. For instance, can you estimate how much of the total firm exit is accounted for by the 2000–2009 cohort or by sole proprietors? A back-of-the-envelope calculation showing that the observed dissolution is consistent with the known number of non-resident clients of the affected banks would contextualize the magnitude. Similarly, linking the number of service providers (sole proprietors) exiting to the presumed number of shell accounts lost would illustrate the secondary economic cost.

4. **Addressing Alternative Channels:** The narrative attributes the spike in dissolutions to the prohibition of shell-company accounts, but other channels could contribute—such as general shocks to non-resident banking due to reputational damage, tightened KYC rules, or globally falling demand for Latvian intermediaries. Discuss which alternative channels are plausible and how the data support or reject them. For instance, is there any evidence that banks restricted domestic clients simultaneously? Did dissolution rates rise for domestic firms that are not shell-like but operate in sectors that could be affected by tighter foreign deposit monitoring? If possible, control for banking-sector indicators (e.g., deposits, lending activity) or mention whether such data are unavailable.

5. **Policy Context and External Validity:** The paper could better situate the findings in the broader policy debate by discussing how Latvia compares to other grey-listed countries and what lessons (e.g., sequencing of reforms, support for service-sector workers) might generalize. Consider highlighting whether Latvia’s experience suggests that the “laundering premium” is mostly absorbed through firm exit (with minimal macro spillovers) or whether the domestic service sector faced longer-run adjustment costs. This would help policy readers gauge the trade-offs in alternative AML strategies.

6. **Clarify Data Construction Details:** While the Enterprise Register provides rich data, some construction choices need more explanation. For example, how do the authors treat firms with missing municipality codes or those registered in Riga but conducting activities elsewhere? Are dissolution dates reliably recorded (e.g., can we distinguish voluntary dissolutions from forced closures)? Addressing the data cleaning steps and any limitations (e.g., possible lags in updates, firms that briefly disappear) would enhance transparency.

7. **Standard Errors and Clustering:** With only four group-level treated units, standard errors might be inflated or misestimated. Although heteroskedasticity-robust SEs are reported, standard practice for DiD with few clusters is to cluster at the group level or use wild cluster bootstrap. Please clarify the clustering approach and, if possible, report robustness using clustered or bootstrapped SEs to reassure readers that the significance is not driven by misestimated inference.

8. **Quantifying the “Laundering Premium”:** The term “laundering premium” is evocative; consider operationalizing it more precisely. For example, define it as the proportion of Riga’s SIA stock that exits due to the shock relative to a counterfactual baseline, or as the implied share of GDP represented by dissolved firms. This would help readers apply the concept to other settings and better understand the economic magnitude.

Overall, the paper addresses an important question with novel data but would benefit from richer diagnostics around identification, clearer exposition of control groups and timing, and more detailed discussion of alternative explanations.
