## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple and potentially important question: when a country shuts down a banking model built around non-resident shell companies, does anything “real” in the domestic economy disappear too? Using Latvia’s 2018 crackdown on shell-company accounts after the ABLV/FinCEN episode, the paper argues that a large share of formal firms in Riga vanished, implying that financial opacity had been propping up a measurable slice of the local business sector.

A busy economist should care because this is not just a corruption or compliance story. It is a broader question about how illicit-finance infrastructure props up measured economic activity, and what the real domestic adjustment costs of cleaning it up look like.

Does the paper articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs? **Mostly, but not quite.** The current opening is informative and competent, but it starts with institutional detail before fully landing the big economic question. The paper’s best idea is not “Latvia had a bank scandal”; it is “AML enforcement may shrink not just illicit activity, but formal economic activity that had become dependent on opacity.” That should be the first thing the reader understands.

### The pitch the paper should have in the first two paragraphs

Here is the version the paper should be leading with:

> Anti-money-laundering enforcement is usually evaluated by whether it reduces illicit finance. But for countries that have built part of their formal economy around secrecy services, enforcement may also destroy measured domestic economic activity. The key question is therefore not only whether AML works, but what exactly disappears when opacity is removed: pure legal fictions, or also real local businesses that serviced them.
>
> Latvia’s 2018 shutdown of shell-company banking provides an unusually stark test. After the FinCEN action against ABLV Bank and Latvia’s subsequent ban on shell-company accounts, thousands of registered firms disappeared. Using the universe of Latvian firm records, this paper shows that the collapse was concentrated in the firm types, places, and cohorts most exposed to the non-resident banking sector, implying that financial opacity carried a sizable domestic “laundering premium” for Riga’s formal business sector.

That framing gets the paper into an AER-style conversation faster: what does enforcement do to the real economy when illegality and formality are intertwined?

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper’s contribution is to show that shutting down shell-company banking can sharply reduce the stock of formally registered firms and associated local service activity, suggesting that financial opacity sustains a measurable domestic economic premium in the formal sector.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest papers?

**Only partially.** The paper says “first micro-level causal estimate of the domestic cost of AML enforcement,” which is directionally useful, but “first” claims are fragile and not a substitute for intellectual differentiation. Right now, the paper is clearer on what dataset and episode it uses than on what larger claim it establishes relative to neighboring literatures.

It needs to be more explicit about how it differs from:

1. **Banking/crisis papers**: this is not a credit-supply shock story.
2. **Firm regulation papers**: this is not ordinary entry-cost or deregulation/re-regulation.
3. **Crime/corruption papers**: this is not only about reducing illicit activity, but about the formal sector dependence created by opacity.

That distinction is present, but the paper does not yet make it sharp enough.

### Is the contribution framed as a question about the world, or filling a literature gap?

It is **mixed**, with too much drift toward literature-gap language. The strongest version is a question about the world:

- When a jurisdiction monetizes opacity, how much of its formal private sector is actually supported by that opacity?
- What is the domestic adjustment cost of cleaning up illicit-finance infrastructure?

That is much stronger than “the real-sector consequences of AML remain undermeasured.”

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?

Right now they could probably say:  
**“It’s a paper showing that Latvia’s AML crackdown caused many shell-like firms in Riga to disappear.”**

That is decent, but not yet memorable enough. There is still some risk they summarize it as:  
**“another DiD paper about a regulatory shock and firm exits.”**

The paper needs to own a more distinctive conceptual contribution. The term **“laundering premium”** is the right instinct, but it currently reads more like branding than a concept. To work, it needs a tighter definition and a clearer statement of why this is an economically meaningful object, not just a vivid phrase.

### What would make the contribution bigger?

Most importantly, **broaden the economic object of interest beyond firm counts**. Right now the headline outcome is dissolution/registration/stock of firms. That is suggestive, but for AER the contribution becomes much bigger if the paper can say something about one of the following:

- **Employment**: did jobs disappear, or mostly paper entities?
- **Tax base / VAT / payroll / profits**: did public revenue fall?
- **Real estate / office rentals / legal and accounting activity**: did actual local demand contract?
- **Banking deposits / payments flows** linked to the exposed segment.
- **Spillovers to legitimate firms** beyond sole proprietors.

If the paper can connect shell-company shutdown to a meaningful real local margin, then “laundering premium” becomes a substantive economic concept rather than an inference from registry dynamics.

Second, the contribution would be bigger with a more comparative framing:
- Not just “Latvia is interesting,” but “this is a template for what happens when offshore/intermediary economies unwind.”

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

### Closest neighbors

The closest intellectual neighbors seem to come from several adjacent literatures:

1. **Shell companies / beneficial ownership / illicit finance**
   - Sharman (testing international incorporation rules and opacity)

2. **AML / FATF / regulatory enforcement**

   - Likely some law-and-finance/public policy papers on sanctions, de-risking, and correspondent banking

3. **Bank-firm real effects**
   - Chodorow-Reich (bank health and employment/investment)
   - Huber (bank relationships and real outcomes)
   - Khwaja-Mian-type banking exposure papers, conceptually if not directly

4. **Informality / regulatory arbitrage / state capacity**
   - Papers on the real effects of anti-corruption enforcement
   - Papers on how regulatory crackdowns reshape measured formal activity
   - Some connection to the state capacity / tax enforcement literature

### How should the paper position itself relative to these neighbors?

It should **build on and synthesize**, not attack.

The right positioning is something like:
- From the illicit-finance literature, we know shell companies exist and facilitate opacity.
- From the banking literature, we know financial shocks can affect firms through credit supply.
- This paper shows a different channel: enforcement can eliminate firms whose economic function was inseparable from opacity itself, and can also contract the local service ecosystem around them.

That is a useful synthesis. The paper should not overstate hostility toward prior work; it should present itself as identifying a missing margin.

### Is the paper too narrow or too broad?

At the moment it is **slightly too narrow in evidence but slightly too broad in ambition**.

Too narrow because the data are just one country, one event, and mostly one set of outcomes: registry dynamics.  
Too broad because the prose occasionally implies sweeping claims about AML enforcement in general, FATF policy, and formal economic statistics.

For AER, the sweet spot is narrower and sharper:
- “This paper identifies one important but underappreciated margin through which AML enforcement affects local economies.”
Not:
- “This tells us the overall consequences of AML enforcement.”

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?

The paper should probably engage more with:
- **Offshore finance / tax haven / secrecy jurisdiction economics**
- **De-risking / correspondent banking / sanctions compliance**
- **State capacity and enforcement against hidden activity**
- **Urban/service-sector dependence on regulatory arbitrage**
- Possibly **informality/formality measurement** literatures, since one of the most interesting claims is about measured formal activity being inflated by opacity-linked firms

There is also a missed opportunity to connect to the literature on **how measured economic activity reflects legal/institutional definitions**, not just underlying production.

### Is it having the right conversation?

**Almost, but not quite.** Right now it is having a somewhat niche conversation: AML enforcement and shell companies in Latvia. The more impactful conversation is:

> What happens to a local economy when the state removes a form of regulatory arbitrage that had become embedded in formal market organization?

That conversation reaches public finance, development, political economy, banking, and organizational economics—not just AML specialists.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Before this paper, the world looks like this: countries can host sizable non-resident banking sectors and corporate registries that serve opaque financial flows; policymakers know these activities may be illicit or suspect, but it is unclear how much of the surrounding formal economy depends on them.

### Tension

The core tension is excellent: if shell companies are just empty legal wrappers, then shutting them down should mostly clean up the registry without affecting the real economy. But if those shells support a broader ecosystem of lawyers, accountants, address providers, and other local services, then AML enforcement imposes real domestic costs.

That is a very good tension. It is the paper’s strongest storytelling asset.

### Resolution

The paper’s resolution is that the shutdown caused a large and concentrated drop in firm stocks and elevated dissolution among precisely the firms and locations most tied to the non-resident banking model, with some spillovers to local sole proprietors.

### Implications

The implications should be:
1. AML enforcement can destroy more than illicit flows; it can shrink formal measured economic activity.
2. Some of what disappears is likely fictitious or purely instrumental activity, but some is genuinely local service-sector activity.
3. Governments that profit from opacity face a real domestic transition cost when they clean up.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

**Yes, but it is not yet fully disciplined.** The bones are strong. This is not a random collection of regressions. There is a real story here.

But the paper sometimes oscillates between three stories:
1. A paper about AML enforcement,
2. A paper about shell companies,
3. A paper about bank failure / non-resident banking collapse.

It needs to commit to one main story:  
**the real-economy dependence created by financial opacity.**

ABLV, FinCEN, and the ban are setup devices, not the essence. The paper should tell one story all the way through: formal business activity was partially sustained by a model of opacity, and this episode reveals the size of that dependence.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?

I would lead with:

**“When Latvia banned shell-company bank accounts, roughly one in four limited-liability firms in Riga disappeared.”**

That is a strong dinner-party fact. It is vivid and surprising.

### Would people lean in?

**Yes—initially.** This paper does have a hook.

But the very next question economists will ask is crucial:

**“Were these actual businesses, or just dead paper entities getting cleaned out of the registry?”**

That is the question the paper must be prepared to answer better than it currently does.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Likely one of:
- Did employment fall?
- Were these firms doing anything real?
- Is this a shell-company cleanup story or a broader local service-economy story?
- How generalizable is Latvia beyond a very unusual financial center?

That means the current evidence gets attention, but the paper’s staying power depends on whether it can move beyond “many firms disappeared” to “here is what that meant economically.”

### If findings are modest or null

Not applicable: the findings are large. The issue is not whether the effect is interesting; it is whether the paper has shown that the effect is economically deep rather than registrationally mechanical.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

### What is the gap?

This is primarily a **scope-plus-framing problem**, with some **ambition** issues.

- **Framing problem:** The paper has a better idea than it currently sells. It should be framed as a paper about how opacity sustains formal economic activity, not merely a case study of Latvia’s AML enforcement.
- **Scope problem:** Firm counts alone are not enough to persuade top readers that the paper has uncovered economically meaningful domestic costs.
- **Ambition problem:** The current version is careful and competent, but still reads like a well-executed field paper rather than a paper trying to reshape how economists think about enforcement and formal-sector measurement.

I would put **novelty** as a secondary issue. The setting is novel enough. The bigger problem is that the paper has not yet extracted the full conceptual payoff from that setting.

### What is the single most impactful piece of advice?

**If the author changes only one thing, it should be this: add evidence that distinguishes “paper firms disappearing” from “real local economic activity disappearing,” and then reframe the paper around that distinction.**

That is the whole game. If the author can convincingly show that the crackdown reduced not just registry clutter but actual local service activity, employment, tax payments, or other real margins, the paper moves from an interesting case study to a broadly important economics paper.

Without that, the reader is left with an intriguing but ultimately unresolved question: did the formal economy shrink, or did the register get cleaned?
