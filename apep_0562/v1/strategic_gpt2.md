## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether immigration can shift politics even where immigrants do not go. Using France’s 2021 asylum-dispersal plan and Facebook-based measures of cross-department social ties, it argues that departments more socially connected to asylum-receiving places saw larger gains for the far right, suggesting that immigration’s political effects propagate through networks rather than only through local contact.

A busy economist should care because the paper is trying to move the immigration-politics literature from a local-treatment question — “what happens where refugees are placed?” — to a broader equilibrium question — “how far does the political footprint of immigration travel?” That is potentially AER-relevant.

Does the paper articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs? Not quite. The current opening is vivid, but it spends too much time on the contact-hypothesis setup and too little time stating the big economic question in a way that immediately distinguishes the paper from “another far-right voting paper.” The first two paragraphs should say, more directly:

**The pitch the paper should have:**

> Immigration may affect politics far beyond the locations where migrants settle. In modern economies, people learn about policy shocks not only from direct exposure but through geographically dispersed social networks; if so, the electoral consequences of immigration are not local but network-wide.  
>   
> We study this question using France’s 2021 asylum-dispersal reform, which reallocated reception capacity across space, and combine it with department-to-department social connectedness data to measure indirect exposure. We show that far-right vote gains were larger not primarily in departments that hosted asylum seekers, but in departments more socially connected to places that did — suggesting that the political incidence of immigration depends on network structure, not just local demographic change.

That is the AER pitch. The paper’s current opening is close, but still reads too much like a motivated application and not enough like a paper trying to redefine the unit of analysis in this literature.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:**  
The paper’s contribution is to argue that the political effects of asylum dispersal extend beyond host locations and are amplified through interregional social networks.

That is a real contribution. The problem is that it is only partially clear, and not cleanly differentiated from adjacent literatures.

### Is it clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?
Only somewhat. The paper does differentiate itself from refugee-placement papers that study local contact effects, but the differentiation is too binary and too rhetorical. It repeatedly says “the literature studies where immigrants are; we study beyond receiving communities.” That is directionally right, but it needs a sharper statement of what is new:

- not just **another estimate of refugee exposure on voting**
- not just **another SCI application**
- but **an attempt to measure the nonlocal political incidence of immigration shocks**

Right now a smart economist might still summarize it as: “It’s a DiD/shift-share paper on asylum dispersal and RN voting, using SCI as exposure weights.” That is not enough.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or filling a LITERATURE gap?
It starts with a world question, which is good, but then drifts into “we contribute to three literatures” mode. The strongest version is a world question:

- Why is anti-immigrant voting often strongest away from immigrant settlement?
- How far do immigration shocks travel politically?
- Does networked information overturn the standard local-contact framing?

That is much stronger than “there is a gap in the literature on network exposure.”

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the intro?
Not confidently. They would get the general idea, but they would also notice the paper’s own hedging: maybe it is networks, maybe geography, maybe media, maybe proximity. Once the authors concede that much, the novelty blurs. The reader is left with “SCI-weighted exposure matters,” which is interesting but less conceptually novel than the title promises.

### What would make this contribution bigger?
Three specific ways:

1. **Make the object of interest broader than RN vote share.**  
   If the paper could show that network exposure changes beliefs, issue salience, media consumption, turnout, candidate choice, or attitudes toward refugees, the contribution becomes about political transmission rather than one electoral outcome.

2. **Separate network transmission from spatial spillovers.**  
   This is the single biggest conceptual enlargement. Right now the paper wants to claim “networked anxiety without contact,” but it repeatedly admits it cannot distinguish social ties from distance, local media markets, or regional spillovers. Until that is disentangled, the paper’s contribution remains narrower: “exposure through connected places matters.” That is still publishable somewhere, but not yet top-field-defining.

3. **Reframe from immigration specifically to incidence of place-based shocks.**  
   The most ambitious version would be: local policy shocks have nonlocal political incidence through social networks. Immigration is the application. That framing is bigger than “another immigration-and-voting paper in France.”

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

### Closest neighbors
The nearest papers are roughly:

- **Steinmayr (2021)** on refugee exposure and far-right voting in Austria
- **Dustmann, Vasiljeva, and Piil Damm (2019)** on refugee placement and attitudes/outcomes in Denmark
- **Halla, Wagner, and Zweimüller (2017)** on immigration and far-right support in Austria
- **Edo et al. (2019)** on immigration and voting in France
- **Bailey et al. (2018, 2020)** on the Social Connectedness Index and spatial linkages

Potentially also:
- **Müller and Schwarz (2021)** on social media and anti-refugee hate/crime
- broader spatial-political transmission papers using networks, media, or commuting links

### How should the paper position itself?
Mostly **build on** the refugee-placement/contact literature, not attack it. The current framing sometimes implies “the local-contact literature missed the real action.” That is too aggressive given the paper’s own limitations. Better is:

- the local literature estimates **direct effects**
- this paper studies **indirect effects**
- together they speak to the full political incidence of immigration policy

That is a cleaner and more credible positioning.

Relative to SCI papers, it should **borrow the tool, not oversell novelty**. SCI is not the contribution; the substantive claim is.

Relative to media/social-influence papers, it should **synthesize**: immigration shocks may diffuse through interpersonal networks, mediated by media and salience. The paper currently overstates interpersonal communication versus algorithmic exposure without evidence on actual communication content.

### Is it positioned too narrowly or too broadly?
Oddly, both.

- **Too narrowly** in spending lots of time on French asylum institutional detail and RN geography.
- **Too broadly** in claiming a “general network multiplier” across policy domains on the basis of one additional application plus a self-cited working paper.

The paper should narrow the claims and broaden the conversation. Specifically, it should speak less to “France + SCI + SNA” and more to “how place-based shocks propagate politically.”

### What literature does it seem unaware of?
It should speak more to:

- **spatial equilibrium / spillovers / general incidence** in political economy
- **media markets and information transmission across geography**
- **social interactions and peer effects in politics**
- perhaps **diffusion of issue salience** rather than only prejudice/contact

It also leans too heavily on classic contact hypothesis and less on modern political economy work about perceived threat, salience, and mediated exposure. The paper’s real conversation is not only with social psychology; it is with economists studying how voters infer aggregate conditions from local and network information.

### Is it having the right conversation?
Not quite. The most impactful conversation is not “contact hypothesis versus network anxiety.” That framing is catchy, but too psychologized and too narrow. The better conversation is:

**What determines the geographic incidence of politically salient shocks in a networked society?**

That connects immigration, media, regional spillovers, and network structure. That is a much more AER-style conversation.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Existing work studies how receiving refugees affects the politics of host communities. Direct contact often attenuates anti-immigrant backlash, yet far-right support is often strongest in places with few immigrants.

### Tension
If local contact reduces prejudice, why does anti-immigrant politics flourish far from immigrant settlement? One possibility is that the political effects of immigration are not local: they spread through social and informational links.

### Resolution
Using France’s asylum-dispersal reform and social connectedness data, the paper finds that departments more connected to asylum-receiving areas saw larger RN gains, while own hosting effects are muted.

### Implications
The political effects of immigration policy may extend beyond host locations; policymakers may be mismeasuring exposure if they focus only on where migrants settle.

That narrative arc is there, but it is not yet fully disciplined. The paper often reads like a collection of results and caveats around an appealing title. The main culprit is that the story changes register too often:

- first it is about the **halo effect**
- then about **contact hypothesis**
- then about **SCI shift-share**
- then about a **general network multiplier**
- then about **policy design of dispersal**
- then about **France-specific RN geography**

That is too many stories.

### What story should it be telling?
One story, clearly:

> Immigration policy has indirect political effects in places that are socially connected to where immigrants are assigned. Standard local-exposure designs miss this broader political incidence.

Everything else should support that.

- Contact hypothesis: motivating contrast, not coequal story.
- France institutional detail: enough to explain the shock, not a long tour.
- General network multiplier: a concluding implication, not a central claim.
- Halo effect: a motivating puzzle, not an alternative paper.

Right now the paper is serviceable as a narrative. For AER, it needs sharper discipline.

**“How do you know this is social networks and not just geography, media markets, or generic spatial spillovers?”**

And that question lands hard, because the paper itself admits it cannot really separate those channels. That is the central strategic vulnerability.

### If findings are modest or partly null
The null own-hosting result is not, by itself, especially interesting in this draft, because the paper already tells me it is imprecisely measured and likely attenuated. So it does not function as a strong counterpoint. It reads less like a meaningful null and more like an unusable comparison.

The interesting result is not the null. It is the asymmetry between local and connected exposure. But because local exposure is blurry, that contrast is less powerful than the paper wants it to be.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The gap is mostly **framing plus scope**, with a real dose of **novelty risk**.

### Framing problem?
Yes. The science may or may not hold up, but strategically the story is not yet framed at the right level. The paper is more than a France refugee paper, but less than the “general network multiplier” manifesto it sometimes wants to be.

### Scope problem?
Yes. One electoral outcome in one country with one inferred channel is a bit thin for the ambition of the title and claims. To excite the top people in this field, the paper needs either:
- stronger mechanism separation, or
- broader outcomes, or
- a more generalizable framing and evidence package.

### Novelty problem?
Potentially. The idea that anti-immigrant politics can be strongest away from immigrants is not new. The idea that social ties transmit political effects is not new. The paper’s novelty lies in combining those two. That is promising, but only if it can convincingly claim something more precise than “weighted exposure to connected places matters.”

### Ambition problem?
A bit. The paper is competent and high-energy, but still somewhat safe empirically and over-aspirational rhetorically. It wants the upside of a big conceptual paper without yet doing the extra work to rule in its concept.

### Single most impactful advice
**Narrow the claim from “social-network anxiety” to “nonlocal exposure through connected places” unless you can credibly separate networks from geography and media; then rebuild the introduction around the broader concept of the political incidence of immigration shocks.**

That one change would do two things at once:
- make the paper more credible
- make it more important

Right now the authors are overreaching on mechanism and underexploiting the bigger idea. If they fix that, the paper becomes much easier to place in the AER conversation.
