**SUBJECT:** Strategic Positioning – "The Electoral Constable: PCC Elections and Crime Investigation Quality"

---

### 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether replacing appointed bureaucrats with elected officials improves or distorts the core "production" of public safety: criminal investigations. Using a 2011 UK reform that introduced elected Police and Crime Commissioners (PCCs), the author tests for electoral cycles in charge rates and find that, outside of the idiosyncratic COVID-19 period, electoral accountability fails to generate detectable improvements—or even manipulations—in how police solve crimes.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this pitch with commendable honesty, but it leads with the "Suggestive Finding" (a 1.3pp decline) before admitting it’s a null result. This is a mistake for the AER. 

**Revised Pitch:** "Does democratic oversight improve the quality of law enforcement, or merely incentivize the manipulation of visible outputs? Exploiting the introduction of elected Police and Crime Commissioners in England and Wales, I find that electoral accountability fails to generate the political cycles common in fiscal policy. Under normal conditions, investigation quality remains stubbornly indifferent to the electoral calendar, suggesting that institutional 'operational independence' may effectively insulate public service delivery from democratic incentives."

---

### 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:** The paper provides a rare, quasi-experimental null result showing that democratic accountability does not induce cyclical performance shifts in the quality of criminal investigations.

- **Differentiation:** It moves beyond the "hiring" cycles of Levitt (1997) or "profiling" cycles of Masera (2021) to look at the *outcome* of the process (charge rates).
- **Framing:** It is currently framed as an evaluation of a specific UK reform. To reach the AER, it needs to be framed as a test of the **limits of the Agency Model** in public service delivery.
- **Clarity:** A smart economist would say: "It’s a DiD on UK police elections that finds no effect once you ignore the COVID year."
- **Enhancement:** The contribution would be much bigger if the author explored *why* the null exists. Is it because voters don’t care about charge rates (low salience), or because Chief Constables successfully block PCC interference (operational rigidities)?

---

### 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

The paper sits at the intersection of **Political Budget Cycles (PBC)** and the **Political Economy of Policing**.

- **Closest Neighbors:** Levitt (1997), Drazen & Eslava (2005), Ferraz & Finan (2011), and Masera (2021).
- **Positioning:** It should position itself as a "boundary" paper. PBC literature usually finds effects because politicians control the purse strings directly; this paper shows that when a "Chief Professional" (the Chief Constable) stands between the politician and the output, the cycle breaks.
- **Missing Conversations:** The paper needs to speak to the literature on **Bureaucratic Insulation**. Why does the "Electoral Constable" fail to move the needle where the "Electoral Mayor" succeeds? (e.g., Alesina and Tabellini, 2007, on bureaucrats vs. politicians).

---

### 4. NARRATIVE ARC

- **Setup:** Shift from appointed to elected oversight to improve accountability.
- **Tension:** Politicians want to look "tough on crime" before elections, but police work is a "black box" that is hard to ramp up quickly.
- **Resolution:** No evidence of a cycle. The "results" in the pooled data are a mirage created by the 2021 pandemic disruption.
- **Implications:** Electoral accountability is a blunt instrument. Simply changing the boss doesn't change the factory floor if the middle management is insulated.

**Evaluation:** The narrative arc is "The Honest Scientist." It discovers a result, realizes it's driven by an outlier, and pivots to a null. For the AER, this arc needs to be more "The Theoretical Puzzle"—why doesn't the most high-stakes accountability mechanism in the UK work?

---

### 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

**The Fact:** "In the UK, electing the person in charge of the police had zero impact on how many crimes actually got solved."
**The Reaction:** People will reach for their phones unless you explain the COVID-2021 reversal. The fact that the 2024 election shows an *increase* in charge rates while 2016/2021 show *decreases* suggests the "cycle" is actually just noise or local shocks.
**The "So What":** If the null is true, then the 2011 reform—one of the largest shocks to British policing in 50 years—was essentially "theatre" regarding technical performance.

---

### 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

- **Front-load the heterogeneity:** Don't sell the pooled result in the abstract only to debunk it in Section 5. Admit the fragility immediately and make the *fragility* the subject of the paper.
- **The Control Group:** With only 2 control forces (and one being the "City of London" which is an outlier in every sense), the author needs a Synthetic Control Method (SCM) or a more robust discussion of why the Met is a valid counterfactual for rural Devon.
- **Appendix:** Move the drug-offence placebo to the main results; it’s a strong "low-discretion" benchmark that helps the story.

---

### 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

Currently, this is a very good *Journal of Public Economics* or *Journal of Law and Economics* paper. To make it an **AER** paper, the author must solve the **Ambition Problem**.

A null result is only AER-worthy if it fundamentally challenges our understanding of a mechanism. The author should use the 2021/2024 reversal to argue that **institutional rigidities and operational independence** are stronger than electoral incentives. 

**Single biggest piece of advice:** Abandon the "Evaluation of PCCs" framing and adopt a "Limits of Democratic Oversight of Expert Bureaucracies" framing.
