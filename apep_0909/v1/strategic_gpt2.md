## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether electing civilian overseers of police creates electoral cycles in policing performance. Using England and Wales’ Police and Crime Commissioner (PCC) system, it studies whether police forces with elected oversight look different from the two forces with appointed oversight in the run-up to elections, focusing on investigative outcomes such as charge rates and cases closed with no suspect identified; the headline conclusion is basically no meaningful cycle in normal times, with the only apparent effect concentrated in the COVID-disrupted 2021 election.

A busy economist should care because this is a clean, policy-relevant test of a broader question: when we make public-service oversight more democratic, do politicians distort measurable performance around elections, as in political budget cycle models, or not?

### Does the paper articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Not quite. The current introduction is competent, but it leads with “does policing get better?” and then quickly narrows to “investigation quality before elections.” That creates some drift. The real paper is not about whether elected oversight improves policing overall; it is about whether electoral accountability generates **political cycles in police investigative outcomes**. That narrower question is sharper and more publishable than the broader “does democracy improve policing?” claim, which the paper does not actually answer.

The first two paragraphs should say, more directly:

> Elections often distort public policy: governments shift spending, taxation, and visible service delivery before voters go to the polls. But we know much less about whether electoral incentives distort the quality of law enforcement, a sector where politicians may be rewarded for visible “toughness” while the underlying quality of investigation is harder for voters to observe.  
>  
> This paper studies that question using England and Wales’ 2011 reform that created directly elected Police and Crime Commissioners in 41 police force areas while leaving two forces under appointed governance. I ask whether elected police oversight generates pre-election cycles in investigation outcomes. The answer is mostly no: pooled estimates suggest deterioration before elections, but that pattern is driven almost entirely by the COVID-disrupted 2021 election; outside that episode, there is no detectable electoral cycle in charge rates or case-resolution quality.

That is the pitch the paper should have.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### Contribution in one sentence

The paper’s contribution is to test whether elected police oversight produces electoral cycles in criminal investigation outcomes, and to show that in England and Wales such cycles are not detectably present under normal conditions.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest papers?

Only partially. The paper cites the right general areas, but the differentiation is still a bit generic: “political budget cycles, policing, PCC reform.” What is new is not just “another electoral accountability paper” or “another policing paper.” It is specifically:

- **electoral cycles in police performance rather than police inputs or misconduct**
- **a uniform national institutional setting with synchronized election timing**
- **a mostly null result that pushes back on the assumption that election incentives mechanically induce performance manipulation**

That differentiation needs to be much sharper.

Right now, a smart economist might still summarize it as: “It’s a DiD paper on PCC elections and police outcomes.” That is not enough for AER-level positioning.

### World question or literature-gap question?

The paper mostly frames itself as a world question, which is good: what does electoral accountability do to law enforcement quality? But it keeps slipping into “this contributes to three literatures” mode. That weakens the opening. A stronger introduction would stay in the world longer:

- Democracies increasingly elect police overseers.
- We know elections distort some public policies.
- Do they distort policing?
- Here is unusually clean evidence that they mostly do not, at least on investigative outcomes.

That is stronger than “the PCC reform literature has limited quasi-experimental evidence.”

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new?

Right now: maybe, but not crisply. They would probably say: “It looks at whether police overseers up for election affect charge rates around elections, and mostly finds no effect except in 2021.” That is acceptable, but it still sounds like a niche institutional paper.

To get beyond “another DiD paper about X,” the novelty needs to be phrased as:

- We have lots of evidence on election cycles in spending and some evidence on police staffing or profiling.
- We have very little on whether elections distort the **quality of case processing and investigation**, which is an underappreciated but socially important performance margin.
- The surprising fact is not a large cycle, but the absence of one.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Specific ways to make it bigger:

1. **Reframe the outcome as bureaucratic performance under electoral pressure, not just “crime outcomes.”**  
   Charge rates and “no suspect identified” are bureaucratic throughput/quality outcomes. That connects to a much broader state-capacity conversation.

2. **Lean much harder into visibility vs. quality.**  
   The interesting conceptual distinction is that elections may improve visible policing while leaving hidden investigative quality unchanged or worse. If the paper can contrast a visible margin and a less visible one, the contribution becomes larger. At present it gestures at this but does not operationalize it well enough.

3. **Compare with more electorally salient policing margins.**  
   If the paper could show that there are no cycles in investigation quality but some movement in more visible outcomes—patrol intensity, response times, stop-and-search, public-order enforcement, victim-facing metrics—that would create a more powerful story: elections shift what is visible, not what is substantively valuable.

4. **Broaden the implications beyond PCCs.**  
   The paper should be sold as evidence on the limits of electoral accountability in expert bureaucracies with operational independence. That is much bigger than “evaluation of the PCC reform.”

Potentially also relevant:

- literature on **state capacity / performance measurement**
- literature on **policing metrics and crime clearance/charge outcomes**
- public administration work on elected vs appointed governance

### How should it position itself relative to those neighbors?

It should mostly **build on and connect**, not attack.

- Relative to political budget cycles: “That literature shows elections distort visible policy margins; policing lets us ask whether similar cycles extend into frontline law-enforcement quality.”
- Relative to elected-sheriff papers: “Existing work shows elected law-enforcement leaders can affect staffing, profiling, and visible enforcement; this paper tests a different margin—investigative case outcomes—in a centralized institutional setting.”
- Relative to PCC-reform papers: “Most debate over PCCs is institutional and descriptive; this paper uses administrative data to test one concrete mechanism by which elections might matter.”

The paper should not overclaim that England and Wales is “cleaner” in a way that implicitly trashes the U.S. literature unless it can really cash that out. Better to say “institutionally distinct and complementary.”

### Is it positioned too narrowly or too broadly?

Currently, too narrowly in some places and too broadly in others.

- **Too narrowly** when it talks like an evaluation of the PCC reform. That sounds UK-specific and niche.
- **Too broadly** when it implies it answers whether electoral accountability improves policing generally. It does not.

The sweet spot is: **electoral accountability in policing, with implications for bureaucratic performance under democratic oversight**.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?

The paper feels underconnected to:

1. **Bureaucratic performance / multitasking / metric manipulation**  
   This is maybe the biggest missed conversation. If elections reward visible signals, what happens to harder-to-observe dimensions of state performance? That is a broad economics and political economy question.

2. **State capacity and administrative quality**  
   Investigation outcomes are an administrative-state outcome, not just a criminal justice outcome.

3. **Public administration / governance design**  
   Elected vs appointed oversight is a classic governance question. There is likely a non-econ literature here the paper should mine selectively.

4. **Criminal justice process literature**  
   Not just policing. Charge decisions and “no suspect” outcomes connect to prosecutors, case complexity, and downstream system congestion.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?

Not fully. The most impactful framing is probably **not** “yet another electoral accountability paper” and not “a UK policing reform evaluation.” The best conversation is:

> When elections govern expert bureaucracies, do they create opportunistic cycles in visible outputs, hidden quality, both, or neither?

That is a big conversation. The current draft is adjacent to it, but not squarely in it.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Elections often create cycles in public policy. Reformers increasingly apply democratic accountability to policing through elected overseers. In principle, that could improve performance or induce distortion.

### Tension

Policing is a hard case. Oversight is electoral, but operations are professionalized and partly insulated. Voters care about safety, but many dimensions of investigative quality are hard to observe. So it is genuinely unclear whether elections should generate cycles here.

### Resolution

The paper finds suggestive pooled pre-election deterioration in charge rates and higher no-suspect closures, but these effects are driven overwhelmingly by the anomalous 2021 COVID election. Outside that episode, there is little evidence of electoral cycles in investigative outcomes.

### Implications

Electoral accountability in policing does not automatically produce the opportunistic performance cycles seen in some other domains. Either elected overseers lack enough leverage over operational casework, or elections affect more visible policing margins than investigative quality.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

Serviceable, but not strong. At present it feels somewhat like a collection of careful empirical exercises organized around a null result rather than a paper with a fully worked-out story.

The reason is that the paper has not quite decided what kind of null it is:

- “PCCs don’t matter”  
- “elections don’t distort hidden-quality margins”  
- “COVID generated spurious pooled effects”  
- “operational independence insulates police casework from electoral incentives”

These are different stories. The best one is the second, with the fourth as interpretation:

> Elections distort many public policies, but not all state functions are equally electorally manipulable. In policing, investigative quality appears insulated from electoral cycles, likely because it is both less visible to voters and less directly controlled by elected overseers.

That is a real story.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?

I would lead with:

> “Unlike budget policy, elected police oversight in England does not seem to generate election-time cycles in investigation quality; the only apparent cycle is in the COVID-disrupted 2021 election.”

That is the most interesting fact.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

Middle outcome. Some would lean in because policing plus elections is interesting. Others would drift unless you immediately connect it to the bigger question of whether democratic accountability distorts bureaucratic performance. On its current framing, it is too easy to hear “small null result from a specialized UK setting.”

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Likely:

1. “Maybe elections affect more visible police activity, not charge rates?”  
2. “Is this really about lack of power or lack of effect?”  
3. “Why should I learn from England and Wales with only two non-PCC forces?”

Those are strategic questions, not just econometric ones. The paper needs better answers in the introduction itself.

### Is the null result interesting?

Potentially yes, but the paper has to work harder to make it feel like a successful finding rather than a failed search for significance.

The null is interesting if framed as:

- a test of whether a classic political-economy mechanism extends to a high-stakes public service;
- evidence that some bureaucratic domains are insulated from election cycles;
- a caution against assuming that elected oversight necessarily politicizes operational performance.

The null is less interesting if framed as: “we ran the obvious design and mostly didn’t find much.” The current draft is too close to the latter.
