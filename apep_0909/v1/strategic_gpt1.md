## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether electing police overseers changes how police perform when elections approach. Using the staggered rhythm of PCC elections in England and Wales and comparing elected-governance forces to the two forces that retained appointed governance, it studies whether electoral accountability produces pre-election changes in crime investigation outcomes such as charge rates and “no suspect identified” closures.

Why should a busy economist care? Because this is a clean institutional setting to ask a broader question: do elections improve hard-to-monitor public service quality, or do they instead push bureaucracies toward visible but shallow performance management? That is a first-order political economy question well beyond policing.

Does the paper articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs? **Only partially.** The ingredients are there, but the pitch is too quickly swallowed by design details and by the paper’s eventual null. The introduction currently reads like a competent empirical paper rather than a paper with a live big question. It needs to lead with the world question, then explain why this setting is unusually informative, and only then preview the nuanced answer.

### The pitch the paper should have

> Elections are supposed to improve accountability, but in many public services the key outcomes voters care about are hard to observe and easy to game. Policing is a canonical case: elected overseers may pressure agencies to deliver better public safety, but they may also shift effort toward electorally visible activities at the expense of investigative quality.
>
> This paper studies that tradeoff in England and Wales, where most police forces are overseen by directly elected Police and Crime Commissioners while two forces retained appointed governance. Comparing crime-outcome patterns around three election cycles, I ask whether police investigation quality systematically changes when elected oversight becomes electorally salient. The answer is mostly no outside the exceptional COVID election: elected police governance does not appear to generate regular political cycles in measurable investigative performance.

That is the paper’s strongest version: big question first, setting second, answer third.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:** The paper provides evidence that directly elected police oversight in England and Wales does **not** generate robust pre-election cycles in measurable investigation quality under normal conditions, despite a setting where electoral incentives are unusually visible.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest papers?

**Not clearly enough.** The introduction cites political budget cycles, elected sheriffs, and PCC reform debate, but the differentiation is still blurry. Right now the paper risks sounding like: “another electoral-cycle paper, but in policing.” That is not enough.

The paper needs to distinguish itself along three dimensions:

1. **Outcome margin:** not budgets, staffing, arrests, or stops, but **case resolution / investigation quality**.
2. **Institutional margin:** not local politicians timing spending, but **elected civilian oversight of operational agencies**.
3. **Takeaway:** not “elections distort behavior” but “in this domain, electoral accountability may have surprisingly limited bite on measurable quality.”

That last point is actually what makes the paper potentially interesting. It should not be embarrassed by the null; it should build the paper around the substantive boundary condition.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a literature gap?

At present it is **mixed**, with too much tilt toward the literature-gap formulation. The stronger framing is world-facing:

- Weak: “There is limited quasi-experimental evidence on PCC reform.”
- Strong: “We still do not know whether elections improve or distort police performance when the relevant outputs are hard for voters to verify.”

The latter belongs in AER territory; the former sounds field-journal.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the intro?

A smart economist could probably say:  
“It's a DiD paper on PCC elections and police outcomes in England.”

That is not enough. They should instead be able to say:  
“It asks whether electoral accountability changes bureaucratic quality in policing, and the surprising answer is that it doesn’t produce normal electoral cycles in investigation outcomes.”

Right now the novelty is too submerged under specification and coefficient preview.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Several possibilities:

1. **Shift from “crime outcomes” to “bureaucratic performance under elections.”**  
   The current framing is too sectoral. Make it a broader paper about whether electoral incentives penetrate operational bureaucracies.

2. **Emphasize mechanisms tied to visibility vs quality.**  
   The big contribution would be stronger if the paper could distinguish more clearly between electorally visible policing margins and back-office investigative quality. Even without new identification, the paper could reframe existing outcomes through that lens.

3. **Use a more obviously welfare-relevant or salient outcome.**  
   Charge rates and no-suspect closures are meaningful, but the paper would feel bigger if anchored in outcomes economists instantly map to welfare or state capacity: serious-crime clearance, victim-facing resolution, case attrition, or offense categories where investigative quality is especially central.

4. **Lean harder into the negative result as a boundary condition.**  
   The paper’s real intellectual content may be: elections often move budgets and staffing, but they may not move deeply operational quality metrics in professionalized public agencies. That is a bigger claim than “we find no effect.”

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Elections are often thought to improve accountability, but they can also distort public service provision. In policing, elected overseers might pressure agencies to show results, yet the quality dimension of policing is hard to monitor.

### Tension

We have evidence of electoral cycles in budgets, staffing, and some policing behaviors, but we know much less about whether elections alter **investigative quality**, especially in a system where elected officials oversee but do not directly run police operations. England and Wales offer a rare institutional contrast.

### Resolution

The pooled data initially suggest pre-election deterioration in charge rates and increases in no-suspect closures in PCC-governed forces. But those effects are largely driven by the anomalous 2021 COVID election; outside that period, there is little evidence of systematic electoral cycles in investigation quality.

### Implications

Electoral accountability may have weaker effects on operational quality than on visible spending or enforcement margins, either because police oversight officials lack leverage over professionalized bureaucracies or because the outcomes voters care about are too distant from election-time manipulation. That is a meaningful constraint on broad theories of electoral accountability.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

**Serviceable, but not sharp.** The paper does have a story, but it keeps slipping into “collection of results” mode:

- pooled estimates,
- decomposition,
- placebo,
- heterogeneity,
- leave-one-out,
- discussion.

What’s missing is a strong controlling sentence: **this is a paper about the limits of electoral accountability in professional bureaucracies.**

Without that, the results feel like they are searching for significance and then retreating to a null. The paper should instead narrate from the beginning:

1. Elections could matter here.
2. This setting is unusually good for testing that.
3. The headline result is that they mostly do not.
4. That non-result is theoretically informative.

That is the story.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

“England created directly elected police commissioners, but there’s little evidence that police investigation quality systematically improves or deteriorates around those elections outside the COVID shock.”

That is the most interesting fact because it cuts against the generic expectation that elections always create cycles.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

**Some would lean in, but only if framed correctly.**  
If presented as “a DiD on PCC elections and charge rates,” phones.  
If presented as “a test of whether elections actually bite in hard-to-monitor bureaucratic tasks,” interest.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Probably:  
“If elections move budgets and visible policing, why don’t they move investigative quality?”  
That is exactly the follow-up the paper should want, because it points to the larger conceptual contribution.

### Are the null/modest findings themselves interesting?

**Potentially yes, but the paper has not fully earned that yet.** The paper does say “the null result under normal conditions is itself informative,” but this reads defensive. To make the null compelling, the paper needs to argue more clearly that:

- many theories would predict some cycle here;
- policing is a domain where political incentives plausibly could distort hidden quality;
- therefore learning that they don’t, in ordinary times, is substantively important.

At present, the null still risks feeling like a failed search for an effect rather than a deliberate test of a meaningful prediction.

   The stacked DiD explanation arrives too early and too heavily. In the intro, one sentence is enough. The main claim should come first.

4. **Promote the conceptual interpretation of the null.**  
   The discussion should begin not with “this paper tests whether…” but with “the main lesson is that electoral oversight does not appear to penetrate operational investigative performance in ordinary times.”

5. **Trim appendicial-feeling material from the main text.**  
   Standardized effect size tables, some of the coefficient detail, and possibly some offense-by-offense heterogeneity can be demoted unless they directly serve the central story.

6. **Make the conclusion do more than summarize.**  
   The conclusion should answer: What should political economists now believe differently? What should scholars of policing now believe differently? What does this imply for reforms that elect oversight officials?

### Are important results buried?

The most important result is actually the **collapse of the pooled effect once 2021 is excluded** and the interpretation of that collapse. That should be elevated to the core main result, not treated as a robustness exercise. Right now the paper’s structure implies:
- main result = there is an effect,
- robustness = maybe not.

But the honest and stronger story is:
- main result = there is no regular electoral cycle,
- auxiliary fact = the exception is the COVID election.

That structural reversal would help a lot.
