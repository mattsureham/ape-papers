### 1. Idea Fidelity
The paper deviates substantially from the original idea manifest, undermining its execution of the proposed research design. The manifest envisioned a **within-force event study** stacking all four PCC elections (including the 2012 first round) using quarterly data from 2005/06 onward, with force fixed effects (FE) and calendar-quarter FE to identify electoral cycles relative to pre-PCC baselines. Instead, the paper employs a **stacked difference-in-differences (DiD)** comparing PCC forces (treated) to only two non-PCC placebo forces (Metropolitan Police and City of London), starting data from 2014/15 and omitting the 2012 election entirely due to inconsistent outcome coding pre-2014. Key misses include: (i) no analysis of secondary outcomes like police staffing levels; (ii) no heterogeneity by competitive seats, PCC party alignment, or incumbent status; (iii) placebo tests limited to drugs and COVID exclusion, omitting the full pre-PCC period (2005-2012) and explicit low-discretion offence checks beyond drugs. While the research question on PCC electoral cycles in investigation quality (charge/no-suspect/evidential rates) is preserved, the shift to a cross-force DiD sacrifices the manifest's cleaner within-force identification, rendering the paper a different empirical exercise.

### 2. Summary
This paper examines whether elections for Police and Crime Commissioners (PCCs) in England and Wales generate political cycles in crime investigation quality, using a stacked DiD design that contrasts 41 PCC forces against two non-PCC controls (Metropolitan Police and City of London) around the 2016, 2021, and 2024 elections. Pooled results suggest modestly lower pre-election charge rates in PCC forces, but these are driven by the COVID-disrupted 2021 election and vanish when excluded, yielding a null under normal conditions. The findings contribute a novel quasi-experimental test of electoral accountability in policing, highlighting limits to cyclical manipulation in investigation outcomes.

### 3. Essential Points
The paper has three critical flaws that must be addressed for AER:Insights consideration; failure to fix them warrants rejection.

1. **Weak and non-credible controls undermine identification**: With only two non-PCC forces (a massive urban force and a tiny financial district), parallel trends are implausible—London's unique crime mix (high property crime, protests) and operational differences likely violate assumptions. Leave-one-out sensitivity confirms this: estimates swing wildly (e.g., pre-election charge effect from -1.3 pp to -0.2 pp or -2.4 pp). Authors must either (i) drop this DiD entirely and revert to a within-force event study (per manifest, using pre-2014 data despite coding changes, with dynamic leads/lags and force × calendar-quarter FE); or (ii) expand controls via synthetic controls, triple differences (e.g., × high-discretion offences), or UK-wide administrative data matching (e.g., staffing or priorities).

2. **Omission of 2012 election and pre-2014 data misses key variation**: Stacking only three elections (post-2014) halves the power (164 → ~123 force-elections) and biases toward mature PCCs, ignoring the reform's initial shock. Home Office data exists back to 2005/06 (as confirmed in manifest smoke test); authors must harmonize pre-2014 outcomes (e.g., map "cautions/PNDs" to modern categories) and include 2012, or explicitly justify/test exclusion with pre-trends from 2005-2012 placebos.

3. **Mismatch between hypothesis and findings requires reframing**: The manifest hypothesized *rising* pre-election charge rates (electoral push for results); the paper finds *falling* rates (suggesting neglect), but interprets ambiguously without theory. Null excluding COVID is honest but underpowered—must add pre-trends event studies (not just aggregated pre/post), report all event-time coefficients (e.g., -8 to +7), and test hypothesis direction explicitly (two-sided vs. one-sided).
