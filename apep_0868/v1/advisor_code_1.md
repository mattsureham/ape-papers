**Summary**

The paper exploits the regulatory boundary between ERCOT and the national interconnection as a quasi-experiment to study whether Winter Storm Uri’s catastrophic blackouts translated into measurable county-level employment losses. Using QCEW data over 2018–2023 and a TWFE DiD framework (augmented by county trends and event studies), the author finds that the storm had no detectable employment impact—ERCOT counties exhibit no negative employment deviation in Q1 2021, and post-period differences reflect pre-existing growth. This suggests that while Uri had enormous insurance, property, and mortality costs, the quarterly labor market showed remarkable resilience.

**Essential Points**

1. **Accounting for Outage Severity Within ERCOT:** The paper treats all ERCOT counties as equally “treated,” yet the idea manifest explicitly proposed exploiting DOE EAGLE-I blackout intensity (customer-hours per capita) as a continuous treatment. Without this variation, the identifying contrast is reduced to a binary grid membership which conflates exposure to Uri with other systematic differences between ERCOT and non-ERCOT counties. The authors should incorporate the outage intensity measure (or at least distinguish high- vs. low-outage counties within ERCOT) to show that results hold even when restricting to counties with actually large outages. This is critical for establishing that the null effect is not due to averaging across a mix of heavily and lightly affected ERCOT counties.

2. **Parallel Trends / Pre-Trends:** The event study shows a steadily rising trend for ERCOT counties relative to non-ERCOT before the storm, suggesting a violation of the parallel trends assumption unless properly addressed. While county-specific linear trends are included, it is unclear whether these trends are sufficient—especially since the positive baseline effect disappears only after adding trends. The authors need to provide stronger justification (e.g., placebo tests, pre-trend tests with standard errors) that the trend specification yields credible counterfactuals. Moreover, there is no discussion of whether differential shocks (e.g., technology-sector growth) might have differentially affected ERCOT after 2019, making the post-period estimates confounded. A more flexible approach (e.g., synthetic control, matching on pre-trends) could better ensure validity.

3. **Mechanisms and Timing:** The paper’s main claim is about Q1 2021 employment, yet Uri occurred in mid-February, meaning the quarter already includes two months of recovery. The precise timing of losses and recoveries matters: if firms rehired or reopened quickly, quarterly data may simply be too coarse to capture transient but economically meaningful disruptions. Without higher-frequency data (weekly, payroll processing) or intermediate indicators (UI claims spikes, detailed industry breakdowns), the null result might just reflect measurement timing rather than resilience. The authors should address this measurement concern more directly—perhaps by exploiting monthly BLS CES or state unemployment insurance claims—or at least argue convincingly why quarterly data should detect the type of persistent employment dislocation one would expect from a large blackout.

**Suggestions**

1. **Incorporate EAGLE-I Outage Intensity:** Return to the original plan of using DOE’s outage data to stratify ERCOT counties. Construct customer-hours lost per-capita (or share of customers without power for X hours) and interact it with post-Uri periods (or use it as a continuous treatment in a continuous DiD). This would allow you to estimate a dose-response function and test whether counties that actually experienced more severe outages had systematically different employment trajectories. If the null persists even with this richer treatment variation, it strengthens the causal claim.

2. **Balance Pre-Trends through Matching or Synthetic Control:** To mitigate the pre-trend concern, consider constructing a synthetic control for ERCOT counties using weighted averages of non-ERCOT counties that match on 2018–2020 employment trends and covariates (wage levels, sector shares, metro status). Alternatively, implement propensity score or coarsened exact matching on pre-treatment employment growth rates, industry composition, and demographic factors before estimating DiD. Presenting results from these alternative strategies would reassure readers that the null is not an artifact of latent unobserved trends.

3. **Explore Industry-Level or Monthly Outcomes:** Since Uri disrupted electricity and hence affected specific sectors (e.g., construction, manufacturing, hospitality), assess whether particular industries show any response, even if aggregate employment does not. Similarly, if accessible, analyze monthly employment/wage/establishment figures (e.g., from CES or state workforce data) to detect short-lived shocks. Even showing data that peak layoffs (UI claims) or electricity-dependent industries experienced temporary slowdowns would enrich the narrative about resilience.

4. **Control for Differential Time-Varying Confounders:** The period 2019–2023 overlapped with the pandemic and Texas’s unique economic trajectory (e.g., housing boom, migration). While quarter fixed effects capture statewide shocks, ERCOT and non-ERCOT counties likely diverged in exposures to COVID (metro vs. rural) or stimulus (e.g., airport closures). Include additional controls or interaction terms (e.g., metro status × post, COVID case rates, oil price exposure) to ensure the estimated coefficient is not capturing these heterogeneous trends. Alternatively, show that excluding pandemic-era quarters (2020–2021) yields similar patterns, underscoring robustness.

5. **Clarify Interpretation: Null vs. “Resilience”:** The conclusion attributes the null effect to resilience, yet without evidence on what offset potential losses (e.g., reconstruction spending). Consider investigating spillovers: perhaps some ERCOT counties experienced positive employment effects exactly because of post-storm reconstruction. More descriptive statistics (e.g., employment changes by sector, UI claims) could help test this narrative. If such data are unavailable, temper the language to emphasize that the labor market impact was undetectable rather than definitively showing structural resilience.

6. **Discuss External Validity and Mechanisms:** ERCOT’s outage was unique—ordered blackouts, extreme temperatures, and water/pipe damage. Elaborate on how generalizable the findings are to other types of outages or disasters. Explain why employment effects might differ for longer outages or when the grid failure coincides with supply-chain disruptions. This will help policymakers understand the limits of the null result when evaluating grid interconnection investments.

7. **Report Robust Standard Errors for Event Study:** The event study table omits standard errors for some critical quarters (e.g., 2020Q4), and the pattern of significance is hard to interpret without visual aids. Plot the coefficients with confidence intervals to show the smooth pre-trend and the absence of a discontinuity at Uri. Also, cluster-robust inference may be weak with unbalanced treatment groups; consider wild bootstrap inference for the event-study coefficients too.

8. **Address Multiple-Treatment Concerns:** Some non-ERCOT counties (e.g., El Paso) did experience outages due to other factors (e.g., local generation issues). Confirm that the non-ERCOT group indeed remained mostly unscathed during Uri. If there are known exceptions, consider excluding them or conducting sensitivity checks that drop these counties to ensure the control group represents an untreated counterfactual.

By attending to these points—particularly integrating outage intensity, fortifying the parallel trends argument, and exploring higher-frequency or sectoral data—the paper will present a much more convincing case that a catastrophic grid failure indeed left no trace in the labor market.
