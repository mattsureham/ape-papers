## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether the damage from an interest rate ceiling disappears once the ceiling is removed. Using Kenya’s 2016 lending-rate cap and its 2019 repeal, the paper argues that smaller, SME-oriented banks pulled back from lending during the cap and did not return afterward, suggesting that temporary credit-market interventions can have persistent effects on financial intermediation.

A busy economist should care because the broader question is not “did Kenya’s cap reduce lending?” but “can temporary regulation permanently rewire markets?” That is a big question with implications for credit policy, financial development, and the reversibility of bad regulation.

### Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Mostly yes, and better than many submissions. The opening gets quickly to the policy episode and the hysteresis question. That said, the introduction then sprawls. It starts with a strong world question, but quickly slips into a catalog of mechanisms, side literatures, and suggestive descriptive facts before the central contribution has been cleanly staked out.

### What should the first two paragraphs say instead?

The paper should lean harder into the irreversibility question and less into “here is a neat Kenyan natural experiment.” The Kenya episode is the vehicle, not the destination.

### The pitch the paper should have

“Governments often intervene in credit markets under the assumption that any damage can be undone later. This paper asks whether that assumption is wrong. Studying Kenya’s 2016 lending-rate cap and its 2019 repeal, I show that the banks most exposed to the cap cut lending during the policy and did not recover after repeal; instead, they remained shifted toward safer assets. The core implication is that even temporary price controls can create persistent scarring in financial intermediation.”

That is the AER version of the story: a general lesson about reversibility of market interventions, illustrated through a clean policy episode.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### Contribution in one sentence

The paper’s contribution is to argue that interest-rate ceilings can have persistent, not merely contemporaneous, effects on bank lending because temporary caps may durably shift banks away from relationship-based credit provision.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?

Only partially. The paper says it is the first to study reversibility/hysteresis after repeal, and that may well be true in this specific setting. But the differentiation is still too claim-based and not sharp enough conceptually.

Right now the reader hears:
- prior papers show caps reduce credit;
- this paper shows the reduction persists after repeal.

That is a real distinction, but not yet a large one. It risks sounding like “same design, longer window.” For AER, the contribution needs to feel like a new question, not just an added post period.

The sharper differentiation is:
- Existing work asks whether caps reduce credit while in force.
- This paper asks whether markets recover after distortionary regulation is removed.
- The answer speaks to dynamic policy incidence and institutional scarring, not merely static rationing.

That is a stronger conceptual separation.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a gap in a literature?

It starts as a world question, which is good. But too much of the introduction reverts to “the literature has not yet studied X.” The world question is much stronger: Are credit-market distortions reversible? Do banks lose lending capacity, not just lending volume?

The paper should stay with the world question throughout.

### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what’s new?

At present, maybe, but not confidently. They might say: “It’s a DiD on Kenya’s rate cap showing small banks cut lending and the effect persisted after repeal.” That is decent, but it is still “another DiD paper about a credit regulation shock.”

The introduction does not yet force the reader to summarize it as: “This is a paper about regulatory hysteresis in credit markets.” That is the summary you want.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Most importantly: make the paper less about **Kenya’s cap** and more about **the persistence of institutional damage from temporary regulation**.

Specific ways to enlarge it:
1. **Elevate the dependent variable conceptually.**  
   The key outcome is not just loan-to-asset ratios; it is the persistence of banks’ withdrawal from relationship lending. If the paper can better characterize the lost margin as SME/intermediated/risky credit, the contribution becomes much bigger.

2. **Strengthen the post-repeal object.**  
   The current framing is “no reversal.” Bigger is: “repeal alone is not enough to restore market function.” That is a policy-relevant proposition economists care about beyond this setting.

3. **Tie to a broader economic mechanism.**

4. **Reduce the fintech/digital-credit side quest unless it can be made central.**

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Before this paper, economists know from theory that binding interest-rate ceilings can cause credit rationing. They know much less about whether those effects unwind when the policy is reversed.

### Tension

The standard mental model of policy reversal is symmetric: impose cap, lending falls; repeal cap, lending recovers. But that need not be true if banks lose relationships, shift organizational capacity, or discover safer assets and do not come back.

### Resolution

In Kenya, the banks most exposed to the cap reduced lending during the cap and remained relatively withdrawn even after repeal, while government securities holdings stayed elevated.

### Implications

The costs of interest-rate ceilings may persist long after repeal, so evaluating such policies using only the treatment period understates their long-run welfare cost. More generally, temporary regulation may leave durable scars on market organization.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

Yes, more than many empirical papers. There is a real story here. But it is diluted by overstuffing. The paper currently tries to tell four stories:
1. rate caps reduce lending,
2. repeal does not reverse the effect,
3. banks substitute into government debt,
4. borrowers may shift to digital credit.

Only one or two of these can be main-stage. The actual story should be:

**Temporary price controls can have persistent effects because they change bank behavior and capabilities, not just prices. Kenya’s cap-and-repeal episode shows this.**

Everything else should support that narrative, not compete with it.

At present, some sections read like a collection of sensible results looking for a hierarchy. The hierarchy should be:
- Main result: persistence after repeal.
- Supporting mechanism: persistent portfolio reallocation.
- Broader interpretation: relationship capital/organizational scarring.
- Speculative extension: borrower migration to digital credit.

That is a good dinner-party fact.

Economists would lean in, at least initially. The “policy reversal didn’t reverse the effect” fact has bite. It’s better than the usual “regulation reduced lending” paper.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Immediately: **Why didn’t lending come back?**  
And then: **Is this about banks losing capability, or about demand/borrowers changing, or about something else in Kenya post-2019?**

That tells you what the paper must do narratively: it does not need to fully prove the mechanism, but it must persuade the reader that the persistence is economically meaningful and not just a mechanical postscript.

### If findings are modest, is the null interesting?

The findings are not null, but the key novelty is a kind of “non-reversal” result. That is interesting if and only if the paper makes the case that reversibility is the economically relevant counterfactual. It mostly does, but it could do this more aggressively.

The paper should say more explicitly:
- policymakers often assume repeal cures the distortion;
- therefore learning that repeal may be insufficient is itself first-order.
