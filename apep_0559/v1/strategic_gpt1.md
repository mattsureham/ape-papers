## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether the damage from interest-rate ceilings disappears when the ceiling is removed. Using Kenya’s 2016 cap on bank lending rates and its 2019 repeal, the paper argues that smaller, SME-oriented banks cut lending during the cap and did not return after repeal, suggesting hysteresis in credit intermediation rather than a temporary distortion.

A busy economist should care because the question is bigger than Kenya: many countries cap lending rates, and the policy debate usually assumes these controls are reversible. If temporary price controls can permanently shrink relationship lending to risky borrowers, then the welfare costs of financial repression are much larger than standard static analyses imply.

**Does the paper itself articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Mostly yes, but not optimally. The current opening is better than average and already has a real question, but it still reads a bit like “Kenya offers a nice policy experiment” rather than “here is a fundamental fact about regulated credit markets.” The first two paragraphs should do less literature throat-clearing and more claim-staking: temporary price controls in credit markets may create durable scars, and Kenya gives a rare chance to test that.

**The pitch the paper should have:**

> Interest-rate ceilings are usually evaluated as temporary distortions: cap rates, credit falls; remove the cap, credit returns. This paper asks whether that presumption is wrong.  
>  
> I study Kenya’s 2016 lending-rate ceiling and its 2019 repeal to test whether credit supply rebounds when a cap is lifted. It does not: small, SME-focused banks contract lending during the cap and remain shifted away from private credit after repeal, consistent with persistent damage to relationship-based intermediation. The broader implication is that the costs of credit-market price controls may be dynamic and long-lived, not merely contemporaneous.

That is the AER version of the story: not “I have a Kenyan DiD,” but “I have evidence that temporary financial regulation can permanently alter market structure.”

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:**

The differentiation needs to be sharpened along three dimensions:

1. **Not just effect, but reversibility.**

2. **Not just average credit, but composition of intermediation.**

3. **Not just Kenya, but a general dynamic mechanism.**

The world question is the right anchor. The literature should be supporting cast.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Before this paper, the standard view is that interest-rate ceilings reduce credit while they are in force because banks cannot price risk, especially for riskier borrowers. But the implicit assumption in much policy discussion is that repeal restores the status quo.

### Tension
The tension is that this assumption of reversibility may be wrong. Credit markets are not frictionless spot markets; they depend on relationships, soft information, and internal bank capabilities. A temporary cap may therefore leave lasting damage even after the policy is undone.

### Resolution
Using Kenya’s cap and repeal, the paper finds that smaller, SME-focused banks reduced lending relative to larger banks during the cap, and this gap persisted or widened after repeal. At the same time, these banks remained more tilted toward government securities and continued to exhibit signs of a strategic retreat from risky lending.

### Implications
The implication is that the cost of interest-rate ceilings is not just temporary rationing; it may include durable contraction in formal credit supply to informationally opaque borrowers. Policymakers should therefore evaluate these caps as potentially path-altering interventions, not reversible short-run fixes.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
Yes, more than most submissions. There is a real setup-tension-resolution structure here. The trouble is that the paper weakens its own story by overloading it with side plots: cross-country evidence, digital credit, regulatory uncertainty, welfare discussion, and multiple literatures. The main story is strong enough without these adornments.

At moments it still feels like a collection of plausible results and mechanisms surrounding one central figure. The story it should tell is simpler:

1. Kenya imposed a cap.  
2. Banks most exposed to risk-based pricing cut lending.  
3. Repeal did not restore lending.  
4. Why? Because price controls can destroy lending relationships and shift banks into safer business models.  
5. Therefore, reversibility cannot be assumed.

That is the paper.
