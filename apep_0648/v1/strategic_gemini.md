## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper examines the impact of "constitutional carry" (permitless concealed carry) laws on public safety by analyzing the 2019–2024 wave of state adoptions. It finds that while these laws are associated with a reduction in firearm homicides (consistent with deterrence), they simultaneously lead to a nearly equal increase in firearm suicides (consistent with easier access to lethal means during impulsive crises), resulting in a net-zero effect on total firearm mortality. 

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this pitch effectively by the third paragraph, but the first two paragraphs spend too much time on the "story" of a man in Alabama and general policy context. 

**The pitch the paper should have (Paragraph 1-2):**
"Does the deregulation of concealed firearms increase or decrease public safety? Between 2019 and 2024, sixteen U.S. states adopted 'constitutional carry' laws, eliminating permit requirements and creating a massive natural experiment in firearm access. This paper uses a staggered difference-in-differences design to show that these laws do not change the total number of gun deaths, but rather reshuffle their composition: they reduce firearm homicides by 10% while increasing firearm suicides by 10%."

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution:** The paper identifies that firearm deregulation operates on two opposing behavioral margins—deterrence and access—which can lead to a zero net effect on aggregate mortality.

**Evaluation:**
- **Differentiation:** It is well-differentiated from the "Shall-Issue" literature (Lott & Mustard; Donohue et al.) by focusing on the more extreme "Constitutional Carry" regime and explicitly measuring suicide as a primary outcome alongside homicide.
- **Framing:** It is framed as answering a question about the **WORLD** (how gun laws affect death) rather than just a gap in literature.
- **Explainability:** A smart economist could easily explain this: "It’s the paper that shows permitless carry swaps homicides for suicides."
- **Bigger Contribution:** To move from "very good" to "AER-level," the paper needs to move beyond state-level counts. The contribution would be much larger if it could identify *who* is dying. For example, if the homicide reduction is among young men but the suicide increase is among older men, the welfare implications (and the "compositional reshuffle" story) become much more powerful.

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

**Neighbors:** 
1. Donohue, Aneja, and Weber (2019) – RTC and violent crime.
2. Lott and Mustard (1997) – The original deterrence hypothesis.
3. Duggan (2001) – Firearm ownership and homicide.
4. Miller et al. (2007) – Means restriction and suicide.

**Strategy:** 
- The paper currently acts as a **synthesizer**. It provides a "peace treaty" between the Lott (deterrence) and Donohue (escalation/access) camps by suggesting both might be right, but on different margins.
- **Missing Literature:** The paper should speak more to the **Health Economics** literature on "deaths of despair" and the **Public Economics** literature on "offsetting behavioral responses" (e.g., Peltzman effects).
- **Narrow/Broad:** It is currently positioned somewhat narrowly as a "gun paper." It should be positioned more broadly as a paper about how aggregate policy metrics (total deaths) can mask massive, offsetting welfare shifts.

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

- **Setup:** The US is rapidly deregulating guns via permitless carry.
- **Tension:** Proponents claim it stops crime; opponents claim it causes carnage. Both assume a one-way effect on violence.
- **Resolution:** Both are wrong about the total, but right about the pieces. Homicide goes down, suicide goes up.
- **Implications:** Policy debates focused on "violence" are too narrow; we must look at "access-driven" self-harm.

**Evaluation:** The narrative arc is very strong. It is not just a collection of results; it is a "discovery" of a hidden trade-off.

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

**The Dinner Party Fact:** "Passing permitless carry laws is basically a trade: you save one person from being murdered but cause one more person to commit suicide."

**Reaction:** People will lean in. It challenges the priors of both the Left (who expect total deaths to rise) and the Right (who expect only the 'deterrence' benefit).

**Follow-up:** "Are the people carrying the guns the ones committing suicide, or is it just that there are more guns in the house now?" This is the question the author needs to answer (or at least address with more granular data).

---

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

- **Front-loading:** The event study for homicide is clean; the suicide pre-trend is "messy" (marginal significance at k=-2, -3). The author needs to be much more upfront about this.
- **Section 6 (Discussion):** This is the most "AER" part of the paper. It should be expanded. The welfare analysis—comparing the value of a life saved from homicide vs. a life lost to suicide—is what will get this paper cited in top journals.

---

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The paper is currently a very high-quality "Applied Micro" paper (think *JHR* or *JMP*). To make it an *AER* paper, it needs **Ambition and Mechanism.**

**The Gap:** Currently, it’s a black box. "Law passes -> numbers change." 
**The Fix:** Use the county-level data mentioned in the data section to look at heterogeneity. Do these effects happen in high-gun-ownership counties or low-gun-ownership counties? Do they happen in urban (homicide-heavy) or rural (suicide-heavy) areas? 

**Single most impactful advice:** Decompose the results by urban/rural or by demographic groups (age/race) to prove that the "deterrence" and "access" mechanisms are actually hitting the expected populations.
