**Summary**  
The paper documents a new “erosion” in the Black–White earnings ratio in U.S. social assistance (NAICS 624) despite rapid Black employment growth, and asks whether the ACA’s Medicaid expansion helped or hurt the sector’s racial wage parity. Using state-quarter QWI data for 2005–2023 and Callaway–Sant’Anna difference-in-differences with never-treated states as a comparison, the authors find that expansion raised earnings for both Black (3.6 %) and White (1.8 %) workers, with a positive but imprecise effect on the ratio. The findings suggest Medicaid expansion lifted the wage floor, albeit without conclusive evidence that it meaningfully narrowed the racial gap.

**Essential Points**  
1. **Identification / Parallel Trends Robustness.** The credibility of the ATT on the B/W earnings ratio hinges on whether never-treated states provide a valid counterfactual, especially since these states differ systematically (geographically, politically, and in HCBS markets). The event study’s pre-period estimates are noisy and occasionally significant, undermining confidence in parallel trends. More rigorous balance diagnostics (e.g. pre-trends for the earnings level trajectories by race) or robustness checks (e.g. synthetic control, inverse probability weighting on pre-trends) are needed to show that expansion states were not already on a different path.  
2. **Disentangling Compositional vs. Wage Effects.** The manifest emphasizes that the racial erosion may stem from compositional shifts within NAICS 624, and that easing the erosion requires understanding whether expansion affected wages within occupations or simply added lower-paying jobs. The current empirical analysis, while documenting average earnings changes, does not exploit firm/subsector variation or explore within-industry decomposition (e.g. distinguishing HCBS-heavy subsectors from others). Without this, it remains unclear whether the estimated earnings gains reflect wage increases for incumbents, changes in hours, or compositional shifts, especially since employment effects are imprecise.  
3. **Outcome measurement and ratio interpretation.** The paper treats state-level average monthly earnings as a direct proxy for wages, but QWI earnings aggregate across hours and job types. If Medicaid expansion induced differential hours or schooling shifts by race (e.g. new Black hires working part-time), the log-earnings ATT may misstate the wage story, and the ratio effect may fail to capture real parity changes. The authors should either provide auxiliary analysis (e.g. hours per worker from other sources, controlling for sectoral composition) or temper interpretations of the ratio ATT accordingly.

**Suggestions**  
1. **Clarify and operationalize the manifest’s broader policy variation.** If the analysis is being intentionally limited to Medicaid expansion, explicitly state that and justify the choice. Alternatively, reintroduce the additional shocks (minimum wage changes, HCBS budget shifts) through a multi-policy framework or triple-difference to better capture the “segregation dividend” theory. For example, leverage variation in state-level minimum wage trajectories as an additional source of exogenous wage pressure, or link HCBS waiver expansion timing to subsector employment composition, which would more fully realize the manifest’s identification strategy.  
2. **Strengthen the parallel-trends evidence.** Implement placebo tests that compare treated states to subsets of never-treated states matched on pre-trend trajectories, or use pre-treatment fits to construct inverse probability weights. Present figures showing the raw (race-specific) earnings trends by treatment group alongside the counterfactual trajectories used by Callaway–Sant’Anna; this would help readers assess the plausibility of the identifying assumption. If the event-study coefficients are volatile, consider collapsing them into broader windows or reporting joint tests for pre-trend flatness.  
3. **Decomposition of NAICS 624.** Use NAICS 624 subcodes (e.g. 6241–6244) to explore whether the earnings effects are concentrated in HCBS-heavy categories versus child day care or vocational rehabilitation. If HCBS is the hypothesized channel, showing that Medicaid expansion effects are larger (or exclusively) in subsectors tied to Medicaid reimbursement would provide stronger evidence for the mechanism and address compositional concerns. Where micro-level data is scarce, proxy for HCBS intensity using state Medicaid spending shares or HCBS waiver dates.  
4. **Address measurement limitations more directly.** Given that QWI reports average monthly earnings, consider supplementing with other data (e.g. CPS/ACS for hours, OES for occupation-level wages) to adjust earnings for hours or to show that average earnings track wage distributions. If such data is unavailable, add sensitivity analyses (e.g. restricting to states where QWI hours are stable) or discussion explaining why hours-induced bias is unlikely to overturn conclusions.  
5. **Interpret the ratio ATT with care.** Since the ratio estimate is positive but imprecise, avoid implying that Medicaid expansion definitively moved the ratio. Instead, present the ratio ATT as bounds or employ bounding exercises (e.g. the implied ratio effect under the assumption that wages rose proportionally across races). This would make it clearer that the key contribution is documenting the erosion and showing that expansion lifted earnings broadly, rather than proving that it equalized them.  
6. **Enhance transparency about the sample.** Provide an appendix table listing the 47 states used, their expansion dates, and any inclusion/exclusion criteria (e.g. missing QWI data). Also report how balanced the panel really is after trimming (e.g. percent of state-quarters with both Black and White data) to reassure readers that the analysis is not driven by differential suppression.
