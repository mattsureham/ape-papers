## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether the ACA’s Medicaid expansion changed racial earnings inequality in the social assistance sector, a part of the care economy where Black employment rose rapidly even as Black-white earnings parity deteriorated. The headline finding is that Medicaid expansion raised earnings in social assistance for both Black and White workers, with somewhat larger gains for Black workers, but did not clearly reduce the racial earnings gap.

Why should a busy economist care? In principle, because this is about the distributional incidence of a major social insurance expansion on the low-wage care workforce: when government finances more care, who benefits on the worker side, and does it reinforce or mitigate racial inequality?

Does the paper articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs? Not quite. The current introduction leads with a little-known descriptive fact, which is interesting, but the paper’s real claim to general interest is broader: public insurance expansion reshapes labor market outcomes in a racially stratified care sector. Right now the first two paragraphs read like a niche sector paper with a striking statistic, rather than a paper about how the modern welfare state affects inequality through labor demand.

### The pitch the paper should have

“Medicaid expansion did more than increase insurance coverage: it also increased demand for paid care work in one of the most racially stratified parts of the U.S. labor market. This paper studies whether that demand shock raised earnings in social assistance and whether publicly financed care expansion narrowed or widened Black-white earnings inequality. We show that Medicaid expansion increased earnings for both Black and White workers in social assistance, with somewhat larger gains for Black workers, but no precise reduction in the racial earnings gap. The broader lesson is that expanding the care economy can lift pay without necessarily closing racial inequality within it.”

That is a stronger AER-facing opening than “here is an undocumented erosion in one sector.”

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### Contribution in one sentence

The paper contributes by showing that Medicaid expansion raised earnings in social assistance, but offers only suggestive evidence that it narrowed Black-white earnings inequality in that sector.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3-4 papers?

Only partially. The paper says it contributes to Medicaid expansion, racial convergence, and HCBS workforce literatures, but the differentiation is still fuzzy. A reader can see that it is “Medicaid expansion x race x care workforce,” but not yet why that combination changes what we know in a major way.

The paper is closest to:
- work on the labor-market effects of Medicaid expansion and ACA implementation;
- work on the care economy / HCBS workforce;
- work on racial earnings inequality and sectoral sorting.

But the introduction does not yet crisply say: existing Medicaid-expansion papers study coverage, utilization, hospitals, or aggregate labor supply; this paper instead studies incidence on worker earnings in a specific publicly financed labor market. That is the differentiator that matters.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or filling a gap in a LITERATURE?

Mostly as a literature gap plus an undocumented fact. That is weaker. The stronger world question is:

**When the government expands publicly financed care, does the resulting labor demand improve pay in low-wage care jobs, and does it reduce or reproduce racial inequality within the care workforce?**

That is a real-world question. “No paper has studied racial wage dynamics in NAICS 624” is not enough for AER.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?

Not cleanly. Right now they would probably say: “It’s a staggered-DiD paper on Medicaid expansion and racial earnings in social assistance.” That is not fatal, but it is not what you want.

You want them to say: “It shows that public insurance expansion raises wages in the care workforce, but may not materially narrow racial inequality because the care economy is segmented.” That is a much better takeaway.

### What would make the contribution bigger?

A few possibilities, listed in descending order of value:

1. **Shift the core framing from ‘racial gap in one sector’ to ‘distributional incidence of publicly financed care expansion.’**  
   This is mostly a framing move, but an important one. The care economy is large and growing; Medicaid is a major purchaser. The paper should be about how public spending transmits to worker pay and inequality.

2. **Show sharper exposure heterogeneity tied to Medicaid intensity.**  
   The current story rests heavily on “social assistance” as a broad bucket. The paper would feel bigger if it could distinguish Medicaid-exposed subsectors or states with higher baseline HCBS intensity. That would make the mechanism and stakes much more credible and interesting.

3. **Elevate the null on the racial ratio into the main message, if that is truly the result.**  
   Right now the paper seems to want credit for suggestive racial equalization while admitting the main ratio result is imprecise. That is awkward. A bigger contribution might actually be: public insurance expansion lifted wages but did not meaningfully close racial inequality. That is a clearer and more policy-relevant conclusion.

4. **Connect earnings effects to the structure of worker sorting.**  
   If the deeper story is that care-sector growth can coexist with racial stratification because Black workers disproportionately enter lower-paid segments, then the paper needs more direct evidence on sorting, subsectors, occupations, or wage distribution. Without that, “erosion paradox” feels more like branding than a resolved insight.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

The U.S. has expanded publicly financed care through Medicaid, especially under the ACA. Social assistance is a rapidly growing, racially diverse, low-wage sector, and Black workers are increasingly represented in it.

### Tension

A major public spending expansion could either reduce racial inequality by lifting pay in low-wage care jobs, or worsen it if growth primarily creates low-paid jobs disproportionately filled by Black workers. At the same time, this sector shows a puzzling long-run erosion of Black-white earnings parity despite strong Black employment growth.

### Resolution

Medicaid expansion appears to have raised earnings for both Black and White workers in social assistance. The point estimates are larger for Black workers, but the paper cannot show a clear reduction in the Black-white earnings ratio.

### Implications

Public insurance expansions can benefit workers in the care economy, but wage gains alone may not undo racial stratification within care labor markets. Policymakers should not assume that expanding care demand automatically yields racial equity in labor-market outcomes.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the pieces, but not a fully disciplined arc. Right now it oscillates between two stories:

1. the undocumented “erosion paradox” in social assistance; and  
2. the effect of Medicaid expansion on racial earnings inequality.

Those are related, but not identical. The paper has not decided which is the main story and which is supporting context.

My view: the current paper is **a collection of suggestive results looking for a story**. The descriptive erosion fact is striking but under-explained. The policy effect is policy-relevant but modest on the main gap outcome. The discussion then leans heavily on composition and subsector sorting that the paper does not directly show.

### What story should it be telling?

It should tell one clean story:

**Medicaid expansion increased labor demand in publicly financed care, raising earnings in social assistance, but this demand shock did not clearly reduce racial inequality because the care workforce is segmented.**

The “erosion paradox” should support that story as motivation, not become a parallel storyline.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?

“Medicaid expansion appears to have raised earnings in social assistance by a few percentage points, including for Black workers, but it did not clearly close the Black-white earnings gap in that sector.”

That is the most honest lead.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

A subset would lean in: labor, public finance, health, and inequality economists. The average economist would not automatically lean in unless the framing is broadened. “Social assistance NAICS 624” is not naturally gripping; “public insurance expansion affects wages in the care economy” is.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Almost certainly:  
**“Is this really about Medicaid-financed HCBS demand, and where in the care sector is it happening?”**

That is the central follow-up because the current sector definition is broad and the mechanism is asserted more than shown.

### If findings are null or modest, is the null itself interesting?

Potentially yes, but the paper has not fully embraced that. A null on the racial ratio is interesting if the paper frames it as:

- public spending can raise wages without reducing racial inequality;
- expanding a sector with stratified job ladders may lift all boats but preserve hierarchy;
- equity in employment quantity is not the same as equity in pay.

That is a publishable and useful lesson. But the paper currently seems reluctant to own the null, instead calling it “suggestive evidence.” That weakens the strategic positioning. If the flagship result is imprecise, the paper should either make the null the insight or find a more precise margin.
