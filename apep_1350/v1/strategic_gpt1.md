## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether the ACA’s Medicaid expansion changed racial earnings inequality in the low-wage care economy, focusing on social assistance jobs where Black employment grew dramatically even as Black-white earnings parity eroded over time. A busy economist should care because the paper tries to connect a major social insurance reform to the distributional consequences of care-sector growth by race, in a sector that is both large and policy-driven.

Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs? Not quite. The current opening is intriguing, but it leads with a descriptive fact that is more surprising than consequential, then pivots into Medicaid expansion before fully clarifying why this sector is economically important and why this policy is the right lens. The current intro makes the paper sound like “an interesting sectoral fact plus a policy test,” rather than a broader paper about how public insurance expansions shape inequality in the care workforce.

### The pitch the paper should have

“Public insurance expansions do not just affect coverage; they also reshape labor demand in the care economy. This paper studies whether the ACA’s Medicaid expansion changed racial earnings inequality in social assistance, a rapidly growing, policy-dependent sector that employs a rising share of Black workers. We first document a striking and previously underappreciated fact: despite being one of the few sectors with historically near-parity Black-white earnings, social assistance experienced substantial erosion in that parity as the sector expanded. We then ask whether Medicaid expansion mitigated or amplified that erosion.”

That version gives the reader the world-level question first: how does a major public program affect racial inequality in a growing labor market? The descriptive erosion fact then becomes the motivating puzzle, not the whole story.

The paper documents a long-run deterioration in Black-white earnings parity within social assistance and shows that Medicaid expansion raised earnings in that sector for both Black and White workers, with suggestive but inconclusive evidence of larger gains for Black workers.

At present, those distinctions are implied rather than driven home.

The paper is strongest when framed as a **question about the world**: when the state expands publicly financed care demand, does that reduce or reproduce racial inequality in the care workforce? That is much better than “the literature has not looked at Black-white wage dynamics in NAICS 624.”

The introduction gestures toward the world question, but it still slips into “this literature hasn’t examined…” mode too quickly.

That is not enough for AER positioning.

What you want them to say is:

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
The care economy is growing, heavily shaped by public financing, and disproportionately employs Black workers. Social assistance historically looked relatively equal on Black-white earnings compared with many other sectors.

### Tension
As the sector expanded, that apparent parity eroded. A major policy expansion—Medicaid expansion—may have either worsened inequality by channeling Black workers into lower-paid jobs or reduced inequality by increasing reimbursement-driven labor demand and lifting pay.

### Resolution
Medicaid expansion increased earnings in social assistance for both Black and White workers. Point estimates are larger for Black workers, but the direct effect on the Black-white earnings ratio is imprecise.

### Implications
Public insurance expansions appear to raise pay in the care workforce, but the evidence is weaker on whether they meaningfully reduce racial inequality. More broadly, sectoral growth can coincide with widening racial earnings gaps even in sectors that appear equitable in aggregate.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
It has **the outline** of one, but not a fully successful arc. The biggest problem is that the paper promises a stronger resolution than it delivers. The title, “The Erosion Paradox,” and the opening paragraphs set up a paper about explaining the erosion. But the actual analysis mostly estimates the effect of Medicaid expansion on contemporaneous earnings, and finds only suggestive evidence on the ratio. So the story risks becoming:

- striking descriptive pattern,
- plausible policy candidate,
- modest result,
- lots of discussion filling the gap.

That is a sign of a paper whose descriptive and causal pieces are not yet fully integrated.

### If it’s a collection of results looking for a story, what story should it be telling?
The paper should not pretend it has solved the “erosion paradox.” It hasn’t. It should tell a more disciplined story:

1. There is a surprising long-run fact: racial parity eroded in a growing care sector.
2. Medicaid expansion is one important test case for how policy-driven demand affects inequality in that sector.
3. The evidence suggests expansion raised earnings broadly and may have modestly favored Black workers, but it does not explain the broader long-run erosion.
4. Therefore, the key implication is that **care-sector expansion and racial equity are not the same thing**.

That is actually a stronger and more honest story than the current “resolve the ambiguity” language.

That is the most interesting combined fact.

### Would people lean in?
Some would, especially health, labor, and public economists. But many would not fully lean in yet, because the ultimate finding on the headline inequality outcome is suggestive rather than decisive. The descriptive fact is interesting; the causal payoff is more muted.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
They would immediately ask:  
**“Is this really about Medicaid-driven HCBS demand, or is it about compositional change, minimum wages, occupation mix, or hours?”**

That is the natural substantive follow-up. Not because of identification nitpicking, but because that is the actual economic mechanism question.

### If findings are modest, is the modesty itself interesting?
Yes, potentially—but the paper does not yet make that case sharply enough.

A null or modest ratio effect could be important if framed as:  
“Large public spending expansions can raise earnings in low-wage care sectors without materially changing racial earnings inequality.”  
That is a meaningful lesson.

But the current draft still sounds a bit like it wanted a cleaner equalizing result and didn’t quite get it. It needs to own the modest finding and explain why that matters. Otherwise it reads as a near-miss.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The gap is mainly a combination of **framing problem** and **ambition problem**, with some **scope problem**.

### Framing problem
The science may be adequate for a solid field journal, but the paper is not yet framed around a big enough economic question. It is framed as a sector-specific treatment effect paper. AER wants the broader claim: what does this teach us about the labor-market incidence of the welfare state and racial inequality in the care economy?

### Scope problem
The paper is too dependent on a single sector-level outcome that is not cleanly tied to job quality or mechanism. To feel AER-level, it likely needs more scope—either richer outcome margins, sharper mechanism evidence, or a broader comparison that elevates the result beyond one NAICS code.

### Novelty problem
The descriptive fact may be novel. The causal exercise is less obviously novel unless it is linked to a bigger conceptual point. “Medicaid expansion affects worker earnings in social assistance” is not by itself enough.

### Ambition problem
This is the deepest issue. The paper is careful, but safe. It documents an interesting fact and runs a sensible policy design. But it does not yet persuade the reader that the result changes how top people in labor/public/health should think about the world.

### Single most impactful advice
**Reframe the paper as a study of how public insurance expansion shapes racial inequality in the care economy, and then align the evidence around that claim rather than around a narrow NAICS-624 treatment effect.**

If they can only change one thing, it should be the framing. Right now the paper’s best asset is not the exact ATT—it is the intersection of three big topics: the welfare state, the care economy, and racial inequality. The paper should lean hard into that.
