### 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
This paper examines whether the "compliance cliff" created by state-mandated audit thresholds—which impose $10,000–$100,000 in costs on nonprofits—leads to strategic revenue reporting. Using a multi-state bunching design and IRS data, the author finds a "well-identified null," suggesting that these major regulatory notches do not meaningfully distort the size or reporting of the nonprofit sector.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this clearly. The first two paragraphs effectively set the stakes (implicit tax of 2–20% on the marginal dollar) and the method. 
*   **The Pitch it Needs:** While the current pitch is good, it could be more provocative by emphasizing the "Compliance Paradox." It should lead with: *"Economists typically expect agents to bunch at notches involving 20% tax jumps; why do thousands of nonprofit managers seemingly ignore a $20,000 bill? This paper uses this failure of standard theory to uncover the frictions—optimization costs, signaling value, and private ordering—that define the modern regulatory state."*

### 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**The Contribution:** This is the first multi-state bunching analysis of charitable audit mandates, providing a robust null result that challenges the universality of notch-induced behavioral distortions in the presence of institutional frictions.

*   **Differentiation:** It moves significantly beyond Yildirim et al. (2018) by using 34 states and a difference-in-bunching placebo design that separates regulatory avoidance from round-number bias.
*   **Question vs. Literature:** It currently straddles both. To be AER-ready, it needs to lean harder into the "Question about the World": *Is the nonprofit sector’s growth path distorted by state regulation?*
*   **Clarity:** A smart economist would say "It’s a bunching paper where nothing happened, but the 'nothing' is surprising given the cost."
*   **Bigger Contribution:** The contribution would be larger if it could definitively test *why* the null exists. Is it because nonprofits are "bad" at managing revenue (frictions), or because they "want" the audit (signaling)? More data on donor responses to audits would bridge this.

### 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
The paper sits at the intersection of **Public Economics (Bunching/Notches)** and **Nonprofit Economics**.
*   **Neighbors:** Kleven & Waseem (2013), Chetty et al. (2011), Yildirim et al. (2018), and the "Regulation of Entry" literature (Djankov et al., 2002).
*   **Strategy:** It should "Synthesize" rather than "Attack." It confirms the *theory* of notches but provides a boundary condition for *practice*.
*   **Missing Conversations:** The paper should speak more to the **Accountability/Signaling literature**. If audits are a "quality signal" (per the Krishnan citation), the notch isn't just a cost; it’s an investment. The paper should position itself as a test of "Mandates vs. Voluntary Certification."

### 4. NARRATIVE ARC
*   **Setup:** States impose massive audit costs on nonprofits over certain revenue thresholds.
*   **Tension:** Standard theory predicts a "cliff" of missing organizations just above the threshold.
*   **Resolution:** There is no cliff. The "average" nonprofit is unresponsive.
*   **Implications:** Regulation is less distortionary than feared, but likely because other forces (donors/frictions) are more powerful than the law.
*   **Evaluation:** The arc is clear but slightly flat. It feels like a "results looking for a story." The story should be: *The Statutory Law vs. The Private Law.* If foundations require audits anyway, the state mandate is "inframarginal."

### 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
At a dinner party, I would lead with: *"Nonprofits have to pay a $20,000 'fine' the moment they hit $500k in revenue, yet they don't even try to hide their income to avoid it."*
*   **Response:** People would lean in. The follow-up: *"Are they just incompetent at accounting, or do they actually want the audit?"*
*   **Null Result:** The null is interesting because the "treatment" (the cost) is so large. A null on a $50 fee is boring; a null on a $20,000 fee is a puzzle.

### 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
*   **Front-loading:** The heterogeneity by NTEE code (Science vs. Animal Welfare) is actually more interesting than the pooled null. Move some of the mechanism discussion from the conclusion to the results.
*   **Appendix:** Move some of the polynomial robustness (Table 4) to an appendix to keep the narrative flow.
*   **Data:** The mention of "Form 990 XML data" on page 7 sounds like a "future work" excuse. For AER, the author should probably just *do* that analysis now to address the measurement error concern.

### 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The gap is **Ambition/Scope.** Currently, it's a very high-quality technical note. To be an AER article, it needs to solve the puzzle it creates.
*   **Single Biggest Improvement:** Use the heterogeneity (Science vs. Others) to formally test the "Optimization Friction" vs. "Signaling" hypotheses. If science orgs bunch because they have professional accountants, but human services orgs don't, that tells a bigger story about the professionalization of the third sector.
