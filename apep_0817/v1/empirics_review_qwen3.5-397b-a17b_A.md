**2. Summary**
This paper investigates whether delays in FEMA disaster declarations causally reduce household assistance, exploiting variation in processing lags across 364 major disasters since 2010. While naive OLS estimates suggest that slower declarations significantly reduce per-household aid, the author employs an instrumental variables strategy using concurrent FEMA workload to show this relationship is driven by disaster severity rather than bureaucratic speed. The study concludes that declaration lag itself has no detectable causal effect on assistance amounts, challenging policy proposals for automatic declaration triggers.

**3. Essential Points**
1.  **Contradictory Instrument Mechanism:** The identification strategy relies on concurrent disaster load, but the first-stage results contradict the theoretical mechanism. The manifest argues load creates capacity constraints (slowing processing), yet Table 3 shows high load *reduces* lag (accelerates processing). If high-load periods trigger "accelerated protocols" or political priority, the instrument is no longer exogenous capacity noise but reflects endogenous prioritization of severe events. This fundamentally undermines the exclusion restriction.
2.  **Instrument Balance Failure:** The paper acknowledges that the concurrent load instrument is correlated with average damage severity (Table 3, Col 4). Since severity directly determines assistance amounts, this violates the exclusion restriction. Controlling for observed damage is insufficient if damage measurement is noisy or if severity affects outcomes through channels other than the observed controls (e.g., media attention, state-level matching funds).
3.  **Data Aggregation Loss:** The manifest highlighted access to 6.4 million individual records, but the analysis aggregates to the city-disaster level (57k observations). This aggregation obscures within-city heterogeneity (e.g., housing tenure, income) and inflates the first-stage F-statistic mechanically by multiplying the disaster-level instrument across cities. The analysis should leverage the individual-level data to improve precision and test for heterogeneous effects.

**4. Suggestions**
The paper addresses a policy-relevant question with a compelling null result, but the identification strategy requires substantial reinforcement to support the strong causal claims made in the abstract. Below are recommendations to strengthen the empirical approach and interpretation.

**Refining the Identification Strategy**
The current IV strategy is the paper's weakest link due to the sign reversal in the first stage and the balance failure. I recommend exploring alternative sources of exogenous variation in processing speed. For instance, FEMA regional offices have varying baselines efficiencies; you could use a shift-share instrument based on pre-disaster staffing levels in the relevant FEMA region interacted with national shock periods. Alternatively, consider administrative deadlines or government shutdowns that mechanically constrain FEMA processing capacity regardless of disaster severity. If retaining the concurrent load instrument, you must rigorously test the "priority protocol" hypothesis. If high-severity disasters get fast-tracked during busy seasons, the instrument is picking up severity, not just queue length. You could test this by examining whether the instrument predicts *denial rates* or *appeals*, which should not be affected by speed but are affected by severity.

**Leveraging Individual-Level Data**
The decision to aggregate to the city level is puzzling given the manifest's data access. Aggregation introduces ecological fallacy risks and prevents you from controlling for household-level confounders. I strongly encourage re-estimating the models using the 6.4 million individual records. This would allow you to include household fixed effects (for those registering for multiple disasters, if applicable) or at least control for individual tenure status (renter vs. owner), which is crucial since IHP affects these groups differently. Furthermore, individual data would allow you to test whether speed matters more for vulnerable subgroups (e.g., low-income registrants) who may have less liquidity to wait for aid, even if the average effect is null.

**Outcome Measures and Welfare Implications**
The paper focuses exclusively on *amounts* of assistance approved. However, the theoretical argument for speed usually revolves around *liquidity constraints* or *consumption smoothing*, not necessarily total aid eligibility. A delay might not change the final grant amount but could force households into high-interest debt or prevent timely repairs that mitigate secondary damage. I suggest broadening the discussion or empirical tests to include proxies for financial distress. For example, do slower declarations correlate with higher rates of post-disaster eviction filings, utility shutoffs, or credit score drops in the affected zip codes? If speed doesn't affect FEMA dollars but affects household solvency, the policy implication remains relevant.

**Clarifying the "Null" Result**
The conclusion that "speed does not matter" is strong. However, the IV estimates are imprecise (often failing to reject zero but with wide confidence intervals). The abstract claims the effect is "small," but the standard errors suggest you cannot rule out economically meaningful effects. I recommend presenting power calculations or minimum detectable effect sizes. Additionally, distinguish between *declaration lag* and *aid delivery lag*. Households care about when money hits their bank account, not necessarily when the President signs the declaration. FEMA often provides Rapid Needs Assistance before formal declaration. If the data allows, analyze the time from registration to disbursement rather than incident to declaration.

**Conclusion**
This paper has the potential to be a valuable contribution to the disaster economics literature by challenging the conventional wisdom that bureaucratic speed is inherently welfare-enhancing. However, to meet the bar for publication, the identification strategy must be reconciled with the observed data patterns (specifically the first-stage sign and balance), and the analysis should exploit the available individual-level data to rule out heterogeneity and aggregation bias. Addressing these points will significantly bolster the credibility of the causal claims.
