## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether mandatory pay equity audits reduce the gender wage gap, using Sweden’s 2017 reform that extended written audit requirements to smaller firms. The reason to care is straightforward: many countries are adopting pay transparency and audit mandates, but we still do not know whether these policies actually change pay-setting behavior or merely generate paperwork.

Does the paper articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs? Only partly. The current opening is competent, but it leads with “information is a prerequisite for action” and then immediately pivots to identification and the Danish paper. That is too methods-forward, too fast. A busy economist should first hear the world question: when governments force firms to measure and document gender pay differences, do wages actually change, and how quickly?

### The pitch the paper should have

“Governments around the world are adopting pay transparency and pay equity audit mandates in the hope of reducing gender wage gaps. But the central policy question remains open: when firms are required not just to disclose pay information, but to systematically audit and document gender-based pay differences, does compensation actually become more equal?

This paper studies Sweden’s 2017 expansion of mandatory written pay equity audits to smaller firms. I show that industries more exposed to the newly covered 10–24 employee firms experienced a gradual narrowing of the gender wage gap over the following years, suggesting that audit mandates can work—but only with long lags consistent with firms learning about gaps and adjusting pay over multiple compensation cycles.”

That is the right first-page story. The design can come after the policy question and main finding, not before.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper provides suggestive evidence from Sweden that extending mandatory written pay equity audits to smaller firms gradually reduced gender wage gaps, highlighting that audit mandates may operate with long implementation lags.

### Is this clearly differentiated from the closest papers?

Somewhat, but not sharply enough. The paper names three differences:
1. documentation mandate rather than disclosure,
2. smaller firms,
3. delayed dynamics.

Those are plausible distinctions, but right now they read as variants on an existing transparency literature rather than a genuinely new object. The author needs to explain why **documentation** is economically distinct from **disclosure**. Is the key mechanism employer learning, regulator enforceability, internal HR accountability, worker bargaining, or all of the above? If the distinction is just legalistic, it will not land. If the distinction is that audits force firms to diagnose and justify pay structures even when workers never see the data, then that is more interesting.

### World question or literature gap?

The paper is mostly framed as a world question, which is good, but it periodically slips into “this literature lacks evidence on X” and “I contribute methodologically” language. The stronger version is: **When governments require firms to look for inequities, do firms change compensation?** That is a world question. The literature discussion should support that, not replace it.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?

Not confidently. Right now they might say: “It’s another pay transparency DiD, but in Sweden and at the industry level.” That is not enough for AER territory.

What they should be able to say is: “This paper argues that mandatory **internal pay auditing** for small firms changes wage-setting slowly, even without public disclosure, which suggests the policy works through organizational learning and compliance rather than just worker bargaining.”

That is the differentiated hook.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Most importantly: make the mechanism and object more distinct.

Specific ways to make it bigger:
- **Lean harder into audit vs disclosure.** If this is really about internal diagnosis and compliance rather than public transparency, that is the main conceptual contribution.
- **Show where in the distribution effects arise.** If the paper could speak to whether the reform matters more in initially high-gap industries or occupations, the contribution would feel less average-effect and more substantive.
- **Connect to enforcement and organizational response.** If the policy changes because written documentation creates inspectable records, then the paper should frame itself as evidence on how regulation disciplines firms through documentation requirements.
- **Make timing the central result, not a side note.** The “slow dividend” is the only truly memorable idea in the draft. It should be the headline contribution.
- **Potentially broaden the outcome framing.** Instead of only “gender wage ratio,” the paper could be framed as asking whether audits change firm wage-setting practices, with the wage gap as the key manifestation. Even absent new outcomes, the framing can do some of this work.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Countries are increasingly adopting pay transparency and equity policies to reduce gender wage gaps, but we do not know whether requiring firms to audit pay differences actually changes compensation.

### Tension

Most existing evidence is on disclosure/reporting mandates, often for larger firms; much less is known about internal audit/documentation mandates, especially for smaller firms where pay gaps may be less visible and HR systems weaker. Moreover, if audits work through internal learning and compliance, effects may emerge slowly rather than immediately.

### Resolution

Industries more exposed to Sweden’s extension of written pay audit requirements to smaller firms appear to experience a gradual narrowing of the gender wage gap, with effects showing up only several years later.

### Implications

Policy evaluations conducted too soon may understate the effect of audit mandates, and governments may be able to influence pay-setting not only by informing workers but by forcing firms to measure, document, and justify internal wage structures.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the ingredients of one, but the arc is not fully disciplined. At present, it feels like:
- policy relevance,
- then identification challenge,
- then Sweden,
- then some results,
- then a mechanism claim,
- then a methodological apology.

That is not the strongest story.

The paper should be telling one clean story:
1. Governments increasingly require firms to audit pay.
2. We do not know whether audits change wages, especially in small firms.
3. Sweden’s reform lets us study that.
4. The notable finding is not a dramatic immediate effect, but a delayed one.
5. That delayed effect is exactly what we should expect if audits work by forcing firms to discover and correct inequities over time.

Right now the paper has that story, but it is competing with a second story: “I found a feasible design using public tabulations.” That second story is not AER-facing. It belongs in a data note, not as part of the main narrative.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?

“I’d lead with this: when Sweden forced small firms to produce written pay equity audits, the gender wage gap seems to close only gradually, with the clearest effects appearing five years later.”

That is the memorable fact.

### Would people lean in?

Some would, but only if presented that way. If presented as “I use a continuous treatment intensity DiD across 19 industries,” they will reach for their phones. If presented as “audit mandates may work slowly by changing how firms set pay,” they will lean in.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Probably one of these:
- “Is this really about worker information, or about compliance and internal HR processes?”
- “Why small firms?”
- “How different is an audit mandate from a disclosure mandate?”
- “Are the effects economically meaningful, or is this mostly suggestive timing evidence?”

Those are exactly the questions the introduction should anticipate.

### If findings are modest, is that okay?

Yes, but only if the paper embraces that what is interesting is **the pattern and timing**, not point-estimate triumphalism. The draft currently toggles awkwardly between bold policy language and repeated admissions of imprecision. Better to say: this is credible early evidence that the policy may matter, and the most important lesson is that evaluations after one or two years can miss the action.

If the paper tries to sell this as definitive evidence that audits reduce pay gaps, it will overclaim and invite dismissal. If it sells itself as evidence about **how these policies unfold over time**, the modesty becomes much more acceptable.

Be honest: this is not currently close.

### What is the gap?

Mostly:
- **Framing problem**
- **Novelty problem**
- secondarily **ambition problem**

#### Framing problem
The paper has a potentially good story—mandatory auditing changes firm behavior slowly—but it is not written with sufficient confidence and clarity around that idea.

#### Novelty problem
At present, the paper risks reading as one more transparency-policy evaluation in a crowded literature. The author must sharpen why written audits for small firms are economically different from reporting/disclosure mandates already studied.

#### Ambition problem
The paper is careful and competent, but safe. It accepts too quickly being “suggestive industry-level evidence.” That may be true, but for AER the paper needs a bigger animating claim about how regulation affects organizations.

### What would excite the top 10 people in this field?

A version of this paper that says:

“We’ve been thinking about pay transparency as a worker-information policy. But Sweden suggests another mechanism matters: forcing firms to formally audit and document pay inequities can change wage-setting through internal learning and regulatory accountability, especially in small firms, and these effects emerge only slowly.”

That is the interesting intellectual move. If the paper can own that move, it gets much more serious.

### Single most impactful piece of advice

**Reframe the paper around the idea that mandatory pay audits are not just transparency policies but organizational measurement mandates, and make the delayed adjustment dynamic the central contribution rather than a secondary empirical pattern.**
