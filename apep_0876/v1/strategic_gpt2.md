## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a classic and policy-relevant question: when taxes on high earners rise, do they actually leave? It uses the 2017 federal SALT deduction cap as a shock to the effective cost of living in high-tax states and argues that migration responses are strongly increasing with income: modest for lower earners, meaningfully larger at the top, and driven mostly by outflows rather than forgone inflows.

A busy economist should care because this is not just another tax-mobility estimate. If persuasive, it reframes the tax flight debate away from the binary “the rich flee / the rich don’t flee” and toward a more economically sensible object: the income gradient of migration elasticity.

Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs? Not quite. The opening is decent, but the introduction is still too literature-led and “gap”-framed. The real hook is a world question: **how mobile is the tax base, and for whom?** The paper should lead with the policy tension, then state immediately that the SALT cap created a national natural experiment that lets the author trace the migration response across the income distribution within one common institutional setting.

### The pitch the paper should have

“States worry that taxing high earners will drive away their tax base, but the empirical debate has stalled between studies finding almost no migration response and studies finding very large responses for elite earners. This paper shows that both views can be true: using the 2017 federal SALT deduction cap as a shock to the effective cost of living in high-tax states, I estimate the income gradient of tax-induced interstate migration and find that responsiveness rises sharply with income, with the main adjustment occurring at the top.”

“A reader should care because the relevant policy object is not the average migration elasticity, but who moves when the tax price of a state rises. That determines whether progressive state taxation mainly reshuffles a few top taxpayers or meaningfully erodes the broader tax base.”

That is cleaner, more world-facing, and more memorable than “no study has done the full income gradient.”

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper’s contribution is to show, using the SALT cap as a common national shock, that tax-induced interstate migration is sharply increasing in income, with most of the response concentrated among high earners and operating through outmigration.

### Is this clearly differentiated from the closest papers?

Partially, but not sharply enough.

Right now the paper differentiates itself by saying no prior study estimates the “full income gradient” in one setting. That is true as a design contribution, but it is not yet a compelling substantive differentiation. The more important distinction is:

- Young and Varner-type papers ask whether millionaire migration is large in specific state tax episodes.
- Kleven-Landais-Saez-type papers ask whether elite, highly mobile superstars respond strongly.
- This paper asks whether these seemingly conflicting findings are reconciled by a **strongly nonlinear income gradient in mobility within a common institutional environment**.

That is the real intellectual contribution. It should be stated more aggressively.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a literature gap?

Too much as a literature gap. The phrase “what no study has done…” is usually a warning sign in top-journal positioning. The stronger frame is: **states need to know whether tax flight is an average phenomenon or a top-tail phenomenon**. That is a question about the world and about fiscal capacity under decentralized taxation.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?

At the moment, maybe, but with some fuzziness. They might say: “It’s a DiD/triple-diff paper on the SALT cap and migration by income bracket.” That is not enough.

The introduction needs to make the memorable takeaway impossible to miss: **the paper is about heterogeneity, not just another estimate of tax flight**. The novelty is not the SALT cap by itself; it is that the SALT cap allows the author to map the migration elasticity over the income distribution in a unified setting.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Several possibilities, in order of importance:

1. **Translate the gradient into fiscal incidence/revenue consequences.**  
   Right now the paper stops at migration rates. For AER-level ambition, the reader wants to know whether the observed top-tail mobility is large enough to matter for the tax base in revenue terms. Even a back-of-the-envelope linking headcount migration to AGI or taxable-income exposure would enlarge the contribution substantially.

2. **Sharpen the top tail.**  
   The top bracket is \$200K+, which is too coarse for a paper about high-income mobility. The debate in this literature is often about the very top: top 1%, top 0.1%, millionaires. If the data do not permit that, this is a real ceiling on the paper’s eventual impact. The current top-coded bin makes the central substantive claim feel a bit blunter than it should.

3. **Connect migration to a broader margin of adjustment.**  
   The paper briefly notes it cannot study income shifting or residence reporting. Fine. But strategically, the paper would be bigger if it framed migration as one component of tax-base elasticity rather than as the whole object. Even without new estimates, the framing could acknowledge that mobility is just one margin and explain why this margin is independently important.

4. **Elevate the mechanism beyond outflow/inflow decomposition.**  
   “Mostly outflows” is useful but not enough for a top-field-journal pitch. A bigger mechanism would say something about who leaves for where, or whether the response is to no-income-tax states, lower-cost states, or nearby substitutes. That would tie the facts more tightly to tax competition.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

States fear that taxing top earners may induce exit and erode their fiscal capacity. Existing evidence is mixed, ranging from near-zero migration responses among millionaires to very large responses among highly mobile superstars.

### Tension

The literature’s disagreement may be because it is asking the wrong aggregate question. The true object may be heterogeneous responsiveness: maybe most taxpayers barely move, while top earners respond more. But clean evidence on the full income gradient within a common setting is scarce.

### Resolution

Using the SALT deduction cap as a shock that hit high-income taxpayers in high-tax states hardest, the paper finds a monotonic income gradient in migration responses, with the largest effects among the top bracket and most of the adjustment through outflows.

### Implications

The tax base is mobile, but not uniformly so. Progressive state taxation may be more feasible than “tax flight” rhetoric suggests for the broad population, yet still face meaningful erosion at the top of the income distribution where revenue stakes are highest.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

Serviceable, but not yet sharp. It has the ingredients of a good narrative, but the story is diluted by moving too quickly into design and estimates. The paper sometimes reads like a collection of related findings—gradient, triple-diff, outflow/inflow decomposition, robustness—rather than one disciplined answer to one big question.

### If it is a collection of results looking for a story, what story should it be telling?

The story should be:

> The public debate asks the wrong question. The issue is not whether “the rich flee” in some average sense; it is how migration elasticity changes with income, because that determines the real fiscal constraint on progressive state taxation. The SALT cap lets us observe that gradient in one national setting, and the answer is that mobility is concentrated at the top.

That story makes every result serve one purpose:
- Bracket-by-bracket estimates: map the gradient.
- Triple difference: summarize the top-vs-rest contrast.
- Outflow/inflow: explain how the top-tail response materializes.
- Revenue implications: why the gradient matters.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?

“I’d lead with: when the SALT cap raised the effective cost of living in high-tax states, migration responses were not flat—they rose strongly with income, and the top bracket’s response was about three times the bottom bracket’s.”

That is the most interesting fact in the paper.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

Economists would lean in—briefly. Tax mobility and SALT remain salient, and the heterogeneity angle is genuinely more interesting than another average treatment effect. But they will lean in only if the presentation is sharper and more ambitious. As currently written, the paper risks sounding like a competent empirical note rather than a field-shifting result.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Likely one of these:
1. “Is the effect economically large enough to matter for state revenue?”
2. “Is this really migration, or just broader post-2018 sorting in expensive coastal states?”
3. “What happens at the true top tail—millionaires, top 1%, top 0.1%?”
4. “Where do these people go?”

Those are exactly the questions the paper should anticipate more strategically in framing.

### If the findings are modest, is the modesty itself interesting?

Yes, potentially. In fact, the paper’s best interpretation may be: **migration responses exist but are concentrated and not catastrophic in aggregate**. That is policy-relevant. But the paper needs to own that. It should not oversell the result as large-scale tax flight; it should instead argue that the important lesson is the concentration of mobility in the high-income tail.

One problem: the low-income “placebo” complicates the clean mechanism story. I won’t referee the design here, but from a strategic perspective it weakens the elegance of the headline and makes the heterogeneity interpretation do more work. The paper handles this candidly, which is good, but the existence of that result means the author must frame the paper as documenting an **income-graded reduced-form sorting response to the SALT shock**, not as a pristine measure of direct tax-migration elasticity.
