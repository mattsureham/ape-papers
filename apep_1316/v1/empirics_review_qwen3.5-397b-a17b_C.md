# Review: The Appeals Lottery: Veterans Law Judge Discretion at America's Largest Federal Tribunal

## 1. Idea Fidelity

The paper adheres closely to the core identification strategy and data sources outlined in the Original Idea Manifest. It successfully exploits the Caseflow Automatic Case Distribution (ACD) system, utilizes the publicly available BVA decision text files, and constructs the proposed judge-leniency instrument. The institutional details regarding 5 USC 3105 and the rotation system are accurately represented. However, there is a significant deviation regarding the research scope. The Manifest explicitly promised estimation of causal effects on "veteran mortality, healthcare utilization, employment, and housing stability" using linked administrative records. The submitted paper stops at establishing the validity of the instrument (First Stage) and explicitly defers the Second Stage outcome analysis to future work. While establishing the instrument is a valuable contribution in itself, this represents a narrowing of the original empirical promise.

## 2. Summary

This paper documents substantial heterogeneity in grant rates across Veterans Law Judges (VLJs) at the Board of Veterans Appeals, leveraging quasi-random case assignment via the Caseflow ACD system. The authors construct a leave-one-out judge leniency instrument that exhibits exceptional strength (First Stage $F \approx 225$) and passes rigorous balance tests. A key contribution is the identification of a "subjectivity premium," where judge discretion varies systematically with the ambiguity of medical evidence, exerting the strongest influence on mental health claims compared to mechanical rating issues.

## 3. Essential Points

1.  **Scope Deviation on Outcome Analysis:** The Manifest committed to estimating causal effects on veteran welfare outcomes (mortality, employment), which is the primary policy motivation for the instrument. The paper currently delivers a valid instrument but no Second Stage estimates. For an *AER: Insights* piece, which prioritizes concise, high-impact empirical results, presenting only the First Stage may feel incomplete unless framed strictly as a data note. The authors must either link the administrative data to deliver the promised Second Stage or rigorously defend why the instrument validation alone constitutes a sufficient contribution for this venue.
2.  **Definition of the Complier Population (Remands):** The paper excludes remands (34% of cases) from the main analysis, treating them as procedural rather than substantive. However, the placebo test shows lenient judges remand *less*. This implies the instrument affects both the grant/deny margin and the remand margin. The Local Average Treatment Effect (LATE) interpretation depends critically on how these margins are handled. If lenient judges convert remands into grants, the compliers are veterans on the edge of procedural sufficiency, not just merit. The authors need to clarify whether the instrument identifies the effect of benefits for those who would otherwise be denied versus those who would otherwise be delayed via remand.
3.  **Inference with Finite Clusters:** Standard errors are clustered at the VLJ level ($J=106$). While this exceeds the common rule-of-thumb threshold ($J>50$), recent econometric literature (e.g., MacKinnon and Webb, 2017) suggests that standard cluster-robust inference can still be distorted with heterogeneous cluster sizes and few clusters. The paper reports two-way clustering in robustness checks, but given the high stakes of the policy implications, employing inference methods robust to few clusters (e.g., wild cluster bootstrap) would strengthen the credibility of the significance claims, particularly for the heterogeneity analysis where subsample cluster counts drop significantly.

## 4. Suggestions

**Second-Stage Implementation and Power Calculations**
The most critical next step is fulfilling the Manifest's promise to estimate outcome effects. The paper notes that linking to VA/SSA records is a "feasible next step," but for a revision, you should at least demonstrate feasibility or provide power calculations. Given the First Stage coefficient of $\approx 0.78$ and the sample size ($N \approx 11,000$), calculate the Minimum Detectable Effect (MDE) for key outcomes like mortality or employment. If the MDE is too large to detect meaningful policy effects, acknowledge this limitation explicitly. If feasible, even a reduced-form estimate (Intent-to-Treat of Judge Leniency on Outcomes) would be highly valuable. This would transform the paper from a methodological validation into a policy-relevant causal estimate, aligning it with the *AER: Insights* mission.

**Refining the Remand Margin**
The treatment of remands warrants deeper economic interpretation rather than just exclusion. In administrative law, a remand is often a "soft denial" that delays benefits significantly. If lenient judges remand less, they are effectively speeding up benefit receipt for marginal cases. Consider constructing a composite outcome variable (e.g., "Positive Outcome" = Grant OR Favorable Remand) or analyzing time-to-decision as an outcome. This captures the "speed vs. accuracy" trade-off inherent in administrative adjudication. Additionally, discuss whether the exclusion of remands introduces selection bias. If strict judges remand complex cases that later become grants upon return, excluding remands might underestimate the total benefit flow generated by strict judges over a longer horizon.

**Heterogeneity and Mechanism Deep Dive**
The "subjectivity premium" finding is the paper's most novel conceptual contribution. Expand this section to discuss *why* mental health claims are more subjective. Is it due to the lack of biomarkers? The reliance on veteran testimony? You could strengthen this by text-analyzing the decision narratives (which you have parsed). For instance, do lenient judges use different language regarding "credibility" or "symptom severity" in mental health cases compared to strict judges? Adding a few descriptive statistics on the text content (e.g., average length of decision, frequency of specific legal citations) could provide mechanistic evidence supporting the subjectivity claim, moving beyond the reduced-form heterogeneity.

**Inference and Robustness Enhancements**
Regarding the clustering issue, implement the wild cluster bootstrap percentile-t method for your main specifications. This is now standard practice in judge IV literature when the number of judges is in the low hundreds. Additionally, check for serial correlation in judge decisions. If a judge becomes more lenient over time (learning effects or burnout), the leave-one-out instrument might be noisy. Consider interacting leniency with time trends or including judge-specific linear time trends in the robustness checks to ensure the instrument captures persistent judge heterogeneity rather than temporary shocks.

**Policy Context and External Validity**
The paper mentions the Appeals Modernization Act (AMA) of 2017 as a limitation. This is crucial. The AMA fundamentally changed the appeals lanes (Direct Review, Evidence Submission, Hearing). Does judge leniency vary by lane? If the AMA allowed veterans to select lanes, and certain lanes are assigned to specific judges or panels, the random assignment assumption could be compromised. You should test balance separately for pre-AMA and post-AMA periods if possible, or control for appeal lane fixed effects. Furthermore, discuss external validity: do these VLJ effects generalize to the initial claim stage (C&P examiners) or other agencies (SSDI)? A brief comparative table of judge leniency magnitudes across literature (SSDI, Immigration, Criminal) would contextualize your $F=225$ and coefficient sizes.

**Writing and Formatting for AER: Insights**
*AER: Insights* has strict length constraints (typically 4-5 pages). The current LaTeX draft is concise but ensure the main text fits within the journal's specific formatting guidelines. The abstract is strong, but the Introduction could be tightened to foreground the policy stakes earlier. Currently, the mortality/employment link is buried in the "Limitations" section. If you cannot estimate these outcomes, frame the paper explicitly as "The Appeals Lottery: Instrument Validation and Discretion Patterns" to manage reader expectations. Finally, ensure the data appendix includes a clear statement on data availability and code replication, as the Manifest highlighted the public nature of the text files as a key advantage.

**Final Thought on Magnitudes**
The First Stage coefficient of 0.784 is economically large and statistically precise. However, ensure the interpretation of the "1 unit increase in leniency" is clear to readers. A 1 unit increase implies moving from a judge who never grants to one who always grants, which is counterfactual. Continue to emphasize the Interquartile Range (IQR) effects (as you did in the text: 9.4 percentage points) as the primary metric for policy discussion. This magnitude implies that reforming judge assignment or standardizing decision criteria could alter benefit receipt for nearly 10% of appellants—a substantial welfare implication worthy of the Second Stage analysis promised in the original idea.
