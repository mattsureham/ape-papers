## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple and important question: when regulators force lenders to intervene against “persistent” credit card debt, do households actually deleverage, or do lenders and borrowers simply adjust on other margins? Using the UK’s 2018 persistent-debt rule, the paper argues that the regulation reduced the relative importance of credit card borrowing while increasing card pricing, implying that targeted consumer protection can reshape the composition and price of household credit rather than just reducing indebtedness.

A busy economist should care because this is not just a paper about UK credit cards. It is about a broad policy question: when regulation targets a profitable but harmful contract feature, where does the incidence go?

Not quite. The current introduction is competent, but it opens as a somewhat familiar “here is a harmful market practice” setup and only gradually gets to the bigger question. The most interesting part of the paper is not “persistent debt exists” or even “the FCA acted.” It is the combination of two margins: regulation changed the composition of borrowing and appears to have triggered repricing. That is the hook.

The introduction should lead with the general question first, then make the UK policy a sharp setting for answering it.

> Consumer financial regulation often targets a narrow set of borrowers, but lenders can respond by adjusting prices and product supply for everyone else. This paper studies that incidence question using the UK’s 2018 “persistent debt” rule, which forced credit card issuers to intervene when customers revolved debt for too long. I show that the rule shifted consumer credit away from credit cards and toward other forms of borrowing, while the credit card–loan rate spread widened—suggesting that targeted deleveraging can come with broad repricing.

That is the AER version of the pitch. It is about regulatory incidence in consumer credit, not just one UK rule.

2. **Behavioral household finance papers** — these are about why borrowers remain in expensive debt.
3. **Industrial-organization/regulatory incidence papers** — these are about how firms adjust on margins not directly constrained.

The distinctive claim here is that **mandatory intervention regulation** differs from **price regulation**: it targets a subset of borrower trajectories, and therefore invites substitution and incidence questions.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Before this paper, regulators worried that many cardholders remained trapped in high-interest revolving debt for years, even while making payments. Credit card contracts are particularly vulnerable to inertia and misunderstanding because minimum payments permit debt persistence without formal delinquency.

### Tension

The regulatory response seems attractive on its face: force lenders to intervene when borrowers remain in persistent debt. But the core question is unresolved: does such a rule actually reduce harmful debt, or does it simply move borrowing elsewhere and induce lenders to recover costs through repricing?

### Resolution

The paper’s resolution is that the rule appears to have reduced the relative role of credit cards, with effects that line up with the rule’s escalation structure, while lenders simultaneously widened card pricing relative to personal loans.

### Implications

The implication is that targeted consumer protection may work less by reducing aggregate indebtedness than by changing product mix and redistributing regulatory costs across market participants. That should change how economists think about both the effectiveness and incidence of consumer-finance regulation.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has one, but it is not yet cleanly executed. At present the paper feels somewhat like **a collection of empirical findings in search of a unifying claim**:

- card balances relative to loans,
- threshold timing,
- rates,
- net lending,
- share of credit,
- placebo caveats.

These pieces can support a compelling story, but the manuscript currently oscillates between:
1. “the rule reduced persistent debt,”
2. “actually it mostly shifted composition,” and
3. “the deeper result is repricing.”

That creates narrative instability.

### What story should it be telling?

The paper should tell one story:

> The FCA designed a targeted intervention to reduce persistent revolving debt. The policy mattered—but its main effect was not simple deleveraging. Instead, it altered the composition of borrowing and induced repricing, revealing that the incidence of targeted consumer protection extends beyond the borrowers it directly aims to help.

Everything should serve that story. The threshold evidence, the balance evidence, and the pricing evidence are all supporting acts to that central claim.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Immediately:

> “Did the policy actually improve welfare, or did it just reshuffle borrowing and spread costs to other consumers?”

That is the right follow-up question, and the paper should anticipate it more directly. It need not answer it fully, but it needs to frame the results as informative about that question.

A second follow-up would be:

> “Is this about borrower behavior, lender incentives, or product substitution?”

Again, the paper should be more explicit that its core contribution is showing these margins move together.

### If findings are modest or partial, is that okay?

Yes—if framed correctly. The paper’s strongest version is not “we prove a huge drop in harmful debt.” It is “we show the policy changed equilibrium margins in economically meaningful ways, but not in the simplistic way regulators might hope.”

That is publishable if sold as a lesson about incidence and substitution rather than a triumphalist policy evaluation.
