### 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper tests whether the world’s first statutory mandate for supply-chain diversification—the EU’s Critical Raw Materials Act (CRMA)—actually forced firms to diversify their mineral sourcing. Using a continuous-treatment difference-in-differences design, the author finds a precisely estimated zero effect: the mandate, which lacks immediate enforcement or subsidies, has failed to move the needle on import concentration in its first two years. This matters because it challenges the efficacy of "soft" industrial policy and highlights the massive friction of switching costs in global value chains.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this well, but it buries the "why" in the third paragraph. The first two paragraphs focus on the "what."

**The Pitch the Paper Should Have:** 
"Can governments legislate supply-chain resilience into existence? This paper evaluates the EU’s Critical Raw Materials Act, the first law to mandate a 65% ceiling on import concentration for strategic minerals. I find that this mandate has had zero impact on actual sourcing behavior, demonstrating that without financial 'carrots' or regulatory 'sticks,' statutory targets are insufficient to overcome the deep-seated inertia of global mineral supply chains."

---

### 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution:** The paper provides the first empirical evidence that "mandates without mechanisms" fail to restructure trade relationships in the short run, even under high geopolitical tension.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It differentiates itself well from the theoretical work (Antràs, 2020) by providing a "clean" test of a specific, novel policy instrument.
*   **Framing:** It is framed as a question about the **WORLD** (Does industrial policy work?), which is its strongest asset.
*   **Clarity:** A smart economist would say: "It’s a paper showing the EU’s mineral targets are currently just paper tigers."
*   **Bigger Contribution:** To move from "interesting" to "essential," the paper needs to more aggressively contrast the CRMA with the US Inflation Reduction Act (IRA). Is the difference the *magnitude* of the incentive or the *type* of incentive?

---

### 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

*   **Closest Neighbors:** Antràs (2020) on GVC resilience; Barrot & Sauvagnat (2016) on input specificity; Evenett et al. (2024) on the "new" industrial policy.
*   **Positioning:** It currently sits as a "policy evaluation." It should move toward "testing trade theory." It should frame itself as a test of **switching costs and contract rigidity**. 
*   **Niche vs. Broad:** It is currently a bit niche (EU policy). To broaden, it needs to speak more to the *political economy* of why governments pass "toothless" laws.
*   **Missing Lit:** It needs to engage more with the literature on **"Announcements as Signals."** Does the mandate signal future tariffs? If so, why is there no anticipatory effect?

---

### 4. NARRATIVE ARC

*   **Setup:** Global supply chains are dangerously concentrated (e.g., China/Rare Earths).
*   **Tension:** The EU passed a historic law to fix this, but the law has no immediate penalties. Theory suggests firms might ignore it; policy-makers hope for a "signaling" effect.
*   **Resolution:** Firms ignored it. Concentration remains unchanged.
*   **Implications:** Industrial policy requires "hard" instruments (subsidies/tariffs) to overcome the high sunk costs of mineral infrastructure.

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong. However, the "Resolution" section is weakened by the pre-trend violation mentioned in Section 4.3. The author needs to lean into the "even under favorable trends, it failed" argument more forcefully to maintain the narrative.

---

### 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

*   **The Lead Fact:** "The EU mandated a 65% import cap, and two years later, HHI hasn't moved by a single point."
*   **Reaction:** People will lean in because everyone is talking about "de-risking" from China. 
*   **The Follow-up:** "Is it because they can't diversify, or because they know the EU won't actually punish them?" This is the question the paper needs to answer more deeply in the discussion.

---

### 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

*   **Front-loading:** The "Dual-Shock Decomposition" (Section 4.2) is the most intellectually interesting part—distinguishing between the EU's "pull" and China's "push." This should be elevated.
*   **Appendix:** The "leave-one-mineral-out" tests are standard; keep them brief.
*   **Discussion:** Section 6 is excellent but needs to be more "economic" and less "policy-focused." Use the null result to estimate a lower bound on the switching costs for these minerals.

---

### 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The gap is **Ambition**. Currently, it feels like a very high-quality *Journal of Public Economics* or *Journal of International Economics* paper. 

**Single Most Impactful Advice:** 
The author needs to transform the "null result" from a policy failure into a **structural parameter estimate.** Use the fact that firms *didn't* move despite the law to back-out the implied switching costs or the "credibility gap" of EU enforcement. If the author can say, "For a mandate to work, it would need a subsidy of at least $X/ton to overcome current inertia," it becomes a landmark paper.
