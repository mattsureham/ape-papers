## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple, policy-relevant question: when governments impose headline diversification mandates on strategically important imports, do firms actually rewire supply chains? Studying the EU’s Critical Raw Materials Act—the first law to set an explicit cap on dependence on any one foreign supplier for strategic minerals—the paper finds little short-run evidence that highly concentrated mineral imports diversified more than less-concentrated ones.

A busy economist should care because the broader question is not really about 17 minerals. It is about whether modern industrial policy can move real economic organization through targets and mandates alone, or whether supply chains are too sticky to respond without subsidies, procurement rules, tariffs, or other hard instruments.

### Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Not quite. The current opening is decent, but it still reads like a policy note attached to a design. It gets to “first econometric test” too quickly and frames the contribution as testing a regulation rather than answering the more general economic question: **Can soft industrial policy mandates change sourcing behavior in concentrated global supply chains?**

The first two paragraphs should be less “here is my empirical strategy” and more “here is the world-level puzzle.” Right now, the introduction risks sounding like “a DiD paper on the CRMA.” For AER, the opening needs to make clear that the CRMA is a sharp test of a first-order issue in industrial organization, international trade, and political economy of industrial policy.

### The pitch the paper should have

Governments around the world are trying to reengineer supply chains for strategic goods, but it is still unclear whether regulation can actually force diversification when production is geographically concentrated and switching suppliers is costly. The EU’s Critical Raw Materials Act offers a rare test: it sets the world’s first explicit legal ceiling on dependence on any single foreign supplier for key minerals, effectively asking firms to diversify away from dominant sources such as China.

This paper studies whether such a mandate changed sourcing behavior in the short run. Using trade data across minerals with different pre-policy exposure to the concentration cap, it finds little evidence that the most exposed minerals diversified more after the policy. The broader message is that industrial policy targets may be politically salient but economically weak unless paired with instruments that directly alter firms’ incentives or expand alternative supply.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### Contribution in one sentence

The paper’s contribution is to show that a prominent real-world diversification mandate in strategic minerals did not generate detectable short-run changes in import concentration, suggesting that industrial policy targets alone may not reconfigure supply chains.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest papers?

Only partially. The paper repeatedly says “first econometric test,” but that is not enough. “First test of policy X” is rarely a sufficient top-journal contribution unless policy X is itself central enough, or unless the paper uses policy X to settle a broader debate. The current draft does not clearly distinguish itself from:

- broader work on supply-chain resilience and firm adjustment to shocks,
- descriptive policy work on critical minerals dependence,
- event-driven studies of export controls and trade rerouting,
- and the growing empirical literature on industrial policy implementation.

The differentiation is still too legalistic and too narrow. The paper is strongest if it says: existing work shows supply chains are concentrated and sticky; many governments now try to reshape them; here is unusually clean evidence that a highly visible diversification target by itself did not move sourcing. That is stronger than “this is the first paper on the CRMA.”

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a gap in a literature?

Mostly as filling a literature gap. That is weaker.

It should be framed as a world question:

- **Do diversification mandates work?**
- **How fast can industrial policy change concentrated international supply chains?**
- **Are governments overestimating what statutory targets can accomplish without complementary tools?**

Those are live questions about the world. “No paper has yet estimated the effect of the CRMA” is true but not exciting enough.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?

Right now, maybe, but with some hesitation. They might say: “It’s a paper on whether the EU critical minerals act changed import concentration, and it finds no effect.” That is understandable, but still sounds like “another DiD paper about a recent policy.”

The introduction does not yet arm the reader with a sharper takeaway like: “This is evidence that declaratory industrial policy is not enough to rewire supply chains in the short run.” That is the version colleagues would remember.

### What would make the contribution bigger?

Several possibilities:

1. **Make the object of study broader than the CRMA.**  
   Treat the CRMA as one revealing case of a larger class of “mandates without instruments.” The title and introduction should advertise that larger category.

2. **Sharpen the contrast with hard industrial policy.**  
   The paper repeatedly mentions the IRA/CHIPS comparison, but too loosely. If it could systematically compare “soft target-setting” versus “policies that attach money or market access to compliance,” the contribution would immediately get larger.

3. **Show implications for economically meaningful margins beyond import HHI.**  
   The current outcome is sensible but narrow. A bigger paper would connect concentration changes to prices, import volumes, supplier entry, contract proxies, downstream industry exposure, or stock-market reactions in exposed EU sectors. Even if those are not feasible, the paper should explain why concentration is the economically central margin.

4. **Develop mechanism more explicitly.**  
   The discussion says “switching costs, search frictions, contracts, geological constraints.” Right now this is plausible but generic. If the paper can more concretely show that non-response is strongest where those frictions should bind most, the story becomes more economic and less policy-journalistic.

5. **Extend the framing from “null effect” to “policy implementation failure under supply rigidity.”**  
   That is bigger than a null; it becomes evidence on the boundary conditions of state capacity and industrial policy.

### How should the paper position itself relative to those neighbors?

Mostly **build on and synthesize**, not attack.

This is not a paper that overturns trade theory or disputes the supply-chain literature. In fact, the findings are broadly consistent with the idea that concentrated sourcing is persistent because of fixed costs, contracts, and limited alternative capacity. The paper should therefore say:

- the theory and prior descriptive work imply high inertia,
- policymakers nevertheless behave as if targets can induce rapid diversification,
- this paper provides evidence on whether that optimism is justified.

That is a clean contribution. No need for overstated claims of novelty.

### Is the paper positioned too narrowly or too broadly?

At present, oddly, both.

- **Too narrowly** because much of the paper reads as if the audience is specialists in EU critical-minerals policy.
- **Too broadly** because it occasionally gestures at all industrial policy, all resilience policy, and CHIPS/IRA/Japan without doing enough conceptual work to justify those leaps.

The paper needs a more disciplined middle ground: it is a paper for economists interested in **how industrial policy instruments translate—or fail to translate—into changes in firm behavior in global supply chains.**

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?

The paper seems under-engaged with at least three strands:

1. **Implementation/administrative-state literature in economics and political economy.**  
   The core message is really about the gap between announced policy and realized market response. That is not just trade; it is implementation.

2. **Literature on anticipation, policy signaling, and target-setting.**  
   If firms react to proposals, expectations, or symbolic commitments, that matters conceptually even if the paper’s empirical horizon is short.

3. **Industrial organization of procurement/supplier relationships.**  
   If the explanation is switching costs and contracts, the paper should sound more grounded in the economics of buyer-supplier relationships and less like a general commentary on “inertia.”

### Is the paper having the right conversation?

Not yet fully. It is currently having the conversation “Did the CRMA work?” The more impactful conversation is “What kinds of industrial policy instruments can actually rewire global sourcing?” That is the right conversation for AER readers.

The unexpected but valuable connection would be to the literature on **policy implementation and organizational frictions**: governments can announce ambitious quantitative goals, but the ability to move decentralized private sourcing decisions depends on whether the policy changes incentives at the margin.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

The world has become acutely aware of dependence on concentrated foreign suppliers for strategic inputs, especially critical minerals. Governments respond by announcing diversification strategies and treating concentration itself as a policy problem.

### Tension

It is unclear whether a mandate or statutory target can actually change behavior in markets where supply is geologically concentrated, processing capacity is lumpy, and buyer-supplier relationships are sticky. Policymakers speak as if announcing a ceiling on dependence should produce diversification; economics suggests that might be wishful thinking.

### Resolution

The paper studies the EU’s flagship diversification mandate and finds no detectable short-run differential diversification among the most exposed minerals.

### Implications

The implication is not merely “the CRMA failed.” It is that **soft industrial policy may be mostly expressive unless paired with tools that change payoffs or capacity**. That matters for current debates on de-risking, friend-shoring, strategic autonomy, and the design of industrial policy worldwide.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

Serviceable, but not fully coherent. It has the ingredients of a strong narrative, but they are not organized with enough discipline. At points it feels like a collection of results—main regression, event study, China decomposition, robustness, null interpretation—rather than one integrated story.

The strongest story it could tell is:

1. **Governments want diversification.**
2. **The EU introduced the clearest legal mandate yet.**
3. **If mandates alone can work, this is where we should see it.**
4. **We do not.**
5. **Therefore, the binding constraint is not policymakers’ declarations but economic frictions and missing complementary instruments.**

That is the story. Everything else should serve it.

At present, some material distracts from that story:
- the “first econometric test” language,
- overemphasis on technical design in the intro,
- the China decomposition, which feels somewhat bolted on,
- and the conclusion’s slogan-like rhetoric, which is memorable but a bit thin.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?

“The EU passed the world’s first legal cap on dependence on any one foreign supplier for strategic minerals, and two years later the most exposed minerals do not appear to have diversified any more than the less exposed ones.”

That is a good opening fact.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

Some would lean in—especially trade, IO, political economy, and macro-network people—but only if the presenter quickly pivots from “EU minerals policy” to “what industrial policy can and cannot do.” If the conversation stays at the level of a specific regulation and a null finding, attention will fade quickly.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Probably one of these:

- “Why didn’t firms respond—because the policy lacked teeth, or because diversification was impossible anyway?”
- “Is this just too early, given 2030 targets?”
- “How generalizable is this beyond critical minerals?”
- “How does this compare with policies that attach subsidies or procurement access to compliance?”

Those are healthy follow-up questions. In fact, the paper should anticipate them much better in the introduction and discussion.

### Is the null result itself interesting?

Yes, but only conditionally. AER can publish nulls if the setting is important and the null meaningfully updates beliefs. This one can be interesting because the policy is high-profile and because there was real public rhetoric around urgent de-risking.

But the paper must work harder to show that this is not just “we looked too soon and found nothing.” Right now that concern hangs over the whole exercise. The draft says the null is informative, but the case is not yet fully persuasive at the framing level. It needs to say more clearly:

- why a short-run response was plausible ex ante,
- what margins could have moved quickly if the mandate mattered,
- and what the absence of movement tells us about the mechanism of policy transmission.

Otherwise it risks reading like a failed experiment conducted before the experiment had time to work.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The gap is primarily **framing and ambition**, with some **scope** concerns.

This is not mainly a technical problem. The central issue is that the paper currently reads like a competent, timely evaluation of one EU policy rather than a field-shaping statement about what industrial policy can achieve in reconfiguring global supply chains.

### Is it a framing problem?

Yes, strongly. The science may be fine for what it is, but the story is too policy-specific and too close to a narrow null-result event study. It needs to become a paper on the economics of industrial policy implementation under supply rigidity.

### Is it a scope problem?

Also yes. The current scope is thin for AER because the paper leans heavily on one setting, one main outcome family, and a short window. To reach AER level, it likely needs either:
- richer outcomes/mechanisms, or
- a broader comparative frame, or
- a much more powerful conceptual contribution.

### Is it a novelty problem?

Somewhat. The high-level message—supply chains are sticky, mandates alone may not work—is plausible and not shocking. The novelty must therefore come from the setting and the clarity of the evidence. Right now the paper does not yet elevate that enough.

### Is it an ambition problem?

Yes. The paper is respectable but safe. It asks a bounded question and answers it narrowly. AER papers usually make readers feel that they have learned something broader about how the economy works, not merely about one regulation.

### The single most impactful piece of advice

**Reframe the paper as evidence on the limits of target-based industrial policy in concentrated global supply chains, and reorganize every section around that broader economic question rather than around the CRMA as a standalone case.**

That one change would do the most to move the paper from niche policy evaluation toward something that belongs in a general-interest top journal conversation.
