## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple and policy-relevant question: when a major government announces a legal mandate to diversify strategically important imports, do firms actually rewire supply chains? Looking at the EU’s Critical Raw Materials Act and trade patterns for strategic minerals, the paper’s headline claim is that the answer is no—at least in the short run, concentration barely moved.

A busy economist should care because “resilience industrial policy” is now everywhere, and the profession still knows surprisingly little about whether these policies change real sourcing behavior versus merely expressing political intent. If the first statutory import-diversification mandate in the world does not visibly move trade flows, that is potentially an important fact.

**Does the paper articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Reasonably, but not optimally. The current introduction gets to the point quickly, which is good, but it frames the contribution too much as “the first econometric test of the CRMA” and too little as a broader question about whether industrial policy mandates can overcome supply-chain inertia. “First econometric test” is not an AER pitch. “Mandates without instruments may not move markets” is.

**What the first two paragraphs should say instead:**  
The paper should open with the world fact, then the broader question, then the headline answer. Something like:

> Governments across Europe, the United States, and Asia are trying to make supply chains more resilient by reducing dependence on dominant foreign suppliers, especially in critical minerals. But a central unanswered question is whether announcing diversification targets actually changes firms’ sourcing decisions, or whether supply chains remain sticky unless governments also change relative prices through subsidies, procurement, or trade restrictions.
>
> This paper studies the first major real-world test of that question: the EU Critical Raw Materials Act, which sets a legal benchmark that no more than 65 percent of EU consumption of strategic minerals should come from a single foreign country. Using bilateral trade data across minerals with different pre-policy exposure to the mandate, I find little evidence of short-run diversification. The broader implication is that industrial policy goals alone may not be enough to reallocate sourcing in concentrated upstream markets.

That is the pitch the paper should have.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution in one sentence:**  
The paper claims to show that the EU’s landmark critical-minerals diversification mandate did not materially alter short-run import concentration, suggesting that industrial policy targets without stronger instruments may not change sourcing behavior.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest papers?
Only partially. The paper says “first causal evidence” and “first econometric test,” but that is not enough differentiation. The real neighbors are not just papers on critical minerals; they are broader papers on supply-chain resilience, sourcing responses to shocks, and industrial policy instruments. Relative to that literature, the paper’s distinctive angle is:

1. a policy that directly targets concentration rather than prices,
2. a setting where the state wants diversification but does not strongly subsidize or compel it,
3. a result suggesting that declaratory policy may be weak.

That differentiation is present, but it is not sharpened enough. Right now, a smart economist could still summarize it as “another reduced-form paper on whether a new policy changed trade outcomes.” The paper needs to tell readers why this policy is conceptually different from tariffs, sanctions, domestic content rules, export controls, or procurement subsidies.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or as filling a gap in a LITERATURE?
Too much the latter. “First econometric test” and “contributes to several literatures” are literature-gap moves. The stronger framing is a world question:

- Can governments diversify concentrated supply chains by setting targets alone?
- Are firms responsive to resilience mandates absent price incentives or enforcement?
- What kinds of industrial policy tools actually move upstream trade patterns?

That is a much bigger and more interesting question than “no one has yet estimated this policy.”

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the intro?
Not confidently enough. They might say: “It’s a DiD on the CRMA and finds no effect.” That is not a strong introduction outcome. They should instead be able to say: “It studies whether non-price diversification mandates can move highly concentrated strategic import markets, and finds little immediate response—suggesting limits of resilience policy by proclamation.”

### What would make this contribution bigger?
Several specific ways:

1. **Broaden the dependent variable from import concentration to adjustment margins.**  
   Right now the paper mostly says concentration did not move. Bigger would be to ask: did firms start experimenting at the extensive margin, even if concentration did not yet fall? Examples: entry of new suppliers, small trial shipments, within-EU processing investments, contract announcements, stockpiling, or increased sourcing from “approved” partners.

2. **Make the policy-comparison explicit.**  
   The paper gestures at the IRA/CHIPS contrast, but that comparison could be the whole point: mandates alone versus mandates tied to subsidies or tax credits. A cross-policy comparative framing would be much larger than a single-policy event study.

3. **Separate “no effect” from “too early to see effects.”**  
   This is the obvious follow-up question readers will ask immediately. If the paper can document that there was also no movement in leading indicators of future diversification, the null becomes much more consequential.

4. **Lean harder into mechanism.**  
   The mechanism section is currently generic: switching costs, contracts, geology. The paper needs a sharper empirical mechanism story if it wants to rise above “policy didn’t do much.” For instance: null effects are strongest where processing is bottlenecked, where supplier alternatives are sparse, or where dependence is on China rather than a geopolitically aligned producer.

5. **Use the setting to answer a broader industrial-policy design question.**  
   The bigger contribution is not “the CRMA didn’t work yet.” It is “non-price industrial policy tools do little in concentrated input markets unless they alter returns to switching.”

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
The world has become newly anxious about concentrated supply chains in strategic upstream inputs, especially where China dominates. Policymakers are responding with ambitious resilience policies, and the EU’s CRMA is a marquee case because it writes diversification directly into law.

### Tension
There is a gap between policy ambition and economic reality. Supply chains are sticky, mineral markets are geologically and technologically constrained, and mandates may lack bite if they do not alter prices or penalties. So the core tension is: **Can a legal diversification target move sourcing on its own?**

### Resolution
The paper’s answer is basically no, at least so far. More-exposed minerals do not appear to diversify more than less-exposed minerals.

### Implications
The implication is potentially important: industrial policy design matters. Targets and declarations may not be enough; governments may need subsidies, procurement, trade restrictions, stockpiles, or other harder instruments if they want real supply-chain reorganization.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
It has the bones of one, but it is not yet fully disciplined. Right now it is somewhere between a real story and a collection of sensible exercises around a null result. The reason is that the paper keeps slipping from a big claim—“mandates without teeth don’t work”—into a much smaller claim—“this one policy does not seem to have moved one set of trade outcomes in a short window.”

The story it **should** be telling is:

1. Governments are trying to legislate resilience.
2. This is a clean test of whether mandates alone can rewire concentrated sourcing.
3. They appear not to.
4. Therefore, the design of industrial policy instruments—not just policy objectives—matters critically.

That story is coherent and worth hearing. The present draft gets there, but too late and too cautiously.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?
I would lead with: **“The EU passed the world’s first legal cap on dependence on any one foreign supplier for strategic minerals, and the paper says it had basically no short-run effect on import concentration.”**

That is a decent lead. People would at least look up.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
Initially lean in. But the very next question would determine whether they stay engaged:

- “Is that because mandates don’t work, or because it’s way too early?”
- “Was there truly no adjustment at any margin?”
- “How different is this from the US approach, where subsidies and domestic-content rules create stronger incentives?”

If the paper cannot answer those follow-ups, interest will dissipate.

### If the findings are null or modest, is the null itself interesting?
Yes, potentially. Null results can be important when:
1. the policy is highly salient,
2. theory or policy rhetoric predicts movement,
3. the null helps discipline real-world beliefs.

This paper meets (1) and partly (2). It needs to make (3) much more forcefully. The paper should be explicit that policymakers, journalists, and perhaps some economists talk as if announcing diversification goals is itself an action. The interesting null is that **declaration is not implementation**.

Right now, though, the paper is at risk of feeling like a failed attempt to find effects because the window is short and because the policy is obviously long-horizon. To make the null feel substantive rather than merely premature, the paper needs to sharpen what a meaningful early response would have looked like and show that even those early markers are absent.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

Bluntly: in its current form, this is **not yet an AER paper**. The main gap is not just framing, though framing is part of it. It is a combination of **scope, ambition, and novelty**.

### What is the gap?

#### 1. Framing problem
Yes. The paper currently sells itself as “the first test of the CRMA.” That is too small. AER needs a question readers outside this niche immediately recognize as first-order.

#### 2. Scope problem
Also yes. The paper is trying to infer something broad about industrial policy effectiveness from one narrow outcome—short-run import concentration—for a short horizon in a policy that was designed with a 2030 endpoint. That leaves the paper vulnerable to the “too early / wrong margin” objection. It needs either richer outcomes or a more disciplined claim.

#### 3. Novelty problem
Partly. The “policy announced, little immediate trade response” fact is not inherently surprising. To be top-tier, the paper must convert that into a sharper conceptual contribution about policy instrument design or supply-chain inertia.

#### 4. Ambition problem
Yes. The paper feels competent but safe. It documents an intuitively plausible null in a salient policy setting. That is useful, but AER usually wants either a genuinely new empirical fact of broad importance, a substantial conceptual advance, or a setting that decisively reshapes how we think about a big question. This draft is not there yet.

### Single most impactful advice
**Reframe the paper around a broader and more ambitious question—whether industrial policy can rewire concentrated supply chains without changing firms’ economic incentives—and then add evidence on early adjustment margins or comparative policy design so the paper speaks to that question rather than merely reporting a short-run null on one EU regulation.**

That is the one thing. If the authors do only one thing, it should be to stop writing this as a paper about the CRMA and start writing it as a paper about the limits of non-price resilience mandates in concentrated input markets.
