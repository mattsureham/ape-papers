## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
This paper examines the impact of the U.S. Gainful Employment (GE) Rule on the for-profit education market, specifically testing whether the effects of transparency are reversible. By exploiting the 2015 implementation (disclosure and sanction) and the 2019 repeal (removal of both), it asks whether information-driven market contraction persists even after the regulatory "threat" is gone. It finds a massive, permanent contraction in the for-profit sector that suggests information creates a "chill" and reputational damage that persists regardless of the current regulatory environment.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this well in the abstract and the first paragraph. It successfully pivots from a narrow education policy question to a broader "puzzle" about the symmetry of information effects. This is the correct "AER-style" hook.

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**One-sentence contribution:** The paper documents that information disclosure in the higher education market triggers an irreversible restructuring of the sector, where even the removal of disclosure and sanctions fails to induce market re-entry or recovery.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It differentiates itself from Cellini et al. (2020) by looking at the *full cycle* (including repeal) and by focusing on program-level exit rather than just enrollment.
*   **Framing:** It is framed as a question about the WORLD (specifically the permanence of "reputational damage" vs. "regulatory sanctions").
*   **What would make it bigger?** To reach the AER’s bar for "General Interest," the author needs to strengthen the evidence for the **information** mechanism versus the **sunk cost** mechanism. If the exit is permanent simply because it’s expensive to reopen a school, that’s a less "exciting" economic insight than if the exit is permanent because the "for-profit" brand was irrevocably stigmatized in the eyes of consumers.

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
The paper sits at the intersection of the **Economics of Information** (Jin & Leslie, 2003; Dranove et al., 2003) and **Education Markets** (Deming, Goldin, Katz, 2012).

*   **Closest neighbors:** Cellini, Darolia, and Turner (2020) on GE effects; Jin and Leslie (2003) on hygiene grades; and the broader literature on collective reputation (Tirole, 1996; Deming et al., 2016).
*   **Strategy:** It currently builds on these neighbors. It should more aggressively position itself as a test of **Asymmetric Information Response**.
*   **Missing Conversations:** The paper should speak more to the **Industrial Organization (IO)** literature on market entry/exit and sunk costs. If the "Disclosure Chill" is just a story about irreversible investment under uncertainty, it should cite Dixit and Pindyck. If it’s about consumer learning, it needs to link to models of Bayesian updating where "bad news" is harder to forget than "no news."

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC
*   **Setup:** The for-profit sector was booming but suspect; the GE Rule introduced a "quality score."
*   **Tension:** The rule was repealed in 2019. Standard theory might suggest a rebound once the "tax" (sanction/disclosure) is removed.
*   **Resolution:** No rebound occurred. The sector continued to collapse. 
*   **Implications:** Information is "sticky." Once you tell the public a sector is low-quality, you cannot "un-tell" them by simply stopping the reports.

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong. It is a "story" paper, not just a "result" paper.

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
At an economics dinner party, you lead with: *"When the government stopped telling people which for-profit colleges were bad, the colleges still died. The reputational stain was permanent."*

People lean in because this contradicts the "regulatory burden" narrative and supports a "consumer power" narrative. The follow-up question is: *"Is it just because it's hard to reopen a building, or did the students actually stop wanting to go there?"* The paper needs to answer that follow-up more convincingly to survive an AER referee.

---

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
*   **Front-loading:** The "Repeal Test" in Table 4 is the most important part of the paper for a general audience. It should be elevated.
*   **Mechanisms:** Section 5.3 and the Discussion need more empirical meat. Can the author show "Search" data (Google Trends) during the repeal to show that consumer interest stayed low even when the scores vanished? That would prove the "Information" channel over the "Sunk Cost" channel.

---

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The distance is **Medium**. The science is competent, and the story is "Big." 

**The Gap:** The paper currently admits that "pre-existing sectoral decline and contemporaneous regulatory actions prevent clean causal attribution." An AER paper usually needs to solve that problem rather than just acknowledge it. 

**Single Biggest Advice:** Use the **"Low-Risk" programs (Healthcare)** more effectively as a "Stigma" test. If healthcare programs—which passed the test—still exited at high rates after the repeal, it proves the *entire sector's brand* was destroyed by the disclosure of its peers. This "Spillover of Stigma" is a much bigger AER story than just "Regulations killed the bad schools."
