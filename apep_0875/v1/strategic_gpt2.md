## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether public quality disclosure can permanently shrink a market even after the disclosure regime is repealed. Using the rise and fall of the federal Gainful Employment rule, it documents that for-profit college programs exited at much higher rates after the government began publishing program quality information, and that repeal did not bring them back—suggesting that disclosure may trigger durable market restructuring rather than temporary adjustment.

A busy economist should care because this is potentially a broad point about the economics of information policy, not just a paper about for-profit colleges: when governments publish quality scores, they may not merely discipline firms in the short run; they may irreversibly reorganize markets.

### Does the paper articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Not quite. The current opening is vivid and energetic, but it muddies the central claim by mixing three stories: existential sanctions, public scores, and a sector already in decline. The paper’s best hook is not “for-profits were hit hard by GE.” That is already plausible. The hook is “repeal did not undo the contraction.” That asymmetry is the interesting idea, and it should be front and center immediately.

The current first two paragraphs also overstate the paper’s ability to isolate “information” from “regulation,” then walk that back later. For editorial positioning, the introduction should lead with the asymmetry as a fact and frame the paper as evidence consistent with irreversible disclosure effects, while being explicit that this is not a clean decomposition of disclosure versus sanction.

### The pitch the paper should have

> Governments increasingly regulate markets by publishing quality information. But when a disclosure regime is later withdrawn, does the market revert—or does the information release leave permanent scars?  
>   
> This paper studies that question in U.S. for-profit higher education using the 2015 introduction and 2020 repeal of the Gainful Employment regime. I show that for-profit programs contracted sharply relative to comparable public programs after the rule’s introduction, and that repeal did not reverse that contraction. The central contribution is to document an asymmetry: quality disclosure and associated regulatory scrutiny can trigger market exit that appears persistent even after the formal regime is removed.

That is the AER-facing pitch. It makes the paper about a general economic question and uses education as the setting.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### Contribution in one sentence

The paper’s contribution is to document that the introduction of the Gainful Employment disclosure/sanction regime was associated with a large contraction of for-profit college programs, while repeal did not restore the sector, consistent with asymmetric and potentially irreversible effects of public quality disclosure.

The paper needs sharper differentiation from:

- **Deming, Goldin, and Katz (2012)** on the rise and character of the for-profit sector.
- **Cellini and Goldin (2014)** on for-profit/postsecondary institutions and quality.
- **Deming et al. (2016)** on value-added / returns / collective reputation issues in for-profit higher ed.

- Plus classic disclosure papers like **Jin and Leslie (2003)**, **Dranove et al. (2003)**, **Figlio and Lucas (2004)**.

The current introduction says, in effect: prior work studied enrollment and student outcomes; I study program survival over the implementation and repeal cycle. That is clear enough, but it still sounds like “another policy evaluation in for-profit education” rather than a broader result about how disclosure policies reshape supply.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or filling a gap in a LITERATURE?

It starts as a world question, which is good, but repeatedly falls back into literature-gap framing: “first program-level tracking,” “no published study tracks the universe of exits,” etc. That weakens it.

The stronger world question is:

- **Can government quality disclosure permanently shrink low-quality sectors, even if the formal rule disappears?**

That is much stronger than:

- **No one has yet tracked program exits over the whole GE cycle.**

The latter is true but small.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the intro?

At present, maybe, but not crisply. A smart economist might say: “It’s a DiD paper showing for-profit college programs shrank a lot around Gainful Employment, and they didn’t rebound after repeal.” That is not bad. But they might also say: “So it’s another paper on the for-profit sector getting crushed in the 2010s.” That is the danger.

The introduction does not yet force the reader to understand that the new thing is **the onset/withdrawal asymmetry** and the broader idea of **irreversible disclosure effects**.

### What would make the contribution bigger?

Specific ways to enlarge it:

1. **Reframe the core estimand around asymmetry, not effect size.**  
   The paper’s most interesting fact is not “GE hurt for-profits.” It is “repeal did not reverse the effect.” Everything should orbit that asymmetry.

2. **Link more directly to market structure and supply reallocation.**  
   If there is any evidence on whether public/nonprofit programs expanded in the same fields/markets as for-profit exit occurred, that would greatly elevate the paper. “Disclosure shrank for-profits” is narrower than “disclosure reallocated educational supply across sectors.”

3. **Show who/what learned.**  
   The paper currently invokes students, employers, and reputation, but at a very high level. Any evidence tying the effect more concretely to information salience—media, search, applicant behavior, employer responses—would make the story much bigger.

4. **Clarify whether the phenomenon is sector stigma or program-specific revelation.**  
   Right now the paper gestures toward collective reputation. If that is the real story, it should be brought to the front. “Disclosure of program quality created sector-wide stigma” is more novel than “bad programs closed.”

5. **Connect to reversibility in regulation generally.**  
   The paper could be more ambitious by making the claim that some information policies have hysteresis: repealing the rule does not repeal the information. That is a broader economics insight.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Governments increasingly publish quality information to discipline providers. In higher education, the Gainful Employment regime created public quality signals and sanctions targeted at for-profit programs, in a sector already under scrutiny.

### Tension

We know disclosure can affect behavior while it is in force, but we know much less about what happens when disclosure is withdrawn. If the effects work mainly through contemporaneous incentives, repeal should permit recovery. If disclosure changes beliefs, reputation, and market structure, repeal may not undo the damage.

### Resolution

The paper finds that for-profit programs contracted sharply after GE and did not recover after repeal. The continued elevated exit is interpreted as evidence consistent with durable disclosure effects and irreversible restructuring.

### Implications

Quality disclosure may have long-lived consequences that survive policy reversal. Policymakers should think of disclosure regimes not as temporary nudges but as interventions that can permanently alter provider survival and sector composition.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

Serviceable, but not clean. The paper has the ingredients of a strong narrative, but it keeps interrupting its own story. It wants to tell an asymmetry story, but repeatedly collapses into a standard policy-effect paper about for-profit decline.

The biggest narrative problem is that the paper introduces a provocative mechanism—“disclosure chill”—before earning it, then spends much of the discussion explaining why attribution is difficult. That creates whiplash: strong conceptual claim up front, cautionary retreat later.

### If it is a collection of results looking for a story, what story should it be telling?

The story should be:

> “This is a paper about reversibility. The GE regime generated a public signal about sector quality. Once that signal was released and amplified, the market did not return to baseline when the rule disappeared. In settings with stigma, sunk exit costs, and collective reputation, information policy may be one-way.”

That story is much better than:

> “For-profit colleges were already declining, then GE happened, and the decline continued after repeal.”

The second is true but not editorially exciting.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Immediately:  
**“How do you know this is disclosure rather than the general crackdown and collapse of the sector?”**

The paper does not need to fully answer it here, but it does need to preemptively define its claim in a way that survives that challenge. The correct strategic answer is not “we prove it’s disclosure.” It is: “We document an asymmetry around policy introduction and withdrawal that is hard to reconcile with a purely temporary sanction story, though it cannot cleanly isolate disclosure from broader GE-era scrutiny.”

If the paper owns that, the finding remains interesting.

### If findings are modest or null, is the null itself interesting?

The paper’s “null” is actually its best result: no recovery after repeal. That is interesting, but the manuscript does not fully exploit it. It should present the non-rebound not as an afterthought, but as the central empirical fact.
