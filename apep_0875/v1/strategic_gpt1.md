## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether public quality disclosure can permanently shrink a market even after the disclosure regime is repealed. Using the rise and repeal of the Gainful Employment rule, it documents that for-profit educational programs exited at much higher rates after federal publication of debt-to-earnings metrics, and that repeal did not bring them back.

Why should a busy economist care? Because the paper is trying to make a general claim about disclosure policy: information release may not just reallocate demand in the short run; it may trigger irreversible exit and market restructuring. That is a live question well beyond higher education.

**Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Not quite. The ingredients are there, but the introduction starts too deep in institutional detail and too quickly slides into “this paper documents...” language. The big idea is stronger than the current opening. The paper should lead with the general economic question first, then use GE as the setting.

### The pitch the paper should have

> Governments increasingly publish quality scores to discipline markets, from hospitals to schools to financial products. But an unanswered question is whether disclosure is reversible: when bad information is withdrawn, do firms recover, or does disclosure permanently reshape the market by triggering exit, stigma, and sunk-cost adjustment?
>
> This paper studies that question in U.S. for-profit higher education. I show that after the federal government publicly disclosed debt-to-earnings measures under the Gainful Employment rule, for-profit programs contracted sharply relative to comparable public programs, and repeal of the rule did not reverse that contraction. The pattern suggests that disclosure can have one-way effects on market structure even when the formal regulatory regime disappears.

That is the AER-worthy pitch. It is about **reversibility of disclosure-induced market restructuring**, not mainly about one education rule.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper’s contribution is to document that the introduction and subsequent repeal of a federal quality-disclosure regime in for-profit education were associated with asymmetric market responses—large exit after disclosure, but no recovery after repeal—consistent with irreversible disclosure effects on market structure.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest papers?

Only partially. Right now the paper differentiates itself mostly on:
1. **Program-level tracking**
2. **Full cycle: implementation plus repeal**
3. **Irreversibility framing**

Of these, only the third is potentially big. The first two are useful but not by themselves AER-level. A reader could easily come away thinking: “This is a descriptive DiD on for-profit closures under GE.” That is not enough.

The paper needs to more forcefully distinguish itself from:
- papers on **for-profit college decline and regulation**
- papers on **disclosure/report-card effects**
- papers on **market exit under reputational or regulatory shocks**

At present, the differentiation is too data/setting-based, not enough idea-based.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or filling a gap in a LITERATURE?

It is trying to do the former, which is good, but it repeatedly retreats into the latter. The strongest version is:

- **World question:** Are disclosure policies reversible, or do they permanently reshape markets by causing exit?
  
The weaker version, which the paper too often slips into, is:

- **Literature gap:** No one has tracked all for-profit programs through implementation and repeal of GE.

The first belongs in the AER conversation. The second sounds like a field-journal contribution.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?

Not cleanly. They might say: “It’s another paper on the Gainful Employment rule showing for-profits shrank a lot.” The repeal angle may register, but the general intellectual contribution is not crisp enough.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Several possibilities:

1. **Make the object of study market structure, not survival coefficients.**  
   Show reallocation patterns: who replaced exiting for-profit programs? Public colleges? Community colleges? Different fields? Different geographies? If the market restructured, show the restructuring.

2. **Lean much harder into reversibility.**  
   The paper’s distinctive feature is the introduction + repeal sequence. That should be the centerpiece, not a later result. Everything should orbit the question: when information is withdrawn, what remains?

3. **Develop a sharper mechanism than “disclosure chill.”**  
   “Chill” is evocative but vague and a bit journalistic. Better would be a cleaner mechanism taxonomy:
   - consumer learning
   - collective reputation
   - irreversible exit/sunk costs
   - regulatory stigma / political salience  
   The paper need not prove which one dominates, but it should frame the alternatives more economically.

4. **Use a stronger comparison group or framing comparison.**  
   Public programs are a control group, but the big story might be more convincing if framed around:
   - exposed vs less-exposed for-profit programs,
   - markets with stronger student information frictions,
   - fields where employer beliefs are more salient,
   - local substitution to public/community-college supply.  
   Even in framing terms, this would make it more than “for-profits vs publics.”

5. **Emphasize asymmetry as the result.**  
   Not “GE reduced survival.” Plenty of policies reduce survival.  
   The bigger contribution is: **disclosure entry and disclosure withdrawal are not mirror images.**

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Governments increasingly use disclosure and scorecards to improve market performance. In many markets, the standard question is whether disclosure changes quality, demand, or firm behavior.

### Tension

We know much less about whether disclosure changes market structure in a durable way. If bad information is later withdrawn, do markets rebound? Or are disclosure shocks one-way because of reputation, learning, and irreversible exit?

### Resolution

In for-profit higher education, federal publication of debt-to-earnings outcomes coincided with sharp program exit, and repeal of the disclosure regime did not produce recovery. The contraction looks asymmetric and persistent.

### Implications

Disclosure policy may have durable consequences beyond its formal legal life. Policymakers should think of scorecards not merely as temporary information tools, but as interventions that can permanently alter entry, exit, and sector composition.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the **raw material** for a clear arc, but in current form it feels somewhat like a collection of results with an ex post story attached. The phrase “disclosure chill” is trying to do too much narrative work without enough conceptual scaffolding.

The paper should tell a simpler, stronger story:

1. Disclosure policies are often treated as reversible.
2. But market exit may make them effectively irreversible.
3. GE provides a rare introduction-and-repeal setting.
4. The key fact is asymmetry: contraction on the way in, no recovery on the way out.
5. Therefore, disclosure regimes can outlast themselves through market structure.

That is the story. Everything else should support it.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

Yes, initially. The repeal non-recovery fact is interesting. Economists like asymmetries and irreversibility.

Immediately: **“But how do you know this is disclosure rather than the broader anti–for-profit crackdown and secular decline?”**

Right now the manuscript itself repeatedly concedes that it cannot cleanly separate disclosure from sanctions and contemporaneous sector-wide shocks. That honesty is good, but it also undercuts the sharpness of the contribution.

### If findings are modest or partially confounded, is the result still interesting?

Yes—**if** the paper squarely embraces itself as a paper about **asymmetric policy reversal** rather than a clean causal estimate of disclosure alone. The non-recovery fact can be interesting even if the exact mechanism remains unresolved. But then the framing must be disciplined:

- not “I prove disclosure caused irreversible damage”
- but “I document durable contraction around a disclosure-and-sanction regime whose repeal did not reverse market exit”

That is still publishable in a strong field journal, maybe better if sharpened. For AER, the paper needs to turn that into a more general lesson.

- **framing problem**

The paper currently documents contraction. A top paper would more fully characterize the restructuring:

The setting is important, but many economists will think they already know that for-profit colleges declined under pressure in the 2010s. To overcome that, the paper has to make the **repeal asymmetry** and **general disclosure lesson** unmistakably central.
