**Summary** — The paper documents a large decline in for-profit program survival associated with the implementation of the 2015 Gainful Employment Rule using IPEDS panel data, and it argues that the 2019 repeal did not reverse these effects, implying an irreversible “disclosure chill.” The author estimates a two-period DiD with program and year fixed effects, finds a roughly 30 percentage point drop in survival for for-profit programs post-2015 relative to public peers in the same CIP fields, and shows a modest additional decline after repeal. Heterogeneity tests are used to argue that the effect is broad rather than concentrated in the high-risk CIPs that failed the GE thresholds.

---

**Essential Points**

1. **Parallel trends and the DiD contrast.** For-profit programs were already diverging from public programs before 2015, as acknowledged in the paper’s placebo test and survival table. The main identification relies on the assumption that the acceleration in exits is entirely due to GE. With such strong pre-existing differences, the two-way fixed-effects estimate is mechanically capturing a mixture of GE effects and continuation of the prior trend. The paper needs a more credible control strategy: e.g., flexible pre-trends (CIP-specific linear or quadratic trends), synthetic control, or an event-study that presents the full dynamics to convince readers that the discontinuity at 2015 is not just the continuation of the existing decline. Without this, the estimated 29.7pp decline is not interpretable as causal.

2. **Misaligned control group.** Public programs are not a clean counterfactual for for-profit programs because they were never subject to the same funding or regulatory pressures, and their program mix, student composition, and incentives differ substantially. This is particularly concerning given that the paper pools a forward cohort of programs active in 2011, which selects for relatively established programs—public and for-profit—but these cohorts may respond differently to secular forces (e.g., demographic shifts, macro shocks, accreditation changes). The author needs to either demonstrate (through balance tests or alternative controls) that public programs provide a valid counterfactual or seek a cleaner within-sector comparison (e.g., GE-passing vs. GE-failing programs, or programs in the same institution that are exempt from GE but otherwise comparable).

3. **Failure to exploit actual treatment intensity.** The original idea highlighted the availability of program-level GE disclosure outcomes (pass/zone/fail status, debt-to-earnings ratios), which provide quasi-random within-institution variation in the likelihood of sanctions and reputational hits. The paper, however, treats the entire for-profit sector as uniformly treated, foregoing the richer identification that would compare programs that failed or were near failure to those that passed. Without leveraging this variation, the paper cannot distinguish the disclosure effect from broader sectoral shocks or the threat of sanctions. Incorporating the GE disclosure data would allow for more convincing tests of the disclosure vs. sanction mechanism (e.g., “failing” programs versus “passing” in the same institution or CIP).

Given these concerns, the current specification does not yet support the causal claims. The authors should revise the empirical strategy substantially before publication.

**Suggestions**

1. **Address parallel trends with richer dynamics.** Present an event-study plot (or coefficient table) showing the year-by-year difference between for-profit and public program survival from at least 2008 through 2023. Include leads to demonstrate whether the divergence begins only at 2015 or earlier. If the lead coefficients are significant, consider controlling for flexible pre-trends (e.g., interact CIP categories or sector indicators with time polynomials) or use a residualization approach that nets out the pre-existing linear trend before estimating the treatment effect. Alternatively, focus on a cleaner comparison group where the pre-trend is flatter (see below). These exercises will help readers gauge how much of the effect is driven by GE implementation.

2. **Incorporate the GE disclosure files and treatment heterogeneity.** Merge the DOE’s disclosure data (pass/zone/fail status and debt-to-earnings ratio) with the IPEDS program panel. With this, you can estimate triple-differences: for-profit + GE-subject programs in failing/zone status versus those passing, before and after disclosure/policy changes, possibly within the same institution or CIP. This would isolate the effect of reputational harm from the mere presence of a disclosure regime. In particular, if disclosure (rather than sanctions) is the key, you should see large effects even among programs that passed but were implicated by the label “GE program,” and the repeal should have differential impacts depending on the visibility and persistence of the disclosed score.

3. **Rethink the control group or supplement with within-sector variation.** One possibility is to use private nonprofit or public certificate programs that were also classified as “ge programs” but faced different stakes, or to exploit variation in implementation timing across award levels or CIP fields (some programs may have been exempt). Another approach is to compare programs within the same for-profit institution that differ in their risk of failing GE (e.g., healthcare vs. cosmetology). If the disclosure chill is sector-wide, comparing across programs within active for-profit institutions should still reveal differential effects, and there may be less concern about confounding trends than when comparing to public programs.

4. **Discuss selection from the forward cohort.** The decision to track only programs active in 2011 introduces survivor bias: programs that opened after 2011 are excluded even though GE may have affected their birth and closure decisions. Explain why this choice was made and whether the results are sensitive to including all observed programs (e.g., by allowing entry) or by starting new cohorts in later years. At a minimum, acknowledge that the estimated survival effects apply to the stock of programs existing in 2011 and not to new program creation.

5. **Clarify mechanism for post-repeal effect.** The 2019 repeal estimate is interpreted as evidence of irreversibility, but the design compares 2017–2019 to 2020–2023. Given that the post-repeal window is short and coincides with Covid-related shocks, the persistence of decline may reflect pandemic-related enrollment disruptions rather than the absence of disclosure. Consider interacting the post-repeal period with Covid indicators, or limit the analysis to the years before 2020 to isolate the effect of repeal from pandemic noise. Additionally, you could compare the exit rates of programs that failed GE (and thus suffered reputational hits) to those that passed across the repeal window to see whether “bad” programs were more irreversible than “good” ones.

6. **Strengthen enrollment substitution evidence.** The paper raises an important welfare question but only reports one imprecise state-level result. Augment this with more direct evidence: e.g., track student flows at the county level using the IPEDS fall enrollment survey; exploit geocoded proximity to measure whether public institutions near exiting for-profit programs absorbed displaced students; or use Graduate Outcomes (if available) to see whether completion shortfalls were offset by public sector gains. Even suggestive evidence about the composition of exiting students (fields, states) would enhance the policy relevance of the findings.

7. **Robustness to clustering and standard errors.** Clustering at the state level may be too coarse if there are many treated programs per state; consider two-way clustering by state and CIP field or use block jackknife if state counts are limited. Also, given the long panel and large number of programs, consider bootstrap-based inference or wild-cluster methods to ensure SEs are reliable.

8. **Tie narrative more tightly to economic theory.** The discussion invokes conceits like “collective reputation” and “irreversible investments.” Explicitly link these concepts to the empirical patterns by, for example, modeling the expected shutdown decisions of a program facing both regulatory sanctions and demand shocks due to bad publicity. This will help readers understand why disclosure could produce such large, irreversible effects, and how policy design can mitigate them.

Overall, this is a promising empirical question with rich data. By enriching the identification strategy with the available disclosure files, sharpening the control group, and better addressing pre-trends, the paper can make a convincing contribution to the literature on disclosure regulation and market restructuring.
