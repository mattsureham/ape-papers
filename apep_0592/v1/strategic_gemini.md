## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks: what happens to a local labor market when the government suddenly destroys its "fifth-largest industry"? Using linked census data, the author tracks 8.7 million men to show that while Prohibition initially benefited non-alcohol workers by reallocating resources and reducing competition for skilled jobs, it eventually backfired by destroying the "saloon infrastructure"—the informal networks of job referrals and credit that immigrant and working-class men relied on for mobility. It’s a story about why the "creative" part of creative destruction might take a decade to turn into "destruction."

**Evaluation:** The paper does a decent job in the first two paragraphs, but it leans a bit too heavily on the "what" of Prohibition rather than the "so what" of economic theory. 
**The pitch the paper should have:** "Economists often view industry destruction through the lens of creative destruction—a painful but efficient reallocation of resources. This paper uses the sudden exogenous shock of US State Prohibition to show that destroying an industry also destroys the 'non-market' social infrastructure embedded within it, leading to a 'reversal of fortune' where short-run gains from resource reallocation are wiped out by the long-run erosion of informal labor market networks."

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution:** The paper identifies a non-monotonic relationship between industry destruction and local labor market health, where short-term occupational upgrading is eventually reversed by the loss of informal social infrastructure.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It differentiates well from the "Direct Effects of Prohibition" literature (Miron, etc.) and the "Local Labor Market Shocks" literature (Autor/China Shock) by focusing on *indirect* spillovers on the *non-displaced* workforce.
*   **Question vs. Gap:** It is currently framed as answering a question about the WORLD (how do markets adjust to vanished industries?), which is a strength.
*   **What would make it bigger?** The paper needs to connect more deeply to the "search and matching" literature. If the saloon was a matching technology, can we see the "quality" of matches falling? (e.g., do people switch jobs more often in the 1920s because their initial 1920 match was poor?).

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

*   **Neighbors:** Autor, Dorn, and Hanson (2013) on local shocks; Blanchard and Katz (1992) on regional evolution; Abramitzky, Boustan, and Eriksson (2012) on historical mobility; and Bleakley and Lin (2012) on path dependence.
*   **Positioning:** It should **synthesize** the trade-shock literature with the social-capital literature. Currently, these feel like two different papers joined by a "reversal" result. 
*   **Missing Conversations:** The paper should speak more to the **Economics of Networks**. The saloon is essentially a physical node in a job-referral network. When the node is deleted, the network fragments.

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

*   **Setup:** The 1910 American economy, where saloons are the dominant social and economic hub for the working class.
*   **Tension:** Prohibition destroys this hub. Standard theory suggests resources (labor/capital) will simply flow to better uses (the "Creative Destruction" hypothesis).
*   **Resolution:** Resources *do* flow (short-run upgrading), but the underlying "matching engine" is broken.
*   **Implications:** 1920s workers in formerly high-alcohol areas begin to lag behind as their "social capital" evaporates.

**Evaluation:** The narrative arc is very strong—it’s a "paradox" story, which the AER loves. The transition from short-run gain to long-run loss provides the necessary "tension."

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

At a dinner party, I would lead with: *"Prohibition was actually great for the average worker’s career—for exactly ten years. Then it ruined their social networks, and they fell behind."* 

People would definitely lean in. The follow-up question is: *"If the saloon was so important, why didn't the market just create a 'Dry Saloon' or a neighborhood club to replace the networking function?"* The author touches on this (soda fountains, etc.), but needs a more robust answer to why the market failed to provide a substitute for the saloon's "informal" functions.

---

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

*   **Front-loading:** The "Dynamic Reversal" is the AER-level result. Currently, it's buried in Section 5.5. This should be signaled much more aggressively in the Intro and perhaps even the title (e.g., "The Life and Death of Labor Market Infrastructure").
*   **The Appendix:** The pre-trend discussion in Section 4.2 is a bit of a "downer" right before the results. While honest, it might be better to integrate the "earlier period" results into a single "Temporal Dynamics" section to show the whole 1900–1930 trajectory.

---

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The "gap" is the **pre-trend.** The fact that 1900–1910 looks like the 1910–1920 result but 6x larger (Table 6) is a potential "dealbreaker" for a top-5 journal. It suggests that "high-alcohol counties" were just "fast-growing urban counties" and the paper is just picking up 30 years of urban divergence.

**The single most impactful piece of advice:** Use the "Long-Run Reversal" to save the identification. If the pre-trend is positive, and the treatment is positive, but the 1920s are **negative**, that negative reversal is much harder to explain away as a simple "urban trend." Shift the paper’s weight away from the "level" of the 1910 effect and toward the **change in the slope** after the 1920s.
