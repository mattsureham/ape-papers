## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks what happens to workers outside a targeted industry when that industry is wiped out. Using state prohibition as a historical episode of abrupt industry destruction, it studies whether closing saloons and related alcohol businesses changed occupational mobility for the much larger set of non-alcohol workers whose local labor markets were organized around that industry. Busy economists should care because the broader question is important: when policy or technology destroys an industry, are the spillovers to adjacent workers mainly reallocative, or do they also destroy informal institutions that support labor market matching?

The paper does **not** currently articulate this pitch as clearly as it should in the first two paragraphs. The opening is vivid, but the introduction quickly slides into a somewhat diffuse list of possible channels and literatures. The reader gets history, methods, and many results before the core question is sharpened. More importantly, the paper undersells the most interesting broad question—industry destruction as destruction of labor market infrastructure—and oversells a setting-specific story about Prohibition.

### What the first two paragraphs should say instead

Something like:

> Economists know a great deal about what happens to workers in industries that contract or disappear. We know much less about what happens to everyone else living and working around those industries—especially when the disappearing industry also served as local labor market infrastructure. This paper studies that broader question using state prohibition, which abruptly shut down a large, spatially concentrated industry before national prohibition made the policy universal.
>
> I ask whether closing saloons affected the occupational trajectories of non-alcohol workers in exposed local labor markets. Saloons were not just drinking establishments: in many working-class neighborhoods they functioned as hiring halls, credit intermediaries, and social hubs. Using linked full-count census panels and cross-county variation in pre-prohibition alcohol employment, I show that exposure is associated with modest short-run occupational upgrading for adjacent workers, especially immigrants and workers in manufacturing and retail, but worse occupational outcomes a decade later. The central claim is not simply that Prohibition mattered, but that destroying an industry can initially reallocate labor productively while later damaging the informal institutions that help labor markets function.

That is the AER-facing pitch. It starts with a world question, uses Prohibition as a vehicle, and foregrounds the dynamic reversal immediately.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper offers historical evidence that destroying a large, locally embedded industry can spill over onto non-industry workers in ways that are initially positive but later negative, consistent with industry closure affecting both resource allocation and informal labor market infrastructure.

Only partially. The paper names three broad literatures—Prohibition, local labor market adjustment, and social infrastructure—but the differentiation is still fuzzy. Right now the contribution reads as a bundle of claims:

- “first spillovers paper on Prohibition,”
- “individual-level evidence on adjacent workers,”
- “evidence on social infrastructure,”
- “dynamic reversal.”

Those are not yet cleanly ranked. The introduction needs one central novelty. My advice: make the novelty **the dynamic spillover to non-industry workers from destroying a socially embedded industry**. Everything else should support that.

Relative to nearby papers, the paper is differentiated in setting, but not yet in conceptual move. A smart economist may still summarize it as “another historical DiD/shift-share paper about Prohibition and occupational mobility,” which is not what the author wants.

### World question or literature gap?

It is mixed, and that is a weakness. The stronger framing is clearly the world question:

- When an industry disappears, what happens to adjacent workers?
- Does industry destruction also destroy labor market institutions?
- Are short-run reallocative gains offset by long-run matching losses?

The paper too often lapses into “this literature hasn’t examined X.” That is second-tier framing. AER papers usually lead with a substantive economic question, not a missing cell in the literature review.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?

Not cleanly enough. They would likely say:  
“It's a historical paper on state prohibition showing some modest occupational spillovers, lots of heterogeneity, and maybe a reversal later.”  

That is too mushy. The reader should be able to say:  
“It shows that when you kill a big local industry that also served as social infrastructure, nearby workers may do a bit better at first and worse later.”

That is memorable.

### What would make the contribution bigger?

Specific ways to make it bigger:

1. **Commit to one central outcome conceptually tied to the claim.**  
   OCCSCORE is serviceable, but for the “social infrastructure” story the more natural high-level outcomes are job stability, occupational persistence, transitions into self-employment, unemployment/nonemployment, or migration. Right now the headline outcome is occupational income score, but the most story-relevant facts may actually be switching/churning and self-employment. If the paper wants to be about matching institutions, it should lean harder on outcomes that look like matching quality, not just occupational rank.

2. **Clarify whether the paper is about adjacent workers or local equilibrium.**  
   At present “general equilibrium” is too grand for what is shown. If the author can show sectoral reallocation across more clearly delineated neighboring industries, or local compositional changes in employment structures, that would make the scope more convincing. Otherwise, call it spillovers to non-alcohol workers, not general equilibrium.

3. **Elevate the dynamic reversal from one result to the organizing fact.**  
   That is the most interesting thing here. A top-field audience is much more likely to care about “short-run gains, long-run losses” than “there was a small positive average effect in 1910–1920.”

4. **Build a tighter bridge to modern industry-shock debates.**

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Before this paper, economists understand that industry collapse can hurt directly exposed workers and can reshape local economies, but we know less about spillovers to workers outside the collapsing sector—especially when the sector also hosts informal institutions that support job search, credit, and social coordination.

### Tension

State prohibition did more than reduce alcohol consumption; it abruptly destroyed a large, locally embedded industry. The tension is whether adjacent workers benefited from reallocation of resources and reduced competition, or were harmed because the saloon was also labor market infrastructure. Those predictions plausibly point in opposite directions, and perhaps at different horizons.

### Resolution

The paper finds modest short-run occupational upgrading among non-alcohol workers in more exposed counties, concentrated among immigrants and workers in manufacturing and retail, alongside more occupation switching and lower self-employment. A decade later, the pattern reverses, with lower occupational scores in previously more exposed treated places.

### Implications

The paper wants readers to infer that industry destruction has two margins: reallocating resources and dismantling informal institutions. That would matter for how economists think about adjustment after industry closures and how policymakers evaluate shocks to local economies.

### Does the paper have a clear arc?

There is an arc, but it is not cleanly disciplined. The paper is strongest when read as:

1. Saloons were an industry and an institution.
2. Prohibition destroyed both.
3. Adjacent workers initially gained.
4. Later they lost.
5. Therefore industry destruction may have delayed costs through institutional loss.

But the paper often dissolves that arc into a long catalog of channels, subgroup results, robustness exercises, and caveats. It sometimes feels like a collection of plausible results looking for a hierarchy.

### What story should it be telling?

It should tell a simpler story:

- **Act 1:** Saloons were not only producers of alcohol; they were labor market infrastructure.
- **Act 2:** Prohibition shut them down.
- **Act 3:** In the short run, resources were reallocated and some adjacent workers advanced.
- **Act 4:** In the longer run, the missing infrastructure showed up as worse trajectories.
- **Act 5:** The broader lesson is that industry closure can destroy institutions as well as jobs.

That is a publishable narrative. The current draft contains it, but does not ruthlessly prioritize it.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?

I would lead with:  
**When states shut down saloons, non-saloon workers in more exposed places appear to move up occupationally at first—but a decade later they move down.**

That is the one fact that gets attention.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

Some would lean in—especially economic historians, labor economists, and people interested in local adjustment. But at present many would still reach for their phones because the paper does not yet convert that fact into a sufficiently sharp general lesson. The reader senses something interesting, but also senses hedging, modest magnitudes, and a setting that could remain niche.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Immediately:  
**Why does it reverse?**  

And then:  
**Is this really about social infrastructure, or just differential trends / urban change / immigrant assimilation / compositional dynamics?**

That follow-up is actually good news strategically: it means the interesting part of the paper is not the existence of an effect, but the interpretation of the dynamic pattern. The paper should embrace that.

### If findings are modest: is the modesty itself interesting?

Yes, but only if framed properly. The short-run main effect is small in standardized terms, and the paper knows that. Small average effects are fine if the intellectual payoff lies in heterogeneity and dynamics. The draft partially makes that case. However, the paper cannot simultaneously say “the average effect is modest and causally fragile” and still expect the reader to care about a dozen second-order subgroup estimates unless it gives them a compelling conceptual reason to do so.

So the modest main effect is not fatal. It becomes interesting if the paper says:

- the average is small because reallocative gains and institutional losses offset,
- the offset unfolds over time,
- and the pattern is strongest where the saloon likely mattered most as infrastructure.

That is the “so what.”

Some of this belongs in appendix or a more integrated mechanism section. The current sequencing feels like the author does not trust any one result enough, so they offer all of them.

6. **The conclusion mostly summarizes.**  
   It should do more synthesis and less recap. Specifically, it should tell the reader what belief changes: economists should think of certain industries as bundles of production plus local intermediation.

### Is the paper front-loaded with the good stuff?

No. The reader has to work too hard before understanding the real punchline. The good stuff is present, but not efficiently surfaced.

### Are there results buried that should move up?

Yes:

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

Be honest: in current form, this is **not yet an AER paper**. The gap is not mostly econometric—it is strategic.

### What is the main problem?

Primarily a **framing and ambition problem**, with some novelty risk.

- **Framing problem:** The paper has an interesting idea but presents itself as a descriptive Prohibition paper with many results and many caveats.
- **Ambition problem:** It stops short of making the strongest conceptual claim it plausibly can—that some industries are local labor market institutions, and destroying them has dynamic spillovers beyond direct job loss.

Top people in labor/economic history would need to feel that this paper changes how they think about industry decline, not just how they think about Prohibition.

Right now the paper says:
