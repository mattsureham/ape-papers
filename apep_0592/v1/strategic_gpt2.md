## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks: when a major local industry is destroyed, what happens not to the workers in that industry, but to everyone else whose labor market was organized around it? Using state prohibition as a historical shock, the paper argues that closing saloons initially coincided with modest occupational upgrading for non-alcohol workers, especially immigrants and workers in manufacturing/retail, but that these gains later reversed—suggesting that industry destruction can remove not just jobs, but labor-market infrastructure.

Why should a busy economist care? Because the underlying question is broad and important: when an industry disappears, are the spillovers to adjacent workers positive, negative, or dynamic over time? That is a first-order question for trade shocks, decarbonization, automation, and place-based policy.

### Does the paper articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Not quite. The current introduction is vivid and well written, but it starts as a Prohibition paper and only gradually reveals the broader economic question. The author knows the paper is really about industry destruction and informal labor-market infrastructure; the first two paragraphs should say that immediately. Right now the reader has to infer the bigger paper from a historical setup.

### The pitch the paper should have

Here is the pitch the paper should have in the first two paragraphs:

> What happens to workers outside a collapsing industry when that industry structures local labor markets? This paper studies that question using state prohibition, which between 1907 and 1919 shut down a large, spatially concentrated alcohol sector and abruptly removed saloons that functioned not only as employers, but also as job-matching, credit, and social institutions in working-class neighborhoods.
>
> Using linked full-count census panels, I study non-alcohol workers’ occupational trajectories in places more exposed to the saloon economy before prohibition. I show a striking dynamic pattern: in the short run, exposed workers experienced modest occupational upgrading, especially in manufacturing, retail, and among immigrants; by 1930, those gains reversed. The paper’s central claim is that destroying an industry can generate both conventional reallocation effects and slower-moving losses from dismantling informal labor-market infrastructure.

That framing says “this is a paper about the world,” not “this is a Prohibition paper with some labor outcomes.”

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper’s contribution is to show that the destruction of a major local industry can have dynamic spillover effects on adjacent workers—short-run occupational gains and longer-run losses—potentially because industries embed informal labor-market infrastructure as well as jobs.

### Is this clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?

Only partially. The paper cites literatures on Prohibition, local labor market shocks, and social infrastructure, but the differentiation is still fuzzy.

What it is trying to be distinct from:
1. **Direct-effects Prohibition papers** on drinking, mortality, crime.
2. **Local labor market shock papers** on import competition / sectoral decline.
3. **Historical mobility papers** using linked census panels.
4. **Social capital / social infrastructure papers** showing that institutions matter for local economic outcomes.

What is missing is a crisp “relative to X, Y, and Z, this paper uniquely studies spillovers on non-industry workers and emphasizes the dynamic reversal.” That should be stated much more sharply.

Right now a smart economist could still come away saying: “It’s another historical difference-in-differences paper about prohibition and occupational mobility.” That is a problem.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a literature gap?

Mixed, but too often it slips into gap-filling. The stronger frame is clearly the world question:

- When an industry disappears, who else is affected?
- Do adjacent workers benefit from reallocation or suffer from loss of local institutions?
- Are the effects short-run and long-run different?

That is the frame to lean into. “The Prohibition literature hasn’t studied this” is true but not enough for AER positioning.

### Could a smart economist explain what is new?

Not reliably in the current version. They might say:
- “It studies spillovers from Prohibition on non-alcohol workers.”
That is okay but not big enough.
- The stronger version would be:
  “It argues that destroying an industry affects not just displaced workers but also nearby workers through labor-market infrastructure, and that the sign of the spillover can reverse over time.”

That second sentence is the one the introduction needs to make unforgettable.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Several possibilities, strategically:

1. **Make the dynamic reversal the central result, not a later twist.**  
   That is the one thing here that sounds less like a standard local-labor-market paper and more like a new claim about adjustment dynamics.

2. **Elevate labor-market infrastructure from speculative mechanism to organizing concept.**  
   Right now “saloons as infrastructure” is vivid but somewhat essayistic. If the paper wants to matter broadly, it needs to treat saloons as an exemplar of a general category: institutions that combine matching, credit, information, and community.

3. **Broaden the outcome space toward labor-market functioning, not just occupational status.**  
   If the paper is really about matching infrastructure, then outcomes like job stability, occupational persistence, entrepreneurship, and mobility should be central, not subsidiary.

4. **Clarify the comparison class.**  
   Is this paper about destruction of a consumer-facing urban service industry, or about any concentrated industry? If the latter, it needs more discipline in drawing parallels. If the former, that narrower but cleaner claim may actually be stronger.

5. **Reframe away from “modest positive average effect.”**  
   The average short-run effect is small and methodologically fragile even by the paper’s own account. Strategically, hanging the paper on that coefficient undersells it and invites dismissal. The bigger contribution is about heterogeneity and temporal dynamics.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

### Closest neighbors

The paper’s nearest neighbors are probably:

1. **Autor, Dorn, and Hanson** on local labor-market effects of import competition.
2. **Topalova (2010)** on local adjustment to trade shocks.
3. **Blanchard and Katz (1992)** on regional labor market adjustment.
4. **Hornbeck (2012)** on long-run local effects of historical shocks.
5. On the historical linked-data side: **Abramitzky, Boustan, Eriksson, Feigenbaum, Ward** style work on mobility and occupational transitions.
6. On Prohibition specifically: **Miron**, **Dills and Miron**, **Owens**, possibly **Okrent/Blocker** as historical background rather than economic neighbors.
7. On institutions/social capital: not just **Powers (1998)**, which is mainly historical context, but conceptually also **Putnam-ish** social capital work, and economics papers on networks and matching, though the paper currently doesn’t engage that literature enough.

### How should it position itself relative to those neighbors?

- **Build on**, not attack, the Prohibition literature: “that literature measured direct effects; I use the same policy episode to study a different margin.”
- **Extend** the local-labor-market-shock literature: “that literature emphasizes directly exposed workers and equilibrium adjustment; I study adjacent workers and the loss of informal infrastructure.”
- **Connect to** networks/matching/social capital literatures: “industry decline can remove institutions that facilitate matching and entrepreneurship.”

The paper should not overclaim that it overturns creative destruction or the local-adjustment literature. It does not. But it can credibly say it adds an underexplored margin: spillovers through embedded institutions.

### Is it positioned too narrowly or too broadly?

Paradoxically, both.

- **Too narrowly** in that it sometimes reads like a niche Prohibition/economic history paper.
- **Too broadly** when it jumps from saloons to sweeping claims about industry destruction in general without enough conceptual structure.

The right audience is broader than economic history but narrower than “all industry destruction everywhere.” The proper zone is: **historical evidence on how local industry shutdowns reshape adjacent labor markets through institutions and reallocation.**

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?

It seems underconnected to:
- **Labor market networks / job search / matching** literature.
- **Social capital and institutions as economic infrastructure** in modern empirical work.
- **Agglomeration / local amenities / neighborhood institutions** literatures.
- Potentially **entrepreneurship and small business ecosystems** literatures, given the self-employment result.

If the argument is really about saloons as labor-market infrastructure, it should be speaking to labor economists who study search frictions and networks, not only to economic historians.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?

Not fully. The current conversation is: “Prohibition had spillover effects too.”  
The better conversation is: “Industry shutdowns destroy institutions as well as jobs, and this can reverse the trajectory of adjacent workers.”

That second conversation is much more AER-relevant.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Before this paper, economists mostly think of Prohibition as a policy affecting drinking, crime, and health, and think of industry destruction mainly in terms of direct displacement and local reallocation. Saloons, however, were not just firms; they were neighborhood institutions embedded in labor markets.

### Tension

The tension is genuinely interesting: shutting down a major industry could help adjacent workers in the short run through reallocation and reduced competition, but hurt them in the longer run by destroying informal matching and credit networks. Which force dominates?

### Resolution

The paper’s resolution is that both forces may operate: there is modest short-run occupational upgrading for exposed non-alcohol workers, especially among immigrants and in manufacturing/retail, but this pattern reverses by 1930.

### Implications

The implication is that evaluations of industry destruction that focus on directly exposed workers or short-run outcomes may miss important spillovers and dynamic losses from destroying local social/economic infrastructure.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the ingredients of one, but the story is not yet cleanly organized. Right now it still feels somewhat like:
- a historical setup,
- a main regression,
- a batch of heterogeneity tables,
- then a more interesting long-run reversal.

That is backwards. The dynamic reversal is the narrative payoff and should be much closer to the front. The paper’s current structure gives the reader a modest average short-run effect first, which is not strong enough to carry 50 pages. Only later do we discover the more original claim.

### What story should it be telling?

The story should be:

1. **Industries embed institutions.**
2. **State prohibition abruptly destroyed one such industry and its institutions.**
3. **Adjacent workers initially benefited from resource reallocation.**
4. **But over time, exposed places lost labor-market infrastructure, and those gains reversed.**
5. **Therefore, the effects of industry destruction are dynamic and extend beyond the directly displaced.**

That is a coherent AER-style narrative. The current draft has this story, but buried under too much scene-setting and too much defensive methodological exposition in the introduction.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?

I would lead with:  
**“When states shut down the saloon industry, non-alcohol workers in more exposed places initially moved up the occupational ladder—but by the next decade those gains had reversed.”**

That is the hook.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

Some would lean in—if presented that way.  
If presented as “I study occupational income scores around Prohibition using linked census data,” many would reach for their phones.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Immediately:
- “Why would it reverse?”
And then:
- “Is that about networks, matching, or something else?”
- “How general is this beyond Prohibition?”

Those are good questions. They mean the paper is close to an interesting conversation.

### If findings are modest: is the modest result interesting?

The modest average short-run effect by itself is not very interesting. A tiny OCCSCORE movement with caveats is not an AER story. The paper is right not to oversell that coefficient.

What is potentially interesting is:
- spillovers on workers outside the target industry,
- concentration among immigrants and certain sectors,
- and especially the sign reversal over time.

So the null/modest point is: **the average effect is modest, but the dynamics and composition are not.** The paper should say this much more clearly.
