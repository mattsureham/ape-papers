# Research Ideas

## Idea 1: When the Fifth-Largest Industry Vanished: State Prohibition and General Equilibrium Labor Market Restructuring, 1910-1930
**Policy:** Staggered state adoption of prohibition laws (33 states going dry between 1907-1919) destroyed America's fifth-largest industry — breweries, distilleries, saloons, and supply chains. Adoption dates from Beienburg (2020): 10+ distinct timing cohorts from 1907 (GA, OK) through 1919 (KY), with ~15 states never dry before national prohibition in 1920.
**Outcome:** IPUMS full-count census + Census Linking Project crosswalks (1910-1920-1930). Key variables: OCC1950 (harmonized occupations), OCCSCORE (occupational income score), IND1950 (industry codes identifying alcohol-related industries), CLASSWKR (self-employment), LABFORCE (labor force participation). City-level alcohol industry employment share from 1910 census as continuous treatment intensity.
**Identification:** Staggered DiD with continuous exposure (Callaway-Sant'Anna + Bartik). Two sources of variation: (1) cross-state prohibition timing (33 states, 1907-1919), (2) within-state cross-city alcohol industry concentration. Sample EXCLUDES alcohol industry workers — the question is about spillover effects on everyone else. Pre-trends testable with 1900-1910 linked panel. Built-in placebos: workers in zero-exposure cities.
**Why it's novel:** Aizer, Grafton, and Perez (NBER 2025) track alcohol industry workers and their families. Howard and Ornaghi (JEH 2022) use county-level aggregates. No paper studies general equilibrium effects on non-alcohol workers using individual-level linked census panels. Novel mechanisms: saloon destruction disrupting working-class social infrastructure (job referrals, labor organizing), and saloon-to-restaurant conversion opening female employment.
**Feasibility check:** Confirmed: 33 states with 10+ timing cohorts; IPUMS full-count census freely available; Census Linking Project crosswalks freely downloadable; Beienburg (2020) appendix provides adoption dates. Sample size: 8-10M non-alcohol workers in cities with non-zero exposure.
