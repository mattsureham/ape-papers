## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple question with potentially broad relevance: when governments scrap a complex enforcement regime and replace it with a streamlined one, do places that were most invested in the old system suffer the biggest disruption? Using the UK’s 2014 anti-social behaviour reform, the paper finds essentially no differential deterioration in anti-social behaviour in places that had relied most heavily on the old ASBO regime, suggesting that enforcement capacity may be more portable across legal tools than one might expect.

A busy economist should care because this is, at least in aspiration, a paper about whether institutional capital is embedded in specific rules and tools, or in people and organizations that can adapt when the rules change. That is a real question. The problem is that the paper currently presents it too much as “here is a UK policing reform no one has studied causally” and not enough as “here is evidence on how resilient state capacity is to formal legal reorganization.”

### Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Not quite. The opening is vivid, but the introduction quickly slips into a “gap in the literature” frame and then into policy examples that are much bigger than the evidence can support. The first two paragraphs should do less scene-setting around ASBOs and less claiming generality from EU financial supervision/DHS, and more clearly state the economic question: when legal tools are replaced, is organizational know-how tool-specific or portable?

### The pitch the paper should have

“Governments regularly simplify and consolidate enforcement regimes, but it is unclear whether these reforms raise efficiency or instead destroy valuable institutional capital embedded in legacy procedures. This paper studies that question using the UK’s 2014 replacement of 19 anti-social-behaviour enforcement tools with 6 new powers, and asks whether places most reliant on the old regime suffered larger enforcement breakdowns after the switch. They did not: despite large cross-area differences in prior ASBO use, high-reliance areas saw no relative increase in anti-social behaviour, suggesting that frontline enforcement capacity is more organizationally portable—and less tied to specific legal instruments—than critics of regulatory consolidation often assume.”

That is the version with a shot. The current intro is serviceable, but still reads too much like a competent applied micro paper on a niche criminal justice reform.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### Contribution in one sentence

The paper’s contribution is to provide evidence that replacing a highly used legacy enforcement toolkit with a streamlined legal regime need not differentially weaken outcomes in places most invested in the old tools, implying that institutional enforcement capacity may be less tool-specific than feared.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?

Only partially. It is differentiated mechanically—few papers study this exact reform—but not yet conceptually. Right now the contribution sounds like: “No one has done a DiD on this UK reform.” That is not enough for AER. The paper needs to be much sharper about how it differs from:

1. policing/crime papers on enforcement inputs and technology,
2. papers on public-sector organizational capacity,
3. papers on legal/regulatory design and simplification.

At present, the “closest papers” it names are mostly generic crime papers on police, sentencing, IT, and response times. Those are not really the intellectual neighbors. The actual novelty is not “another policing paper,” but “evidence on whether formal institutional redesign destroys tacit organizational capital.”

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or filling a gap in a LITERATURE?

It oscillates, but too often falls back on literature-gap language: “no prior economics paper estimates...” That is a weak frame. The stronger frame is about the world: governments constantly reorganize enforcement institutions; should we expect costly breakdowns where the old regime was deeply entrenched?

### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what’s new?

Right now, maybe, but not cleanly. They would likely say: “It’s a DiD on the UK ASBO reform and finds no effect.” That is not enough. You want them to say: “It tests whether institutional capital in enforcement is tied to specific legal tools, and it finds surprising resilience to wholesale legal redesign.”

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Most importantly, not more “robustness,” but more conceptual reach. Specifically:

- **A different framing:** make this a paper on the portability of institutional capital under legal redesign, not on anti-social behaviour policy per se.
- **A different outcome set:** if feasible, outcomes more directly tied to enforcement adaptation would enlarge the paper—use of new powers, case processing, injunction issuance, breach rates, court throughput, complaints, victim satisfaction. The current headline outcome is one step removed.
- **A different mechanism:** showing whether high-ASBO places substituted quickly into the new tools would make the null much more interesting. Right now the null could mean resilience, irrelevance, or offsetting channels.
- **A different comparison:** the big comparison is not just high- vs low-ASBO places; it is settings where institutional investment in old tools was plausibly high vs low across multiple tool types. Reliance on ASBO intensity alone narrows the contribution.
- **A different implication:** if the authors want top-journal reach, they should make clear what belief changes: economists often think institutions are sticky because tacit knowledge is hard to reconfigure; this case says formal legal architecture can change without obvious front-line breakdown.

As written, the contribution is interesting but small.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Governments often inherit sprawling enforcement regimes and periodically simplify them. There is a common belief that simplification should improve performance by reducing bureaucracy.

### Tension

But organizations may have accumulated tool-specific know-how, routines, and relationships around the old regime. If so, replacing the legal toolkit could destroy institutional capital precisely where the old system was used most intensely.

### Resolution

In this case, it apparently did not. Areas that had relied far more heavily on the old ASBO regime did not experience larger post-reform deterioration in anti-social behaviour.

### Implications

The implication is that enforcement capacity may be more robust and portable than critics of legal redesign assume. More broadly, legal consolidation may alter formal rules without necessarily impairing the underlying productive capacity of public organizations.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the ingredients, but the arc is not yet fully under control. The paper sometimes reads like a collection of sensible components: institutional background, empirical setup, null result, cautious interpretation. What is missing is a stronger narrative spine tying everything to one central economic idea.

The story it should be telling is not “the ASBO reform had no effect.” The story is: **formal legal redesign tests whether institutional capital is embedded in tools or in organizations; this reform is a clean opportunity to observe that; the evidence favors organizational portability over tool-specific lock-in.**

That story is present, but not dominant enough.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?

“I’ve got a paper showing that when the UK scrapped 19 anti-social-behaviour enforcement tools and replaced them with 6 new ones overnight, places most invested in the old regime did not perform worse afterward.”

That is a decent opening fact.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

Initially, some would lean in—because institutional reorganization is a real and under-studied question. But many would reach for their phones if the next sentence is “using ASBO intensity across 42 police forces...” The hook is conceptual, not design-based. The paper needs to keep the audience at the conceptual level for longer.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Almost certainly: **“Why not?”**  
Did organizations substitute quickly to the new tools? Were ASBOs mostly symbolic? Did the reform lower legal burdens enough to offset transition costs? Was anti-social behaviour too noisy or too indirect an outcome? Those are the natural follow-up questions, and the paper currently cannot answer them very fully.

### Is the null result itself interesting?

Potentially yes. A null can absolutely be interesting here, because a lot of commentary and intuition points toward disruption after wholesale legal change. But the paper needs to work harder to make the null feel informative rather than merely unsurprising.

Right now it says “well-powered null” often enough, but that is not the real issue. The issue is interpretability. The null is interesting if it teaches us that institutional capital is portable. It is less interesting if it just means anti-social behaviour was unaffected for many possible reasons. The paper needs to make the null more theoretically consequential.

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

Without rewriting the paper entirely, a few changes would help a lot.

### 1. Rewrite the introduction around one question
The introduction should revolve around one question: **is institutional capital tool-specific or portable across legal regimes?** Right now the intro contains too many elements—vignette, broad policy examples, literature gap, identification, null, contributions. It needs tighter prioritization.

### 2. Cut or tone down overclaiming examples
The references to DHS, CFPB, and EU supervisory restructuring are rhetorically tempting but currently overextend the paper. Either:
- drop them, or
- use them only as motivation for why the question matters, while being explicit that this paper speaks to a narrower class of frontline enforcement reforms.

### 3. Move some identification/inference detail later
The introduction currently gets into design detail fairly early. For editorial positioning, that is too much too soon. Readers should learn the main fact and why it matters before they learn about permutation tests and cluster counts.

### 4. Bring the null’s meaning forward
The paper should tell readers earlier why a null here is informative. Not “because the p-value is large,” but because there was a strong prior that high-investment areas would face transition costs.

### 5. Streamline the literature review
The literature section in the introduction should be shorter and more selective. Fewer generic crime citations; more precise conceptual anchoring in state capacity, organizations, and legal design.

### 6. Results presentation
The current paper is reasonably front-loaded with the main finding, which is good. But some of the most important interpretive material is buried in the discussion. The mechanisms that could rationalize resilience should be previewed earlier, because they help the reader see why the result matters.

### 7. Conclusion
The conclusion mostly summarizes. It would add more value if it more explicitly stated the paper’s external lesson in bounded terms: legal reorganization does not automatically destroy enforcement capacity, especially when human and relational capital can transfer across tools.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

Bluntly: in its current form, this is not an AER paper. The issue is less “is the design competent?” and more “is the contribution large enough, and framed in a way that matters to a broad economics audience?”

### What is the gap?

Primarily a **framing problem**, secondarily a **scope problem**, and to some extent an **ambition problem**.

- **Framing problem:** The paper’s best idea is about institutional capital and organizational resilience, but it is framed as a niche policing-reform study with a literature-gap contribution.
- **Scope problem:** One reform, one country, one broad outcome, one treatment proxy. That is thin for the level of generality the paper wants.
- **Ambition problem:** The paper is careful and competent, but safe. It stops at “null effect on ASB” rather than pushing to explain what that null implies about institutions.

I am less worried about novelty in the narrow sense—this exact reform is not exhausted—but the broader question has not yet been made large enough.

### What would excite the top 10 people in this field?

They would need to come away believing one of two things:

1. this paper reveals something genuinely new about the nature of state capacity and institutional capital; or
2. this paper opens a broader research agenda on how public organizations absorb legal and administrative redesign.

Right now it does neither strongly enough.

### Single most impactful piece of advice

**Reframe the paper around the portability of institutional capital under legal redesign, and make every section serve that question rather than the narrower claim that “no one has causally studied the 2014 ASBO reform.”**

If the author can only change one thing, that is it.
