## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper studies whether governments can radically simplify an enforcement regime without reducing enforcement effectiveness. Using the UK’s 2014 reform of anti-social-behaviour law—which replaced 19 tools with 6 nationwide—the paper asks whether places that had invested most heavily in the old toolkit suffered larger deterioration in anti-social behaviour after the switch; it finds essentially no such differential effect.

A busy economist should care because the underlying question is broader than ASBOs: when states reorganize legal and administrative instruments, is institutional capacity embedded in the tools themselves or in the organizations using them? That is a potentially important question for public economics, political economy, law and economics, and state capacity.

### Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Partly, but not well enough for AER. The current opening has a nice concrete hook, but the framing is still too “policy episode + coined phrase” and not yet anchored in a first-order economics question. The introduction says “governments consolidate fragmented regulatory regimes” and introduces the “toolkit trap,” but it does not crisply tell the reader why this particular reform speaks to a general question economists care about: whether organizational know-how is tool-specific or portable across legal regimes.

The first two paragraphs should say less about “here is a reform” and more about “here is a general economic problem.” Right now the paper risks sounding like a careful DiD on an obscure UK institutional reform. The better version makes the paper about institutional capital, adaptation, and the costs of administrative simplification.

### The pitch the paper should have

Governments regularly abolish old legal instruments and replace them with simpler, consolidated enforcement regimes. Whether such reforms improve performance or instead destroy accumulated institutional know-how is a basic question about state capacity: is enforcement expertise embedded in specific legal tools, or is it portable across organizational forms?

This paper studies that question using the UK’s 2014 anti-social-behaviour reform, which replaced 19 enforcement tools with 6 on a single national date. Comparing police forces with very different reliance on the pre-reform regime, I find no evidence that the forces most invested in the old toolkit experienced worse post-reform anti-social behaviour outcomes, suggesting that enforcement capacity is more organizationally portable—and less tool-specific—than critics of administrative simplification often assume.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Several possibilities:

1. **Make the object of study institutional capital, not ASB policy.**  
   The paper should be framed as evidence on whether bureaucratic capability is tool-specific. That is already latent, but underdeveloped.

2. **Show adaptation in the replacement tools, not just outcomes.**  
   The paper would be much bigger if it could trace substitution into the new powers: not just “ASB did not rise,” but “high-ASBO areas rapidly adopted the new instruments.” That would convert a null on outcomes into a result about organizational adaptation.

3. **Open the black box on mechanism.**  
   Evidence on personnel continuity, specialized unit persistence, legal standard changes, or implementation support could help the reader understand why the null is informative rather than merely inconclusive.

4. **Widen the comparative claim.**  
   If the paper could compare this reform to other administrative consolidations, or at least explicitly link to similar reorganizations in other domains, the contribution would feel less parochial.

5. **Use outcomes that speak more directly to enforcement capacity.**  
   Anti-social behaviour rates are noisy and partly endogenous to reporting/classification. A bigger paper might pair them with direct measures of enforcement use, case processing, sanctions, complaints, or victimization.

The single biggest enlargement would be: **show how high-investment areas transitioned into the new toolkit.** Without that, the paper is “no detectable harm”; with that, it becomes “institutional capability was portable.”

### Tension

The tension is real and potentially strong: simplification could reduce bureaucratic friction, or it could destroy institutional capital in places most reliant on the old regime. The reform offers a clean test because everyone was switched at once, but some places had far more legacy investment to lose.

### Resolution

The paper finds no differential worsening of anti-social behaviour in the places most invested in the old toolkit. The null is interpreted as evidence against the hypothesis that replacing legacy legal tools necessarily impairs frontline enforcement.

### Implications

The implication should be: institutional capability may reside less in specific legal instruments and more in transferable personnel, relationships, and organizational routines. Therefore fears of administrative simplification may often be overstated—at least when transition support exists.

It has the bones of one, but not the full execution. Right now it is still somewhat a **collection of sensible empirical sections attached to a decent idea**. The story exists, but the paper has not yet fully committed to it.

Two problems weaken the arc:

1. **The tension is underdeveloped.**  
   The introduction names the “toolkit trap,” but the reader never fully feels why it should be a serious prior. The paper needs sharper articulation of why old tools create embedded know-how and why replacing them could be costly.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?

I would lead with:  
“The UK abolished 19 anti-social-behaviour enforcement tools overnight and replaced them with 6, yet the police forces most reliant on the old regime did not perform detectably worse afterward.”

That is the memorable fact.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

Some would lean in—but only if it is delivered as a state-capacity result, not as an ASBO result. “A paper on anti-social behaviour orders in England and Wales” is phone-reaching territory for most economists. “A paper showing that bureaucratic know-how may survive wholesale legal reorganization” is much more interesting.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Immediately: **“How do you know that means institutions adapted, rather than the outcome being too noisy or the reform not mattering?”**

That is the right question, and the paper currently does not answer it as strongly as it needs to. Since this memo is not about identification or robustness per se, the strategic point is: the paper needs more interpretive evidence so that the null reads as informative, not incidental.

### If the findings are null or modest: is the null result itself interesting?

Potentially yes. But null papers only work at the top journals if they do one of two things:

1. kill a widely held and substantively important prior, or  
2. sharply bound an effect that policy elites thought was first-order.

This paper gestures at that, but the case is not fully made. To make the null interesting, the paper must convince readers that:
- many observers expected high-ASBO areas to struggle,
- the reform was genuinely large,
- the outcome margin is one on which disruption should have shown up,
- and the absence of disruption updates beliefs about institutional portability.

Right now it is not yet fully there; it still risks feeling like “a failed search for an effect.”
