## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether withdrawing a place-based rural development program pushes communities toward the far right. Using France’s 2015 reclassification of rural tax-incentive zones, it argues that removing a low-visibility, business-facing subsidy did **not** produce a detectable surge in RN support, suggesting that the political effects of “state withdrawal” depend on whether voters can see and attribute the policy change.

A busy economist should care because the paper speaks to a large and active claim in political economy: that economic retrenchment mechanically fuels populism. If the result is right, the important lesson is not “economic shocks do nothing,” but “the political consequences of retrenchment vary sharply by policy instrument, salience, and attribution.”

**Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Not quite. The opening is reasonably strong, but it drifts too quickly into a generic “economic distress causes populism” literature review and only later reveals the sharper angle: this is a test of whether an **obscure, employer-facing place-based policy** matters politically. The paper currently sounds like it is trying to adjudicate the full “state withdrawal causes far right” thesis, which is too broad relative to what the design and setting can carry.

**What the first two paragraphs should say instead:**

> Across advanced democracies, economists increasingly argue that communities turn to populist parties when the state retreats. But most evidence comes from highly visible shocks—trade exposure, welfare cuts, plant closures, immigration inflows—where voters can readily perceive the change and blame national elites. We know much less about whether the same political backlash follows the withdrawal of less visible, place-based economic support.
>
> This paper studies that question using France’s 2015 reform of *Zones de Revitalisation Rurale* (ZRR), which removed thousands of rural communes from eligibility for business tax exemptions. I show that losing ZRR status did not generate a robust increase in far-right voting. The broader lesson is a boundary condition on the “economic grievance → populism” narrative: when state support is indirect, technical, and hard for voters to observe, its withdrawal may have little political payoff for the far right.

That is the pitch. It is narrower, truer to the evidence, and more interesting.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

2. **Key conceptual difference:** low-salience, indirect, employer-facing support versus visible, household-facing cuts.

Without that, a smart reader may reduce the paper to “another local-shock paper with a null-ish result.”

But there is risk they would say, “It’s a DiD on rural France and RN votes.” The introduction contains the ingredients of a bigger contribution, but the paper keeps interrupting itself with caveats, estimator details, and secondary exercises before the central idea hardens in the reader’s mind.

### What would make this contribution bigger?
Most importantly, **directly substantiate the salience/attribution angle**. Right now the most interesting interpretation is also the most weakly evidenced. Specific ways to enlarge the contribution:

- **Add a comparison to a more visible form of state withdrawal** in the same setting or country: school closures, post office closures, maternity ward closures, rail station closures, etc. Then the paper becomes about visible vs. invisible retrenchment, which is much bigger than one program.

The biggest version of this paper is not “ZRR didn’t move RN.” It is “political backlash to place-based retrenchment depends on instrument salience and citizen attribution.”

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Tension
But much of that evidence comes from shocks that are visible and easy to attribute. It is unclear whether withdrawing **technical, indirect, place-based support** triggers the same political response.

### Resolution
In France’s rural tax-zone reform, removing eligibility for a business-facing subsidy does not produce robust evidence of increased far-right voting.

### Implications
The political consequences of retrenchment may depend less on “economic harm” in the abstract and more on whether citizens perceive the withdrawal, connect it to the state, and update their politics accordingly.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?
“Thousands of rural French communes lost place-based tax incentives, and there’s no robust evidence that this pushed them toward Le Pen.”

That is at least mildly intriguing. Better yet:

“France withdrew rural subsidies from thousands of communes, and it didn’t reliably boost the far right—maybe because voters barely noticed the policy.”

That version makes people lean in.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
They would lean in **if** the second clause is the claim. They would reach for their phones if the paper is presented as:
- a France-specific institutional DiD,
- with a fragile negative coefficient,
- on an obscure tax classification.

So the salience/attribution framing is doing enormous work here. Without it, the paper is a niche applied political economy exercise. With it, it becomes a challenge to a broad style of argument in the field.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Immediately: **“Did people even know the program was taken away?”**

That is the paper’s central vulnerability and also its path to importance. If the author cannot answer that question directly, they need to at least organize the paper around it and be honest that this is the interpretive hinge.

### If findings are null or modest, is the null result itself interesting?
Potentially yes—but only if framed correctly. A null on an obscure program is not inherently interesting. A null that identifies a boundary condition on one of the field’s hottest political economy narratives **is** interesting. The author must make the case that learning “not all retrenchment generates populist backlash” changes how we think, not just that one reform happened not to move one vote share.

At present, the paper is halfway there.

The science may be decent, but the story is not yet at the right altitude. The paper is not really about “does state withdrawal fuel the far right?” It is about **which kinds of state withdrawal are politically legible enough to fuel the far right**. That is the AER version.

### Scope problem

- stronger evidence on the salience mechanism, or
- a comparative element across policy instruments, or

### Novelty problem
The general question—economic distress and populism—has been studied heavily. What is novel here is the boundary condition. But the paper has to insist on that novelty and document it better.

### Ambition problem
The paper currently feels a bit too safe and a bit too defensive. It is competent, but it has not fully claimed the bigger conceptual territory available to it.

## Single most impactful advice

**Reframe the paper around salience and attribution—make it a paper about why some forms of state withdrawal generate populist backlash and others do not, rather than a paper about one French tax-zone reform.**

That one change would improve the introduction, the literature positioning, the mechanisms, and the “so what” all at once.
