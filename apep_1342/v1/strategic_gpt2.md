## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple, policy-relevant question: when regulators impose a price cap in a thin-margin consumer credit market, do firms smoothly adjust, or does the market collapse through mass exit? Using the UK payday lending cap, the paper argues the regulator predicted modest borrower exclusion, but the realized contraction was dramatically larger, suggesting standard cost-benefit frameworks miss equilibrium supply destruction.

A busy economist should care because this is not really about payday loans per se. It is about whether a common style of policy analysis systematically understates the supply-side consequences of price regulation in competitive, low-rent markets.

### Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Not quite. The current opening gets the facts on the table, but it reads like an event study plus a new label (“supply destruction multiplier”) rather than a sharp economic question. The paper needs to lead less with “here is a big collapse” and more with “here is a general problem in regulatory economics: policymakers model rationing on the demand side when the relevant margin is firm exit.”

### The pitch the paper should have

“Price caps are usually evaluated as if firms remain in the market and consumers simply borrow less at lower prices. But in markets with many firms, high fixed costs, and little inframarginal rent, a binding cap may instead trigger mass exit and market concentration. Studying the UK payday lending cap, this paper shows that the regulator’s predicted reduction in access was modest, while realized lending and firm participation collapsed, suggesting that standard regulatory cost-benefit analysis can badly miss equilibrium supply responses.”

That is the AER version of the pitch. It makes the paper about a broader economic problem, not just an ex post postmortem of one UK rule.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper’s contribution is to document that the UK payday lending price cap produced far larger market contraction than the regulator predicted, and to argue that this gap arose because standard cost-benefit analysis ignored endogenous firm exit in a thin-margin market.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3-4 papers?

Only partially. Right now the paper is differentiated mainly by:
1. the UK setting,
2. the scale of the contraction,
3. the comparison between predicted and realized outcomes.

That is not yet enough. There is a lot of work on payday lending regulation, consumer credit access, and interest-rate caps. The paper needs to say much more precisely: what do we learn here that papers on payday loan bans, rate caps, and consumer credit supply did not already show? Is the novelty:
- explicit comparison of ex ante regulatory CBA to ex post market outcomes?
- focus on market structure and mass exit rather than borrower welfare?
- a generalizable warning about equilibrium supply in low-margin industries?

At present, the paper implies all three, but commits fully to none.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or filling a gap in a LITERATURE?

It is trying to answer a world question, which is good: what actually happens when you cap prices in a thin-margin market? But it keeps slipping into literature-gap language around what the FCA did not model. The stronger version is not “the regulator omitted X.” It is “in the world, price caps can convert a competitive market into an oligopoly because exit, not price adjustment, is the operative margin.”

### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what's new?

Not cleanly. Right now they would probably say: “It’s a descriptive paper showing the UK payday lending cap led to a huge contraction, bigger than regulators expected.” That is interesting, but it still sounds like “another policy shock in consumer finance.” The paper has not yet made the conceptual novelty vivid enough.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Most importantly, one of these:

- **Elevate the paper from a UK payday case to a general proposition about price regulation in low-rent markets.** That means explicitly linking to industries where price caps/ceilings matter and explaining conditions under which supply destruction should occur.
- **Show a market structure implication, not just a volume implication.** The concentration shift is potentially more important than the lending decline. If the paper can frame the cap as a policy that both shrank supply and reallocated market power, that becomes a broader IO/regulation contribution.
- **Bring in welfare-relevant downstream outcomes.** Not referee-style robustness, but strategically: what changed for borrowers after the collapse? Did substitutes appear? Did overdrafts, illegal lending, BNPL, arrears, or defaults move? Without some implication for households or equilibrium substitution, the paper risks being “interesting but incomplete.”
- **Use the ex ante/ex post mismatch more ambitiously.** The big idea could be: regulatory CBAs are systematically biased when they assume fixed market structure. That could be a broad contribution if supported and framed properly.

If the author only keeps “the market shrank a lot more than expected,” the contribution is real but not AER-sized.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Before the paper, the conventional evaluation of payday loan price caps focuses on borrower protection versus credit access, often implicitly assuming firms remain and quantities adjust smoothly.

### Tension

But if firms in this market operate near break-even, that framework may be badly wrong: a binding price cap may not merely ration marginal borrowers; it may wipe out suppliers and transform market structure. The tension is between the regulator’s modest predicted effect and the realized collapse.

### Resolution

The paper documents that the UK payday market contracted massively after the cap, with firm exit and concentration far beyond what the FCA anticipated, and argues that this is consistent with a supply-side collapse rather than a standard demand-side reduction in borrowing.

### Implications

The implication is that regulatory cost-benefit analysis for price caps may be systematically misleading if it treats market structure as fixed and ignores endogenous exit.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the bones of one, but not yet a fully persuasive arc. Right now it feels like:
- startling descriptive fact,
- phase decomposition,
- regional heterogeneity,
- new term (“supply destruction multiplier”),

That is close, but still a bit like a collection of descriptive findings seeking a grander story.

### What story should it be telling?

Not “look how wrong the FCA was.”  
Rather: **“Economists and regulators often think about price caps using the wrong margin of adjustment.”**

That story is stronger because:
- it starts from an economic idea,

- ends with a conceptual lesson.

The “two waves of exit” material is useful, but it currently risks muddying the core narrative. The compensation wave is interesting, but strategically it distracts from the cleaner claim about the cap. If retained, it should serve the story by clarifying what was cap-related versus what was not, not by turning the paper into a chronology of firm deaths.
