## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper argues that the UK’s 2015 payday lending price cap did not merely trim a high-cost credit market; it effectively destroyed it. The broader claim is that regulators systematically underpredict the effects of price caps when they analyze borrower demand but ignore supplier exit in thin-margin markets.

A busy economist should care because this is not really a payday-loan paper; it is a paper about how regulated markets can jump discontinuously when policy pushes many firms past viability thresholds. If true, that is potentially relevant far beyond consumer credit.

### Does the paper articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Mostly yes, but in a way that is too case-specific and too slogan-heavy. The current opening gives the dramatic facts, but the paper’s true ambition is buried: this is about the failure of standard regulatory incidence/CBA logic in markets with endogenous exit. The phrase “supply destruction multiplier” is memorable, but right now it reads more like branding than an organizing economic idea.

### What the first two paragraphs should say instead

The introduction should open with the general question first, then the UK case as the sharpest illustration:

> Price regulation is often analyzed as if firms remain in the market and quantities adjust smoothly. But in thin-margin industries with high fixed costs, a binding price cap may instead trigger mass exit and a discrete collapse in supply. How large can that gap be between standard ex ante predictions and realized market contraction?
>
> This paper studies the UK payday lending cap of 2015, a setting in which the regulator predicted that only 7–11 percent of borrowers would lose access to credit. In the years that followed, active lenders fell from roughly 240 to fewer than 20 and loan originations dropped by nearly 90 percent. I use this episode to show that the regulator’s cost-benefit framework missed the central equilibrium margin—firm exit—and to argue that price-cap analysis in thin-margin consumer finance markets must model market structure, not just borrower demand.

That framing makes the paper legible to IO, public finance, and regulation economists—not just consumer finance specialists.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### Contribution in one sentence

The paper’s contribution is to document that the UK payday-loan price cap produced far greater market contraction than regulators predicted and to frame that gap as evidence that price-cap analysis can badly misfire when it ignores endogenous firm exit in thin-margin markets.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?

Not yet clearly enough. The paper gestures at “broad applicability,” but it does not sharply distinguish itself from at least three nearby literatures:

1. **Payday lending and borrower welfare papers** that ask whether access to payday credit helps or harms households.
2. **Consumer credit regulation papers** that study caps, restrictions, or disclosure rules and their effects on prices/borrowing.
3. **Industrial organization/regulation papers** about price controls, entry/exit, and market structure.
4. **Ex post policy evaluation vs ex ante regulatory forecasting** papers, which is actually where this paper could be most distinctive.

Right now, a smart reader might summarize this as: “It’s another reduced-form paper showing that regulation shrank payday lending.” That is too small. The paper needs to insist that its novelty is **not** merely that quantity fell, but that:
- the regulator made a quantitatively precise ex ante forecast,
- the realized contraction was an order of magnitude larger,
- and the discrepancy appears tied to a particular modeling omission: equilibrium supplier exit.

That is a cleaner and more original contribution.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or filling a gap in a LITERATURE?

It is halfway between the two, and should move decisively toward the former.

The stronger world question is:

- **How do price caps affect market structure when many firms operate near break-even?**
- **Can standard policy analysis dramatically understate supply responses because it assumes smooth adjustment instead of threshold exit?**

The weaker version is:
- “The literature has not measured the gap between predicted and realized effects in payday lending.”

The paper currently slips too often into the latter.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the intro?

Not cleanly enough. They could likely say: “It documents that the UK payday cap caused a much bigger contraction than expected.” But they might still think: “Okay, but is that substantively different from existing work showing these markets shrink under regulation?”

The paper needs a one-sentence novelty claim that sticks:
- **This is an ex post audit of a regulator’s ex ante CBA, showing that the missing equilibrium margin was supplier exit.**

That is more memorable than “supply destruction multiplier.”

### What would make the contribution bigger?

Three possibilities, in descending order of impact:

1. **Reframe around predictive failure in regulation rather than payday lending per se.**  
   The big paper is: regulators using partial-equilibrium consumer-surplus logic can badly miss market collapse in thin-margin industries.

2. **Show broader external relevance through comparisons.**  
   Even a short comparative section on similar caps in other countries or adjacent consumer credit products would make this feel like a general phenomenon rather than an isolated case.

3. **Add a more economically meaningful outcome than loan counts alone.**  
   The strongest additional outcome would be a market-structure or substitution outcome that speaks directly to equilibrium: concentration, product substitution, movement into illegal/informal credit, bank overdrafts, rent-to-own, etc. Right now “loan originations fell” is important but not enough to elevate the paper on its own.

### How should the paper position itself relative to those neighbors?

It should **build on** the payday/consumer credit literature, but **pivot toward** the regulation/IO/policy-evaluation literature.

It should not “attack” the borrower-welfare papers. Those papers ask a different question: whether credit access helps or harms borrowers. This paper asks a prior question: what happens to market structure when regulation compresses margins? That distinction should be explicit.

It can more forcefully critique **regulatory CBA practice**. That is the right target, not individual academic papers.

### Is the paper currently positioned too narrowly or too broadly?

Paradoxically, both.

- **Too narrowly** in evidence: it is one country, one product, one reform.
- **Too broadly** in rhetoric: it claims a “documented empirical regularity with broad applicability” without enough comparative scaffolding.

A better position is: **a sharply informative case study with broader theoretical implications**. That is credible.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?

It seems under-engaged with at least four conversations:

1. **Industrial organization of regulated markets**  
   The paper needs more explicit engagement with models/empirics of price controls causing exit, consolidation, and quality/selection responses.

2. **Retrospective evaluation of regulatory impact analysis / cost-benefit analysis**  
   This may be the paper’s best contribution, but the literature isn’t foregrounded.

3. **Household finance / substitution after credit withdrawal**  
   Even if the paper does not estimate welfare, it must show awareness that loan contraction is not welfare by itself.

4. **Political economy / legal-institutional literature on redress and compensation liabilities**  
   Since the paper makes a two-phase argument, it needs to show why the compensation wave matters conceptually rather than just descriptively.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?

Not quite. It is currently having the conversation: “How much did the UK payday market shrink?”  
The more important conversation is: **“Why do policy analysts systematically underpredict discontinuous supply responses to price regulation?”**

That shift would substantially improve its odds.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Before this paper, the world looks like this: regulators evaluate price caps mostly through consumer-demand and borrower-welfare lenses, with the implicit assumption that firms adapt and markets continue operating, perhaps at smaller scale.

### Tension

The UK regulator predicted modest access loss, yet the market appears to have imploded. That creates a sharp puzzle: was this simply a bigger-than-expected quantity response, or does it reveal a more fundamental failure in how policy analysts think about supply in thin-margin industries?

### Resolution

The paper documents that contraction was vastly larger than predicted and that the timing of exits is consistent with a first wave linked to the cap and a later wave linked to compensation liabilities. The paper interprets this as evidence that the key omitted margin in the ex ante analysis was endogenous firm exit and cascading market consolidation.

### Implications

Economists and regulators should rethink price-cap analysis in markets where firms have high fixed costs and low margins; partial-equilibrium reasoning can be badly misleading. The implication is methodological and policy-relevant: model viability thresholds and market structure, not just borrower demand.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the ingredients, but not yet a fully disciplined arc. At present it feels like:
- dramatic aggregate facts,
- some decomposition,
- some regional evidence,
- then a broad claim about regulation.

That is closer to a **collection of facts looking for a theory of significance** than a fully realized AER narrative.

### What story should it be telling?

The story should be:

1. **Regulators thought this was a standard price cap problem.**
2. **In fact, it was a market-viability problem.**
3. **That distinction matters because it changed the outcome from modest rationing to near-total supplier exit.**
4. **This case reveals a broader blind spot in regulatory CBA for thin-margin markets.**

Everything in the paper should serve that arc. The two-phase decomposition is useful only insofar as it helps isolate the cap-induced collapse from later legal liabilities. The regional heterogeneity analysis is useful only insofar as it supports a market-wide supply shock interpretation. If those pieces do not clearly serve the arc, they read as filler.

Probably one of these:
- “Okay, but did borrowers end up better or worse off?”

For editorial purposes, the key point is that the paper can survive the second question much better than the first. It should therefore foreground generalizability, not welfare. It should explicitly say: *this paper is about market structure and predictive error, not borrower welfare.* That honesty helps.

### If findings are modest or null

Not applicable here; the findings are not null. The issue is instead whether the findings are **big in the right dimension**. Market contraction is dramatic. The challenge is converting dramatic descriptive facts into a broader economics contribution.

The paper is competent, but safe. It needs to do more than say “regulators should model supply.” It needs to show why this episode changes how economists should think about price-cap policy design.

**Recast the paper as an ex post evaluation of regulatory cost-benefit analysis under endogenous firm exit, and build the entire introduction, literature review, and results sequence around that broader question rather than around the payday-lending case itself.**

That is the one change that would most increase its chances.
