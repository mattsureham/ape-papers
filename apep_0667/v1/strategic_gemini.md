## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
This paper examines whether eliminating a shadow currency (paper food stamps) disrupts illicit drug markets. By exploiting the staggered rollout of Electronic Benefit Transfer (EBT), the author tests if severing the link between welfare benefits and cash-based drug trafficking reduced drug poisoning deaths. A busy economist should care because this is a fundamental test of how "payment infrastructure" affects the informal economy and whether "nudge-adjacent" anti-fraud policies have any meaningful downstream health impacts.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this well in the abstract and the first paragraph. However, it currently leads with the institutional history of the 1990s. To be more "AER-ready," it should lead with the broader economic question of market friction.

**The Pitch the Paper Should Have:**
"How resilient are illicit markets to disruptions in their payment infrastructure? This paper uses the elimination of paper food stamps—a primary 'shadow currency' for street-level drug transactions—to estimate whether removing a major source of liquid capital reduces drug-market activity. Despite the massive scale of the EBT transition, I find a precisely estimated null effect on drug mortality, suggesting that informal economies adapt almost instantly to the removal of specific media of exchange."

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**Contribution:** The paper identifies the limits of payment-infrastructure disruption as a tool for illicit market intervention, providing a precisely estimated null on a high-stakes health outcome.

- **Differentiation:** It differentiates itself from Wright et al. (2017) by moving from aggregate crime (which includes many non-drug channels) to drug-specific mortality and expanding from a single state to a national panel. 
- **Framing:** It is framed as a question about the WORLD (how do drug markets adapt?), but leans heavily on the literature gap in the intro. 
- **Verdict:** A smart economist would say: "It's a very clean null that suggests drug dealers don't care about EBT; they just found another way to get paid."
- **How to make it bigger:** The paper needs to show *how* they adapted. Adding data on other crime types (e.g., petty theft or robberies) would test the "substitution to other cash sources" mechanism mentioned in the discussion.

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
The paper sits at the intersection of **Public Economics (Transfer delivery)** and **The Economics of Crime/Illicit Markets**.

- **Neighbors:** Wright et al. (2017) on EBT/Crime; Hastings & Washington (2010) on EBT/Consumption; Reuter (2006) on drug market resilience.
- **Strategy:** It should **synthesize** the "delivery mechanism" literature with the "market resilience" literature. 
- **Missing Conversations:** The paper needs to speak to the **Industrial Organization of Illicit Markets**. Is the drug market a "platform"? If you remove one payment rail, do they just move to the next? It should also reference the "Cashless Society" literature (e.g., Rogoff) regarding the role of physical currency in crime.

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC
- **Setup:** Paper food stamps were a $27B liquid asset often traded for drugs at 50 cents on the dollar.
- **Tension:** Policy-makers assumed EBT would "kill" this market by making benefits non-transferable.
- **Resolution:** It didn't. Drug deaths didn't budge.
- **Implications:** Anti-fraud technology in welfare may improve "program integrity" but it is a failed tool for drug policy.

**Evaluation:** The arc is clear. It is a "The Dog That Didn't Bark" story. The story is currently "results looking for a mechanism." To fix this, Section 6 (Mechanisms) needs to be more than a list of guesses; it needs even a small amount of descriptive evidence on substitution.

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
**The Fact:** "We replaced paper food stamps with cards to stop people from buying drugs with them, and it had zero effect on overdose deaths."
**Reaction:** People will lean in. It’s counter-intuitive to the "common sense" of 1990s policy.
**Follow-up:** "So, did they just start stealing more, or did the drug dealers start taking EBT cards at a higher discount?" This is the question the paper *must* attempt to answer to stay in the AER.

---

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
- **Front-load the Mechanism:** Move the discussion of "Why we might see a null" (inelastic demand vs. payment substitution) earlier, perhaps at the end of the intro.
- **Visuals:** The paper needs a clear "Event Study" plot in the main text (currently only described). For an AER paper, the visualization of the null is as important as the table.
- **Appendix:** The "Standardized Effect Sizes" (Table 5) is excellent and should be moved to the main text to prove the "precisely estimated" part of the "precisely estimated null."

---

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
**The Gap:** Currently, it's a very high-quality "Policy Evaluation" that belongs in *AEJ: Policy*. To make it *AER*, it needs to be an "Economics Paper" about **Market Adaptation.**

**Single most impactful advice:** Don't just report the null on deaths; find a "positive" result on a proxy for the *cost of adaptation*. If EBT made it harder to buy drugs, did it lead to a temporary spike in property crime (theft for cash) or a change in the "street price" of food stamps? Even a small amount of data on the *process* of substitution would elevate this from a "program evaluation" to a "market study."
