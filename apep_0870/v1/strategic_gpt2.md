## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple, policy-relevant question: when governments require online platforms to police copyrighted uploads, does that hurt employment in the sectors supposedly protected or endangered by the rule? Using the staggered implementation of the EU Copyright Directive’s Article 17 across member states, the paper finds essentially no effect on broad information/creative-sector employment.

A busy economist should care because Article 17 became a global symbol of platform regulation: critics predicted major economic harm, supporters predicted stronger creative markets, and this paper claims the first causal evidence on whether either side’s employment fears materialized.

### Does the paper articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?
Not quite. The introduction is reasonably competent, but it muddies the target. It says “creative-sector employment,” but the actual main outcome is NACE J, which is much broader than the creative sector. That mismatch is strategically dangerous. The paper wants the salience of “the creative economy,” but the design really speaks to broad information-and-communication employment. Sophisticated readers will notice this immediately and discount the claim.

The first two paragraphs should be much more disciplined:
1. Start with the world question: when platform liability for copyrighted content increases, who bears the cost?
2. Then say why Article 17 is the right setting and exactly what can be learned: not “the creative economy” writ large, but whether one of the world’s most controversial platform regulations changed employment in the broad sector most exposed to digital content markets.

### The pitch the paper should have
Article 17 of the EU Copyright Directive was sold and attacked as a consequential intervention in the digital economy: critics warned that mandatory upload filtering would suppress platforms and creative activity, while supporters claimed it would strengthen copyright markets. This paper uses the staggered implementation of Article 17 across EU countries to ask whether that shock changed employment in the broad information-and-communication sector most exposed to digital content regulation, and finds no detectable effect. The result suggests that one of the world’s most debated platform copyright mandates did not generate large employment consequences at the sectoral level.

That is cleaner, more honest, and more AER-like. It is a world question about incidence and economic consequences of platform regulation, not a gap-filling exercise about one directive.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution
The paper’s contribution is to provide causal evidence that the EU’s Article 17 upload-filter mandate had no detectable effect on broad information-and-communication-sector employment.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest papers?
Only partially. The paper says there is “no causal evidence” on employment consequences, which may well be true in this narrow setting, but the introduction does not do enough to distinguish itself from:
- broader platform regulation papers,
- copyright and digital piracy papers,
- policy evaluations of online content regulation,
- and the growing body of “controversial regulation, modest realized effects” papers.

Right now the differentiation is mostly “first paper on this policy using staggered DiD.” That is not enough for AER positioning. The differentiation needs to be substantive: this is not just another policy evaluation; it is evidence on whether platform-side liability rules have real labor-market bite.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a gap in a literature?
It oscillates, but too often slides into “there is no evidence on X.” That is weaker. The stronger framing is:
- What happens economically when governments shift copyright enforcement obligations onto platforms?
- Do highly salient digital regulations actually reshape employment, or are they mostly symbolic/formalizing policies?

That is a world question. The current draft too often sounds like it is filling a missing cell in the literature matrix.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?
They could say: “It estimates a null effect of Article 17 on broad ICT employment using staggered adoption.” That is understandable, but not memorable. The risk is exactly what you warned about: “another DiD paper about X.”

The reason is that the paper’s novelty is currently procedural and contextual, not conceptual. The reader sees:
- staggered transposition,
- broad sectoral employment outcome,
- null result,
- some placebo and DDD.

That sounds competent, not important.

### What would make the contribution bigger?
Several concrete possibilities:

1. **Sharper outcome variable**
   The biggest issue is outcome mismatch. If the headline is about the “creative sector,” the paper needs outcomes closer to actually exposed activities: audiovisual production, music, publishing, creator-side firms, platform-adjacent firms, firm entry/exit, self-employment, or occupation-level creative jobs. Broad NACE J is too diluted. This is the single biggest substantive limitation to contribution size.

2. **Mechanism on who absorbed compliance costs**
   The paper hints at three mechanisms in discussion, but none is really shown. A stronger paper would distinguish whether the policy was irrelevant because:
   - large platforms had already complied,
   - small platforms were exempt,
   - costs were shifted to users/advertisers,
   - or losses on the platform side were offset by gains for rights holders/creators.
   Even one convincing mechanism would make the null much more informative.

3. **Reframe around platform regulation incidence**
   Instead of “does this hurt the creative economy?,” frame it as “who pays when enforcement is deputized to platforms?” Employment is only one margin. This can make even a null labor-market result feel like part of a bigger economic question.

4. **Use a more policy-relevant comparison**
   If possible, compare Article 17 to other digital regulations that genuinely changed platform behavior, or contrast sectors with high copyrighted user-generated content exposure against low-exposure digital sectors. That would make the result less “broad sector unchanged” and more “high-exposure margins also unchanged.”

5. **Bring in composition outcomes**
   Employment levels may be too blunt. An AER-caliber version might show no aggregate employment effect but meaningful composition changes: concentration, firm size distribution, entry, freelance work, or labor reallocation within digital media. That would transform a null from “nothing happened” to “the adjustment margin was elsewhere.”

It should **build on and connect**, not attack. This is not a paper that overturns a flagship empirical result. Its leverage comes from stitching together conversations that usually do not speak to each other:
- the legal/policy debate on Article 17,
- economics of copyright and creative markets,
- and political economy of platform regulation.

The paper should say: prior work explains why Article 17 might matter; we show what happened on one important economic margin.

### Is it positioned too narrowly or too broadly?
Oddly, both.

- **Too narrowly** in the sense that it is heavily tied to the details of one EU directive and one empirical design.
- **Too broadly** in the sense that it overclaims to speak about “the creative economy” when the measured outcome is much broader and more weakly exposed.

The right middle ground is: a paper about the economic incidence of platform copyright mandates, using Article 17 as a globally salient test case.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?
It under-engages with:
- the economics of intermediary liability,
- the digitization/creative production literature beyond legal commentary,
- industrial organization of platforms and compliance technologies,
- and labor-market composition in digital creative industries.

It also should speak more directly to the broader literature on whether salient regulations have large real effects or are mostly absorbed by large firms with preexisting compliance capacity. That conversation is larger than copyright.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?
Not quite yet. Right now it is having a somewhat cramped conversation: “Here is a causal estimate of a controversial EU policy.” The more impactful conversation is:
- When policymakers impose compliance obligations on large digital intermediaries, do those obligations generate large real-side effects?
- Are fears of job destruction under platform regulation overstated because dominant firms have already internalized these practices?

That is a more interesting and less niche conversation.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
A major digital regulation shifted copyright enforcement obligations from rights holders and reactive notice-and-takedown systems toward platforms themselves. Public debate predicted dramatic consequences for platforms, creators, and the internet economy.

### Tension
Despite the intensity of the debate, there is little evidence on whether these mandates actually changed real economic outcomes. The core empirical puzzle is whether highly visible platform regulation meaningfully alters labor-market outcomes, especially in sectors linked to digital content.

### Resolution
The paper finds no detectable effect on broad information-and-communication employment following national implementation of Article 17.

### Implications
The most controversial part of the directive appears not to have had large sector-level employment consequences. That implies either low effective incidence, successful absorption by large incumbents, offsetting gains and losses, or that the economically meaningful effects lie on margins other than aggregate employment.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
It has the raw ingredients, but the arc is not fully under control. Too often it reads like:
- introduction,
- institutional detail,
- econometrics,
- results,
- robustness,
- null.

That is a serviceable paper structure, not a memorable narrative. The current manuscript is perilously close to being “a collection of null estimates plus specification checks.”

### What story should it be telling?
The story should be:

**Setup:** Governments increasingly deputize platforms to enforce rules.  
**Tension:** Everyone claims this will reshape the digital economy, but we do not know whether the burden shows up in real outcomes.  
**Resolution:** In the canonical case—Article 17—it does not show up in broad sectoral employment.  
**Implications:** Either the burden was borne by large platforms with little labor-market pass-through, or the action is on narrower margins than aggregate jobs.

That is a coherent story. It turns the paper from “Article 17 evaluation” into “evidence on the real incidence of platform mandates.”

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?
“One of the most politically explosive digital regulations in Europe—the upload-filter mandate—appears to have had no measurable effect on broad information-sector employment.”

That is a reasonably good opener.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
Some would lean in, but only briefly. The topic is salient enough to get attention, but the follow-up reaction will be: “Interesting, but broad information-sector employment seems pretty far from the policy’s most exposed margin.” If the author cannot answer that immediately, attention is lost.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Almost certainly:
- “But are you measuring the right outcome?”
Then:
- “Maybe this just formalized what big platforms were already doing?”
And:
- “Could there still be effects on narrower creative subsectors, entry, market structure, or creator incomes?”

Those follow-up questions basically define the paper’s strategic problem. The result is potentially interesting, but the current outcome variable makes the null feel less decisive than the prose suggests.

### Is the null itself interesting?
Yes, but only if sold correctly. Nulls are interesting when they discipline exaggerated beliefs. Here, the policy was high-profile and the debate featured dramatic predictions. So “no large employment effect” can be informative.

But the paper must avoid sounding like a failed attempt to find an effect. Right now it mostly succeeds, but it overshoots by claiming too much. The right claim is not “the upload filter tax is zero” in a sweeping sense. The right claim is:
- there is no detectable broad sectoral employment effect,
- and that itself is informative given the salience of the policy and the precision of the estimate.

The title is punchy, but also risky. It advertises a level of generality the data do not fully support.

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

### What would make it read better?

1. **Shorten the methodological throat-clearing**
   The paper gets to the result relatively quickly, which is good, but it still spends too much introduction real estate on estimator names, standard errors, and MDE discussion. That material belongs later or more compactly. The introduction should foreground the world question and the substantive result.

2. **Move some robustness detail out of the introduction**
   The fourth introductory paragraph is overloaded with leave-one-country-out, alternative controls, pre-trend nuance, placebo specifics. That is too much too early. Save some of that for results. The introduction should tell me what the paper asks, what it finds, and why it matters.

3. **Be more disciplined about outcome labeling**
   Stop calling NACE J “creative-sector employment” without qualification. Call it “broad information-and-communication employment” and acknowledge upfront that this is a sector-level test of broad labor-market incidence, not a direct measure of narrow creator employment.

4. **Promote the most interesting interpretive point**
   One of the paper’s more interesting ideas is that Article 17 may have formalized existing compliance among large platforms. That belongs much earlier—ideally in the framing, not buried as one of several discussion mechanisms.

5. **Demote or drop the standardized effect size section**
   The SDE table feels imported from a meta-analysis template rather than organically useful to this paper. It does not help the narrative and signals “workflow product” rather than polished article. I would cut it or relegate it fully to appendix.

6. **The conclusion should do more than summarize**
   Right now the conclusion mostly restates the result. It should instead crystallize the broader implication: major platform mandates may have less aggregate labor-market bite than public debate implies, especially when they target large incumbents with existing compliance systems.

### Is the good stuff front-loaded?
Somewhat, yes. The main finding appears early, which is a strength. But the prose spends too much effort proving diligence before fully winning interest.

### Are important results buried?
The most strategically important caveat—that the outcome is broad and may attenuate effects on exposed subsectors—is present, but it is framed as limitation rather than as a central interpretive lens. That should be integrated earlier. Also, the placebo’s meaning is potentially important, but currently reads as a routine check rather than part of the substantive interpretation.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

Be honest: this is not there yet.

### What is the gap?
Primarily **scope and framing**, secondarily **novelty**.

- **Framing problem:** The paper’s best version is about the incidence of platform regulation. The current version is about one directive and one null estimate.
- **Scope problem:** The main outcome is too aggregated relative to the claim. This shrinks the paper’s substantive punch.
- **Novelty problem:** “First DiD on Article 17 employment” is not enough. AER needs either a much sharper substantive margin, a broader conceptual contribution, or both.
- **Ambition problem:** The paper is competent but safe. It demonstrates due diligence around a null, but does not yet convert that null into a larger claim about political economy, platform governance, or creative markets.

### What would excite the top 10 people in this field?
A version that shows one of two things:

1. **Either**: despite enormous controversy, Article 17 had no effect not just on broad sector employment but also on the more exposed margins where theory says it should bite—creative occupations, firm entry, small platforms, creator-side activity, etc.

2. **Or**: aggregate employment was unchanged, but the policy meaningfully reshaped market structure or composition—favoring incumbents, discouraging entry, shifting work toward licensed/professional creators, or changing the platform/rights-holder split.

Either version would feel much bigger than the current broad-null exercise.

### Single most impactful piece of advice
**Rebuild the paper around a sharper and more credible measure of the economically exposed margin—if the paper cannot get closer to actually affected creative/platform outcomes, it must radically scale back its claims and reframe as evidence on the absence of broad sectoral labor-market effects from platform copyright mandates.**

That is the fork in the road. Either improve the outcome, or narrow the claim. Right now it has the modest outcome and the expansive claim, which is the least persuasive combination.
