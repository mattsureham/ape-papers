## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether the massive wave of U.S. bank branch closures—specifically those triggered by mergers—disproportionately harms Black mortgage applicants. By using a county's exposure to recent bank mergers as an instrument for local branch closures, the author finds that losing a branch significantly widens the Black-White mortgage denial gap, likely because minority borrowers rely more on the "soft information" and relationships maintained at physical locations. 

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this well in spirit, but the first two paragraphs are a bit too "narrative" for the AER. They focus on the "banker who knew their name" before getting to the data. 

**The Pitch the Paper Should Have:**
"Over 20,000 bank branches closed in the U.S. between 2010 and 2023, largely driven by post-merger consolidation. While these closures are often framed as efficiency-enhancing, I show they impose a 'consolidation tax' that falls disproportionately on Black borrowers, widening the Black-White mortgage denial gap by 1.7 percentage points for every 1% increase in merger-induced closures. Using a shift-share IV design based on local merger exposure, I provide the first causal evidence that physical banking infrastructure remains a critical gatekeeper for racial equity in credit markets."

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-Sentence Contribution:** This paper identifies bank mergers as a structural driver of racial inequality in the mortgage market, providing a causal link between industry consolidation and the widening of racial denial gaps.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It is well-differentiated from Nguyen (2019) by moving from credit *quantity* in small business to *distributional gaps* in mortgages. It differentiates from the "discrimination" literature (Bartlett et al. 2022) by focusing on *market structure* rather than lender behavior.
*   **World vs. Literature:** It frames itself as answering a question about the WORLD (how mergers affect people), which is an AER-level strength.
*   **The "Smart Economist" Test:** A reader would say: "It’s the branch closure paper that proves physical banks still matter for the racial wealth gap."
*   **Make it Bigger:** The contribution would be massive if it could definitively show *why* digital banking fails to fill this gap. Currently, the "soft information" mechanism is argued via heterogeneity, but more direct evidence on the "digital divide" would seal the deal.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

*   **Closest Neighbors:** Nguyen (2019, AEJ: Applied), Bartlett et al. (2022, JFE), Bayer et al. (2018, RFS), and Erel et al. (2012, JF).
*   **Positioning:** It builds on Nguyen (methodology) and Bartlett (mortgage context) to synthesize a new argument: **Antitrust policy is a civil rights issue.** 
*   **The Conversation:** The paper is currently in a "Banking/Finance" conversation. To make it AER, it needs to speak more to the **Industrial Organization (IO)** and **Urban Economics** crowds. It needs to argue that branch networks are a "local public good" with high switching costs.
*   **Missing Pieces:** It should engage more with the literature on "banking deserts" and the "spatial mismatch" hypothesis in labor economics.

*   **Setup:** Bank mergers are common; regulators focus on HHI (market power) and efficiency.
*   **Tension:** If "soft info" is race-contingent, then closures aren't just "trimming fat"—they are cutting off specific populations from the credit ladder.
*   **Resolution:** Closures widened the BW denial gap significantly; OLS was biased because banks close branches in gentrifying areas (masking the harm).
*   **Implications:** Merger review must include "Racial Impact Statements."

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The paper is currently a "Very Strong Field Journal" paper. To make it AER, it needs **Ambitious Scope.**

**The Gap:** It’s currently a county-level story. To be truly AER, it should strive for a **general equilibrium** or **welfare** statement. If Black borrowers are denied at Branch A, do they eventually get a loan at Branch B at a higher price? Or do they drop out of the housing market entirely?

**Single Biggest Piece of Advice:** Use the loan-level data to track the "migration" of rejected borrowers. If you can show that these borrowers don't just "switch" but actually "disappear" from the credit market, you’ve moved from a finance paper to a foundational welfare paper.
