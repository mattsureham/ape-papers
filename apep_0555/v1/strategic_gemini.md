## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
This paper exploits Nigeria’s catastrophic 2022-2023 naira redesign—which abruptly removed 76% of cash from circulation—to identify how monetary scarcity fractures informal markets. By comparing cash-dependent local staples to banking-mediated imports within the same markets, it identifies a 9% price spike for cash-mediated goods and reveals a critical mechanism: while consumers face higher transaction costs (driving prices up), supply chains break down at the farmgate (driving local prices down), with the net effect determined by supply-chain length.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this reasonably well, but the first two paragraphs are a bit "case-study heavy." They focus on the Nigeria/India comparison before the broader economic intuition. 
*The pitch it should have:* "Cash is the essential infrastructure of the informal world, yet we treat its removal as a mere policy tweak. This paper shows that an abrupt demonetization acts as a massive regressive tax, creating market-level price wedges that persist long after the 'crisis' ends by disrupting the very links between farmers and consumers."

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
The paper identifies two offsetting channels—consumer transaction costs vs. supply-chain disruption—that determine the net price response to a liquidity shock in an informal economy.

**Evaluation:**
- **Differentiation:** It differentiates well from the India literature (Chodorow-Reich et al., 2020) by using the local-vs-imported rice "natural experiment" to isolate the supply-chain mechanism, which the Indian data didn't allow as cleanly.
- **Question vs. Gap:** It frames itself as a question about the world (how do markets handle cash scarcity?), but leans heavily on "filling a gap in the Africa literature." The former is much more AER-worthy.
- **Explanation:** A smart economist would say: "It’s a paper showing that cash scarcity makes retail prices rise but farmgate-linked prices fall because middlemen can't buy from farmers."
- **Bigger Contribution:** To make this bigger, the author needs to bridge the "monetary" and "development" worlds more aggressively. Is this a paper about the **Microfoundations of Money Demand in the Informal Sector**? If so, the welfare calculation (Section 7.4) needs to move from a "back-of-the-envelope" aside to a central, theoretically-grounded result.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
The paper sits at the intersection of Monetary Economics and Development.

- **Neighbors:** Chodorow-Reich et al. (2020) on India; Aggarwal & Narayanan (2022) on agricultural disruption; Aker (2010) on market integration.
- **Positioning:** It builds on Chodorow-Reich but "attacks" the lack of mechanism decomposition in previous work.
- **Missing Literatures:** It should speak more to the **Search and Matching** literature in macro. If I can't find a guy with cash, my "match" fails. The "transaction cost" here is essentially a search friction. Connecting to the "Money as a Memory" or "Medium of Exchange" literature (e.g., Kocherlakota) would elevate the theoretical stakes.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC
- **Setup:** Informal economies run on cash; governments want to formalize.
- **Tension:** Formalization through "shock" demonetization is intended to track money but might actually destroy the markets it tries to formalize.
- **Resolution:** The data shows a "wedge" is driven between goods. Local staples get more expensive for the poor, even as farmers get less for their crops. 
- **Implications:** "Shock formalization" is a regressive tax that permanently reorders markets.

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong, but the "resolution" is currently muddled by the randomization inference (RI) results. The author is being *too* honest about the p-value of 0.41 in the intro. In a strategic memo, I'd tell them: "Move the RI discussion to the robustness section. Don't let a conservative inference method kill the narrative of your primary result in the first 5 pages."

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
At a dinner party, I’d lead with: *"Nigeria took away everyone's cash to catch tax evaders, and it ended up making the price of local rice drop for the wrong reasons—because the middlemen couldn't afford to pay the farmers, so the food just sat there rotting while city people went hungry."*

Economists would lean in. They would ask: *"Did they eventually switch to mobile money?"* The paper’s answer—that the price wedge **persisted** through December 2023—is the most "lean-in" fact it has.

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
- **Front-load:** The Rice vs. Imported Rice (Section 4.3) is the "sexy" part of the paper. It should be highlighted earlier as the "Identification Crown Jewel."
- **Trim:** The "Institutional Background" (Section 2) is a bit long. Busy economists know what a cash crunch looks like; get to the data faster.
- **Appendix:** The "Standardized Effect Sizes" (Table 5) are confusing and actually make the result look smaller. Keep them in the appendix.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The gap is **Ambition**. Currently, it feels like a very high-quality "applied micro" paper about a specific event in Nigeria. To be AER, it needs to be a paper about the **Fragility of Informal Exchange**. 

**The Single Most Impactful Advice:** Shift the framing from "The effects of the Nigerian Naira redesign" to "How the Medium of Exchange Sustains Supply Chains." Use the Nigerian case as the *evidence*, but make the *story* about the fundamental role of physical liquidity in preventing the collapse of the intermediary margin.
