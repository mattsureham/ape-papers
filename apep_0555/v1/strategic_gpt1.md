## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple, important question: what happens to real markets when cash suddenly disappears in an economy where most exchange still runs on cash? Using Nigeria’s 2022–23 demonetization, the paper argues that cash scarcity disrupted food markets, raising prices for cash-dependent goods on average while lowering prices for some locally sourced staples with especially cash-intensive supply chains.

A busy economist should care because this is not really a paper about Nigeria or even demonetization per se; it is a paper about whether the medium of exchange is market infrastructure. If cash is not a neutral veil but an operational input into trade, then monetary policy, financial inclusion, informality, and food-market functioning are much more tightly linked than standard framing suggests.

### Does the paper articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Not quite. The current introduction is competent and informed, but it leads too much with “first evidence in Sub-Saharan Africa” and with the India literature. That makes the paper sound like a regional extension paper. The better pitch is world-facing: governments periodically try to force economies away from cash; what breaks when they do, and why?

The first two paragraphs should say something like:

> In much of the developing world, cash is not just a payment instrument; it is the infrastructure that coordinates trade. When governments abruptly withdraw currency from circulation, they are not merely changing liquidity conditions—they may disable the transaction technology on which informal markets depend. Yet we still know surprisingly little about how such cash shocks propagate through real markets, especially food markets where goods are perishable, margins are thin, and supply chains rely on repeated small cash transactions.
>
> This paper studies Nigeria’s 2022–23 naira redesign, which removed most cash from circulation in a highly cash-dependent economy. I show that when cash vanished, food prices moved sharply and unevenly: cash-dependent goods became more expensive on average, but some locally sourced goods with especially cash-intensive intermediation became cheaper relative to imported substitutes. The core message is that demonetization operates through two distinct margins—consumer transaction costs and intermediary supply-chain disruption—and that understanding both is essential for evaluating monetary reforms in informal economies.

That is the pitch this paper should own.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### Contribution in one sentence

The paper’s contribution is to show that sudden cash withdrawal can distort food markets through two separable channels—raising transaction costs for cash-dependent purchases while simultaneously disrupting cash-intensive intermediation—and to illustrate this using Nigeria’s demonetization.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest papers?

Only partially. The paper does differentiate itself from India-focused demonetization papers and from generic food-price papers, but the differentiation is still too descriptive: “first evidence from Sub-Saharan Africa,” “within-rice mechanism test,” etc. That is not enough for AER positioning.

The closest differentiation should be:

1. Existing demonetization papers mostly estimate aggregate output or market-level disruption.
2. This paper uses within-market cross-commodity variation to say something more conceptual: **cash shocks do not simply reduce activity; they reallocate relative prices depending on how cash enters the supply chain.**
3. The rice comparison is valuable not because it is cute, but because it isolates the coexistence of two opposing channels within the same broad consumption category.

Right now, a smart economist might still summarize it as “another DiD paper on demonetization and prices.” The introduction needs to make unmistakably clear that the paper is about the **economic role of cash in organizing exchange**.

### World question or literature-gap question?

The paper drifts between the two. Its strongest version is a world question: *What does removing cash do to market functioning in a largely informal economy?* Its weaker version is a literature-gap question: *There is lots on India, less on Africa.* The introduction currently leans too much toward the latter.

For AER, it needs to be framed much more as a question about the world.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new?

Today: maybe, but not reliably. Some would say “it’s a paper on Nigeria demonetization and food prices using differential cash dependence.” That is serviceable but not memorable.

The memorable version is:  
**“They show that demonetization works through two opposing market channels—consumer-side cash scarcity and trader-side supply-chain disruption—and that the sign of price effects depends on which channel dominates.”**

That is new enough to travel.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Most importantly: make the paper less about a binary classification of commodities and more about a broader economic claim about **cash intensity along the supply chain**. A bigger paper would do one or more of the following:

- Tie outcomes more explicitly to **market functioning**, not just prices: availability, stockouts, arrivals, dispersion, or margins.
- Strengthen the mechanism angle by connecting to **which stage of the chain breaks**—farmer-to-assembler, wholesaler-to-retailer, retail-to-consumer.
- Reframe around **relative price distortions created by payment technology shocks**, not around a single episode of demonetization.
- Show that the sign reversal is not just “rice is weird,” but part of a broader organizing principle: short-chain goods vs long-chain goods, perishables vs storable goods, or direct-sale vs intermediary-heavy goods.

If they can elevate the paper from “cash-dependent vs banking-mediated commodities” to “payment technology as a determinant of supply-chain resilience,” the contribution gets much bigger.

Build on them, but pivot the conversation.

- Relative to informality/financial inclusion: “informality is not just tax evasion or low productivity; it is also dependence on a specific exchange technology.”

So: build on, don’t attack. But the paper should be more assertive that it is changing the object of interest, not merely changing country.

Currently, oddly, both.

- Too narrow in the sense that it is heavily anchored to Nigeria and to the demonetization episode.
- Too broad in the sense that it gestures at several literatures—demonetization, food prices, informality, financial inclusion—without fully declaring which conversation it wants to lead.

It needs a more disciplined center of gravity. The best center is: **real effects of payment technology shocks in informal economies**.

### What literature does it seem unaware of?

Not wholly unaware, but under-engaged with at least three conversations:

1. **Money/search/means-of-payment literature** in macro and theory: the claim that money is market infrastructure should speak to that tradition more directly.
2. **Industrial organization / supply-chain resilience** literatures: if the central mechanism is intermediation breakdown, this should connect to work on bottlenecks, chain length, and contracting frictions.

Not yet. It is having the “demonetization in Africa” conversation. That is publishable somewhere, but not the highest-value conversation.

The more powerful conversation is:  
**What happens when the transaction medium is itself disrupted, and what does that reveal about how informal markets function?**

That connects monetary economics, development, market design, and supply chains in a way that could interest a wider AER audience.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

In informal economies, food markets rely heavily on cash, but economists often treat the medium of exchange as secondary relative to transport, information, and competition. Governments sometimes force rapid shifts away from cash through demonetization or currency reform.

### Tension

We do not know whether such shocks mainly make purchases harder for consumers, choke off intermediation for traders, or both. And those two mechanisms predict different price effects.

### Resolution

The paper finds that cash-dependent commodities become relatively more expensive on average during Nigeria’s cash shortage, but local rice becomes relatively cheaper than imported rice, suggesting that consumer transaction costs and trader-side supply disruption operate simultaneously.

### Implications

Cash is not merely a nominal object; it is a piece of market infrastructure. Abrupt cash withdrawal can create regressive, heterogeneous distortions in real markets, especially in food systems and informal supply chains.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

Yes, but it is not as clean as it could be. The ingredients are all there. In fact, the paper has a better story than many competent reduced-form papers. The problem is that the story is somewhat buried under extensive institutional detail, literature review, and cautionary scaffolding.

The strongest narrative is:

1. Cash is infrastructure.
2. Removing it should affect markets through two opposite channels.
3. Nigeria provides a stark test.
4. Relative prices move in ways that reveal both channels.
5. Therefore, the medium of exchange shapes market organization and welfare.

That is a real narrative. The paper should trust it more.

At present, the introduction spends too much time cataloguing what happened and too little time dramatizing the conceptual puzzle. The “two channels with opposite signs” is the paper’s narrative engine; it should be front and center by page 2.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at dinner?

“During Nigeria’s demonetization, goods sold through cash-dependent supply chains got more expensive relative to banking-mediated goods—but local rice got cheaper relative to imported rice, suggesting that cash scarcity simultaneously taxed buyers and broke trader intermediation.”

That is the interesting fact.

### Would people lean in?

Yes, if presented that way. “Nigeria demonetization raised prices by 8.8%” is decent but not irresistible. “The sign of the price effect flips depending on where cash matters in the supply chain” is much more likely to make economists lean in.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Most likely:  
“So is the big lesson about consumer liquidity, trader intermediation, or market power—and how general is this outside Nigeria?”

That is exactly the follow-up the paper should want, because it invites the broader framing.

### If findings are modest or null

This is not really a null paper. But one internal strategic problem is that the paper itself repeatedly undercuts its own force by dwelling on inferential fragility in the front half of the paper. Intellectual honesty is good; editorially, though, it makes the story feel smaller than it is.

The right move is not to hide caveats, but to avoid making the introduction read like a preemptive rebuttal letter. Put the conceptual claim first. Put the inferential humility later. Right now the reader learns about limitations before fully understanding why the paper is interesting.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The main gap is **framing plus ambition**, not basic competence.

### Is it a framing problem?

Yes, strongly. The science may be good enough to interest referees, but the paper is not yet claiming the biggest thing it plausibly can. It is framed as a careful episode study from Nigeria. An AER version would be framed as a paper about **how exchange technology shapes real market outcomes**.

### Is it a scope problem?

Somewhat. The paper has one main outcome—prices—and a compelling mechanism vignette in rice. That may be enough for a field journal, but for AER the scope still feels a bit narrow. The paper would be more ambitious if it connected prices to a broader market-function outcome or demonstrated more general heterogeneity by supply-chain structure.

### Is it a novelty problem?

Moderately. Demonetization has been studied. Food price disruptions have been studied. DiD on crisis timing is familiar. What rescues novelty is the two-channel mechanism and the supply-chain framing. That novelty needs to be made unmistakable.

### Is it an ambition problem?

Yes. The paper is smart but safe. It reports a country episode, documents some relative price effects, and offers a plausible mechanism. An AER paper would more boldly redefine the question: not “what did Nigeria’s demonetization do?” but “what do cash shocks reveal about the organization of informal markets?”

### Single most impactful advice

**Rewrite the paper around one big claim: cash is market infrastructure, and demonetization creates predictable relative-price distortions depending on where cash sits in the supply chain.**

If they do only one thing, do that. Everything else follows.

That means:
- Stop selling “first evidence from Sub-Saharan Africa” as the main contribution.
- Stop leading with the literature gap.
- Lead with the conceptual insight and use Nigeria as the sharp empirical test.

If they can make readers walk away saying “this paper taught me something general about money and markets,” then it has a shot. If readers walk away saying “interesting Nigeria episode,” it does not.
