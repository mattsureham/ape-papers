## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks what happens to real markets when cash suddenly disappears in a cash-based economy. Using Nigeria’s 2022–23 naira redesign, it studies how a severe cash shortage changed food prices, showing that cash-dependent goods moved differently from goods traded through more formal banking channels, and that the effects differed sharply across supply chains.

A busy economist should care because this is not really a paper about one Nigerian policy episode; it is a paper about whether the medium of exchange is part of market infrastructure. If removing cash distorts food markets in predictable ways, then demonetization, financial inclusion, informality, and market integration all need to be thought about together.

**Does the paper articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Not quite. The current opening is competent and informed, but it is too “topic + literature gap + setting” and not enough “big economic question + striking answer.” The first two paragraphs should do less scene-setting and more conceptual work. Right now the intro sounds like an applied policy paper on Nigeria; it should sound like a paper about the economics of exchange infrastructure, with Nigeria as the clean episode.

**The pitch the paper should have:**

> Cash is not just a veil over exchange in informal economies; it is part of the market’s operating infrastructure. This paper studies what happens when that infrastructure is abruptly removed, using Nigeria’s 2022–23 demonetization, which temporarily withdrew most currency from circulation in an economy where everyday food trade is overwhelmingly cash-based.
>
> I show that the shock did not affect all food markets equally: goods sold through cash-intensive domestic supply chains moved differently from goods distributed through formal banking-linked channels, and within rice, local and imported varieties moved in opposite directions. The broader lesson is that demonetization is not only a monetary intervention; in cash-reliant economies it is also a supply-chain and market-design shock, with first-order distributional consequences.

That is the AER version of the paper. The first sentence should make a claim about the world. The second should introduce the natural experiment. The third should preview the mechanism heterogeneity. Only after that should the author locate the paper relative to India and the demonetization literature.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:**  
The paper argues that abrupt cash withdrawal in a highly informal economy disrupts food markets through two distinct channels—consumer-side transaction costs and intermediary-side supply-chain disruption—and uses within-market commodity variation in Nigeria to separate them.

That is a real contribution. The problem is that the paper does not always present it in the strongest possible form.

### Is it clearly differentiated from the closest papers?
Partially, but not sharply enough. The paper distinguishes itself mostly by:
1. being outside India,
2. focusing on food prices,
3. using commodity-level variation in “cash mediation intensity,” and
4. highlighting the local-versus-imported rice comparison.

Of these, the most publishable differentiation is not “first evidence from Sub-Saharan Africa.” That is nice but not enough for AER. The strongest distinction is the **mechanism decomposition**: demonetization is shown to produce opposite price effects depending on where in the supply chain cash matters. That is the angle that separates this from “another reduced-form demonetization paper.”

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a gap in a literature?
Too much as a literature gap. The intro repeatedly says “first evidence in Sub-Saharan Africa” and “extends the Indian literature.” That is second-tier framing. The stronger framing is a world question:

- When an economy uses cash as transactional infrastructure, what kind of economic shock is demonetization?
- Does it raise prices by making transactions harder, or lower prices by breaking intermediary trade?
- Which goods and households bear the burden?

Those are world questions. The paper has material to answer them, but it keeps slipping back into “there is little evidence outside India.”

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?
A reasonably smart economist would say: “It’s a DiD paper on Nigeria’s demonetization and food prices, comparing local versus imported goods.” That is not enough. They should instead say: “It shows that removing cash is simultaneously a transaction-cost shock and a supply-chain shock, and the sign of the price effect depends on commodity supply-chain structure.” That is much better.

At present, the paper contains that idea, but it is not yet the unmistakable headline.

### What would make this contribution bigger?
Several possibilities:

1. **Make the supply-chain heterogeneity the main object, not a mechanism add-on.**  
   Right now the all-commodity result is presented as the main finding and the rice result as nuance. Strategically, the nuance is the paper. The paper gets much bigger if it becomes a theory-of-incidence paper: when does demonetization raise prices, and when does it depress them?

2. **Use more direct heterogeneity by supply-chain length/intermediation.**  
   If the author can classify goods by short versus long chains, perishability, local farmgate dependence, or number of trading layers, the paper becomes much more than “cash-mediated versus banking-mediated.” It becomes a general statement about the organization of trade.

3. **Elevate incidence and distribution.**  
   The paper gestures at poor households, but that part is still mostly rhetorical. A stronger connection to expenditure shares, local consumption baskets, or producer-versus-consumer exposure would make the stakes feel larger.

4. **Frame the imported/local comparison as exposure to different exchange technologies, not simply origin.**  
   “Imported” versus “local” can sound ad hoc. “Banking-mediated” versus “cash-mediated” sounds economically grounded. The paper should be relentless on that distinction.

5. **Potentially expand beyond prices to market functioning.**  
   If there are data on missing observations, product availability, market attendance, trader activity, or volume proxies, that would enlarge the scope. Prices alone can support a good paper; prices plus market-functioning margins can support a much bigger one.

### Closest neighbors
The nearest conversations appear to be:

2. **Aggarwal et al. / related India agriculture-market papers** on mandi arrivals and agricultural disruption after demonetization.
3. **Lahiri (or adjacent work)** on heterogeneous demonetization effects and financial access.
4. **Aker and related market integration / information frictions papers** on food markets in developing countries.
5. Potentially **Rogoff’s anti-cash/formalization arguments** and the informality/financial inclusion literature.

### How should the paper position itself relative to those neighbors?
Mostly **build on and reframe**, not attack.

- Relative to the India demonetization papers: “They established that cash withdrawal matters; this paper shows why effects differ across goods because cash enters market organization in different ways.”
- Relative to food market integration papers: “Those papers emphasize transport and information frictions; this paper adds the medium of exchange as a market friction.”
- Relative to informality/financial inclusion: “The move from cash to formal payments is not frictionless, and abrupt forced formalization can disrupt exchange.”

The paper should not overclaim by saying the Indian literature could not address mechanism at all; it should say this setting gives unusually transparent within-market comparisons that illuminate mechanism more directly.

### Is the paper positioned too narrowly or too broadly?
A bit of both, oddly.

- **Too narrowly** in its repeated emphasis on “first evidence in Sub-Saharan Africa.”
- **Too broadly** when it gestures at many literatures in quick succession without clearly choosing the primary conversation.

The paper needs one home base. My advice: the primary conversation should be **development/market design/informality**, not “demonetization studies” narrowly construed. Demonetization is the shock; the paper is about how payment infrastructure shapes market outcomes.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?
A few areas it should probably speak to more explicitly:

1. **Money/search and payments economics**, at least lightly.  
   Not a full theory literature review, but some connection to the idea that the medium of exchange affects allocative outcomes would sharpen the economics.

2. **Supply-chain/intermediation literature in development.**  
   The rice result is really about traders and intermediation. There is likely more to say here.

3. **Market microstructure of informal trade / working capital constraints.**  
   The paper is effectively about liquidity constraints in trade networks.

4. **State capacity / policy implementation failures.**  
   The paper is partly about what happens when reform outstrips administrative capacity. That could broaden audience interest.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?
Not fully. The current conversation is “demonetization outside India.” The more impactful conversation is:

> In informal economies, cash is market infrastructure. Removing it changes transaction technology, intermediation, and incidence across households and supply chains.

That is a much better AER conversation.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Before this paper, economists know that demonetization can be disruptive, especially from India, and that many developing-country food markets remain informal and cash-based. But we do not have a clean picture of how cash scarcity maps into prices across different kinds of goods within the same local markets.

### Tension
The core puzzle is that cash scarcity could plausibly push prices either way. It could raise prices by making transactions more costly, or lower prices by breaking the ability of traders to move goods through the chain. If both are true, then average effects conceal a more interesting structure.

### Resolution
The paper finds that, on average, cash-dependent goods become more expensive relative to banking-mediated goods, but some commodities—especially local rice—move the other way relative to close substitutes. The paper interprets this as evidence of two channels operating simultaneously.

### Implications
The implication is that demonetization is not a uniform aggregate demand shock or generic “disruption.” It changes relative prices according to supply-chain cash dependence, meaning its incidence is regressive and commodity-specific. More broadly, the medium of exchange shapes market efficiency.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
It has the pieces, but the arc is not yet disciplined enough. At present it often reads like:

1. Here is demonetization in Nigeria.
2. Here is the empirical design.
3. Here are several results.
4. Here are many robustness checks.
5. Here is a mechanism section.

That is more “well-executed project” than “inescapable story.”

### What story should it be telling?
The paper should tell this story:

1. **Cash is infrastructure in informal food markets.**
2. **Removing infrastructure creates two opposing forces: harder transactions and broken intermediation.**
3. **The sign of the price effect therefore depends on supply-chain structure.**
4. **Nigeria provides a rare setting where within-market comparisons reveal this clearly.**
5. **This changes how we think about demonetization, formalization, and payment-system reform.**

The rice result should not be treated as a side mechanism. It is the dramatic twist in the story. Without it, the paper is a nice applied estimate. With it, the paper becomes conceptually interesting.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?
I would not lead with “8.8 percent.” I would lead with:

> When Nigeria withdrew most cash from circulation, food prices did not all move in the same direction: cash-dependent goods got more expensive on average, but local rice got cheaper relative to imported rice, suggesting that demonetization simultaneously taxed transactions and broke supply chains.

That is the memorable fact.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
Economists would lean in if presented that way. If presented as “a DiD on Nigerian food prices during demonetization,” many would reach for their phones. The paper’s success depends heavily on how it is pitched.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Likely:
- “So is cash scarcity mostly a consumer transaction-cost shock or a trader working-capital shock?”
- “How general is the sign flip beyond rice?”
- “What determines which commodities go up and which go down?”
- “Does this tell us something broader about forced formalization?”

Those are good questions. The paper should anticipate them more directly in the introduction.

### If the findings are modest or fragile, is that still interesting?
Yes, but the paper should not define itself by fragility. Right now it overemphasizes inferential caution in a way that is admirable scientifically but unhelpful editorially. The issue is not to hide caveats; it is to avoid letting the paper’s central identity become “interesting but statistically awkward.” Strategic positioning should be:

- the paper documents a striking pattern,
- offers a mechanism-based interpretation,
- and acknowledges uncertainty in magnitude and precision.

Not:
- here is a result,
- but here are many paragraphs explaining why you should not believe it too strongly.

That is a tonal problem, not a scientific one.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The gap is mostly **framing and ambition**, with some **scope** issues.

This is not primarily a “bad paper with weak positioning.” It is a paper with a potentially strong conceptual point that is currently presented as a careful country episode. That is too safe.

### Is it a framing problem?
Yes, strongly. The paper should not sell itself as:
- first evidence from Nigeria,
- an extension of India,
- a DiD on food prices.

It should sell itself as:
- evidence that the medium of exchange is a production and exchange input in informal markets,
- and that demonetization has heterogeneous incidence because it changes both transaction technology and supply-chain liquidity.

That is the framing shift.

### Is it a scope problem?
Somewhat. To really excite the top people in this area, the heterogeneity story needs to be broader than one local/imported rice comparison. The paper wants to claim a general two-channel mechanism. To make that claim land, it should show more systematic variation across commodity characteristics tied to supply-chain length, perishability, intermediation, or working-capital needs.

### Is it a novelty problem?
Not fatal, but present. Demonization papers exist; food market shock papers exist. “First in Sub-Saharan Africa” is not enough novelty for AER. The novelty must be the mechanism and the re-interpretation of cash as infrastructure.

### Is it an ambition problem?
Yes. The paper is competent but too content to be careful and modest. AER papers usually try to alter how the field conceptualizes something. This paper has an opening to do that, but it is still written like a polished field paper rather than a field-defining one.

### Single most impactful piece of advice
**Reframe the paper around the proposition that cash is market infrastructure, and make heterogeneity in price responses across supply-chain structure—not the average 8.8% effect—the core contribution.**

That one change would improve the introduction, abstract, results ordering, literature positioning, and conclusion all at once.
