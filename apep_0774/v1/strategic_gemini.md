## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
This paper asks whether workers actually respond to new information about workplace hazards by quitting or reducing hours, a fundamental but under-tested assumption of compensating differentials theory. Using mandatory U.S. mine inspections as exogenous information shocks, the author finds that "severe" hazard findings trigger a massive 22.8% decline in mine-level employment over two years, far exceeding any pre-existing trends.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this well, though the first two paragraphs are slightly bogged down in literature citations (Rosen, Viscusi). The "pitch" the paper should lead with is: 
*"Economists assume workers demand higher wages for risk, but this requires workers to accurately perceive and act on risk signals. We show that when a federal inspector publicly labels a mine as dangerous, workers don’t just ask for a raise—they leave. This 'voting with feet' creates a powerful, market-based disciplinary mechanism for workplace safety that operates alongside government fines."*

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**The Contribution:** The paper provides the first large-scale, establishment-level empirical proof that public safety information shocks trigger significant labor supply withdrawals (reallocation) in a high-risk industry.

*   **Differentiation:** It moves beyond the cross-sectional "hedonic wage" literature (which assumes equilibrium) to show the *dynamic* adjustment process. It is more macro-relevant than Mas (2008) because it deals with life-and-death safety rather than just pay disputes.
*   **World vs. Literature:** It starts with a question about the WORLD (Do miners leave dangerous jobs?), which is excellent.
*   **What would make it bigger?** To be a "slam dunk" AER paper, the author needs to bridge the gap to wages. If employment falls but wages *also* don't rise to compensate, it suggests a major market friction. If wages do rise, it confirms the full Rosen (1986) cycle. Without wage data, we only see half the "compensating differential" story.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
The paper sits at the intersection of Labor (Compensating Differentials) and Public/IO (Regulatory Oversight).
*   **Neighbors:** Viscusi (1979) on risk premia; Mas (2008) on labor supply responses to unfairness; Johnson (2005) on OSHA's efficacy.
*   **Strategy:** The paper should **synthesize** the labor supply literature with the "Information Provision as Regulation" literature (e.g., hygiene grades for restaurants). It currently frames itself a bit too much as a narrow "mine safety" paper.
*   **Missing Conversations:** It should speak to the "Job Search/Matching" literature. The 16.7% drop in hours vs. 8.4% drop in headcount suggests workers are "searching while on the job," a key nuance in modern labor theory.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC
*   **Setup:** We assume the labor market prices risk through wages.
*   **Tension:** However, if workers can't see the risk, or can't leave when they do, the market fails. Does information actually empower workers?
*   **Resolution:** Yes. A severe inspection report is a "catalyst" that turns a slow decline into a mass exodus.
*   **Implications:** Transparency is as important as enforcement. Regulators don't just need to fine firms; they need to tell the workers.

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong. The author’s "honest discussion" of pre-trends (page 8) is actually a narrative strength—it frames the inspection as a "coordination device" that crystallizes existing fears.

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
At a dinner party: *"When a coal mine gets a bad safety grade, 1 in 5 workers quits within two years."*
*   **Reaction:** Lean in. It’s a clean, intuitive, and surprisingly large effect.
*   **Follow-up:** "Do they go to better mines, or just leave the industry?" The paper can’t answer this yet (limitation), but the fact that the question is asked proves the result is interesting.

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
*   **Front-load the Dose-Response:** Table 4 (Dose-Response) is more intellectually satisfying than the binary "Severe/Clean" split. Move the dose-response graph/table earlier.
*   **Appendix:** The discussion on "Small vs. Large Mines" is vital to kill the "mechanical percentage change" critique. It should stay in the main text.
*   **Conclusion:** The policy section is a bit generic. It should more aggressively argue for "Information as Regulation" as a low-cost alternative to traditional fines.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The paper is currently a very high-quality *Applied Micro* paper (think *AEJ: Applied*). To make it **AER**, it needs more **Ambition regarding the Mechanism.**

**The Gap:** Why does it take 8 quarters for the full effect to manifest? Is it a search friction? Is it a "learning-by-doing" process where they see if the mine improves? 

**Single Biggest Advice:** Explore the "Reallocation" more deeply. If the author can show that workers from "Severe" mines move to "Clean" mines (even using regional aggregates or MSHA's own history of where miners go), the paper becomes a landmark study on how information improves labor market sorting/efficiency.
