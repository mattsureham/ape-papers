**Idea Fidelity**  
The manuscript broadly pursues the original idea manifest: it studies the CCDI anti-corruption campaign using the Wang (2020) dataset merged with prefecture/city fiscal data to evaluate changes in expenditure composition. The focus shifts somewhat from the manifest’s promised education vs. infrastructure test to a discovery of a “compliance shift” toward science and technology spending, but this is a defensible evolution of the research question. That said, the paper omits several key elements of the manifest’s identification strategy: the staggered DiD design (beyond a brief description) is not fully deployed in the empirical section, and the proposed instrument (inspection round assignment × provincial GDP rank) is absent. The manifest’s emphasis on causal inference via heterogenous timing is not fully realized, which weakens the link between the intended contribution and the executed analysis.

**Summary**  
The paper investigates whether Xi Jinping’s anti-corruption enforcement altered the functional composition of prefectural fiscal spending in China using a continuous-treatment DiD based on the number of CCDI investigations. It finds no reduction in extractable infrastructure spending or increases in education shares; instead, more intensely inspected cities reallocate toward science and technology expenditure—what the authors label a “compliance shift”—while total fiscal outlays rise. The result challenges the classic Mauro (1998) prediction that corruption distorts spending away from human-capital categories.

**Essential Points**  
1. **Endogeneity of Continuous-Intensity Treatment.** The core identifying variation—the log count of investigations—likely reflects unobserved corruption severity, political salience, or fiscal ambitions that also drive spending choices. The manuscript relies on city and year fixed effects plus placebo tests, but these do not fully address the concern that the CCDI may have focused on fiscally ambitious or centrally prioritized cities ex ante. Without a credible source of exogenous variation (e.g., inspection round scheduling, administrative quotas, or instrumental variables), the estimated “compliance shift” may capture pre-existing heterogeneity rather than a causal effect of enforcement. Please either implement the instrument discussed in the manifest or provide stronger quasi-experimental evidence (e.g., exploiting variation in CCDI inspection timing or random batch assignments) to support causal claims.

2. **Treatment Timing and Dynamic Controls.** Nearly all cities receive investigations almost simultaneously (216 in 2013), so the post-period indicator interacts with intensity but lacks temporal leverage to distinguish immediate enforcement shocks from broader fiscal trends after 2013 (political priorities, central transfers, economic slowdown). The event study presented treats intensity as interacting with year dummies, but the pre-treatment coefficients (some marginally significant) and the lack of a clear “untreated” counterfactual undermine confidence in parallel trends. The Callaway-Sant’Anna results introduce problematic pre-trend tests. The paper should clarify how variation in treatment timing (2013 vs. later cohorts) identifies the effect, and it should consider more flexible dynamics (e.g., city-specific linear trends, matched controls, or explicitly modeled inspection arrival times) to disentangle enforcement from contemporaneous shocks.

3. **Interpretation of Science Spending Increase.** The compliance-shift interpretation is intriguing, but the paper does not convincingly rule out alternative mechanisms. Science & technology expenditure may rise simply because it is flexible, discretionary, and politically attractive, not because it is auditable. Without complementary evidence—e.g., showing that science spending becomes more auditable, that transfers for science are tied to central directives, or that procurement formats change—the policy implication remains speculative. The authors should augment their mechanism section, perhaps by examining the composition of science spending (capital vs. personnel), its ties to central Five-Year Plan directives, or whether similar patterns emerge in other centrally prioritized categories.

**Suggestions**  
- **Implement staggered DiD or instrument.** Given the manifest’s emphasis on staggered enforcement and the availability of inspection round data, the authors should more fully exploit this design. For example, construct treatment cohorts based on the actual CCDI inspection waves (2013–2017) and estimate cohort-specific ATT using Callaway-Sant’Anna or Sun and Abraham. Alternatively, operationalize the proposed instrument—inspection round assignment interacted with provincial GDP rank—to isolate exogenous variation in enforcement intensity. Demonstrating that results are robust to these approaches would substantially strengthen the causal claim.

- **Address anticipatory or confounding fiscal policies.** The central government significantly changed fiscal policy during 2007–2016 (stimulus, structural reform). Can the authors control for central transfers, infrastructure subsidies, or provincial-level directives? Including province-by-year trends, controls for major policy shifts, or examining whether the implementation of science projects correlates with central grants would help isolate the effect of enforcement. If such data are unavailable, a placebo test using non-fiscal outcomes (e.g., agricultural subsidies) would add credibility.

- **Enrich the mechanism analysis.** To support the compliance shift narrative, consider (i) analyzing whether science spending increases in categories that are demonstrably auditable (e.g., R&D grants, science parks) versus ambiguous line items; (ii) showing whether audited cities experience greater reporting transparency or external monitoring post-enforcement; and (iii) checking whether the effect on science spending is stronger in provinces with stronger central science priorities (Five-Year Plan alignment). A short qualitative example or descriptive evidence on how science spending is evaluated centrally would help.

- **Reassess the role of infrastructure spending.** The paper reports an increase in fixed asset investment as a share of GDP, but the interpretation of FAI as a proxy for extractable infrastructure deserves scrutiny. Fixed asset investment may capture broader investment categories, including public housing or environmental projects. If possible, break down FAI into municipal infrastructure vs. other components, or use procurement data if available. Alternatively, compare the effect on project-level approvals versus budgeted amounts to see whether actual extraction opportunities change.

- **Clarify sample selection and treatment variation.** The near-universal treatment and narrow variation in treatment timing raise questions about generalizability. Please report the distribution of investigations (e.g., by ranking, department, pre-2013 corruption indices) to assess whether higher-intensity cities differ systematically. Consider weighting the regression by city population or GDP to ensure results are not driven by large municipalities. A supplementary table showing descriptive differences between high- and low-intensity cities would be helpful.

- **Expand robustness in appendix.** The standardized effect sizes table is useful, but the paper should also provide placebo outcomes (e.g., weather spending shares) and alternative standard error clusters (province-level). Additionally, sensitivity to functional form (e.g., linear vs. log intensity) would reassure readers that the results are not artefacts of log specification.

- **Temporally extend the analysis.** The dataset extends to 2016, but the fiscal data seem to go to 2016 as well; if more recent data are available, extending the post-treatment window would reveal whether the compliance shift persists or reverses. Even if only through 2016, a panel through 2019 (if obtainable) would capture longer-run adjustments.

Overall, the topic is important and the data are rich, but the paper needs a tighter causal strategy and a deeper engagement with mechanism to make a convincing contribution to the literatures on corruption and fiscal policy.
