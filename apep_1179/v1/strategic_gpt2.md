## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple, important question: when corruption is punished, do governments stop favoring “kickback-friendly” spending categories like infrastructure and shift toward socially valuable spending like education and health? Using variation in the intensity of China’s 2012 anti-corruption campaign across prefecture-level cities, the paper argues that the answer is not the classic one from Mauro: enforcement did not reduce infrastructure-oriented spending, but was instead associated with more spending on science and technology, which the authors interpret as a shift toward categories that signal compliance with central priorities.

Why should a busy economist care? Because the paper takes a famous claim in political economy and public finance—that corruption distorts the composition of public spending—and tries to test its reversal on the enforcement margin, in a setting where anti-corruption was massive, consequential, and politically salient.

### Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Mostly, but not quite. The opening sets up Mauro clearly, and the second paragraph introduces the China setting. But the current intro still reads like a paper whose main objective is “test Mauro in China,” when the more interesting hook is broader: anti-corruption may change *how local governments perform compliance* rather than simply reduce distortions. Right now the “compliance shift” appears as a twist after the null on education/infrastructure. For AER positioning, that twist should be the paper’s central idea from the outset, not a salvage operation after the canonical prediction fails.

### The pitch the paper should have

A stronger first two paragraphs would say something like:

> A central claim in political economy is that corruption distorts not only the level of public spending but its composition: corrupt officials favor categories such as infrastructure where rents are easier to extract, and underprovide harder-to-steal-from goods such as education and health. If that view is right, then anti-corruption enforcement should reallocate budgets away from extractable investment and toward human capital.
>
> We test that prediction using China’s nationwide anti-corruption campaign, one of the largest enforcement shocks in modern government. We show that stronger enforcement did not shift local spending away from infrastructure-related categories or toward education. Instead, it increased the budget share devoted to science and technology. The implication is that anti-corruption enforcement may not simply “clean up” public finance; in centralized states, it may redirect local spending toward categories that visibly align with central priorities—a pattern we call a compliance shift.

That is much sharper. It starts with a big world question, brings in China as the test bed, and makes the non-Mauro result the headline, not the backup plan.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper’s contribution is to show that in China’s anti-corruption campaign, stronger enforcement was associated not with the predicted shift from extractable spending toward education/health, but with a reallocation toward science and technology spending, suggesting that enforcement changes the political signaling value of spending categories.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest papers?

Partially, but not enough. The paper differentiates itself from:
1. Mauro-style cross-country corruption-and-composition papers,
2. the China anti-corruption literature focused on firms, consumption, or bureaucratic behavior,
3. broader theories of extractability.

That is the right neighborhood. But the differentiation still feels defensive and method-based: “first city-level causal test,” “within-country quasi-experimental design,” etc. That is not what readers will remember. They will remember one of two things:
- either “anti-corruption didn’t reduce infrastructure; it increased science spending,” or
- “this is another China anti-corruption reduced-form paper.”

Right now the introduction does not do enough to ensure they remember the first.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a gap in a literature?

It starts with a world question, which is good. But it drifts too often into gap-filling language: “first city-level causal test,” “no prior study examines the fiscal composition channel.” Those lines are useful but secondary. The stronger framing is:

- What does anti-corruption *actually do* to the state?
- Does it make spending less corruptible, or just more politically legible to superiors?

That is a real world question.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the intro?

Not yet with confidence. A smart economist could probably say: “It’s a DiD paper on China’s anti-corruption campaign and local budgets; they don’t find the standard education/infrastructure shift.” That is not enough. They should instead say: “It shows anti-corruption changes spending composition in a way that reflects bureaucratic compliance with central priorities, not just less corruption. That’s the twist.”

The paper has that insight, but it is not yet the dominant impression.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Several possibilities:

1. **A better direct test of the core theory.**  
   The paper’s main theoretical opponent is the extractability hypothesis, yet the infrastructure measure is fixed asset investment over GDP, which is only loosely tied to public procurement. Strategically, this makes the paper look as if its core test is indirect. The contribution would become much bigger if it had a direct government spending category for transport/construction/capital projects.

2. **A clearer central-priority mechanism.**  
   If “science and technology” is the key finding, the paper needs to show more convincingly that this is a politically prioritized, compliance-signaling category. That could mean tying the pattern to Five-Year Plan targets, transfers, provincial promotion incentives, or textual evidence from policy directives. Right now the mechanism is plausible but thin.

3. **A more general framing beyond China.**  
   The paper could become more important if it made clear this is not “weird China.” The broader claim is: in hierarchical states, anti-corruption campaigns can reallocate spending toward categories that are easy to justify upward, not necessarily toward categories that maximize welfare. That would connect to state capacity, multitask bureaucracy, and performance signaling.

4. **Sharper welfare stakes.**  
   “Science and technology” is not obviously better or worse than infrastructure in welfare terms. The paper needs to clarify whether this is a socially productive reallocation, a cosmetic one, or an ambiguous one. Without that, the result risks sounding like a classification exercise.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

### Closest neighbors

The obvious neighbors are:

1. **Mauro (1998)** on corruption and the composition of government expenditure.
2. **Delavallade (2006)** on corruption and public spending composition.
3. **Shleifer and Vishny (1993)** for the underlying theory of corruption and “extractable” projects.
4. Papers on **China’s anti-corruption campaign**, such as work on firms, innovation, luxury consumption, and bureaucratic behavior—some of which the paper cites (e.g. Xu; Giannetti et al.; Lin; Li).
5. Potentially the broader political-economy literature on **top-down discipline, bureaucratic incentives, and compliance in authoritarian systems**.

### How should the paper position itself?

It should **build on Mauro and qualify him**, not “attack” him in a narrow empirical sense. Mauro is too important and too broad a target for a single-country design to “overturn.” The strongest position is:

- Mauro identifies a broad cross-sectional pattern.
- This paper studies a different margin: what happens when corruption is suddenly policed within a centralized political system.
- On that margin, the expected reversal does not occur; instead, spending shifts toward categories that are politically legible to the center.

That is a refinement, not a takedown. That framing is more credible and more useful.

### Is the paper positioned too narrowly or too broadly?

At the moment, it is oddly both:
- **Too narrow** in empirical conversation: “China anti-corruption campaign + city fiscal shares.”
- **Too broad** in rhetorical ambition: “complicating Mauro” and making claims about anti-corruption generally.

The right scale is: a China paper with implications for a broad theory of how enforcement interacts with bureaucratic incentives in centralized states.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?

The missing conversation is not just corruption. It is also:

- **State capacity / bureaucratic incentives / multitask principal-agent models**
- **Authoritarian accountability and political signaling**
- **Target-based governance in China**
- **Public finance composition under political incentives**
- Potentially **fiscal federalism in centralized systems**

This paper’s most interesting idea is not “corruption affects budgets.” We knew that. It is “discipline changes what subnational governments choose to make visible.” That belongs in a broader state-capacity and political-control conversation.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?

Not fully. It is currently having a “does Mauro survive this test?” conversation. That is useful, but not the highest-value conversation. The more impactful framing would connect the paper to:

- how anti-corruption campaigns reshape bureaucratic behavior,
- how local officials respond to central scrutiny,
- and how enforcement changes the composition of policy effort.

That is a more interesting and less crowded conversation.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

The world before this paper: economists believe corruption can distort public spending toward categories that are easier to steal from, especially infrastructure, and away from human capital. If enforcement works, we might expect the composition of spending to improve.

### Tension

The puzzle is whether anti-corruption enforcement actually undoes those distortions in practice, especially in a centralized authoritarian setting where local officials answer upward to the center. The implicit tension is between two models:
1. enforcement reduces rent-seeking and improves allocation;
2. enforcement changes political incentives and encourages visible compliance.

### Resolution

The paper finds little support for the classic reallocation story toward education and away from infrastructure. Instead, stronger enforcement is associated with increased science and technology spending, which the authors interpret as spending that better signals alignment with central priorities.

### Implications

The implication is that anti-corruption may alter the *politics* of budgeting more than the *economics* of extractability. Enforcement may produce compositional changes, but not the ones predicted by the canonical corruption-distortion view.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the pieces of one, but the arc is still somewhat underdeveloped. Right now it reads as:

- test Mauro,
- null on education,
- interesting significant result on science,
- propose “compliance shift.”

That is a results sequence, not yet a fully integrated story. The “compliance shift” comes off as an ex post label for the one category that moved.

### What story should it be telling?

The paper should tell a two-model story from the beginning:

- **Model 1: Extractability.** Corrupt officials favor stealable categories; enforcement should push budgets toward cleaner categories.
- **Model 2: Compliance signaling.** Officials under scrutiny reallocate toward categories that are easy to justify to superiors and aligned with central priorities.

Then the empirical contribution is not just “Mauro null, science positive.” It is: “the data line up better with compliance signaling than with simple de-corruption.”

That story gives the paper conceptual coherence.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?

I would say: “China’s anti-corruption campaign did not shift local budgets away from infrastructure and toward education, as the standard corruption story would suggest; instead, places hit harder by enforcement increased the share of spending on science and technology.”

That is the dinner-party fact.

### Would people lean in?

Some would lean in, but only if the speaker immediately explains why “science and technology” matters. On its own, that category is not self-evidently exciting. If framed as “anti-corruption changes budget allocations toward politically approved categories rather than less-corruptible ones,” then yes, people lean in. If framed as “we found a 0.16 percentage point increase in science share,” they reach for their phones.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Probably one of these:
1. “Why science and technology specifically?”
2. “Is that really compliance, or just a contemporaneous industrial policy push?”
3. “Is the core result that anti-corruption doesn’t improve allocation, or just that your proxies are noisy?”
4. “Is this China-specific or general to hierarchical states?”

Those are actually healthy follow-up questions. They indicate there is a potentially interesting paper here. But they also reveal what the paper must answer better to feel AER-level.

### If findings are null or modest, is the null itself interesting?

Yes—the null is potentially very interesting because Mauro’s composition hypothesis is influential. Learning that anti-corruption does *not* reverse the classic pattern on the enforcement margin is informative. But the paper does not yet fully earn that null, strategically speaking, because the direct measure of infrastructure is imperfect and health is missing. So the null is interesting in principle, but the current version does not make it as decisive as it needs to be.

The positive science result is doing a lot of the paper’s work. If that is the case, then the paper must make the science result conceptually richer.
