## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple and important question: when a major anti-corruption crackdown hits local governments, do public budgets move away from rent-rich infrastructure and toward socially productive spending like education and health? Using variation in the intensity of China’s post-2012 anti-corruption campaign across prefecture-level cities, the paper argues that the answer is not the canonical one from the corruption literature: instead of reducing infrastructure-oriented spending, enforcement is associated with more spending on science and technology, which the authors interpret as a shift toward categories that signal compliance with central priorities.

A busy economist should care because this is, in principle, a direct test of one of the most durable claims in political economy and public finance: corruption changes not just how much governments waste, but what they buy. If that claim fails in one of the world’s biggest anti-corruption episodes, that is potentially interesting.

### Does the paper articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Not quite. The opening is competent, but it is still framed like a standard “we test Mauro in China” paper. The stronger pitch is not “here is another setting in which to revisit corruption and budget composition,” but rather: **anti-corruption enforcement may not produce the expected reallocation toward classic social sectors; instead it may induce local governments to substitute toward politically legible, center-approved categories.** That is a broader and more surprising world question.

Right now the paper reaches its most distinctive idea — the “compliance shift” — only after first marching through a more conventional hypothesis test. That undersells the paper. If the science-and-technology result is the distinctive fact, it should be on page 1, paragraph 1.

### The pitch the paper should have

Here is what the first two paragraphs should effectively say:

> A central idea in political economy is that corruption distorts public budgets toward activities that are easy to monetize — especially construction and procurement — and away from harder-to-steal-from sectors like education and health. That logic implies that anti-corruption crackdowns should improve the composition of public spending, not just reduce theft. Yet in centralized states, enforcement may discipline local officials in a different way: it may push them to spend more on categories that are politically visible and aligned with central priorities, even if those are not the sectors emphasized by classic corruption theories.
>
> We study this question in the context of China’s 2012 anti-corruption campaign, one of the largest enforcement episodes in modern history. Using cross-city variation in enforcement intensity, we ask whether crackdowns moved local budgets away from extractable investment and toward human capital. They did not. Instead, more heavily exposed cities shifted spending toward science and technology, with little evidence of a move toward education and no decline in investment-oriented spending. The broader implication is that anti-corruption enforcement may reshape public budgets through political compliance rather than through the textbook channel of reducing extractive spending.

That is the paper’s real hook.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper argues that China’s anti-corruption campaign did not reallocate local public spending away from extractable investment and toward education, but instead shifted spending toward science and technology, suggesting that enforcement induces political compliance in budgeting rather than the classic Mauro-style composition change.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest literature?

Only partially.

The paper does a decent job saying it is the “first city-level causal test” of Mauro’s composition hypothesis using within-country enforcement variation. But that is still a bit too procedural. “First city-level test” is not, by itself, a memorable contribution unless readers already care intensely about Mauro (1998). The paper needs to differentiate itself along a more substantive dimension:

- not just “better design than Mauro,” but
- “anti-corruption enforcement changes budget composition through obedience to the center, not by unwinding the standard extractability distortion.”

That would distinguish it from:
1. cross-country corruption-and-spending papers,
2. China anti-corruption papers focused on firms/consumption/bureaucratic effort,
3. generic public finance papers on expenditure composition.

At present, a smart economist might still summarize this as: “It’s a DiD on China anti-corruption and local spending shares; they mostly don’t find Mauro and instead see some science spending increase.” That is not yet sharp enough.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or filling a gap in a LITERATURE?

Too much as a literature gap, not enough as a world question.

The stronger world question is: **When governments crack down on corruption, what kind of state capacity do they actually produce — cleaner allocation, or more politically compliant allocation?** That is a big question. Right now the intro leans too heavily on “we provide the first test of Mauro in this context” and “no prior study examines fiscal composition in China’s anti-corruption campaign.” Those are respectable claims, but not AER-level framing unless attached to a large conceptual payoff.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new?

They could, but not crisply enough. Right now they would likely say something like:

> “They use China’s anti-corruption campaign to test whether enforcement changes local spending composition. It doesn’t really move education, but science spending goes up.”

That is not bad, but it still sounds like “another DiD paper about X.” The phrase “compliance shift” is an attempt to solve this, but the concept is not yet anchored strongly enough to broader theory.

### What would make the contribution bigger?

Several possibilities, in descending order of strategic value:

1. **Make the conceptual contribution bigger, not just the result set.**  
   Build a more explicit contrast between two models:
   - anti-corruption as reducing extractive distortions;
   - anti-corruption as increasing bureaucratic compliance with central priorities.  
   If the paper can frame itself as adjudicating between these two views of state discipline, it becomes much more ambitious.

2. **Show the reallocations map onto centrally prioritized categories more systematically.**  
   Right now “science and technology” does too much work. One category is a thin reed for a new concept. If there are other categories that visibly align with central plans, innovation drives, industrial upgrading, environmental goals, or similarly auditable/cadre-evaluable sectors, that would make the “compliance shift” feel real rather than post hoc.

3. **Tie the result to political incentives, not just budget shares.**  
   For example, does the shift appear more strongly where cadre evaluation or central oversight is stronger? Does it line up with Five-Year Plan priorities in a measurable way? Again, not as an identification issue, but as a narrative issue: make the mechanism legible.

4. **Use a cleaner budget outcome than fixed asset investment/GDP if possible.**  
   Even though you asked me not to referee methods, from a positioning standpoint the current “infrastructure” proxy is weak enough that it blunts the paper’s central confrontation with Mauro. The paper knows this and admits it. But strategically, if the main theoretical contribution is “no movement away from extractable spending,” you need an outcome that readers will accept as actually capturing public investment composition.

5. **Health matters.**  
   Mauro’s prediction is education and health versus infrastructure. Missing health is a real hole in the strategic scope of the paper. If there is any way to bring in health-related expenditure or service outcomes, the paper becomes much more complete.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

### Closest neighbors

The nearest conversations seem to be:

1. **Mauro (1998)** on corruption and the composition of government expenditure.
2. **Delavallade (2006)** on corruption and public spending composition.
3. **Tanzi and Davoodi (1997/1998)** on corruption, public investment, and expenditure distortions.
4. China anti-corruption papers such as:
   - **Lin et al.** on luxury consumption / anti-extravagance,
   - **Xu and Yao / Xu et al.** on firm productivity or business consequences of anti-corruption,
   - **Giannetti et al.** on innovation,
   - papers on bureaucratic slack / investment slowdown under anti-corruption.
5. Broader political economy work on **top-down monitoring, cadre incentives, and local compliance in China**, e.g. work by **Landry**, **Li and Zhou**, **Chen, Kung, and coauthors**, depending on the exact angle.

There is also a less obvious but potentially richer neighbor set:

6. Literature on **state capacity and bureaucratic incentives in authoritarian systems**.
7. Literature on **performance management / target-driven governance / multitask bureaucracy**.
8. Literature on **political centralization and local fiscal behavior**.

### How should the paper position itself relative to those neighbors?

- **Build on Mauro, don’t merely “test” him.**  
  The paper should say: Mauro’s logic is about extractability under decentralized corruption, but enforcement in a centralized political hierarchy may operate through a different margin — compliant reallocation toward politically rewarded sectors. That is a conceptual extension, not just a replication attempt.

- **Synthesize anti-corruption and authoritarian governance literatures.**  
  This is where the paper can be most interesting. It should connect corruption enforcement to cadre management and political signaling, not just to public-finance composition.

- **Do not “attack” the China anti-corruption literature.**  
  Instead say those papers focus on firms, consumption, or bureaucratic behavior; this paper asks what governments themselves buy after a crackdown. That is complementary.

### Is the paper currently positioned too narrowly or too broadly?

Slightly too narrowly in one sense, slightly too broadly in another.

- **Too narrowly** because it presents itself as a test of a specific hypothesis from one classic paper plus a contribution to the China anti-corruption literature. That makes the audience feel niche.
- **Too broadly** when it gestures toward general anti-corruption policy design “in any centralized system” without fully building that bridge.

The sweet spot is: **a paper about how anti-corruption enforcement shapes state behavior in hierarchical political systems, using China as the canonical case.**

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?

It seems under-connected to:
- the literature on **authoritarian monitoring and central-local relations**;
- the literature on **target-based governance and political signaling**;
- the literature on **multitask bureaucrats**, where stronger monitoring on one dimension can shift effort/composition toward visible metrics;
- potentially the literature on **state capacity versus distortionary compliance**.

The current “compliance shift” concept would be much stronger if embedded in those literatures. As written, it risks sounding like a label attached to one positive coefficient.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?

Not yet. It is having a decent conversation, but not the best one.

The highest-impact conversation is not “does China confirm Mauro?” It is:

> “What does anti-corruption enforcement actually do to the state? Does it produce cleaner government, or more obedient government?”

That is a much better conversation, and the current results fit it better than they fit the narrower Mauro-replication frame.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Before this paper, the standard intuition is that corruption distorts public spending toward sectors where officials can extract rents, especially infrastructure and procurement, and away from human capital sectors like education and health. A major anti-corruption crackdown should therefore improve expenditure composition.

### Tension

But that prediction may fail in a centralized regime like China, where local officials are not just maximizing illicit rents; they are also responding to signals from above. Anti-corruption may therefore alter behavior not by making budgets less extractable, but by making them more legible to superiors.

### Resolution

The paper finds no meaningful shift toward education and no reduction in investment-oriented spending; instead, more heavily exposed cities increase the budget share of science and technology spending.

### Implications

The implication is that anti-corruption campaigns may reorganize local government priorities through political compliance rather than through straightforward de-renting. That matters for how economists think about corruption, state capacity, and the policy consequences of top-down enforcement.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the skeleton of one, but not a fully convincing arc yet.

At present, the paper still reads somewhat like a sequence of empirical outputs:
- main null on education,
- positive result on total spending,
- positive result on science share,
- weak infrastructure proxy,
- then a discussion section that proposes “compliance shift.”

That is a **results-first, story-later** structure. For AER ambitions, the story needs to organize the empirical material from the outset.

### What story should it be telling?

The paper should be telling this story:

1. **Classic view:** corruption distorts public budgets toward extractable projects.
2. **Alternative view:** in centralized systems, enforcement changes political incentives more than economic opportunities.
3. **Test case:** China’s anti-corruption campaign is large enough to tell these stories apart.
4. **Finding:** enforcement does not produce the textbook reallocations; instead it induces a move toward centrally valorized categories.
5. **Implication:** anti-corruption can strengthen hierarchical compliance without necessarily improving allocative efficiency in the way standard models predict.

That is a coherent arc. The current draft comes close, but the argument needs to be made earlier and more explicitly.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?

I would lead with:

> “One of the biggest anti-corruption crackdowns in modern history did not shift local public spending away from infrastructure and toward education; it shifted spending toward science and technology instead.”

That is the cleanest and most surprising fact in the paper.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

Some would lean in — but only if presented properly.

If you pitch it as “we test Mauro (1998) in China,” many economists will assume they know the punchline. If you pitch it as “anti-corruption may create compliance states rather than clean states,” that is much more attention-grabbing.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Immediately:

> “Why science and technology? Is that really anti-corruption, or just industrial policy / Five-Year Plan spending happening at the same time?”

That is the key strategic vulnerability. The paper sees this and gestures toward central priorities, but that question is exactly what readers will ask. The paper needs to embrace that question and frame it as part of the contribution rather than treating it as an annoying alternative interpretation.

### If findings are null or modest, is the null interesting?

Yes — the null on education is potentially very interesting, because it cuts against a canonical corruption narrative. But the paper must make the case that this is **not** “we failed to find the expected effect.” It is:

- a rejection of a highly cited prediction,
- in a large, salient setting,
- with a positive alternative pattern that suggests a different model of bureaucratic response.

If the paper leans too hard on “suggestive findings” and caveats, the null starts to feel like a failed experiment. It needs more confidence in the substantive message.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

At present, the gap is mostly **framing plus ambition**, with some **scope** concerns.

### Is it a framing problem?

Yes, significantly.

The paper has a potentially interesting fact, but it is framed as a fairly modest test of a classic hypothesis in one country. The AER version is not “China revisits Mauro”; it is “anti-corruption enforcement changes the political logic of local budgeting.”

### Is it a scope problem?

Also yes.

The paper currently leans heavily on one standout outcome — science and technology spending — and one weak proxy for infrastructure. That is not enough breadth for the conceptual claim it wants to make. To sustain “compliance shift,” the paper needs a broader map of where spending goes and why those categories are politically meaningful.

### Is it a novelty problem?

Moderately.

The anti-corruption-in-China design itself is not novel enough anymore. The novelty has to come from the conceptual reframing and from budget composition as a window into how states respond to discipline. Without that, it risks feeling like a competent application.

### Is it an ambition problem?

Yes.

The paper is careful, competent, and somewhat conservative. A top journal paper here would be more willing to make — and then substantiate — a larger claim about the nature of enforcement in centralized states.

### Single most impactful piece of advice

**Reframe the paper around a broader conceptual claim — that anti-corruption enforcement in hierarchical states may produce politically compliant budgeting rather than the textbook de-renting of public expenditure — and then organize the evidence around that claim rather than around a narrow test of Mauro (1998).**

That is the one change that would most improve its prospects.
