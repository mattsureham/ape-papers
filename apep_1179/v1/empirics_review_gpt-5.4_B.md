## 1. Idea Fidelity

The paper pursues the core question in the manifest quite closely: whether China’s post-2012 anti-corruption campaign changed the composition of local public spending, using the Wang (2020) investigations data and city-level fiscal outcomes. It also engages directly with the Mauro-style hypothesis that enforcement should shift spending away from more extractable categories and toward education/health-like categories.

That said, the paper departs from the original design in several important ways. First, it largely abandons the staggered-timing design emphasized in the manifest, because treatment timing is nearly universal in 2013; instead it uses a post-2013 indicator interacted with total 2013–2016 investigations. Second, it substitutes the China City Statistical Yearbook and fixed asset investment/GDP for the more policy-relevant functional expenditure categories in the Finance Statistics data proposed in the manifest. This is a consequential deviation: fixed asset investment is not a government budget category and is only an indirect proxy for infrastructure spending. Third, the manifest’s strongest identification idea—first investigation timing with modern staggered DiD—does not survive in the implemented paper, and the replacement strategy relies on intensity variation that is plausibly endogenous to underlying corruption, development, and fiscal trajectories.

## 2. Summary

This paper studies whether anti-corruption enforcement during Xi’s CCDI campaign altered the composition of prefecture-level public spending in China. Using variation in the number of investigated officials across cities, the paper finds no meaningful shift toward education spending and no reduction in infrastructure-related activity; instead, it reports a modest increase in science and technology expenditure shares and interprets this as a “compliance shift” toward centrally favored, more auditable spending categories.

## 3. Essential Points

1. **The treatment variable is post-treatment and likely endogenous, which undermines the causal interpretation.**  
   The main regressor is total investigations in 2013–2016 interacted with a post-2013 dummy. But total investigations over 2013–2016 are themselves an outcome of the campaign and may reflect local corruption intensity, political targeting, administrative capacity, or contemporaneous shocks that also affect spending. This is not just a generic endogeneity concern: the paper is effectively using realized campaign intensity measured over the post period to explain post-period fiscal outcomes. The placebo with pre-2013 investigations is not sufficient to address this. Without a credible source of quasi-random variation in enforcement intensity or timing, the estimates should be presented as descriptive correlations rather than causal effects.

2. **The outcome data do not adequately test the headline mechanism.**  
   The paper’s central claim is about reallocation away from extractable infrastructure and toward less corruptible social spending, but the implemented outcomes do not map cleanly to that claim. Education share is directly observed, but health expenditure is absent; science and technology is introduced as the main positive finding even though it is not central to the motivating theory. More seriously, “fixed asset investment/GDP” is not local government infrastructure expenditure and includes broad economy-wide investment, so it is not an appropriate counterpart to fiscal composition measures. As a result, the conclusion that enforcement does not reduce infrastructure spending is not supported by the evidence currently shown.

3. **The evidence for identification is internally inconsistent and too weak for the paper’s claims.**  
   The event-study pre-trends for science share show significant pre-period coefficients, and the Callaway-Sant’Anna exercise reportedly fails the parallel trends pre-test. The manuscript then dismisses these failures rather than confronting them. Combined with near-universal treatment and limited untreated comparison units, the design does not provide a convincing counterfactual. In AER: Insights format, the paper needs a much cleaner empirical design or a much more modest interpretation.

## 4. Suggestions

The paper asks a good question and has the seed of a potentially interesting contribution, but it needs substantial redesign before it can support the causal claims now made. My suggestions below are intended to help the authors either rescue the identification or reposition the paper more credibly.

**1. Rebuild the empirical design around treatment timing or externally assigned inspection exposure, not realized total investigations.**  
The current continuous-treatment DiD is the paper’s central weakness. A more credible design would exploit variation tied to CCDI inspection scheduling at the province level or other pre-determined features of campaign rollout. If the authors can map prefectures to exogenous or plausibly exogenous inspection rounds, they could estimate effects from the onset of provincial inspection exposure rather than the ex post count of investigated officials. Even if timing variation is limited, the paper would be stronger using “inspection arrival” as treatment than using cumulative investigations measured over the entire post period. If the authors retain intensity, they need a persuasive first-stage story: why would some places receive more investigations for reasons orthogonal to spending trends? At minimum, they should explore whether enforcement intensity is predicted by pre-2012 city characteristics such as GDP, industrial structure, fiscal size, province capital status, SOE concentration, prior infrastructure intensity, and corruption history. If it is, the identifying assumption becomes quite implausible.

**2. Use the correct fiscal outcome data, even if this requires a narrower sample or more laborious data work.**  
The paper would be much stronger if it returned to the Finance Statistics source from the original idea and used actual local government expenditures by function: education, health, social security, transport, urban maintenance, agriculture, capital construction, etc. That would allow a direct test of the composition hypothesis. Right now, the “infrastructure” result is based on fixed asset investment/GDP, which is too far from the object of interest. If health spending truly cannot be recovered at the city level in the current data, the paper should not frame itself as a broad test of Mauro’s prediction. More generally, the data section should clarify whether the city fiscal variables are comparable across years and cities, how off-budget spending is treated, and whether changes in accounting conventions around the Budget Law or other reforms affect interpretation.

**3. Reframe the “science share” result much more cautiously unless it is shown to be substantively and theoretically meaningful.**  
The main positive finding is statistically precise, but economically small, and it appears somewhat discovered after the main prediction failed. The paper should be careful not to overstate this as a new mechanism. Why should science and technology spending be interpreted as especially auditable rather than simply politically favored? Does it rise because of central subsidy programs unrelated to anti-corruption? Is the increase concentrated in certain years, provinces, or city types? A useful next step would be to show the full budget reallocation across all observable functional categories and test whether science rises at the expense of some specific margin. Without that, the “compliance shift” interpretation feels speculative.

**4. Address the near-universal-treatment setting more transparently.**  
The paper correctly notes that binary treatment is uninformative because almost all cities are treated, but this has broader implications. With 253 of 258 cities treated and most first treated in 2013, the research design is effectively comparing more- and less-exposed places after a national shock. That is a much weaker design than a standard DiD with clean treated and untreated units. The paper should say this plainly and scale back claims. It would also help to show the distribution of treatment intensity, whether a few large cities drive the variation, and whether results survive winsorizing or excluding the top decile of investigations.

**5. Improve the event-study analysis and take pre-trend failures seriously.**  
For the science-share result, two pre-period coefficients are significant, which is not trivial given the small number of pre-period years. The paper should report a joint test of pre-trends, not just individual coefficients. It should also show the event-study graphically with confidence intervals, because tables can obscure pattern recognition. If pre-trends remain problematic, the authors should consider outcome-specific detrending, matched comparisons, or more flexible controls for pre-period characteristics interacted with time. But if the science-share result only emerges under specifications with visible pre-trend concerns, the proper conclusion is that the evidence is suggestive rather than causal.

**6. Do not rely on the Callaway-Sant’Anna exercise as supportive evidence in its current form.**  
As written, this section weakens the paper. The estimator is applied to a setting with very limited timing variation, and the authors acknowledge that the pre-test rejects. Saying that the rejection likely reflects “selection into cohort timing rather than a violation of the identifying assumption” is not reassuring; it is essentially an admission that timing is endogenous. Unless the authors can justify the timing variation credibly, I would either remove this analysis or present it as a failed robustness check that motivates reliance on some alternative design.

**7. Strengthen the discussion of measurement and units.**  
Several effect descriptions are confusing. For example, the abstract says “0.0016 percentage points per log-unit,” but the reported coefficient appears to be on a share measured from 0 to 1. If so, the effect is 0.16 percentage points, not 0.0016 percentage points. Precision in units matters, especially when the headline result is small. Likewise, the interpretation of high-rank investigations should be clarified, since senior-official investigations may proxy for political salience rather than local anti-corruption intensity per se.

**8. Clarify whether the paper is about anti-corruption enforcement or broader centralization/discipline under Xi.**  
The “compliance shift” idea could be interesting if framed more broadly as a response to political discipline rather than a clean anti-corruption effect. The same campaign may have altered cadre incentives, promotion criteria, reporting pressure, center-local relations, and access to transfers. If the authors cannot isolate anti-corruption from these bundled political changes, they should say so and interpret the findings as the fiscal consequences of campaign-style political discipline. That framing would be more honest and may also better fit the science-spending result.

**9. Add more institutional evidence linking investigations to fiscal decision-making.**  
The mechanism section is currently speculative. The paper would benefit from institutional detail on who controls city budget composition, whether investigated officials were party secretaries/mayors/finance bureau leaders, whether investigations triggered turnover, and how quickly budgets could respond. If possible, distinguishing investigations of top city leaders from lower-level cadres would be more informative than simply counting all investigations. Not all officials are equally relevant for expenditure allocation.

**10. Consider a more modest contribution if identification cannot be strengthened.**  
There may still be a publishable descriptive contribution here: anti-corruption campaign intensity is associated with rising total spending and slightly higher science-and-technology budget shares, with no evidence of a relative increase in education shares. But that is a different paper from a causal test of Mauro’s composition hypothesis. A credible descriptive paper would emphasize patterns, not policy effects, and would spend more time validating measures and exploring heterogeneity.

Overall, I like the question and the ambition. But in its present form, the paper’s causal claims go beyond what the design and data can support. The authors’ best path forward is to either (i) obtain the correct fiscal composition data and rebuild identification around plausibly exogenous enforcement timing/exposure, or (ii) substantially moderate the claims and reposition the paper as descriptive evidence on how campaign intensity correlates with local budget patterns.
