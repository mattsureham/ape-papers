**Idea Fidelity**  
The paper significantly diverges from the original manifest. The manifest envisioned a multifaceted evaluation of the infrastructure access multiplier: using staggered state-level WIC EBT rollout to instrument vendor exits, exploiting a Bartik-style independent-vendor exposure measure, and leveraging Ohio’s phased county rollout to trace causal impacts on a suite of infant health outcomes (birth weight, preterm birth, breastfeeding). The submitted draft instead relies solely on a state-level difference-in-differences design with low birth weight as the single outcome, never cleanly linking EBT rollout to vendor exits or showing that vendor exits influenced participation or access. The Bartik and within-state county variations outlined in the manifest are absent. The paper therefore misses critical components of the proposed identification strategy and narrows the research question to a state-level treatment-on-treated effect of EBT adoption on LBW.  

**Summary**  
The paper investigates whether the U.S. WIC program’s transition from paper vouchers to Electronic Benefit Transfer (EBT) affected infant health through state-level low birth weight rates. Using staggered state adoption between 2004 and 2019, the author reports two-way fixed effects and Callaway–Sant’Anna estimates that are essentially zero, and argues that the 2010-2020 modernization did not produce measurable health harms despite documented vendor exits. The null is framed as evidence that vendor consolidation did not translate into adverse downstream outcomes.  

**Essential Points**  
1. **Causal Chain from EBT to Vendor Exits to Health Effects Is Broken.** The paper claims to study the “access cost” from vendor exits, but the empirical strategy never instruments vendor network changes nor demonstrates that EBT adoption meaningfully reduced access. Instead, it directly regresses LBW on EBT adoption, implicitly treating adoption as the treatment of interest, not vendor exits. Without linking EBT rollout to access shocks at the relevant margin (e.g., through the 5.4pp dropout that Meckel documents or other vendor-level data), it is unclear whether the null reflects no health impact of vendor exits or simply no effect of EBT per se. To evaluate the “infrastructure multiplier,” the paper needs to show that EBT caused meaningful vendor exit variation and that the remaining variation in vendor access is what drives health.  

2. **Measurement of Outcomes and Timing Blurs Treatment Variation.** The County Health Rankings data aggregate birth outcomes into multi-year rolling averages, and the analysis uses a two-year lag to align EBT implementation with the CHR release. This introduces substantial temporal imprecision: for states adopting in, say, 2015, the corresponding LBW observation may blend 2012–2016 births. Such averaging weakens the identifying variation and may bias estimates toward zero. A valid design would require sharper timing (e.g., using natality microdata directly or narrower aggregation) or a clear justification that the averaged measure still captures the expected contemporaneous effect. As is, the strategy risks conflating treatment and control periods and undermines the credibility of the DiD.  

3. **Interpretation of Null Results Ignores Heterogeneous Access Effects and Alternative Mechanisms.** The discussion emphasizes that null findings imply vendor exits are inconsequential, but the paper never explores heterogeneous effects where vendor losses may have been largest (rural/tribal areas, counties losing multiple vendors). Aggregation to the state level can easily mask localized harms. The manifest mentioned Ohio county rollout and exposure intensity measures precisely to uncover such heterogeneity. Without them, there is insufficient evidence to conclude that access shocks had no health impact; the null may simply reflect averaging over areas that never experienced an access shock. The paper should either incorporate finer spatial variation or clearly frame the results as state-average effects that may hide substantial local responses.  

Given these issues, the manuscript is not yet ready for publication; the authors should not proceed without addressing the broken identification chain and measurement concerns.  

**Suggestions**  
- **Re-center the research design around vendor exits rather than EBT as the “treatment.”** If you aim to test the infrastructure access multiplier, begin by replicating Meckel’s finding (or using other vendor-level data if available) to show that EBT rollout indeed led to meaningful vendor exit variation across states and counties. Consider constructing a state-level measure of independent vendor share (or vendor density per 1,000 WIC participants) and using EBT adoption as an instrument for that measure. This would allow you to estimate the reduced-form effect of vendor access and the local average treatment effect on infant health, which is closer to the original idea.  

- **Leverage exposure heterogeneity and within-state variation.** The manifest highlighted two additional sources of identification: (1) pre-EBT independent vendor share to capture exposure intensity (a Bartik-style approach), and (2) Ohio’s phased county rollout. You should pursue at least one of these extensions. For example, combine WIC vendor directories with adoption dates to construct a measure of vendor loss exposure by county or by pre-treatment vendor mix, then interact that with EBT timing or use it in an IV strategy. Even if such data are challenging, exploring county-level rollout (if documented) or exploiting the fact that some states implemented EBT in phases (e.g., county-by-county) would provide much stronger evidence on localized access shocks.  

- **Move to more granular natality data if possible.** The CHR multi-year averages obscure the timing of treatment, and the paper currently has to rely on a two-year lag to try to align EBT adoption with outcome measurement. If you can obtain the restricted-access NCHS natality files (or even the public-use microdata with county identifiers), you could construct state-year or county-year LBW/gestational age outcomes with precise dates. This would allow you to estimate event studies, check parallel trends more credibly, and avoid the attenuation caused by rolling averages. If restricted data are not feasible, at least clearly show how the CHR averaging window interacts with treatment timing (e.g., what years they cover) and consider using a model that accounts for the weighting (e.g., weighted regression that aligns adoption dates with the centers of the multi-year averages).  

- **Expand outcomes and heterogeneity analyses.** The manifest mentioned preterm birth and breastfeeding, yet the paper focuses solely on low birth weight. Including additional health outcomes would strengthen the claim that WIC vendor exits had no downstream effect. Even if these variables are not available in CHR, some might be accessible via natality files or state maternal/child health reports. Additionally, explore heterogeneity by rurality, vendor density, or income to assess where access shocks may have been most severe. This could involve interacting EBT adoption with county-level measures of store density or travel distance from WIC participants to vendors.  

- **Clarify the interpretation of the null and the policy implications.** Right now, the discussion interprets the null as evidence that modernization did not harm infant health, which is too strong given the level of aggregation. Instead, reframe the conclusion: the state-level average effect is null, but there may still be pockets of harm or adaptation. Emphasize that the results are consistent with the resilience or substitution mechanisms you mention, but also that more granular research is needed to rule out localized impacts.  

- **Provide additional robustness checks tailored to the identification assumptions.** For instance, document the distribution of adoption dates and show that treated and not-yet-treated states have similar pre-trends using event-study plots (potentially using simulated data if CHR smoothing makes direct event-study difficult). Consider placebo tests that shift adoption dates or use “never-treated” states as controls. If you cannot do pre-trends because of data averaging, explain explicitly how the averaging could bias the estimates (e.g., attenuation) and whether that matters for your conclusions.  

- **Address the manifest’s feasibility claims more transparently.** The manifest stated that the project was “ready,” citing data sources and sample size. In the paper, explicitly describe what data were actually used, what was feasible, and what limitations arose (e.g., inability to access vendor microdata or Ohio county rollout). This will help readers understand the constraints and avoid misinterpretation.  

In sum, the current draft provides a clean DiD estimate but fails to connect EBT-induced vendor exits to infant health, making the central identification and conclusion weak. Strengthening the causal chain, improving measurement, and incorporating richer heterogeneity would better realize the ambitious original idea.
