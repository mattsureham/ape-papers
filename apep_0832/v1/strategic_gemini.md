## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper investigates whether the "modernization" of the WIC program—specifically the transition from paper vouchers to Electronic Benefit Transfer (EBT)—harmed infant health by causing small, independent grocery stores to exit the program. While previous work in the *AER* (Meckel, 2020) showed these mandates caused a significant "supply-side" shock (vendor exits), this paper finds that this disruption did not translate into worse birth outcomes at the state level. It is a "well-powered null" that suggests the resilience of the social safety net to administrative shifts.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this pitch quite well. The anecdotal opening about the Mississippi mother is classic *AER* style—setting a human-scale tension that the data then tests at scale. 

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**The Contribution:** The paper provides the first comprehensive empirical evidence that the supply-side vendor disruption caused by WIC EBT mandates did not result in detectable harm to infant health at the state level.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It is clearly differentiated from Meckel (2020), which focused on the *mechanism* (vendor exit) without the *outcome* (health). It also updates the conversation started by Ambrozek et al. (2025).
*   **Question vs. Gap:** It frames itself as answering a question about the **world**: *Does administrative modernization hurt the poor?* This is a strong framing.
*   **Clarity:** A smart economist would immediately understand the "So What." It’s not "another DiD paper"; it’s a "completion of a narrative arc" regarding a specific policy reform.
*   **Bigger Contribution:** To make this contribution "bigger" for the *AER*, it needs to move beyond the state-level null. The state-level analysis is likely too aggregate to capture the pain of the "Mississippi mother" mentioned in the intro. A county-level analysis or a focus on "food deserts" would transform this from a "probably zero" paper to a "who exactly was protected and who was exposed" paper.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

*   **Closest Neighbors:** Meckel (2020, *AER*); Hoynes, Page, & Stevens (2011, *JPE*); Muralidharan et al. (2016, *AER*); Finkelstein & Notowidigdo (2019, *QJE*).
*   **Positioning:** It currently acts as a "rebuttal/extension" to Meckel. It should lean harder into the **"Safety Net Modernization"** literature (Muralidharan/Finkelstein). The real conversation is: *Is fraud reduction a free lunch?*
*   **Missing Conversations:** The paper should speak more to the **Industrial Organization of Retail** (e.g., how big-box retailers like Walmart act as a stabilizer for social programs).
*   **Framing:** The current framing is "Right." It’s a health/labor paper that uses an administrative shock to talk about welfare.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

*   **Setup:** The government modernizes WIC to stop fraud.
*   **Tension:** This modernization kills off the "mom-and-pop" stores that the poor rely on (The Meckel Fact).
*   **Resolution:** Surprisingly, the babies are fine.
*   **Implications:** Fraud-prone vendors were likely "low quality" or "phantom," and the efficiency of the modern system (or the dominance of supermarkets) absorbed the shock.

**Evaluation:** The arc is excellent. It is a "clean" story. However, the resolution feels a bit "hand-wavy" regarding *why* the null exists. It needs more data-driven evidence on the substitution to supermarkets to close the loop.

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

*   **The Lead Fact:** "When the government forced WIC onto EBT cards, 5% of stores quit, but birth weights didn't budge."
*   **Reaction:** People will lean in because it’s counter-intuitive. 
*   **Follow-up:** "But what about the women in rural areas with no Walmart?" 
*   **The Null:** This is a "good" null. It challenges the assumption that "more vendors always equals better outcomes."

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

*   **Front-loading:** The paper is well-structured.
*   **Data limitation:** The use of state-level data from "County Health Rankings" is the Achilles' heel. Using state-level aggregates for a phenomenon that happens at the street-corner level is a major weakness.
*   **The Appendix:** The standardized effect sizes (Table 5) are very helpful for an *AER* audience and should be mentioned prominently to prove the null isn't just a lack of power.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

In its current form, this is a very strong *Journal of Policy Analysis and Management (JPAM)* or *Journal of Health Economics (JHE)* paper. To make it an *AER* paper, it needs **granularity.**

The *AER* audience will be skeptical of a state-level null. If the author could obtain **county-level birth records** (or better yet, microdata) and show that even in counties with the *highest* rates of vendor exit, birth outcomes remained stable, the paper would be a "slam dunk."

**Single Most Impactful Advice:** Move from state-level aggregates to county-level analysis to prove the null holds even where the "treatment" (vendor exit) was most intense.
