## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple, policy-relevant question: when universities produce more computer science and engineering graduates, do the local places that train them actually build larger tech sectors, or do the gains leak away to superstar hubs? Using county-level variation in STEM degree expansion during the post-2009 boom, the paper argues that more local STEM production substantially raises local Information-sector employment and earnings, mainly by helping incumbent firms retain jobs.

A busy economist should care because this is really a paper about whether higher education policy is also place-based industrial policy. If local STEM expansion creates local tech ecosystems, then universities are not just education providers; they are regional economic infrastructure.

### Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Reasonably, but not sharply enough. The current introduction gets to the question, but it still reads a bit like “here is a policy issue, here is my design.” The strongest version of the paper is not “a Bartik IV on STEM completions.” It is: **do local talent-producing institutions generate locally captured innovation and employment, or are they just feeder systems for other places?**

The first two paragraphs should lean much harder into that tension.

### The pitch the paper should have

“American regions increasingly invest in universities to build local innovation economies, especially by expanding computer science and engineering programs. But a central question remains unresolved: when a place trains more STEM graduates, does that investment create local tech employment, or do the graduates leave and the returns accrue elsewhere?

This paper studies whether university STEM expansion functions as a local innovation supply chain. Exploiting the large national increase in CS and engineering degree production from 2009 to 2022, I show that places with greater preexisting STEM training capacity experienced larger increases in local Information-sector employment and earnings. The central finding is that expanding STEM production does not merely increase the number of graduates; it appears to thicken the local tech labor market and stabilize incumbent firms.”

That is the story. The econometrics should come after that.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### Contribution in one sentence

The paper’s core contribution is to argue that expanding university STEM degree production has large, locally captured effects on tech-sector employment, implying that higher education expansion can operate as place-based innovation policy through a labor-supply channel.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?

Only partially. The paper names adjacent literatures, but the distinction is still blurrier than it should be. Right now the contribution risks sounding like a mashup of:
- university spillovers,
- local labor market multipliers,
- and agglomeration in tech.

That is fine substantively, but editorially it creates a positioning problem: the reader may not know whether the paper is mainly about universities, local labor markets, or innovation geography.

The paper needs a crisper “not this, but that” contrast:
- **Not** university R&D or research parks,
- **Not** generic human capital externalities,
- **Not** high-tech job multipliers once jobs already exist,
- **But rather** the local economic incidence of producing STEM talent itself.

That distinction is the heart of the paper’s originality.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or filling a gap in a LITERATURE?

Mostly the world, which is good. The question “do locally trained STEM workers build local tech ecosystems?” is a world question. The problem is that the introduction then slides too quickly into literature-accounting mode (“contributes to three literatures”). That weakens the momentum.

For AER, the introduction should stay with the world question longer and treat the literature as supporting context, not as the main organizing device.

### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what’s new?

Maybe, but not confidently. Right now they might say: “It’s a shift-share paper showing STEM degree growth increased local tech employment.” That is competent but not memorable.

The more memorable version is: “It shows the local returns to producing STEM talent are real and large; universities seem to act as local innovation supply chains rather than just exporting graduates.”

That phrase—local incidence of STEM production—should be the conceptual center.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Three possibilities:

1. **A broader and more economically meaningful outcome set.**  
   The Information sector is too narrow to carry the whole paper. If the claim is about local technology ecosystems, the paper should ideally speak to a broader set of tech-intensive outcomes: software-related occupations, patenting, startup formation, productivity, business dynamism, or even non-Information sectors that absorb STEM talent. Right now the outcome choice makes the paper feel narrower than the framing.

2. **A clearer local-incidence framing.**  
   The biggest conceptual question is not just whether employment rises, but whether the region captures the returns to training talent. Evidence on retention, migration, or local absorption would make the story much bigger. As written, the paper repeatedly asserts “retaining graduates” without directly showing retention.

3. **A more convincing mechanism narrative.**  
   The “retention dividend” is interesting, but it currently feels like a suggestive decomposition, not a full mechanism. A bigger paper would show whether the effect comes from incumbent-firm expansion, reduced vacancy duration, changes in worker flows, or local reallocation across sectors.

If the author could enlarge one dimension, I would choose **scope of outcomes plus direct local-incidence evidence**. That would move it from “nice regional labor paper” toward “important paper on the geography of innovation.”

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

### Closest neighbors

The closest neighbors appear to be:

- **Moretti (2010)** on local multipliers from high-tech/tradable jobs.
- **Moretti (2012)** broadly on the new geography of jobs / human capital and place.
- **Kantor and Whalley (2019)** on university agglomeration effects / local economic impacts of research universities.
- **Valero and Van Reenen (2019)** on universities and local economic growth.
- **Hausman (2022)** on university innovation / local spillovers from university research.
- Potentially **Bound et al.** and related work on STEM labor markets and geography.
- Possibly papers on local human capital spillovers and place-based policy, including **Kline and Moretti**-adjacent work.

### How should the paper position itself relative to those neighbors?

Build on them, but sharpen the contrast.

- Relative to **Valero–Van Reenen / Kantor / Hausman**: “those papers are largely about universities as research producers or anchor institutions; this paper isolates universities as talent producers.”
- Relative to **Moretti**: “those papers ask what happens when high-tech jobs arrive; this paper asks what happens when high-tech workers are produced locally.”
- Relative to STEM labor market papers: “those papers study returns to STEM education or national labor supply; this paper studies local equilibrium effects of where STEM supply expands.”

That is a strong triad. It gives the paper a clean intellectual map.

### Is the paper currently positioned too narrowly or too broadly?

Oddly, both.

- **Too broadly** in claiming contributions to three large literatures without fully delivering to each.
- **Too narrowly** in operationalizing “local technology ecosystem” as mainly NAICS 51 employment and earnings.

The result is that the paper sounds ambitious but empirically reads specialized.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?

It seems light on at least two conversations:

1. **Spatial equilibrium / migration / brain drain / local retention**  
   Since the paper’s motivating question is whether graduates stay or leave, it should engage more directly with the literature on geographic mobility of college graduates, local talent retention, and brain drain/brain gain.

2. **Innovation geography beyond industry codes**  
   If the paper wants to talk about ecosystems, it should know and cite work on entrepreneurship, innovation clusters, and the geography of knowledge production—not just local labor markets.

It may also need to speak more explicitly to:
- labor market matching and thick-market models,
- local human capital externalities,
- and education as industrial policy.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?

Not quite yet. It is currently having a somewhat standard “universities + agglomeration” conversation. The more impactful conversation is:

**When regions invest in STEM education, who captures the returns—the training region or somewhere else?**

That is a first-order economics question involving human capital, migration, place-based policy, and innovation geography. It is a better conversation than “another local spillovers paper.”

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Regions and policymakers increasingly treat universities as anchor institutions and expand STEM training in hopes of building innovation economies. But it is unclear whether producing more STEM graduates benefits the local economy or merely supplies talent to existing superstar cities.

### Tension

Human capital is mobile. The very places that train talent may not be the places that capture its productivity. So the key puzzle is whether local STEM production actually thickens local tech labor markets or instead leaks away geographically.

### Resolution

The paper finds that places with larger STEM expansion experience larger increases in Information-sector employment and earnings, with the employment effect arising more through lower job destruction than increased job creation.

### Implications

If correct, the findings imply that university STEM expansion can be a meaningful local development strategy, not just an educational investment. They also suggest that labor-supply expansion can stabilize incumbent tech activity, not merely create startups.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the ingredients, but the arc is weaker than it should be because the paper overstates one mechanism and underdevelops the main tension.

The central tension is **local capture versus leakage**. But the resolution is measured mostly through local industry outcomes, not through actual retention or migration evidence. So the narrative feels slightly mismatched: the paper motivates one big question and answers a nearby one.

Also, the paper risks being “a collection of plausible results” because:
- the employment result is the headline,
- the earnings result is a complement,
- the job destruction result is interesting but somewhat secondary,
- the BA-share result is surprising but not fully integrated into the story.

The BA-share result especially feels like a result in search of a narrative. Is the paper about STEM creating broader local employment ladders? About complementarity between technical and nontechnical labor? About occupational restructuring inside tech? It does not yet know.

### What story should it be telling?

The cleanest story is:

1. **Places expand STEM training hoping to build local tech economies.**
2. **This may fail if graduates leave.**
3. **But in fact, local STEM production appears to generate locally captured employment growth.**
4. **The main channel is not startup creation but strengthening incumbent firms and broadening the surrounding workforce.**

That is coherent. But the paper should drop or subordinate anything that does not serve that arc.
