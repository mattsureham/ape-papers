## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether one of the most famous U.S. financial panics—the Panic of 1907—left long-run scars on individual workers, not just banks and firms. Using linked census records, it argues that workers employed in sectors more reliant on bank credit suffered worse subsequent occupational trajectories in the states hit hardest by the panic. A busy economist should care because the paper tries to connect financial instability to worker-level mobility in a formative historical episode, potentially linking banking history, labor scarring, and the origins of central banking.

Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs? Not quite. The current opening is competent, but it starts from “this historical event was important” and “there is a literature gap,” rather than from the sharper world question: **when banks fail, how much of the damage shows up in workers’ lives, and is that a timeless feature of capitalist economies?** The introduction gets there eventually, but too slowly and too academically.

What the first two paragraphs should say instead:

> Financial crises are usually narrated through collapsed intermediaries, frozen credit markets, and regulatory reform. But the deeper question is whether banking panics leave lasting scars on ordinary workers’ economic trajectories. If they do, the welfare costs of financial instability are much larger than bank failures themselves, and the case for lender-of-last-resort institutions looks fundamentally different.
>
> The Panic of 1907 is the ideal test. It was the last major U.S. financial crisis before the Federal Reserve, severe enough to reshape American monetary institutions, yet we still do not know what it did to individual workers. Using nearly 8 million linked census records, this paper shows that workers in more bank-dependent sectors suffered worse occupational mobility in the states most exposed to the panic, providing micro evidence that financial crises scar labor market outcomes even in the pre-Fed economy.

That is the pitch. It is stronger because it leads with the big economic question, uses 1907 as the setting rather than the selling point, and tells the reader immediately why this matters beyond economic history.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:**  
The paper provides individual-level historical evidence that a major banking panic depressed subsequent occupational mobility for workers in credit-dependent sectors in the places most exposed to the crisis.

### Is this clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?
Only partially. The paper says “first individual-level evidence” several times, which is a claim of form rather than substance. That is not enough by itself. “First” claims are brittle and often not that interesting unless they unlock a genuinely new question. The paper needs to do more to distinguish itself from:
- historical banking panic papers that trace geographic propagation through networks,
- modern finance-real economy papers on local credit shocks,
- labor scarring papers on recessions/displacement,
- historical mobility papers using linked census panels.

Right now the contribution reads as: “take linked historical census data + apply a DDD around a famous shock.” That is respectable, but not yet memorable.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a gap in a literature?
Too much the latter. The introduction repeatedly says “no study has…” and “this paper fills that gap.” That is the language of a field-journal paper. AER papers usually sound like they are changing how we think about a broad phenomenon in the world.

The stronger frame is:
- Financial crises may impose persistent labor-market costs, not just temporary output losses.
- Those costs may be a deep structural feature of credit economies, observable even before the modern welfare state and central banking.
- Therefore, banking stabilization institutions matter partly because they prevent worker scarring, not merely bank distress.

That is a world question. The paper is adjacent to it, but does not yet fully own it.

### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what's new?
At present, probably: “It’s a historical DiD/DDD paper on the Panic of 1907 and occupational mobility using linked census data.” That is not fatal, but it is not what you want.

What you want them to say is:  
“Interesting paper—it shows that banking panics damage workers’ long-run career ladders, and that this was true even in 1907. So the labor-market costs of financial instability predate the Fed and modern labor institutions.”

That version is both clearer and bigger.

### What would make this contribution bigger?
Be specific:

1. **Show the panic changed life trajectories, not just occscore arithmetic.**  
   Occupational income score is acceptable, but narrow and abstract. The paper would feel larger if it showed economically intuitive margins:
   - movement into lower-status broad occupational classes,
   - exit from white-collar/skilled work,
   - persistence in downgraded occupations,
   - sector switching out of credit-dependent industries.

2. **Make heterogeneity do conceptual work.**  
   Right now heterogeneity is mostly there to support the design. Bigger would be:
   - stronger effects for younger workers, recent migrants, foreign-born workers, or initially upwardly-mobile workers,
   - weaker effects where alternative credit channels were stronger,
   - stronger effects where local industrial structure made working-capital needs especially acute.

3. **Connect more directly to the institution-building question.**  
   If the ambition is partly about why central banking matters, then the paper should lean harder into the idea that 1907 revealed labor-market externalities of banking fragility. That is a more ambitious claim than “here is a historical estimate.”

4. **Use the setting to say something general.**  
   The paper currently says, in effect, “modern findings also existed historically.” Bigger would be:  
   “Worker scarring from financial crises is not contingent on modern labor contracts, unemployment insurance, or postwar institutions; it is a recurring consequence of disruptions to working capital.”

That would be a more publishable idea.

That bridge is the paper’s natural value-add.

Also, if the paper wants to make a case about worker welfare rather than merely occupation codes, it may need to speak more to the literature on **career ladders and persistent labor market disruptions**, not just historical mobility.

Almost, but not quite. Right now it is having the conversation:

The better conversation is:  
“What do banking crises do to workers’ long-run economic mobility, and what does 1907 reveal about why lender-of-last-resort institutions matter?”

That is a much more interesting room to be in.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Before this paper, we know the Panic of 1907 was a landmark banking crisis that helped precipitate the Federal Reserve. We also know from modern work that financial crises can depress employment and earnings. But the worker-level consequences of classic pre-Fed panics remain poorly documented.

### Tension
The main tension should be:  
**Were banking panics mainly financial-system events, or did they permanently alter workers’ economic trajectories?**  
A subsidiary tension: core panic states were richer and more dynamic, so aggregate comparisons could misleadingly suggest good worker outcomes exactly where the panic hit hardest.

### Resolution
The paper’s resolution is that once one focuses on workers in more bank-dependent sectors relative to less exposed workers within the same states, the panic is associated with worse occupational trajectories. In other words: the industrial Northeast could still look upwardly mobile overall while exposing certain workers to crisis-related scarring.

### Implications
The implications are:
- financial instability has worker-level costs that persist,
- those costs are not unique to modern economies,
- the welfare case for financial stabilization institutions extends beyond banks,
- aggregate regional progress can mask distributional harm within places.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
It has the pieces, but the arc is only **serviceable**. Too much of the paper reads like a sequence of empirical claims organized around the design, not a story organized around the economic question.

The current story is:
1. Panic happened.
2. Literature gap exists.
3. Here is the dataset.
4. Here is the DDD.
5. Here are robustness checks.
6. Therefore workers were scarred.

The better story is:
1. Financial crises may scar workers, but we rarely observe this in settings without modern institutions.
2. The Panic of 1907 is a natural historical laboratory because it was severe, geographically uneven, and politically consequential.
3. The challenge is that the hardest-hit places were also the most advanced economically.
4. The key empirical insight is to compare more bank-dependent and less bank-dependent workers within place.
5. The result overturns the misleading aggregate picture and reveals distributional damage.
6. That changes how we interpret both the panic and the motivation for central banking.

That is a paper with a narrative spine.

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

### What fact would you lead with at a dinner party of economists?
I would lead with:  
“The Panic of 1907 didn’t just hurt banks—it appears to have pushed workers in credit-dependent sectors onto worse occupational paths for years afterward.”

That is the right headline, not the exact coefficient.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
Some would lean in, especially economic historians, macro-finance people, and labor economists. But many would reach for their phones unless the presenter immediately connects the historical episode to a broader claim:  
“this suggests worker scarring from financial crises is a deep feature of credit economies, not just a modern postwar phenomenon.”

Without that broader claim, it sounds like a clever historical design. With it, it sounds like a potentially important idea.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Probably one of these:
- “Is this really about finance, or just industrial restructuring in the Northeast?”
- “Does occupational income score capture anything economically meaningful here?”
- “Why should I update my beliefs about financial crises from this historical episode?”
- “What is the mechanism—loss of working capital, unemployment, migration, sector switching?”

Those questions are telling. The paper needs to anticipate them in its framing, not just in appendices or robustness tables.

### If the findings are modest: is the result itself interesting?
Yes, but only if sold correctly. The magnitude is not enormous; it is historically plausible and directionally interesting. That means the value of the paper is not “look at this giant estimate,” but rather:
- the shock is iconic,
- the data are unusually rich,
- the result uncovers a type of harm economists care about,
- and it does so in a setting before modern stabilization policies.

If the paper is framed as “we found a moderate negative DDD coefficient,” it will feel incremental. If framed as “even in 1907, banking crises damaged workers’ long-run career trajectories,” it becomes much more interesting.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

Be honest: the gap is meaningful.

### What is the main problem?
Mostly **an ambition-and-framing problem**, with some **scope problem**.

The paper is competent and has a real hook. But in current form, it feels like a solid field-journal paper in economic history or labor/finance history:
- famous event,
- new linked microdata,
- plausible design,
- moderate effect,
- “first evidence” claim.

That is not enough for AER. To get closer, it needs to become a paper about a broader economic idea, using 1907 as the proving ground.

### Is it a framing problem?
Yes, strongly. The paper undersells the broad question and over-relies on novelty-by-setting. “First individual-level evidence” is not an AER frame. “Financial crises leave persistent worker scars even in premodern institutional environments” is much closer.

### Is it a scope problem?
Yes. The evidence is a bit too narrow relative to the breadth of the claim. If the paper wants to speak to worker scarring and the rationale for central banking, one occupational index plus a weak homeownership mechanism is thin.

### Is it a novelty problem?
Somewhat. The general intuition—that credit contractions harm workers in exposed sectors—is not novel. The novelty is in historical setting, scale, and worker-level evidence. That is real, but not sufficient by itself for AER unless the paper extracts a bigger conceptual lesson.

### Is it an ambition problem?
Yes. The paper is careful and safe. It aims to document one historical effect rather than to reshape how economists think about the labor-market consequences of financial crises.

### Single most impactful piece of advice
**Rewrite the paper around a larger claim: that worker scarring is a fundamental consequence of financial crises, and use the Panic of 1907 as the clean historical demonstration—not as the contribution in itself.**

That one change would force improvements in the introduction, literature framing, results organization, and conclusion. It would also reveal whether the existing evidence base is broad enough for the claim; if not, the author would know exactly what additional outcomes or mechanisms are needed.
