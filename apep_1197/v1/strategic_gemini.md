## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether the financial "scarring" effects well-documented in modern recessions—where workers suffer long-term career damage from temporary crises—were present in the pre-Federal Reserve era. Using a massive 8-million-person linked census panel, it finds that workers in banking-dependent sectors in states hit hardest by the Panic of 1907 experienced significant and persistent occupational downgrading compared to their peers in agriculture. It suggests that the "real" costs of financial instability are a fundamental feature of market economies, justifying the institutional shift toward a central bank as a means of protecting household livelihoods, not just bank balance sheets.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this clearly. The first two paragraphs effectively bridge the gap between a classic "macro" historical event (the 1907 Panic) and modern "micro" labor concerns (scarring/hysteresis). The current pitch is strong.

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution:** This paper provides the first individual-level evidence of long-term occupational scarring from a pre-Federal Reserve financial crisis, demonstrating that the real costs of 19th-century panics extended far beyond the banking sector to the career trajectories of millions of workers.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It differentiates itself well from Frydman et al. (2023) by moving from bank-level/firm-level outcomes to the *individual* worker. It is distinct from the general mobility literature (Abramitzky et al.) by focusing on a specific macro-shock rather than secular trends.
*   **Question vs. Literature:** It is framed as a question about the WORLD (Does financial instability inherently scar workers?), which is a major strength.
*   **Explanation to a Colleague:** A smart economist would say: "It uses the new IPUMS machine-learning links to show that the 1907 Panic actually knocked people off their career ladders for at least a decade."
*   **Making it Bigger:** To make this contribution larger, the author could explore **heterogeneity by age**. Are young workers (entering the labor market) scarred more than older workers? This would connect to the "lucky graduates" literature (Schwandt & von Wachter).

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

*   **Closest Neighbors:** Frydman, Hilt, & Zhou (2023) on 1907 networks; Davis & von Wachter (2011) on modern scarring; Abramitzky, Boustan, & Eriksson (2012/2014) on historical mobility; Yagan (2019) on hysteresis.
*   **Strategy:** The paper "builds" on Frydman et al. and "synthesizes" historical data with modern labor frameworks. It should perhaps "attack" (gently) the view that pre-Fed crises were "sharp but short" or primarily financial-sector nuisances.
*   **Conversation:** The paper is having the right conversation, but it could speak more to the **Political Economy of the Fed**. It mentions this in the intro, but the results could be framed as a direct empirical test of the *necessity* of a lender of last resort for the real economy.

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

*   **Setup:** The Panic of 1907 is the "canonical" pre-Fed crisis, usually studied as a failure of the payments system.
*   **Tension:** While we know the banks failed, we don't know if the workers suffered lasting damage or if the flexible, unregulated labor market of the Gilded Age allowed them to bounce back quickly.
*   **Resolution:** They didn't bounce back. Workers in credit-reliant sectors stayed in lower-prestige/lower-income occupations years after the recovery.
*   **Implications:** Financial crises have a "micro" footprint that outlasts their "macro" indicators.

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong. It is not just a "collection of results"; it is a targeted inquiry into the persistence of economic shocks.

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

At a dinner party, I would lead with: **"Even in the 'wild west' of Gilded Age capitalism, a bank panic in New York could demote a skilled worker in New Jersey to a manual laborer for a decade."**

People would lean in. The follow-up question would be: "Did they eventually recover, or was this a permanent shift in American inequality?" The 1920 census (once linked) would be the "holy grail" follow-up.

---

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

*   **Front-loading:** The paper is well-structured, but Table 1's compositional differences are so stark that the DDD logic needs to be hammered even harder before the results to prevent "urbanization" skeptics from tuning out.
*   **Appendix:** The home ownership mechanism (Table 3, Col 4-5) is interesting but inconclusive; it could be moved to the appendix to keep the focus on the labor market.
*   **Buried Results:** I want to see a "Transition Matrix" (e.g., what specific occupations did they move from/to?). This is likely in the author's back pocket and belongs in the main text to give the "Occscore" color.

---

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The paper is very close. It has the **scale** (8 million observations) and the **relevance** (connecting 1907 to modern scarring).

**The Gap:** It feels a bit "safe." To reach AER-level ambition, the author needs to move beyond "X caused Y" and tell us more about **who** was most vulnerable.
*   **The Single Biggest Advice:** Analyze **age-at-shock**. If the scarring is driven by young workers losing their first "good" job, it connects the paper to the massive modern literature on human capital formation and "entry conditions." This turns a history paper into a fundamental labor paper.
