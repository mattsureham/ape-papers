## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether the Panic of 1907 did more than disrupt banks and output—did it permanently alter workers’ career trajectories? Using nearly 8 million men linked across the 1900 and 1910 censuses, it argues that in states hit hardest by the panic, workers in credit-dependent sectors experienced persistent occupational downgrading relative to agricultural workers, suggesting that financial crises can leave long labor-market scars even in the pre-Fed era.

A busy economist should care because this is a classic macro-finance question with micro stakes: when credit collapses, is the damage just a short recession, or does it reshape workers’ lives for years? The paper’s promise is to connect a canonical historical banking panic to modern questions about worker scarring, financial propagation, and the value of stabilization institutions.

### Does the paper articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?
Not quite. The current opening is competent and informed, but it leads with history and a literature gap rather than the punchy world question. Paragraph 1 says the panic was important and understudied at the worker level. Paragraph 2 says there is a literature and this paper fills a gap. That is adequate, but not AER-level positioning.

The first two paragraphs should more directly say:

**The pitch the paper should have:**

> Financial crises are usually studied through banks, firms, and aggregate output. But the central welfare question is what they do to workers: when credit markets freeze, do workers simply endure a temporary downturn, or do they suffer lasting career damage?  
>   
> The Panic of 1907 is an unusually revealing setting for this question. It was the last major U.S. financial crisis before the Federal Reserve, before deposit insurance, and before modern labor-market protections. Using nearly 8 million men linked across the 1900 and 1910 censuses, I show that workers in credit-dependent sectors in the states most exposed to the panic experienced persistent occupational downgrading relative to comparable agricultural workers in the same states. The result suggests that labor-market scarring is not a feature of modern recessions alone, but a deeper consequence of financial instability.

That framing puts the world question first, makes the historical setting serve the modern question, and states the answer up front.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### Contribution in one sentence
The paper provides individual-level historical evidence that a major banking panic generated persistent occupational downgrading for workers in credit-dependent sectors, implying that financial crises scar workers’ career paths, not just short-run output.

### Is this clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?
Only partially. The paper distinguishes itself mostly through “first individual-level evidence from a pre-Fed financial crisis,” which is true as a novelty claim but not yet a fully satisfying intellectual contribution. “First in setting/data” is weaker than “changes what we think about how financial crises work.”

The paper’s closest neighbors seem to be:
- **Frydman, Hilt, and Zhou / related recent work on the Panic of 1907 and correspondent networks**  
- **Chodorow-Reich (2014)** on bank credit shocks and employment  
- **Yagan (2019)** on employment hysteresis after the Great Recession  
- **Jacobson, LaLonde, and Sullivan (1993)** and subsequent displacement/scarring literature  
- Possibly **Feigenbaum** / **Abramitzky-Boustan-Eriksson** style linked historical census work on mobility

Relative to those, the paper is not yet sharply framed. Is the contribution:
1. showing that worker scarring from financial crises existed long before modern labor institutions,
2. showing that financial crises propagate through sectoral credit dependence to occupational mobility,
3. or using linked historical census data to reveal long-run worker-level consequences of panics?

All three are plausible, but the introduction does not force the reader to settle on one.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or filling a gap in a LITERATURE?
Too much the latter. The phrase “this paper fills that gap” appears early and drives the positioning. That is usually a warning sign. AER papers generally answer a substantive question about the world and only secondarily note that no one has done it before.

The better world question is: **Do financial crises leave lasting scars on workers’ careers, and is that true even in a pre-modern financial system?**

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the intro?
They could, but likely in a deflationary way:  
“It's a historical DiD/DDD paper showing that the Panic of 1907 hurt occupational mobility in bank-dependent sectors.”

That is not fatal, but it means the paper is currently more “competent applied economic history” than “must-read economics.” The reader does not yet walk away with a sharp new belief.

### What would make this contribution bigger?
Specific ways:

1. **A sharper outcome concept.**  
   Right now the outcome is occupational income score change. That is acceptable, but somewhat indirect. The contribution would feel larger if the paper could show something more intuitive and economically vivid:
   - transition into lower-status occupations,
   - exit from white-collar or skilled work,
   - increased downward occupational mobility,
   - reduced upward mobility conditional on baseline occupation,
   - persistence into 1920 if linkable.

2. **Mechanisms that feel tied to finance rather than broad structural change.**  
   Home ownership is not the right mechanism to elevate the paper. It feels tacked on and unconvincing as the main transmission channel. Better mechanisms would be:
   - stronger effects in occupations more dependent on working capital,
   - larger effects in urban counties or places more exposed to correspondent withdrawals,
   - heterogeneity by baseline skill/rung on occupational ladder,
   - differential recovery among stayers vs movers, younger vs older workers, immigrants vs natives.

3. **A more ambitious comparison.**  
   The paper should ask whether 1907 looked like later crises in its worker consequences. Even a non-causal comparative framing could help:
   - Is the pattern similar to modern displacement after credit shocks?
   - Does the sectoral pattern mirror later financial crises more than ordinary recessions?

4. **A stronger framing around institutions.**  
   The paper hints that this matters for why the Fed was created. That could be powerful, but only if treated carefully. The key point would be: instability in the payments and banking system had real labor-market costs, not just financial-sector costs. That is broader and more important than “therefore the Fed was justified.”

### How should the paper position itself relative to those neighbors?
Mostly **build on and connect**, not attack.

- Relative to the 1907 banking-history papers:  
  “They show where the panic went; I show what it did to workers.”
- Relative to modern labor-scarring papers:  
  “They show career scarring in modern recessions; I show this mechanism predates modern institutions.”
- Relative to historical mobility papers:  
  “They study long-run mobility in general; I show that a specific macro-financial shock altered occupational trajectories.”

That triangulation is promising. The paper should present itself as a bridge between macro-finance and labor, using economic history as leverage.

### Is it currently positioned too narrowly or too broadly?
Oddly, both.

- **Too narrowly** because a lot of the paper reads like a niche economic history exercise about the Panic of 1907.
- **Too broadly** because it gestures at huge claims about financial crises, worker scarring, and the Fed without enough narrative discipline.

It needs a clearer center of gravity: **financial crises have worker-level career effects, and 1907 provides unusually clean historical evidence of that proposition.**

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of, or under-engaged with?
It is relatively thin on:
- the broader literature on **credit shocks and labor-market adjustment**
- the literature on **worker reallocation across sectors after macro shocks**
- possibly **Great Depression / bank failure** worker or household consequence papers
- literature on **occupational mobility as a margin of adjustment**, not just earnings or employment

It also underuses the literature on **why history matters for external validity**. The historical setting is not just quaint—it helps isolate the phenomenon in a world without modern stabilizers.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?
Mostly, but not optimally. Right now it is having three conversations at once:
1. Panic of 1907 history,
2. labor-market scarring,
3. historical mobility.

The most impactful conversation is probably **macro-finance meets labor**. The history should be the setting, not the audience. The mobility literature is useful, but should be secondary.

The unexpected but valuable connection is to the literature on **the real incidence of financial instability**. That is the conversation that gives the paper broader appeal.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Before this paper, we know the Panic of 1907 was a major financial event and we know from modern evidence that recessions and credit shocks can scar workers. But we do not know whether a classic pre-central-bank financial panic had similar worker-level consequences.

### Tension
The places hardest hit by the panic were also richer, more urban, and on steeper upward trajectories. So the raw data go in the “wrong” direction: hard-hit states actually look better on average. That creates a real narrative tension—how do you detect worker harm inside booming, structurally advantaged places?

### Resolution
Within hard-hit states, workers in banking-dependent sectors did worse relative to agricultural workers, consistent with the panic disrupting occupational advancement through the credit channel.

### Implications
Financial crises impose persistent costs on workers, not just banks and output aggregates. The labor-market consequences of banking instability appear to be a structural feature of market economies, which raises the stakes of financial stabilization policy.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
Yes, but it is only **serviceable**, not fully compelling.

The strongest element of the paper is actually the contrast between:
- the **positive raw cross-state pattern**, and
- the **negative within-state sectoral pattern**.

That is a good story. It gives the paper a puzzle and a resolution. But the paper does not lean on that enough as the central narrative device.

Instead, it sometimes feels like:
- historical background,
- data description,
- a DDD result,
- several checks,
- some discussion.

In other words, it risks reading like a collection of sensible results rather than a tightly engineered story.

### What story should it be telling?
The story should be:

> The Panic of 1907 hit the most advanced states, so aggregate and cross-state comparisons are misleadingly reassuring. But once you look inside those states, the workers most exposed to a collapse in commercial credit suffered lasting career damage. Financial crises can coexist with long-run regional dynamism while still scarring the workers tied to the financial system.

That is a much better story than “this is the first individual-level paper on a pre-Fed panic.”

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party?
“I can now link almost 8 million men across the 1900 and 1910 censuses, and it looks like the Panic of 1907 knocked workers in credit-dependent sectors down the occupational ladder for years.”

That is reasonably interesting.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
Some would lean in, especially macro-finance, labor, and economic history economists. But the reaction would depend heavily on the framing. If presented as “another historical diff-in-diff,” phones come out. If presented as “banking crises have always scarred workers’ careers, even before the Fed existed,” people lean in.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Probably one of these:
- “Is this really about finance, or just urban-industrial restructuring?”
- “How big is the mobility effect in intuitive terms?”
- “Does it persist beyond 1910?”
- “Why is occupation score the right margin?”
- “What is special about agriculture as the comparison group?”

Those are substantive framing questions, not technical ones.

### If the findings are modest, is the modesty interesting?
Yes—but only if the paper leans into what the estimate means. The effect is not enormous, and the paper should not oversell it. But modest persistent occupational scarring visible three years after a brief panic, using a very coarse decennial occupational measure, is actually a meaningful result. The right way to sell it is not “huge effect,” but:

> “Even with a blunt long-horizon occupational measure, we can still detect persistent worker damage from a short but severe financial panic.”

That makes the modesty part of the argument.

2. **Front-load the key puzzle.**

   “The paper’s contribution is to show that a canonical banking panic generated persistent worker-level occupational scarring through sectoral credit dependence.”  
   Right now that sentence is never stated cleanly.

5. **Cut the weaker mechanism discussion.**  
   The home ownership section does not add much. It does not feel like a central mechanism and dilutes the main story. If space is scarce, this is appendix material.

6. **Bring the key result forward.**  
   The reader currently waits through a lot of context before seeing the paper’s conceptual novelty. The introduction should reveal, very early, both:

   The conclusion mostly summarizes. It should end on a sharper broader takeaway: financial architecture matters because crises alter workers’ life trajectories, not just annual GDP.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

### What is the gap?
Mostly a **framing and ambition problem**, with some **scope** issues.

- **Framing problem:** The science may be there, but the paper presents itself too much as “first historical worker-level evidence on the Panic of 1907” rather than as a big statement about the real incidence of financial crises.
- **Scope problem:** The outcome and mechanism set are a bit thin for a top general-interest placement. One occupational mobility outcome and a weak homeownership mechanism do not quite feel like a fully developed AER package.
- **Ambition problem:** The paper is careful, competent, and safe. It does not yet make the reader feel that their understanding of financial crises has changed.

### Is it a novelty problem?
Not exactly. The setting and data are novel enough. The issue is that novelty is doing too much work. The paper needs a stronger conceptual payload than “this setting has not been studied at the individual level.”

### What would excite the top 10 people in this field?
A paper that convincingly says:

> Financial crises scar workers through occupational reallocation, and this mechanism is visible even in the classic pre-Fed panic. The labor-market incidence of banking instability is therefore deep and historically persistent.

To get there, the paper likely needs:
- a more forceful framing around worker-level incidence of financial crises,
- more intuitive evidence on what “occupational scarring” means,
- and ideally at least one more compelling mechanism or heterogeneity dimension tied tightly to credit dependence.

### Single most impactful advice
**Reframe the paper around a big world question—whether banking crises permanently alter workers’ careers—rather than around being the first individual-level study of the Panic of 1907.**

That one change would improve the introduction, literature positioning, narrative arc, and perceived contribution all at once.
