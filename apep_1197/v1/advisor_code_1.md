**Summary**  
The paper provides the first individual-level evidence that the Panic of 1907 caused occupational downgrading for workers in banking-dependent sectors. Using the IPUMS MLP linking of the 1900 and 1910 full-count censuses, it exploits the cross-state variation in panic severity and within-state sectoral exposure to banking credit in a DDD framework. The main result is a modest but statistically precise $-1.56$ point (≈–0.13 SD) decline in occscore for banking-exposed workers in core-panic states relative to agricultural peers, and this finding is robust to multiple falsification and sensitivity checks.

**Essential Points**  
1. **Identifying Assumption and Sectoral Selection:** The DDD hinges on the assumption that, absent the panic, the occupational trajectory gap between banking-dependent versus agricultural workers would have evolved similarly across states of different severity. However, core-panic states are also precisely the ones undergoing the most rapid industrialization and hence may have had divergent sector-specific trends unrelated to the panic (e.g., factory expansion that disproportionately raised banking-sector occscore). To bolster credibility, the paper needs to provide direct evidence that pre-1907 trends in occscore growth were parallel across sectors and states, or, absent pre-treatment outcomes, softer proxies (e.g., comparing 1890–1900 occupational shifts via other linked data or external industrialization metrics). Without meeting this assumption, the negative DDD may conflate the panic with differential secular sectoral dynamics.

2. **Measurement of Treatment Intensity and Heterogeneity:** The treatment is coded at the state level as a three-tier severity index, but the original idea relies on county-level correspondent network exposure (as in Frydman et al.). Aggregating to the state obscures substantial within-state heterogeneity and introduces classical measurement error that attenuates estimates, but more importantly it risks conflating counties that had very different network ties. The authors should either reconstruct a more granular treatment (e.g., county-level suspension rates or the Frydman exposure measure averaged within states) or demonstrate that within-state variation is minimal. Otherwise, the causal interpretation is compromised by aggregation bias.

3. **Mechanism Interpretation and Alternative Channels:** The paper claims that the effect works through banking-dependent labor markets rather than wealth destruction, yet the evidence is somewhat indirect (null DDD on ownership, literacy placebo). There remain plausible alternative explanations: panic states may have experienced greater labor demand shocks or migration flows that differentially affected banking workers. Without direct evidence on changes in employment, unemployment, or firm-level credit availability, the narrative risks overreaching. Consider incorporating any available county-level banking outcomes (e.g., bank suspensions, call loan volume) to directly tie the treatment to local credit shocks, or demonstrate that sectoral employment growth (even from aggregate yearbooks) aligns with the DDD pattern.

**Suggestions**  
- **Strengthen the Parallel Trends Argument:** Given the lack of pre-period panel data for 1900–1910, the paper should be explicit about how it assesses the plausibility of the key assumption for the DDD. One possibility is to leverage the 1890 occupational data (even if only at the state or county level) to show that sectoral occupational upgrading was not more pronounced in core-panic states before 1907. Alternatively, use cross-sectional variation in other outcomes (e.g., occupational share of manufacturing) to argue that banking-dependent sectors were not already diverging in ways that would bias the interaction. Sensitivity analyses where the interaction is estimated with alternative control sectors (e.g., non-banking-dependent manufacturing vs. farming) would help.

- **Explore More Granular Treatment Measures:** The manifest emphasizes Frydman et al.’s county-level correspondent network data. The paper should either (a) implement that finer treatment by merging county-level suspension rates or banking exposure to the individual data, acknowledging the greater measurement precision, or (b) provide a strong justification for why the state-level classification suffices (e.g., there is little within-state heterogeneity, or almost all core-panic counties lie in core states). If aggregation is unavoidable, consider instrumenting the state severity tiers with historical network centrality to assess robustness to measurement error.

- **Address Potential Confounders in Banking-Dependence Classification:** The construction of `BankDep` from broad occupation groups could capture other attributes beyond banking exposure (urban location, immigrant composition, unionization). The paper already controls for demographics, but further robustness could include interactions between `BankDep` and urbanization proxies, or subset analyses within urban-only or rural-only samples to ensure the effect is not driven by urbanization differences within a state. Additionally, the business-cycle interpretation would be strengthened by showing that the same sectors did not behave similarly in states with low panic severity (i.e., there is no analogous negative interaction).

- **Document Linking Quality and Sample Selection:** The MLP linking process might be more accurate for urban, native-born, or literate populations, potentially coinciding with the banking-dependent workers most affected by the panic. The authors should report linking rates by sector and by panic severity and show these do not systematically vary in ways that would bias occupational change. If such data are unavailable, provide a sensitivity analysis (e.g., weighting by estimated linking probability or replicating results in a high-confidence subset).

- **Expand the Mechanism Analysis:** Beyond literacy and home ownership, consider additional outcomes that the census provides (e.g., place of residence changes, employment status, industry codes in 1910 beyond occupation) to triangulate the financial disruption mechanism. If possible, merge in county-level data on bank suspensions or the number of banks per capita to test for effect heterogeneity along these dimensions. Even a descriptive comparison (e.g., did counties with more suspended banks show larger DDD effects?) would enhance the narrative.

- **Clarify the Interpretation of the Cross-State Positive Coefficient:** The positive cross-state coefficient on panic severity is interpreted as reflecting the structural advantages of core states. It would be helpful to formally decompose the cross-state association into urbanization, immigration, and sectoral composition channels (for instance, by demonstrating how adding covariates sequentially flips the coefficient). This would reassure readers that the DDD isolates a financial effect rather than adjusting for pre-existing advantages post hoc.

- **Consider Alternative Outcomes Beyond Occscore:** Although occscore is a convenient continuous metric, it captures only between-occupation moves. If the data allow, report results for binary outcomes (e.g., moving from agricultural to non-agricultural occupation) or use coarser occupational classes to show consistency. This would make the findings more interpretable and show that the scarring is not an artefact of the scoring system.

- **Transparency about Autonomously Generated Research:** Since the manuscript is machine-generated, it might benefit from a short discussion (possibly in an appendix) on how standard practices (e.g., pre-analysis plans, replication files) are handled to ensure scholarly rigor. This could also include a note on computational reproducibility given the large dataset.

These additions would bolster the credibility of the causal claims and enrich the paper’s contribution to the historical financial crisis and labor-market scarring literatures.
