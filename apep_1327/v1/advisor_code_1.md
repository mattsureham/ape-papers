**Idea Fidelity**  
The paper substantially pursues the manifest’s core idea: using chain pharmacy closures (CVS, Walgreens, Rite Aid) as quasi-exogenous shocks to dual SNAP-pharmacy infrastructure and tracing downstream consequences for Medicaid beneficiaries. It matches the proposed datasets (T-MSIS linked to NPPES), the empirical puzz le (pharmacy access loss without ED surge), and the key identification devices (staggered DiD with event study, Rite Aid bankruptcy IV, last-pharmacy heterogeneity). However, the manuscript drops a few elements from the manifest: it never discusses SNAP-authorized retail status after the introduction, nor explicitly contrasts closures of dual SNAP-pharmacies with “pure” grocery or pharmacy-only exits in the empirical results. Also, while the manifest emphasizes the dual-service loss, the analysis focuses almost entirely on pharmacy outcomes and ED utilization; the food-access dimension is only mentioned in motivation and is not operationalized. Clarifying how the dual SNAP-pharmacy infrastructure translates into the empirical strategy (e.g., by ruling out simultaneous food-store closures or showing the overlap matters) would better honor the manifest.

**Summary**  
The paper studies the 2018–2024 wave of CVS/Walgreens/Rite Aid closures by constructing a ZIP-month panel from T-MSIS claims linked to NPPES, and estimates the effect of these exits on Medicaid pharmacy utilization and emergency department (ED) visits. The two-way FE event-study reveals sharp, persistent declines in J-code claims and beneficiary counts following closures but no detectable increase in ED use—even in ZIPs that lose their last chain pharmacy—suggesting beneficiaries substitute rather than flood acute care. A Rite Aid bankruptcy IV, heterogeneity analysis, and robustness checks support the null on ED utilization.

**Essential Points**

1. **Clarify the SNAp dimension and dual-service mechanism in the empirical tests.** The manifest frames the research question as about dual SNAP-pharmacy infrastructure losses, yet the analysis never empirically tests whether losing a SNAP-authorized chain pharmacy worsens both food and medication access. The paper should either (a) show that the closures studied indeed coincide with SNAP-authorized outlets (e.g., link SNAP retailer data to the ZIPs/NPI-level closures) and demonstrate the compound loss matters for outcomes, or (b) tone down the dual-service framing and focus solely on pharmacy access. As written, the policy narrative does not line up with the evidence.

2. **Justify the parallel trends more fully and explore potential differential selection.** The identification hinges on closures being exogenous, but chain-level restructuring decisions might still correlate with local Medicaid demand (e.g., stores closing where profitability or demand declined). The event study shows flat pre-trends, but treated and control ZIPs differ markedly (see Table 1). Please show (a) whether pre-trends hold within narrower strata (e.g., propensity-score matched ZIPs, or controlling for pre-period PC adjustments); (b) balance on observable covariates (Medicaid penetration, demographic indicators, prior pharmacy/ED levels); and (c) whether closures correlate with local economic shocks. Without this, the null on ED utilization could reflect endogenous treatment timing.

3. **Address the IV exclusion restriction and first-stage interpretation.** The Rite Aid bankruptcy instrument is appealing, but the exclusion restriction is strong: Rite Aid presence might proxy for pharmacies concentrated in certain geographies that differ in trends (e.g., more urban/suburban). The first-stage F-statistic is reported, but the reduced-form/IV results are mostly omitted in the main tables, and the interpretation (e.g., local average treatment effect) is unclear. Provide full first-stage, reduced-form, and IV tables, discuss the plausible exclusion threat (e.g., was Rite Aid closure correlated with local health policy changes or demand shocks), and consider alternative instruments (e.g., store-specific lease expiration schedules) or sensitivity analyses.

**Suggestions**

1. **Strengthen the dual-access narrative empirically.** Since the idea hinges on chain pharmacies being both SNAP retailers and Medicaid pharmacists, consider merging the SNAP Retailer Historical Database into the analysis. At minimum, report the share of closures that are SNAP-authorized, and—if feasible—exploit variation in the SNAP authorization status (e.g., comparing ZIPs that lose a SNAP-authorized chain pharmacy with those that lose a pharmacy-only outlet). This allows you to test whether losing dual service produces different outcomes than losing a pharmacy in isolation. If the SNAP dimension cannot be operationalized, revise the framing to focus squarely on pharmacy closures and their downstream health consequences, while still mentioning the overlap as motivation.

2. **Augment the DiD with richer controls or alternative estimators.** Because treatment timing is correlated with pharmacy demand, incorporate ZIP-specific trends or covariate interactions (e.g., pre-closure Medicaid enrollment trends, income, urbanicity). Use recent methods for staggered adoption (e.g., Callaway and Sant’Anna) to estimate group-time ATTs and report aggregated estimates that are robust to heterogeneity. These exercises will reassure the reader that the ED null is not driven by heterogeneous timing or residual correlation between closures and local trajectories.

3. **Disaggregate ED outcomes by diagnosis or payer.** The null finding on ED claims could mask heterogeneous responses: some conditions might spike while others decline. If data permit, split ED visits into drug-related versus non-drug-related ICD codes, or examine ED visits resulting in admissions versus discharges. Also, consider analyzing non-ED acute care (urgent care, hospitalizations) to understand whether beneficiaries shift to other acute settings. This would strengthen the interpretation that beneficiaries substitute rather than deteriorate.

4. **Explore the substitution channel explicitly.** The Discussion hypothesizes that patients transfer to independents or stockpile medications. Can you test this by tracking non-chain pharmacy billing in the ZIP? For example, if independent pharmacy claims rise after a chain exit, this supports substitution; if not, it raises risk of unmet need. Even a simple placebo test (e.g., do independent pharmacy claims increase following chain closures?) would make the mechanism more concrete.

5. **Extend the post-period where possible and discuss long-term effects.** The paper notes the 14-month maximum may be too short to capture delayed health consequences. Consider augmenting the panel (if feasible) or using administrative proxies (e.g., chronic-condition hospitalizations) with longer lags. At a minimum, perform a back-of-the-envelope calculation or simulation showing how long a delayed effect would need to be before it becomes detectable, to contextualize the null.

6. **Provide more detail on sample construction and robustness.** For transparency, describe how you match NPI names to chains, handle NPIs with multiple ZIP codes, and define closure (e.g., absence of billing for how many months). Present robustness tables showing alternative closure definitions (e.g., requiring a minimum of 3 months of zero billing) or different aggregation levels (e.g., census tract). Also clarify why only ZIPs with at least one chain pharmacy billing “in T-MSIS” enter the sample—does this exclude rural areas entirely?

7. **Report economic magnitudes relative to baseline.** The log estimates are large, but the summary statistics show wide dispersion. Convert the log effects into percentage changes (you already do) and express the ED confidence interval in terms of absolute claims (e.g., ED visits per 1,000 Medicaid enrollees) to make the null more tangible for policymakers.

8. **Clarify the IV interpretation and the Wu-Hausman result.** The paper states the Wu-Hausman rejects equality of OLS and IV, implying measurement error attenuation, but no table shows the OLS vs. IV comparison. Provide a table comparing OLS and IV coefficients for pharmacy and ED outcomes, along with the Hausman test statistic and p-value, so readers can assess whether the IV effect size materially differs.

9. **Address potential spillovers across ZIPs.** Pharmacies close at the ZIP level, but beneficiaries may drive to adjacent ZIPs. Consider controlling for neighboring ZIP pharmacy availability (e.g., using spatial lags or distance to nearest remaining chain). Alternatively, document that spillovers are limited (e.g., by showing the treatment effect decays with distance).

10. **Clarify the policy takeaway and limitations.** The paper currently interprets the null on ED use as reassurance, but acknowledges the possibility of delayed harm. In the conclusion, explicitly state that the findings apply to the 14-month window and to emergency department visits observable in T-MSIS; caution against overstating the null, and suggest directions for future work (e.g., linking to mortality or chronic disease registries).
