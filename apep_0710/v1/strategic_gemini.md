## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
This paper uses the Russian invasion of Ukraine as a shock to examine the resilience of public institutions. It employs a difference-in-discontinuities design around Ukraine’s e-procurement threshold (UAH 200,000) to show that while the formal requirement for competition collapsed post-invasion—especially in frontline regions—substantive price savings persisted, likely due to the "sunlight" of the underlying digital transparency.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this well in the first paragraph, but it leans heavily on the "unique setting." To be a true AER contender, the pitch should pivot from "Ukraine is a unique case" to "How do digital-first institutions mitigate the collapse of the rule of law?" 

**The pitch the paper should have:**
"Do modern, transparent institutions survive the ultimate stress test of total war? Leveraging a difference-in-discontinuities design in Ukraine, I show that while formal competitive mandates evaporate under conflict, the 'transparency residue' of e-procurement prevents a total collapse in fiscal efficiency. This reveals a critical distinction between formal procedural erosion and substantive institutional death in the digital age."

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**One-sentence contribution:** The paper identifies the causal erosion of formal procurement rules during conflict while finding unexpected resilience in substantive price outcomes due to radical transparency.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It differentiates well from the "peacetime threshold" literature (Coviello & Mariniello; Palguta & Pertold). It moves beyond "thresholds save money" to "shocks break thresholds."
*   **World vs. Literature:** It frames itself as answering a question about the world (institutional resilience), which is strong.
*   **The "Smart Economist" Test:** A colleague would say, "It’s a paper showing that even if you let guys skip auctions during a war, having the data public keeps them from totally robbing the till."
*   **Bigger Contribution:** To make this "AER big," the author needs to dig into the *firm-level* side. Are the same pre-war "good" firms winning the emergency contracts, or is the erosion driven by a shift to politically connected "war-profiteer" firms?

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
The paper sits at the intersection of **Public Procurement** (threshold effects) and **Development Economics** (institutions and conflict).

*   **Neighbors:** Coviello & Mariniello (2014); Palguta & Pertold (2017); Besley & Persson (2010); and Dell & Querubin (2018).
*   **Strategy:** It builds on the threshold papers but *attacks* the Besley & Persson "State Capacity" paradigm by suggesting that institutional quality isn't a single scalar—it can be bifurcated (formal rules vs. transparency norms).
*   **Missing Conversations:** The paper is surprisingly quiet on the **Political Economy of Transparency** (e.g., Casaburi and Troiano). Is transparency a substitute for enforcement when the state is distracted by war?

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC
*   **Setup:** Ukraine built "ProZorro," the gold standard of transparent procurement.
*   **Tension:** A full-scale invasion forces the suspension of those rules to prioritize speed over competition. Does the "shadow" of the system still work when the "teeth" of the law are removed?
*   **Resolution:** Formal competition drops by 16pp (75% of the premium), but price savings only drop by an insignificant 0.5pp.
*   **Implications:** Digital transparency is more robust than the legal mandates it supports.

**Evaluation:** The arc is exceptionally clear. It avoids being a "collection of results" by focusing on the disconnect between *procedure* and *outcome*.

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
*   **The Lead Fact:** "When Russia invaded, Ukraine let frontline mayors skip auctions, but they still didn't significantly overpay because the transactions were still posted online in real-time."
*   **Response:** People lean in. It’s a counter-intuitive "good news" story in a bleak setting.
*   **Follow-up:** "Wait, if they didn't overpay, why do we need the mandatory auctions in the first place?" (This is the "So What?" the paper needs to answer: is the premium for *savings* or for *legitimacy*?)

---

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
*   **Front-loading:** The "Triple-Diff" (frontline vs. rear) is the most interesting part and should be integrated earlier into the main narrative rather than treated as an extension.
*   **Appendix:** The bandwidth sensitivity is standard; keep it short.
*   **Mechanism Section:** The paper currently speculates in the conclusion (Section 6) about why savings didn't drop. This needs a dedicated Section 5.4. Use the bid data to show if the *distribution* of bids changed.

---

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The "AER Distance" is **Medium**. The science is sound, but the *ambition* is currently "good applied micro" rather than "general interest theory-testing."

**Single Most Impactful Advice:**
The author must prove the *mechanism* of the price-savings resilience. Is it because the "shadow of the future" (post-war accountability) keeps agents honest, or because the ProZorro API allows civil society (DOZORRO) to monitor them in real-time? Without identifying *why* the savings didn't collapse when the rules did, the paper is just an interesting observation.
