## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple, important question: when a state comes under extreme stress, do its market-design institutions keep working? Using Ukraine’s procurement threshold and the wartime suspension of normal rules, the paper studies whether a celebrated transparency reform, ProZorro, continued to induce competitive procurement after Russia’s 2022 invasion.

A busy economist should care because this is not really a paper about one procurement threshold in one country; it is a paper about whether formal rules retain bite during crisis, and about what “institutional resilience” means in a setting where hundreds of billions in public spending will pass through weakened state capacity.

### Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Not quite. The current opening is competent, but it leads with procurement’s share of GDP and the mechanics of the threshold before fully landing the bigger question. The paper’s strongest asset is not “another procurement-threshold RD,” but “a live test of whether a flagship anti-corruption institution survives war.” That should be unmistakable immediately.

### What the first two paragraphs should say instead

Something like:

> States build rules to make public spending competitive, transparent, and less corrupt. But the key question is not just whether those rules work in normal times; it is whether they survive crisis. Russia’s invasion of Ukraine created an unusually sharp test of institutional resilience: a country with one of the world’s most transparent e-procurement systems simultaneously faced existential military pressure and legal authorization to bypass normal competition.
>
> This paper studies whether ProZorro’s competitive discipline survived that shock. Exploiting Ukraine’s UAH 200,000 procurement threshold and the wartime suspension of above-threshold auction requirements, I show that the threshold’s effect on competitive procedure use collapsed after the invasion, especially in frontline regions, while pricing outcomes eroded much less. The core lesson is that war weakened formal compliance with competitive procurement rules more than it eliminated all competitive pressure—an important distinction for how economists think about institutional degradation and postwar reconstruction.

That is the pitch. It tells me the question, the setting, the empirical leverage, the main result, and why it matters.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper’s contribution is to show that armed conflict can sharply reduce the effective bite of procurement rules—measured at a statutory threshold—even within a highly transparent digital procurement system.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?

Only partially. The introduction cites the threshold-procurement papers and then says, in effect, “I do this in wartime.” That is a start, but not enough. The paper needs to sharpen how it differs along the dimension that matters:

- **Coviello and Mariniello / Coviello et al.** style papers: thresholds affect prices and competition in normal settings.
- **Palguta and Pertold-Gebicka** style papers: thresholds induce manipulation and procedural distortions.
- **Institutional resilience / conflict papers**: conflict weakens state capacity and institutions, usually in macro or historical ways.

This paper’s differentiator is not just “Ukraine + war.” It is: **a real-time, micro-level measure of institutional erosion using administrative data and a known rule discontinuity.** That is potentially interesting.

But the paper currently undersells a key problem: its strongest result is on **competitive procedure usage**, which is very close to the rule itself. So the contribution can sound like: “wartime emergency exemptions reduced the threshold’s effect on using competitive procedures.” That is true, but it risks sounding mechanical. The paper has to work harder to argue that this is measuring genuine institutional erosion rather than merely legal substitution.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a literature gap?

It is mixed, but drifts too often into literature-gap language: “No prior paper has studied procurement thresholds in a wartime context.” That is not a strong AER sentence. The stronger framing is about the world:

- Can high-transparency institutions maintain discipline under extreme shock?
- Does war destroy formal rule compliance, substantive market competition, or both?
- Is institutional erosion geographically concentrated where pressure is greatest?

Those are world questions. The introduction should live there.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?

Right now, they would probably say: “It’s a procurement-threshold paper for Ukraine showing wartime exemptions weakened competitive procurement.” That is okay, but not yet memorable.

You want them to say: “It uses the invasion to measure institutional resilience in real time and finds that formal procurement rules lose bite under war, especially at the front, even in a best-in-class transparency system.”

That is much better.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Several possibilities:

1. **Lean harder into the formal-vs-substantive distinction.**  
   This is the most promising path already in the paper. If war crushed formal procedure compliance but not pricing as much, that is interesting. The paper should foreground that contrast as the central puzzle, not leave it as an interpretive aside.

2. **Use outcomes that are less mechanically tied to the rule.**  
   Competitive procedure type is the cleanest institutional measure, but it is also the most proximate to the legal change. A bigger paper would show what happened to:
   - winning supplier concentration,
   - repeat contracting with the same vendors,
   - delivery speed,
   - contract amendments/renegotiations,
   - ex post performance or completion,
   - entry by new suppliers,
   - local vs nonlocal suppliers.
   
   Any of these would help establish whether wartime erosion changed market structure, not just forms.

3. **Reframe around resilience of digital transparency systems.**  
   The paper hints that transparency may preserve discipline even when formal auctions disappear. That is a much more distinctive and ambitious claim than “the threshold effect changed.”

4. **Connect more directly to reconstruction finance.**  
   If the paper can say something credible about where procurement discipline held and where it did not, that matters for how reconstruction money should be monitored. That broadens the audience.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

### Closest neighbors

The closest neighbors appear to be:

1. **Coviello and Mariniello (2014)** on public procurement and thresholds in Italy.
2. **Palguta and Pertold-Gebicka (2017)** on procurement thresholds/manipulation in the Czech Republic.
3. **Szucs (2017)** on procurement thresholds in Hungary.
4. **Bosio et al.** / broader public procurement and regulation literature.
5. On conflict and institutions: **Besley and Persson**, **Dell**, **Acemoglu et al.**-type work on state capacity, conflict, and institutional persistence.

### How should the paper position itself relative to those neighbors?

- **Build on** the procurement-threshold literature. Don’t pretend to overturn it.
- **Bridge to** the conflict/state-capacity literature.
- **Differentiate from** both by emphasizing micro, real-time measurement of rule erosion under shock.

The paper should not “attack” the procurement papers. It should say: those papers tell us what competitive rules do in normal times; this paper asks whether those rules remain operative when the state is under existential stress.

### Is the paper currently positioned too narrowly or too broadly?

At present, it is oddly both.

- **Too narrowly** as a procurement-threshold paper.
- **Too broadly** when it gestures at “institutional resilience under conflict” without fully delivering the broader conceptual stakes.

It needs a more disciplined bridge: this is a **micro paper with macro institutional relevance**. Say that clearly.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?

It should likely speak more to:

- **State capacity and public finance under crisis**
- **Bureaucratic compliance and rule enforcement**
- **Digital governance / e-government / transparency systems**
- **Corruption and monitoring under emergency procurement**
- **Reconstruction and fragile-state procurement**

Right now, it feels too anchored in the RD-threshold literature and too lightly connected to the political economy of institutions.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?

Almost, but not quite. The most impactful conversation is probably not “procurement thresholds in another setting.” It is:

> What parts of state capacity survive war, and what parts fail first?

Procurement is then the measurement domain. That is the right conversation.

An especially fruitful unexpected connection would be to the literature on **rules versus discretion in emergencies**. Wartime procurement is fundamentally about when bureaucratic discretion substitutes for formal competition, and whether transparency mitigates the damage. That is a richer conversation than the current one.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Ukraine built a highly regarded digital procurement system intended to force public contracting into open competition and reduce corruption.

### Tension

War creates exactly the conditions under which such rules are hardest to maintain: urgency, disruption, damaged capacity, and legal emergency exemptions. So does the institution survive, or does formal competition collapse?

### Resolution

The threshold’s effect on competitive procedures drops sharply after the invasion, with larger erosion in frontline areas. Pricing effects are much less dramatic.

### Implications

Formal compliance with procurement rules is fragile under extreme stress, but transparency may preserve some substantive discipline even when formal procedures are suspended. That matters for how to design crisis procurement and postwar reconstruction oversight.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the ingredients of one, but not a fully realized arc. Right now it still reads somewhat like a collection of threshold estimates plus wartime splits. The story is there, but the paper hasn’t fully committed to it.

### What story should it be telling?

It should be telling this story:

1. **Ukraine is a hard test case for institutional resilience because ProZorro was unusually transparent and rule-based.**
2. **War put that institution under direct pressure through both necessity and legal exemption.**
3. **The key empirical question is whether the institution lost formal bite, substantive bite, or both.**
4. **The answer is: mostly formal bite, especially near the front.**
5. **Therefore, digital transparency may be more resilient than formal procedural rules under crisis—but not fully.**

That is a coherent AER-style narrative. The current draft gestures at this but does not structure the paper tightly enough around it.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?

I would say:

> In Ukraine, a procurement threshold that used to strongly trigger competitive auction mostly stopped doing so after the invasion—especially in frontline regions—even though pricing outcomes deteriorated much less than procedure usage did.

That is the interesting fact.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

Some would lean in, but only if you say it the right way. If you pitch it as “an RD on a procurement threshold,” phones come out. If you pitch it as “a micro test of whether a flagship anti-corruption institution survives war,” people listen.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Immediately:

> “Okay, but is the result just mechanically about emergency legal exemptions, or does it show that procurement performance itself deteriorated?”

That is the central strategic issue for the paper. The current draft does not fully disarm this question because the main strong result is on procedure usage, while savings are modest and insignificant.

### Are the modest findings still interesting?

Potentially yes. The null-ish pricing result could be genuinely interesting if framed properly:

- It may imply that **transparency disciplines prices even when formal competition is relaxed**.
- Or that **the savings metric is too noisy in wartime to detect deterioration**.
- Or that **bureaucrats selectively used discretion when competitive procedures were infeasible, not simply to overpay**.

But the paper needs to make the case that this is a substantive insight rather than a failed attempt to find price effects. Right now the “informal competitive pressure” interpretation is plausible, but still speculative.

The paper would be much stronger if it explicitly centered this as the main puzzle: **why does formal competition collapse without an equally large collapse in observed savings?**

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

### What is the gap?

Primarily, this is a **framing and ambition problem**, with some **scope** concerns.

- **Framing problem:** The paper currently presents itself as a procurement-threshold study in wartime. That is publishable somewhere, but not enough for AER.
- **Ambition problem:** The truly big question is institutional resilience under extreme stress. The paper touches this but doesn’t fully own it.
- **Scope problem:** The evidence is strongest on a proximate institutional margin and weaker on substantive economic outcomes. That leaves the contribution feeling narrower than the title promises.
- **Novelty problem:** Moderate, not fatal. The setting is novel; the design is familiar. So the story has to carry more weight.

### Be honest: how far is it from exciting the top 10 people in the field?

At present, medium-to-far. The raw ingredients are better than the current draft makes them look, but the paper in its current form still feels like a competent application rather than a must-read.

The top people will ask:
- Is this measuring institutional erosion or just emergency legal discretion?
- What survived: transparency, competition, prices, entry?
- What is the general lesson beyond Ukraine?

Until the paper answers those questions more directly, it will not feel like an AER paper.

### Single most impactful advice

**Reframe the paper around the distinction between formal rule erosion and substantive market discipline under crisis, and make that contrast—not the threshold design itself—the centerpiece of the contribution.**

That is the move. If the paper can persuade readers that war disabled formal procurement rules but transparency preserved some competitive discipline, then it has a real idea. If not, it remains “another threshold paper, this time during war.”
