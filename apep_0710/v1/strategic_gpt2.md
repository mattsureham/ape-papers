This paper has a potentially AER-relevant setting and a real-world question of obvious importance, but in its current form it reads more like a competent applied micro note than a field-defining paper. The core issue is not that the topic is unimportant; it is that the paper has not yet decided whether it is about procurement rules, institutional resilience under extreme stress, or wartime state capacity. Until it chooses, the contribution will feel smaller than the setting deserves.

## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether a major state institution—competitive public procurement—continues to function during war. Using Ukraine’s procurement threshold and the sudden suspension of normal rules after the Russian invasion, it studies whether the price and competition advantages of mandatory auctions disappeared under wartime conditions, and finds that the formal competitive-procedure margin eroded sharply, especially near the front.

A busy economist should care because this is not really about one procurement platform; it is about whether rules-based state capacity survives extreme shocks, and whether transparency can preserve some discipline even when formal legal requirements collapse.

### Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?
Not quite. The current introduction gets the ingredients on the table, but it leads with procurement’s GDP share and threshold mechanics before making the bigger claim about institutional resilience under war. The paper currently sounds like “an RD procurement paper in Ukraine during wartime” before it sounds like “a paper about how war degrades state capacity in real time.” That is the wrong order for AER positioning.

### What the first two paragraphs should say instead
The introduction should open with the world question, not the design:

> Modern states rely on rules-based procurement to convert public money into goods and infrastructure. But we know remarkably little about whether these institutions survive extreme stress: when war arrives, do governments temporarily bend procurement rules while preserving discipline, or does conflict rapidly erode the competitive foundations of state capacity?
>
> This paper studies that question in Ukraine, where the Russian invasion hit one of the world’s most transparent e-procurement systems. I use the statutory threshold that normally mandates open competitive auctions and the wartime suspension of those rules to measure how the “competition premium” of procurement changed after February 2022. The main finding is stark: the legal bite of competition rules falls sharply in wartime, especially in frontline regions, but price discipline appears to erode much less than formal procedure use—suggesting that transparency may preserve some market discipline even when formal institutions weaken.

That is the pitch. The institutional detail and threshold design can come immediately after.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### Contribution in one sentence
The paper’s contribution is to show that armed conflict weakens the effective enforcement of competitive procurement rules in real time, with the erosion concentrated in exposed regions and larger on formal procedure use than on realized price savings.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?
Only partially. The paper names the procurement-threshold RD literature and then says “no prior paper has studied procurement thresholds in a wartime context.” That is true but not enough. “Same design, new context” is rarely a top-journal contribution by itself. The paper needs sharper differentiation along one of two dimensions:

1. **Relative to procurement RD papers:** this is not just another threshold paper; it studies when threshold-induced competition ceases to bind under institutional stress.
2. **Relative to conflict/state-capacity papers:** this is not another long-run conflict persistence paper; it gives high-frequency, within-country evidence on the immediate deformation of state capacity during war.

Right now those two comparisons are present, but neither is developed strongly enough.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a gap in a literature?
It is mixed, but still too literature-gap oriented. “No prior paper has studied procurement thresholds in a wartime context” is a literature claim. The stronger world claim is: **When the state is under existential stress, what parts of rules-based governance break first, and what parts remain surprisingly resilient?** That is the question the paper should own.

### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what's new?
At present, they would probably say: “It’s a diff-in-disc paper on Ukrainian procurement showing the auction threshold mattered less after the invasion.” That is accurate but not memorable.

What you want them to say is: “It shows that war sharply eroded the enforceability of competitive procurement rules, but not necessarily all price discipline, implying that transparency and formal competition are separable dimensions of institutional resilience.”

That is a stronger and more conceptually portable contribution.

### What would make this contribution bigger?
Most importantly, the paper needs to elevate the distinction between **formal institutional erosion** and **substantive market discipline** from a speculative aside to the central conceptual contribution.

Specific ways to make it bigger:
- **Mechanism:** Lean much harder into the contrast between procedure type, bidder participation, and prices. If formal competition collapses but savings do not, the paper should be explicitly about which margins of institutionality matter under stress.
- **Comparison:** Compare wartime erosion not just across frontline/rear regions, but across categories where urgency is inherently higher vs lower, or standardized goods vs complex procurement. That would speak directly to whether emergency necessity or institutional opportunism drives the effects.
- **Outcome expansion:** Add outcomes that more directly capture procurement quality or corruption risk if available: repeat winners, contract concentration, supplier entry, time-to-award, amendments, or ex post completion proxies. This would move the paper from “rules changed” toward “the functioning of public markets changed.”
- **Framing shift:** Make the central result about the decomposition of state capacity into legal form, market competition, and transparency—not about one threshold discontinuity per se.

### How should the paper position itself relative to those neighbors?
It should **build on** the procurement threshold literature, not merely cite it as a methodological predecessor. And it should **connect to** the conflict/state-capacity literature by arguing that procurement is a concrete, measurable margin of state capacity.

The strongest move is synthesis:
- Procurement threshold papers tell us what competition rules do in normal times.
- Conflict/state-capacity papers tell us institutions matter under stress but usually with long-run or coarse measures.
- This paper combines them to show, in real time and at transaction level, which part of a state institution degrades under war.

That is a much better position than “first wartime procurement threshold paper.”

### Is the paper currently positioned too narrowly or too broadly?
Too narrowly in method, too broadly in implication. It spends more space than necessary signaling the design lineage, while making very large claims about institutional erosion without enough conceptual scaffolding. It needs a narrower conceptual claim than “first causal evidence on how armed conflict degrades procurement institutions” if it cannot show multiple dimensions of degradation; but it also needs broader framing than “the threshold effect fell.”

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?
It seems under-engaged with:
- The empirical literature on **state capacity measured through administrative performance**
- The literature on **e-governance and transparency systems**
- The literature on **public procurement and corruption risk indicators**
- Potentially literature on **organizational resilience** or **bureaucratic adaptation under crisis**

Speaking only to procurement-RD papers leaves too much value on the table.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?
Not yet. The paper is currently having a conversation with a narrow procurement micro audience, while occasionally gesturing at institutions under conflict. The more impactful conversation is: **What happens to rules-based state capacity during acute shocks, and can transparency substitute for formal enforcement?** That is the conversation that could travel across public finance, development, political economy, and macro-institutions.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Before this paper, we know competitive procurement rules can lower prices and increase competition in normal settings, and we know conflict can weaken state capacity. But we have little direct evidence on how a functioning, modern administrative institution changes at the moment of wartime stress.

### Tension
War creates an ambiguity. On one hand, emergency procurement flexibility may be necessary for speed and survival; on the other, those same exemptions may dismantle the very competitive discipline that procurement reforms were built to create. Which happened in Ukraine?

### Resolution
The legal/procedural bite of the procurement threshold fell sharply after the invasion, especially in frontline areas. Yet the price-savings margin eroded less clearly, suggesting a divergence between formal procedural compliance and substantive market discipline.

### Implications
This matters for how economists think about institutional resilience, for how donors and governments should design procurement during emergencies, and for Ukraine’s reconstruction architecture. It suggests that some institutional components fail faster than others, and that transparency may be a partial substitute for formal competition when crisis suspends rules.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
It has the raw materials for one, but it is not fully executed. At present, it still feels somewhat like a collection of standard empirical sections wrapped around a good setting. The paper tells you what it estimated and what it found, but less clearly what the central conceptual takeaway is.

### What story should it be telling?
The best story is:

**War does not simply destroy institutions wholesale; it selectively erodes some margins of state capacity faster than others. In Ukraine’s procurement system, formal competitive rules weakened sharply, but transparency may have preserved some pricing discipline.**

That is a real story. It has setup, tension, surprise, and implications. The paper should be organized around that decomposition.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?
“After the Russian invasion, the legal threshold that normally forced Ukrainian public contracts into competitive auctions lost most of its bite—especially near the front—but prices did not deteriorate nearly as much as procedure use did.”

That is an interesting sentence.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
Lean in, initially. The setting is salient and the institutional question is important. But the next thirty seconds matter. If the follow-up is just “I estimate a diff-in-disc around the 200,000 UAH threshold,” attention will drop. If instead the follow-up is “it suggests transparency may preserve some discipline even when formal competition collapses,” people stay engaged.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Almost certainly: **If formal competition collapsed but prices did not, what actually kept discipline in place?**  
That is the paper’s most promising question—and right now it is not fully answered.

A second likely question: **Is this about emergency necessity or about institutional backsliding/opportunism?**  
The current paper points toward an answer but does not yet convert that into the paper’s central interpretive payoff.

### If findings are null or modest
The non-result on price savings can still be interesting, but only if framed correctly. As written, it risks feeling like “we expected savings erosion but did not quite get it.” That is weak. The paper should instead argue affirmatively that this divergence is itself the main fact: formal institutions and economic discipline are not the same thing, and war can decouple them.

That reframing turns a modest result into a conceptually interesting one.

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

### Without rewriting the paper, what would make it read better?

   The threshold-RD lineage does not need to occupy so much prime real estate in the introduction. One concise paragraph is enough.

3. **Front-load the main empirical fact earlier.**
   The reader should learn within the first two pages that the threshold’s effect on competitive procedure use falls by roughly two-thirds after the invasion, with stronger erosion in frontline areas and weaker movement in savings. That pattern is the hook.

4. **Elevate the formal-vs-substantive erosion distinction into the main results architecture.**
   This should not appear as a post hoc interpretation after the main table; it should structure the results section itself.

5. **Shorten institutional background.**
   It is clear and useful, but longer than necessary for a top-journal audience. Much of the legal detail can be compressed or moved.

6. **Cut or soften “first causal evidence” language.**
   It reads overstated and invites skepticism. Better to say the paper provides “transaction-level evidence” or “real-time evidence” on institutional erosion under conflict.

7. **The conclusion should do more than summarize.**
   The best paragraph in the conclusion is the one about formal versus substantive erosion. Expand that insight and trim generic recap.

8. **Remove anything that signals “V1” or prototype status.**
   Phrases like “exceeds the scope of this V1” are damaging in a serious submission. They tell the editor the paper is unfinished.

9. **The autonomous-generation acknowledgement is a strategic problem.**
   Even setting aside any editorial concerns, it makes the paper feel like a demonstration artifact rather than a polished scholarly contribution. That does not help AER positioning.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The gap is mostly **framing plus ambition**, with some **scope** issues.

- **Framing problem:** The paper has a better question than the introduction suggests.
- **Ambition problem:** It settles too quickly for showing that one threshold effect shrank after war.
- **Scope problem:** To be AER-level, it likely needs a richer decomposition of what exactly eroded—formal rules, competition, prices, supplier behavior, or all of the above.
- **Novelty problem:** “Threshold RD in a dramatic setting” is not enough on its own.

If the paper stays at current scope, it feels more like a strong field-journal paper in public economics/development/political economy than an AER paper. To move upward, it needs to become the paper economists cite when they want an example of **how crisis deforms state capacity in real time**.

### Single most impactful advice
**Reframe the paper around the divergence between formal rule erosion and substantive market discipline, and make that divergence—not the threshold design—the central contribution.**

That is the move that could change how the paper is perceived.
