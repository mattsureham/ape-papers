1. **Idea Fidelity**
The paper deviates significantly from the Original Idea Manifest in three critical dimensions: identification strategy, temporal scope, and sample size. First, the manifest proposed a spatial Regression Discontinuity Design (RDD) using distance to the HUC-12 divide (1–5km bandwidth) combined with a staggered Difference-in-Differences (DiD) design exploiting biennial listing cycles (1998–2024). The submitted paper implements a cross-sectional comparison with HUC-8 fixed effects, omitting both the spatial bandwidth restriction and the temporal variation in listing status. Second, the manifest confirmed data availability for 100,000+ facilities; the paper analyzes only 2,276 facilities (major permits only), without explaining the attrition from the proposed universe. Third, the manifest emphasized "staggered temporal variation" as a key identifier; the paper treats listing status as static (2024 cycle). These deviations weaken the causal claims relative to the proposed design.

2. **Summary**
This paper investigates whether Clean Water Act Section 303(d) impairment listing improves facility compliance by comparing facilities in listed versus non-listed HUC-12 subwatersheds within the same HUC-8 watershed. Using cross-sectional data from the 2024 reporting cycle, the author finds a precise null effect: listing status does not reduce violation rates for major NPDES dischargers. The result suggests that for facilities already under NPDES scrutiny, the additional regulatory label of 303(d) listing provides no incremental enforcement bite.

3. **Essential Points**
1.  **Identification Strategy Deviation (RDD vs. FE):** The core contribution claimed in the manifest was the "ridgeline discontinuity" (spatial RDD), which relies on the assumption that facilities very close to a topographic divide are similar except for regulatory assignment. The submitted paper abandons the distance-to-boundary running variable in favor of HUC-8 fixed effects. This is a substantial downgrade: HUC-8 units are large (~700 sq miles), and facilities in listed vs. non-listed HUC-12s within an HUC-8 may differ systematically (e.g., downstream vs. upstream, industrial zoning). Without the spatial bandwidth restriction, the "as-good-as-random" assignment claim is untenable.
2.  **Omission of Temporal Variation (DiD):** The manifest specified a staggered DiD design to exploit the biennial listing cycle. This would allow for facility fixed effects, controlling for time-invariant unobservables (e.g., facility age, technology). The cross-sectional approach cannot distinguish whether facilities in listed watersheds are inherently more prone to violations (selection) rather than responding to the listing. The null result may reflect an inability to control for this selection rather than a true lack of effect.
3.  **Sample Selection and Power:** The sample shrinks from the proposed 100,000+ facilities to 2,276. While the author restricts to "major" permits, the manifest did not specify this exclusion. Furthermore, the standard error (0.016) allows us to rule out only relatively large effects. Given the high variance in compliance data, the study may be underpowered to detect the modest regulatory impacts typical of environmental enforcement, making the "precise null" claim potentially overstated.

4. **Suggestions**
The paper addresses a policy-relevant question with a creative conceptual hook, but the empirical execution does not yet match the ambition of the design. The following recommendations aim to align the analysis with the original identification strategy and strengthen the causal inference.

    *   **Implement the Spatial RDD as Proposed:** The manifest's strongest feature was the use of distance to the HUC-12 boundary as a running variable. I strongly recommend reverting to this design. Plot violation rates against distance to the ridgeline for listed vs. non-listed sides. Apply the 1–5km bandwidth specified in the manifest. This would restore the credibility of the "local randomization" assumption. Currently, comparing any facility in a listed HUC-12 to any in a non-listed HUC-12 within an HUC-8 invites selection bias (e.g., facilities cluster downstream where pollution accumulates). A discontinuity design would mitigate this.

    *   **Recover the Temporal Dimension (DiD):** The manifest confirmed 13+ waves of data (1998–2024). Discarding this temporal variation loses the ability to include facility fixed effects. Even if the full time series is noisy, a staggered DiD estimator (e.g., Callaway and Sant'Anna, 2021) would allow you to control for time-invariant facility heterogeneity. If listing *does* have an effect, it should appear as a change in trend post-listing. If the null persists in a DiD framework, the finding is much more robust. If you must stick to cross-sectional data, you must explicitly discuss the limitation regarding time-invariant unobservables.

    *   **Clarify Sample Construction and Power:** The reduction from 100,000+ potential facilities to 2,276 requires explanation. Is this due to geocoding failures, missing HUC-12 assignments, or the "major permit" restriction? If the latter, justify why minor permits (which may be more sensitive to regulatory changes) are excluded. Additionally, provide a Minimum Detectable Effect (MDE) calculation. Given the standard error of 0.016 and mean violation rate of 0.45, what magnitude of effect can you rule out? If the MDE is larger than typical regulatory impacts found in literature (e.g., Greenstone et al. 2012), the null result is less informative.

    *   **Refine the Mechanism Discussion:** The paper suggests "regulatory redundancy" as a key mechanism—that NPDES already covers these facilities. However, 303(d) listing triggers TMDLs, which *should* tighten permit limits upon renewal. The cross-sectional data cannot capture permit renewal cycles. If the data allows, check whether permit limits (effluent limits) actually differ between listed and non-listed facilities in your sample. If limits are identical, the "redundancy" hypothesis is strengthened. If limits are tighter but violations are unchanged, enforcement is the issue. This distinction is vital for policy implications.

    *   **Address Spatial Correlation:** Water quality and enforcement are spatially correlated. Even with HUC-8 FE, spatial autocorrelation in the error term could bias standard errors. Use spatial HAC standard errors or cluster at a higher geographic level (e.g., HUC-6) to ensure inference is robust. The current clustering at HUC-8 may be insufficient given the spatial nature of the treatment.

This visually demonstrates whether the "ridgeline" logic holds empirically. If there is no visible jump at the boundary in the raw data, the regression result is less surprising.
