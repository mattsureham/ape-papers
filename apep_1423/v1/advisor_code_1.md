**Summary**

The paper studies whether CWA 303(d) impaired-waters listing, which triggers TMDL development and tighter permit scrutiny, has any incremental effect on point-source compliance. Using facilities located in HUC-8 watersheds that contain both listed and non-listed HUC-12 subwatersheds, the author reports a precise null: the within-HUC-8 difference in violation rates (13-quarter share of quarters with a violation) between listed and non-listed sides is statistically and economically negligible. The conclusion is that listing, at least for major NPDES dischargers, does not alter compliance behavior beyond baseline monitoring.

**Essential Points**

1. **Geographic discontinuity needs to be clearer.** The identification claim rests on the “ridgeline discontinuity,” yet the empirical work drops all spatial distance information and compares all facilities within the same HUC-8. HUC-8 units average 700 square miles and can contain very different economic and hydrological subregions; merely controlling for HUC-8 FE may not eliminate confounding. For a genuine discontinuity design, the comparison should be limited to facilities close to the shared HUC-12 boundary (e.g., within 1–5 km). Without restricting to nearby pairs, the design amounts to conventional HUC-12 level fixed effects, leaving room for systematic differences between listed and non-listed subwatersheds that correlate with violations. The authors need to demonstrate that the ridgeline discontinuity holds locally (via distance-to-boundary controls or RD plots) or explain why broader HUC-8 variation is sufficient.

2. **Timing and reverse causality remain unresolved.** The outcome and treatment are measured contemporaneously (2024 ATTAINS listing versus the 13-quarter compliance rate). Yet a facility’s violations plausibly contribute to the listing decision; the paper acknowledges this but offers only qualitative arguments. To claim causality, the authors must exploit the biennial listing cycle: use lagged listings or establish that facilities on one side of the boundary could not have mechanically caused listing differences. An event-study or leads/lags analysis around the listing date would help. Without addressing this, the null could simply reflect the fact that violations and listings move together because violations help produce listings, not because listings affect violations.

3. **Limited evidence on the mechanism.** The central claim is that listing adds no enforcement bite beyond existing NPDES monitoring. Yet the analysis only shows a null in violations. It is unclear whether permit limits, monitoring frequency, or inspections actually change across the boundary. The paper should provide evidence that facilities in listed subwatersheds indeed experience the promised “tighter limits and enhanced scrutiny” (perhaps via permit data) to validate that the treatment is implemented. Otherwise the null could stem from listing not being enforcement-meaningful rather than from facilities ignoring an enforcement signal.

**Suggestions**

1. **Implement a proper spatial-discontinuity check.** Supplement the current HUC-8 fixed-effects regression with specifications that are explicitly local to the watershed boundary. Construct a measure of distance to the nearest HUC-12 ridge and estimate effects separately for facilities within, say, 1 km, 3 km, and 5 km of the boundary. This would mirror standard border-discontinuity studies and would strengthen the claim that the differences are driven by ridgelines rather than larger HUC-12 heterogeneity. A plot of violation rates versus distance to the boundary (on both sides) would visually demonstrate the presence or absence of a discontinuity.

2. **Leverage time-series variation in listing status.** The manifest emphasized staggered listings across the biennial ATTAINS cycles, but the current implementation is cross-sectional. Reconstruct facility-level panel data aligning facility violations with listing status in each assessment year, so that establishments appear as “treated” only after their subwatershed is listed. An event-study around the listing year would show whether compliance behavior changes following listing, while also letting you test for pre-trends. This would mitigate concerns about reverse causality and help quantify lagged treatment effects.

3. **Expand the outcome space and mechanisms.** In addition to violation rates, consider other outcomes that should respond to listing if it has any bite: permit limit tightening (change in effluent limits), additional monitoring frequency, number of inspections, or penalties issued. Showing that these intermediate steps occur strengthens the argument that the treatment is real. If facilities’ permits do not change post-listing, documenting that fact is itself an important result and would reshape the interpretation away from facilities ignoring enforcement to regulators failing to translate listings into permit upgrades.

4. **Control for facility characteristics and upstream pollution.** Since different industries may cluster in different subwatersheds, introduce controls for facility type (e.g., SIC/NAICS codes, wastewater treatment plant vs. industrial) and size where available. Additionally, use upstream pollution/starvation measures—perhaps the density of upstream facilities or land use composition—to ensure that the within-HUC-8 comparison is not picking up systematic differences in the sources of pollution feeding into each subwatershed. Balance tests on observable characteristics can further demonstrate that the treated and control facilities are comparable near the boundary.

5. **Assess heterogeneous effects by enforcement apparatus.** The null result may mask differences across states or facility types. For example, smaller states or states with weaker enforcement may rely more on 303(d) listings as leverage, so the effect could be larger there. Similarly, minor permits may respond differently than majors. Present heterogeneous estimates (e.g., by state enforcement budget, facility size, or pollutant type) to assess whether the null is universal or concentrated.

6. **Clarify the functional interpretation of the null.** The interpretation that “listing adds a label but not a lever” would be more persuasive if backed by regulatory documents or interviews showing that TMDLs and permit revisions are slow and do not automatically tighten limits. Citing case studies where TMDLs were developed but permit limits remained unchanged would contextualize the null. This would help policymakers understand whether the solution is to accelerate permit revisions or to find alternative enforcement tools.

7. **Discuss external validity and policy implications carefully.** The sample is limited to major permits near boundaries within HUC-8 units containing both listed and non-listed waters. Explain how representative these facilities are of the broader universe of dischargers. Are there systematic differences (e.g., more industrial, more urban) that limit generalization? Also, unpack what a null means for non-major or unpermitted dischargers, which may face less baseline scrutiny. This will help readers understand whether the result should temper expectations about 303(d) listing more broadly or primarily for the ridgetop-delineated major point sources you study.

8. **Report power calculations / minimum detectable effects.** The paper asserts that the standard error rules out meaningful effects, but a brief power analysis (e.g., what is the smallest violation-rate change detectable with the given sample and clustering) would make the null more interpretable. This is especially important because the result is a precise null; readers need assurance that the study was well-powered to detect a policy-relevant effect.

9. **Engage with alternative interpretations.** One could argue that facilities respond to listing, but the effect materializes via noncompliance types not captured in ECHO (e.g., discharge composition) or over longer horizons than 13 quarters. While acknowledging these possibilities, the authors could cite supporting evidence or explain why they are unlikely. Doing so would strengthen the conclusion that the null reflects actual regulatory ineffectiveness rather than measurement limitations.

Overall, the paper tackles an important question with an innovative idea, but it needs to buttress the identification with more localized spatial evidence, leverage temporal variation, and connect the treatment to concrete changes in the regulatory environment. Addressing these suggestions would clarify whether the ridgeline discontinuity truly identifies the causal effect of 303(d) listing.
