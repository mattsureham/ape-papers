**Idea Fidelity**

The paper diverges substantially from the idea outlined in the manifest. Rather than estimating the intended continuous-treatment DiD using local-authority-level tooth extraction counts from the 2015/16–2024/25 Hospital Episode Statistics and exploiting IMD scores as proxies for pre-SDIL sugar exposure, the paper instead models biennial prevalence of dental decay (not carious extractions) from the National Dental Epidemiology Programme (NDEP) across 156 upper-tier LAs from 2007/08–2023/24. The key empirical focus is a null test of differential convergence in decay rates rather than estimating a reformulation “dose-response” from variation in pre-policy sugar exposure via extraction rates. Several manifest elements are missing: no analysis of extraction counts, no mention of mechanism testing via reformulation shares, and no exploration of non-caries extractions as falsification. The data, treatment definition, and policy question in the paper shift from the manifest. As such, fidelity is low.

**Summary**

The paper evaluates whether the UK Soft Drinks Industry Levy differentially reduced childhood dental decay in more deprived local authorities by interacting a post-SDIL indicator with the standardized IMD 2019 score in a two-way fixed effects panel of NDEP decay prevalence. The estimates are consistently small and statistically insignificant, and event-study coefficients indicate that the deprivation gradient was converging prior to the policy, undermining the identifying assumption. The author interprets the null as evidence that the reformulation dividend was distributionally uniform and that convergence is driven by secular trends rather than the levy.

**Essential Points**

1. **Identification fails because the policy variation is absent.** The SDIL is national, and the paper relies solely on cross-sectional IMD variation to proxy for “dose.” If IMD captures long-standing differences in multiple confounders, and those differences were already evolving (as the paper’s own event study shows), the identifying assumption that the difference between high- and low-deprivation areas would have been stable absent the levy is implausible. This is not just a pre-trend concern—it undermines the core variation. A credible design would require a convincing argument (and empirical evidence) that the post-2018 interaction term isolates the reformulation shock rather than ongoing convergence; the current evidence (pre-trend coefficients larger than treatment) suggests the opposite. Without a valid counterfactual, the estimation cannot distinguish reformulation effects from secular convergence.

2. **Mismatch between treatment proxy and substantive mechanism.** The manifest intended to link IMD to pre-SDIL sugar exposure through extraction data; here, IMD only proxies deprivation but the paper does not demonstrate that higher-IMD areas indeed had higher exposure to levy-liable drinks that reformulated. The treatment is therefore a broad deprivation score rather than a sugar-specific exposure, and any differential effect could be confounded by numerous correlated trends (e.g., access to dental care, fluoridation, targeted health programs). A credible design requires additional evidence that the “dose” variation captures the reformulation channel rather than other time-varying deprivation-related factors.

3. **Outcome choice and sample limit comparability to policy target.** The manifest aimed to use extraction counts, the outcome that prior ITS studies identified as responding to the SDIL. Using biennial decay prevalence, particularly of five-year-olds, changes the interpretation: the NDEP measured children’s decay at one point in time, with survey coverage changing over waves and potential measurement error. Moreover, the sample is unbalanced and limited to data through 2023/24, with only three post-treatment waves, reducing power to detect changes. The paper needs to justify why this outcome better captures the reformulation dividend, and why the panel of 156 LAs suffices to estimate a fairly small differential effect.

Given these issues, the paper currently cannot establish the intended causal claim. Addressing them likely requires more substantial restructuring; if not, the paper should be rejected until a plausibly identified strategy is presented.

**Suggestions**

- **Revisit the treatment variation.** To credibly interpret IMD × Post as reformulation exposure, provide direct evidence that ILAs with higher IMD scores consumed more levy-liable drinks prior to 2018 and that reformulation affected those drinks uniformly. This could involve, for example, linking IMD to survey data on SSB consumption or to IMS sales data, and showing that the reformulation-induced sugar reduction was larger (or more consequential) for high-IMD areas. Without this, the treatment is just deprivation and absorbs many confounders.

- **Strengthen pre-trend assessment and consider alternative specifications.** The paper already reports an event study showing convergence before the policy, but no specification adequately addresses it. Consider estimating the effect using methods designed for convergence: (i) interact IMD with local-authority specific linear trends in the pre-period only and then test for post-period breaks; (ii) implement a “late adoption” comparison by leveraging any local variation in SDIL anticipation or composition if available; (iii) apply methods such as Rambachan and Roth’s sensitivity analysis for differential trends to quantify how much trend variation needs to be attributed to the policy for the estimate to change sign. This would provide a more nuanced picture of whether the observed null is informative.

- **Justify and/or broaden the outcome measure.** If the goal is to speak to the manifest’s emphasis on extractions, consider incorporating extraction counts as an alternative (perhaps in an appendix) and show whether the results differ. If decay prevalence is used, explain how measurement error, survey coverage changes, and the biennial nature of the data affect the estimates. Specifically, clarify whether the outcome captures cumulative exposure to sugar reduction during the relevant period (ages 0–5). Additionally, explore whether more frequent (annual) proxies or aggregated hospital data could improve power and validity.

- **Address potential confounders that vary with IMD over time.** Since IMD is time-invariant, any time-varying policy or socioeconomic improvement correlated with deprivation could bias the interaction. To address this, control for time-varying covariates that might plausibly affect caries trends differently by deprivation (e.g., local dental service capacity, fluoridation implementation, public health funding). Alternatively, include interactions between year fixed effects and other pre-trend indicators to soak up differential trends unrelated to the SDIL.

- **Provide a clearer link to the manifest’s policy question.** The original idea emphasized the supply-side reformulation dividend observed in hospital extractions. Even if the current data differ, explicitly argue how these decay prevalence results complement or challenge that literature. For instance, you could compare the magnitude of aggregate decline from ITS studies to the uniform change implied by this DiD and discuss why distributional claims were plausible in the first place. This would help readers place the null within the larger evidence base.

- **Expand the discussion of power and interpretation of null.** The paper reports a minimum detectable effect, but it would be helpful to contextualize whether this threshold is policy relevant and to explore Bayesian bounds or confidence intervals for effect heterogeneity (e.g., the implied maximum change in the deprivation gap consistent with the data). This would make the null more informative and less likely to be dismissed as purely inconclusive.

- **Clarify the sample and estimation details.** The paper should provide a table of LA-level characteristics, explain how missing waves are handled, and describe whether the same cohort of children is tracked. Additionally, the permutation test is a useful robustness check but could be made more informative by explaining its interpretation in the presence of only 156 units.

Overall, the paper raises an important question but requires a more rigorous connection between the reformulation mechanism, the treatment intensity variation, and the outcome dynamics. Strengthening these components would increase confidence that the null result reflects the policy rather than pre-existing trends or measurement artifacts.
