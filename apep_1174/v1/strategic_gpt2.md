## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple and policy-relevant question: under U.S. environmental “cooperative federalism,” do pollution outcomes depend on whether a facility is inspected by the EPA or by a state agency? Using nationwide inspection records linked to facility emissions, the paper’s central claim is that federal inspectors do not appear to reduce toxic releases more than state inspectors, suggesting that who inspects matters less than whether inspection happens at all.

A busy economist should care because this is really a paper about whether decentralization changes real regulatory outcomes. The Clean Air Act’s enforcement architecture is a canonical case of shared authority, and the paper tries to say something broader: when multiple layers of government enforce the same rules, are differences in institutional incentives consequential for the world?

### Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Mostly yes, but not quite sharply enough. The current introduction is competent and readable, but it spends too much of its early energy on institutional detail and normative stakes before crystallizing the core empirical surprise. The paper’s first two paragraphs should get faster to the punchline: economists think incentives differ across federal and state enforcers, but we do not know whether those differences actually affect emissions.

### The pitch the paper should have

“American environmental regulation relies on a basic institutional gamble: states do most enforcement, while the EPA retains the power to step in. If state regulators are systematically weaker than federal regulators, this delegation should show up in higher pollution at facilities inspected by states rather than the EPA. Using nationwide Clean Air Act inspection records linked to facility emissions, this paper asks whether regulator identity changes pollution outcomes—and finds little evidence that it does. The result suggests that the key margin is inspection itself, not whether the inspector carries a federal or state badge.”

That is the AER-facing version: a big institutional question, a concrete empirical setting, and a result that changes how we think about decentralization.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper provides evidence that, in Clean Air Act enforcement, federal inspectors do not generate meaningfully different facility-level pollution outcomes than state inspectors.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3-4 papers?

Only partially. The paper cites adjacent enforcement papers, but the differentiation is still thinner than it needs to be. Right now the contribution risks sounding like: “another enforcement paper showing either small or null effects using administrative data.” The distinct angle is not “I estimate an effect of inspections on pollution”; it is “I isolate whether the identity of the enforcer matters within a shared regulatory regime.” That distinction is potentially interesting, but the paper does not yet make it feel like a major departure from prior work on inspections, deterrence, and environmental compliance.

It especially needs to separate itself from:
- papers on whether inspections matter at all,
- papers on whether penalties matter,
- papers on whether decentralization is desirable in theory,
- papers on audit design in other countries.

The true differentiator is not enforcement per se; it is comparative institutional effectiveness within a cooperative federalist structure.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a gap in a literature?

It is mostly framed as a world question, which is good. The strongest framing is: does decentralizing enforcement to states actually change pollution outcomes? That is much stronger than: “there is no quasi-experimental estimate of the federal-state enforcement gap.” The latter is a literature gap; the former is a first-order question about institutional design.

The paper should lean even harder into the world question.

### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what’s new?

Maybe, but not confidently enough. Right now they might say: “It’s a DiD/IV paper on EPA versus state inspections and finds no effect on TRI releases.” That is not yet a memorable contribution.

The introduction should make the novelty legible in plain English:
- not whether regulation matters,
- not whether inspections matter,
- but whether the level of government enforcing the same law matters.

That is the sentence people will remember.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Several things could enlarge the contribution materially:

1. **A better outcome variable**
   The biggest limitation to the paper’s ambition is that TRI is self-reported and only imperfectly maps to the emissions margin the Clean Air Act is meant to regulate. If the paper could show similar patterns using non-self-reported measures—ambient pollution, monitored emissions, violations, enforcement follow-through, or local health proxies—the contribution becomes much larger.

2. **A mechanism comparison**
   The paper hints at a reporting channel but does not turn it into a major contribution. If federal inspectors do not change actual emissions but do change reporting accuracy, violation detection, or penalty sequencing, that is a much richer and more publishable story than a simple null.

3. **A stronger institutional framing**
   The paper should be framed as evidence on **decentralized enforcement**, not just “EPA vs state inspectors.” The badge is not the point; the governance structure is.

4. **Heterogeneity that speaks to theory**
   The paper itself notes that differences might be larger where state capacity is weak or political capture is stronger. If the null persists exactly where the theoretical federal-state gap should be largest, the paper becomes far more interesting.

5. **A comparison beyond one program**
   If the paper could connect this to other delegated enforcement domains—water, waste, OSHA-state plans, tax administration, labor standards—it would feel much less like a niche environmental paper and more like a general institutional paper.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

The U.S. enforces environmental law through cooperative federalism: Washington writes the rules, states do most front-line enforcement, and the EPA can intervene. There are strong reasons to think federal and state enforcers may differ in incentives, capture, capacity, and stringency.

### Tension

If those differences matter, then the allocation of enforcement authority should affect pollution. But despite decades of debate, there is little direct evidence on whether federal versus state inspection actually changes environmental outcomes.

### Resolution

Using linked inspection-emissions data, the paper finds little evidence that facilities inspected by the EPA have lower toxic releases than comparable facilities inspected by states.

### Implications

The practical implication is that the main policy margin may be inspection coverage rather than the identity of the inspector. The broader implication is that concerns about decentralized enforcement may be overstated in settings where rules and enforcement technology are standardized—though that broader claim needs to be made more cautiously.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the bones of one, but it is not fully developed. The paper is not exactly a bag of results, but it does feel slightly like a competent empirical exercise searching for a larger claim. The story should be cleaner:

- **Big question:** Does decentralization of enforcement change real outcomes?
- **Test case:** EPA-state delegation under the Clean Air Act.
- **Finding:** Not much evidence that enforcer identity changes emissions.
- **Interpretation:** In shared enforcement systems, standardized rules may compress institutional differences more than critics assume.
- **Implication:** Reform should focus on coverage, incentives, and follow-up rather than simply shifting authority upward.

That is a real story. Right now the paper only tells pieces of it.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?

“I looked at nearly two decades of Clean Air Act inspections and can’t find evidence that facilities inspected by the EPA pollute less than similar facilities inspected by states.”

That is the dinner-party line.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

Some would lean in—especially economists interested in regulation, public economics, or federalism—because the question is intuitive and institutionally important. But many would immediately ask whether the outcome measure is the wrong one. That is the paper’s central strategic vulnerability.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Almost certainly: **“Does that mean state regulators are actually as effective, or just that TRI releases are too noisy/self-reported to detect the difference?”**

That is exactly the right question, and it means the paper’s discussion of mechanisms and measurement is not ancillary; it is central to whether the result is interesting.

### If the findings are null or modest: is the null itself interesting?

Potentially yes. A well-established fear in environmental governance is that delegation to states creates a race to the bottom or a systematic enforcement gap. A carefully documented null on realized pollution outcomes could be important.

But the paper has not fully earned the null yet as a strategic matter. For nulls to travel in top journals, they usually need one of three things:
1. a very big question,
2. very persuasive measurement of the relevant outcome,
3. a sharp reason the null is surprising.

This paper has (1), somewhat has (3), and is weak on (2). The paper needs to make the null feel informative rather than like “we didn’t find anything.”

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

At the moment, the main gap is a combination of **framing problem** and **scope problem**, with some **novelty risk**.

- **Framing problem:** The paper undersells its broadest question—does decentralized enforcement alter real outcomes?—and oversells narrower institutional details.
- **Scope problem:** One outcome, especially a self-reported one, is too narrow to carry a broad institutional claim.
- **Novelty risk:** Without a stronger framing and richer evidence, it can feel like a respectable environmental-applied paper rather than a field-defining contribution.

I do not think the core issue is ambition in the sense of effort. The paper is trying to say something large. The problem is that the evidence base, as presented, is narrower than the claim.

### The single most impactful piece of advice

**Reframe the paper as evidence on the consequences of decentralized enforcement, and then strengthen that framing with at least one non-self-reported outcome or mechanism that distinguishes true enforcement equivalence from mere reporting differences.**

If the author could only change one thing, that is it. As written, the paper’s biggest weakness is that the key null result is too easy to interpret as a measurement story. The path to AER is not a fancier econometric presentation; it is making the null answer a bigger question with evidence that readers cannot dismiss as artifact.
