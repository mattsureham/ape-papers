## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper investigates how a massive tax shock to private education—the UK’s 2025 imposition of 20% VAT—spills over into the public sector through the housing market. It asks whether making private schools more expensive forces families to bid up prices for homes near high-quality state schools, effectively "taxing" school quality twice. Economists should care because this tests the "safety valve" theory of private education using a rare, large-scale exogenous price shock.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this well, but the first two paragraphs lean too heavily on the "double payment" hook. It should lead more aggressively with the **policy shock** itself. 

*The pitch the paper should have:* 
On January 1, 2025, the UK eliminated the VAT exemption for private school fees, overnight increasing the cost of private education by nearly 20% for 7% of the nation's students. This paper exploits this massive price shock to test if private schools act as a "safety valve" that moderates house price premiums for high-quality state schools. Using 5 million transactions, I find that while the housing market responded instantly to the policy announcement, the effect surprisingly reduced the school quality premium, suggesting that wealth effects or supply anticipations may override simple demand reallocation in the short run.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution:** The paper provides the first quasi-experimental test of whether private school costs determine the capitalization of state school quality into house prices.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It is well-differentiated from Black (1999) and Gibbons & Machin (2003) by moving from "measuring the premium" to "shocking the premium." 
*   **World vs. Literature:** It frames itself around a world-changing event (the UK VAT policy), which is high-impact.
*   **Explainability:** A smart economist would say: "It's the paper that checks if the UK's private school tax made house prices in good school districts explode."
*   **Making it bigger:** To be truly AER-sized, the contribution needs to solve the "wrong sign" puzzle. Finding a *negative* DDD when theory predicts a *positive* one makes this a "puzzle" paper. It needs to lean into why the Fack-Grenet (2010) prediction failed—is it because of a "wealth shock" to the very people who live in those areas?

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

*   **Closest Neighbors:** Black (1999) on valuation; Fack & Grenet (2010) on the safety valve; Mian & Sufi (2009) on policy capitalization.
*   **Positioning:** It currently "attacks" Fack & Grenet by finding the opposite result. This is a bold but dangerous position given the identification caveats.
*   **Niche vs. Broad:** It is positioned broadly enough, but it misses a critical conversation: **Public Finance / Incidence.** It should be speaking to the literature on who actually bears the burden of "luxury" taxes when there are strong local substitutes.
*   **Missing Literature:** It needs more on the "Wealth Effect" in housing. If the people living near good schools are the ones whose "disposable income" just took a £10k/year hit per child, that's a negative income shock that should lower house prices.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

*   **Setup:** Families choose between paying for "free" good schools via house prices or private schools via fees.
*   **Tension:** The UK government taxed fees to fund state schools. Theory says house prices near good state schools should soar as families flee the private sector.
*   **Resolution:** Prices actually *fell* relative to the trend (the negative DDD). 
*   **Implications:** The "safety valve" might actually be a "wealth anchor"—when you tax the alternative, you impoverish the very households that support the premium.

**Evaluation:** The arc is currently "Result looking for a story." The author is too honest about the placebo failure in the intro, which kills the tension. The story should be: "The Great Policy Reversal: Why taxing private schools didn't do what we expected."

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

*   **The Lead Fact:** "Taxing private schools in the UK actually *lowered* the premium for living near a top-tier state school by 5%."
*   **Reaction:** Lean in. It’s counter-intuitive.
*   **Follow-up:** "Wait, did the rich people just get poorer, or did they stop moving?"
*   **Null/Modest Results:** The "failed" temporal placebo is a major problem. If the result is just a continuation of a COVID-era trend, the "So What" becomes "Nothing to see here."

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

In its current state, it is a "Research Note" or a *Journal of Public Economics* paper. To make it **AER**, it needs to resolve the **Causal vs. Descriptive** crisis.

The paper admits the temporal placebo is significant. In the AER, you cannot lead with "I found a result but it's probably a pre-trend." 

**The single most impactful piece of advice:**
Pivot the paper from a "test of the VAT policy" to a "study of how the housing market capitalizes political shocks." Use the 2024 Election as a "surprise" event. If the author can show that the *slope* of the trend changed exactly on election night, the pre-trend argument is weakened. 

Also, the author must explain the negative sign. Is it an **Income Effect**? Use the Land Registry data to look at the very top of the market (£2m+ homes) vs the middle. If the effect is only at the top, it’s an income shock to the wealthy, not a demand shift to state schools.
