**Idea Fidelity**

The paper departs materially from the idea laid out in the manifest. The original proposal centered on exploiting the Energistyrelsen lotteries within Denmark’s køberetsordning to create an IV for household-level financial co-ownership and thereby estimate the causal effect on NIMBYism, property values, and voting behavior. In contrast, the current draft relies entirely on a municipality-level staggered difference-in-differences design that compares municipalities receiving new wind capacity under the scheme to those that did not, ignoring the lottery-generated exogenous variation and the rich microdata (lottery winners/losers, individual properties, municipal election precincts, planning complaints) that were part of the manifest. The paper never links to the actual k\oe beretsordning applicant data nor constructs the instrumental variable strategy originally promised, so it fails to pursue the identification strategy or data richness stated in the manifest.

**Summary**

The paper investigates whether Denmark’s mandatory k\oe beretsordning community ownership requirement affected property values and green party vote shares, using a Callaway–Sant’Anna staggered DiD across 97 municipalities. While naive two-way fixed-effects regressions suggest a negative effect on log property values, the CS-DiD estimate is a precisely estimated null, and placebo evidence shows existing wind sites already had lower property growth. The authors conclude that financial ownership does not mitigate local disamenity costs nor shift political preferences, suggesting limits to community benefit schemes.

**Essential Points**

1. **Identification strategy deviates from the proposed lottery IV and is vulnerable to confounding.** The paper’s main design compares municipalities with new capacity to other municipalities, but treated municipalities are systematically different: more rural, already hosting wind, and experiencing different pre-trends (as the placebo confirms). Without exploiting the randomized allocation among applicants, there is no clear source of exogenous variation in financial ownership, so the null may reflect selection into turbine siting rather than the causal effect of co-ownership. The analysis should either implement the lottery-based IV or, at minimum, provide a convincing quasi-experimental design (e.g., instrumental variable using lottery winners, regression discontinuity around 4.5 km, or event study on application timing). As it stands, the paper fails to credibly isolate the ownership channel that motivated the study.

2. **The treatment definition blurs ownership with general wind development, undermining the research question.** The core question concerns whether financial stakes reduce opposition. The municipality treatment—“new onshore wind capacity under the scheme”—implicitly combines turbines with community ownership and turbines without it (all turbines had the community offer but losers/no-participants still bear disamenities). Without data on who actually purchased shares, the treatment is better interpreted as “municipality receives a new turbine under VE-loven,” not “growth in financial ownership stakes.” Thus the null might simply reflect the fact that adding turbines (with or without ownership) has limited marginal impact on municipal averages. This weakens the causal link between ownership and NIMBYism and fails to answer the stated question. The authors need to better articulate what is identified and show how the financial stake, not just turbine presence, is driving variation.

3. **Outcome measurement and aggregation obscure local effects and omit key outcomes from the manifest.** The manifest emphasized property values within proximity, voting (municipal-level), and planning complaints, ideally using microdata on lottery winners/losers. The paper aggregates property values to the municipality-year level, likely diluting the impact of turbines that affect only a small share of houses. Similarly, the green vote share combines multiple parties without reporting heterogeneity by precinct or the actual planning complaint data that would speak to NIMBYism. Without these granular outcomes and the lottery data, the paper limits its ability to detect the theoretically motivated effects and leaves the causal mechanism ambiguous.

Given these foundational issues, the paper should be rejected or restructured around the originally promised lottery-based design before resubmission.

**Suggestions**

1. **Return to the lottery as the identification lever.** Seek access to the Energistyrelsen køberetsordning applicant registry to identify winners and losers of past lotteries. This will provide the exogenous variation necessary to instrument for financial ownership. Even if only high-level data are available (e.g., winning probability by distance band), document it and construct an IV strategy where the instrument is lottery win status among applicants. This directly ties into the stated research question and allows the authors to argue that any observed effect is due to financial stakes rather than turbine presence.

2. **Use more granular outcome data to detect local effects.** Instead of municipality averages, focus on property transactions or valuations within the 4.5 km priority zone (or nearest neighbor buffers). Danish cadastral or BBR data allow for precise mapping; linking turbine coordinates to property plots would let you estimate distance gradients and exploit spatial discontinuities. For voting, consider precinct-level data if available, or at least break down green vote share by municipality area to capture local sentiment. Planning complaint data from Energiklagenævnet would provide a direct measure of opposition; incorporate it as an outcome or robustness check.

3. **Clarify the contrast being estimated and the mechanism.** If the municipality-level DiD is retained as an ancillary exercise, explicitly state that it estimates the effect of building a new turbine under the scheme, not the effect of financial co-ownership per se. Consider separating the analysis: (i) the lottery IV for ownership among applicants, (ii) a DiD for turbine siting that captures aggregate disamenity costs, and (iii) heterogeneity analyses that link ownership share size to outcomes. This modular structure would make the empirical strategy transparent and help readers understand the mechanism even when granular data are unavailable.

4. **Address the parallel trends concern systematically.** The placebo evidence demonstrates pre-trends, but the paper does not adjust for them beyond using CS-DiD. Consider matching treated municipalities to controls on pre-treatment trends in property values/income/wind capacity, or use synthetic control methods to ensure better comparability. At the very least, show event studies for the key outcomes with pre-trends plotted for each cohort separately and discuss any deviations. This will bolster confidence that the null is not driven by violations of the identifying assumption.

5. **Engage with the magnitude of financial stakes in more detail.** The discussion mentions annual dividends per share but stops short of quantifying whether the financial return is economically meaningful for the average household. Provide back-of-the-envelope calculations or histogram of share holdings to show the expected present value relative to property value changes. If data permit, link lottery wins to dividend receipts and show the share of households that would benefit materially. This strengthens the argument about the policy relevance of the null result.

6. **Expand robustness checks to include alternative control groups.** The current controls include “never treated” and “always treated” municipalities, but there may be better matches (e.g., municipalities with similar wind potential but differing treatment timing). Try restricting the sample to rural municipalities or those within the same region to reduce heterogeneity. Additionally, use placebo treatments on years before 2017 to show that the estimated effects do not appear when treatment is assigned randomly in the pre-period.

7. **Document data limitations and provide replication materials.** Since the paper now relies on publicly available municipality-level data, detail any steps taken to construct the panel (e.g., matching rules, handling of municipal mergers). Include an appendix with summary statistics for the lottery data (if obtained) and sample selection. Sharing code/data through a repository would help others assess and build on your analysis.

By pursuing these suggestions, especially those that reintroduce the lottery-based identification and leverage more granular outcomes, the paper can better align with the original idea and provide more credible causal evidence on the effectiveness of community ownership as a policy tool.
