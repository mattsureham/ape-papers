**RE:** Strategic Positioning of "The Stakeholder Illusion: Community Wind Ownership and the Limits of Financial Alignment"

---

### 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether giving local residents a direct financial stake in "not-in-my-backyard" (NIMBY) infrastructure can mitigate local opposition. Exploiting a Danish mandate that forced wind developers to offer 20% equity to neighbors, the author finds a precisely estimated null effect: community ownership fails to protect property values or shift political support for green parties. The result suggests that for major infrastructure, financial "carrots" cannot buy off the fundamental disamenity costs or non-pecuniary objections of the local population.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this well in its first paragraph. It correctly identifies the "tension between aggregate climate benefits and local disamenity costs" as the central motivation. 

**The pitch the paper should have (Tightened):**
"Decarbonization requires building infrastructure where people live, but local opposition remains the primary bottleneck. This paper provides the first quasi-experimental test of the 'financial alignment' hypothesis: can mandatory co-ownership buy community acceptance? Using Denmark’s unique equity-sharing mandate, I find that even generous financial stakes fail to mitigate the negative impact of wind turbines on property values or political preferences, suggesting that the 'NIMBY' constraint is not primarily a financial one."

---

### 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**The Contribution:** The paper provides the first revealed-preference (market-based) evidence that financial co-ownership models fail to attenuate the negative externalities of renewable energy infrastructure.

- **Differentiation:** Most existing literature (e.g., Jørgensen 2020) relies on stated-preference surveys. This paper moves to revealed preferences (property values and voting). It differentiates itself from the "wind disamenity" literature (e.g., Hoen et al. 2015) by specifically isolating the *ownership* policy channel.
- **Question vs. Literature:** It answers a question about the **WORLD** (Can we solve NIMBYism with money?). This is a high-level, policy-relevant question that resonates beyond environmental economics.
- **Clarity:** A smart economist would say: "It's a precisely estimated null showing that Coasean bargaining via equity shares doesn't actually stop property values from tanking when a turbine goes up."
- **Bigger Contribution:** The contribution would be exponentially larger if the author could access the "lottery" data mentioned in Section 4.3. Currently, the municipality-level analysis is "coarse." A within-municipality comparison of individuals who won the lottery to buy shares vs. those who lost would be the "Gold Standard" AER version of this paper.

---

### 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

- **Neighbors:** Fischel (2001) on the Homevoter Hypothesis; Callaway & Sant’Anna (2021) on DiD methods; and the broader Coase (1960) tradition of incentive alignment.
- **Positioning:** The paper should position itself as a **challenge** to the Coasean optimism prevalent in policy circles. It’s not just "building on" the literature; it’s providing a sobering "reality check" to a decade of survey-based optimism.
- **Niche vs. Broad:** It is currently positioned well—broad enough to interest political economists and urban economists, not just "energy" people.
- **Unexpected Connection:** The paper could bridge to the **Behavioral Economics** literature on "Moral Saliency" or "Crowding Out" (e.g., Gneezy and Rustichini). Does offering money turn a "protection of the landscape" issue into a "dirty bribe," actually reinforcing opposition?

---

### 4. NARRATIVE ARC

- **Setup:** Global decarbonization requires local wind power; governments use financial stakes to lubricate the process.
- **Tension:** Economics suggests financial alignment works (Coase), but psychological/aesthetic concerns may be "lexicographic" (unpriceable). 
- **Resolution:** The Danish experiment—the most generous in the world—shows no effect. The property value "hit" happens regardless of who owns the turbine.
- **Implications:** Siting policy needs to move beyond "How much should we pay them?" to "How do we get genuine consent?"

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong. It follows a "Trial of a Theory" structure. However, the "Placebo" finding (Section 5.3) is actually the most striking part of the story and should be elevated in the narrative. It proves the disamenity is real and large, making the failure of the ownership scheme even more damning.

---

### 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

- **Lead Fact:** "Denmark forced wind developers to give 20% of the project to the neighbors, and it still didn't stop property values from dropping or people from voting against green parties."
- **Reaction:** Lean in. It’s counter-intuitive to the standard economic model of "everyone has a price."
- **Follow-up:** "Was the 20% just too small? Or is the noise/visual impact so bad that no amount of money fixes it?" (The paper addresses this in Section 6, which is good).

---

### 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

- **Front-loading:** The paper is well-structured.
- **Appendix:** Move the "Leave-one-out" analysis to the appendix. 
- **Promotion:** Move the "Standardized Effect Sizes" (Table 5) and the "Placebo" results (Table 4, Panel B) closer to the main results. The Placebo isn't just a robustness check; it's the evidence that there was a "wound" for the "bandage" (ownership) to fix.
- **Conclusion:** The conclusion is excellent, specifically the "frontier of siting policy" sentence.

---

### 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

**The Gap:** Currently, this is a very high-quality "Applied Economics" paper (think *AEJ: Policy* or *JPubE*). To reach the *AER*, it needs to overcome the "coarseness" of the municipality-level data.

**Single Most Impactful Advice:**
Address the "participation" vs. "offering" distinction: Does the null result exist because the *policy* failed to move the needle, or because the *financial returns* were actually trivial when liquidated? You need to prove that the "Stakeholder Illusion" isn't just a "Small Stakes" problem. If the author can find a way to show that even in areas where take-up was high, property values still fell, the paper becomes a "must-cite" for the energy transition.
