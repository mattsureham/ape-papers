### 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
This paper asks whether national drug legalization creates "enforcement externalities" for neighboring countries that maintain prohibition. Exploiting Canada’s 2018 legalization as a natural experiment, it finds that U.S. border counties in prohibition states saw no detectable increase in drug arrests, effectively ruling out even modest spillovers despite a documented surge in federal-level seizures at the border.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this well in the first two paragraphs, particularly by framing the issue as a choice between "independent reforms" and "coordinated international policy." However, it should lead more aggressively with the **"CBP Seizure Paradox"** mentioned later.
*   **The pitch it should have:** "When Canada legalized cannabis, federal seizures at the U.S. border spiked by nearly 300%, yet this paper shows that local police in neighboring U.S. counties saw zero impact on their arrest blotters. By exploiting a sharp international policy discontinuity and a COVID-era border closure, I show that well-monitored international borders act as a complete 'enforcement firewall,' allowing nations to pursue independent drug policies without imposing costs on their neighbors."

### 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**Contribution:** The paper provides the first bounded null estimate of international drug policy spillovers, demonstrating that international borders (unlike domestic state borders) successfully contain the enforcement costs of legalization.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It is well-differentiated from Hansen et al. (2020), which found significant spillovers at *state* borders. The "international vs. domestic" distinction is the paper's strongest selling point.
*   **Framing:** It is framed as a question about the **WORLD** (specifically, the feasibility of sovereign drug policy).
*   **The "Smart Economist" Test:** They would say "It’s the paper that shows why Canada’s pot laws didn't break the U.S. border." It avoids being "just another DiD" because of the unique international border context and the COVID-closure mechanism test.
*   **Making it bigger:** To be a "slam dunk" AER paper, it needs to move beyond arrests. The contribution would be much larger if it included **price data** (to show if the "firewall" exists because the market didn't move or because police didn't care) or **emergency room/public health data** (to see if the spillover is social even if it isn't "legal").

### 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
*   **Closest Neighbors:** Hansen et al. (2020, JPE); Gavrilova et al. (2019, Econ Journal); Masera and Rossi (2023, REStat).
*   **Strategy:** The paper should **synthesize** the domestic spillover literature to highlight its own contrast. It effectively "attacks" the assumption that findings from US-state borders (open) apply to US-international borders (hard).
*   **Niche vs. Broad:** Currently slightly too focused on "cannabis." It should pivot toward the broader literature on **"Policy Asynchrony"** and **"Jurisdictional Externalities."**
*   **Unexpected Connection:** It should speak to the **Trade/Border literature** (e.g., Feenstra). Is a border a "friction" or a "filter"? This paper suggests it is a highly efficient filter for illegal commodities.

### 4. NARRATIVE ARC
*   **Setup:** Global trend toward drug liberalization vs. traditional international treaty obligations (The Single Convention on Narcotic Drugs).
*   **Tension:** If I legalize, do I "poison" my neighbor's backyard? Domestic evidence says "Yes."
*   **Resolution:** At the international level, the answer is "No." The border is a effective barrier.
*   **Implications:** "Policy containment" is possible. International friction is a feature, not a bug, for sovereign experimentation.

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong. The COVID-19 closure acts as a "second act" that attempts to prove the border is the primary mechanism, though the results there are a bit "muddy" (as the author admits).

### 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
*   **Lead Fact:** "Federal seizures went up 300%, but local arrests didn't move an inch."
*   **Reaction:** Lean in. It’s counter-intuitive. Most would expect "leaky" borders to increase local crime or police work.
*   **Follow-up:** "Is it because the cops just stopped caring, or because the drugs never made it past the checkpoint?" (The paper struggles to answer this, which is its main weakness).

### 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
*   **Front-load the Paradox:** Move the "CBP Seizure Paradox" (Section 5.3) into the Introduction. It’s the "hook" that makes the null result interesting.
*   **Shorten:** The "three-regime" COVID discussion is necessary but gets bogged down in caveats. It could be more concisely presented as a supplementary test rather than a co-equal "main result."
*   **Appendix:** Move the "Leave-one-state-out" table to the appendix; the text summary is sufficient.

### 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The gap is **Mechanism and Scope.** A "bounded null" on arrests is interesting, but "arrests" are a choice made by police. We don't know if the drugs arrived and were ignored, or if the drugs never arrived.

**Single most impactful advice:** Incorporate data on **drug prices** (from crowdsourced "Price of Weed" or similar) or **marijuana-related hospitalizations** in those same border counties. If the author can show that *both* arrests (police response) and health/price outcomes (market presence) remained flat, the "Firewall" story becomes an ironclad AER paper on the economics of borders.
