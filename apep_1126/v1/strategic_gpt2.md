## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple, policy-relevant question: when one country legalizes cannabis and its neighbor does not, does the neighbor bear local enforcement costs near the border? Using Canada’s 2018 legalization and U.S. county arrest data, the paper finds essentially no detectable increase in drug arrests in nearby prohibition-state counties, suggesting that this international policy asymmetry was more contained than many feared.

A busy economist should care because the paper speaks to a first-order issue in policy federalism and international externalities: when do governments impose cross-border costs on others, and when can policy divergence coexist without meaningful spillovers? The setting is intuitive, salient, and potentially generalizable to other cross-border policy wedges.

**Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Almost, but not quite. The opening is competent and topical, but the pitch is still more “here is a setting” than “here is the economic question and why the answer matters.” The first paragraph leans on border length and seizure statistics; the second broadens to other countries. What is missing is a sharper claim about the underlying economics: this is a paper about whether international legalization creates **enforcement externalities** and therefore whether **policy coordination** is necessary.

### The pitch the paper should have

> Countries are increasingly diverging in cannabis policy, but we know little about whether legalization in one jurisdiction imposes enforcement costs on neighboring jurisdictions that keep prohibition. Canada’s 2018 nationwide legalization created an unusually clean test: a single-date policy shock along the entire U.S.-Canada border, where nearby U.S. counties remained subject to state and federal prohibition.  
>  
> Using county-level U.S. drug arrest data, this paper asks whether Canadian legalization increased local enforcement activity in adjacent U.S. prohibition counties. I find no detectable increase in drug arrests near the border, and the estimates are precise enough to rule out anything but modest positive spillovers. The broader implication is that even large cross-border policy differences need not generate substantial local enforcement externalities when borders are formal, monitored, and policed.

That version makes the paper sound like a contribution to a broad question in public economics and political economy, not simply a border DiD on cannabis arrests.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:**  
The paper provides what it claims is the first evidence on whether a national cannabis legalization creates measurable cross-border local enforcement spillovers in a neighboring prohibition country, and finds that Canada’s legalization did not increase drug arrests in nearby U.S. counties.

Only partially. The paper names nearby studies, but the differentiation is too mechanical: “they study domestic spillovers, I study an international border.” That is true, but not yet enough. A top-field reader will ask: why should an international border matter economically, not just geographically? The paper needs to say more explicitly that the international setting changes the relevant theory of spillovers because it combines a sharp legal wedge with strong border enforcement, staffed ports of entry, and limited informal crossing—conditions under which externalities may be contained rather than diffused.

Right now the contribution risks sounding like: **same policy question, new geography**. It needs to sound like: **different margin of policy transmission, revealing when cross-jurisdiction externalities are or are not likely to emerge**.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or filling a gap in a LITERATURE?
It starts with the world, then slides back into literature-gap language (“no published paper uses the international US-Canada border…”; “this paper fills that gap”). That is weaker than it should be. AER papers usually answer a question about how the world works; the literature gap is supporting evidence, not the main event.

The stronger framing is:

- Governments worry that unilateral legalization exports enforcement burdens.
- Here is a clean test of whether that happened.
- It did not, under this type of border environment.
- Therefore the incidence of policy spillovers depends on institutional containment.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?
At present, maybe, but not confidently. They could probably say: “It’s a DiD on whether Canada’s legalization affected U.S. border-county arrests, and they find nothing.” That is not nothing—but it is still perilously close to “another DiD paper about X.”

What they should be able to say is: “It shows that a big international legalization shock did **not** export local enforcement costs across a heavily monitored border, which is informative for when cross-jurisdiction policy divergence requires coordination.” The introduction is not yet delivering that level of interpretive clarity.

### What would make this contribution bigger?
Most importantly: **elevate the object of interest from ‘drug arrests’ to ‘containment of cross-border policy externalities.’**

Specific ways to make it bigger:
1. **Different framing:** Make the paper about when policy externalities are containable, not just about cannabis.
2. **Different outcomes:** If feasible, add outcomes that speak more directly to the spillover concern policymakers actually have—seizures, local charges, impaired driving, property crime, hospitalizations, overdose calls, or border-county law-enforcement resource use. Drug arrests are a narrow and noisy proxy.
3. **Different mechanism evidence:** The paper hints at a containment story via staffed ports of entry and the seizure/arrest divergence. That could be the core mechanism. Right now it is just a subsection called “The CBP Seizure Paradox.”
4. **Different comparison:** The paper could be much stronger if it explicitly contrasts its finding with the domestic-border literature: internal state borders show spillovers; an international border does not. That comparative insight is the real intellectual value.
5. **Different framing of the null:** The paper’s best asset is that the null is bounded. It should emphasize not merely “we find no effect” but “we can rule out large enforcement externalities.”

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Jurisdictions are increasingly diverging in cannabis policy. A common concern is that legalization in one place may push drug activity, smuggling, or enforcement burdens onto nearby jurisdictions that retain prohibition.

### Tension
The U.S.-Canada border offers a particularly clean and salient test: a sharp legal asymmetry along a long border, with public concern heightened by increased federal seizure statistics. The puzzle is whether those apparent border pressures translated into local enforcement burdens in nearby U.S. communities.

### Resolution
They did not, at least in recorded county-level drug arrests. The estimates are centered near zero and bounded tightly enough to rule out large positive spillovers.

### Implications
Policy divergence does not automatically require international coordination. Along a monitored international border, legalization can be institutionally contained rather than exported as local enforcement burden.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
It has the skeleton of one, but it is not fully realized. Right now the paper reads a bit like:

1. Interesting policy event  
2. DiD setup  
3. Null result  
4. Some diagnostic exercises  
5. Conclusion

What is missing is stronger narrative tension and interpretation. The best narrative is not “we looked for spillovers and found none.” The best narrative is:

- Many people expected legalization to export enforcement costs.
- Federal seizure data seemed to support that concern.
- Yet local arrest data show no corresponding spillover.
- Therefore border institutions may absorb shocks that would otherwise diffuse locally.

That story is already latent in the paper—especially in the “CBP seizure paradox”—but it is buried and underleveraged. That subsection is arguably the paper’s most interesting interpretive move and should be integrated into the main narrative rather than treated as an aside.

Without that, it risks feeling like a failed hunt for an effect. With that framing, it becomes a meaningful null.
