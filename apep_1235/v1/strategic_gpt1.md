## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether a large, sudden exchange-rate appreciation can permanently change the industrial structure of local labor markets. Using the Swiss National Bank’s 2015 abandonment of the euro floor, it argues that municipalities more exposed through pre-shock manufacturing concentration experienced a long-lasting shift out of the secondary sector and only delayed absorption into services.

Why should a busy economist care? Because the paper is trying to turn a very familiar question—do trade shocks reshape places?—into a cleaner and rarer one: can a discrete macro shock generate persistent local structural transformation rather than a short-run disruption?

**Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Not quite. The introduction is competent and readable, but it leads with the Swiss event and then quickly slips into design language. The core pitch should be less “I exploit shock X with treatment Y” and more “economists do not know whether exchange-rate shocks temporarily hit exporters or durably reorganize local economies.” Right now the paper’s first paragraphs undersell the world-level question and overstate how settled the causal framing is.

**What the first two paragraphs should say instead:**

> Large exchange-rate movements are among the most important shocks facing open economies, but we know surprisingly little about whether they merely compress exporters’ margins for a few years or permanently reshape the places built around traded-goods production. When a currency appreciates sharply, do manufacturing regions recover, or do they begin a lasting transition out of industry and into services?
>
> This paper studies that question using the Swiss National Bank’s unexpected abandonment of the EUR/CHF floor in January 2015, which caused an immediate appreciation of the franc. I show that Swiss municipalities more concentrated in the secondary sector before the shock experienced a persistent relative decline in secondary-sector employment and only delayed growth in services, consistent with a slow local reallocation process rather than a transitory disruption.

That is the paper’s best version of itself: a paper about **when macro shocks become place-based structural change**.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:**  
The paper claims that a sudden exchange-rate appreciation can act as a persistent local structural shock, producing long-run reallocation from manufacturing-oriented municipalities toward services, with reallocation arriving only after several years.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?
Only partially. The paper says “China shock, but with an exchange-rate shock” and “Swiss franc shock, but spatial.” That is plausible, but not yet sharp enough. A smart economist may still hear: “another local-labor-markets exposure paper showing manufacturing places decline and services eventually grow.”

The differentiation should be:

1. **Shock type:** discrete exchange-rate appreciation, not import competition trend.
2. **Unit of adjustment:** local economic structure, not firms or sectors alone.
3. **Dynamic pattern:** immediate contraction, delayed reallocation.
4. **Claim:** exchange-rate shocks can be “ratchets,” not temporary wedges.

The title already hints at that with “structural ratchet,” but the introduction does not fully build the concept.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a gap in a literature?
Mixed, leaning too much toward literature-gap framing. The strongest version is a world question:

- **Weak framing:** “No one has studied spatial effects of the 2015 franc shock.”
- **Strong framing:** “Economists do not know whether exchange-rate appreciations create durable place-based deindustrialization.”

The latter is much stronger and more AER-relevant.

### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what’s new?
At present, maybe, but not crisply. They might say:  
“It's a DiD on Swiss municipalities after the franc appreciation, showing manufacturing-heavy places shifted toward services.”  

That is not enough. You want them to say:  
“It argues that abrupt currency appreciations can permanently reallocate local economies, with a multi-year destruction-before-reallocation dynamic.”

### What would make this contribution bigger?
Specific ways to make it bigger:

- **Different outcome variable:** The biggest missed opportunity is worker welfare or adjustment margins—earnings, unemployment, out-migration, commuting, vacancies, business formation. Without these, the paper shows composition, but not whether this was economically costly or efficient.
- **Different mechanism:** Separate whether “services rose” via local absorption, commuting inflows, establishment entry, public sector growth, or sector relabeling. Right now “reallocation” is more asserted than demonstrated.
- **Different comparison:** Compare the exchange-rate shock to other trade shocks explicitly. Is adjustment faster or slower than China-shock-type import competition? The paper gestures at this but does not operationalize it.
- **Different framing:** Emphasize the general insight that macro stabilization events can have highly uneven local structural consequences. That opens the door to macro, trade, and urban/regional audiences at once.

If the author could add one dimension, it should be **worker adjustment and welfare margins**, not another robustness table.

Potentially also:

- **Topalova** and other local trade-shock papers,
- exchange-rate pass-through / firm adjustment papers in international macro and trade.

### How should the paper position itself relative to those neighbors?
Mostly **build on them**, not attack them.

- Relative to the China shock literature: “This is the exchange-rate analogue with a sharp event date.”

- Relative to regional adjustment papers: “This paper brings exchange-rate shocks into the regional adjustment conversation.”

It should not posture as offering “cleaner identification” than the China shock literature; that invites an argument the paper does not need. Strategically, the paper is safer and stronger saying: **a different shock environment reveals a different adjustment margin**.

### Is the paper currently positioned too narrowly or too broadly?
A bit too narrowly in setting, and too broadly in claim.  
It is narrow because it is very Swiss-institution-specific. It is broad because it sometimes sounds like it has nailed causal structural transformation from exchange rates in general.

The right balance is:

Use Switzerland as a sharply observed laboratory for a broader proposition about currency shocks and place-based adjustment.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?
A few conversations feel underexploited:

- **Regional adjustment and migration/commuting** after local shocks.
- **Exchange-rate exposure at firm level** in international trade and macro.
- **Structural transformation / deindustrialization** beyond trade-shock papers.
- **Place-based persistence / hysteresis** literature.

### What fields should it be speaking to?
At minimum:
- Trade
- Macroeconomics/international macro
- Labor
- Regional/urban economics

Right now it is mostly speaking to trade/labor. It should more deliberately invite macro and regional economists in.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?
Almost, but not quite. The highest-impact conversation may not be “another local trade shock” paper. It may be:

> “When do macro shocks leave permanent geographic scars or trigger permanent structural transformation?”

That is a more interesting conversation and a more AER-ish one.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Before this paper, economists know that trade shocks can reshape local labor markets, but evidence mostly comes from import competition or trade liberalization episodes that unfold gradually. Exchange-rate shocks are common and important, yet it is unclear whether they produce only temporary pain for exporters or durable local sectoral change.

### Tension
The 2015 Swiss franc appreciation is an unusually sharp and unexpected macro shock. But there is ambiguity: Swiss manufacturing could adapt and recover, or the shock could permanently tip exposed places away from industry. That tension is real and potentially compelling.

### Resolution
The paper finds that more exposed municipalities experienced a persistent decline in secondary-sector share, while services expanded only later. The implied story is not rapid local recovery but a slow reorganization of local economic structure.

### Implications
Exchange-rate appreciations may have long-lived place-based consequences, and policy should think not only about short-run exporter pain but about medium-run transition support in affected local economies.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
Yes, but only in rough form. The ingredients are there. The problem is that the paper sometimes reads like a collection of sensible regression outputs rather than a tightly organized argument. The core narrative should be:

1. Manufacturing places in Switzerland were not already hollowed-out relics; if anything, they were holding or deepening specialization.
2. Then came a sudden macro shock.
3. The post-2015 path is not a temporary dip and rebound; it is a reversal.
4. Services eventually grow, but only after years.
5. Therefore, exchange-rate shocks can permanently redirect local economic structure.

That is the story. Every table and section should serve that story.

Right now there is too much emphasis on “I ran event study / static DiD / short-run vs long-run / robustness” and not enough emphasis on the conceptual progression from **shock → break → delayed reallocation → structural ratchet**.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?
“An unexpected 15 percent appreciation of the Swiss franc did not just temporarily hurt exporters—it appears to have permanently shifted more manufacturing-intensive Swiss municipalities away from the secondary sector, with services only absorbing the shock years later.”

That is a decent lead fact.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
They’d lean in initially, because the 2015 SNB event is memorable and dramatic. But the next 30 seconds matter. If the paper stays at “secondary share fell, tertiary share rose,” some will drift. If instead it emphasizes:

- sudden macro shock,
- permanent place-based effects,
- delayed reallocation,
- relevance beyond Switzerland,

then it holds attention.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Probably one of these:
- “Was this really manufacturing rather than construction?”
- “Did workers suffer—wages, unemployment, migration?”
- “Why is this more than just compositional mean reversion in small industrial towns?”
- “How general is this beyond Switzerland’s unusual export structure?”

Those are exactly the strategic vulnerabilities. Again, not because referees should not assess them, but because these are the points determining whether the paper feels important.

### If findings are modest: is the modesty itself interesting?
The findings are not null, but they are a bit modest in substantive texture. Sector shares are a blunt object. The paper partly overclaims by calling this “structural transformation” when the observed evidence is mostly broad-sector reallocation. The result is still interesting, but the paper needs to convince readers that broad sector-share movement is a meaningful fact, not a thin proxy.

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

### What would make it read better?

#### 1. Front-load the conceptual result, not the design
The introduction gets to the findings reasonably quickly, but still too much space is spent on empirical strategy language before the reader fully understands the paper’s stakes. Move from:

- dramatic shock,
- big world question,
- main finding,
- implication,

and only then to the design.

#### 2. Shorten the institutional background
The background is fine but slightly overlong for what it delivers. This is a paper with a famous event; most readers do not need a long explanation of the floor and its removal. Compress and use the space for more conceptual positioning.

#### 3. Move some “honest caveats” out of the introduction’s center of gravity
The paper is admirably transparent, but the current draft sometimes narrates its own limitations too prominently and too early. Editors appreciate candor, but readers also need momentum. Keep the caveats, but don’t let them dominate the opening act.

#### 4. Promote the dynamic pattern as the main result
The best result is not the pooled post coefficient. It is the **reversal plus delayed service absorption**. That should be visually and narratively central. The event study and short-vs-long decomposition are the paper’s heart.

#### 5. Demote standard static tables
The static DiD table is useful but should not feel like the headline. This paper lives or dies on dynamics and interpretation, not on one average post effect.

#### 6. The robustness section should be shorter
This is not where the paper will win or lose. AER readers care much more about the question, finding, and mechanism. Keep robustness concise and push lower-value variants to the appendix.

#### 7. The conclusion currently mostly summarizes
It should do more synthesis. It should end by saying what belief changes:
- exchange-rate shocks can leave lasting local footprints,
- local adjustment is slow,
- stabilization and transition policy are linked.

Also: drop or substantially rethink the acknowledgements section as currently written. For a serious submission, the autonomous-generation framing is distracting at best and damaging at worst. Even for a private memo, strategically this is an obvious own-goal.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The gap is mostly **ambition and framing**, with a meaningful **scope** issue.

### Is it a framing problem?
Yes, significantly. The paper’s best idea is larger than the current framing. It should be about **macro shocks becoming local structural change**, not merely about the 2015 Swiss franc episode.

### Is it a scope problem?
Yes. The outcomes are too narrow for the size of the claim. Broad sector shares are enough for a solid field-journal paper, but for AER the paper likely needs either:
- stronger mechanism evidence,
- stronger worker-level consequences,
- or sharper sectoral resolution.

### Is it a novelty problem?
Somewhat. The broad template is familiar: exogenous shock × pre-exposure → local adjustment. So novelty has to come from the **shock type**, **dynamic adjustment pattern**, and **broader conceptual claim**. If those are not sharpened, the paper will feel derivative.

### Is it an ambition problem?
Yes. The paper is careful, competent, and understated—but also somewhat safe. It does not fully cash out the possibilities of the setting. Top people in the field will ask: why is this more than a clean Swiss repackaging of the local-trade-shock playbook?

### Single most impactful piece of advice
**Reframe the paper around a bigger claim—exchange-rate appreciations as persistent place-based structural shocks—and then add one outcome or mechanism that shows what “reallocation” actually meant for workers or firms, not just sector shares.**

That is the move that could most change its trajectory.
