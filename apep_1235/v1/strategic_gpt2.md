## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether a large, unexpected exchange-rate appreciation can permanently reshape local economies, rather than just temporarily hurting exporters. Using the Swiss National Bank’s sudden 2015 abandonment of the euro floor, it argues that municipalities more exposed through pre-shock industrial structure saw persistent declines in the secondary sector and only delayed expansion in services, implying slow local reallocation after a macro shock.

Why should a busy economist care? Because this is a potentially important bridge between international macro/trade shocks and local labor market adjustment: if exchange-rate shocks operate like a “structural ratchet,” then nominal/financial events can have long-lived real and spatial consequences.

**Does the paper itself articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Mostly, but not sharply enough. The current opening is competent and readable, but it undersells the broader question and overcommits too early to the Switzerland-specific setup. It leads with an event before fully establishing the bigger economic question. The paper needs to tell the reader immediately that this is about whether exchange-rate shocks cause **permanent local structural transformation**.

### The pitch the paper should have

> Exchange-rate movements are among the most consequential macro shocks facing open economies, but we know remarkably little about whether they merely disrupt local production temporarily or permanently reallocate activity across sectors and places. This paper studies that question using the Swiss National Bank’s unexpected 2015 abandonment of the EUR/CHF floor, which generated an immediate appreciation of the franc and sharply reduced the competitiveness of Swiss tradables.
>
> I show that Swiss municipalities more exposed through their pre-shock industrial composition experienced a persistent decline in secondary-sector employment share, while service-sector expansion arrived only years later. The core finding is that exchange-rate shocks can function as a local “structural ratchet”: they trigger immediate contraction in exposed tradable activity, but reallocation into other sectors is slow, making the resulting structural change durable.

That is the paper’s best self. Right now the introduction is close, but still reads a bit like “Swiss franc shock paper” rather than “big question about local adjustment to macro shocks.”

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:**  
The paper claims that a large, discrete exchange-rate appreciation can induce persistent local sectoral reallocation, with immediate contraction in exposed tradables and delayed expansion in services.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest papers?
Only partially. The paper gestures at three literatures—China shock, cleansing/scarring, and prior franc-shock work—but the differentiation is not yet crisp.

A reader will currently hear something like: “This is a local-labor-market DiD around a plausibly exogenous shock showing manufacturing down, services up later.” That is respectable, but not yet distinctive enough for AER. The closest papers have already taught us that trade shocks can have persistent local effects and slow adjustment. So the paper needs to clarify what is genuinely new:

- not just **trade shocks matter locally**;
- but specifically **exchange-rate shocks**, which are macro-financial and nominal in origin, can have similarly persistent local real effects;
- and perhaps more importantly, **the dynamics are asymmetric**: destruction is fast, reallocation slow.

That asymmetry is probably the most portable idea in the paper.

### World question or literature gap?
The paper is mostly framed as a **world question**, which is good: do exchange-rate shocks permanently change local economic structure? That is stronger than “there is no municipal-level paper on the 2015 Swiss franc shock.”

But it slips at times into literature-gap language (“nascent literature on the January 2015 franc shock itself”). That is not enough. AER papers do not get in because no one has run this exact design on this exact episode.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the intro?
Not quite cleanly. Right now they might say:  
“It's another local trade-shock paper, but with the Swiss franc appreciation.”  
That is not fatal, but it is not what you want.

The introduction should leave them saying:  
“This paper shows that exchange-rate appreciations can act like local deindustrialization shocks, and that the reallocation margin is delayed enough to make the structural change persistent.”

### What would make this contribution bigger?
Most importantly: **tighten the object of study**.

Right now the main outcome/treatment is “secondary sector,” which includes construction. That weakens the conceptual sharpness of the contribution. Even if this is unavoidable in the data, it makes the headline feel blurrier than it should.

Specific ways to make the contribution feel bigger:

1. **Reframe around local adjustment dynamics, not just sector shares.**  
   The strongest finding is not simply that one share falls and another rises. It is the timing: immediate contraction, delayed absorption. Make that the centerpiece.

2. **Elevate the labor-market consequences of delayed reallocation.**  
   If the paper has any credible margin on total employment, establishment counts, FTE per establishment, commuting, exits, or worker displacement, that would enlarge the stakes. “Sector shares moved” is modest; “local adjustment took years and created a transition gap” is bigger.

3. **Sharpen the exposure concept toward tradables.**  
   Even descriptive evidence showing that exposed municipalities are those specialized in export-oriented manufacturing rather than construction-heavy places would help the story feel like it is about exchange rates, not just broad structural change.

4. **Frame it as a macro-to-local transmission paper.**  
   That is the angle that can travel beyond Switzerland.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Before this paper, we know that trade shocks can have large and persistent local effects, but less is known about whether sudden exchange-rate appreciations produce similar long-run local sectoral changes.

### Tension
An exchange-rate appreciation could be temporary pain: firms cut margins, raise productivity, hedge, reorient exports, and recover. Or it could trigger permanent local deindustrialization if exposed tradable activity contracts quickly and local reallocation is slow.

### Resolution
The paper finds that municipalities more exposed through pre-shock secondary-sector concentration saw sustained declines in the secondary sector after 2015, while service expansion occurred only with a lag.

### Implications
Exchange-rate shocks may permanently reshape local economies, not just temporarily disrupt them; and transition assistance may matter because reallocation is delayed.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
It has the ingredients of one, but the arc is still somewhat underdeveloped. At present, the paper is perilously close to being a **collection of sensible reduced-form results** around a plausible event. The title promises a big idea—“The Structural Ratchet”—but the narrative does not yet fully earn that phrase.

The story it should be telling is:

1. **Macro shock arrives suddenly**  
2. **Tradable local economies contract immediately**  
3. **Reallocation is not contemporaneous**  
4. **That lag turns a nominal shock into durable structural change**

That is a real narrative. Right now, too much of the paper reads like:
- here is the event,
- here is the design,
- here are the share regressions,
- here is some discussion.

The **timing asymmetry** is the story. Everything should serve that.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?
I would lead with:

> After the 2015 Swiss franc appreciation, municipalities more exposed through their industrial base did not simply bounce back; nearly a decade later they had durably smaller secondary sectors, and service growth showed up only years after the initial hit.

That is the most interesting fact.

### Would people lean in?
Some would lean in—but only if you phrase it right.

If you say, “I have a Swiss municipality DiD using secondary-sector shares,” phones come out.  
If you say, “A sudden exchange-rate appreciation appears to have permanently changed the industrial composition of local labor markets, with a multi-year gap before reallocation,” economists will listen.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Immediately:
- “Is this really about manufacturing tradables, or are you picking up broader local trends?”
- Then: “What happened to workers during the transition—employment, wages, migration, commuting, firm exit?”

That second question is crucial. It reveals the paper’s main strategic limitation: it has a compelling reallocation pattern, but limited visibility into what that meant for welfare or labor-market adjustment.

### Are the findings modest?
Yes, somewhat. The findings are not null, but they are modest in the sense that sectoral shares shift rather than dramatic aggregate collapse. That is okay—but the paper must make the case that the **dynamic pattern** is the finding, not just the average magnitude.

The null-ish total employment result could actually help if framed well:  
this was not merely local collapse; it was **slow compositional transformation**.  
That is more interesting than the paper currently makes it seem.
