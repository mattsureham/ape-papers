## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether legalizing consumer fireworks worsens air pollution on the Fourth of July. Using staggered state-level legalization and EPA monitor data, it argues that deregulation shifts fireworks use from centralized public displays to dispersed private combustion, increasing short-run PM2.5 exposure on the one day of the year when this source matters most.

A busy economist should care because the paper is really about whether politically popular consumer deregulation creates measurable environmental externalities that standard environmental policy ignores. The setting is vivid and memorable; the deeper issue is whether decentralized household activity can undo some gains from conventional pollution control.

**Does the paper articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Mostly yes on hook, only partly on significance. The opening fact is strong, and the question is easy to grasp. But the introduction leans quickly into environmental-health background and methods, rather than sharpening the broader economics question: why is fireworks legalization an informative case for deregulation, household pollution, and regulatory blind spots?

**What the first two paragraphs should say instead:**

> Every July 4, U.S. air pollution spikes sharply for reasons that are obvious to households but largely absent from economics: millions of consumers simultaneously burn fireworks. Over the last two decades, many states have made that activity legal, shifting pyrotechnic use from a limited number of professional displays to widespread private combustion. This raises a simple but economically important question: when states deregulate a popular consumer product, do they create a measurable environmental externality?
>
> This paper studies that question using staggered state legalization of consumer fireworks and daily PM2.5 readings from EPA monitors nationwide. I show that legalization increases the excess July 4 pollution spike relative to nearby non-holiday days, with no comparable effects on placebo holidays. The broader point is that consumer-oriented deregulation can generate concentrated pollution episodes that fall outside the usual industrial and transportation sources emphasized in environmental economics.

That version makes the paper about the world, not just about July 4.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution in one sentence:**  
The paper provides quasi-experimental evidence that legalizing consumer fireworks increases Independence Day particulate pollution by shifting emissions from centralized public displays to dispersed household use.

### Evaluation

**Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest papers?**  
Somewhat, but not fully. The paper distinguishes itself from descriptive atmospheric-science work and from studies of fireworks bans in China, but the differentiation still feels defensive and narrow: “first quasi-experimental paper on U.S. fireworks legalization.” That is true, but by itself it is not an AER-level contribution. “First paper on X” is rarely enough unless X is obviously central.

What is missing is a more forceful distinction from broader literatures:
- pollution from episodic shocks,
- environmental consequences of consumer regulation/deregulation,
- household production of externalities,
- salience and political neglect of temporary pollution.

**World question or literature-gap framing?**  
The paper is closer to a **world question** than many submissions, which is good: does legalization worsen air quality? But it keeps slipping into literature-gap language (“first causal estimate,” “no prior work uses quasi-experimental methods”). The stronger framing is the real-world puzzle: governments tightened pollution rules for major emitters while simultaneously legalizing a pollution-intensive consumer activity.

**Could a smart economist explain what's new after reading the introduction?**  
Yes, but probably as: “It’s a DiD paper showing fireworks legalization raises July 4 PM2.5.” That is clear, but also sounds smaller than the paper wants it to sound. Right now the novelty is legible, but the ambition is not.

**What would make this contribution bigger?**
Specific ways:
1. **Move from ambient concentration to welfare relevance.** Even one serious step toward exposure or health consequences would enlarge the paper substantially. Not full mortality estimates, but something beyond “PM2.5 matters.”
2. **Show reallocation, not just more pollution.** The most interesting mechanism is that legalization decentralizes emissions. If the paper could document that pollution increases especially in residential/suburban monitors relative to downtown/central-display areas, that would be much more distinctive.
3. **Quantify the aggregate externality.** How large is the added pollution in person-exposure or monitor-days above thresholds? The current effect is interpretable but not yet consequential.
4. **Frame as consumer deregulation under neglected externalities.** Compare fireworks to other politically popular but diffuse sources. The current framing leaves the paper stranded in a fireworks niche.
5. **Use heterogeneity that speaks to economic theory.** Border states, urban density, baseline enforcement, or professional-display prevalence could help tell a broader story about substitution between centralized and decentralized activity.

The biggest upgrade would be to make the paper about **decentralized consumer externalities created by deregulation**, with fireworks as a sharp case.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
The world has made long-run progress on regulating industrial and transportation pollution, but there remain short-lived spikes from activities outside standard environmental policy. Fireworks are one such activity, and states have recently moved toward legalization rather than restriction.

### Tension
We know fireworks create pollution in a physical sense, but we do not know whether legalizing consumer access actually worsens ambient air quality in a policy-relevant way. It could be that legal sales mostly formalize preexisting illegal use, or that substitution from public to private fireworks changes the spatial pattern more than the total amount.

### Resolution
The paper finds a positive increase in excess July 4 PM2.5 after legalization, with null effects on placebo holidays, and suggestive evidence that fuller legalization matters more than sparklers-only regimes.

### Implications
Deregulating a consumer product can create a measurable environmental externality that is concentrated, underregulated, and absent from the legislative calculus. Environmental policy may miss important household sources, especially when they are episodic and politically salient in a positive way.

### Evaluation
The paper has a **serviceable** arc, but it is not yet a fully satisfying narrative. At present, it is a bit too close to “here is a clever design applied to an obvious setting.” The tension is weak because the reader’s prior is already that fireworks pollute. So the real narrative needs to be recentered around something less obvious:

- not “do fireworks create pollution?”  
- but “does legalization materially change realized exposure, and why did regulation move in exactly the opposite direction?”

Right now it is somewhat a **collection of sensible results looking for a bigger story**. The story it should tell is:

> States liberalized a consumer activity whose externality is temporally concentrated and politically neglected. Fireworks provide a clean test case for whether household deregulation generates measurable pollution that standard environmental policy does not address.

That is a stronger arc.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?
“States that legalized consumer fireworks experienced a larger Fourth of July PM2.5 spike, suggesting that consumer deregulation creates real ambient pollution costs, not just more legal sales.”

### Would people lean in?
For a minute, yes. The topic is vivid and intuitive. Economists like papers with memorable institutional settings.

But then the room will quickly ask: **How big is it really?** And **why should this matter beyond fireworks?**

That is the paper’s challenge. The setting gets attention; the current scale and framing do not yet sustain it.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Probably one of these:
1. “Is this just one noisy holiday effect, or does it meaningfully affect exposure and welfare?”
2. “Does legalization increase total fireworks use, or just move activity from public shows to private neighborhoods?”
3. “Is the lesson about fireworks, or about deregulation of household externalities more generally?”

Those are exactly the questions the current paper does not fully answer.

### If findings are modest or null-ish
This paper is in an awkward middle ground: the estimates are positive and plausible, but imprecise. That is not fatal if the paper makes the right case. A null or modest result can be interesting if the question is important and priors are ambiguous. But here the priors are not ambiguous—people already think fireworks pollute—so the paper needs to show either:
- that legalization changes pollution by a policy-relevant amount, or
- that the result illuminates a broader class of neglected externalities.

Otherwise it risks reading like a partially underpowered confirmation of intuition.

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

### What would make it read better?

1. **Shorten the methods language in the introduction.**  
   The introduction currently spends too much prestige capital explaining estimator choice and “clean identification.” That is not the editorial margin to press before referees. Use that space to elevate the question and implications.

2. **Bring the main economic insight forward.**  
   The key conceptual mechanism—legalization shifts emissions from centralized public displays to dispersed private combustion—is excellent and should appear earlier and more forcefully. It is the paper’s best chance at being more than a holiday-pollution note.

3. **Trim institutional detail unless it serves the economics.**  
   The state-by-state rollout section is useful but overlong relative to the payoff. Some of it can move to appendix or be compressed into a table plus a concise paragraph.

4. **Front-load the punchline more cleanly.**  
   The reader should know, by page 2, not just that the estimate is positive, but why this changes how we think about regulation.

5. **Be careful not to oversell dose-response if the table doesn’t support it clearly.**  
   The prose says the full-legalization estimate is larger, but the table appears to show the opposite numerically. Even ignoring econometrics, this is narratively damaging. The paper cannot afford sloppiness in presenting its central mechanism.

6. **The conclusion should do more than summarize.**  
   Right now it restates findings and says policymakers ignored the externality. The conclusion should instead say what broader class of policies this informs and what economists should learn from the case.

7. **Move some defensive prose out of the main text.**  
   Repeated reminders about small numbers of treated states and estimator properties are better handled more compactly. The paper currently sounds self-protective rather than agenda-setting.

### Are there buried results that should be promoted?
The placebo logic is actually one of the paper’s strongest storytelling tools. Not because of identification per se, but because it sharpens the economic mechanism. That should remain prominent.

What is missing is a more prominent descriptive figure showing:
- the national July 4 spike,
- and ideally how it changes pre/post legalization in treated vs untreated states.

AER readers often need one visual that makes the paper feel inevitable.
