### **Review of “The Cash Curtain That Never Lifted: Greece’s POS Mandate and the Persistence of Informality”**

**1. Idea Fidelity**
The paper largely adheres to the original research idea. It exploits the staggered, sector-level POS terminal mandate (Law 4446/2016) as a natural experiment, uses Eurostat SBS data on establishments, employment, and wages, and implements a staggered difference-in-differences (DiD) framework with never-treated control sectors. The core research question—whether the mandate increased formal business activity—is directly addressed. However, the paper deviates in one notable way: the main specification uses a national sector-year panel (10 sectors, 80 observations) rather than fully exploiting the **regional-sector panel** (13 NUTS2 regions × sectors) described in the original manifest. The regional panel is included only as a robustness check (col. 4 of Table 1). The original plan’s power advantage—using within-region, across-sector variation and absorbing region-by-year shocks—is thus underutilized in the primary analysis.

**2. Summary**
This paper provides a well-identified, quasi-experimental test of whether mandating electronic payment terminals formalizes economic activity. Using Greece’s sector-level POS mandate and Eurostat business statistics, it finds precisely null effects on the number of formal establishments, employment, and wages. The result is robust across specifications and suggests that payment technology alone, without complementary enforcement, fails to reduce informality.

**3. Essential Points** (Critical issues that must be addressed)
*   **Outcome Mismatch: Measuring the Wrong Margin?** The paper’s null finding may stem from measuring *extensive* margin formalization (new formal establishments/employees) while the policy likely targets the *intensive* margin (underreporting of revenue by *existing* formal businesses). The POS mandate creates a transaction paper trail, which should reduce revenue concealment, not necessarily cause informal firms to register. The chosen outcomes (establishment/employment counts) may be insensitive to the policy’s primary mechanism. The authors must explicitly confront this mismatch: Does theory predict an extensive-margin effect? If not, they should incorporate intensive-margin outcomes (e.g., average wages, revenue per establishment) or directly analyze VAT revenue data mentioned in the manifest.
*   **Suspicious Standard Errors in Heterogeneity Analysis.** In Table 5 (heterogeneous effects by sector), the standard errors for all five sector-specific coefficients are reported identically as “(0.0161)”. This is highly improbable and suggests a computation or reporting error (e.g., mistakenly reporting a common residual SE instead of cluster-robust SEs). This table must be corrected, as it currently undermines credibility. Standard errors should be recalculated with appropriate clustering.
*   **Insufficient Evidence for Parallel Trends.** The event study (Table 3) is presented as the primary parallel trends test. However, for log establishments, the large, significant pre-trend coefficients at lags 5 and 4 are dismissed as a “liberalization artifact.” This is not convincing without demonstrating that the result is robust to dropping the problematic sector(s). The core identification assumption requires showing parallel trends for the *estimation sample*. The authors should show the event study for the restricted sample (2014–2019) used in Column 2 of Table 4. If pre-trends are not flat in the main sample, the validity of the DiD design is in doubt.

**4. Suggestions** (Constructive recommendations for improvement)
*   **Reframe the Contribution Around Enforcement Capacity.** The discussion section correctly points to the “enforcement gap.” This should be the paper’s central narrative from the introduction onward. Frame the paper not just as a test of a POS mandate, but as a test of the hypothesis that **enforcement technology is ineffective without enforcement capacity**. Cite literature on the necessity of third-party information *verification* (e.g., Pomeranz, 2015) more prominently.
*   **Leverage the Regional Panel as Primary Specification.** The regional-sector panel (814 observations) offers greater statistical power and better controls for local economic shocks (e.g., tourism booms) that differentially affect treated service sectors. Make Equation 2 (with region × year FEs) the main specification. Present the national panel as a supplementary/aggregated view. This aligns with the original design and strengthens internal validity.
*   **Conduct a Formal Power Analysis.** With a precise null result, it’s crucial to demonstrate that the design had power to detect a plausible effect. What is the Minimum Detectable Effect (MDE) given the sample size and variation? Given the large Greek shadow economy, a 5-10% increase in formal employment might be a policy-relevant target. Can the design rule this out? A power calculation (or simulation) would make the null finding more informative.
*   **Improve Event Study Presentation.** Convert Table 3 into a graphical event-study plot (coefficients with 95% CIs over time). This is the standard in applied microeconomics and makes pre-trends and post-effects immediately visual. The current table format is cumbersome.
*   **Deepen the Institutional Context on Enforcement.** The background section notes the mandate did not auto-transmit data to tax authorities. Provide more concrete details: What *were* the enforcement procedures? Were audits linked to POS data? Were there penalties for non-use? Quantify any public investment in audit capacity following the mandate. This context is critical for interpreting the null result.
*   **Address the Capital Controls Confound More Directly.** The paper notes that capital controls (2015) accelerated e-payment adoption, potentially diluting the mandate’s effect. This could also violate the “no-anticipation” assumption if businesses adjusted formalization in 2015-16. Consider a robustness check: (a) define the “pre-period” as 2012-2014, (b) test for a break in trends in 2015, or (c) control for region-sector-specific time trends.
*   **Explore Alternative/Complementary Outcome Data.** As noted in the manifest, secondary data like Eurostat LFS (self-employment counts) or VAT revenue by sector could provide a more direct test of the intensive margin. Even if these data are noisier, a descriptive analysis showing no visible break in trend around 2017 would bolster the main conclusion.
*   **Clarify Policy Implications in the Conclusion.** The conclusion is well-written but could be more directive. Given this null result, what *should* governments do? Pair POS mandates with real-time tax reporting (like Uruguay’s e-invoicing)? Invest in audit departments? The paper’s value is in guiding future policy design; make those lessons explicit.
*   **Technical Corrections:**
    *   **Table 2 (Summary Stats):** The note says “Wages in EUR millions,” but the table header says “Wages (EUR M)”. Make this consistent (preferably “Wages (million EUR)”).
    *   **Table 4 (Robustness):** The row “RI *p*-value” is empty. Remove it or populate it.
    *   **General:** Ensure all *p*-values are reported consistently (e.g., to three decimal places) and that significance stars align with a stated convention (e.g., * p<0.10, ** p<0.05, *** p<0.01).
