## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper examines the impact of Greece’s 2017 mandate requiring 196 professions to install POS terminals, a policy aimed at formalizing Europe’s largest shadow economy. Using a sector-level difference-in-differences design, the author finds a "precise zero": the mandate failed to increase formal establishments, employment, or wages, likely because the digital paper trail was never integrated into tax enforcement. This provides a critical cautionary tale for high-income countries attempting to use technology as a shortcut for institutional audit capacity.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this well in the abstract and the first three paragraphs. However, it leads with the "Greek crisis" narrative, which is slightly dated. It should instead pivot immediately to the global trend of "technological formalization" to grab the AER reader.

**The pitch it should have:** 
"Governments worldwide are mandating electronic payment infrastructure under the assumption that digital trails automatically lead to formalization. This paper tests this 'technological shortcut' hypothesis using Greece's 2017 POS mandate, which combined a supply-side terminal requirement with a demand-side consumer incentive. I find that even in a high-informality EU setting, technology without integrated enforcement yields zero formalization, suggesting that the 'cash curtain' is held in place by institutional capacity, not just transaction medium."

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution:** The paper provides a clean quasi-experimental null result showing that mandating payment technology is insufficient to reduce economic informality absent a "closed-loop" enforcement architecture.

**Evaluation:**
- **Differentiation:** It sits well against Pomeranz (2015) and Naritomi (2019) by showing the *failure* of the same logic (third-party reporting) when the "threat" component is missing.
- **Framing:** It is currently framed as a "Greek policy evaluation." To get into the AER, it needs to be framed as a "Theory of Enforcement" paper.
- **Clarity:** A smart economist would say: "It’s a paper showing that card readers don't stop tax evasion if the government doesn't actually look at the data."
- **Bigger Contribution:** To make it bigger, the author needs to explore *why* it failed beyond "anecdotal evidence." Are there sectors where it worked better? Can the author merge this with actual VAT revenue data or bank-level data?

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

**Closest Neighbors:**

3. **Ulyssea (2020, AER):** Firm informality and development.

**Positioning Strategy:** The paper should position itself as the "missing link" or the "boundary condition" for the Pomeranz/Naritomi logic. It says: "We know third-party reporting works in Chile and Brazil; here is why it fails in an EU context." 

**Missing Conversation:** It needs to speak more to the *political economy* of enforcement. Why would a government mandate the hardware but not the data integration? Is it "security theater" for international creditors (the Troika)?

- **Setup:** A high-informality country adopts a "silver bullet" technology to force formalization.
- **Tension:** Digital trails are created (supply side) and incentivized (demand side), which should—by standard theory—increase the cost of remaining informal.
- **Resolution:** A resounding null. The "plumbing" was installed, but the water never flowed.
- **Implications:** Policy focus must shift from "infrastructure" to "information integration."

**Evaluation:** The arc is clear but lacks "color." It feels a bit like a technical report. It needs more "tension" regarding the 2015 capital controls—did the mandate fail because the "easy gains" were already taken by the crisis-induced shift to cards?

**Reaction:** People will lean in because it's a counter-intuitive failure. The immediate follow-up: "Did VAT revenues go up even if employment didn't?" If the author can't answer that, the paper feels like it's missing the most important outcome.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The gap is **Scope and Mechanism.** 

A null result on "Establishments and Employment" is interesting, but it's a "blunt" outcome. To excite the top of the field, the author needs to show **where the money went.** If firms installed POS terminals but didn't formalize, did they:
1. Under-report the digital sales anyway?
2. Run two sets of books?
3. Only use the POS for a small fraction of sales?

**Single most impactful advice:** Find a way to link this to **VAT revenue data** or **Firm-level financial disclosures.** If you can show that VAT revenue stayed flat despite a surge in card transactions, you have a "Smoking Gun" paper on the limits of technological enforcement.
