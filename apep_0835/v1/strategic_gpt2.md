## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple, policy-relevant question: when a high-informality country forces businesses to install card readers, does economic activity move out of the shadows and into the formal sector? Using Greece’s 2017 POS mandate, the paper argues that the answer is no: payment technology by itself did not increase formal establishments, employment, or reported wages, suggesting that digitization without enforcement is not enough to reduce informality.

A busy economist should care because governments all over the world are betting on digital payments as a tax-enforcement tool. If the finding is right, it cuts against a popular policy instinct: “install the technology and compliance will follow.”

### Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Reasonably well, but not sharply enough. The current opening is vivid and readable, but it spends too much time on scene-setting before crystallizing the broader question. The first two paragraphs should move faster from Greece to the general issue: can payment digitization substitute for state capacity?

### The pitch the paper should have

“Governments increasingly use digital payment mandates to fight tax evasion, on the theory that replacing cash with traceable transactions will pull firms into the formal economy. But does payment technology actually formalize activity, or does it only matter when paired with credible tax enforcement?

I study Greece’s 2017 mandate requiring card-payment terminals in a wide set of high-cash service sectors. Despite being introduced in one of Europe’s most informal economies, the mandate produced no detectable increase in formal establishments, employment, or reported wages. The lesson is that creating a digital trail is not the same as using it: payment infrastructure alone is a weak anti-informality policy.”

That is the version that belongs in an AER introduction.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### Contribution in one sentence

The paper’s core contribution is to show that mandating electronic payment acceptance, even in a highly informal economy, did not measurably formalize business activity absent complementary enforcement.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?

Only partly. The paper gestures at a distinction—this is about a POS mandate rather than aggregate card adoption or stronger information systems—but the differentiation is not yet crisp enough. Right now the contribution risks sounding like: “another reduced-form paper on a tax-compliance technology with a null effect.”

The paper needs to be much more explicit about how this differs from:
- aggregate correlations between digital payments and tax revenue,
- electronic invoicing or e-receipt systems that automatically transmit data,
- third-party reporting settings where enforcement is built in,
- consumer-incentive programs like receipt lotteries.

The paper’s distinctive angle is not “technology and informality” in general. It is: **a weak-form digitization policy that changes acceptance infrastructure but not automatic reporting or enforcement.**

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or as filling a gap in a LITERATURE?

Mostly the former, which is good. The strongest version is: **Do digital payment mandates actually formalize economic activity?** The weaker version is: **There is not yet a causal estimate for Greece’s POS mandate.** The paper slips into the second framing too often.

For AER, this should be framed squarely as a world question:
- Can digitization substitute for enforcement capacity?
- When does a digital paper trail matter?
- Are governments overestimating what payment technology can do by itself?

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?

Not confidently enough. Right now they might say: “It’s a DiD on a Greek POS mandate and finds null effects.” That is not a strong enough takeaway.

The goal is that they instead say: “It shows that payment acceptance mandates are fundamentally different from true information-reporting reforms; if data are not automatically integrated into enforcement, digitization alone may do little.”

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Several possibilities:

1. **Better outcome framing.**  
   “Formal business activity” measured with establishments, employment, and wages is sensible but indirect. A bigger paper would get closer to:
   - VAT remittances,
   - reported sales,
   - tax declarations,
   - card-share of transactions,
   - business survival/new registrations,
   - audit activity or tax collections.

2. **Sharper mechanism evidence.**  
   The paper’s main interpretive claim is about enforcement. But the evidence on mechanism is mostly conceptual. The paper would be much bigger if it could show one of the following:
   - terminal adoption rose but card usage did not;
   - card usage rose but reported tax liabilities did not;
   - sectors with stronger audit exposure responded more;
   - places/firms more exposed to tax-data integration responded more;
   - effects differ by business types where evasion via cash is most feasible.

3. **A more incisive comparison.**  
   The paper would be stronger if it explicitly compared the Greek POS reform to reforms where electronic transactions were automatically transmitted to the tax authority. That contrast would elevate the paper from “null result in Greece” to “which digital reforms work, and why.”

4. **A broader framing around state capacity.**  
   The contribution gets bigger if positioned not as a Greek tax paper, but as evidence on the limits of technology as a substitute for administrative capacity.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

### Closest neighbors

The closest neighbors appear to be:

- **Pomeranz (2015, AER)** on third-party reporting and VAT enforcement in Chile.
- **Kleven, Kreiner, and Saez / Kleven (2016 JEP or related work)** on why third-party information matters for tax compliance.
- **Naritomi (2019, AER or close neighbor depending exact citation context)** on consumer-side enforcement / receipt incentives in Brazil.
- **Artavanis, Morse, and Tsoutsoura (2016, QJE)** on self-employed income underreporting in Greece.
- **Hondroyiannis and Papaoikonomou / related Greece digital-payments papers** on aggregate digital payments and VAT revenue.
- Potentially **Bergolo et al.** on e-invoicing / tax enforcement in Uruguay.

### How should the paper position itself relative to those neighbors?

It should **build on and separate itself from** them.

- Relative to **Pomeranz/Kleven**: this paper should say, “Those papers show information matters when tax authorities can use it. I study a policy that generated the possibility of digital records without true third-party reporting.”
- Relative to **Naritomi**: “Consumer pressure works when receipts are linked to an administrative system. Greece offered payment incentives but did not close the enforcement loop.”
- Relative to **Artavanis et al.**: “We know Greece had large evasion. The open question is whether digital-payment mandates reduce it.”
- Relative to aggregate Greece papers: “Those correlations do not identify the effect of the mandate itself and cannot disentangle crisis-driven payment adoption from the mandate.”

The paper should not “attack” these papers. It should use them to define a taxonomy:
1. payment adoption,
2. receipt incentives,
3. electronic invoicing,
4. third-party reporting,
5. payment acceptance mandates.

Then it can say: the Greek reform is category 5, and category 5 appears weak without categories 3 and 4.

### Is the paper positioned too narrowly or too broadly?

At present, oddly both.

- **Too narrowly** in design and data: it reads like a country-policy evaluation.
- **Too broadly** in rhetoric: it occasionally jumps from one Greek POS reform to sweeping claims about digital anti-informality policy in general.

The right move is to narrow the claim but broaden the conceptual contribution. Not “digital payment programs don’t work,” but “acceptance mandates are not the same thing as enforceable digital reporting systems.”

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?

It should speak more directly to:
- the tax administration/state capacity literature,
- the digitization-of-government literature,
- the firm informality literature beyond tax compliance alone,
- the payments literature in IO/household finance if the mechanism runs through transaction behavior,
- public finance work on salience and compliance technologies,
- political economy of administrative reform, if enforcement gaps are institutional rather than technological.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?

Almost, but not quite. It currently speaks mainly to “informality and tax compliance.” The more interesting conversation is: **what kinds of digital reforms create usable state capacity, and which only create visible hardware?** That is the higher-level conversation top journals care about.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Greece has severe tax evasion and a large shadow economy. Policymakers, under intense fiscal pressure, turned to digital payments as a modern anti-informality tool.

### Tension

The policy logic sounds powerful: if transactions move from cash to cards, they become traceable, and formalization should follow. But there is a conceptual tension: does traceability matter if the state does not automatically collect, match, and enforce against the resulting information?

### Resolution

The paper finds no detectable effect of the POS mandate on formal establishments, employment, or wages.

### Implications

Digitizing payment acceptance is not enough. The effectiveness of digital anti-evasion policy hinges less on the presence of terminals than on whether transaction information is integrated into enforcement.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

Mostly yes. This is one of the paper’s strengths. The title, opening anecdote, and conclusion all point toward the same story. But the middle of the paper sometimes reverts to “here is a policy evaluation with several outcomes and specifications,” which dilutes the narrative.

The paper is not a pure collection of results looking for a story. There is a story. The problem is that the story is stronger than the evidence currently marshaled to support its mechanism. The narrative says “enforcement gap”; the evidence says “null reduced-form effect on formal-sector aggregates.” That is plausible, but not fully closed.

### What story should it be telling?

Not “Greece’s POS mandate failed.” That is too local.  
The story should be: **Digital reforms differ in whether they merely create transactional infrastructure or actually create enforceable information. Greece is an example of the former, and the former may do little.**

That framing gives the paper conceptual legs.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?

“In one of Europe’s most informal economies, forcing hundreds of professions to install card readers did not measurably increase formal employment, establishments, or wages.”

That is a clean and memorable fact.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

Some would lean in—especially public finance, development, and macro/public-policy economists—because the policy intuition is so widespread. But many would immediately ask: “Did card use actually increase?” and “Are these the right outcomes?” If the speaker cannot answer those, interest will fade quickly.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Probably one of these:
1. “So did the mandate actually change payment behavior?”
2. “Why should formal employment or wages move if the reform mainly affects underreported revenue?”
3. “Is this a statement about POS mandates specifically, or about digital tax enforcement more broadly?”
4. “What distinguishes this from e-invoicing and third-party reporting reforms that do work?”

Those are exactly the questions the paper needs to anticipate and front-load.

### If findings are null or modest: is the null interesting?

Yes, potentially very interesting. But only if the paper makes clear that this is not “nothing happened in Greece”; it is “a widely discussed policy lever may be much weaker than policymakers think when not linked to enforcement architecture.”

At present, the paper is somewhat effective on this front, but it needs to press harder. A null result is valuable here because:
- the policy was salient and nationwide,
- the country had unusually high informality,
- the reform targets a mechanism many policymakers believe in,
- the null helps distinguish hardware adoption from actual enforceable reporting.

Without that framing, it risks feeling like a failed attempt to detect an effect in coarse data.

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

### What would make it read better?

   This is not because the econometrics are unimportant, but because the paper’s main bottleneck is strategic positioning, not technical credibility. The narrative should not bog down in estimator labels too early.

6. **Either cut or reframe the heterogeneity section.**

7. **The conclusion is decent, but it can do more than summarize.**  
   It should end by stating the broader taxonomy of digital reforms:
   - acceptance mandates,
   - reporting mandates,
   - enforcement integration.
   
   That would leave the reader with a general lesson rather than a country-specific moral.

### Is the good stuff front-loaded?

Mostly yes. The abstract is strong and the intro gives the answer early. That is good. But the conceptual hook should be even more front-loaded than the specific estimates.

### Are there results buried that should be in the main results?

The most important “result” for positioning is not another robustness check; it is the paper’s ability to show that this policy changed infrastructure but not formalization. If there is any direct evidence on POS diffusion, card usage by sector, or administrative non-integration, that belongs prominently in the main text. Without it, the paper feels one step removed from the policy mechanism.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

Be honest: in current form, this is not yet an AER paper.

### What is the gap?

Mostly a mix of **scope problem**, **novelty problem**, and **framing problem**.

- **Framing problem:** The paper’s best idea is bigger than its current presentation. It should be a paper about the limits of digitization without enforcement, not merely a Greek null DiD.
- **Scope problem:** The outcomes are too indirect for the size of the claim. For AER, the paper likely needs evidence closer to the mechanism or policy target.
- **Novelty problem:** The broad idea that information matters only when enforceable is already in the literature. So to be publishable at the frontier, this paper needs to show something sharper: not merely that “technology alone may not work,” but specifically which component of digitalization is missing and why that distinction matters empirically.

I do **not** think the central problem is ambition in the writing; the title and setup are actually ambitious. The issue is that the empirical payload currently does not fully match the conceptual ambition.

### What would excite the top 10 people in this field?

One of two things:

1. **Mechanism-resolving evidence**
   - Did terminal adoption occur?
   - Did card transaction share rise?
   - Did reported tax liabilities or sales fail to rise?
   - Did effects appear only where tax administration could actually use the data?

2. **A stronger comparative framing**
   - Put Greece in a broader conceptual or empirical comparison with reforms that involved real-time reporting, e-invoicing, or receipt-linked enforcement.
   - Then the paper becomes: “which digital compliance technologies work?” rather than “what happened in Greece?”

### Single most impactful advice

If the author could change only one thing: **rebuild the paper around the distinction between payment acceptance and enforceable information, and add evidence that directly speaks to that distinction.**

That is the only route by which this can become more than a competent null policy evaluation.
