## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether a salient regulatory threshold in the nonprofit sector—the IRS rule requiring organizations above \$200,000 in gross receipts to file the more burdensome Form 990 rather than the simpler 990-EZ—actually distorts organizational growth. Its main message is a null: nonprofits do not appear to bunch meaningfully below the threshold or suppress growth to avoid crossing it, suggesting that this particular compliance discontinuity is too small to operate as a real “ceiling.”

Why should a busy economist care? In principle, this is about a broad question: when do regulatory thresholds distort firm behavior, and when do they not? That is an important question. But in the current draft, the paper feels much more like a sector-specific application with a null result than like a paper that changes how economists think about compliance thresholds generally.

### Does the paper articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Reasonably, but not strongly enough. The introduction currently starts from the institutional detail and then narrows quickly to the specific IRS threshold. It does not fully earn the reader’s attention by stating the bigger economic question first. Also, it overclaims “natural experiment” language and then immediately retreats because the paper cannot actually study the reform directly. That weakens confidence in the opening.

### What the first two paragraphs should say instead

The first two paragraphs should foreground the general question and only then introduce the nonprofit setting as a clean test case. Something like:

> Many regulations create size-based reporting thresholds, and economists often worry that such thresholds distort behavior by inducing firms and organizations to stay artificially small. But we know much less about when these compliance discontinuities are actually large enough to matter. This paper studies a simple and policy-relevant case: the IRS rule requiring nonprofits above \$200,000 in gross receipts to file the full Form 990 rather than the shorter 990-EZ.  
>
> If reporting burdens materially constrain organizational decisions, nonprofits should cluster below this threshold and exhibit slower growth when they approach it. I find little evidence of either pattern. Bunching at \$200,000 is no larger than at placebo round numbers, and organizations near the threshold do not show slower revenue, expense, or asset growth. The broader implication is that not all disclosure thresholds meaningfully distort scale; some apparent “compliance cliffs” are simply too small to change behavior.

That pitch is cleaner, world-facing, and more ambitious.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper shows that the IRS Form 990 filing threshold at \$200,000 does not appear to create economically meaningful bunching or growth suppression among small nonprofits, implying that this compliance discontinuity is not a binding constraint on organizational scale.

Only partially. The paper distinguishes itself from the general bunching literature and from SEC-threshold papers by saying: “here is a threshold where bunching fails to materialize.” That is fine as a start, but the differentiation is not yet sharp enough. Right now, the reader can understand that this is “a null bunching paper in nonprofits,” but not why this null substantially advances the conversation.

The draft needs to be much clearer on the exact novelty relative to:
1. prior work documenting bunching at the old nonprofit threshold,
2. broader papers on reporting/compliance thresholds,
3. papers on optimization frictions and when bunching should be absent.

At present, the contribution reads more as “we checked whether this threshold matters, and it doesn’t” than “we learned something general about when compliance thresholds do and do not distort behavior.”

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or filling a gap in a LITERATURE?

Mixed, but too literature-gap oriented in places. The strongest framing is world-facing: **Do disclosure thresholds actually keep organizations small?** The weaker framing is: **the literature has not studied nulls enough.** The former is stronger and should dominate.

### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what’s new?

Not confidently. They would probably say: “It’s a paper on whether nonprofits bunch below the Form 990 threshold, and they don’t find much.” That is not yet an AER-level contribution statement. It still sounds like “another DiD/bunching paper about a threshold.”

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Several possibilities:

- **Different framing:** Recast the paper as about the **limits of compliance-threshold distortion**, not about a specific IRS form. The central question should be: when do size-dependent disclosure rules matter enough to change behavior?
- **Different comparison:** The paper would be bigger if it could compare **low-cost nonprofit thresholds** to **high-cost corporate thresholds** in a unified framework. Even a structured comparative discussion would help.
- **Different mechanism:** The current mechanism discussion is speculative. A bigger paper would directly separate **preparation cost** from **disclosure cost** and ask which dimension of regulation actually bites.
- **Different outcome:** Growth is sensible, but the more distinctive margin may be **organizational form, filing behavior, accounting outsourcing, program composition, fundraising strategy, or compensation disclosure avoidance**. If nonprofits do not suppress revenue, do they respond elsewhere?
- **Different scope:** The paper would be stronger if it could say something like: “Thresholds distort behavior only when fixed compliance costs exceed X percent of budget,” rather than only “this one threshold does not seem to matter.”

As written, the paper is competent but too small in ambition.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

The world is full of regulatory thresholds, and economists worry they create bunching and real distortions. In the nonprofit sector, the Form 990 threshold is a plausible case where organizations might stay small to avoid more onerous disclosure.

### Tension

The puzzle is whether this threshold actually acts as a binding ceiling. Theory says yes if compliance costs matter; but it is also plausible that modern filing technology and modest incremental burden make the threshold behaviorally irrelevant. The old literature and intuition point one way; today’s institutional environment may point another.

### Resolution

The paper finds essentially no meaningful bunching at \$200,000 and no evidence that organizations near the threshold grow more slowly. By the paper’s account, this compliance cliff is not much of a cliff.

### Implications

The implications should be: economists and policymakers should not assume all reporting thresholds create distortions; whether they bind depends on the magnitude of compliance costs relative to organizational scale and available technology. That is the paper’s broader lesson.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the bones of one, but not a fully satisfying arc. The paper currently reads somewhat like a collection of sensible tests around a single null finding. The story exists, but it is not yet powerful enough.

The main weakness is that the “tension” is underdeveloped. Why should we have expected this threshold to matter ex ante? The paper says theory predicts bunching, but the institutional details it later gives also suggest the costs may be tiny at \$200,000. So the reader is left with: “Maybe this matters, maybe it doesn’t.” That is not enough tension for a top general-interest journal.

### What story should it be telling?

The story should be:

1. **Economists have learned from many settings that thresholds can distort behavior.**
2. **But we still do not know how large a compliance discontinuity has to be before organizations actually change real decisions.**
3. **The nonprofit Form 990 threshold is a useful test case because it is a clean, common, policy-relevant disclosure discontinuity.**
4. **The answer is that this threshold does not distort scale in detectable ways.**
5. **Therefore, the existence of a formal compliance cliff is not enough; the effective burden has to be large relative to organizational budgets and frictions.**

That is a real narrative. The current draft only partially delivers it.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?

“I looked at whether small nonprofits stay under the \$200,000 IRS reporting threshold to avoid filing the long Form 990, and basically they don’t.”

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

A few public finance or nonprofit economists would lean in. Most economists would probably wait for the second sentence. The key is whether that second sentence is:

- weak: “So there’s no effect,” or
- strong: “So not all compliance thresholds distort behavior; many apparent cliffs are too small to matter.”

The paper currently lands closer to the weak version.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Likely one of these:

- “Is that because the compliance costs are trivial?”
- “How does this compare to SEC or VAT thresholds?”
- “Is there another margin where they respond instead?”
- “Why should I care about one nonprofit filing cutoff?”

That last question is the dangerous one. The paper needs a better answer.

### If findings are null or modest: is the null itself interesting?

Potentially yes, but only if the paper makes the null do conceptual work. A good null paper teaches us where a widely used mechanism does **not** operate and why. This draft gestures at that, but it does not yet fully convert the null into a positive lesson.

Right now it still risks feeling like a failed attempt to find bunching rather than a paper about the boundaries of threshold effects. To avoid that, the paper must more explicitly argue:

- why many economists would have expected distortion here,
- why learning there is none is informative,
- and what this teaches us about threshold design more generally.
