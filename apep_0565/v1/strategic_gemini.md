## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
This paper documents a massive "credential cliff" in South Africa, where arbitrary one-point differences on high-school exit exams (the "matric") sort students into tiers that the labor market rewards with vastly different employment outcomes. It shows that South Africa is a global outlier in its education premium (17pp) and proposes a multi-cutoff RDD "blueprint" to eventually measure the causal impact of these labels using restricted-access microdata.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates the pitch well in the first paragraph, emphasizing the mechanical and life-altering nature of the 49% vs. 50% score. However, it over-promises on the "RDD" aspect. The first two paragraphs lead the reader to believe they are about to see an RDD result, only for the paper to pivot to descriptive aggregate data.

**The pitch the paper should have:**
"In South Africa, a single percentage point on a matric exam determines a student’s entire educational trajectory and employment prospects. This paper documents a 'credential cliff'—a 20-percentage-point gap in employment absorption between matric completion and a post-school credential—and positions South Africa as a global outlier in the labor market returns to education. While I develop the formal RDD framework necessary to estimate causal effects at these thresholds, I use aggregate and cross-country data to show that these gaps are structural, stable over time, and were exacerbated by the COVID-19 pandemic."

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**The Contribution:** The paper documents the extreme convexity of South Africa’s education-employment gradient and provides the theoretical and empirical roadmap for a multi-cutoff RDD in this setting.

- **Differentiation:** It differentiates itself from South African literature (Van der Berg, Spaull) by focusing on the *pass-tier* thresholds rather than just school quality. Internationally, it links to the "marginal student" RDD literature (Zimmerman, 2014; Kirkeboen, 2016) but offers a unique "triple-cutoff" national setting.
- **World vs. Literature:** It frames itself as answering a question about the **world** (specifically the South African bottleneck), which is a strength.
- **Explainability:** A smart economist would say, "It’s a descriptive paper documenting huge employment jumps at education thresholds in SA, plus a 'how-to' guide for an RDD that the author hasn't actually run yet."
- **Bigger Contribution:** The contribution is currently held back by the **lack of the actual RDD results**. To make this an AER-level contribution without that microdata, the paper would need to lean much harder into the *signaling vs. human capital* theory, perhaps using the aggregate data to more decisively rule out alternative explanations for the "cliff."

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
- **Neighbors:** *Zimmerman (2014)* on college admission RDD; *Banerjee et al. (2008)* on SA labor market structure; *Cattaneo et al. (2020)* on multi-cutoff designs; *Spence (1973)* on signaling.
- **Positioning:** It builds on the RDD literature and synthesizes it with the SA labor market literature. It is currently positioned a bit too much as a "methodology preview."
- **Niche/Broad:** It risks being a "niche" SA paper unless it can frame the South African case as the "canary in the coal mine" for labor markets with high search frictions and rigid formal sectors.
- **The "Right" Conversation:** It should speak more to the **Search and Matching** literature. The "cliff" is a symptom of a market where employers are so overwhelmed by applicants that they use coarse credentials as a primary filter.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC
- **Setup:** A high-unemployment economy with a legacy of unequal education.
- **Tension:** A massive "cliff" exists where credentials yield huge rewards, but the system is designed to limit those credentials.
- **Resolution:** The paper finds the cliff is stable, widening after COVID, and is a unique structural feature of SA. It offers a blueprint for causal proof.
- **Implications:** Policy should focus on capacity at the post-school level, not just matric pass rates.

**Evaluation:** The arc is currently "interrupted." It sets up a causal tension but provides a descriptive resolution. It feels like "The Prequel" to a major paper.

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
- **The Fact:** "In South Africa, having a diploma makes you 20% more likely to have a job than just having a high school degree—the largest such gap in the world."
- **The Reaction:** People lean in at the 20% number, but reach for their phones when you explain you don't have the individual-level data to prove it's causal yet.
- **Follow-up:** "How much of that is just smarter kids getting the diploma?" (The paper tries to answer this with Oster bounds, but that's rarely enough for the AER).

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The gap is **data access**. As the editor, I cannot send a paper to referees that says "I have designed a great study but I don't have the data to run it."

**The single most impactful piece of advice:** Focus the paper entirely on the **structural anomaly of the South African labor market**. Make it a "Short Paper" (AER: Insights style) that uses the aggregate data and the cross-country comparison to pose a challenge to standard labor models. Stop calling it an RDD paper; call it a "Structural Bottleneck" paper.
