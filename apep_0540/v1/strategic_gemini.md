### 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
This paper examines the "dark side" of infrastructure development: the decade-long construction phase that precedes the benefits of new transit. Using the massive Grand Paris Express project as a quasi-experiment, the authors find that active construction depresses nearby property values by over 7%, suggesting that "value capture" financing and welfare models may be significantly overestimating the net benefits of urban mega-projects by ignoring the prolonged pain of their delivery.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this well in the first paragraph, but it leans slightly too heavily on the "Grand Paris" context as a case study. To hit the AER bar, the pitch should emphasize the *temporal misalignment* of capitalization. 
**Revised Pitch:** "Standard transit capitalization models assume a smooth transition from announcement to opening. By documenting a 15-percentage-point reversal from anticipation premiums to construction-phase losses, this paper shows that the 'gestation cost' of infrastructure is a first-order welfare and financing concern that can wipe out a decade of expected wealth gains for local residents."

---

### 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**Contribution:** The paper identifies and quantifies a persistent negative externality of transit construction that reverses prior anticipatory gains, challenging the monotonicity assumption in infrastructure capitalization literature.

- **Differentiated?** Yes. Most literature (Gibbons/Machin, Billings) focuses on the "before" and "after." This paper identifies a "during" that lasts long enough to define the entire ownership period of a median household.
- **Question about the World or Literature?** It starts as a question about the world (Paris) but pivots effectively to a question about the validity of value-capture financing—a core policy mechanism.
- **Explanation to a colleague:** "It's the paper that shows the jackhammers eat the transit premium for ten years."
- **Bigger Contribution:** To be truly AER-level, it needs to move from "construction is loud/annoying" (an engineering fact) to "why doesn't the market price this correctly at the DUP stage?" (an economic puzzle). Is it salience, myopia, or credit constraints?

---

### 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
- **Closest Neighbors:** *Gibbons and Machin (2005)* on transit capitalization; *McMillen (2004)* on noise; *Gamper-Rabindran and Timmins (2022)* on tunnels.
- **Positioning:** It should **synthesize** the environmental externality literature with the urban finance literature. Currently, it feels a bit like a real estate paper; it needs to be an *applied micro* paper.
- **Unexpected Connection:** The authors should connect to the **Behavioral Economics** literature on myopia or the **Political Economy** of "NIMBYism." If construction is this painful, local opposition isn't just "obstruction"—it’s a rational response to a decade of wealth erosion.

---

### 4. NARRATIVE ARC
- **Setup:** Transit increases property values; cities use this to fund projects.
- **Tension:** Mega-projects take 10-15 years to build. If the "disamenity" period is long enough, the net present value for current residents might actually be negative.
- **Resolution:** Construction causes a ~7% price drop and significant compositional sorting (wealthier buyers flee).
- **Implications:** Value capture taxes are being levied on "gains" that are actually "losses" during the construction phase, creating a fundamental mismatch in infrastructure finance.

---

### 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
At a dinner party: "We found that if you buy a house near a future metro station in Paris, you'll actually lose €22,000 in wealth the moment they start digging, and you won't see a cent of 'transit premium' for a decade."
- **Response:** "So, if I'm a mayor, I'm taxing people for a benefit they won't live to see?" This is a strong "lean in" moment.

---

### 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
- **Front-load the Mechanism:** The "Compositional Sorting" (Section 7.1) is actually more interesting than the 7.4% price drop. It suggests a "flight of the wealthy" from construction zones. Move this earlier to show it’s not just "noise" but a fundamental shift in neighborhood demand.
- **Appendix:** Move the detailed description of "shaft excavation" and "slurry treatment" to an appendix. Focus more on the price dynamics.
- **Heterogeneity:** Table 3 (Heterogeneity by Line) is a bit messy. It would be more powerful to group lines by "Baseline Income of Neighborhood" to show if the poor suffer more from the disamenity.

---

### 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The paper is currently a very high-quality *Journal of Urban Economics* paper. To make it **AER**, it needs more **ambition regarding the "Why."**

**The single most impactful piece of advice:**
The authors should develop the "Temporal Misalignment" story (Section 7.3) into a formal welfare analysis. They need to calculate the **Intertemporal Net Present Value** for a resident with a 7-year tenure. If the NPV is negative because the construction phase is so long, it provides a rigorous economic theory for why people fight transit—not because they are "anti-progress," but because the lifecycle of infrastructure is longer than the lifecycle of a household's residence.
