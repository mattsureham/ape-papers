## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple and potentially important question: when the government suddenly injects large amounts of mandated, nearly free labor into a shortage sector, does that displace paid workers or instead help the sector expand? Using Switzerland’s 2009 reform that massively expanded civilian-service placements in health and social care, the paper argues that paid employment in those sectors rose relative to other services, suggesting complementarity rather than crowd-out.

A busy economist should care because this is not just a Swiss institutional curiosity. It speaks to a broad question about labor-market incidence and production constraints: when low-cost labor arrives, do firms substitute away from regular workers, or can extra labor unlock expansion in constrained sectors such as healthcare?

### Does the paper articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Reasonably well, but not optimally. The current opening has the ingredients, but it is too “paper about a reform” and not enough “paper about a first-order economic question.” It gets to the policy shock quickly, which is good, but the framing is still narrower than it should be. The big idea is not really “Swiss civilian service”; it is “what happens when the state supplies cheap labor to a constrained sector?” That is the hook.

Also, the intro currently gives away too much design detail too early and then muddies the message by introducing caveats before the contribution is fully sold. The first two paragraphs should be crisper, bigger-picture, and more world-oriented.

### The pitch the paper should have

Governments around the world subsidize labor in shortage sectors through service programs, public placements, immigration policy, and quasi-mandated work schemes. The core economic question is whether these labor injections crowd out regular employment or instead relax bottlenecks that let constrained sectors expand. Healthcare is the canonical setting, because production often requires teams, supervision, and minimum staffing levels.

This paper studies a rare, sharp policy shock to that question: Switzerland’s 2009 reform of civilian service, which abruptly quadrupled admissions and sent a large wave of near-zero-marginal-cost workers into health and social care. I show that paid employment in those sectors rose relative to comparable services after the reform, consistent with mandated labor acting as a complement rather than a substitute in a labor-constrained care economy.

That is the AER version of the pitch.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper’s contribution is to show that a sudden government-created influx of mandated, low-cost labor into healthcare and social care can increase rather than reduce paid employment, implying complementarity in a constrained sector.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3-4 papers?

Only partially. The paper says it is different from conscription papers because those study effects on conscripts rather than host sectors, and different from immigration/labor-supply papers because the labor shock here is mandated and quasi-free. That is true, but the differentiation is still somewhat schematic.

Right now the introduction risks sounding like: “Here is another labor supply shock paper, but in a different institutional setting.” For AER, that is not enough. The paper needs to be much more explicit about what this setting uniquely identifies conceptually:

- The labor is **not market-priced** at the point of use.
- The labor is **allocated into institutions** rather than into local labor markets generally.
- The receiving sector is one where **nonlinear production and staffing constraints** plausibly matter.
- The shock therefore tests not just labor-demand elasticity, but the boundary between **substitution and capacity expansion**.

That is the real conceptual distinction.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a gap in a literature?

At present, too much of the contribution paragraph is framed as filling literature gaps. “The conscription literature has not studied X,” “the labor supply shock literature has studied Y,” etc. That is useful but second-order.

The stronger framing is world-facing: **Do low-cost public labor programs shrink regular jobs, or can they unlock expansion where production is bottlenecked?** That is a substantive question about how sectors work, not about who has or has not written a paper yet.

### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what’s new?

Right now, maybe, but not cleanly. They could probably say: “It’s a DiD on Swiss civilian service and healthcare employment; surprisingly, paid jobs go up.” That is decent. But they might also say: “It’s another labor-supply-shock paper with sector-level DiD.”

That second reaction is dangerous. The paper needs a sharper sentence saying why this setting is unlike standard labor-supply shocks and why the result changes how we think about labor scarcity in care sectors.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

A few specific routes:

1. **Make the paper about production constraints, not just employment.**  
   Employment alone is one step short. If the paper could show that the extra mandated labor expanded beds, patient volume, occupancy, wait times, or service provision, then the claim becomes much bigger: not just “paid jobs rose,” but “cheap auxiliary labor relaxed bottlenecks and increased output.”

2. **Show where complementarity is strongest.**  
   If complementarity is the contribution, heterogeneity is the substance. Is it strongest in residential eldercare? Highly regulated institutions? High-skill-intensive settings where support labor frees professional labor? That would turn a generic finding into an economic mechanism.

3. **Connect to team production.**  
   The result is more interesting if framed as evidence that care production uses workers in teams with minimum staffing thresholds. Then the paper is about production technology, not just policy incidence.

4. **Tighten the counterfactual comparison.**  
   Even without changing the empirical design, the paper’s framing should distinguish “labor injected into shortage sectors” from “general labor supply shocks.” The comparison class should be carefully chosen conceptually, not just institutionally.

5. **Use an outcome that speaks more directly to crowd-out.**  
   Paid employment is good, but wages, vacancies, overtime, reliance on temporary staffing, or occupational composition would all sharpen the crowd-out/complementarity interpretation.

If the author could only enlarge one margin substantively, I would want **output/capacity outcomes**. That would make the paper much more AER-like.

### Closest neighbors

1. **Angrist (1990)** and the broader conscription/service literature

2. **Card (1990)** and immigration/labor-supply-shock papers more generally  
   As baseline crowd-out/substitution references, though this is a different margin.

3. **Acemoglu, Autor, and Lyle (2004)** on WWII female labor supply  
   Again, as a canonical paper on labor-supply shocks and substitution/complementarity.

4. **Dustmann, Schönberg, and Stuhler (2017)** / **Borjas (2017)**  
   For the modern labor-supply-shock framing, even if the mechanisms differ.

5. A healthcare workforce / care-sector production literature  
   The paper cites OECD and Buchan, but that is too policy-report-heavy. It needs real economics neighbors in health labor markets, staffing constraints, and team production in care.

### How should the paper position itself relative to those neighbors?

Mostly **build on and redirect**, not attack.

- Relative to conscription papers: “They ask what service does to the conscript; I ask what conscript labor does to the receiving sector.”
- Relative to immigration/labor-supply papers: “Those study market labor entering labor markets; I study government-supplied, near-free labor entering specific institutions.”
- Relative to healthcare workforce papers: “Those debate shortage remedies; I provide evidence on one unusual but informative remedy that reveals how constrained care production works.”

I would not pitch this as “challenging crowd-out theory” in a broad sense. That overstates it. It is more accurate and more credible to say it identifies a setting where crowd-out logic can fail because production is constrained and labor inputs are complementary.

### Is the paper currently positioned too narrowly or too broadly?

Paradoxically, both.

- **Too narrow** in the institutional description: very Switzerland/ZIVI/Tatbeweis-specific.
- **Too broad** in the claim “this challenges standard crowd-out predictions.”

The right positioning is narrower in theoretical claim, broader in economic relevance. This is a paper about **public labor injections into constrained service sectors**, with Switzerland as the clean setting.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?

Most notably, it seems under-connected to:

- **Health economics / health labor market** work on staffing constraints, nurse shortages, hospital labor demand, and care quality.
- **Personnel economics / organizational economics** on team production and complementary tasks.
- Possibly **public economics / state capacity** literatures on how public provision expands when auxiliary labor or fiscal support loosens bottlenecks.
- A literature on **volunteers, national service, and public employment programs**, if there are economics papers on AmeriCorps-type programs, workfare placements, or subsidized public labor.

Right now the paper talks to labor and conscription, but the result itself seems more naturally at home in the intersection of labor, health, and organizational production.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?

Not quite. The most impactful conversation is not “conscription papers have ignored host sectors.” That is true but niche.

The better conversation is: **What determines whether additional labor substitutes for or complements regular employment in sectors with bottlenecks, regulation, and team production?** That connects this paper to labor economics, health economics, and public economics in a much more central way.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Healthcare and social care face chronic staffing shortages. Governments sometimes try to ease those shortages with low-cost or subsidized labor. Standard economic intuition says cheap labor may displace paid workers.

### Tension

But that intuition may fail in care sectors, where production requires teams, supervision, and threshold staffing. So what actually happens when a government suddenly floods those sectors with near-free mandated workers?

### Resolution

In Switzerland, after the 2009 civilian-service reform, sectors receiving most of this labor saw paid employment rise relative to comparison sectors. The paper interprets this as complementarity rather than crowd-out.

### Implications

The implication is that labor injections into shortage sectors may expand capacity instead of shrinking regular employment, especially where labor inputs are task-differentiated and production is bottlenecked. That matters for national service, public workforce policy, and how economists think about labor-demand responses in nonstandard production environments.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has a serviceable arc, but it is not yet disciplined enough. The current manuscript alternates between three different stories:

1. A labor-supply-shock paper.
2. A conscription paper.
3. A healthcare-shortage paper.

These are related, but the paper has not fully decided which is the main story. As a result, parts of the introduction feel like a collection of literatures and estimates rather than a single compelling narrative.

Worse, the paper undercuts its own story too early by foregrounding caveats and qualification before the reader has fully internalized why the result matters. The author is being honest, which is good, but editorially the paper lacks hierarchy.

### What story should it be telling?

The paper should tell one clean story:

- **Care sectors are capacity-constrained.**
- **Switzerland provided a sudden influx of cheap auxiliary labor.**
- **Instead of displacing regular workers, this relaxed bottlenecks and coincided with expansion in paid employment.**
- **Therefore, in constrained service production, labor can be complementary even when it is cheap and externally supplied.**

That is the paper. Everything else should serve this story.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?

I would lead with: “When Switzerland quadrupled civilian-service placements into healthcare and social care, paid employment in those sectors rose rather than fell.”

That is a good fact. It is intuitive enough to grab attention and counterintuitive enough to provoke interest.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

They would lean in initially. The surprise sign is strong. “Cheap labor increased paid jobs?” is a good seminar hook.

But then the very next question would be: **Why?** And immediately after that: **How do you know this is not just those sectors growing anyway?**

Since you explicitly asked me not to evaluate identification, I won’t turn this into a methods memo. Strategically, though, that second question is a clue: the paper cannot rely on the sign of the coefficient alone. It needs a richer mechanism or capacity-expansion story to hold attention at the top level.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Likely one of these:

- “What tasks were these civilian servants doing that made paid labor more valuable?”
- “Did output or capacity expand—more patients, beds, services?”
- “Is this a healthcare story or a general labor-market story?”
- “Would this happen in a competitive sector, or only in rationed care sectors?”

Those are exactly the questions the paper should anticipate and lean into.

### If the findings are modest or vulnerable, is the result itself still interesting?

Yes, the finding is interesting even if interpreted cautiously, because the sign itself pushes against a common presumption. But the paper must make clear that the value is not “we found a statistically significant positive coefficient.” The value is “in this kind of sector, crowd-out is not inevitable; bottlenecks matter.”

If the result remains somewhat fragile or qualified, the paper should embrace a more disciplined claim: not “cheap labor broadly complements paid employment,” but “in a constrained care setting, a large labor subsidy did not visibly crowd out paid jobs and may have enabled expansion.” That is still publishable in a good field journal; for AER, it probably needs more reach.
