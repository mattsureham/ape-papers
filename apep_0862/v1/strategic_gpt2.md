## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple, important question: when the government injects a large amount of mandated, very-low-cost labor into a sector with chronic staffing shortages, does that displace paid workers or instead expand the sector and pull in more paid employment? Using Switzerland’s 2009 civilian-service reform, which sharply increased the supply of conscript labor to health and social care, the paper argues that paid employment rose rather than fell, suggesting complementarity rather than crowd-out.

A busy economist should care because this is not really a “Swiss conscription” paper; it is a paper about how subsidized or quasi-free labor interacts with labor demand in constrained service sectors. That question speaks to healthcare staffing, public service provision, national service, workfare, and the broader economics of labor-market adjustment.

### Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Mostly, but not quite. The opening is already better than many submissions: it begins with a vivid institutional fact and moves quickly to the economic question. That said, the introduction still reads a bit like “here is an unusual institutional setting” rather than “here is a first-order economic question with broad stakes.” The current first two paragraphs are also somewhat overloaded with institutional detail before fully landing the big-picture question.

More importantly, the paper’s best pitch is **not** “I study civilian service in Switzerland.” It is: **what happens when near-free mandated labor enters a shortage sector?** The Swiss setting is the experiment, not the message.

### The pitch the paper should have

Here is what the first two paragraphs should say instead:

> Governments often try to relieve labor shortages by subsidizing workers, expanding training, or loosening immigration. But there is a more extreme policy margin that economists understand surprisingly little about: what happens when the state delivers labor to employers at nearly zero marginal cost? Standard crowd-out logic suggests employers should substitute this quasi-free labor for paid workers. Yet in sectors with binding capacity constraints, the opposite may occur: low-cost auxiliary labor may enable expansion and increase demand for paid staff.
>
> This paper studies that question using Switzerland’s 2009 reform of civilian service, which abruptly removed a key barrier to opting out of military service and caused civilian-service admissions to quadruple. Because two-thirds of service days flowed into health and social care, the reform created a large, sudden infusion of mandated labor into sectors facing chronic staffing shortages. I show that paid employment in those sectors rose relative to other service sectors after the reform, suggesting that in constrained care markets, mandated labor can complement rather than crowd out paid work.

That is the paper’s AER-facing opening. The institutional specifics can come immediately after.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### Contribution in one sentence

The paper’s contribution is to show that a large government-induced influx of near-free mandated labor into Swiss health and social care was associated with higher, not lower, paid employment, implying complementarity in a shortage sector where standard crowd-out intuition would predict substitution.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?

Only partially. The paper distinguishes itself from the classic conscription literature by saying those papers study effects on conscripts rather than host sectors. That is true, but as written, this still feels a bit like a niche literature gap rather than a major economic contribution.

The sharper differentiation should be from papers on:
- labor supply shocks,
- public-service staffing constraints,
- subsidized labor / low-wage labor,
- immigration and task complementarity,
- and the economics of bottlenecks in care sectors.

Right now the paper says, in effect, “no one has studied this exact institutional setting.” That is not enough. It needs to say, “this setting cleanly tests a broader hypothesis that existing literatures can’t cleanly isolate.”

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or filling a gap in a LITERATURE?

It starts with a world question, which is good. But by the contribution paragraph, it slips into literature-gap mode: “the conscription literature studies X, I study Y.” That weakens it.

The stronger framing is:
- **World question:** When labor is delivered to shortage sectors at near-zero cost, do firms replace paid workers or expand production?
- **Economic mechanism:** Complementarity arises when auxiliary labor relaxes bottlenecks rather than substitutes one-for-one for skilled labor.
- **Policy relevance:** This matters for healthcare staffing, national service, public employment subsidies, and labor shortages in regulated care markets.

### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain what’s new?

At present, they might say: “It’s a DiD paper on Swiss civilian service showing no crowd-out in healthcare.” That is too small.

The author wants them to say: “It shows that in bottlenecked care sectors, adding low-cost mandated labor can increase paid employment rather than displace it—because it expands capacity.” That is a much stronger takeaway.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

The biggest ways to make it bigger are not more polishing; they are deeper scope and framing:

1. **Move from employment effects to sectoral expansion effects.**  
   The paper repeatedly hints that facilities may have expanded beds, hours, services, or throughput. If it can show that, the result becomes much more meaningful. Otherwise readers may ask whether this is just a compositional labor-market artifact.

2. **Show mechanism more concretely.**  
   “Capacity expansion,” “demand revelation,” and “pipeline” are all plausible, but currently they are just verbal possibilities. A top-journal version would need at least one mechanism moved from speculation toward evidence.

3. **Exploit treatment intensity if possible.**  
   The paper itself notes canton-level deployment intensity as a promising extension. That may be the real paper. A triple-difference or dose-response design across regions/intensity would immediately increase ambition and credibility of the substantive claim.

4. **Connect more directly to the economics of task complementarity.**  
   If civilian servants mostly do lower-skill support tasks, and paid workers do licensed/skilled tasks, then this is a paper about multi-task production in care. That is a broader and more durable contribution than “here is one policy reform.”

5. **Use outcomes that speak to welfare or production.**  
   Employment alone is an incomplete endpoint. If the paper could show effects on service provision, patient volumes, waiting times, or institutional capacity, the contribution becomes much larger.

It should **build on and bridge** them, not attack them.

- Relative to healthcare staffing papers: “Most debate focuses on training, migration, and mandates; this setting reveals how auxiliary labor can relax non-wage capacity constraints.”

Somewhat too narrowly in setting, but oddly too broadly in its generic citations. The paper has the classic problem of many young empirical papers: broad claim, narrow evidence, and a literature review that gestures in several directions without fully joining any conversation.

It should narrow to the **right** broad conversation:  
**how labor injections interact with production constraints in care sectors.**

That is the audience.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?

At least at the level of framing, it seems underengaged with:
- task-based production / labor complementarity,
- public economics of in-kind labor provision,
- workfare / subsidized labor programs,
- healthcare production-function papers,
- and potentially organizational economics of staffing constraints.

If this paper is really about “free labor in bottlenecked sectors,” it should speak to those audiences more directly.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?

Not fully. Right now it is having a somewhat second-tier conversation: “conscription literature hasn’t looked at host sectors.” True, but small.

The better conversation is:
- What happens when the state relaxes staffing bottlenecks by providing auxiliary labor?
- Under what production conditions does low-cost labor crowd out versus crowd in paid employment?
- Why are shortage sectors different from competitive labor markets in standard crowd-out models?

That is a more interesting and less expected connection.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Before this paper: economists typically expect that if employers receive labor at close to zero marginal cost, they will substitute away from paid workers. Meanwhile, healthcare and social care are sectors with chronic staffing shortages and potentially binding operational constraints.

### Tension

These two ideas point in opposite directions. Standard substitution logic predicts crowd-out. But in care production, auxiliary labor may relax bottlenecks and allow facilities to expand, which could increase demand for paid staff. We do not know which force dominates.

### Resolution

The Swiss reform sharply increased civilian-service labor in health and social care, and paid employment in those sectors rose relative to controls. The paper interprets this as evidence of complementarity rather than displacement.

### Implications

The implications are potentially important: in constrained service sectors, low-cost labor may function less like a substitute for paid labor and more like a capacity-expanding input. That matters for healthcare workforce policy, national service, and labor-shortage interventions more generally.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the bones of one, yes. But the arc weakens in two ways:

1. **The paper is a little too eager to report estimation details before fully developing the conceptual tension.**
2. **The discussion admits a major caveat—that the treated sectors were already on steeper trajectories—without re-centering the story around what can still be learned.**

So the narrative is there, but it is fragile. At moments it feels like a collection of empirical outputs—baseline DiD, event study, placebo, leave-one-out, trends specification—rather than a tightly controlled story.

### If it is a collection of results looking for a story, what story should it tell?

The story should be:

> In labor markets, adding cheap labor usually sounds like substitution. But in care sectors, production is constrained by teams, supervision, and capacity. Switzerland accidentally ran a large-scale test of that distinction. The results suggest that when auxiliary labor eases bottlenecks in a shortage sector, paid employment can rise rather than fall.

Everything in the paper should be subordinated to that idea. Right now, too much space is spent narrating the reform and not enough on the economics of why this is a discriminating test.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?

“Switzerland suddenly flooded health and social care with near-free civilian-service workers, and paid employment in those sectors appears to have increased rather than decreased.”

That is a good lead fact. It has surprise value.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

They would initially lean in. The result cuts against a standard intuition, and the institutional setting is unusual enough to be memorable.

But then the next question comes very quickly:  
**“Why? What exactly were these workers doing, and how do you know this is sectoral expansion rather than just underlying healthcare growth?”**

If the paper cannot answer that, interest will fade.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Likely one of these:
- “What is the mechanism—task complementarity, supervision needs, demand expansion, or pipeline?”
- “Is this just healthcare trending up because of aging?”
- “Do services/output rise too, or only employment?”
- “Can you show stronger variation in exposure than just treated sectors versus controls?”

That is revealing: the paper’s hook is strong enough, but the second beat needs strengthening.

### If the findings are modest or fragile, is the null/modesty itself interesting?

This is not a null paper, but it is a somewhat fragile positive paper. The author is admirably honest that sector-specific trends absorb much of the effect and permutation inference is only marginal. That honesty is good editorially, but it creates a positioning problem: the paper currently wants credit both for a bold substantive finding and for a cautious “tailwind to an already-growing sector” interpretation.

Those are not the same paper. The author needs to choose:
- either make the causal claim more convincing with richer variation/mechanism,
- or scale the claim down and present this as suggestive evidence against simple crowd-out logic.

Right now it wants the headline of the first and the evidentiary humility of the second.

The paper’s own most important buried insight is this line: the estimated employment differential is much larger than the direct labor input, which suggests the reform may have accelerated an expanding sector rather than mechanically replacing labor. Some, but not much. It mostly summarizes and generalizes. It would add more value if it explicitly reframed the paper’s lesson as conditional:
- crowd-out in competitive sectors,
- complementarity in constrained team-production sectors.

That conditionality is the real idea.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

Honest answer: in current form, this is not yet an AER paper. The central idea is promising, but the paper is still too small and too vulnerable to feel field-defining.

### What is the main gap?

Primarily a **scope/ambition problem**, with some **framing problem** layered on top.

- **Framing problem:** the paper undersells the big economic question and oversells the narrow institutional gap.
- **Scope problem:** the paper shows an employment pattern, but not enough else. For AER, the paper likely needs either richer evidence on mechanisms/outcomes or a more powerful design exploiting cross-canton exposure.
- **Ambition problem:** it is a competent paper built around a neat reform, but it does not yet feel like it changes how economists think about labor injections in constrained sectors.

I would not call it mainly a novelty problem. The setting is novel enough. The problem is that novelty of setting is not the same as novelty of insight.

### What would excite the top 10 people in this field?

One of two upgrades:

1. **A stronger design with richer variation in exposure**  
   If the author can move from sector-level before/after to canton-sector-time intensity variation, the paper becomes much more persuasive and much less dependent on one coarse treated-vs-control comparison.

2. **A broader outcome set showing capacity expansion**  
   If the paper can show that service provision increased—beds, admissions, waiting times, occupancy, patient flow, facility openings, etc.—then the complementarity story becomes much more substantive and important.

Ideally, both.

### Single most impactful advice

If the author can only change one thing, it should be this:

**Rebuild the paper around evidence that the reform expanded care-sector capacity—not just employment—because without that, the result remains an interesting but fragile sector-level correlation rather than a top-journal insight about how low-cost labor affects constrained production.**
