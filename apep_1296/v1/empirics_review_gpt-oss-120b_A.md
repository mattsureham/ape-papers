**Referee Report – “The Invoice Ledger as Detective: Lithuania’s Real‑Time Reporting Mandate and the Collapse of Its VAT Gap”**

### 1. Idea Fidelity  

The manuscript follows the research agenda set out in the original idea manifest. It (i) studies Lithuania’s **i.SAF** mandatory invoice‑ledger reporting introduced in October 2016, (ii) exploits a **difference‑in‑differences (DiD)** design with Latvia and Estonia as primary controls, and (iii) augments the country‑level analysis with a **continuous‑treatment** specification that interacts sector‑level “B2B invoice intensity” with the post‑reform dummy.  

Where the paper deviates from the manifest is modest:  

* The promised **firm‑size stagger** (i.SAF‑T rollout) is mentioned in the background but never used as a separate identification source. The original plan called for a within‑Lithuania DiD that could help isolate the effect of the *invoice‑ledger* component from other i.MAS reforms; the current version relies solely on the cross‑country comparison.  

* The manifest suggested a **“bunching at the VAT‑registration threshold”** test to assess behavioral responses around the filing cost. No such test appears.  

* The list of control countries in the manuscript is broader (Finland, Poland) than the manifest (Latvia, Estonia). While this can improve power, the authors do not justify the inclusion of these more distant economies nor examine whether they satisfy the “parallel‑trend” assumption.  

Overall the core idea—using i.SAF as a natural experiment and exploiting sector‑level invoice intensity—is retained, but the paper omits two of the three auxiliary identification strategies outlined in the manifest.

### 2. Summary  

The paper provides the first causal estimate of Lithuania’s universal B2B invoice‑ledger mandate (i.SAF) on VAT compliance. Using a country‑level DiD with Baltic neighbors and a sector‑level continuous‑treatment model, it finds that the VAT gap fell by **≈10 percentage points** more in Lithuania than in the Baltic average, and that high‑intensity sectors experienced faster GVA growth after the reform. The results are positioned as evidence for the EU’s forthcoming ViDA e‑invoicing directive.

### 3. Essential Points  

1. **Identification – Parallel Trends and Few‑Cluster Inference**  
   - The paper does not present an **event‑study** or pre‑trend graph for the VAT gap (or VAT/GDP) that would demonstrate that Lithuania’s trajectory was parallel to the controls before October 2016. Given the heterogeneity of Baltic economies, the parallel‑trend assumption is non‑trivial.  
   - With **only one treated unit** and **five countries** in total, conventional cluster‑robust SEs are unreliable. While the authors supplement with randomization inference and a leave‑one‑out test, the reported **p‑values (≈0.40)** reflect low power rather than robustness. The paper should adopt a **wild‑cluster bootstrap** (Cameron, Gelbach & Miller 2008) or **permute‑treatment** across a larger set of potential control countries (e.g., all EU members) to obtain more credible inference.

2. **Outcome Measurement – Reliability of the VAT‑Gap Series**  
   - The primary outcome, the **VAT‑gap** from the CASE/European‑Commission reports, is a **top‑down, model‑based estimate** that aggregates several indirect components (e.g., shadow economy). Its measurement error may be correlated with institutional changes (e.g., improved statistical methods) unrelated to i.SAF.  
   - The paper should triangulate with **bottom‑up indicators** (e.g., audited VAT receipts, audit‑selection rates, or the number of “discrepancy flags” generated by i.SAF) to confirm that the observed drop is not driven by a change in the estimation methodology.

3. **Mechanism – Construction and Validity of the B2B‑Invoice‑Intensity Proxy**  
   - The sector‑level intensity measure is derived from a **single input‑output table** (2010) and treats the *share of domestic intermediate inputs* as a stand‑in for “invoice cross‑matchability.” This is a coarse proxy that does not account for **cross‑border intra‑EU trade**, **industry‑specific invoicing practices**, or **temporal changes** in input structures.  
   - Without firm‑level data linking reported invoices to the i.SAF filings, it is difficult to rule out alternative explanations (e.g., differential sectoral growth trends, composition effects). The paper should either **validate the proxy** (e.g., by comparing with firm‑level invoice counts where available) or provide **additional robustness checks** (e.g., using alternative intensity measures such as the proportion of sales that are B2B, or excluding sectors heavily exposed to export‑oriented trade).

If any of these three issues cannot be satisfactorily addressed, the paper should be **rejected**; otherwise, they constitute essential revisions.

### 4. Suggestions  

Below are constructive, non‑essential recommendations that will strengthen the manuscript, improve its credibility, and increase its relevance for policy‑makers.

#### a. Strengthen the DiD Identification  

1. **Event‑Study Graphs** – Plot the VAT gap (and VAT/GDP) for each country over the full sample (2010‑2022) with 95 % confidence bands. Include leads and lags to demonstrate the absence of pre‑treatment divergence and to visualize the dynamics of the effect.  

2. **Synthetic Control as a Complement** – Construct a synthetic Lithuania using a weighted combination of the Baltic and Nordic controls. Compare the post‑2016 trajectory of the VAT gap to the synthetic counterfactual; this provides a visual “gap‑analysis” that does not rely on the linear DiD assumption.  

3. **Placebo Tests on Other Outcomes** – Run the same DiD specifications on outcomes that should **not** be affected by i.SAF (e.g., corporate income tax receipts, immigration inflows). Lack of significant effects will bolster confidence that the identified impact is specific to VAT.

#### b. Expand the Set of Controls and Address Spillovers  

1. **Broader Control Pool** – Include additional EU countries with similar pre‑reform VAT gaps (e.g., Czechia, Slovakia, Hungary). Even if they did not adopt i.SAF, they can serve as additional “donor” units for synthetic control or for permutation tests.  

2. **Check for Regional Spillovers** – Since i.SAF flags discrepancies that may trigger audits of foreign partners, verify that adjacent countries (e.g., Latvia, Estonia) did not experience a contemporaneous change in their own VAT gaps due to cross‑border enforcement. If evidence of spillovers exists, the DiD estimator may be biased downward.

#### c. Refine the Measurement of the VAT Gap  

1. **Alternative Gap Estimates** – Use the **VAT‑revenue‑to‑GDP ratio** as a second dependent variable. Although this measure conflates macro‑economic growth, it is directly observed and can be decomposed (e.g., using a growth‑accounting exercise).  

2. **Audit‑Flag Data** – If the STI publishes the number of discrepancy flags or audit selections generated by i.SAF, include this as a secondary outcome. A rise in flag counts concurrent with the VAT‑gap decline would provide a mechanistic link.  

3. **Robustness to CASE Methodology Changes** – The CASE reports have undergone methodological revisions (e.g., new treatment of the shadow economy). Document the version used each year and, if possible, re‑estimate the gap using a **consistent methodology** across the sample.

#### d. Enhance the Mechanism Analysis  

1. **Alternative Intensity Measures** – Construct a **B2B‑sales share** metric (sales to other domestic VAT‑registered firms divided by total sales) from the same input‑output tables or from Eurostat “turnover by destination” data. Compare results to the intermediate‑input share to test sensitivity.  

2. **Firm‑Size Interaction** – Exploit the **i.SAF‑T rollout** (turnover thresholds 700 k € in 2016, 300 k € in 2017, full coverage 2020). A within‑Lithuania DiD that compares larger firms (treated earlier) to smaller firms (treated later) would isolate the effect of *more detailed* reporting and help separate the impact of i.SAF from i.SAF‑T.  

3. **Bunching Test Around the Filing Cost Threshold** – If filing costs rise with the size of the ledger (e.g., software or compliance costs), a discontinuity test at the 700 k € turnover cut‑off could reveal behavioral responses, as originally envisaged.  

4. **Sector‑Specific Narrative** – Provide case studies of sectors most affected (e.g., electronics, metal trade) using publicly available audit reports or news articles that illustrate how “cross‑matching” disrupted carousel fraud.

#### e. Statistical Inference  

1. **Wild‑Cluster Bootstrap** – Implement the wild‑cluster bootstrap (Cameron, Gelbach & Miller 2008) for the country‑level DiD, reporting both the bootstrap p‑value and the conventional clustered SEs.  

2. **Multiple‑Testing Adjustment** – Given several outcome variables and multiple specifications, apply a **Bonferroni or Benjamini–Hochberg** correction to guard against spurious significance.

#### f. Presentation and Transparency  

1. **Data Availability** – Deposit the constructed sector‑level panel, the VAT‑gap series, and the intensity index in a public repository (e.g., Zenodo). Include a reproducible code appendix.  

2. **Clear Definition of “Post‑2016”** – The paper uses a binary Post dummy that switches on in 2017 (the first full post‑treatment year). Clarify whether the first month of i.SAF compliance (Oct 2016) is excluded and why; an alternative is to treat 2016 as a “partial treatment” year.  

3. **Effect‑Size Interpretation** – Translate the VAT‑gap reduction into **recovered revenue (EUR billions)** and express it relative to total GDP or fiscal balance, to help policymakers gauge the magnitude.  

4. **Discussion of External Validity** – Reflect on how the results may differ for EU countries with lower initial VAT gaps, different industry structures, or where the implementation is delayed (e.g., the 2024 ViDA timeline).  

#### g. Minor Improvements  

* Correct minor typographical errors (e.g., “VAT‑gap” vs “VAT gap” consistency).  
* Align table footnotes with the main text (e.g., indicate which column corresponds to the continuous‑treatment specification).  
* Add a brief **limitations** subsection that explicitly acknowledges the few‑cluster issue, the reliance on top‑down gap estimates, and the coarse intensity proxy.

### Concluding Recommendation  

The manuscript addresses an important policy question and uses a novel, high‑frequency administrative dataset. However, **the credibility of the identification strategy and the robustness of the main outcome measure are currently insufficient**. Provided the authors implement the essential revisions outlined above—especially (i) presenting clear pre‑trend evidence, (ii) strengthening inference with wild‑cluster bootstrap or synthetic‑control methods, and (iii) triangulating the VAT‑gap estimates—the paper would make a valuable contribution to the emerging literature on e‑invoicing and tax compliance.  

I therefore recommend **major revision**. Once the essential points are addressed, the paper will be suitable for publication in *American Economic Review: Insights*.
