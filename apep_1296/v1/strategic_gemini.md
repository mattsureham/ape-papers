## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
The paper examines whether a universal, real-time digital reporting mandate for B2B invoices can eliminate the "VAT gap" in a modern economy. Using Lithuania’s 2016 reform (i.SAF) and comparing it to Baltic neighbors, the authors find a massive 10.4 percentage point reduction in tax evasion, concentrated in B2B-intensive sectors where automated cross-matching is most effective. This provides the first "high-income/EU" evidence for a policy soon to be mandated across the entire European Union.

**Evaluation:** The paper actually does a commendable job of this. The first paragraph is punchy ("A quarter of every euro... simply vanished"). However, it could be sharper on the *economic* mechanism.
**The "AER" Pitch:** "How does the digitization of information friction affect the equilibrium of tax evasion in a complex supply chain? We exploit Lithuania’s shift from private to public (to the state) invoice ledgers to show that real-time third-party verification can near-permanently collapse a 25% tax gap. We demonstrate that the gains are a function of network density, providing a blueprint for the welfare effects of the EU’s upcoming universal e-invoicing mandate."

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**Contribution:** The paper provides the first causal evidence of the fiscal and formalization effects of real-time B2B digital reporting within the EU's specific legal and "carousel fraud" context.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It differentiates itself from Pomeranz (2015) and Bellon (2022) by moving from emerging markets to the EU. The distinction is critical because the EU's "reverse-charge" and cross-border rules create unique evasion opportunities that simple paper trails don't fix.
*   **Question vs. Literature:** It is currently framed heavily as "answering a question about the WORLD" (the ViDA directive). This is a strength.
*   **Clarity:** A smart economist would say: "It's the Pomeranz mechanism applied to the EU, using Lithuania's massive collapse in the VAT gap as a natural experiment."
*   **Bigger Contribution:** To make this "bigger," the authors need to move beyond "revenue went up" to "the structure of the economy changed." Are marginal firms exiting (business demography)? Is there a shift in supply chain links? If they can show *how* the production network re-optimized in response to transparency, it moves from a Public Finance paper to a General Interest paper.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
*   **Closest Neighbors:** Pomeranz (2015) on VAT paper trails; Kleven et al. (2011) on third-party reporting; Fan et al. (2018) on China’s Golden Tax; Naritomi (2019) on consumers as tax auditors.
*   **Positioning:** It should **synthesize** these. It takes the "information at the source" logic of Kleven and the "self-enforcing" logic of Pomeranz and puts them into a 21st-century digital monitoring framework.
*   **Niche vs. Broad:** Currently slightly too focused on the Baltic context. It needs to speak more to the *Digital Economics* and *Industrial Organization* of the firm—how does "radical transparency" to the state change firm behavior?
*   **Missing Conversation:** It should connect to the literature on **state capacity** (Acemoglu/Johnson/Robinson). Is technology a substitute for bureaucratic quality in tax enforcement?

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC
*   **Setup:** Lithuania had the worst VAT compliance in the EU despite being a modern economy.
*   **Tension:** Information was trapped in private paper invoices, allowing "ghost" deductions and "carousel" fraud to flourish.
*   **Resolution:** i.SAF forced all data into a central XML "detective," allowing the state to cross-match every transaction.
*   **Implications:** The VAT gap didn't just shrink; it collapsed. This suggests that "tax morale" is secondary to "tax information."

**Evaluation:** The arc is very strong. It’s a "success story" paper. The main narrative risk is the 2015 Euro adoption—the authors must work harder to prove the "resolution" wasn't just the Euro.

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
*   **The Lead Fact:** "Lithuania went from the worst tax evader in the EU to one of the best in four years just by requiring an XML upload."
*   **Reaction:** Lean in. It’s a "policy miracle" story.
*   **Follow-up:** "Did firms just move the evasion to the B2C (cash) level instead?" (The paper starts to answer this with the GVA analysis, but needs more).

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
*   **Front-loading:** The "VAT gap collapse" (Table 3) is the money shot. It needs to be Figure 1.
*   **Appendix:** The placebo tests in Table 4 are worrisome (pre-trends). They need to be addressed head-on in the main text with a discussion of Euro adoption and economic convergence.
*   **Eliminate:** The "Standardized Effect Sizes" in the main body (Table 5) feels like a meta-analysis tool; move it to the appendix.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The gap is currently **Identification & Scope**.
1.  **Identification:** A DiD with $N=5$ and one treated unit is an uphill battle for the AER. To be an AER paper, the **Sector-Level analysis must become the star**, not the country-level DiD. They need to show that the "B2B Intensity" mechanism is so precise that it couldn't be anything else (like the Euro).
2.  **Scope:** Use the "Business Demography" data mentioned on page 5. If they can show that "evasion-dependent" firms died while "productive" firms grew, this becomes a paper about **Misallocation**.

**Single Biggest Advice:** Shift the spotlight from the 10.4% country-level result (which is prone to many "Baltic convergence" critiques) to the firm-population dynamics: show that the reform cleaned up the supply chain by pruning the "ghost" firms.
