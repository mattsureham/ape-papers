## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
This paper evaluates the efficacy of England’s "Housing Delivery Test," a policy that strips local governments of their discretionary power to block development if they fail to meet housing targets. By exploiting a sharp 75% delivery threshold in a regression discontinuity design, the author tests whether simply reversing the "burden of proof" from the developer to the council can increase housing approval rates in a system traditionally prone to NIMBYism. 

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this well, though the first paragraph is a bit bogged down in UK-specific acronyms (HDT, NPPF). 
**The Pitch the Paper Should Have:** 
"Can central governments force local authorities to build more housing without actually mandating specific approvals? In England, falling below a 75% delivery threshold triggers a 'presumption in favor' of development, legally shifting the burden of proof onto local councils to justify any refusal. This paper uses this sharp administrative threshold to provide the first causal evidence that changing the 'regulatory default'—even without changing substantive rules—can significantly increase housing supply in high-resistance areas."

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**One-sentence contribution:** The paper provides the first causal estimate of how shifting the legal burden of proof in land-use planning affects housing approval rates.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It is well-differentiated. While Hilber and Vermeulen (2016) look at the general cost of constraints, this paper looks at a specific *institutional mechanism* of override.
*   **Question vs. Gap:** It currently straddles the line. It frames itself as "filling a gap" (no study has estimated this yet) but hints at a bigger question: **Do legal defaults matter in the face of local political resistance?** 
*   **Bigger Contribution:** To make this "AER big," the paper needs to move beyond "Approval Rates" as the only outcome. It needs to show if this translates into actual *starts* or *completions*, and crucially, it needs to explore the *strategic behavior* of developers. Do developers wait for a council to fail the test before submitting "risky" applications?

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
*   **Neighbors:** Hilber & Vermeulen (2016) on UK supply; Glaeser & Gyourko (2003) on zoning; Madrian & Shea (2001) on defaults/nudges.
*   **Strategy:** The paper should lean harder into the **"Regulatory Defaults"** and **"Institutional Design"** literature. It is currently positioned as a "Housing Paper." It would be more impactful as a "Political Economy of Institutions" paper that uses housing as its laboratory.
*   **Missing Conversations:** The paper needs to speak to the "Legal and Economics" literature on the burden of proof and the "Mechanism Design" literature regarding how central principals control local agents.

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC
*   **Setup:** Housing is scarce; local councils block it because of NIMBY pressure.
*   **Tension:** The central government tries to force their hand, but local councils are "street-level bureaucrats" with many ways to resist. Does a "presumption" actually override local will, or is it just paper-thin?
*   **Resolution:** It works. Shifting the default leads to an ~8–10 percentage point increase in approvals.
*   **Implications:** The *form* of the law matters. You don't need a total takeover; you just need to tilt the scales of justification.

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong but the "tension" is currently under-explored. I want to know *how* councils try to fight back and why they fail once the presumption is triggered.

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
At a dinner party, the lead fact is: **"In England, if a town fails its housing target, the law basically tells them: 'you have to prove why this house shouldn't exist, rather than making the builder prove why it should.' And that simple switch increases approvals by 10%."**

Economists would lean in. The follow-up question is: "Does that mean they just approve more *bad* projects, or did it actually unlock the bottleneck?"

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
*   **Front-loading:** The "How it works in practice" section (p. 4) is gold. It should be more prominent.
*   **Visuals:** The author admits the lack of a graphical RDD plot is a limitation. For an AER submission, this is **non-negotiable**. I need to see the jump at 75% visually to believe the 0.16 p-value isn't just noise.
*   **Appendix:** The standardized effect sizes (Table 6) are interesting but should be integrated into a "Discussion of Magnitude" section in the main text.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The distance is currently **Medium-to-Far**, primarily due to **statistical power** and **scope**. A p-value of 0.16 on the primary nonparametric result is a "referee killer" for the AER unless the narrative and the mechanism-testing are spectacular.

**Single Most Impactful Advice:** 
The author must move beyond "approval rates" and use the **PS2 data** to look at the *composition* of applications. Did the *type* of applications change? If the "tilted balance" makes it easier to pass, do developers respond by submitting more ambitious/controversial projects? This turns a "policy evaluation" into a "behavioral response" paper.
