## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple and important question: when governments cap bank interest rates, do they actually make credit cheaper for households and firms, or do they just push borrowers out of regulated banks and into more expensive shadow/digital lenders? Using Kenya’s 2016–2019 bank lending rate cap and its repeal, the paper argues that the cap lowered posted bank rates but redirected some borrowers toward uncapped digital credit, potentially increasing the effective cost of borrowing for the very people the policy was meant to protect.

A busy economist should care because this is not really a Kenya paper; it is a paper about regulatory incidence when regulation applies only inside part of the market. The broader claim is that in a world with rapidly growing fintech and nonbank credit, traditional price controls may backfire through sectoral reallocation rather than the textbook quantity adjustment alone.

### Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Not quite. The current introduction is competent, but it opens too institutionally and too locally. It spends precious early real estate on Kenyan details before telling the reader the general economic question. The “why should I care?” arrives in paragraph three, when it should be sentence one.

The first two paragraphs should say, more directly:

> Interest rate caps are intended to protect borrowers from expensive credit. But in modern credit markets, regulation often covers banks and not the growing universe of digital and nonbank lenders. The central question is therefore not just whether a cap lowers bank rates, but whether it lowers the cost of credit faced by borrowers once substitution across regulatory perimeters is taken into account.
>
> Kenya’s 2016 cap on bank lending rates, followed by its 2019 repeal, provides an unusually sharp setting to study this issue. This paper shows that while the cap reduced formal bank lending rates, areas more exposed to formal banking saw faster growth in uncapped digital borrowing, suggesting that the cap shifted borrowing toward more expensive lenders rather than simply making credit cheaper.

That is the pitch the paper should have. Start with the economics, not the chronology.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Before this paper, the world looks like this: governments impose lending rate caps to protect borrowers; economists expect some mix of lower prices for infra-marginal borrowers and rationing for marginal borrowers; meanwhile, digital lenders are rapidly expanding outside the traditional banking perimeter.

### Tension

The key tension is that the textbook logic of a binding cap was developed for a market where the regulated sector was basically the market. But in modern credit markets, a cap may not reduce borrowing so much as **reallocate** it. The puzzle is whether borrowers shut out of banks end up borrowing less, or just borrowing elsewhere—possibly at higher cost.

### Resolution

The paper’s resolution is: in Kenya, the bank cap lowered formal lending rates, but areas more exposed to banking saw faster digital credit growth during the cap, consistent with substitution into uncapped lenders.

### Implications

The implication is that partial regulation can be self-undermining. Policymakers should evaluate caps at the level of the whole credit market, not just regulated-bank prices, and economists should think about price controls in the presence of adjacent, lightly regulated substitutes.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the ingredients, but the arc is not fully disciplined. Right now it reads somewhat like:

1. Kenya cap happened.
2. Here are several outcomes.
3. Here is some county evidence.
4. Here is a welfare calculation.

That is more a collection of related results than a tightly staged story.

### What story should it be telling?

It should tell one clean story:

1. **Policy goal:** make credit cheaper.
2. **Classical concern:** caps ration bank credit.
3. **Modern twist:** rationed borrowers can move into uncapped digital credit.
4. **Evidence:** that is what appears to have happened in Kenya.
5. **Takeaway:** in segmented markets, regulating one sector can raise the effective cost of credit for targeted borrowers.

Everything in the paper should serve that five-step arc. Some current detours—especially the diffuse discussion of credit/GDP and some of the back-and-forth around aggregate banking outcomes—dilute the story.

I would lead with:

> Kenya capped bank lending rates to make borrowing cheaper, but the places most exposed to bank credit saw the fastest growth in uncapped digital borrowing at vastly higher prices.

That is the memorable fact.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

They would lean in—if that is the framing. They will not lean in for “Kenya’s lending rate fell by 3.4 percentage points.” They might for “a consumer-protection cap may have pushed borrowers toward 90% APR digital credit.”

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Immediately: “Is the paper really showing substitution by rationed borrowers, as opposed to general digital-credit diffusion?” That is exactly the right question, and the paper should anticipate it conceptually in the introduction rather than waiting for the reader to infer the mechanism.

A second follow-up would be: “How general is this beyond Kenya?” The paper should answer with a perimeter/regulatory-boundary framing, not with Kenya-specific exceptionalism.

### If findings are modest or mixed, is that okay?

Yes, because the most interesting finding is not the modest aggregate formal-market effects; it is the compositional reallocation toward digital lenders. The paper should stop pretending that every result is equally central. The aggregate credit-to-GDP material feels like obligatory furniture. The substitution result is the hook.
