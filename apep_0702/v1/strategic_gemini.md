## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper exploits a unique "cap-and-repeal" natural experiment in Kenya to show that interest rate ceilings on banks drove borrowers into the arms of unregulated digital lenders charging 90% APR. By linking county-level bank density to a surge in mobile lending, the paper demonstrates that financial regulation can "bite back" by forcing the very households it intends to protect into much more expensive, shadow-banking alternatives.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this well in the second and third paragraphs. However, the first paragraph is a bit bogged down in Kenyan legislative history.
**The pitch it should have:** "While 76 countries currently use interest rate caps to protect consumers, we know little about how these caps interact with the burgeoning FinTech sector. This paper shows that in Kenya, a cap on bank rates acted as a massive subsidy for unregulated digital lenders, causing a $2.8 billion shift toward credit that was six times more expensive than the formal loans it replaced."

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:** The paper identifies a "digital escape valve" mechanism where interest rate caps on formal banks catalyze a massive, welfare-reducing migration of borrowers to unregulated, high-interest FinTech platforms.

*   **Differentiation:** It moves beyond the standard "caps cause rationing" literature (e.g., Alper et al. 2019) by providing the first empirical link to the FinTech "shadow" sector. It builds on the "regulatory arbitrage" theory of Buchak et al. (2018) but applies it to an emerging market context with much higher stakes (90% APR vs 14%).
*   **Question vs. Literature:** It frames the contribution as answering a question about the **world** (the unintended consequences of popular populist financial policies).
*   **What would make it bigger?** To be a "slam dunk" AER paper, the contribution needs to move from "this happened in Kenya" to "this is a generalizable feature of modern financial systems." The author should attempt to model or empirically test the *threshold* of digital infrastructure required for this escape valve to open in other countries.

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

*   **Neighbors:** Buchak et al. (2018) on shadow banks; Fuster et al. (2019) on technology in lending; and the classic Stiglitz & Weiss (1981) on credit rationing.
*   **Positioning:** It should position itself as the **modern empirical sequel to Stiglitz & Weiss.** In the 1980s, rationing meant you got no credit; in the 2020s, rationing means you get 90% APR digital credit. 
*   **Missing Conversations:** The paper needs to speak more to the **Household Finance** literature. It treats borrowers as a monolith. Connecting this to the literature on "financial literacy" or "present-biased preferences" would explain *why* borrowers take the 90% APR "escape valve" rather than simply consuming less.

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

*   **Setup:** Kenya is a global leader in mobile money, but bank spreads are high.
*   **Tension:** The government caps rates to help the poor, but banks respond by cutting off risky borrowers. Where do those borrowers go?
*   **Resolution:** They go to the "digital escape valve"—a sector the law didn't cover. This is a massive "perverse effect" where the cap actually increased the average cost of credit for the marginalized.
*   **Implications:** Regulation must be "perimeter-aware." Capping one sector in a multi-platform financial ecosystem is a recipe for disaster.

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong. It’s not just a collection of results; it’s a cautionary tale with a clear "villain" (unintended consequences).

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

*   **The Fact:** "Kenya tried to cap interest rates at 14%, and it resulted in a $2.8 billion surge in 90% APR loans."
*   **The Reaction:** Lean in. Every central banker in a developing country is currently debating this exact trade-off.
*   **Follow-up:** "Did the borrowers know how much they were paying, or was this predatory?" This is the question the author needs to be ready to answer (or at least discuss in the welfare section).

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

*   **Front-loading:** The county-level FinAccess results are the "gold" of this paper. The cross-country DiD (Table 2) is a bit weak (low N, marginal significance). I would move the country-level stuff to a "Context" section and make the **County-level Substitution** the primary focus of the paper.
*   **The Appendix:** The "Standardized Effect Sizes" (Table 5) are helpful but belong in the paper's main body to help the reader understand the magnitude of the $2.8 billion figure.

---

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The paper is currently a very good "case study." To make it an AER paper, it needs to be a **"Theory-Infused Empirical Breakthrough."**

The gap is the **"Why."** Why did borrowers switch to 90% APR instead of just waiting for the cap to be repealed or using informal networks (Chamas)? 
*   **Ambition Problem:** The paper is currently too safe with its "back-of-the-envelope" welfare. 
*   **Single Biggest Improvement:** Develop a simple structural model of borrower choice between "Capped Banks" and "Unregulated FinTech" to show that the "Digital Escape Valve" is an inevitable result of asymmetric regulation in the presence of high search costs or urgent liquidity needs.
