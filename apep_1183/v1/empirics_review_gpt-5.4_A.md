## 1. **Idea Fidelity**

The paper captures the broad research question in the manifest—whether the Panic of 1907 produced individual occupational scarring using linked census data—but it departs from the original idea in the most important place: identification. The manifest’s key innovation was to use county-level exposure to failed New York trust companies through the pre-panic correspondent network digitized by Frydman, Hilt, and Zhou. That would have tied treatment intensity directly to the proposed mechanism and offered plausibly exogenous geographic variation based on network topology. The paper instead replaces this with a much coarser, literature-based state severity classification and then interacts that with an indicator for “banking-dependent” sectors. This is a substantial retreat from the original design.

The data side also only partially follows the manifest. The paper uses the MLP linked panel as proposed, but the headline sample falls from the manifest’s 15.4 million prime-age men to 7.9 million in the final estimating sample. That is not inherently problematic, but the paper does not adequately discuss what drives this loss, whether linkage/merging success differs by state or sector, or whether selection into the final sample threatens interpretation. Finally, the manifest emphasized county-level treatment and sectoral heterogeneity with agriculture as a comparison group; the paper retains the latter but not the former, and that change is consequential because it weakens the causal claim.

## 2. **Summary**

This paper links men across the 1900 and 1910 censuses to study whether the Panic of 1907 caused persistent occupational downgrading. Using state-level panic severity interacted with an indicator for employment in banking-dependent sectors, the paper finds that workers in more finance-dependent sectors in “core” panic states experienced lower growth in occupational income scores than agricultural workers in the same states.

The question is interesting and the data are potentially powerful. However, in its current form the empirical strategy does not convincingly isolate the causal effect of the panic from differential sectoral trends across places, and the paper falls well short of the identification strategy suggested by its own motivating framework.

## 3. **Essential Points**

1. **The identification strategy is not yet credible enough for a causal interpretation.**  
   The central specification is not really a difference-in-difference-in-differences design in the usual sense, because there is no pre/post panel around the shock at the relevant time scale; instead, the outcome is a decade change and treatment is cross-sectional state severity. The identifying assumption is that, absent the panic, the 1900–1910 occupational trajectory gap between banking-dependent and agricultural workers would not vary systematically with state panic severity. That is a very strong assumption and is especially implausible here because core-panic states were exactly those with different industrial structures, urban growth, immigrant inflows, and sectoral transformation. State fixed effects remove level differences across states, but they do not remove differential *sector-by-state trends*. The current placebo on literacy does not address this concern. Without a more direct exposure measure and stronger validation, the paper should not claim causal effects.

2. **Treatment measurement is too coarse and insufficiently tied to the mechanism.**  
   The use of a three-bin state severity measure is a major weakness relative to the available historical network data. The paper repeatedly invokes Frydman et al. and the correspondent network as the mechanism, but the estimates do not use correspondent exposure, trust-company links, or even county-level bank suspension intensity. As written, “core panic state” is a proxy for being a northeastern, urban, industrialized state. That is too close to the outcome-generating forces the paper is trying to separate from the panic. The paper needs to move much closer to the original county-level/network-based design, or at minimum show that the state classification strongly predicts observed bank distress in independent data and is not simply a relabeling of industrialization.

3. **The empirical evidence presented as mechanisms/falsification is not well matched to the research question.**  
   Several results are overinterpreted. The cross-state regressions by sector are not falsification tests; they are still cross-sectional comparisons likely contaminated by the same confounds. The literacy placebo is weak because literacy between ages 18–50 and 28–60 over a decade is not an informative benchmark for occupational mobility. The home ownership analysis is similarly hard to interpret and not obviously linked to the transmission mechanism. More basically, if the paper is about “occupational scarring,” it should demonstrate actual downward mobility patterns, sector switching, exits from skilled occupations, or increased movement into farming/laboring—not just a modest average change in occscore.

## 4. **Suggestions**

The project is promising, and I would encourage the authors to re-center the paper around a sharper design. The main recommendation is straightforward: **use the county-level or bank-market exposure implied by the correspondent network data rather than a hand-coded state severity index**. If Frydman, Hilt, and Zhou provide county-level measures of exposure to New York trust-company failures, bank suspensions, or correspondent balances at risk, that should become the treatment variable. Even if the final estimating sample becomes smaller, the gain in credibility would be substantial. A county-level continuous treatment also allows much richer controls and more transparent event-mechanism logic than a three-level state ranking.

A closely related suggestion is to align the unit of exposure with the likely labor-market geography. Workers are linked individually, but the shock should vary at least at the county level, ideally measured at the 1900 county of residence. That would let the authors include state fixed effects and compare workers across counties within the same state, which is much closer to the conceptual variation in the manifest. If treatment is at the county level, the authors could also add baseline county controls—urban share, manufacturing employment share, bank density, foreign-born share, population growth, railroad access, and pre-panic occupational composition. Those will not “solve” identification, but they would make it more plausible that the residual variation reflects financial exposure rather than broad structural change.

The paper also needs much more direct validation of the treatment. At present, the historical narrative says correspondent-network exposure caused panic severity, but the empirical implementation never shows this. A useful table or figure would report whether the treatment measure predicts independent markers of local distress: bank suspensions, clearinghouse certificate issuance, deposit losses, or contractions in local manufacturing activity around 1907–08. Even reduced-form evidence from county- or state-level historical banking reports would help. If the mechanism is truly the freezing of correspondent balances and working capital, the paper should show that places with greater exposure saw sharper banking disruption first.

A second broad recommendation is to strengthen the logic of the “banking-dependent sector” dimension. Right now, the classification is quite coarse, and the choice of agriculture as the comparison group does a lot of work. But agriculture differed from manufacturing/trade/services in many ways besides credit dependence: seasonality, geographic concentration, migration patterns, life-cycle occupational mobility, literacy acquisition, and measurement of occupation in census records. I would strongly suggest unpacking the sector interaction into separate estimates by manufacturing, trade, services, and clerical work, with agriculture not as the only benchmark but also relative to less credit-intensive nonagricultural sectors if possible. If the theory is right, one would expect the strongest effects where working-capital dependence was highest. The paper currently asserts this but does not really test it.

More generally, the authors should be careful not to describe the interaction estimate as automatically “the financial channel.” It may instead reflect that northeastern industrial states experienced different sectoral reallocation from 1900 to 1910. To address this, the paper could implement several more demanding comparisons:

- compare more similar sectors to each other, rather than banking-dependent sectors only versus agriculture;
- interact treatment with baseline occupation score bins to ensure the estimate is not driven by mean reversion or ceiling effects;
- allow flexible controls for baseline county industrial structure;
- test whether results remain when restricting to counties with mixed sector composition;
- estimate specifications separately for native-born and foreign-born workers, or for urban and rural counties, to assess whether the estimate is really concentrated in credit-sensitive contexts.

I also encourage the authors to revisit the outcome measures. Occscore changes over a decade are a useful start, but “occupational scarring” would be more convincing if the paper showed concrete mobility margins: probability of moving to farming, laboring, or unemployment-adjacent categories; probability of remaining in the same broad sector; probability of downward occupational rank movement; and perhaps transitions into self-employment or nonownership statuses if available. Because occscore is noisy and based on 1950 rankings, decomposing the average effect into transition matrices would make the result much more interpretable.

On sample construction, the drop from 15.4 million prime-age linked men to 7.9 million observations deserves fuller treatment. The authors should provide an appendix table showing attrition at each stage and whether missing occupation/state data differ systematically by severity tier, sector, race, nativity, or urbanization. Given the known issues in historical linking, some discussion of linked-sample representativeness is essential. If IPUMS provides linkage weights or representativeness adjustments, the authors should report weighted results. At minimum, they should show that core findings are similar across subgroups with different expected linkage quality.

Inference also deserves more care. Standard errors clustered at the state level with roughly 49 clusters are acceptable but not ideal when treatment variation is essentially cross-state. Once the paper moves to county-level exposure, clustering at the appropriate spatial level and reporting wild-cluster bootstrap p-values where relevant would improve confidence. As it stands, the combination of enormous individual-level N and coarse treatment assignment makes it especially important to separate statistical precision from design-based credibility.

The robustness section should be reframed. Excluding New York is useful, but the binary treatment specification and the county farm-share control do not address the main concern. The latter actually underscores the problem: once one proxy for urbanization is added, the cross-state coefficient disappears, which suggests the treatment measure is deeply entangled with structural development. Rather than presenting this as validating the design, the paper should acknowledge it as a warning sign and use it to motivate a more granular exposure variable. Similarly, the “stayers” analysis using state non-movers is not very informative if treatment is assigned at the state level and migration within state/county labor markets remains possible.

The placebo strategy could also be improved substantially. Literacy change is not a persuasive placebo for prime-age men over a decade. Better placebos would be outcomes less plausibly affected by short-run credit disruption but measured similarly, or pseudo-treatments based on pre-1907 periods if feasible. For example, if any earlier linked sample could be used to ask whether the same treatment measure “predicts” 1890–1900 occupational changes, that would be far more compelling. Even if the individual panel is unavailable, county-level pre-trend tests with historical census aggregates would help: did “high-exposure” counties already have differential manufacturing-versus-agriculture occupational upgrading before 1907?

Finally, I would recommend softening the paper’s claims throughout. This is an interesting historical descriptive exercise with the potential to become a convincing causal study, but it is not there yet. Terms like “caused,” “identifies,” and “first individual-level evidence” should be used more carefully until the treatment is tied directly to correspondent-network exposure and the sector-by-place confounding concern is addressed. A more modest framing—evidence consistent with occupational scarring in places and sectors more exposed to the panic—would better match the current design.

In short, the topic is strong, the data are exciting, and the paper could become publishable if it returns to the original insight in the manifest: exploit pre-panic correspondent-network exposure at a fine geographic level. Right now, the empirical approach is too far from that idea, and the resulting identification is not persuasive enough for AER: Insights.
