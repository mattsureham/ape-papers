**Summary**  
The paper evaluates whether Directive 2011/7/EU differentially improved small-firm survival in EU countries with historically long payment delays. Using a triple-difference estimator with country-size-year fixed effects, the paper finds that small firms in high-delay countries actually saw a marginally larger death-rate gap relative to large firms after the directive, contrary to the policy’s intent. Robustness checks (binary intensity, micro vs. large firms, leave-one-out) and event-study diagnostics are provided to support this null/negative finding.

**Essential Points**

1. **Parallel trend requirement of triple difference** – The identification rests critically on the assumption that, absent the directive, the small-vs-large death rate gap would have evolved similarly across high-delay and low-delay countries. The event-study coefficients for years –5 through –1 are noisy, and there are sizable (and statistically significant) late pre-trends for years –9 to –8 that the narrative attributes to data coverage. Given the continuous treatment and aggregated data, clearer diagnostics (e.g., formal tests for differential pre-trends that focus on years immediately before the directive, placebo “treatments” in earlier periods, or a falsification with a non-policy year) are needed to reassure readers that the triple difference captures the directive rather than lingering compositional shocks.

2. **Endogeneity of payment-delay intensity** – Payment culture is treated as a pre-determined, exogenous continuous treatment. Yet countries with long payment delays (Italy, Greece, Portugal) also experienced sovereign debt shocks, banking crises, and policy changes over the same period that could differentially affect small firms. Without controlling for such potential confounders (e.g., country-level GDP growth, credit conditions, or other SME-targeted policies) and without exploiting within-country variation (e.g., sectors or regions that faced different payment norms), it is hard to interpret the positive coefficient as causal. Provide more evidence or robustness checks that align differences in payment delays exclusively with the directive (e.g., controlling for contemporaneous macro shocks, showing that other “non-treated” size comparisons do not produce similar patterns, or using an IV for payment delay).

3. **Interpretation of sign and policy implications** – The positive coefficient is marginally significant and interpreted as showing the directive failed or backfired. Yet without a clear mechanism (e.g., did enforcement weaken small firms’ bargaining power, or did better-recording of deaths change reporting?) such claims risk overreaching. The paper should either tone down the causal language (“failed to help”) or bolster it with additional evidence—perhaps by correlating the triple interaction with enforcement proxies, claimant behavior, or payment behavior data to demonstrate the directive indeed had no intended effect. Otherwise, emphasize the descriptive nature of the puzzling positive coefficient.

**Suggestions**

1. **Strengthen pre-trend diagnostics** – Extend the event study to include a placebo “pseudo-treatment” year (e.g., 2010) and report the triple interaction for that year. Alternatively, re-run the event study dropping every other pre-treatment year to check whether the post-2013 rise in the coefficient simply reflects regression noise. Consider visualizing the raw small-minus-large death-rate gap in high-delay versus low-delay countries over time to demonstrate that there is no dramatic divergence pre-2013. Including a formal F-test for equality of pre-treatment coefficients would also help.

2. **Augment control variables or fixed effects** – The current specification uses saturated country×size, country×year, and size×year effects, which leave little room for additional covariates. Nevertheless, controlling for time-varying country shocks that plausibly affect small firms differentially (e.g., annual GDP growth, sovereign bond spreads, government SME programs) could help. If these covariates cannot enter linearly due to collinearity with fixed effects, consider using lagged values or interacting them with size dummies to absorb heterogeneous macro shocks. At a minimum, show that the results are robust when excluding countries undergoing major macro disturbances (Greece, Cyprus) to alleviate concerns that the aftermath of the sovereign crisis drives the positive coefficient.

3. **Explore heterogeneous mechanisms** – Since the main interpretation is “rights without enforcement,” provide empirical proxies for enforcement intensity, such as national-level directives enforcement scores, public procurement late-payment statistics, or survey-based usage of the directive by SMEs. Even if such data are only available for a subset of countries or years, a correlation between higher enforcement/use and smaller (or negative) triple-difference coefficients would substantiate the policy narrative. Alternatively, split the sample by countries with stronger rule-of-law/government effectiveness to see whether the directive’s impact differs, which would lend credibility to the mechanism that enforcement matters.

4. **Consider alternative outcomes** – The paper already examines three-year survival rates, but these coefficients are imprecise. It would be helpful to analyze additional outcomes that are more proximal to the directive’s mechanism—such as the birth rate (does the directive deter entry/compliance costs?) or average size-class growth rates. Exploring outcomes available at the sector or regional level could also provide more granular variation and increase statistical power.

5. **Discuss economic significance alongside statistical significance** – The large positive coefficient is statistically marginal; the narrative would benefit from framing it in terms of potential confidence intervals and discussing the range of plausible effects (from zero to ~+1 percentage point). Provide a table that shows how the effect compares to other determinants of the death rate (e.g., the structural difference between size classes or the effect of a business cycle). This contextualization would help readers interpret whether the coefficient reflects a meaningful worsening for small firms or statistical noise.

6. **Clarify sample and intensity definitions** – The paper standardizes payment days and uses a binary above-median split but does not describe how missing Intrum values (if any) were handled. Include an appendix figure showing the distribution of payment delays across countries, and indicate how sensitive the results are to altering the threshold (e.g., top quartile) or to imputing missing delay measures. Similarly, clarify that small firms include zero-employee units, which may behave differently (e.g., more self-employment). Presenting results separately for zero, 1–4, and 5–9 employee classes could uncover whether the effect is driven by self-employed units rather than micro-enterprises.

7. **Broaden discussion of limitations** – The discussion mentions enforcement gaps but does not fully grapple with the possibility that the directive reduced payment days but still left survival unchanged (i.e., the coefficient could be zero under the true effect, and the positive sign could come from measurement error). Acknowledge the limitations of country-level aggregates (lack of treatment heterogeneity, inability to see firm-level payment behavior) and suggest future directions (e.g., using invoice-level data or legal claims) that could better trace the causal chain.

By addressing these points, the paper would better justify the causal claim, provide richer evidence for the enforcement interpretation, and strengthen its policy relevance.
