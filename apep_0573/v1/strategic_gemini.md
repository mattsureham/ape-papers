**SUBJECT:** Strategic Positioning of "Can Procedure Produce Competition? Evidence from EU Procurement Reform"

---

## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper evaluates a massive, continent-wide attempt to increase competition in the €2 trillion EU public procurement market through procedural simplification (the 2014 Directives). Exploiting staggered national transposition dates across 28 countries, the author finds a precisely estimated zero effect on competition metrics—single-bidder rates and bidder counts—suggesting that "paperwork" reforms are insufficient when structural barriers to entry (like bonding and incumbency) dominate.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this pitch quite well in the first two paragraphs. It correctly frames the "puzzle of persistent low competition" despite regulatory efforts. However, it could be more forceful in the second paragraph by explicitly stating that this wasn't just *a* reform, but the "most comprehensive revision in a decade," making the null result more shocking.

**Revised Pitch:**
*Public procurement accounts for 12% of GDP, yet one-third of EU contracts attract only a single bidder. This paper evaluates the 2014 EU Procurement Directives—the most ambitious attempt in a generation to solve this via procedural simplification—and finds that these 'stroke-of-the-pen' reforms failed to move the needle on competition. These results suggest that the bottleneck for government efficiency is not administrative red tape, but deep-seated structural barriers that require fundamentally different policy interventions.*

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution:** The paper provides the first comprehensive, quasi-experimental evaluation of the 2014 EU Directives, demonstrating that procedural simplification alone fails to increase market entry in the presence of structural barriers.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It differentiates itself well from single-country studies (Coviello & Mariniello; Cingano et al.) by operating at the EU-wide scale, though it must fight the perception that it's "just an EU-wide DiD."
*   **World vs. Literature:** It is framed as a question about the WORLD (Does procedure produce competition?), which is a major strength.
*   **The "Smart Economist" Test:** A reader would say, "It’s a DiD showing that making it easier to bid doesn't actually bring in new bidders." This is clear but runs the risk of being seen as "useful but expected."
*   **Making it bigger:** To elevate this from a "policy evaluation" to a "top-tier theory-testing" paper, the author needs to lean harder into the **Auction Theory** mismatch. The paper identifies a "Suggestive finding on award efficiency" (the award ratio fell). If the author can prove that the reform helped incumbents lower prices *without* new entry, it changes the story from a "failed reform" to a "different-mechanism reform."

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

*   **Closest Neighbors:** *Bosio et al. (2022)* on procurement regulations; *Cingano et al. (2023)* on Italian simplification; *Szucs (2023)* on EU-wide patterns.
*   **Strategy:** The paper currently "adds to" these. It should instead **challenge** the optimistic bias in the "simplification" literature. Cingano et al. find success in Italy; this paper should position itself as the "general equilibrium" or "broad-scale" reality check.
*   **Missing Links:** It should speak more to the **Industrial Organization** literature on entry (e.g., Berry 1992). The "So What?" for an IO economist is: "How high are the structural entry costs in procurement if a 100% reduction in administrative costs does nothing?"

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

*   **Setup:** The EU moves to electronic bidding and simplified forms to break the "single-bidder" curse.
*   **Tension:** If "red tape" is the primary barrier, we should see an explosion in SME participation and bidding. But we don't.
*   **Resolution:** Precise nulls. Procedural levers are empty.
*   **Implications:** Policy must pivot to breaking incumbency and structural risk-sharing (bonding).

**Evaluation:** The arc is clean. However, the "Resolution" is currently a bit flat because it's a null. The narrative would be stronger if the author highlighted the "Award Ratio" result more—if prices fell while bidders stayed the same, the reform actually changed *bidder behavior* even if it didn't change *market structure*.

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

*   **The Lead Fact:** "The EU spent years mandating e-procurement and simpler forms, and the single-bidder rate didn't budge by even 0.1%."
*   **Reaction:** Economists will lean in because it validates the "Structural over Procedural" worldview, but they might reach for their phones if they think the null is just due to "noisy transposition dates."
*   **The Null:** This is a "Great Null." It's rare to see a reform this large have an effect this small. It suggests the European Commission's entire theory of change for the last decade was wrong.

---

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

*   **Front-load the Mechanism:** Move the "Structural vs. Procedural" discussion from the conclusion to the introduction.
*   **Appendix:** The robustness checks (Bacon, Rambachan-Roth) are great but take up too much mental space in the results. Keep them tight to prove the null is "real," then get back to the *economics* of why it happened.
*   **Visuals:** Figure 2 is the most important chart. It needs to be even cleaner to show the "nothing happened" story immediately.

---

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The gap between this and the AER is **Economic Theory**. Right now, it's an excellent policy evaluation. To make it an AER paper, it needs to quantify the "Structural Barriers."

**Single Most Impactful Advice:** Use the null result to back-calculate a lower bound on the *structural* costs of entry. If the procedural simplification reduced bid costs by $X$ (estimate this from other literature), and entry didn't change, what does that imply about the magnitude of the incumbency advantage? Turn the null from a "failure of policy" into a "measurement of a fundamental market friction."
