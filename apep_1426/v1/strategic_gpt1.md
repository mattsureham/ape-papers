## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether exogenous competition for television attention reduces news coverage of workplace safety. Using pre-scheduled mega-events like the Olympics and Super Bowl as competing-news shocks, it shows that safety-related TV coverage falls during those weeks, and interprets this as evidence that media visibility of regulatory risk is fragile and potentially relevant for deterrence.

A busy economist should care only if the paper is ultimately about something larger than newsroom allocation—namely, whether media attention is an input into regulatory enforcement and compliance. In its current form, though, the paper mostly demonstrates crowd-out in coverage, not deterrence or compliance effects. That makes the pitch narrower than the introduction wants it to be.

### Does the paper articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Not quite. The first two paragraphs try to sell a big question—how employers learn about enforcement risk and whether media visibility affects compliance—but the paper only delivers a smaller result: that safety news is displaced by sports spectacles. So the current opening overpromises. It invites the reader to expect a paper on regulation, deterrence, and workplace safety outcomes, then delivers a paper on media attention allocation.

### The pitch the paper should have

Here is the version the first two paragraphs should say instead:

> Regulatory deterrence depends not only on what agencies do, but also on what firms hear about. In practice, information about workplace hazards and enforcement actions reaches employers through a media environment with fixed attention and intense competition from unrelated spectacles. This paper asks whether pre-scheduled mega-events mechanically crowd out television coverage of workplace safety, making regulatory risk less visible even when underlying hazards do not change.
>
> Using closed-caption data from major U.S. cable news networks from 2015–2023, I show that Olympic and Super Bowl weeks significantly reduce workplace-safety coverage. The result does not establish that compliance changes, but it identifies a neglected input into deterrence: the media visibility of enforcement is itself endogenous to unrelated news pressure.

That is a cleaner and more honest pitch. It is smaller, but it is coherent.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper shows that pre-scheduled mega-events crowd out television coverage of workplace safety, implying that the media visibility of regulatory enforcement is itself sensitive to exogenous shocks in news demand.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?

Only partially. The paper names relevant literatures, but the novelty is still blurry because it sits between three familiar moves:

1. competing-news instruments crowd out coverage;
2. media coverage matters for public outcomes;
3. OSHA publicity can deter violations.

Right now the reader could reasonably summarize the paper as: “It’s an Eisensee-Strömberg-style competing-news paper applied to workplace safety, but only the first stage.” That is not a sufficiently sharp differentiation for AER-level positioning.

The closest differentiation the authors seem to want is:
- existing work shows publicity can deter;
- this paper shows organic media visibility is exogenously variable and can be displaced.

That is a defensible contribution, but it is much more modest than the introduction suggests.

### Is the contribution framed as a question about the world, or a gap in a literature?

It begins as a question about the world—how employers learn about enforcement threats—but then slides into “we establish a necessary condition” and “apply a competing-news instrument to a new domain.” That is literature-gap framing. The stronger framing is world-facing: firms’ perceived enforcement risk depends on what media happen to cover, and that visibility is vulnerable to unrelated spectacles.

Still, even that world-facing framing has limits because the paper does not actually show that employer beliefs or compliance behavior move.

### Could a smart economist explain to a colleague what’s new?

Yes, but not in a way that sounds big. They would likely say: “It shows that workplace safety coverage on TV gets crowded out by the Olympics and Super Bowl, which matters if you think media attention is part of deterrence.” They would not say: “This changes how we think about workplace safety regulation.” That is the problem.

So no, the introduction does not yet arm the reader with a crisp, high-level answer to “what’s new?” beyond “another competing-news application.”

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Most importantly: connect the visibility shock to an economically meaningful downstream outcome.

Most obvious possibilities:
- OSHA inspections, citations, penalties, or violation severity at high frequency;
- workplace accidents, fatalities, or incident reporting, if timing permits;
- search behavior, firm attention, or employer information acquisition around safety topics;
- geographic heterogeneity based on local exposure to affected stations or industries more likely to respond to media salience.

A second route would be mechanism:
- show the content crowded out is specifically enforcement-related safety news, not generic workplace mentions;
- show displacement is larger when OSHA or major accidents would otherwise have been especially salient;
- show differential effects in places or sectors where television remains a more important information source.

A third route would be framing:
- pitch this as a paper on endogenous regulatory salience, not just workplace safety news.

But bluntly, the biggest improvement is not a different outcome variable inside the current design. It is adding the second stage of the argument: does reduced visibility alter behavior?

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Regulatory agencies cannot directly monitor every firm, so deterrence depends on firms perceiving enforcement risk. Media coverage may be one way firms learn about accidents, sanctions, and the consequences of noncompliance.

### Tension

That informational channel is hard to study because safety incidents are endogenous: bad accidents create both media coverage and enforcement responses. So we do not know whether media visibility itself is stable, or whether it is swamped by unrelated news competition.

### Resolution

Pre-scheduled mega-events reduce television coverage of workplace safety. Therefore, safety visibility is not fixed; it fluctuates with unrelated spectacles.

### Implications

Potentially, the deterrence effect of enforcement depends on the surrounding media environment. Regulatory communication may be less effective when the news cycle is crowded.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

Serviceable, but incomplete. The paper has setup and tension. It has a partial resolution. It does not have a fully satisfying implication because the paper stops one step before the payoff.

This is the core narrative problem: the introduction promises a story about compliance and deterrence, but the paper resolves only a story about coverage displacement. That leaves the reader with “interesting first stage, but where is the paper?”

### Is it a collection of results looking for a story?

A bit. The various pieces—event windows, network heterogeneity, randomization inference, discussion of Johnson—are all trying to support a larger claim the paper cannot quite make. The story it should be telling is:

> Regulatory salience is an endogenous input into enforcement. We document that even highly consequential topics like workplace safety can disappear from public view for reasons unrelated to underlying risk.

That is the strongest coherent story available from the current evidence. It is still probably not enough for AER, but it is at least a real story.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?

“During Olympic and Super Bowl weeks, major cable news networks substantially reduce workplace safety coverage—even though workplace hazards themselves are not changing.”

That is the cleanest fact.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

Polite lean-in, not excitement. Economists who care about media, attention, or regulation will see the intuition immediately. But many will ask, within thirty seconds: “And does that matter for actual violations, inspections, or accidents?” If the answer is no, not in this paper, attention will drop.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Almost certainly: “Does the drop in coverage change behavior?”

Other likely questions:
- Is this just substitution across outlets?
- Why cable TV, specifically?
- How much real-world regulatory salience is lost?
- Are firms actually watching this?

### If findings are modest: is the modesty interesting?

The finding is not null, but it is modest in consequence as currently established. The paper tries to make the result bigger through back-of-envelope calculations equating airtime loss to OSHA press releases. That move is understandable, but because it rests on heroic assumptions and the paper itself admits as much, it does not fully clear the “so what?” bar.

This does not feel like a failed experiment. It feels like an incomplete paper: a credible and somewhat interesting empirical fact that has not yet been turned into a major economics result.
