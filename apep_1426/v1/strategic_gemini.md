## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
This paper argues that regulatory deterrence is not just a function of agency action, but of the media environment that amplifies it. Using "mega-events" like the Olympics as an exogenous shock to news airtime, the authors show that when sports dominate the screen, workplace safety news is crowded out. This matters because it identifies a "visibility cycle" where the effective threat of regulation fluctuates based on the unrelated schedule of the entertainment industry.

**Evaluation:** The paper does a decent job in the first two paragraphs, but it leans too heavily on the "how" (GDELT data, IV strategy) rather than the "why." 

**The Pitch the Paper Should Have:**
"Because OSHA can only inspect 0.8% of workplaces, the efficacy of US safety regulation rests almost entirely on deterrence—the belief that violations will be caught and publicized. We show that this 'threat of shame' is mediated by a zero-sum competition for airtime: when pre-scheduled events like the Olympics occur, coverage of workplace accidents drops by 0.36 standard deviations. This suggests that the real-world power of the law is inadvertently dictated by the sports calendar, creating predictable windows where the 'watchdog' is essentially off-duty."

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**One-sentence contribution:** The paper establishes that organic media coverage of regulatory issues is subject to exogenous "crowd-out" by entertainment, creating a necessary first-stage for a media-driven theory of regulatory deterrence.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It is a direct "domestic" application of Eisensee and Strömberg (2007). It differentiates itself from Johnson (2020) by looking at *organic* news rather than agency-controlled press releases.
*   **World vs. Literature:** It currently sits in the middle. It’s framed as "we extend the literature on media and enforcement," which is weak. It should be framed as: "Does the Super Bowl make factories more dangerous?"
*   **What would make it bigger?** This paper is currently a **First Stage looking for a Second Stage.** The authors admit they don't have the violation data (Second Stage). To be an AER paper, they *must* link this airtime drop to a subsequent spike in workplace injuries or violations. Without that, it’s a "note" on media behavior, not a landmark paper on regulatory economics.

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
The paper sits at the intersection of Political Economy (Strömberg) and Labor/Regulatory Economics (Matthew Johnson).

*   **Closest neighbors:** Eisensee and Strömberg (2007) [QJE], Johnson (2020) [AER], and Gao et al. (2020) [JFE].
*   **Positioning Strategy:** It should **bridge** these. It takes the *mechanism* of the first and applies it to the *outcome* of the second to address the *concern* of the third (media decline).
*   **Missing Conversations:** The paper needs to speak more to **Rational Inattention**. Do managers actually watch the news and update their priors on the probability of an OSHA audit? Or is it a "saliency" story?

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC
*   **Setup:** Regulation relies on deterrence because inspectors are rare.
*   **Tension:** Deterrence requires information, but news airtime is a finite, zero-sum resource.
*   **Resolution:** When the Olympics are on, safety news disappears.
*   **Implications:** Deterrence is seasonal and fragile.

**Evaluation:** The arc is clean but the resolution is currently "half-baked." We see the news drop, but we don't see the bodies. A "collection of results looking for a story" is too harsh, but it is a "mechanism looking for a consequence."

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
*   **The Fact:** "When the Super Bowl is on, the media forgets that factories explode, and OSHA's ability to scare companies into compliance drops by the equivalent of 10 press releases."
*   **Reaction:** Economists will lean in because the IV is clever, but then they will ask the "So What": **"Did anyone actually die because of this?"**
*   **The Null:** If they find the news drops but violations *don't* move, it suggests the "visibility cycle" is an academic curiosity rather than a regulatory crisis.

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
*   **Front-load the Back-of-the-Envelope:** The calculation comparing airtime to OSHA press releases (8-12 releases) is the most compelling part of the paper. It should be in the Intro.
*   **Shorten the GDELT Technicals:** We believe the data exists; move the API query specifics to the appendix.
*   **Expand the "Mechanism" section:** Explain *why* Fox News (noted in the abstract) has the largest displacement. Is it a political leaning, or a difference in their "entertainment vs news" mix?
