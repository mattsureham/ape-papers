## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether media attention to workplace safety is itself scarce and systematically displaced by major pre-scheduled TV spectacles such as the Olympics and the Super Bowl. Using TV closed-caption data, it shows that when these events dominate airtime, coverage of workplace safety falls sharply, suggesting that the information environment surrounding regulatory deterrence is partly driven by unrelated news congestion.

A busy economist should care only if this is framed as a paper about how media allocation shapes the effective reach of regulation, not as a paper about keyword counts on cable news. The potentially interesting idea is that enforcement capacity is not just about inspectors and penalties; it also depends on whether salient information about enforcement gets through.

**Does the paper articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Not quite. The introduction starts with deterrence and OSHA, which is sensible, but then overreaches by naming a broad “visibility cycle” and hinting at compliance effects that the paper does not actually estimate. The actual paper is narrower: it shows exogenous crowd-out of safety coverage. That is a clean fact, but the introduction currently sells a bigger paper than it delivers.

**What the first two paragraphs should say instead:**

> Regulatory agencies do not deter misconduct through inspections alone. They also rely on publicity: employers learn about accidents, violations, and penalties through the news, and that information can amplify the reach of formal enforcement. If media attention is itself scarce, then the effectiveness of regulation may depend in part on unrelated events that crowd out coverage.
>
> This paper studies that media-allocation margin in the context of workplace safety. Using television closed-caption data from major U.S. news networks, I show that pre-scheduled mega-events such as the Olympics and the Super Bowl sharply reduce airtime devoted to workplace safety. The main contribution is not to estimate the full effect of media coverage on violations, but to establish a basic empirical fact: the visibility of workplace safety in mass media is endogenous to competing news pressure, creating a plausible channel through which the media environment can modulate regulatory deterrence.

That is the honest and strongest version of the paper’s pitch.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution in one sentence:**  
The paper documents that pre-scheduled mega-events exogenously crowd out television coverage of workplace safety, implying that the public visibility of regulatory risk is partly determined by unrelated competition for media attention.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?
Only partially. The paper gestures at three literatures—media and enforcement, competing-news instruments, and media decline—but the differentiation is still fuzzy. Right now the reader may hear: “This is an Eisensee-Stromberg-style crowd-out paper applied to OSHA/safety coverage.” That is not yet a distinctive AER-level contribution.

The closest differentiation problem is this: the paper does **not** estimate deterrence, only displacement. So relative to Johnson (2020), it is not “showing media-based deterrence”; it is showing a precondition for it. That is a much smaller claim and needs to be stated cleanly. Relative to Eisensee and Strömberg, it is a domain transfer plus a new data source. Domain transfer can matter, but only if the new domain yields a substantive insight about the world, not just a reusable instrument.

### Is the contribution framed as a question about the WORLD, or as filling a gap in a LITERATURE?
It oscillates. The best version is a world question: **How much does the effective visibility of workplace risk and enforcement depend on unrelated news competition?** But the paper keeps slipping into “necessary condition,” “first stage,” and “new application of a competing-news instrument,” which are literature-gap framings. Those are weaker.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?
At present, many would say: “It’s another competing-news paper, this time on workplace safety coverage.” That is the danger. The paper has not yet made the reader say: “Ah—regulatory deterrence depends on media congestion.” The first is a method/domain summary; the second is an economic idea.

### What would make this contribution bigger?
Most of all: connect the media crowd-out to an economically meaningful downstream margin.

Specific ways to do that:
1. **Outcome upgrade:** Show that crowd-out predicts some real-world enforcement or compliance outcome—OSHA complaints, inspections, citations, Google searches for OSHA, employer safety-related search intensity, or even investor/worker attention—anything beyond coverage itself.
2. **Mechanism upgrade:** Demonstrate that the displaced coverage is specifically enforcement-related rather than generic accident reporting. If the crowd-out is mainly about sensational accidents, the deterrence framing weakens.
3. **Comparison upgrade:** Benchmark workplace safety against other regulatory domains—environmental enforcement, financial misconduct, food safety—to make the paper about a general feature of governance rather than one topic.
4. **Framing upgrade:** Present this as a paper on **media congestion as a state variable in regulation**, not as a TV-news keyword paper.

Right now, the contribution is intelligible but modest.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Formal enforcement is sparse, so deterrence depends partly on public information about violations and penalties. Media coverage may amplify that information.

### Tension
But media attention is limited and endogenous. The very visibility of workplace safety may depend on unrelated events that consume airtime. If so, the informational reach of regulation fluctuates for reasons unrelated to underlying hazards.

### Resolution
The paper shows that during pre-scheduled mega-events, workplace safety coverage on TV falls meaningfully.

### Implications
If media visibility is crowded out by unrelated spectacles, then the effective reach of regulation may vary with the news cycle, and agencies may need to think strategically about timing and publicity.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
It has the bones of one, but the arc is unstable because the paper keeps promising a final act it does not contain. The actual narrative is:

1. Deterrence may depend on publicity.  
2. Publicity may be crowded out.  
3. We show it is crowded out.

That is a coherent short paper. But the introduction, abstract, and conclusion try to smuggle in:
4. Therefore compliance may change materially.

That is speculative, and readers will feel the missing piece. So the paper currently reads somewhat like a collection of suggestive results surrounding a missing main result.

### What story should it be telling?
It should tell a more disciplined story:

> Regulation operates through attention as well as sanctions. Attention is scarce. Pre-scheduled spectacles reallocate it. In workplace safety, this means the public visibility of hazards and enforcement fluctuates for reasons orthogonal to actual safety conditions. That fact matters because it identifies a neglected input into deterrence: the media environment.

That story is smaller, but it is internally consistent.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Almost certainly:  
**“Do violations, inspections, complaints, or employer behavior change when coverage is crowded out?”**

That is the central strategic problem of the paper. The paper anticipates this question but cannot answer it.

### If the findings are modest, is the modesty itself interesting?
The result is not null, but it is still a first-stage-style finding. The paper tries to argue that establishing a necessary condition is useful. That is true, but not enough by itself for AER unless the fact is strikingly general, conceptually deep, or opens a major new empirical agenda. Here it feels more like a plausible intermediate result than a field-defining finding.

So the current version risks feeling like a competent pilot study rather than a completed top-journal paper.
