## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper identifies a systematic measurement error in the U.S. opioid epidemic: counties using elected coroners (often without medical training) report significantly lower drug overdose death rates than adjacent counties using appointed medical examiners. Exploiting border-county pairs within the same state, the author finds that coroners undercount deaths by 2.6 per 100,000, a gap that has doubled since the rise of synthetic opioids (fentanyl). This matters because these "invisible" deaths bias every existing economic study of the crisis and result in less federal funding for the counties that need it most.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this pitch exceptionally well in the first two paragraphs. It immediately hooks the reader with the institutional absurdity (sheriffs vs. pathologists) and the stakes (every regression is wrong). No rewrite is needed; the "hook" is AER-quality.

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution:** The paper provides the first causal estimate of how medicolegal institutional design systematically biases administrative mortality data, showing that the measurement error is large enough to distort national trends and resource allocation.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It clearly moves beyond the existing public health literature (Ruhm, Buchanich) which is largely correlational, by using a border-pair design to isolate the institutional effect from the underlying drug prevalence.
*   **Framing:** It is framed as a question about the **WORLD** (Are we miscounting the dead?) rather than just a gap in the literature. This is its greatest strength.
*   **Clarity:** A smart economist would immediately understand the "New York vs. Ohio" or "Border county" logic. It is not "just another DiD"; it’s a "missing data" paper.
*   **Bigger Contribution:** To make this even bigger, the author should explore **downstream economic consequences**. Does this undercount lead to lower local labor force participation estimates being misinterpreted? Does it affect local property values differently? Connecting it to a specific "wrong" conclusion in a high-profile paper (e.g., Case & Deaton) would be the "knockout blow."

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

*   **Closest Neighbors:** Case & Deaton (2015) on deaths of despair; Ruhm (2017) on overdose underreporting; Meyer et al. (2015) on administrative data errors.
*   **Positioning:** The paper should position itself as the **institutional foundation** for the "Deaths of Despair" literature. It’s not attacking Case & Deaton, but rather providing the "error bars" their work lacks.
*   **Unexpected Connection:** The author mentions the "dark figure" in crime statistics. Strengthening the connection to the **Economics of Information** or **Public Finance** (how formulas based on bad data create poverty traps) would broaden the audience from health economists to general interest.

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

*   **Setup:** The U.S. uses an archaic, fragmented system to count deaths.
*   **Tension:** We rely on this data for billions in funding and all our science, but we suspect the "counters" (coroners) are outclassed by modern drugs like fentanyl.
*   **Resolution:** Coroner counties "miss" thousands of deaths annually, and the problem is getting worse as the chemistry of the epidemic gets more complex.
*   **Implications:** A "perverse feedback loop" where the least-resourced areas stay underfunded because they can't document their own crisis.

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong. It feels like a detective story where the culprit is an administrative rule from the Middle Ages.

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

At a dinner party, you lead with: **"In many U.S. counties, the person deciding if you died of an overdose is an elected official with zero medical training—and they are missing about 1 in every 30 deaths."**

People lean in. The follow-up is: "Does this mean the 'Red State' vs. 'Blue State' overdose gap is just a measurement artifact?" If the paper can answer that, it's a lead article.

---

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

*   **Front-loading:** The "Widening Gap" (Table 3) is actually more interesting than the "Main Result" (Table 2). I would move the temporal analysis earlier to show that the institution-technology mismatch is the driving force.
*   **Appendix:** The "Other" county category (453 counties) is currently excluded. A robustness check showing what happens when they are included/excluded would be helpful.
*   **Conclusion:** The "perverse feedback loop" (Section 6) is the most "AER" part of the paper. It should be expanded into a formal "Back of the Envelope" section with more detail on specific federal grants (SAMHSA/CDC).

---

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The gap is **Ambition**. Currently, it is a very high-quality "fact-finding" paper. To be a "top-of-the-AER" paper, it needs to prove that this measurement error **changes a major economic conclusion**. 

**Single most impactful advice:** Re-estimate a landmark finding (like the impact of Medicaid expansion or PDMPs on mortality) and show that the effect size changes significantly when you "correct" the coroner-county undercounts. If you can show a famous result is actually a "coroner effect," the paper becomes a mandatory cite for the entire field.
