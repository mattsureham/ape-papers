## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper argues that part of the measured geography of the U.S. opioid epidemic is an artifact of who investigates deaths. Counties using elected coroners appear to have lower overdose mortality than neighboring counties using professional medical examiners, suggesting that institutional differences in death investigation generate systematic undercounting of overdose deaths and distort both research and policy targeting.

A busy economist should care because this is, at root, a paper about measurement in one of the most widely used outcome variables in applied micro health, public, and labor economics. If true, it implies that a large class of county-level opioid papers may be estimating policy effects on a mismeasured dependent variable whose error is nonclassical and institutionally structured.

### Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Mostly yes, and better than many submissions. The opening is vivid, concrete, and the basic intuition is immediately legible. The problem is that the intro slightly oversells the forensic color before crisply stating the economics question. It opens like a public health/institutions paper and only later makes clear that the real stakes are for the empirical economics literature using overdose mortality as data.

### What the first two paragraphs should say instead

The first two paragraphs should make the paper’s core claim in this order:

1. Economists use county overdose mortality as if it were comparable across places.  
2. It is not comparable because counties differ sharply in medicolegal death investigation capacity.  
3. This generates systematic measurement error, not just noise.  
4. The paper estimates the size of that error using adjacent counties that differ in death investigation system.  
5. The implications are for both scientific inference and resource allocation.

### The pitch the paper should have

“Economists and policymakers treat county overdose mortality as a comparable measure of the opioid crisis across places. But U.S. counties differ dramatically in who certifies deaths: some rely on professional medical examiners, others on elected coroners with limited forensic capacity. Using adjacent counties within the same state that differ in death-investigation system, I show that coroner counties report substantially fewer overdose deaths than neighboring medical-examiner counties, implying that a meaningful share of the local variation in overdose mortality reflects measurement rather than true incidence. The result matters because county overdose deaths are a workhorse outcome in research on opioid policy and a key input into federal funding decisions.”

That is the AER-relevant pitch: measurement of a canonical outcome, with consequences for inference and public resource allocation.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?

“I can take two adjacent counties in the same state, exposed to broadly similar drug markets, and the one with an elected coroner reports meaningfully fewer overdose deaths than the one with a medical examiner. The difference seems to widen exactly when fentanyl makes forensic identification harder.”

That is a good lead.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

Many would lean in, especially health, public, labor, and applied micro economists who have read or written papers using county overdose mortality. The topic is inherently consequential, and the “who counts the dead?” framing is memorable.

But the lean-in is conditional on quickly clarifying why this is not merely a forensic-public-health curiosity. If presented as “a border DiD about coroners,” some people will drift. If presented as “your dependent variable is systematically wrong,” they will pay attention.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Most likely:

- “How much does this actually change conclusions in the opioid policy literature?”
- Or: “Is this about total overdose undercount, or mainly drug specificity within overdose?”
- Or: “Does this generalize beyond opioids to other causes of death?”

Those are exactly the questions the paper should anticipate more centrally.

### If findings are modest, is the modesty itself interesting?

The estimates are not trivial, but neither are they headline-explosive at the aggregate level. The national undercount is a few thousand deaths, not tens of thousands. So the paper should avoid overclaiming on aggregate national totals.

The more persuasive “so what” is not “the national total is massively wrong,” but rather:

- the error is **systematic**, not random;
- it is **spatially correlated with institutions**;
- it can bias **cross-county comparisons and policy evaluations**;
- and it likely matters most exactly where analysts use county data to infer local treatment effects.

That is an intellectually interesting result even if the aggregate undercount is modest.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The main gap is **ambition and framing**, not raw competence.

This is already a solid, readable paper with a clear empirical fact. But AER-level excitement usually requires one of two things:

1. a truly first-order empirical result, or  
2. a result that changes how economists think about an important class of data, policies, or institutions.

This paper is closer to the second path, but it has not fully committed to it.

### What is the main gap?

Primarily a **framing problem**, secondarily a **scope problem**.

- **Framing problem:** The paper still presents itself too much as a better estimate of an existing public-health concern, rather than as a paper about institutional production of official statistics and the downstream consequences for empirical economics.
- **Scope problem:** It documents the measurement distortion, but does not yet show enough about what that distortion does to the conclusions economists or policymakers actually draw.
- Less of a novelty problem: the underlying concern is known, but the paper’s institutional and border-based framing gives it some novelty.
- Some ambition problem too: it stops at documenting the gap rather than demonstrating how much it changes a live economics question.

### What is the single most impactful piece of advice?

**Use the measurement result to re-interpret a canonical county-level opioid relationship or policy estimate, so the paper shows not just that the data are biased, but that the bias changes substantive economic conclusions.**

That is the biggest upgrade available. If the paper can show that controlling for or correcting medicolegal systems meaningfully alters estimates for a widely studied opioid policy, then it moves from “interesting institutional measurement paper” to “paper that changes how the field should work.”

Without that extra step, I see this as a strong field-journal or good general-interest paper, but not yet an AER paper. With it, the paper has a much more plausible top-journal path because it would speak directly to the economics profession’s own empirical practice.
