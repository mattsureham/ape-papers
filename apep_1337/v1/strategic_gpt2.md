## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether sector-specific trade protection can redistribute earnings across racial groups because different groups are sorted into different industries. Using the 2018–2019 China tariffs, it argues that Asian workers benefited relatively more than White workers because Asians were disproportionately employed in the manufacturing sectors most protected by Section 301.

A busy economist should care because the broader question is important: when policy targets sectors, it may also target demographic groups indirectly through industrial composition. That is a potentially general insight extending well beyond tariffs to industrial policy, place-based policy, and climate subsidies.

Does the paper itself articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs? **Partly, but not well enough.** The current opening is vivid, but it gets too quickly into the race-specific application before establishing the broader economic question. It currently reads like a niche paper on “tariffs and the Asian-White wage gap,” when the stronger pitch is “sectoral policy creates demographic redistribution through composition.”

### The pitch the paper should have

A stronger first two paragraphs would say something like:

> Sector-specific policies do not just redistribute across firms, consumers, and regions; they can also redistribute across demographic groups when those groups are unevenly sorted across industries. This paper studies that broader mechanism in the context of the 2018–2019 U.S. tariffs on Chinese imports.  
>
> I ask whether Section 301 tariffs widened racial earnings gaps by disproportionately benefiting workers employed in the sectors receiving protection. Using quarterly state-industry-race data, I show that the Asian-White earnings gap rose more in tariff-exposed sectors, consistent with a general “composition windfall” mechanism: industrial policy can generate unintended demographic redistribution even when it is formally race-neutral.

That is the AER-adjacent version of the pitch. The current version sounds like a specialized application. The stronger version sounds like a paper about how the world works.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper’s contribution is to propose and document a **composition windfall** channel whereby sector-specific trade protection redistributes earnings across racial groups because those groups are unevenly sorted across industries.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?

**Not clearly enough.** The introduction names several trade-policy papers and some racial-gap papers, but it does not sharply distinguish what is new relative to them. Right now the novelty seems to be:

- a new subgroup outcome,
- in a familiar tariff setting,
- using an ordinary reduced-form design.

That is not yet enough for AER positioning. The paper needs to make clear that it is **not just the first paper to run a race-specific DiD on Section 301**. “No one has looked at Asians before” is not a strong contribution by itself.

The real differentiator, if the author wants one, is the mechanism/general framework:
- policies aimed at sectors can generate demographic redistribution through ex ante sorting;
- this channel is portable across many policy domains;
- the tariff episode is one test case.

That is much stronger than “the Asian-White gap in this context has not been studied.”

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or filling a gap in a LITERATURE?

At present it is split, but it leans too much toward **filling a gap in the literature**. The paper repeatedly says race-specific effects are underexplored, that the Asian-White gap in trade contexts has received little attention, and that QWI can be used for this purpose. Those are literature-gap and data-gap claims.

The stronger framing is world-facing:

- When governments target industries, who actually benefits?
- Are formally race-neutral industrial policies demographically redistributive?
- Does demographic sorting across industries create systematic winners and losers from trade policy?

That is the version that belongs in a top general-interest journal.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the intro?

Currently, they might say: **“It’s a DiD/DDD paper showing that Trump tariffs may have increased Asian relative wages in protected manufacturing.”**

That is not good enough.

You want them to say: **“The paper shows that sectoral policy has an overlooked distributional margin—demographic composition. Tariffs didn’t just reallocate across places and industries; they reallocated across racial groups because of who works where.”**

At present, the paper does not force that interpretation hard enough.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Specific ways to make it bigger:

1. **Broaden the object from one racial comparison to a general equilibrium of demographic incidence.**  
   Instead of centering the paper entirely on Asian vs. White, show a broader incidence map across racial/ethnic groups, or at least motivate why Asian-White is the most informative case within a general framework. Right now the case selection looks somewhat ad hoc.

2. **Connect to industrial policy beyond tariffs.**  
   The conclusion mentions CHIPS and green subsidies, but this should be part of the main framing, not an afterthought. The paper’s audience grows substantially if it is really about demographic incidence of sector-targeted policy.

3. **Show that the main contribution is the mechanism, not the episode.**  
   The paper should treat Section 301 as a setting that reveals a general composition channel. Without that, it remains a narrow episode paper.

4. **Use outcomes that speak more directly to economic incidence.**  
   The earnings result is the centerpiece, but if the broader claim is redistribution, the paper would be more consequential if it could map incidence more fully: employment shares, workforce composition, mobility, exits/entries, within- vs between-sector channels, perhaps even regional heterogeneity tied to industrial sorting. Even conceptually laying this out would enlarge the paper.

5. **Make the comparison set more interesting.**  
   Comparing only Asians to Whites risks sounding like a niche subgroup exercise. A stronger framing would contrast groups based on ex ante exposure predicted by industrial sorting and then interpret Asians as the leading case.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Sector-specific policies like tariffs are typically analyzed as redistributing across consumers, firms, and regions. At the same time, racial groups are unevenly distributed across sectors.

### Tension

If demographic groups sort into different industries, then race-neutral sectoral policy may have group-specific effects. Yet this incidence margin is largely absent from standard analysis of trade protection.

### Resolution

The paper finds that the Asian-White earnings gap widened more in tariff-exposed sectors after Section 301, which it interprets as evidence that protected sectors generated a relative windfall for a group disproportionately employed there.

### Implications

The implication is that trade and industrial policy should be understood as having demographic incidence through industrial composition. More generally, any sector-targeted intervention may redistribute across demographic groups even absent explicit targeting.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

**It has the ingredients, but not a fully controlled arc.** At present it feels somewhat like a collection of results around one specification rather than a tightly organized story.

Problems with the current arc:

- The conceptual idea is broader than the empirical implementation, but the paper does not manage that gap gracefully.
- The paper admits substantial limitations early and often; intellectually honest, yes, but narratively it drains momentum.
- The “composition windfall” idea is the story, but the paper keeps reverting to “Asian-White wage gap under tariffs” as if that were the story.

### What story should it be telling?

The story should be:

1. **Policies target sectors.**
2. **Workers are sorted across sectors by demographic group.**
3. **Therefore policy incidence has a demographic composition channel.**
4. **Section 301 provides a vivid test case because protected sectors were disproportionately Asian in employment composition.**
5. **The evidence suggests this channel mattered.**
6. **This changes how we should think about industrial policy incidence more broadly.**

That is a clean narrative. The current draft only intermittently tells it.

That is the most interesting fact.

**Some would lean in, but many would be skeptical quickly.** The instinctive follow-up is not “wow” so much as:

- “Is this about Asians specifically, or about any group concentrated in protected sectors?”
- “Is this a tariff paper, a race paper, or an industrial policy paper?”
- “How general is this mechanism?”

That is actually good news: the natural questions point toward the broader framing the paper should adopt.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

The key follow-up question would be:

> “Is the interesting result the specific Asian-White finding, or the general principle that policy incidence depends on demographic industry sorting?”

If the author cannot answer “the latter,” the paper feels too small for AER.

### If findings are modest or noisy, is the result still interesting?

Potentially yes. The paper itself already tells us the immediate pre-COVID effect is more modest, and the broader evidence is suggestive rather than decisive. That can still be interesting if the paper makes the case that:

- the object of interest is a new incidence margin,
- even modest effects reveal an overlooked policy channel,
- the specific sign patterns are consistent with ex ante sorting.

But if the paper sells itself mainly as “we estimate the tariff effect on the Asian-White wage gap,” then modest or fragile estimates will feel like a failed niche application rather than a meaningful finding.
