**Idea Fidelity**

The paper largely tracks the idea manifest: it uses the Section 301 tariff exposure index, the QWI Race-Hispanic panel disaggregated by race, and a triple-difference strategy (tariff exposure × Asian vs. White × pre/post July 2018) to assess effects on the Asian-White wage gap. However, important elements from the manifest are missing or only partially implemented. The manifest promised instrumentation via pre-2018 industry×race employment shares to address endogenous composition; no such Bartik-style instrument appears in the paper. Similarly, the manifest anticipated a fine-grained industry exposure index (CBP output linked via HS6-to-NAICS) and results about mechanisms other than wage levels (employment/hiring/separation), whereas the paper works with coarser 2-digit exposure and only briefly touches on hiring. Finally, while the manifest argued for a substantial pre-trend and robustness story, the paper admits pre-trend failures without pursuing stronger solutions (e.g., flexible controls for compositional shifts). These omissions weaken alignment with the initial idea’s emphasis on credible identification and comprehensive mechanism checks.

**Summary**

The paper documents that the Section 301 tariffs coincided with a widening of the Asian-White earnings gap in tariff-exposed sectors, suggesting a “composition windfall” because Asian workers are concentrated in protected industries such as electronics and machinery. Using a QWI-based triple-difference framework with industry tariff exposure × Asian indicator × post-2018 interaction and rich fixed effects, the author finds a statistically significant log-earnings effect that survives state×quarter controls and displays a contrasting sign when the counterfactual race is Black. An event study and pre-COVID versus full-sample comparisons are used to highlight the timing and potential confounding role of the pandemic.

**Essential Points**

1. **Parallel Trends Violation Undermines Identification:** The event study shows significant pre-treatment DDD coefficients (e.g., $t=-8$ and $t=-2$), and the paper’s own Wald test rejects the null. Without convincing remedies (e.g., flexible controls for anticipated tariffs, placebo falsification with non-tariff sectors, or matching on pre-trends), the key identifying assumption fails, so the triple interaction cannot be interpreted causally. The authors must either re-specify the design to restore pre-trend balance (e.g., by controlling for race-specific sectoral trends or by shifting the sample to a shorter window where pre-trends are flat) or clearly frame the results as descriptive correlations.

2. **Endogeneity of Industry Exposure and Race Composition:** The tariff exposure variable is at the 2-digit level and may itself be correlated with unobserved trends that differentially affect Asians and Whites (e.g., technology adoption in electronics). Because exposure is not randomized and is potentially confounded with industry-specific evolutions in demand or skill requirements, and because there is no structural instrument as suggested in the manifest, the interaction may pick up existing sector-race dynamics rather than the causal effect of tariffs. The paper needs to demonstrate that sector exposure is orthogonal to evolving racial wage trends aside from tariffs—such as by conditioning on pre-treatment sector-specific race distributions or employing an instrument constructed from exogenous import shares (e.g., variation in the composition of affected HS codes across sectors).

3. **Mechanism and Interpretation Ambiguity:** The paper’s main coefficient is sizable, yet there is little discussion of economic magnitude beyond converting the interaction into a 5.5% relative gain. Importantly, the Black placebo produces a negative sign, but without describing the composition of Black workers across sectors or providing evidence that retaliatory tariffs indeed hit Black-heavy industries, this placebo is suggestive but not conclusive. Moreover, the hiring-rate result (negative sign) suggests other labor market adjustments; linking these more formally to wage changes would clarify the mechanism. The authors should better integrate measures of racial employment shares, firm profitability, or input-output structure to substantiate the “composition windfall” story rather than relying solely on the triple interaction.

**Suggestions**

- **Strengthen Pre-trend Diagnostics and Adjust for Anticipation:** Since the pre-treatment coefficients are significant, consider re-estimating the event study using a more flexible control—e.g., include sector-specific quadratic trends interacted with race, or allow the timing of “Post” to vary across sectors based on when tariffs affected them (List 1 vs. List 3). Alternatively, estimate the model on a narrower window (e.g., 2016–2019) where the pre-trends look flatter, and present these as the main specification. If anticipation from the March 2018 announcement is driving the spike at $t=-2$, consider modeling treatment as a distributed lag that ramps up from the announcement to implementation rather than a sharp pre/post shift.

- **Operationalize the Bartik Instrument or Additional Controls:** The manifest promised an instrument using pre-2018 industry×race employment shares. Implementing this would add credibility: construct a plausibly exogenous component of Asian employment exposure to treated sectors and use it to instrument the triple interaction, or at least include pre-treatment Asian × sector shares interacted with time trends to soak up existing composition dynamics. If this is infeasible, provide descriptive evidence showing that the sector exposure index is not systematically correlated with trends in Asian vs. White earnings before 2018. Additionally, consider including controls for sectoral capital intensity, offshoring exposure, or the share of skilled occupations to mitigate omitted variable bias.

- **Elaborate on Mechanism and Economic Significance:** Translate the 0.303 log-point coefficient into more tangible terms—e.g., how many dollars per month does that imply in high-exposure sectors, relative to the existing wage premium? Provide an explicit calculation linking the tariff exposure distribution (e.g., difference between NAICS 334 and the median sector) to the implied change in the Asian-White gap. Supplement the wage results with analyses of employment composition, such as whether Asian employment shares rose disproportionately in protected sectors post-tariff. The negative hiring-rate coefficient could mean reduced turnover due to tariff-induced rent increases; discuss whether this aligns with wage increases, and whether wages rise because incumbents stay longer or because firms pay more to retain scarce skilled Asians.

- **Deepen the Placebo and Robustness Portfolio:** The Black-White placebo is interesting but insufficient as a falsification. Add additional placebos—e.g., use non-tariff-exposed sectors only, or replace Asian workers with another group known to be absent from protected industries (e.g., Hispanic workers). Similarly, test whether sectors with similar pre-trends but zero tariff exposure show no effect, or whether the same triple interaction using non-Chinese import exposure (e.g., from Mexico) yields a null result. These robustness checks would bolster the causal claim even if pre-trends remain imperfect.

- **Clarify Sample and Measurement Choices:** The summary statistics table contains inconsistencies (e.g., monthly earnings averages differ between text and table). Ensure all reported figures come from the same data window and state whether amounts are in nominal dollars. Also, explain the sign and interpretation of the retaliatory exposure for agriculture (negative value) and how it enters the continuous exposure variable—does a negative exposure reduce the treatment effect, suggesting harm to the Asian-White gap in those sectors? Finally, discuss whether the use of 2-digit NAICS bias the exposure variation toward manufacturing and potentially hide within-sector heterogeneity; if possible, provide results at 3- or 4-digit level where available.

- **Explicitly Frame the Causal Claim:** Given the acknowledged limitations (pre-trend failure, coarse tariff exposure, COVID contamination), the conclusion should be careful not to overstate causality. Consider rephrasing the contribution as documenting suggestive evidence consistent with a composition channel, and present the results as motivating further investigation rather than definitive proof. If the causal claim is crucial, invest in additional identification strategies (e.g., synthetic control or matching) to triangulate the effect.

Implementing these suggestions will improve the paper’s credibility and sharpen its contribution to the emerging literature on the racial distributional effects of trade policy.
