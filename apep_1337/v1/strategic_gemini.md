**SUBJECT:** Strategic Positioning of "The Composition Windfall: Section 301 Tariffs and the Asian-White Manufacturing Wage Gap"

---

## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether the 2018–2019 "Trump Tariffs" on Chinese imports inadvertently redistributed income across racial groups due to preexisting industrial sorting. The author finds that because Asian workers are disproportionately concentrated in protected sectors like electronics and machinery, the tariffs widened the Asian-White wage gap by approximately 2.7% (in the pre-COVID window). It is a study of how "race-neutral" trade policy becomes "race-salient" through the arithmetic of labor market composition.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this well in the second paragraph, but the first paragraph is slightly too "policy-wonk" and not "econ-theory" enough. 
**The Pitch the paper should have:** "While trade literature focuses on the aggregate or regional welfare effects of protectionism, it often ignores how the benefits of trade rents are distributed across demographic groups. This paper shows that because of deep-seated racial sorting across industries, sector-specific trade shocks act as a de facto racial income policy. Using the 2018 Section 301 tariffs as a natural experiment, I demonstrate that 'compositional windfalls' can significantly alter racial wage gaps even in the absence of discriminatory intent or changes in relative labor demand."

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**The Contribution:** The paper identifies "compositional windfalls" as a distinct channel of racial redistribution in trade policy, specifically documenting the first evidence of trade-induced shifts in the Asian-White wage gap.

- **Differentiation:** It moves beyond the "China Shock" (Autor et al.) focus on the Black-White gap and the "losers" of trade, focusing instead on the "winners" of protectionism and a different demographic group (Asian workers).
- **Question:** It answers a question about the **WORLD**: How does industrial policy interact with demographic stratification? 
- **Clarity:** A smart economist would say: "It's a paper about how the specific industries Trump protected happen to be the ones where Asian workers have the highest seniority and density, leading to an accidental increase in their relative wages."
- **How to make it bigger:** The contribution would be massive if the author could link these "windfalls" to political economy—do these racial groups then change their voting behavior or support for trade protection? (Moving from labor outcomes to political equilibrium).

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

- **Closest Neighbors:** Amiti et al. (2019) and Fajgelbaum et al. (2020) on Tariff impacts; Autor, Dorn, & Hanson (2013) on trade/labor; Lang and Lehmann (2012) on racial gaps.
- **Positioning:** It builds on the "New Protectionism" literature but seeks to synthesize it with the "Racial Wage Gap" literature.
- **Missing Conversation:** The paper needs to speak more to the **Industrial Policy** literature. With the CHIPS Act and Inflation Reduction Act, "picking winners" is back. This paper should position itself as a warning that picking winners means picking *races* and *genders* based on who currently holds those jobs.
- **Unexpected Connection:** It could connect to the **Economic Geography** literature—are these gains staying in specific enclaves (e.g., Santa Clara County)?

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

- **Setup:** U.S. manufacturing is racially stratified; trade policy is sector-specific.
- **Tension:** Policy is framed as "America vs. China," but the internal "Who gets what?" is ignored.
- **Resolution:** Protectionism in high-tech manufacturing creates a windfall for Asian workers, widening the wage gap relative to Whites.
- **Implications:** "Race-neutral" policy is a myth in a stratified economy.
- **Evaluation:** The arc is strong but the "Resolution" is weakened by the author's own admission of messy pre-trends and COVID overlap. The story needs to be: "Even with these data constraints, the *arithmetic* of the windfall is an unavoidable consequence of the policy's design."

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

- **Lead Fact:** "Trump’s China tariffs gave the average Asian manufacturing worker a $150/month raise relative to their White colleagues."
- **Reaction:** Lean in. It’s a counter-intuitive "who benefits" story that cuts across typical political narratives.
- **Follow-up:** "Is this just a high-skill vs. low-skill story in disguise?" (The author needs to be very clear that this persists even when controlling for education/skill proxies).

---

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

- **Front-loading:** The QWI data description is a bit dry. Move the "Racial Composition in Manufacturing" (currently on p. 4) much earlier to establish the "Compositional Windfall" logic immediately.
- **Appendix:** Move the detailed List 1/List 2/List 3 tariff descriptions to an appendix.
- **Visuals:** The paper needs a killer "Figure 1" that shows the correlation between "Asian share of industry" and "Tariff rate" before any regression is run.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The gap between this and the AER is **Ambition vs. Identification**. The author is honest about messy pre-trends and 2-digit NAICS coarseness. At the AER level, "suggestive" isn't usually enough.

**The Single Most Impactful Advice:** Shift the focus from "I found a causal effect" to "I am documenting a structural reality." Enhance the paper by applying this "Compositional Windfall" logic to the **CHIPS Act**. If the author can show that current/future industrial subsidies will have a similar (or opposite) racial distribution, the paper moves from a "suggestive retrospective on 2018" to a "vital framework for 2024 policy."
