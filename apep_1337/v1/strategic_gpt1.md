## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether sector-specific trade protection can redistribute earnings across racial groups even when the policy is facially race-neutral. Using the 2018–2019 Section 301 tariffs and race-disaggregated QWI data, it argues that tariffs benefited Asian workers relative to White workers because Asian workers were disproportionately concentrated in the manufacturing sectors most protected from Chinese imports.

A busy economist should care because the paper is trying to make a broader point: industrial and trade policy may have important **demographic incidence** through preexisting worker sorting across sectors. That is potentially a useful and general idea, well beyond this tariff episode.

Does the paper articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs? **Partly, but not well enough.** The introduction currently starts with a provocative question about race and tariffs, which is good, but then too quickly slides into the particulars of Asian concentration in electronics and the DDD design. The paper’s real sell is not “here is a racial wage-gap paper on Section 301,” but rather “sectoral policy has demographic incidence through composition.” That broader idea should come first and more forcefully.

### The pitch the paper should have

“Economists have spent the last decade studying who gains and loses from trade shocks across places, firms, and skill groups. But sector-specific policies also redistribute across demographic groups when those groups are unevenly sorted across industries. This paper studies that channel in the context of the 2018–2019 Section 301 tariffs, asking whether protection of Chinese-import-competing industries widened the Asian–White earnings gap because Asian workers were disproportionately employed in the sectors most affected.

Using race-by-industry-by-state quarterly data from the QWI, I show that the Asian–White earnings gap grew more in more heavily protected sectors after the tariffs. The broader point is that trade and industrial policy can have substantial racial incidence even without explicitly targeting race, simply because the workforce is compositionally sorted across sectors.”

That is the AER-relevant pitch. The current draft is closer to “did tariffs help Asian workers?” The stronger pitch is “how do facially neutral sectoral policies map into demographic redistribution?”

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper’s contribution is to propose and empirically illustrate a **composition-incidence channel** whereby sector-targeted trade policy changes racial earnings gaps because racial groups are unevenly distributed across industries.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest papers?

**Not yet.** The draft distinguishes itself from papers on regional and aggregate effects of the Trump tariffs, and from racial wage-gap papers more broadly, but the differentiation is still too shallow. Right now the contribution can be heard as: “trade shock paper + race disaggregation.” That is not enough.

The paper needs to sharpen how it differs from at least four adjacent literatures:
1. the China shock / import competition literature,
2. the recent Trump tariff incidence literature,
3. racial wage-gap and occupational sorting work,
4. broader work on distributional incidence of place- or sector-based policy.

The new thing is not simply that the author runs a race-specific DDD. The new thing is the **incidence logic**: demographic sorting across sectors causes ostensibly neutral policy to redistribute across groups.

### Is it framed as a question about the world or about the literature?

It is **mostly framed as a question about the world**, which is good. “Which racial groups benefit from tariffs?” is better than “the Asian-White gap has been understudied.” But the introduction drifts into literature-gap language too often, especially around “this has received little attention.” The world question is stronger and should dominate.

### Could a smart economist explain what is new after reading the introduction?

At present: **only somewhat**. A smart economist would probably say, “It’s a DDD paper showing Section 301 may have widened the Asian-White wage gap because Asians are in protected manufacturing industries.” That is intelligible, but it still sounds like “another DiD paper about X.”

What they should say is: “It shows that the demographic incidence of sectoral policy can be predicted by group-specific industry sorting; the tariff episode is an illustration of that general equilibrium accounting insight.” That is more memorable and more portable.

### What would make the contribution bigger?

Most importantly, the paper needs to become less about a single racial comparison and more about a **general framework for demographic incidence of industrial policy**.

Specific ways to make it bigger:
- **Generalize beyond Asian–White.** Show a systematic mapping from pre-policy race-specific industry composition to subsequent relative earnings changes across multiple groups, not just one featured comparison plus one placebo.
- **Connect to policy incidence more broadly.** Frame Section 301 as one example of a wider class of sector-targeted policies: tariffs, subsidies, procurement, green industrial policy, CHIPS, etc.
- **Make the composition mechanism more central than the race gap itself.** The paper would be stronger if the key outcome were not only “Asian–White earnings gap” but “group-specific exposure predicted by preexisting sectoral sorting explains relative gains/losses.”
- **Show prediction, not just description.** If groups more concentrated in protected sectors gain more, can the paper rank predicted winners and losers ex ante? That would feel more structural and more broadly useful.
- **Potentially shift the comparison unit.** Instead of centering one bilateral racial gap, the more ambitious framing is group-by-policy exposure incidence across all race/ethnicity categories.

The current paper is a narrow application. The underlying idea could support a much bigger paper.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Before this paper, economists know that trade and tariff shocks have uneven effects across consumers, firms, and regions. They also know that racial groups are unevenly sorted across industries and occupations.

### Tension

What is missing is a clear account of how those two facts interact: if policy is targeted at sectors rather than people, can it still systematically redistribute across racial groups? And can we see that in a salient recent policy episode?

### Resolution

The paper claims yes: the Section 301 tariffs disproportionately protected sectors with relatively high Asian employment shares, and the Asian–White earnings gap widened more in those sectors after the tariffs.

### Implications

The implication is that trade and industrial policy have demographic incidence even absent explicit targeting, so economists and policymakers should think of sectoral policy as redistributive not only across places and skill groups but also across demographic groups.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

**Serviceable, but not strong.** The ingredients are there, but the draft still reads too much like a collection of regression results attached to a catchy phrase (“composition windfall”). The narrative loses steam because:
- it oscillates between a broad claim and a very narrow empirical implementation,
- it foregrounds one comparison without showing why that comparison is the best way to understand the broader mechanism,
- and the event-study/pre-COVID discussion makes the empirical pattern look delayed and muddied, which weakens the sense of narrative resolution.

### What story should it be telling?

The story should be:

1. **Sectoral policy is commonly analyzed by place and skill, but not enough by demographic composition.**
2. **Because racial groups sort unevenly across sectors, sector-targeted policy mechanically creates demographic winners and losers.**
3. **The Trump tariffs provide a clean, salient case where protected sectors were disproportionately Asian-intensive.**
4. **Consistent with that composition-incidence channel, relative earnings moved in favor of the group overrepresented in protected sectors.**
5. **Therefore, the incidence analysis of modern industrial policy should incorporate workforce composition explicitly.**

That is a coherent story. The current title and framing are too centered on the empirical fact and not enough on the conceptual mechanism.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?

I would lead with: **“Facially race-neutral tariffs appear to have widened racial earnings gaps because protected sectors were demographically sorted before the policy ever arrived.”**

That is the high-concept fact.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

**Some would lean in, but many would hesitate.** Why? Because the paper’s core empirical contrast sounds narrower than the concept. “Asian-White manufacturing wage gap under Section 301” is not intrinsically broad enough to command immediate top-journal attention. But “demographic incidence of industrial policy through sectoral sorting” absolutely could.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Likely:
- “Is this a general incidence result or just one quirky episode?”
- “How much of this is unique to Asian concentration in electronics?”
- “Can you predict winners and losers across many groups from pre-policy sorting?”
- “Does the same logic apply to subsidies, green policy, and procurement?”

Those are exactly the questions the paper should lean into.

### If the findings are modest: is that okay?

Yes, **if framed correctly.** The estimated magnitudes are not obviously blockbuster, and the paper itself candidly calls the evidence suggestive. That is fine if the paper is sold as documenting a **new margin of incidence** rather than discovering a giant reduced-form effect.

But if the paper is sold as a definitive causal claim about tariffs and the Asian-White wage gap, it will feel too modest and too fragile. If sold as an illustration of a broader incidence mechanism, the modesty is much less of a problem.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

At present, the gap to AER is mostly a combination of **framing, scope, and ambition**.

### What is the main problem?

**Primarily an ambition/framing problem.** The paper has a potentially interesting idea trapped inside a narrow empirical wrapper.

The current version reads like a careful short paper on one racial wage-gap consequence of one tariff episode. That is unlikely to clear the AER bar. The AER version would be a paper about **how to think about the demographic incidence of sector-targeted policy**, with the tariff episode serving as one compelling application.

### Is it also a scope problem?

Yes. The scope is currently too narrow:
- one bilateral racial comparison,
- one policy episode,
- one core outcome,
- and a mechanism asserted more than systematically demonstrated.

To feel top-field rather than competent, it likely needs either:
- a broader cross-group incidence exercise, or
- a more explicit and general conceptual framework that predicts who gains from sector-targeted policies using preexisting composition.

### Is it a novelty problem?

Somewhat. A skeptical reader may think: “Of course if a group is overrepresented in protected sectors, that group may gain relatively.” The paper therefore must not present the intuition as surprising by itself. The novelty must come from **formalizing and documenting this overlooked incidence margin in a salient policy context**, ideally in a way that generalizes.

### Is it an ambition problem?

Yes. The paper is competent but safe. It is too willing to be a small contribution to several literatures rather than a sharp contribution to one big question.

### Single most impactful advice

**Reframe the paper from “Section 301 and the Asian-White wage gap” to “the demographic incidence of sector-targeted policy,” and use the tariff episode as an application rather than the whole point.**

If the author changes only one thing, it should be that.
