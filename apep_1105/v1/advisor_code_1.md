**Summary**

This paper investigates whether the October 2014 rescheduling of hydrocodone combination products—arguably the largest single opioid supply shock in U.S. history—caused displaced opioid users to enter Medicaid-funded addiction treatment. Using cross-county variation in pre-rescheduling HCP share from ARCOS and linking it to MAT claims in T‑MSIS (2018–2024), the author estimates a reduced-form relationship between HCP exposure and downstream treatment demand. The preferred specification yields a positive but statistically insignificant estimate, with large confidence intervals that cannot reject either a meaningful treatment dividend or no effect.

**Essential Points**

1. **Identification requires a clearer operationalization of the shift-share instrument.** The paper refers throughout to a shift-share design, yet the main estimation is a cross-sectional regression of the outcome on the historical HCP share (Equation 1). There is no explicit “shift” (post-2014 indicator) in the estimation sample—the outcomes are averaged over 2018–2024 and the treatment share is pre-2014. This generates two problems: (a) the rescheduling shock does not enter the empirical specification, so the paper is effectively estimating the correlation between historical hydrocodone dependence and contemporary MAT utilization, which allows for many alternative interpretations; (b) without a first-stage or IV strategy, it is hard to assess whether the identifying assumptions of the uplift (i.e., that the shock acts through supply reduction proportional to HCP share) are satisfied. The authors should either implement a genuine shift-share estimator (with a post-2014 indicator or other temporal variation) or explicitly justify why the reduced-form cross-section suffices and reinterpret the design accordingly.

2. **Observable balance issues cast doubt on the exclusion restriction.** The balance table shows that HCP share is significantly correlated (even after state fixed effects) with poverty, racial composition, and population—variables that plausibly influence MAT demand through channels other than the rescheduling. If these observables mark underlying institutional or health-environmental differences, the exclusion restriction is likely violated unless one can argue that the controls capture all such confounders. Given the cross-sectional design, unobserved factors (e.g., treatment stigma, Medicaid generosity within states, or local supply constraints) correlated with both historical prescribing and current treatment demand could fully explain the estimated relationship. The paper needs a more convincing argument (e.g., through placebo outcomes or falsification) that HCP share is orthogonal to these omitted determinants or a modeling approach that accounts for residual confounding—possibly via spatial IV, matching, or exploiting more granular temporal variation.

3. **Power is severely limited, but this should be turned into a strength rather than a caveat.** The estimates are highly imprecise, the sign flips across robustness checks, and the jackknife exposes sensitivity to single states. While the author interprets this as a limitation of cross-sectional identification, it also weakens the substantive claim: the paper cannot reject a zero effect, but the confidence intervals are so wide that they also do not rule out large effects of opposite signs. The authors should more formally quantify the minimum detectable effect and clarify whether the empirical design is informative for policy. Without such exercises, the paper risks ending up as a non-finding. If the goal is to document the difficulty of identifying a treatment dividend, frame this explicitly and present power calculations or bounds that show what range of effects the data can realistically detect.

**Suggestions**

1. **Reinforce the causal design by introducing time variation.** Although T‑MSIS is only available from 2018 onward, one could still construct a quasi-experimental setup by comparing counties with different levels of HCP dependence and observing their MAT utilization before and after the rescheduling, using other data sources for the pre-2014 period (e.g., SAMHSA TEDS, state-level administrative data, or even facility-level MAT claims from Medicaid prior to 2014). If no pre-treatment outcome is available, consider using an event-study-style placebo (e.g., regressing MAT claims on pre-rescheduling HCP share for a falsified “treatment year”) to demonstrate that the relationship only emerges after 2014. Alternatively, if the data permit, treat the rescheduling as a national policy and use variation in county-level “treatment intensity” (HCP share) interacted with a time indicator that collapses to the post-2014 period. This would operationalize the shift in the shift-share instrument and allow for a first-stage estimate.

2. **Augment the set of controls or adopt flexible functional forms to mitigate confounding.** The balance test indicates that HCP share is correlated with demographic and socioeconomic characteristics. To mitigate this, the authors could (i) include richer controls—e.g., state-level opioid policy indicators, measures of treatment provider capacity, Medicaid expansion timing, and county-level health infrastructure; (ii) implement a matched-sample strategy (matching on propensity for high HCP share) to compare similar counties; or (iii) use fixed effects (e.g., hospital referral region or county clusters) if finer geography can be leveraged. Including nonlinear controls for HCP share (e.g., splines or quantiles) might also help capture heterogeneity that would otherwise bias the linear coefficient.

3. **Explore additional outcomes and mechanisms to deepen the story.** The decompositions into MAT modalities are informative, but the inconsistency across specifications suggests further exploration is warranted. For example, consider examining (a) the mix of MAT vs. total SUD claims to see if overall treatment shifts or if increases in MAT are offset by changes elsewhere, (b) other payer sources (if available) to see whether displaced users move outside Medicaid, or (c) downstream health events (e.g., overdose visits) as complementary evidence for substitution into illicit markets. If possible, evaluate whether high-HCP counties experienced differential provider entry or exit post-2014, which could signal supply-driven effects on treatment accessibility rather than demand. Even if none of these show significant effects, documenting patterns can help interpret the null result.

4. **Clarify the power limitations and consider a partial identification approach.** The paper acknowledges wide confidence intervals, but a more formal discussion (e.g., reporting MDEs, confidence interval length relative to outcome mean, or Bayesian credible intervals) would help readers interpret the null. If feasible, the authors could adopt a partial identification perspective: rather than estimating a point estimate, provide bounds on the treatment effect under plausible assumptions about substitution or omitted-variable bias. Such bounds could be framed in policy-relevant terms (e.g., “even if the true effect were at the top of the confidence interval, it would only move X percent of untreated OUD individuals”). This would make the paper’s contribution about the limits of cross-sectional inference, which is an important message in its own right.

5. **Improve the presentation of placebo tests and robustness checks.** The placebo on non-opioid SUD claims is a useful validation, but it could be strengthened by showing the same placebo for other unrelated outcomes (e.g., dental visits or non-opioid chronic care), demonstrating that HCP share does not predict these. Likewise, the robustness table could highlight whether standard errors decrease with population weighting or whether linearity assumptions hold by plotting residuals vs. fitted values. Including a figure that shows the distribution of HCP share and the nonparametric relationship with MAT (e.g., local polynomial regression) could also offer readers intuition about the underlying variation.

Overall, the paper tackles a timely and important question with rich administrative data. Addressing the identification, confounding, and precision issues outlined above—and clarifying the empirical strategy—would substantially strengthen the credibility and informativeness of the results.
