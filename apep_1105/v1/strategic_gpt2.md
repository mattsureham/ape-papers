## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a first-order policy question in the opioid crisis: when policymakers restrict legal opioid supply, do dependent users move into treatment or simply substitute toward illicit drugs? Using the 2014 federal rescheduling of hydrocodone—the dominant prescription opioid at the time—the paper studies whether places more exposed to that supply shock saw more Medicaid-funded medication-assisted treatment.

A busy economist should care because this is exactly the margin on which a huge class of supply-side policies implicitly relies. If supply restrictions do not generate treatment entry, then the welfare case for those policies looks very different.

**Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Mostly, but not sharply enough. The opening is promising, but the paper then quickly slips into event description and design language before fully establishing the big economic question. It also overclaims “the largest single opioid supply shock in U.S. history” and “first causal evidence” before the reader is fully convinced why this is the central missing piece in the broader policy debate. The current intro feels like a paper explaining its design rather than a paper forcing the reader to confront a consequential policy margin.

**What the first two paragraphs should say instead:**  
> Governments routinely restrict access to addictive goods in the hope that some users will quit or seek treatment rather than switch to more dangerous substitutes. In the opioid crisis, that logic has justified a decade of supply-side interventions—from prescribing rules to scheduling changes—but we still know remarkably little about whether cutting prescription opioid access actually moves dependent users into treatment. That missing margin matters for welfare, for optimal policy design, and for how economists think about addiction under constrained legal supply.  
>  
> This paper studies that question using the 2014 federal rescheduling of hydrocodone, which abruptly tightened access to the single most important prescription opioid in the United States. I ask whether counties that relied more heavily on hydrocodone before the reform later experienced greater Medicaid use of medication-assisted treatment. The headline result is not a clean treatment boom: the estimates are positive but too imprecise to support the strong claim that supply restrictions reliably push users into treatment. That finding reframes how we should think about the benefits of opioid supply policy.

That is the pitch the paper should have: **the missing treatment margin in the economics of supply restriction**, not “here is my shift-share design.”

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:**  
The paper examines whether a major prescription-opioid supply shock translated into greater publicly funded addiction treatment use, thereby testing the often-assumed but rarely measured “treatment dividend” from supply-side opioid policy.

Right now, a smart reader could still summarize it as: “another paper on opioid supply restrictions, but with Medicaid treatment claims instead of prescribing or mortality.” That is not enough.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or filling a gap in a LITERATURE?
It is mixed, but still too literature-gap coded. The stronger framing is clearly about the world: **when access to addictive goods is restricted, where do users go?** The paper should lean harder into that. “No one has causally examined this” is weaker than “this is a central policy margin that determines whether supply-side interventions help or merely displace harm.”

### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what's new?
They could, but not crisply. At present, they might say: “It studies whether hydrocodone rescheduling increased Medicaid MAT in more exposed counties, but the results are noisy.” That is descriptively accurate but not intellectually memorable.

You want them to say: **“It tests whether opioid crackdowns create treatment entry—the key missing margin in the supply-side policy debate—and finds little evidence of a reliable treatment dividend.”**

### What would make this contribution bigger?
Several possibilities:

1. **A broader outcome concept.**  
   Medicaid MAT claims are narrow. If the paper could speak to total treatment entry, overdose, criminal justice contact, or illicit substitution jointly, the contribution would become much larger. Right now it captures one payer and one treatment modality universe.

2. **A stronger contrast between treatment and substitution.**  
   The big question is not simply “did MAT rise?” It is “when legal supply falls, is the adjustment margin treatment or illicit substitution?” A paper that could explicitly compare those margins would be much more AER-shaped.

3. **A more general framing beyond opioids.**  
   The paper could position itself as evidence on a broader economic question: how consumers with addiction respond when legal access becomes costlier. That moves it from “opioid policy paper” to “economics of addiction and regulation paper.”

4. **A richer mechanism around treatment capacity.**  
   The buprenorphine hint is potentially the most interesting thing in the paper. If the treatment response exists only where office-based prescribing capacity is available, then the paper becomes about complementarity between restriction and treatment infrastructure. That is a much bigger idea than a simple average effect.

If the author wants to stay with current data, the best upgrade is **framing the paper around the missing transition margin from legal supply to treatment, and making treatment capacity the conceptual mechanism that explains why the response may be weak or heterogeneous.**

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Policymakers have aggressively restricted prescription opioid supply. Those policies are often justified not only by reducing misuse directly, but also by the hope that dependent users will enter treatment.

### Tension
We know a fair amount about prescribing declines and some substitution to heroin/fentanyl, but much less about whether treatment entry offsets those harms. The central puzzle is whether supply-side restrictions create a “treatment dividend” or simply shift users elsewhere.

### Resolution
The paper looks at hydrocodone rescheduling and finds no statistically decisive evidence of a broad increase in Medicaid MAT utilization; the point estimates are positive, with some suggestive indication for buprenorphine, but the main takeaway is that a large, reliable treatment response does not emerge clearly.

### Implications
Economists and policymakers should be more cautious in assuming that restricting prescription access automatically channels dependent users into treatment. If treatment entry is weak, any benefits of supply restrictions depend more heavily on deterrence, prevention, or substitution patterns—and may require complementary treatment capacity investments.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
**Yes in embryo, but it is undercut by the paper’s own execution.** The core story is there. The problem is that the manuscript keeps drifting from narrative to specification talk. It explains the design, then caveats the design, then reports imprecision, then discusses instability. The result is less “here is a consequential answer to a major question” and more “here is an earnest attempt that ran into power/measurement limits.”

That is dangerous editorially. Papers can absolutely succeed with modest or imprecise findings if the question is major and the lesson is sharp. But then the story must be disciplined: **the paper is about ruling out the strong assumption that supply restrictions reliably produce treatment entry**. That is a real insight. The current draft instead reads like it wants credit for asking the question, even though it cannot answer it cleanly.

**What story should it be telling?**  
Not “we estimated a noisy reduced form.”  
It should be: **the missing benefit side of opioid supply policy is treatment entry, and in the largest prescription opioid shock we do not see convincing evidence of a robust treatment-on-ramp. That suggests supply restrictions are not self-executing; treatment capacity is a necessary complement.**

That is a story.

But the author has to make that case much more confidently and cleanly.
