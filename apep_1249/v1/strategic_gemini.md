## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
This paper exploits a unique "on-off" natural experiment—the implementation and subsequent repeal of Australia’s carbon tax (2012–2014)—to estimate the employment effects of carbon pricing in the world’s most coal-dependent electricity sector. Using a continuous-treatment DiD based on state-level coal intensity, the author finds a precisely estimated null effect, suggesting that even in the "eye of the storm," carbon pricing does not necessarily lead to the job losses predicted by political rhetoric or CGE models. 

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this well, though the first two paragraphs could be punchier.
**Suggested Pitch:** "Does carbon pricing destroy jobs in the sectors it hits hardest? I exploit the unique implementation and total repeal of Australia’s carbon tax to provide the first quasi-experimental evidence from the world’s most coal-intensive electricity grid. I find that despite the 'on-off' nature of the tax, employment remained resilient, suggesting that price pass-through and labor rigidities can decouple carbon costs from workforce size."

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**Contribution:** The paper provides the first symmetric "on-off" empirical test of carbon pricing's impact on sectoral employment, ruling out large-scale job destruction in a high-stakes, coal-dependent environment.

**Evaluation:** 
- **Differentiation:** It is well-differentiated from Yamazaki (2017) and Metcalf & Stock (2023) by focusing on the *reversal* of the policy and the specific *sectoral* intensity.
- **Framing:** It is framed as answering a question about the WORLD (do carbon taxes kill jobs?) rather than just a literature gap.
- **Clarity:** A smart economist would say: "It uses the Australian flip-flop to show that even coal miners/power plant workers didn't lose jobs when the tax hit."
- **Making it bigger:** To make the contribution "AER-sized," the paper needs to move beyond the null and more deeply explore the *reallocation* of labor. If total sector employment didn't change, did the *composition*? Did workers shift from coal to gas/renewables within the same state?

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
- **Neighbors:** Greenstone (2002), Walker (2013), Yamazaki (2017), Metcalf & Stock (2023).
- **Positioning:** It should position itself as the "Symmetric Falsification" paper. Most literature looks at the *start* of a policy; this paper's strength is that the "bounce back" (or lack thereof) provides a rare second test of the same mechanism.
- **Unexpected connection:** It could benefit from connecting to the literature on **policy uncertainty**. The fact that the tax was "politically contested from inception" is a feature, not a bug. It speaks to why firms might not fire workers if they expect a reversal—a major contribution to the political economy of climate policy.

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC
- **Setup:** Political narratives claim carbon taxes are "job killers."
- **Tension:** Australia provided the perfect laboratory—extreme coal dependence and a radical policy reversal—yet we don't know what actually happened to the workers.
- **Resolution:** A "Missing Cliff." No jobs lost when the tax started; no jobs gained when it was repealed.
- **Implications:** The "job killer" narrative is empirically unsupported in the short run; the real action is in price pass-through and emissions, not layoffs.

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong. It effectively sets up a "political myth vs. empirical reality" story.

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
- **The Fact:** "Australia taxed the most coal-heavy grid in the world, then repealed it two years later, and the number of power plant workers didn't move an inch."
- **Reaction:** People will lean in. It's counter-intuitive to the "common sense" of the energy industry.
- **Follow-up:** "Wait, so who paid for it?" (The paper answers: consumers, through a 10% retail price hike).

---

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
- **Front-load the Symmetry:** Move the "Post-Repeal" results to be side-by-side with the "Tax Period" results in every chart. The symmetry is the "AER-level" novelty.
- **Mechanism Section:** Section 5.2 is currently a bit speculative. If possible, bring in more data on the 10% price rise to prove the pass-through channel more robustly.
- **Appendix to Main:** If there is any data on worker *churn* or *entry/exit* of firms, that should be moved from the "Appendix" mentality to the "Main Result" to address the "reallocation" question.

---

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
**The Gap:** Currently, it's a very high-quality *Applied Economics* or *JPubE* paper. To hit the AER, it needs to bridge the gap between "this specific Australian event" and "a generalizable theory of labor resilience under environmental shocks."

**Single Biggest Improvement:** Deeper exploration of the **"Option Value of Waiting"** and **"Policy Reversibility."** If the author can show that the *threat* of repeal (political uncertainty) is what stabilized employment, it transforms from a "null result" paper into a major statement on how political economy affects the efficacy and labor-market consequences of climate policy.
