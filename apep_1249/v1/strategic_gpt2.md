## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a politically important question: when Australia imposed—and then repealed—a carbon tax in one of the most coal-dependent electricity systems in the developed world, did electricity-sector jobs fall? Using cross-state variation in coal dependence, it argues that the answer is no: even in the sector most exposed to carbon pricing, there is no visible “jobs cliff” when the tax arrives or rebound when it is removed.

Why should a busy economist care? Because the jobs argument is one of the central political obstacles to carbon pricing, and Australia offers an unusually salient policy episode: a major carbon price that turned on and then off. If even this episode does not generate large employment losses in the most exposed sector, that is potentially useful evidence for climate policy, political economy, and labor-market incidence.

**Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Mostly, but not optimally. The current introduction gets to the question quickly and is readable, but it is still framed a bit too much as “estimating the effect of Australia’s carbon tax” rather than as answering a broader and sharper world question: **do carbon prices actually destroy jobs where they should bite hardest?** It also spends too much early real estate on institutional specifics before fully crystallizing the headline insight.

**What the first two paragraphs should say instead:**

> Carbon pricing is routinely attacked as a job killer, yet credible causal evidence on sectoral employment effects remains surprisingly limited. Australia provides a rare test of this claim: it imposed a substantial carbon price on an unusually coal-intensive electricity system in 2012 and then repealed it in 2014. If carbon pricing causes sharp employment losses anywhere, one would expect to see them in this setting.
>
> This paper studies whether electricity-sector employment fell more in Australia’s coal-intensive states when the carbon tax was in force, and whether those jobs returned when the tax was repealed. Exploiting large cross-state differences in baseline coal dependence, I find no detectable employment decline during the tax period and no rebound after repeal. The core message is simple: in the sector most directly exposed to the policy, the feared employment cliff is missing.

That is the pitch. Cleaner, more general-interest, and more world-facing.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution in one sentence:**  
The paper’s contribution is to show, in Australia’s uniquely reversible carbon-pricing episode, that electricity-sector employment did not fall disproportionately in more coal-exposed states during the tax, nor recover after repeal.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?
Somewhat, but not sharply enough.

The paper says it differs because Australia had a policy repeal, which creates an “on-off” natural experiment. That is a real distinction. But right now the contribution still risks sounding like: *another paper finding small employment effects of environmental policy in one setting*. The novelty is not yet fully converted into a larger conceptual contribution.

What it should say more forcefully is something like:

- Existing work studies **permanent or expanding** carbon pricing or environmental regulation.
- This paper studies a setting with **both introduction and repeal**, allowing a more demanding symmetry test.
- The absence of both a decline on introduction and a rebound on repeal is more informative than just a single null around adoption.

That is the differentiator. Right now it is present, but not used to maximum advantage.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or as filling a gap in a LITERATURE?
It is in between. The opening is world-facing (“carbon pricing destroys jobs”), which is good. But parts of the intro drift into “there is little quasi-experimental evidence in this literature,” which is weaker.

This should be framed as a question about the world:

- When carbon prices hit a highly exposed, politically salient sector, how do firms actually adjust?
- Are employment losses the main margin of incidence, or are costs instead absorbed through prices, margins, output mix, and timing?

That is much stronger than “the literature lacks quasi-experimental evidence for Australia.”

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the intro?
They could, but not crisply enough. Right now the likely summary is:

> “It’s a DiD paper on Australia’s carbon tax and electricity employment, and it finds a null.”

That is not enough for AER-level memorability.

What you want them to say is:

> “It uses Australia’s unusual tax-and-repeal episode to test the core political claim that carbon pricing kills jobs in the most exposed sector—and finds no drop on the way in and no bounce on the way out.”

That is much better.

### What would make this contribution bigger?
Several possibilities:

1. **Better outcome framing:**  
   Employment alone is narrow. The paper already hints that adjustment may have occurred through prices, dispatch, or emissions rather than headcount. If the paper could foreground one of those margins—especially output, generation mix, prices, or plant-level activity—it would feel larger. Right now the reader is left with “jobs didn’t move, maybe because prices passed through.” That is plausible, but not big enough on its own.

2. **Sharper mechanism evidence:**  
   The mechanism section reads as informed speculation. If the paper had direct evidence on pass-through, generation, closures, workforce composition, or timing of adjustment, the story would become more than a null employment paper.

3. **Worker reallocation angle:**  
   A bigger paper would ask not only whether total sector employment fell, but whether workers were displaced, reallocated, or protected. The current outcome is at a very aggregated industry-state level. For AER, a stronger contribution would be along the lines of “no sectoral employment losses, but meaningful within-sector reallocation” or “no net job loss because costs passed through and incumbent labor was hoarded.”

4. **Political economy framing:**  
   The paper could be bigger if framed as evidence on why the politics of carbon pricing are disconnected from realized labor-market effects. The contribution would then speak to policy design and democratic constraints, not just labor demand.

If the author can only enlarge one dimension, I would choose **adding direct evidence on the actual adjustment margin**.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

### Closest neighbors
The paper itself points to the right neighborhood, though not always the right hierarchy. Closest neighbors likely include:

- **Yamazaki (2017)** on British Columbia’s carbon tax and employment
- **Azevedo et al. (2023)** on firm-level effects of carbon pricing / BC-type evidence
- **Metcalf and coauthors / cross-country carbon pricing-employment work** (the exact cited paper may or may not be canonical, but this is the comparative-policy literature it wants)
- **Walker (2013)** on transitional costs of environmental regulation
- **Greenstone (2002)** on environmental regulation and polluting-sector employment
- Potentially **Martin, de Preux, and Wagner (2014/2016)** on the UK Climate Change Levy and firm adjustment
- More broadly, the environmental-regulation incidence literature around the **Porter hypothesis / pollution haven / labor demand impacts**

### How should the paper position itself relative to those neighbors?
**Build on them, don’t attack them.** The right move is:

- Walker/Greenstone: “Environmental regulation can create substantial worker-level or industry-level adjustment costs in some settings.”
- Yamazaki/BC: “Carbon taxes need not reduce net employment.”
- This paper: “In the most politically salient sectoral setting, with introduction and repeal, large employment changes still fail to appear.”

The paper should not imply prior work is inadequate; rather, it should say it adds a uniquely informative policy episode and a sharper test of the political claim.

### Is the paper positioned too narrowly or too broadly?
Currently, **slightly too narrowly in design and too broadly in rhetoric**.

- Too narrowly in design because it is ultimately a state-by-quarter paper on one aggregated sector.
- Too broadly in rhetoric because phrases like “the job-killing narrative finds no empirical support” outrun what the evidence can comfortably sustain.

The sweet spot is: this is evidence about **the employment margin of incidence in the directly exposed electricity sector during a temporary but salient carbon pricing episode**. That’s narrower than the rhetoric but still interesting.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?
A few conversations are underdeveloped:

1. **Incidence and pass-through literature**  
   If the story is that prices absorbed the cost, the paper should be in direct dialogue with tax incidence and regulated-market pass-through work, not just employment papers.

2. **Energy transition / labor reallocation literature**  
   There is now a growing literature on labor adjustment to decarbonization, coal decline, plant closures, and clean-energy transitions. This paper should speak to that literature more directly.

3. **Political economy of climate policy**  
   The motivating question is fundamentally political: why are job-loss claims so potent? The paper could benefit from citing work on political resistance to carbon pricing, distributional conflict, and the salience of concentrated losses.

4. **Short-run vs. long-run adjustment**  
   The paper’s own interpretation hinges on temporariness. It should connect to literature on dynamic labor adjustment, option value, labor hoarding, and irreversible investment under policy uncertainty.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?
Not quite. Right now it is mostly having the conversation: *“What is the employment effect of carbon pricing?”* That is a crowded and somewhat incremental conversation.

A more impactful conversation would be:

- **What margins actually absorb climate policy in highly exposed sectors?**
- **Why do job-loss narratives dominate politics when realized employment effects are small?**
- **Does temporary carbon pricing fail to move labor even when it moves prices and emissions?**

That is the right conversation. It moves the paper from “another policy evaluation” toward “evidence on the anatomy of adjustment to climate policy.”

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
The world before this paper: carbon pricing is politically framed as a major employment threat, especially in fossil-heavy electricity systems. Existing evidence is mixed, often aggregate, and not centered on the most exposed sector in a policy setting that turns on and off.

### Tension
Australia should be the place where employment effects are visible: highly coal-dependent electricity, a nontrivial carbon price, enormous political salience, and a subsequent repeal. Yet it is unclear whether the sector actually adjusted through jobs, prices, output mix, or something else.

### Resolution
The paper finds no detectable employment decline in more coal-exposed states during the tax and no rebound after repeal.

### Implications
The employment margin may be much less important than political debate suggests—at least over short to medium horizons in regulated utility settings. Carbon pricing may work through prices, dispatch, and emissions rather than headcount reductions.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
**Yes, but only in embryo.** It has the ingredients of a narrative, but too often it reads like a competent empirical paper organized around design and robustness rather than a paper with a strong conceptual story.

The best story available is not “here is a null effect.” It is:

> Australia provides a hard test of the claim that carbon pricing kills jobs. In the sector where the effect should be strongest, employment does not fall when the tax starts and does not rise when it ends. This implies that short-run adjustment to carbon pricing occurs on margins other than employment, helping explain why the politics of job loss can be so disconnected from realized labor-market outcomes.

That should be the narrative throughout.

Right now, some sections dilute the story. The paper spends lots of words on routine econometric validation and less on the conceptual payoff of the symmetry test and the alternative margins of adjustment.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?
“Australia taxed carbon in one of the world’s most coal-dependent electricity systems, then repealed the tax—and electricity jobs didn’t fall when the tax came in or rise when it went out.”

That is the most compelling single fact.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
**Moderately lean in.** The policy episode is inherently interesting. The on-off feature is memorable. But the excitement would fade quickly unless the presenter can answer the obvious next question:

### What follow-up question would they ask?
“Okay, so if jobs didn’t move, what did?”

That is the paper’s central strategic problem.

A null in employment can be interesting—but only if it teaches us something positive about adjustment margins or policy incidence. Otherwise, it risks feeling like a failed attempt to find an effect. The paper tries to answer this with pass-through, rigidities, and inframarginal adjustment, but mostly by assertion rather than demonstration.

### Is the null result itself interesting?
Yes, conditionally. It is interesting because:

- the case is politically salient,
- the exposed sector is exactly where one would expect job loss,
- and repeal gives a symmetry test.

But the paper has to make that case harder and cleaner. It must say: **this is not an ordinary null; it is a failed prediction in a setting tailored to the prediction.** That is what gives the null value.

At present, it partly succeeds but not fully. The result is interesting enough for a good field journal or a strong general-interest second tier. For AER, the null needs a bigger conceptual payload.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

Bluntly: in its current form, this is **not yet an AER paper**. The problem is not mainly technical polish. It is that the paper is **smaller than it thinks it is**.

### What is the main gap?

**Primarily a scope-and-ambition problem, with some framing weakness.**

- **Not mainly a framing problem:** the core framing is reasonable.
- **Not mainly a novelty problem:** the repeal setting is genuinely unusual.
- **Mostly a scope/ambition problem:** the paper shows that one aggregate outcome did not move in one sector, and then infers likely mechanisms without directly showing them.

For AER, the top people in the field would want more than “employment didn’t change.” They would want the anatomy of adjustment:
- Did output fall?
- Did generation shift across fuels?
- Did prices absorb the burden?
- Were workers displaced within states or across plants?
- Was there labor hoarding because the policy lacked credibility?
- Why did the politics focus on jobs despite no realized job losses?

Any one of those, done well, could turn this into a much bigger paper.

### Is it a novelty problem?
Partly. The design has novelty, but the **substantive finding**—small or null employment effects of carbon pricing/environmental regulation—is no longer novel enough by itself. The repeal is helpful, but not sufficient.

### Is it an ambition problem?
Yes. The paper currently settles for the most available outcome and the safest interpretation. A top journal paper would use the Australian episode to answer a bigger question about climate-policy incidence and adjustment.

### Single most impactful advice
**Turn the paper from “a null effect of carbon pricing on jobs” into “an anatomy of how a politically explosive carbon tax was absorbed without large job losses,” using direct evidence on the alternative adjustment margin.**

If they can only change one thing, that is the thing.
