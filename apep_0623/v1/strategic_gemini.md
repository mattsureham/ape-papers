## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper uses a rare "policy round-trip"—the 2017 imposition of the SALT deduction cap and its 2025 reversal—to test a foundational assumption in public finance: that tax capitalization is symmetric. It finds a sharp price decline following the tax increase but nearly zero recovery following the tax cut, suggesting that tax shocks can trigger permanent shifts in household sorting that do not unwind when the policy is reversed. This matters because it challenges standard present-discounted-value models of asset pricing and suggests that temporary tax policies can have permanent welfare and distributional consequences.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this pitch exceptionally well. The first two paragraphs clearly state the "ideal natural experiment" and the "stark answer." It successfully frames the paper as a test of a theoretical "symmetry prediction" rather than just a measurement of a specific policy. No rewrite is necessary.

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution:** The paper provides the first large-scale empirical evidence that housing market capitalization of tax shocks is sharply asymmetric, failing to reverse when the tax burden is restored to original levels.

**Evaluation:**
- **Differentiation:** It is well-differentiated. While Kuminoff & Pope (2020) and others have looked at the TCJA's *imposition*, this is the first to exploit the *reversal* (the OBBB).
- **World vs. Literature:** It is framed as a question about the world (how do markets react to reversals?) and a fundamental theoretical property (symmetry).
- **Clarity:** A smart economist would immediately understand the "ratchet effect" story. It is not "just another DiD paper"; it is a "symmetry test" paper.
- **Making it bigger:** To make the contribution truly "Top 5," the paper needs more granular evidence on the *mechanism* of the hysteresis. Identifying who moved (using IRS migration data or similar) would move this from a price-discovery paper to a deep structural sorting paper.

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

The paper sits at the intersection of local public finance (Tiebout/Oates) and behavioral/frictional asset pricing.

- **Neighbors:** Oates (1969) on capitalization; Gyourko & Sinai (2003) on SALT; Cellini et al. (2010) on school finance asymmetry; and the recent TCJA literature (Kuminoff & Pope; Zoeckler & Sommer).
- **Positioning:** It builds on the TCJA literature but effectively "attacks" the implicit assumption in Oates/Tiebout that $V = \sum \frac{Benefit - Tax}{(1+r)^t}$ is a reversible equation.
- **Conversation:** The paper is having the right conversation but could bridge more into the **behavioral economics** of "downward nominal rigidity" or **economic geography** (agglomeration/path dependence). If the sorting is permanent, it’s an urban economics story about multiple equilibria.

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

- **Setup:** Classical theory predicts tax changes capitalize into home prices symmetrically.
- **Tension:** Tax shocks are rarely reversed, so we’ve never been able to see if the "up" and "down" match.
- **Resolution:** Using the 2018–2025 window, we see prices fall as expected but fail to rise upon reversal.
- **Implications:** Policy volatility creates permanent wealth transfers; the "Tiebout sorting" is sticky/path-dependent.

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong. It follows a "mystery" structure: why didn't the prices come back? The interpretation of "household sorting with hysteresis" (Section 7.2) provides a satisfying resolution to the empirical puzzle.

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

**The Fact:** "When the government raised taxes on these homes, prices fell 10%. When the government cut the taxes back to where they started, the prices stayed down."

**Reaction:** This is a "lean in" finding. Economists love a rejection of a simple symmetry/efficiency assumption. The immediate follow-up would be: "Is it just because people don't believe the new law will last?" (The paper addresses this in 7.3, but this is the primary hurdle for AER-level significance).

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The gap is currently **Mechanism vs. Measurement**. The measurement of the asymmetry is clean and professional. However, for the AER, the board will want more than just the fact of the asymmetry; they want to know *why* it happened.

**Single Biggest Improvement:** Use IRS migration data or Census move data to provide empirical evidence for the "Sorting Hysteresis" claim—show that the high-income itemizers who left never came back, or that the demographic "itemization potential" of these zip codes shifted permanently.
