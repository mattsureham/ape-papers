## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper uses the unique "tariff-free" environment of the post-Brexit Trade and Cooperation Agreement (TCA) to isolate the trade-reducing effects of Rules of Origin (ROO). By comparing UK-EU trade across products with varying ROO stringency against a non-EU control group, it finds a "compliance asymmetry": restrictive rules significantly depress UK exports to the EU (where compliance is mandatory for zero-tariff access), but have no effect on UK imports from the EU (where importers choose to pay the modest 4.3% MFN tariff rather than deal with the paperwork).

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this pitch excellently. The first paragraph sets a vivid scene ("nothing changed on paper, yet everything changed in practice"), and the second paragraph clearly states the question and the identification advantage. I would not change it.

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution:** The paper provides the first causal identification of the asymmetric trade-reducing effects of Rules of Origin by exploiting a setting where tariffs remain at zero while ROO vary across products.

**Evaluation:**
- **Differentiation:** It is well-differentiated from Conconi et al. (2018) and Estevadeordal (2000). While those papers established that ROO matter, they often struggled with the ROO-tariff bundle. This paper isolates the "compliance" component.
- **Question vs. Literature:** It frames itself as answering a question about the *world* (why did Brexit trade collapse despite zero tariffs?), which is strong. 
- **Clarity:** A smart economist would immediately grasp the "Compliance Asymmetry" takeaway. It’s more than "another DiD"; it's a paper about the *incidence* of non-tariff barriers.
- **Bigger Contribution:** To make it even bigger, the author could explore the "firm-level" margin. Are small firms the ones exiting (the "compliance tax" as a fixed cost)? If the author can’t get firm data, adding a section on the *welfare* implications of this asymmetry for a small open economy would elevate the "So What?" factor.

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

- **Neighbors:** Conconi et al. (2018, AER), Handley and Limão (2017, AER), Dhingra et al. (2022, RES), and the foundational Estevadeordal (2000, JWE).
- **Positioning:** It currently "builds on" the ROO literature and "decomposes" the Brexit literature. It should probably "attack" or at least "challenge" the standard symmetric modeling of trade costs in CGE models (like those used to predict Brexit's impact), which rarely account for this one-sided compliance burden.
- **Niche vs. Broad:** It’s currently in a healthy middle. To go broader, it should speak more to the **Global Value Chain (GVC)** literature. ROO are essentially a tax on GVCs; the "Compliance Tax" is a friction that forces regionalization.

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

- **Setup:** The TCA is signed; politicians celebrate "zero tariffs."
- **Tension:** Despite zero tariffs, trade volumes tank. Is it just "teething problems" (customs) or a structural shift (ROO)?
- **Resolution:** It’s structural and one-sided. Exporters are trapped by ROO; importers have a "tariff escape hatch."
- **Implications:** Trade agreements shouldn't just focus on "zero tariffs"; the administrative architecture (ROO) is the real barrier for the smaller partner.

**Evaluation:** The narrative arc is very strong. It has a "detective story" feel—investigating the mystery of the missing trade.

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

**The Fact:** "In the UK's new trade deal with Europe, the rules of origin are a 15% tax on exports, but zero on imports because it's cheaper for Brits to just pay the tariff than to prove the sheep is British."

**Reaction:** Lean in. It’s a counter-intuitive finding that explains a major geopolitical event (Brexit's economic failure) through a micro-economic mechanism.

---

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

- **Front-loading:** The paper is well front-loaded. Table 2 is the "money" table and it appears early.
- **Heterogeneity:** Section 5.2 (Table 3) is a bit messy. The author admits the import-side null masks offsetting effects. This needs a cleaner reconciliation. If instruments and minerals show *positive* effects (trade diversion), the paper needs to explain *why* ROO would cause diversion rather than just being a friction.
- **Appendix:** The coding of the ROO-RI (the index) is crucial. While it's in the appendix, a small "Examples of ROO stringency" table in the main text would help the non-trade-specialist reader.

---

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The paper is highly competent and well-identified, but it feels a bit "safe." It's a very good empirical "check" on an existing theory. 

**The Gap:** To be a "slam dunk" AER, it needs to move from "measuring an effect" to "explaining a phenomenon of trade architecture." 

**Single most impactful advice:** Connect the results to the **size of the trading partners**. The paper hints at this in the conclusion (simplification matters for the "smaller partner"). If the author can frame this as a general theory of "Asymmetric Compliance Power" in trade between large and small blocs, it becomes a seminal paper on trade negotiations, not just a paper about Brexit.
