## 1. Idea Fidelity

The paper clearly pursues the core question in the manifest: whether product-specific Brexit rules of origin (ROO) under the TCA generated trade disintegration, using cross-product variation in ROO stringency and a triple-difference design. The broad structure is faithful to the original idea: post/pre, EU/non-EU, and continuous ROO restrictiveness.

That said, the paper departs from several key elements of the original design in ways that materially weaken the contribution. First, the manifest emphasized high-frequency HMRC uktradeinfo data, ideally at HS-8/monthly, supplemented by newly available HMRC preference-utilization data. The paper instead uses annual UN Comtrade data at HS-4 and does not use the preference-utilization data at all. This is a substantial downgrade in both measurement precision and ability to test the proposed mechanism. Second, the manifest proposed coding ROO at a more granular product level (roughly HS-6 / heading-subheading variation from Annex ORIG-2), whereas the paper codes ROO at the HS-2 chapter level using a “representative rule,” which risks severe misclassification and mechanical attenuation or bias. Third, the paper’s “natural experiment” framing in the manifest relied on rich within-product variation under uniform tariffs; the implemented design uses much coarser variation and therefore does less to isolate ROO from broader sector-level Brexit shocks.

So the paper is directionally faithful to the idea, but it misses the most important data and granularity choices that made the original idea potentially compelling.

## 2. Summary

This paper studies whether the product-specific stringency of rules of origin in the EU-UK Trade and Cooperation Agreement reduced bilateral trade after Brexit. Using a DDD design with UK-EU versus UK-non-EU trade, pre- versus post-2021, and variation in a ROO restrictiveness index, it finds a significant negative effect of ROO stringency on UK exports to the EU but no comparable import-side effect, which the authors interpret as a “compliance asymmetry.”

The topic is important and potentially publishable in a short-format journal, but in its current form the paper does not yet convincingly establish that the estimated export effect is causally attributable to ROO rather than broader sector-level Brexit disruption or measurement choices.

## 3. Essential Points

1. **The treatment is measured too coarsely to support the paper’s causal claims.**  
   ROO are coded at the HS-2 chapter level using a “most representative rule,” while outcomes are at HS-4 and the institutional variation in the TCA exists at much finer product levels. This creates large scope for misclassification within chapters and makes it hard to interpret the DDD coefficient as the effect of actual product-level ROO stringency. At minimum, the paper needs to recode ROO at the finest level feasible from Annex ORIG-2 (ideally HS-6 / heading-subheading) and then aggregate carefully to the trade data. Without this, the central empirical object is too noisy and too detached from the policy variation.

2. **The control group and identification argument are not yet persuasive enough.**  
   The paper assumes that UK trade with five non-EU partners provides the correct counterfactual for how UK-EU trade would have evolved absent the TCA, and that other post-Brexit frictions are absorbed by fixed effects because they do not vary with ROO. Both assumptions are quite strong. Products with stricter ROO are concentrated in sectors that also faced especially severe non-ROO Brexit frictions (e.g., SPS checks, border delays, supply-chain disruption, regulatory compliance), so the exclusion restriction is not established by stating that ROO were politically negotiated. The paper needs much stronger evidence on pre-trends, compositional comparability, and differential exposure to other frictions across high- and low-ROO products.

3. **The mechanism behind the export/import asymmetry is asserted rather than demonstrated.**  
   The headline interpretation depends on exporters being constrained by origin compliance while importers can choose to pay MFN tariffs instead. But the paper does not observe preference utilization, MFN use, or tariff-payment behavior, despite the manifest highlighting newly available HMRC preference-utilization data. As written, the asymmetry is an interesting pattern but not a demonstrated mechanism. Incorporating preference-utilization evidence is essential if the paper wants to claim it has identified a “compliance tax” rather than just an asymmetric reduced-form trade response.

## 4. Suggestions

The paper has a promising intuition and an appealing research question. I think the most productive path is to tighten the empirical design around the institutional variation that makes the setting interesting, rather than over-interpret the current estimates.

First, I strongly encourage the authors to rebuild the data around **HMRC monthly product-level trade data**, which was central to the original idea and is much better suited to an AER: Insights-style design. The current annual HS-4 Comtrade panel is too coarse for a policy that changed sharply on January 1, 2021 and whose rules vary within broad sectors. Monthly data would allow event-study graphs, immediate-versus-persistent effects, better handling of 2021 adjustment dynamics, and more convincing pre-trend tests. It would also make it easier to separate transitional disruption from longer-run trade disintegration. Using HMRC rather than Comtrade would likely improve measurement of UK trade around Brexit, which is known to be delicate.

Second, the **ROO coding needs to be substantially improved**. Coding a single chapter-level ROO score for all HS-4 lines in that chapter is not adequate for a paper whose central claim is product-level causality. Annex ORIG-2 contains heading/subheading-specific provisions, and in some sectors alternative rules apply. The authors should map the legal text as closely as possible to the commodity classification used in the trade data, explain coding decisions transparently, and provide examples. If some products cannot be mapped cleanly, that is fine, but those cases should be excluded or treated separately rather than assigned a “representative” rule. A useful robustness check would compare results using different coding choices: strict coding, modal rule, and alternative-rule minimum/maximum restrictiveness.

Third, the paper would benefit from a more explicit discussion of **what variation remains after the fixed effects**. With product×year and partner×year fixed effects, identification comes from whether UK-EU trade in more restrictive products changes differentially relative to the non-EU group. But if ROO is chapter-level and many other Brexit frictions are also chapter-correlated, the coefficient may simply pick up sector-specific Brexit exposure. I would therefore recommend adding controls or interactions for observables likely correlated with both ROO and Brexit disruptions: average EU MFN tariff, SPS-regulated sectors, sectors with extensive just-in-time supply chains, or measures of imported-intermediate intensity from input-output data. These would not “solve” identification, but they would help show whether the result is really specific to ROO.

Relatedly, I would like to see much more convincing **parallel trends evidence**, ideally in event-study form. A single placebo at 2019 is not enough. Show annual or monthly coefficients for interactions between EU, ROO stringency, and each pre-period year. If the estimates are noisy because of the current dataset, that is itself evidence the design may need better data. It would also be useful to present graphs separately for exports and imports, since the asymmetry is the headline result.

The **choice of control partners** needs more justification. Why these five countries? Why not a broader set of non-EU OECD partners, or all non-EU partners excluding those with their own major contemporaneous UK policy shocks? The concern is not only external validity but also whether the sectoral composition and pandemic recovery paths of these countries differ systematically from EU trade. I would suggest showing robustness to alternative control groups: all non-EU high-income partners, only G7 non-EU partners, and perhaps synthetic weighting of control partners to match pre-Brexit UK-EU product composition. Another useful exercise would be to estimate the model at the bilateral partner level rather than aggregating the EU and non-EU groups, which would allow partner-specific trends and more informative standard errors.

I also think the paper should be more cautious in claiming that **tariffs are uniformly zero and thus all cross-product variation comes from ROO**. In practice, the fallback option is paying MFN tariffs, and those vary substantially across products, especially on the EU side. If the burden of complying with ROO is compared against heterogeneous tariff savings, then the relevant “treatment intensity” is not just ROO stringency but something like ROO stringency interacted with the value of preference margins. This is especially important for the compliance asymmetry story. A product with highly restrictive ROO but tiny MFN tariffs may behave very differently from one with equally restrictive ROO and high MFN tariffs. I would strongly recommend interacting ROO restrictiveness with product-specific preference margins or at least showing heterogeneity by MFN tariff bins.

The **mechanism section could become much stronger** with the newly available preference-utilization data. Even if the utilization data only start in 2022, they could still be extremely informative. For example, do high-ROO products show lower preference utilization for UK exports to the EU? On the import side, is utilization low and insensitive to ROO, consistent with firms opting to pay tariffs instead? Even reduced-form correlations would greatly strengthen the credibility of the interpretation. Right now, the paper’s strongest claim is precisely the one it does not directly test.

On estimation, I would encourage the authors to think carefully about the use of **log(trade+1)** with many possibly small or zero flows, particularly if the data are disaggregated further in a revision. PPML with high-dimensional fixed effects would likely be preferable and is now standard in trade applications. The authors should also report whether the results are driven by intensive-margin changes among large products or by disappearance of small trade flows.

The **sector heterogeneity table** currently raises some red flags. Standard errors of exactly 0.000 are not credible and suggest a coding or reporting problem. More substantively, the interpretation of positive import-side coefficients as “trade diversion toward higher-ROO products where the UK lacks domestic alternatives” is speculative. I would treat the sector splits as exploratory unless they can be linked to observed utilization behavior, tariff margins, or clear institutional features.

Presentation-wise, the paper would improve with a clearer separation between **what is identified and what is interpretation**. The reduced-form evidence may support “ROO stringency is associated with a larger relative export decline after Brexit,” but stronger language such as “we isolate the role of ROO” or “this reflects the one-sided burden of origin certification” requires more validation than is currently offered. A shorter, more disciplined paper that makes one carefully supported claim would be more convincing than one that reaches for broad conceptual conclusions.

Finally, I think the paper could become genuinely strong if it leans into the original insight from the manifest: the TCA as a rare setting where **ROO variation can be studied separately from headline tariff changes**. But to realize that promise, the authors need the data and coding precision that match the institutional story. As it stands, the paper has a good question, an interesting suggestive result, and a plausible mechanism, but it does not yet provide sufficiently sharp evidence for a top-field-journal short paper.
