**Idea Fidelity**

The submitted paper broadly follows the original idea: it studies the causal impact of Brexit-era rules of origin by leveraging cross-product ROO variation under the TCA, with a triple-difference design contrasting UK–EU versus UK–non‑EU trade before and after January 2021. However, several key elements from the manifest are missing or substantially altered. Most notably, the analysis relies on annual HS‑4 bilateral trade data from Comtrade rather than the monthly HS‑8 UK HMRC data proposed, and it omits the novel HMRC preference-utilization microdata that the manifest highlighted as a core asset. The ROO index is also coded at the HS‑2 level rather than HS‑6 or HS‑4, reducing the granularity of the treatment variation. These deviations weaken the paper’s ability to deliver the precise, product-level natural experiment promised in the manifest. If the authors cannot access the promised HMRC/commercial data, this mismatch should be transparently acknowledged and its implications (e.g., reduced within-chapter variation) thoroughly discussed.

**Summary**

This paper exploits cross-product heterogeneity in the EU–UK TCA’s rules of origin (ROO) to estimate their causal impact on UK goods trade. Using a triple-difference design (post/pre × EU/non-EU × ROO stringency), the authors find that ROO restrictiveness significantly reduces UK exports to the EU but has no differential effect on UK imports, which they interpret as a compliance asymmetry driven by exporters bearing the documentation burden. The result highlights the role of origin compliance costs in shaping post-Brexit trade disintegration and suggests policy attention to simplifying ROO for exporters.

**Essential Points**

1. **Aggregation and Data Choice Undermine the “Product-Level Natural Experiment”.** The treatment is coded at the HS‑2 level and the outcome is annual HS‑4 trade aggregated to EU/non-EU groups, whereas the manifest envisioned quarterly/monthly HS‑8 UK HMRC data with richer within-product variation and use of preference-utilization microdata. This aggregation substantially weakens the plausibility that variation in ROO restrictiveness is orthogonal to other product-level shocks, especially given that HS‑2 chapters bundle heterogeneous HS‑4/HS‑6 products. The authors should either demonstrate that their ROO-RI heterogeneity is meaningful at the HS‑4 level (e.g., by showing within-chapter variation in the relevant rules or coding ROO at HS‑4/HS‑6) or explain why the more granular data remain inaccessible and how the coarser coding affects identification.

2. **Control Group and Parallel-Trends Assumption Need Stronger Justification.** The counterfactual relies on five non-EU partners (US, Canada, Japan, South Korea, Australia) aggregated into a single group. These countries differ in product mix, supply-chain exposure, and pandemic timing compared to the EU, which the authors absorb via product×partner and partner×year fixed effects, but the core DDD requires the differential trend across ROO levels to be comparable. The placebo test (fake 2019 treatment) is informative but limited. The authors should provide more convincing evidence that ROO-RI is uncorrelated with pre-treatment differential trends between EU and controls—for example by showing separate pre-period trends for each control partner, conducting a synthetic-control-style weighting, or employing alternative control groups (e.g., intra-EU trade) to check robustness.

3. **Mechanism is Not Directly Observed.** The “compliance asymmetry” hinges on the idea that exporters must incur origin verification costs while importers can pay MFN tariffs, but the paper offers no direct evidence on preference-utilization, certification rates, or importers’ tariff choices. Since HMRC preference-utilization data were cited in the manifest and are now publicly available (from 2022), the absence of any utilization analysis weakens the mechanistic claim. Without it, the null import-side DDD could instead reflect measurement noise or differential demand shocks. The authors should incorporate the utilization microdata or, if unavailable, use alternative proxies (e.g., share of EU imports under tariff regimes, evidence from customs filings) to show that more restrictive ROO reduce the probability of claiming preferences on the export side but not on the import side.

**Suggestions**

- **Improve Granularity of the ROO Treatment.** If the HMRC HS‑8 or HS‑6 data remain unavailable, consider coding ROO-RI at the HS‑4 level where possible, or leveraging textual differences within chapters to assign additional variation (e.g., separate scores for agricultural vs. processed products within HS 02). Even if the treatment is coded at HS‑2, appendix tables listing the ROO rules and showing the actual variation in practice would reassure readers that the index captures meaningful differences. A simple robustness where the index is interacted with within-chapter dummies could demonstrate that the results are not driven by a handful of broad chapters.

- **Disaggregate the Control Group.** Provide estimates where the control group is each non-EU partner individually. This would help assess whether one large partner (e.g., the US) drives the results and whether the parallel-trends assumption holds more convincingly. Alternatively, use OECD or COMTRADE data to construct a synthetic non-EU aggregate that more closely matches pre-treatment EU trade in terms of products or values. This can be combined with event-study plots showing pre-trends for ROO high vs. low products within each partner group.

- **Address the Import-Side Heterogeneity in More Detail.** Table 3 shows sector-level heterogeneity but lacks an overarching interpretation. For example, why do instruments or minerals experience positive effects? Is it due to trade diversion, substitution effects, or measurement issues? Adding a short narrative and perhaps linking specific HS codes to known sector policies would sharpen the story. Additionally, if the import-side null arises from offsetting sign across sectors, a weighted-average decomposition (e.g., by share of EU import value) would clarify whether the net effect is indeed near zero or simply undetectable due to noise.

- **Leverage Preference Utilization Data.** Given that HMRC’s BDSPref dataset is now public, use it to test whether ROO restrictiveness reduces the fraction of exports claiming preferences. This could be a second-stage outcome (export value × utilization rate) to show that the compliance tax manifests through lower utilization. Even if only available from 2022 onward, focusing on the overlap years could provide quasi-experimental variation: compare utilization rates across ROO scores within exporters serving the EU, controlling for trends and using non-EU exports as additional controls. Doing so would substantiate the mechanism and differentiate between compliance costs and other barriers.

- **Clarify the Role of Tariffs in Importers’ Decisions.** The argument that importers can pay the UK’s MFN tariff rather than comply with ROO should be supported with data on the actual tariff levels and incidence. How large is the 4.3% MFN relative to average EU preferences? Do importers face administrative costs when paying MFN that might still depend on ROO? Including a short calculation or figure (e.g., histogram of MFN rates by product) would ground the story.

- **Consider Alternative Specifications for the Null Import Result.** The main DDD coefficient could be zero due to attenuation bias (e.g., measurement error in ROO-RI) or aggregation. Running the regression on subsets with high ROO variance or higher trade volumes could reveal whether the null persists in more informative samples. Similarly, a specification using the deviation of each product’s ROO score from its mean within chapter would filter out the coarse chapter-level coding and test if the null is robust to within-chapter contrast.

- **Report Sample Sizes and Balances by ROO Category.** Readers need to know how many products fall into each ROO score and whether high-ROO products are concentrated in certain sectors or partner flows. A table showing the number of HS‑4 lines per ROO score, their average trade shares, and their sectoral composition would help assess whether the estimation is driven by a thin slice of trade. If high-ROO products are mostly low-volume, the export result may depend on a few commodities.

- **Engage More with Alternative Explanations.** Could the export-side effect reflect demand shocks concentrated in high-ROO sectors (e.g., agriculture) rather than compliance? The current fixed effects absorb time-product shocks, but a sector-specific post-Brexit shock correlated with ROO is still possible. Running specifications that interact the ROO effect with sector indicators or controlling for sector-level global demand proxies (e.g., World price indices) would strengthen the causal claim.

- **Clarify the Contribution Relative to Existing Literature.** The introduction claims novelty in asymmetric ROO effects, but past papers (e.g., Conconi et al. 2016, Estevadeordal 2000) also discuss asymmetric burdens. A brief comparison highlighting how this paper adds causal evidence using Brexit’s unique setting would contextualize the contribution. Similarly, explicitly stating how the current result differs from papers that show aggregate Brexit trade losses would help readers orient the contribution.

By addressing these points—especially improving the treatment’s granularity, justifying the control group, and substantiating the compliance mechanism with preference-utilization data—the paper would greatly strengthen its claim to identify the causal impact of ROO.
