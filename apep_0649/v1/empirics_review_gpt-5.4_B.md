## 1. Idea Fidelity

The paper pursues only part of the original idea. The manifest proposed a multi-cutoff spatial RDD using both Companies House firm data and Land Registry transactions to study whether CAZs affect firm entry/exit and property values, with charge-class heterogeneity and some validation using air quality data. The submitted paper narrows the question to residential property values only. That is a legitimate scope reduction for a short paper, but it abandons a major part of the original contribution claim—business dynamics at CAZ boundaries—and therefore should not present itself as delivering the broader “economic activity” contribution envisioned in the manifest.

On identification, the paper stays close to the intended boundary design, but in a weaker form than advertised. The manifest emphasized sharp geographic boundaries and a multi-cutoff spatial RDD; the paper instead implements a pooled difference-in-discontinuities framework around multiple boundaries. That can be appropriate given the concern that boundaries follow major roads, but the execution departs from the original ideal in two important ways: (i) boundary measurement is sometimes based on “circular approximations,” which is a major dilution of the sharp-boundary design, and (ii) the paper does not use the proposed air-quality data to verify that treatment intensity actually changes discontinuously at the boundary or differentially by charge class. In short, the paper follows the spirit of the original idea, but not its strongest empirical version.

## 2. Summary

This paper studies how UK Clean Air Zones affect housing prices near zone boundaries using Land Registry transactions and a difference-in-discontinuities design across seven English cities. The headline result is that Class C zones are associated with a sizable positive capitalization effect at the boundary, while Class D zones show no net effect, which the paper interprets as evidence that air-quality amenities are offset by resident transport costs when private cars are charged.

The question is important and the setting is potentially valuable. But in its current form, the paper does not yet convincingly establish a causal effect of CAZs on property values, mainly because the identifying assumptions are not validated and some evidence points in the wrong direction.

## 3. Essential Points

1. **The identification strategy is not credible as currently validated.**  
   The most serious issue is the treatment of the pre-period placebo. A valid difference-in-discontinuities design requires that the boundary gap would have evolved similarly absent treatment. Yet the paper finds a statistically significant pre-trend/placebo effect of -5.7 percent one year before launch. This is not supportive evidence; it is direct evidence against the maintained assumption of stable pre-trends in the inside-outside gap. The paper’s attempt to reinterpret this as a “reversal” that strengthens the result is not persuasive. You need to show graphically and formally that pre-trends are flat over multiple pre-periods, city by city and pooled, or move to an event-study framework that makes the dynamics transparent. If the pre-trends remain differential, the causal interpretation should be dropped.

2. **Boundary measurement error is potentially severe and may invalidate the design.**  
   The use of “circular approximations” for some CAZ boundaries is very troubling in a boundary-discontinuity design. The entire strategy relies on precise assignment of inside/outside status and accurate distance to the true boundary. Approximate circles can generate substantial misclassification, especially when actual CAZs follow irregular road networks. This is not a minor attenuation issue; it may induce spurious discontinuities or wash out real ones in ways that differ across cities and classes. The paper needs to use official GIS boundary shapefiles or high-quality validated polygons for all cities, document them carefully, and show that results are not driven by cities with approximated boundaries.

3. **The mechanism and class heterogeneity claims are overstated relative to the evidence.**  
   The central interpretation—that Class C reveals amenity gains while Class D offsets them through private-car charges—is plausible, but the paper does not actually show the relevant first-stage facts. There is no evidence that air quality improved more inside the boundary, no evidence that effects differ with actual exposure intensity, and no evidence on vehicle compliance, car ownership, or travel costs near the boundary. Moreover, Class B is a single city, and the class-specific estimates rely on very few clusters at the city/class level. The paper can report heterogeneity by class, but it should not make strong mechanistic or design-prescriptive claims without direct supporting evidence.

## 4. Suggestions

This is a promising short paper topic, and I think there is a potentially publishable design here if the empirical foundation is strengthened considerably. The suggestions below are meant to help you get there.

First, **rebuild the design around transparent event-study evidence**. For this setting, a figure should be doing much of the identification work. Plot the inside-minus-outside price gap over event time relative to CAZ launch, within narrow bands around the boundary, separately by class and ideally by city. If your design is valid, the pre-period should be flat. Right now, the reported placebo suggests otherwise. A well-executed event study would reveal whether there is anticipation, whether effects emerge sharply at launch or earlier, and whether the Class C result is a one-off post period artifact. Even in AER: Insights format, one clean event-study figure would be much more informative than the current narrative treatment of the placebo.

Second, **show the raw discontinuity plots**. The paper is about boundary capitalization, but there are no graphs of prices against signed distance, before and after implementation. You should include local-binned means and fitted lines by pre/post period, for the pooled sample and for at least Class C versus Class D. This will allow the reader to judge whether there is a visible change in the boundary gap and whether the local polynomial specification is sensible.

Third, **tighten the econometric specification and describe it more precisely**. Equation (1) is underspecified relative to the text. You say the local linear polynomial is “allowed to differ on each side of the boundary and in each period,” but the written regression does not actually show those interactions. Please write the exact estimated equation. I would strongly recommend a saturated local specification:
- side-specific linear slopes in distance,
- interactions of those slopes with post,
- city fixed effects,
- calendar-time fixed effects,
- and perhaps city-specific time trends or city-by-quarter effects if sample size permits.  
Given staggered adoption, I would also be cautious with a pooled post indicator unless the event-time dynamics are similar.

Fourth, **address the level of clustering and statistical inference**. Clustering at postcode may be too fine relative to the source of identifying variation, which is the boundary-by-time comparison. With only seven cities and a small number of policy events, inference may be fragile. At minimum, report robustness to clustering at broader geographic levels (e.g., boundary segment, LSOA, city) and, if feasible, use wild bootstrap methods appropriate for few treated clusters. The class-level results especially are vulnerable, since the effective number of policy units is tiny.

Fifth, **reconsider the bandwidth argument**. The fact that estimates grow and become significant only at wider bandwidths is not obviously comforting in an RD-type design. Usually, credibility comes from local comparisons, while wider bandwidths raise concerns about functional-form dependence and compositional differences. Your current interpretation—that the effect extends beyond 500m or that precision improves—is too convenient. You should present bandwidth sensitivity more cautiously, perhaps with bias-corrected local estimates and a figure of estimates over bandwidths. If the result is not robust locally, that should temper the claims.

Sixth, **improve the placebo logic**. The shifted-boundary placebo is currently interpreted as supportive because it yields a significant negative estimate. But that is not a reassuring placebo in the usual sense; a valid placebo should ideally produce no effect. A significant estimate at an artificial boundary suggests there are spatial gradients or neighborhood changes that your model may not adequately capture. A better placebo exercise would use boundaries from future implementation dates, pseudo-launch dates in pre-periods, or placebo boundaries sufficiently far away but within similar urban contexts. You might also exploit non-CAZ urban ring roads as comparison boundaries.

Seventh, **validate treatment intensity with air quality data**, as anticipated in the manifest. Even a simple reduced-form check using nearby monitors would substantially strengthen the paper. If Class C truly generates a positive housing premium through cleaner air, one should see larger inside-versus-outside improvements in NO2 near those boundaries, at least directionally. Without that, the mechanism remains speculative. I realize monitor coverage is sparse, but even suggestive evidence would help, and a null or noisy first stage would be important to know.

Eighth, **make the class comparisons more credible by reporting city-specific results**. Right now, Class C’s 6.9 percent result could be driven by one city, especially Bath, which has very different baseline prices and an earlier launch date than Bradford or Tyneside. Likewise, Class D combines Birmingham, Bristol, and Sheffield, which differ greatly in price levels, charge amounts, and implementation dates. A simple forest plot of city-specific estimates with pooled class averages would be very helpful. If heterogeneity is large within class, the paper’s “Class C vs. Class D” interpretation should be softened.

Ninth, **take composition more seriously**. Housing transactions are not a fixed sample. If CAZs affect which properties transact—for example, flats versus houses, investor-owned versus owner-occupied, leasehold versus freehold—then transaction-price estimates may mix capitalization with composition changes. You already control for property type, but you should go further by showing balance in observables near the boundary pre/post, and by testing whether transaction composition shifts discontinuously after implementation. Tenure, new-build status, and perhaps postcode fixed effects for repeat-sale subsamples would help if feasible.

Tenth, **exploit repeat sales if possible**. Even a modest repeat-sales specification could greatly improve credibility by differencing out time-invariant unit quality. A repeat-sales/event-study design for properties near the boundary would directly address many concerns about compositional change and unobserved heterogeneity. If sample size is too small for the main analysis, a repeat-sales robustness check would still be valuable.

Eleventh, **be more restrained in policy interpretation**. The current conclusion that Class C is the “Goldilocks” policy class is much stronger than the evidence supports. A null difference for Class D does not imply that private-car charges exactly offset air-quality benefits, and certainly not that policymakers should favor Class C. There may be larger health benefits under Class D not capitalized immediately, differential incidence across households, or longer-run adaptation via fleet turnover. I would recast the interpretation as: “In the short run, we find evidence of positive capitalization in Class C cities but not in Class D cities.” That is already interesting without overreaching.

Twelfth, **clean up a few internal inconsistencies and presentation issues**. For example, the text says the largest samples come from Birmingham and Sheffield, but the table shows Bristol and Tyneside as larger. These are small issues, but they matter in a short paper where the empirical narrative must be tight. Similarly, the abstract should reflect the uncertainty in the pooled estimate and not lead with a “striking asymmetry” before the reader sees that the pooled preferred specification is not statistically significant and that the pre-trend evidence is problematic.

Finally, I would encourage you to **either narrow the paper further or restore one missing pillar from the original idea**. For AER: Insights length, a clean paper on property values alone is fine—but then it must be empirically airtight. Alternatively, one way to strengthen the contribution would be to add a compact business-dynamics result using Companies House data, as in the manifest, especially if property effects remain somewhat noisy. That would broaden the economic relevance of the paper and help assess whether these zones create localized economic displacement rather than just amenity capitalization. But I would only do this if it can be done convincingly; right now, the first priority is to make the core property-value design credible.

Overall, the setting is interesting and the question matters, but the current version does not yet support strong causal claims. The paper would improve substantially with better boundary data, transparent dynamic validation, stronger inference, and a more disciplined interpretation of the evidence.
