**1. Idea Fidelity**

The paper stays remarkably close to the manifested idea. It exploits the staggered implementation of the nine comprehensive surprise billing laws, relies on CMS Hospital Compare OP-18b and OP-22 measures, estimates cohort-specific effects with Sun & Abraham (2021), and highlights mechanisms associated with PE-backed staffing firms. **2. Summary**

This study uses Hospital Compare data and a Sun–Abraham estimator to assess whether state surprise billing protections implemented between 2015 and 2018 harmed emergency department quality, measured by median discharge wait time (OP-18b) and leave-without-being-seen rates (OP-22). The aggregate treatment effects are close to zero—estimates of 0.33 minutes and 0.08 percentage points, respectively—with tight confidence intervals that rule out economically sizable changes on these margins. The paper thus argues that, on the observed metrics, surprise billing regulations did not degrade ED quality even in the face of potential private-equity cost-cutting.

**3. Essential Points**

1. **Event-study pattern and differential trends.** The Sun–Abraham figures in Table 3 show large, statistically significant deviations at $k=-4$, $k=-5$, and especially $k=+3$. While the paper attributes $k=-4/-5$ to compositional changes and $k=+3$ to idiosyncratic variation in the earliest cohort, these deviations undermine the parallel trends assumption and raise doubts about interpreting the aggregate ATT as causal. The authors need to demonstrate that these anomalies are not driving the null—for example, by estimating the SA ATT using a balanced subset of hospitals that report in every year, or by showing that restricting to later cohorts (where pre-trends are flat) yields similar results.

2. **Placebo test casts doubt on the identification.** OP-20, used as a placebo, produces a highly significant treatment effect ($-1.41$ minutes). A placebo that is downstream of the same staffing mechanism is not informative; a more suitable placebo (e.g., inpatient quality measures or non-ED process measures) could help distinguish differential trends from a genuine lack of response. The current placebo raises the possibility that treated states were on a different trajectory in ED process metrics unrelated to surprise billing, which would bias the ATT towards zero. The authors should either (a) provide a stronger placebo outcome or (b) demonstrate robustness to including state-specific linear or even quadratic time trends to absorb differential trajectories.

3. **Mechanism and treatment intensity are too indirect.** The implied revenue shock is attenuated because the laws apply only to state-regulated plans (ERISA preemption), so the ATT captures an intent-to-treat average across hospitals with varying exposure. Without a credible first-stage—such as linking the timing of law adoption to actual out-of-network revenue declines at the hospital level, or exploiting heterogeneity in the concentration of PE-backed staffing firms—the null could simply be due to insufficient treatment intensity. The paper would be much stronger if it could (i) estimate heterogeneity across hospitals with different shares of self-insured patients or direct measures of PE staffing penetration, and (ii) report placebo or falsification tests using measures of billing behavior (e.g., out-of-network revenue) to confirm that the laws indeed imposed a meaningful shock in the treated sample.

If these issues cannot be resolved, the paper risks being rejected: the combination of unexplained dynamics, a questionable placebo, and weak treatment intensity undermines the credibility of the null conclusion.

**4. Suggestions**

- **Address compositional pre-trends.** Estimate the SA ATT on a balanced panel (hospitals that report every year 2013–2018) or use other ways (e.g., inverse probability weighting) to ensure that the comparison group in far pre-periods is similar to later cohorts. At minimum, show that coefficients from $k=-4/-5$ become stable when you restrict the sample to cohorts with richer pretreatment data or when you drop those early cohorts entirely. This will help allay concerns that the significant pre-trend estimates reflect meaningful deviations.

- **Strengthen placebo/falsification approach.** Instead of OP-20, use a non-ED quality measure (e.g., inpatient readmissions, surgical mortality, or outpatient process measures) that should not be affected by ED staffing but would reveal state-specific trends. Alternatively, use outcomes from states that implemented other contemporaneous reforms (Medicaid expansions, hospital reimbursement changes) and show that the same pattern does not appear there. Finally, consider estimating the same specification on hospitals in states that passed only partial surprise billing laws (e.g., non-comprehensive or later adopters) to confirm the null is specific to comprehensive reforms.

- **Explore heterogeneity by expected exposure.** The argument that PE-backed staffing firms drove the hypothesized quality response should be supported with more direct measures. Possible approaches include: (a) collapsing administrative data or news reports to code whether a hospital was staffed by TeamHealth/Envision in a given year, (b) interacting treatment with regional indicators for PE penetration derived from market-level data, or (c) using the documented share of self-insured patients (if available from hospital cost reports) to proxy for treatment intensity. At a minimum, interact the treatment indicator with the share of commercially insured ED visits (higher share should mean greater financial exposure) and show that hospitals with more commercially insured patients do (or do not) show differential responses.

- **Control for other policy changes and hospital trends.** Treated states differ in urbanization, Medicaid policy, and insurance market dynamics, which could correlate with ED crowding. Including state (or hospital) *linear* time trends, or even state-by-year controls for key macro variables (e.g., unemployment, Medicaid expansion status, opioid-related ED visits), would make the identifying assumption more plausible. If the data allow, exploit within-state variation by comparing hospitals near the border of treated vs. never-treated states, or within treated states before and after the law using cross-state spillovers. These approaches would help isolate the surprise billing effect from broader ED trends.

- **Clarify economic magnitudes.** While the standardized effect sizes are small, converting them into patient-impact metrics would be helpful. For example, how many additional LWBS patients would a 0.08 percentage point rise imply across an average ED (using typical annual patient volumes)? Similarly, contextualize the 0.33-minute change relative to staffing levels or capacity constraints. This will help readers understand whether the null effects are practically meaningful or simply appear small due to averaging across states.

- **Discuss potential offsetting responses.** The discussion rightly speculates that firms may have adjusted via compensation or billing rather than staffing, but providing data (e.g., from Medicare cost reports or staffing ratios) would strengthen this. If such data are unavailable, note the limitations more explicitly: e.g., the null might result from simultaneous opposite effects (some hospitals cutting staff while others increased efficiency). A short theoretical framing of the expected direction and timing of these adjustments would also aid interpretation.

- **Expand on the placebo OP-20 result.** Since the significant OP-20 coefficient runs counter to the main null, the appendix should show the event study for OP-20 to see if the pattern mirrors OP-18b or is driven by a single cohort. Additionally, reporting whether the OP-20 effect persists once you control for hospital-specific trends would clarify whether it reflects a true response or an identification problem.

- **Consider inference with few treated clusters.** Although there are 50 states (clusters), only nine are treated, which raises concerns about the validity of standard cluster-robust SEs. You might account for this by (a) using wild cluster bootstrap (Cameron et al.) to compute p-values or (b) reporting randomization inference p-values using permuted treatment timings. This would provide more confidence that the null is not due to underpowered inference.

- **Document sample selection and missingness.** The data drop critical access hospitals and other specialties, but the summary statistics show treated hospitals have higher average wait times (likely due to urban location). A table showing how the sample evolves over time—e.g., number of hospitals reporting each year by state—would help assess whether differential reporting drives the results. If hospitals with missing data are systematically different, consider multiple imputation or inverse probability weights to correct for attrition.

Addressing these suggestions would significantly strengthen the paper’s empirical credibility and help clarify whether surprise billing laws truly leave ED quality untouched or whether the null masks more nuanced responses.
