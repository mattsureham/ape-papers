## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether a size-contingent information mandate changes firms’ organizational choices. Using England’s 2022 rule requiring calorie labels for food businesses with 250+ employees, and comparing England to Scotland/Wales and food services to other service sectors, the paper finds no detectable effect on the food-sector size distribution—suggesting that this particular disclosure mandate is too cheap to induce threshold avoidance.

Why should a busy economist care? Because size-based regulation is often presumed to distort firm behavior; if an unusually salient, nationally implemented disclosure rule does **not** generate bunching or downsizing, that is informative about when regulatory thresholds matter and when they do not.

**Does the paper itself articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?** Reasonably well, but not optimally. The current opening is competent and clear, but it still reads like “here is a policy and here is my design.” For AER-level positioning, the first two paragraphs should lead with the broader economic question: **when do regulatory thresholds distort firm growth, and when do they not?** The calorie-labeling case should enter as a sharp test of that bigger idea, not as the idea itself.

**The pitch the paper should have:**

> Economists have learned that firm-size thresholds can distort organization, employment, and growth when crossing the threshold triggers costly regulation. But we know much less about whether the same is true for disclosure mandates, where compliance may be salient yet economically small. This paper studies England’s 2022 calorie-labeling rule, which applies only to food businesses with 250 or more employees, as a clean test of whether a size-based information mandate changes the firm size distribution.
>
> Using universe-level UK business counts and a difference-in-difference-in-differences design comparing England to Scotland and Wales, and food services to other service sectors, I find no detectable effect on the number of firms, the share above the threshold, or the ratio of firms just above to just below it. The result suggests that not all regulatory cliffs bind: for low-cost disclosure mandates, size-based exemptions may be administratively useful without distorting firm structure.

Several possibilities, in descending order of impact:

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### What is the setup?
There is a large economics literature showing that regulatory thresholds can distort firm behavior by creating incentives to stay below size cutoffs.

### What is the tension?
Most of that evidence comes from costly regulations—labor law, environmental compliance, financial disclosure. It is unclear whether the same logic applies to disclosure mandates like calorie labeling, where the costs may be visible but modest. England’s 250-employee threshold creates a clean test.

### What is the resolution?
The paper finds no detectable shift in the firm-size distribution, no evidence of bunching around the threshold, and no decline in the number of large food-service firms after the mandate.

### What are the implications?
The implication is not just about nutrition policy. It is that **threshold regulation is not inherently distortionary**; the economic consequences depend on compliance cost magnitude and form. For low-cost disclosure mandates, exemptions may reduce administrative burden without meaningfully affecting market structure.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
It has the ingredients of one, but the execution is only **serviceable**. The draft is better than many applied papers: there is a clear question, a clean institutional setting, and a result. But the narrative still feels somewhat like “an empirically neat null” rather than “a conceptually important test.”

The risk is that it reads as a collection of competent null results around an English nutrition policy. To become a stronger paper, it should tell the story:

> Economists have learned to expect threshold distortions. This paper asks whether that expectation generalizes to low-cost disclosure mandates. In a sharp institutional setting, it does not.

That is the story.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?
“England created a hard 250-employee cutoff for calorie labeling, and unlike labor or environmental thresholds, firms did not reorganize to avoid it.”

That is the most interesting sentence in the paper.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
A mixed response.

- **Lean in** if framed as a challenge to the broad threshold-distortion intuition.
- **Reach for phones** if framed as “we study calorie labeling in England and find no effect.”

This paper lives or dies on framing.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Probably:
- “Is that because the costs are genuinely trivial, or because the data are too coarse to detect the relevant margin?”
- Closely followed by:
- “Would this generalize to other disclosure mandates or lower thresholds?”

Those are exactly the questions the paper should anticipate and answer strategically in the introduction and discussion.

### If findings are null or modest: is the null itself interesting?
Yes, but only conditionally. Nulls are interesting when they overturn a reasonable prior or bound an important mechanism.

This null can be interesting because:
- the policy created a salient threshold;
- firms had time to anticipate it;
- the threshold literature primes us to expect avoidance;
- finding none suggests the threshold does not bind economically.

But the paper must make that case much harder. Right now it does some of this, but not enough. The result risks feeling like “no effect, perhaps due to low power / coarse data” rather than “a meaningful falsification of the idea that all size-based regulations distort.”

The null becomes valuable when presented as:
- evidence that the **cost elasticity of organizational response** to disclosure mandates is low.
