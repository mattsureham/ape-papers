**Summary**

The paper attempts to estimate the impact of the July 2019 nationwide PFAS soil movement freeze in the Netherlands on municipal-level housing completions, comparing municipalities in PFAS-exposed provinces (Zuid-Holland and Noord-Brabant) to the rest of the country using a two-way fixed effects DiD framework over 2012–2023. It reports a precisely estimated null effect during the freeze and an insignificant (if anything, positive) effect after the November 2019 relaxation, arguing that the strict threshold made the regulation effectively universal and that the freeze was too brief relative to construction timelines to show up in completions data.

**Essential Points**

1. **Treatment Definition and Identification Threats:** Classifying all Zuid-Holland and Noord-Brabant municipalities as “high PFAS” ignores the spatial heterogeneity that motivated the original design (i.e., proximity to the Chemours facility and downstream contamination). PFAS exposure is not uniform across these provinces, and the DiD assumes that the freeze affected these treated provinces but not the controls, despite the manifest’s emphasis on contamination gradients. This coarse treatment likely conflates genuinely exposed municipalities with many that were effectively clean, diluting the “treatment” and undermining the parallel trends assumption. A more nuanced treatment intensity measure (e.g., distance to Chemours, modeled contamination decays) or restricting to municipalities with elevated measured PFAS would better align with the causal mechanism.

2. **Policy Variation Used vs. Mechanism of Effect:** The paper treats the July–November 2019 period as a relatively exogenous shock to treated provinces, yet the manifest stressed that the July policy freeze was nationally binding and only the relaxation partially reintroduced geographic differential effects. If the treatment is universal (as the paper concedes), the assumed causal contrast collapses—there is no variation left to identify the differential effect, making the DiD uninterpretable. The identification relies entirely on the assumption that legalization constraints affected Zuid-Holland/Noord-Brabant more than other provinces, but this is not convincingly argued or empirically demonstrated. Without showing that municipalities in treated provinces actually faced binding constraints (e.g., via measured PFAS concentrations or permit denials), the estimated “null” might just reflect the absence of treatment variation.

3. **Event Study Interpretation and Timing Mismatch:** The event study in Table 3 is presented as evidence of no pre-trends, but the pre-treatment coefficients are large in magnitude and statistically significant at some lags (e.g., -12, -3). This raises concerns about differential trends even before July 2019; the paper dismisses them without investigating. Moreover, the stated mechanism—that housing completions reflect long pipelines—implies that any true effect of the freeze would surface with a lag (after five months). Yet, the event study coefficients in the immediate post-period are also noisy, and no lagged effects are explicitly tested. The paper needs to model and test for delayed responses rather than relying on contemporaneous coefficients.

If additional critical issues exist beyond these three (for instance, the lack of any spatial RDD or the failure to explore heterogeneous effects near Chemours), the project should be reconsidered; however, these three go to the heart of the identification strategy and should be resolved before publication.

**Suggestions**

1. **Reframe the Treatment Using Spatial Exposure:** The original idea envisioned exploiting the PFAS contamination gradient radiating from the Chemours factory. Please reconsider the binary province-level treatment and instead construct a continuous or at least finer-grained measure of PFAS exposure (e.g., municipality-level PFAS concentration averages, distance to Chemours, or modeled downwind/downstream zones). This would allow the paper to implement the promised spatial RDD or dose-response framework and would better capture where the freeze was binding. If actual PFAS measurements are unavailable, consider proxies such as industrial zoning, groundwater PFAS testing sites, or even topographical flow patterns to identify municipalities likely above the 0.1 μg/kg threshold.

2. **Clarify and Test the Identification Assumptions:** The validity of the DiD relies on differential binding of the policy. Provide evidence that municipalities in Zuid-Holland/Noord-Brabant faced more binding constraints during July–November 2019 than elsewhere (e.g., through permit processing delays, construction stoppages reported by local governments, or industry anecdotes). If the policy effectively applied nationwide—as you argue—then the DiD identification collapses, and the analysis should shift to alternative approaches (e.g., national interrupted time series, or exploiting heterogeneity in contamination intensity). Alternatively, focus explicitly on the November relaxation as the source of variation, where PFAS exposure may have again created differentials between heavily contaminated versus clean municipalities.

3. **Model Lagged Responses and Alternative Outcomes:** Because housing completions reflect long-term construction projects, consider estimating distributed-lag models or limiting the analysis to indicators more sensitive to immediate disruptions (such as building permit issuances, excavation permits, or construction starts if available). If only completions are available, explicitly model the expected lag structure: regress completions on treatment indicators lagged by 6–24 months to see whether freeze-period constraints materialize later. This would make the “pipeline lag” mechanism evidence-based rather than speculative. Additionally, explore whether smaller geographic units (e.g., neighborhoods within metropolitan areas) or alternative outcomes (such as construction employment or foundation permit counts) yield clearer signals.

4. **Address Pre-trend Concerns Transparently:** The event study results show several pre-treatment point estimates that are non-zero and even statistically significant. Plot the full set of event-time coefficients (not just selected ones) to visually assess whether pre-trend deviations are systematic. If they are, consider implementing synthetic control or matching on pre-treatment trends, or reweighting to balance pre-trends. At minimum, discuss why the observed pre-period fluctuations do not violate the parallel trends assumption (perhaps they coincide with province-specific shocks that can be accounted for via trends or controls).

5. **Integrate the Dual Discontinuity Structure:** The manifest highlighted the two discontinuities—the July freeze and the November relaxation—and suggested testing for asymmetric effects. Extend the empirical framework beyond the single “freeze vs. post” binary: estimate separate dynamic responses around both discontinuities, possibly by including interactions with event_time around November 2019 as well. This would allow you to assess whether the relaxation produced a catch-up in treated areas, which is important for interpreting the null. If there is genuinely no effect, show that the treatment effect remains indistinguishable from zero even when comparing pre-freeze, freeze, and relaxation periods separately.

6. **Expand Robustness to Include Spatial and Temporal Heterogeneity:** Parallel trends may fail if control provinces experienced their own shocks (e.g., nitrogen crisis, regional construction cycles). Consider constructing a more refined control group by matching municipalities on pre-treatment housing trends and PFAS exposure proxies, or by excluding provinces with known confounding shocks (e.g., Gelderland for nitrogen, Flevoland for policy-induced affordability programs). Additionally, test whether the null effect holds when weighting regressions by municipality population or housing stock, as the current aggregation treats large and small municipalities equally despite likely differences in responsiveness.

7. **Engage with Null Results More Thoroughly:** A null finding can be valuable, but it requires careful handling to avoid appearing as a failure to detect effects. Consider conducting power calculations to demonstrate that the data are sufficiently informative to rule out economically meaningful effects. You already compute standardized effect sizes; extend this by providing the smallest detectable effect size at conventional power levels and clarifying how large a true effect would need to be to be policy-relevant. Such a discussion strengthens the claim that the null is informative rather than ambiguous.

8. **Reconcile Narrative with Empirical Strategy:** The Discussion emphasizes “threshold traps” and universality, but the empirical strategy never directly tests whether the standards bindingness varied by municipality. If the freeze truly affected all municipalities equally, then a DiD across space cannot estimate anything meaningful. Either reorient the empirical work to test the “threshold trap” hypothesis directly (e.g., by showing that PFAS contamination distributions had almost no variation around 0.1 μg/kg) or recalibrate the narrative to acknowledge that, empirically, you are estimating the average effect across provinces with different exposure levels rather than proving universality.

By addressing these issues—aligning treatment definition with actual contamination gradients, providing stronger identification checks, modeling lagged effects, and better motivating the null finding—the paper can retain its valuable focus on PFAS regulation while offering credible empirical evidence on housing supply effects.
