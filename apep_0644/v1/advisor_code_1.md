**Summary**

This paper evaluates whether mandatory salary-range job-posting laws in Colorado, California, Washington, and New York affected employer hiring behavior. Using near-universal QWI data and Callaway–Sant’Anna staggered DiD, the author finds precisely estimated null effects on new hires, recalls, job creation, separations, and turnover, while hinting at a modest (-3.5 log points) decline in new hire earnings. The results are robust across cohorts, gender, industry dispersion, border-style comparisons, and placebo sectors, suggesting that quantity-side disruption is negligible.

**Essential Points**

1. **State-Level Aggregation vs. County-Level Variation:** The manifest and the QWI’s strength lie in fine-grained county-quarter-industry cells, yet the empirical section aggregates to state-industry-quarter panels (1,051 panels). Aggregation risks obscuring heterogeneous treatment exposure within states—especially in high-variation states like California and New York—and may bias estimates if treatment intensity varies (e.g., metropolitan areas with more affected employers). Please justify the aggregation choice: Why is the unit level reduced, and how does this impact identification? Would a county-level estimator (perhaps with fewer industries or quarters for tractability) change the inference or power calculations?

2. **Parallel Trends and Control Group Dynamics:** The identification hinges on never-treated states as controls. However, the treated states are high-wage, high-growth economies, while the controls include low-wage regions. Even with state and industry fixed effects, trends may diverge (e.g., employment growth, sectoral shocks). The paper references Sun–Abraham pre-trend checks in the appendix, but the main text lacks a figure or table for visual inspection. Please present the event-study coefficients (with confidence intervals) for at least one main outcome to demonstrate flat pre-treatment trends. Additionally, provide more detail on the composition of the never-treated group (e.g., are some states economically similar but just later adopters?) and consider adding state-specific linear time trends to ensure the results are not driven by differential trajectories.

3. **Interpretation of the Earnings Decline:** The suggestive -3.5 log point effect on new hire earnings is potentially important but currently underdeveloped. Is this decline concentrated in certain industries, cohorts, or demographic groups? Are the earnings estimates driven by the same sample used for the flow outcomes (new hires per employment) or a limited subset (e.g., because of disclosure thresholds)? Also, is it possible that the earnings decline is mechanically related to wage compression in the data (e.g., lower thresholds for “new hire” earnings)? The current framing (“reanchoring instead of job destruction”) is appealing, but more evidence is needed to rule out alternative explanations (like measurement changes, reporting behavior, or shifts in the composition of new hires). Please expand on the data definition of `EarnHirNS` and explore whether the earnings change is robust to controlling for industry-cohort trends or only present in certain states/times.

**Suggestions**

1. **Clarify the Sample Construction for Earning Outcomes:** The QWI reports average earnings for new hires and stable workers. It would help to know whether the earnings regressions use the same state-industry-quarter panel as the hiring rates or if they are limited by disclosure rules (e.g., cells with fewer than 10 workers are suppressed). If there is suppression, the sample may be systematically different, potentially biasing the earnings ATT. Include a discussion (perhaps in the data appendix) and, if necessary, a robustness check (e.g., restricting the hiring results to the same sub-sample used for earnings to show that the null holds there as well).

2. **Expand on the COVID/Policy Confounders:** The Colorado cohort spans the pandemic recovery period, and although the later cohorts help, it remains crucial to show that Colorado's post-2021 trends are not driven by, say, differential sectoral rebounds or stimulus policies. Consider including an additional robustness check that interacts treated states with a COVID stringency index or applies a synthetic control (or partial synthetic) using pre-pandemic dynamics to ensure the Colorado estimate is not biased. Alternatively, provide a table showing pre-trends in key outcomes for Colorado relative to a carefully selected pre-treatment control set (e.g., neighboring states or states with similar industry mixes).

3. **Address Effect Heterogeneity Beyond Industry Dispersion:** While high- vs. low-wage-dispersion industries are compelling, the paper could further leverage the demographic breakdowns hinted at in the manifest. For example, do female employment flows respond differently than male flows? Do high-education industries show larger changes? The current gender robustness table (Panel B) is helpful but limited to separate male/female samples. Exploring differences in impacts across race or education groups—perhaps through a triple-difference (e.g., treated vs. control, before vs. after, high vs. low dispersion or female vs. male)—would add depth and provide evidence on equity impacts.

4. **Document Standard Errors and Clustering Strategy:** The paper mentions state-level clustering, but the unit of observation is state-industry-quarter. Given only four treated states, clustering at the state level may be too coarse and could underestimate uncertainty. Have you considered wild-cluster bootstrap inference or a block permutation test to account for the small number of treated clusters? At least mention whether inference is robust to alternative standard error treatments, especially for the suggestive earnings effect and separation rate result.

5. **Justify the Control Group Composition:** The “never-treated” pool includes 47 states, but some (e.g., Connecticut or Maryland) previously adopted weaker “upon request” mandates. Do these states have any indirect exposure or spillovers? Clarify why they are treated as untreated and, ideally, re-estimate results excluding states with prior transparency legislation to ensure they are not contaminating the control group. Additionally, for the 2025 cohort states (IL, MN, NJ, VT, MA), which are mentioned in the manifest as future cohorts, perhaps use them in a “not yet treated” analysis (i.e., treat them as controls until their policy date) to see whether the results hold in a more dynamic setting.

6. **Provide More Detail on the Treatment Timing Choice:** Assigning New York to 2023Q4 makes sense operationally, but the law went into effect mid-September 2023. Have you tested whether assigning treatment to 2023Q3 (to capture anticipation or partial exposure) changes the results? You might also explore a “fuzzy” treatment where exposure is measured as the share of the quarter after the law took effect. This would strengthen the credibility of the timing assumption.

7. **Strengthen the Discussion on Mechanisms:** The concluding interpretation posits that firms re-anchor wages downward within the mandated ranges, but this is only weakly supported. It would be valuable to relate the findings to an explicit model or at least cite theoretical mechanisms that could generate null employment effects but negative wage effects. Consider discussing whether the negative wage effect implies a transfer from workers to firms, or if it offsets prior wage dispersion. A short appendix with a stylized model or a graphical illustration of the proposed mechanism would help.

8. **Clarify Terminology Around Placebos and Border Checks:** The robustness table refers to “Colorado border states only,” but the text should make explicit what the comparison entails (does it compare Colorado counties to adjacent-state counties, or does it simply restrict the control group to those neighbors?). If county-level data are available, even a coarse border design (e.g., ±1 year difference) would bolster the argument that local trends are similar absent the mandate. If that is infeasible, explain the limitations and describe any ongoing work to develop such a design.

9. **Consider Additional Outcome Measures:** The manifest mentioned job creation/destruction flows, but the main results table focuses on new hires, recalls, job creation, separations, turnover, and new-hire earnings. Can you add gross job destruction/creation and separation rates for the state-cohort-specific tables to make it easier to see the full profile? It would also be informative to include outcomes like recall-to-new-hire ratios or hiring intensity within firms (if QWI allows). a

10. **Data Availability and Reproducibility:** Since the paper relies on QWI data available through Azure, it would be helpful to include, either in the main text or appendix, more detail on the access protocol, query scripts, and any code used for Callaway–Sant’Anna estimation. Given the policy relevance, providing replication resources (even if limited to aggregated data) would increase credibility.

In sum, the paper tackles a timely question with compelling data and a well-chosen estimator. Addressing the points above—especially those surrounding aggregation choices, pre-trend evidence, and the earnings channel—would bolster the trustworthiness of the null and strengthen the paper’s policy implications.
