### 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
This paper evaluates whether US state mandates requiring salary ranges in job postings—often criticized as "job killers"—actually disrupt labor market flows. Using administrative QWI data and a staggered difference-in-differences design, the author finds a precisely estimated null effect on hiring, job creation, and turnover. The paper suggests that while mandates may compress realized new-hire earnings, they do not discourage firms from creating or filling positions.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this well, but it leads with the policy alarmism. To be a top-tier paper, it needs to lead with the **economic friction**. 
*The pitch it should have:* "While pay transparency is theorized to improve matching efficiency, it may also trigger costly firm-side adjustments or reduced vacancy creation. This paper provides the first comprehensive evidence on the quantity margin of transparency mandates, finding that firms absorb these informational requirements without altering hiring volumes, though suggestive evidence indicates they may re-anchor starting wages downward."

### 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
The paper identifies the "quantity margin" (hiring/flows) as the missing half of the pay transparency literature, which has previously focused almost entirely on the "price margin" (wages).

*   **Differentiation:** It is clearly differentiated from Cullen and Pakzad-Hurson (2024) by focusing on realized flows rather than posted wages. It moves beyond the gender-gap focus of Duchini et al. (2024).
*   **Question:** It frames itself well as a question about the world (Do these laws kill jobs?), but the AER would prefer it framed as a question about **Firm Behavior**: How do firms respond to the removal of information rents?
*   **Smart Economist Test:** They would say: "It’s a high-quality DiD showing that transparency mandates don't have the disemployment effects critics feared."
*   **Making it bigger:** The paper needs to lean harder into the **Earnings/Price tension**. The finding that realized earnings *decline* (p=0.08) while previous literature found posted wages *increase* is the most "AER-worthy" puzzle in the paper. Resolving this tension (e.g., via a simple search-and-bargaining model) would elevate the paper.

### 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
The paper sits at the intersection of Labor (Transparency) and Macro-Labor (Flows/Frictions).
*   **Neighbors:** Cullen & Pakzad-Hurson (2024), Baker et al. (2023), and the Diamond-Mortensen-Pissarides search literature.
*   **Strategy:** It currently "fills a gap." It should instead "test a mechanism." Specifically, it should position itself as a test of whether transparency acts as a tax on hiring or a pure informational shift.
*   **Missing Conversations:** The paper should speak to the **Monopsony** literature. If firms have wage-setting power, transparency should theoretically reduce their ability to price-discriminate. The null result on hiring suggests that firms' "threat point" in vacancy creation hasn't been hit yet.

### 4. NARRATIVE ARC
*   **Setup:** Information asymmetry is a core feature of labor markets.
*   **Tension:** Transparency mandates are intended to help workers, but if they destroy the firm's surplus, they might result in fewer jobs (the "Quantity vs. Price" trade-off).
*   **Resolution:** The quantity effect is zero. Firms keep hiring at the same rate.
*   **Implications:** The "cost" of transparency is not borne through employment destruction but perhaps through subtle wage re-anchoring.

**Evaluation:** The arc is clean but a bit "flat." It feels like a policy evaluation. To get into the AER, the tension needs to be more theoretical: Why *would* we expect a null? Why is the "price" finding in other papers not showing up in "quantities"?

### 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
*   **The Lead Fact:** "When Colorado and California forced firms to post salaries, hiring didn't budge—but starting pay actually might have dropped slightly."
*   **Response:** Economists will lean in for the second half of that sentence. The "null on hiring" is a "phew" for policymakers, but the "decline in earnings" is a "why?" for theorists.
*   **Follow-up:** "Wait, if posted wages go up but realized earnings go down, does that mean firms are posting 'fake' high ranges and then low-balling at the offer stage?" (This is the AER-level question).

### 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
*   **Front-load the Earnings finding:** Currently, the earnings result is treated as a "suggestive" side-note. Even with $p=0.08$, this is the most interesting result because it contradicts the "transparency helps workers" narrative. 
*   **Appendix to Main:** The industry heterogeneity (High vs. Low dispersion) is excellent and should be a central pillar of the paper, as it acts as a "dosage" test.
*   **Conclusion:** Needs to move away from "policymakers should be reassured" toward "what this tells us about firm wage-setting power."

### 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The gap is **Ambition/Mechanism**. Currently, it is a very competent policy evaluation. 

**Single Most Impactful Advice:** Transform the paper from a "Policy Evaluation of Transparency" into a "Study of Firm Bargaining Power." Use the tension between your *negative* earnings result and the *positive* posting results in the literature to argue that firms are strategically using the mandates to anchor expectations lower. If you can show that firms with the most "opaque" previous hiring (high dispersion) are the ones driving the earnings dip, you have a top-5 paper.
