## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether the IRA’s “energy community” bonus credit—a large extra subsidy for clean energy projects in fossil-fuel-dependent, high-unemployment places—has begun to create jobs in the communities it was meant to help. The headline answer is: not yet; two years in, the paper finds no detectable increase in construction or utilities employment, suggesting either that place-based green subsidies are too weak to redirect investment or, more plausibly in the paper’s telling, that the policy’s employment effects operate on a longer timeline than politicians and commentators assume.

A busy economist should care because this is one of the flagship place-based provisions of the IRA and a test case for “just transition” policy: can decarbonization policy actually deliver visible labor-market gains in distressed fossil-fuel regions?

**Does the paper itself articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Mostly, but not sharply enough. The opening has a nice policy hook, but it spends too much time on institutional detail before making the broader stakes unmistakable. The current intro says “this is the first quasi-experimental estimate,” which is fine, but that is not the main reason anyone will care. The reason to care is that this provision is a high-profile real-world test of whether climate policy can double as regional development policy.

**What the first two paragraphs should say instead:**

> The Inflation Reduction Act asks climate policy to do more than reduce emissions: it asks it to help the places most exposed to the decline of fossil fuels. Its “energy community” bonus credit gives clean energy developers substantially larger tax credits if they locate projects in fossil-fuel-dependent, high-unemployment communities. The central question is whether this place-based climate subsidy has begun to deliver what its political logic promises: jobs in distressed energy regions.
>
> This paper provides early evidence on that question. Using county-level employment data and the policy’s time-varying eligibility rules, I estimate the effect of energy community designation on local employment. The headline result is stark: through early 2025, designated counties show no detectable gains in construction or utilities employment. The broader implication is not simply that the policy “doesn’t work,” but that place-based clean energy subsidies may operate on a much slower timetable than contemporary policy debates assume.

That is the pitch the paper should have. Lead with the world question, not the design.

**What would make this contribution bigger? Be specific.**  
Three ways:

At present, the paper is trying to sell a null employment result as a complete answer. It is more naturally a paper about **evaluation timing and policy transmission**.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
The world before this paper: the IRA created a major place-based clean energy subsidy intended to steer investment toward fossil-fuel-dependent communities, and there is intense policy interest in whether climate policy can produce a “just transition.”

### Tension
We do not know whether such subsidies actually generate local jobs where promised, especially in the short run. The policy is politically marketed as immediate community support, but the underlying investment process in energy infrastructure may be slow and constrained.

### Resolution
The paper finds no detectable short-run employment gains in construction or utilities in designated counties through early 2025.

### Implications
We should either lower expectations about what place-based clean energy subsidies can accomplish, or at minimum rethink the timeline on which they should be evaluated. The policy may not be failing; it may be mismatched to the speed of local economic distress.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the ingredients, but the arc is not fully controlled. The paper oscillates between two stories:

1. **Substantive story:** the policy has not yet created jobs.
2. **Defensive story:** the negative estimates may partly reflect selection and pre-existing decline, so don’t over-read them.

That creates a tonal problem. The paper wants to claim an economically important null, but also repeatedly tells the reader why the estimates should not be taken too literally. As a result, the narrative becomes unstable.

In its current form, it feels somewhat like **a collection of sensible results looking for a sharper story**.

### What story should it be telling?

The best story is:

- The IRA energy community bonus is a high-stakes test of place-based climate policy.
- The first observable margin policymakers care about is jobs.
- The early job response is absent.
- That absence is informative because it reveals a central tension in just-transition design: the communities’ need is immediate, but the investment response is slow and mediated by transmission, permitting, and development frictions.

That story can absorb the null and make it intellectually meaningful. The paper should be less preoccupied with proving that every coefficient has one exact interpretation and more focused on the broader lesson about **policy timing and transmission**.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?

That is the lead fact.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

Some would lean in—especially labor, public, urban, and environmental economists—because the IRA and just transition are live topics. But many would quickly ask: “Is that because the policy doesn’t work, or because two years is way too early and employment is the wrong margin?”

That follow-up question arrives immediately, which tells you both the promise and the limitation of the current draft.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Almost certainly:
- “Do designated places get more projects, permits, or queue entries even if jobs haven’t shown up yet?”
- Or: “How much of this is just selection into distress?”
- Or: “Why should I care about a short-run null in county employment if the mechanism is long-run capital deployment?”

That last question is the key strategic vulnerability.

### If the findings are null or modest: is the null itself interesting?

Yes, but only if the paper makes a stronger case that the null is substantively revealing. Right now it partly does. The null is interesting because:
- this is a politically salient policy,
- the absence of short-run local labor effects is itself important,
- and it speaks to a general design problem in place-based transition policy.

But the paper still risks feeling like **a failed early evaluation** rather than **a successful paper about premature evaluation**. To cross that line, it needs either stronger mechanism evidence or much sharper framing.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

Be honest: in current form, this is **not yet an AER paper**. The main gap is not polish; it is strategic ambition.

### What is the gap?

Primarily a **scope and framing problem**, with some **novelty risk**.

- **Framing problem:** The paper has not yet decided whether it is about a tax credit’s employment effect, about early evaluation of the IRA, or about the fundamental mismatch between place-based transition policy and the timelines of capital deployment.
- **Scope problem:** County employment alone is too narrow a lens for a policy whose first effects likely appear in project pipelines, siting decisions, permitting, or queue activity.
- **Novelty problem:** “First DiD on a recent policy, finding no short-run employment effect” is publishable somewhere, but by itself it is unlikely to excite the top people in public/environmental/urban.
- **Ambition problem:** The paper is competent and sensible, but a bit safe. It does not yet convert a timely policy evaluation into a broader economic insight.

### What would excite the top 10 people in this field?

A paper that shows one of the following:
1. The policy **did** redirect project location, but not yet jobs.
2. The policy **did not** redirect even early-stage development, because transmission/permitting/site fundamentals dominate the subsidy.
3. The policy’s effects are **heterogeneous in exactly the way economic theory would predict**—e.g., stronger where interconnection capacity exists, weaker where labor supply constraints bind.
4. The policy reveals a deeper principle: **place-based subsidies are poorly matched to transitions driven by capital-intensive, infrastructure-constrained investment.**

Any of those is more important than the current narrow message.

### Single most impactful piece of advice

**If the author changes only one thing, it should be this:**  
Reframe the paper around the broader question of whether place-based climate policy can generate local economic adjustment on policymakers’ desired timeline, and add at least one upstream investment-margin outcome (project siting, queue entry, announced investment, permitting, or starts) so the paper can distinguish “no jobs yet” from “no response at all.”

That one move would make the story much bigger.
