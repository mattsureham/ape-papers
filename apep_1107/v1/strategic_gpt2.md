## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper studies a striking policy change in Romania that, overnight, shifted almost all payroll tax liability from employers to employees while leaving the overall labor tax wedge roughly unchanged. Using this episode, the paper asks whether changing who is legally responsible for the tax changes the economic composition of compensation; its core claim is that it does not: employer-side non-wage costs fell, gross wages rose, and the compensation package mechanically re-labeled rather than economically transformed.

A busy economist should care because this is a clean, vivid test of a foundational proposition in public finance: statutory incidence should not matter for economic incidence. The episode is unusually large and visually intuitive, which gives the paper potential as a memorable “textbook prediction meets real-world shock” contribution.

**Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Mostly, but not optimally. The current introduction is too eager to announce that it is “testing the statutory incidence irrelevance proposition,” which makes the paper sound like a narrow confirmation exercise. The more compelling pitch is not “we test a textbook proposition again,” but “Romania conducted an extreme real-world relabeling of labor compensation, allowing us to see whether firms and workers experience a relabeling or a real change.”

The first two paragraphs should do three things more clearly:
1. Lead with the extraordinary policy fact.
2. State the world question, not the literature gap.
3. Be upfront that the episode is informative about the **composition of recorded labor costs**, not necessarily about decentralized market adjustment.

### The pitch the paper should have

“Can governments change the economics of labor compensation simply by changing which side of the payslip is labeled as paying payroll taxes? In 2018, Romania abruptly moved almost the entire payroll tax burden from employers to employees while keeping the overall wedge on labor roughly unchanged and requiring firms to adjust gross wages. This reform provides an unusually stark test of whether statutory incidence changes real labor costs or merely their accounting form.

Using Eurostat labor cost data and comparisons to neighboring Central and Eastern European economies, I show that Romania’s reform produced an immediate collapse in employer-side non-wage labor costs and a corresponding rise in gross wages, with the total compensation package largely re-composed rather than re-priced. The broader lesson is that, in this setting, payroll tax labels matter enormously for recorded wage components but much less for the underlying economic cost of labor.”

That is stronger, cleaner, and more honest about what the design can speak to.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:**  
The paper shows that Romania’s near-complete overnight reassignment of payroll tax liability from employers to employees sharply changed the recorded composition of labor compensation but did not appear to materially alter the underlying total cost of labor, consistent with statutory incidence irrelevance in a highly salient, policy-driven setting.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3-4 papers in the literature?
Only partly. The paper names Gruber, Saez, and some European payroll tax studies, but the differentiation is still thinner than it should be. Right now the distinction is basically: “this was bigger and faster.” That is not enough by itself. The paper needs to say more precisely:

- Prior papers mainly study **rate changes**.
- This paper studies a **relabeling shock**: same wedge, different statutory payer.
- Prior papers are often informative about **economic incidence and employment effects**.
- This paper is primarily informative about the **administrative and accounting transmission of incidence into wage vs non-wage components**.

That distinction is meaningful. But the paper currently oscillates between claiming a grand test of incidence theory and admitting that the gross wage mandate makes it a weaker test of market incidence. That unresolved tension blurs the contribution.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or as filling a gap in a LITERATURE?
It begins with a world question, which is good, but it repeatedly reverts to “testing a textbook proposition” and “contributing to the payroll tax incidence literature.” That is weaker. The stronger framing is:

- **World question:** When a government radically re-labels payroll taxes, what actually changes for firms and workers?
- **Implication:** Payroll tax reforms that only shift statutory assignment may mostly rewrite accounting categories rather than alter labor costs.

That is better than “there are few papers on extreme statutory shifts.”

### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what’s new?
At the moment, maybe, but not cleanly. They would probably say: “It’s a DiD on Romania’s payroll tax reform showing wages up and employer contributions down, mostly confirming incidence irrelevance.” That is not yet an AER-level takeaway. The paper needs the novelty distilled into one line:

> “This is not another payroll tax cut paper; it is a paper about a government reassigning the legal payer while holding the wedge roughly fixed, and showing that compensation components re-label almost one-for-one.”

That is memorable. “Another DiD paper about X” is currently too plausible a summary.

### What would make this contribution bigger?
Most importantly, **reframe the estimand**. The biggest opportunity is to stop overselling this as a pure test of competitive incidence theory and instead sell it as evidence on the **malleability of compensation labeling under statutory reform**.

Specific ways to make it bigger:
- **Different outcome framing:** Put the non-wage share / compensation composition at the center from page 1. That is the distinctive outcome.
- **Mechanism:** Explicitly separate “market incidence” from “administrative pass-through under a wage-adjustment mandate.” This does not weaken the paper; it clarifies what is actually interesting.
- **Different comparison:** Compare Romania not just to peers, but conceptually to other reforms where the total wedge changed. The paper’s edge is the near-pure relabeling.
- **Different implication:** Speak to labor market institutions, pay transparency, and how tax systems shape reported wages versus total labor costs. That gives the paper reach beyond a narrow public finance audience.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

### Closest neighbors
The closest neighbors seem to be:

1. **Gruber (1997)** on the incidence of payroll taxation in Chile.
2. **Saez, Schoefer, and Seim (2019)** on payroll taxes / incidence and employment in Sweden.
3. **Anderson and Meyer / German social contribution incidence papers** — the paper cites Anderson (2013), presumably in that vein.
4. **Lehmann et al. / French CSG-type reforms** involving shifts between employer and employee contributions.
5. Broader tax salience and labor taxation work, perhaps **Chetty, Looney, and Kroft (2009)**, though that is more distant.

### How should the paper position itself relative to those neighbors?
**Build on them, not attack them.** The paper should say:

- Gruber and Saez-type papers show what happens when payroll taxes change and markets absorb them.
- This paper studies a different object: a reform that mainly changes **who is legally assigned the payment**, under explicit administrative coordination.
- Therefore, the paper complements the classic literature by showing how a large statutory relabeling maps into observed compensation components.

The right stance is not “we provide the strongest test of incidence theory.” That claim invites pushback and is not really supported by the paper’s own institutional details. The right stance is “we observe the most extreme real-world reclassification of payroll taxation, which is useful for understanding what statutory incidence does and does not change.”

### Is the paper currently positioned too narrowly or too broadly?
Paradoxically, both.

- **Too broadly** in claiming to speak to the deep theorem of tax incidence in general.
- **Too narrowly** in presenting itself as a Romania/CEE natural experiment.

The sweet spot is: a paper about payroll tax design, compensation structure, and statutory labeling, using Romania as a uniquely sharp case.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?
It should probably connect more explicitly to:
- **Labor market institutions / wage setting / payroll administration**
- **Tax salience and paycheck framing**
- **Public economics of tax remittance and withholding**
- Possibly **accounting/measurement issues in labor compensation data**

The non-wage share result is really a measurement and institutional design result as much as a canonical incidence result. The paper should lean into that.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?
Not quite. It is currently trying to have the “classic incidence theorem” conversation. The more fruitful conversation may be:

- How do tax reforms change the *presentation* of compensation?
- How much of labor taxation is economics versus reporting architecture?
- Under what institutions can governments reorganize payroll taxation without changing labor demand?

That is a fresher conversation, and one that could attract broader interest.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Before this paper, economists generally believe that statutory incidence should not determine economic incidence: what matters is elasticities and the total wedge, not whether employers or employees remit the tax. Most empirical evidence comes from rate changes, not from extreme statutory reassignment.

### Tension
Romania implemented an unusually dramatic reform that appears, on its face, to shift a large burden from firms to workers. But did this change real labor costs, or just the labels on compensation? And because the reform included a wage-adjustment mandate, does the episode reveal market incidence, administrative incidence, or simply accounting compliance?

### Resolution
Recorded employer-side non-wage costs collapse, gross wages rise, and the non-wage share of compensation shifts immediately and persistently. The compensation package appears to be re-composed, not fundamentally re-priced.

### Implications
For policymakers, reforms that only reassign payroll tax liability may largely change payslip presentation rather than economic burden. For economists, the episode suggests that statutory assignment can be almost irrelevant for labor-cost composition even under a highly salient, administratively coordinated reform.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
It has a decent arc, but it is not fully disciplined. The main problem is that the paper wants two stories at once:

1. “This is a major test of incidence theory.”
2. “This is really about administrative restructuring under a mandate.”

Those are not the same story. The paper acknowledges this in the threats/discussion sections, but too late. As a result, the narrative feels slightly like a collection of strong descriptive results wrapped in a theory claim that is a bit too ambitious.

### What story should it be telling?
The best story is:

> Governments can radically rewrite the statutory architecture of payroll taxation, and when they do, observed labor compensation re-sorts across wage and non-wage components almost instantly. Romania shows that payroll tax allocation is, to a large extent, an administrative choice in form, even when the economics of total labor cost barely move.

That is a coherent story. It is also more original than “we confirm a theorem.”

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would you lead with?
“Romania moved almost the entire payroll tax burden from employers to employees overnight, and the employer share of compensation immediately collapsed by roughly 35 percentage points while gross wages jumped.”

That is a good opening fact. It is concrete, surprising, and memorable.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
They would initially lean in because the reform is so visually dramatic. But the second sentence matters enormously. If the takeaway is merely “textbook incidence holds,” many will lose interest quickly because that sounds confirmatory. If the takeaway is “this was essentially a state-engineered relabeling of compensation, showing how little statutory assignment matters for total labor costs and how much it matters for reported wage structure,” they will stay engaged.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Almost certainly:  
**“But because the government mandated wage adjustments, what exactly have you learned about incidence rather than compliance?”**

That is the central strategic issue. The paper should anticipate it and own the answer: we learn a lot about the relationship between statutory assignment and measured compensation structure, less about unconstrained market adjustment.

This follow-up does not kill the paper. But if the paper keeps presenting itself as a strong test of competitive incidence theory, that follow-up becomes fatal rather than manageable.

### If the findings are modest or null...
Not applicable here; the findings are large. The issue is not lack of effect. The issue is whether the effect is economically deep or mechanically obvious. The paper needs to persuade readers that “mechanically obvious” is still informative because governments, workers, and analysts often talk as if statutory payer matters in first-order ways.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

At present, the gap is mainly a **framing and ambition problem**, with some **novelty-risk**.

The paper has a genuinely striking institutional episode and very large estimated effects. That is a real asset. But the current manuscript undersells itself by sounding like a narrow confirmation of a theorem, while simultaneously overselling itself by implying it cleanly tests competitive tax incidence. That mismatch leaves it in an awkward middle ground.

### What is the main problem?
Mostly **framing**, secondarily **ambition**.

- **Framing problem:** The paper is not yet asking the most interesting question it can answer.
- **Ambition problem:** It is content to show the obvious first-order response and call that enough.
- **Novelty problem:** Many readers will say, “Of course employer contributions fell and gross wages rose if the law redefined them and firms were told to adjust wages.” Unless the paper reframes that “obviousness” into an important insight about compensation architecture, the novelty will feel thin.

### What would excite the top 10 people in this field?
A paper that says something like:

> “This episode isolates statutory reassignment from total wedge changes and shows that payroll tax systems powerfully shape the recorded decomposition of compensation while leaving the underlying labor cost largely intact. That sharpens our understanding of what payroll tax reforms really do: they often transform labels, transparency, and incidence perceptions more than economic costs.”

That would feel more original and more consequential.

### Single most impactful piece of advice
**Stop selling the paper as the strongest test of tax incidence theory, and instead sell it as a paper about how radically statutory payroll tax reassignment changes the measured composition and visibility of compensation while leaving underlying labor costs largely unchanged.**

That one change would improve the title, abstract, introduction, literature review, and conclusion all at once.
