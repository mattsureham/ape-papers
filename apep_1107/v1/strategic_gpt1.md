## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper studies Romania’s 2018 reform that almost completely shifted payroll tax remittance from employers to employees overnight, while keeping the overall labor tax wedge roughly unchanged. Using cross-country labor cost data, it shows that the recorded composition of compensation shifted mechanically and immediately: employer-side non-wage costs fell, gross wages rose, and the employer/employee statutory split appears largely irrelevant for measured labor costs.

Why should a busy economist care? In principle, because payroll tax incidence is a foundational public finance question, and a near-total overnight statutory reversal is unusually stark. In practice, the current paper undersells the real issue and oversells the result: the interesting question is not “does accounting reclassification show up in labor cost components?” but “what, if anything, do such extreme statutory relabelings change in the real economy, in bargaining, salience, or policy design?”

### Does the paper articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Not well enough. The introduction currently opens with a classic-tax-incidence framing, but by paragraph two it is already clear that this was not a clean market test of incidence: the government also mandated gross-wage adjustments. That makes the current headline claim—“the textbook prediction holds”—feel too strong relative to the setting. A sophisticated reader will immediately wonder whether the paper is just documenting an accounting relabeling plus compliance with an administrative directive.

### The pitch the paper should have

“Governments often relabel payroll taxes between employers and employees while claiming to change firms’ costs or workers’ burdens. Romania’s 2018 reform provides an extreme case: nearly the entire employer payroll contribution was shifted onto employees overnight, under an accompanying mandate to adjust gross wages. We show that such relabeling can dramatically change the recorded composition of compensation without materially changing the underlying labor cost wedge—highlighting the distinction between statutory assignment, administrative presentation, and economic substance.”

That is a more honest and more interesting pitch. It is weaker than the current “textbook incidence confirmed” line, but stronger strategically because it better matches what the paper actually shows.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper documents that Romania’s near-complete overnight reassignment of payroll tax liability from employers to employees led to an immediate and persistent relabeling of compensation from employer non-wage costs into gross wages, with little evidence that the overall cost structure changed beyond that compositional shift.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest papers?

Only partially. The paper names Gruber, Saez et al., and related payroll-tax incidence papers, but the differentiation is not yet persuasive because those papers ask about economic incidence and real margins, whereas this paper mostly shows a compositional/accounting response in a setting with a wage-adjustment mandate. The distinction is there, but it currently weakens rather than strengthens the paper, because the introduction keeps insisting it is a major test of incidence theory instead of leaning into the fact that it is a different object.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a gap in a literature?

Right now it is framed mostly as a literature move: “here is an extreme test of a classic proposition.” That is weaker. The stronger world-facing question is: **When governments reassign payroll taxes across the payslip without changing the total wedge, what actually changes—economic burdens, labor costs, salience, or just accounting categories?**

That is a much better question.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?

At the moment, they would probably say: “It’s another payroll tax incidence paper, but in Romania, with an extreme reform and aggregate data.” That is not enough for AER. Worse, some would say: “It’s a DiD showing that when the government tells firms to move payroll contributions onto workers and raise gross wages, the wage/non-wage split changes.” That sounds mechanical.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

The paper needs one of the following:

1. **A different outcome margin**  
   Move beyond labor-cost composition into outcomes that are not definitional: employment, vacancies, separations, hours, firm exits, wage bargaining outcomes, net wages, compliance, informal work, tax salience, worker perceptions, or political support.

2. **A different mechanism framing**  
   Reframe around **administrative versus economic incidence**, **tax salience**, or **wage-setting institutions**. The interesting mechanism is not market incidence per se; it is whether formal statutory relabeling changes anything once institutions intervene.

3. **A different comparison**  
   Compare Romania to other episodes where payroll tax burdens shifted nominally across sides of the market, ideally in a systematic cross-country framework. A one-off event can be compelling, but only if it teaches something more general.

4. **A different headline claim**  
   The current headline is too close to “we verified a textbook identity.” The bigger claim would be something like: **Statutory reassignment can generate huge visible changes in payslips and official labor-cost components while leaving the underlying wedge largely intact; therefore policymakers and researchers should not read statutory payroll structures as economically meaningful without considering institutional transmission.**

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

### Closest neighbors

The obvious neighbors are:

- **Gruber (1997)** on payroll tax incidence in Chile  
- **Saez, Schoefer, and Seim (2019)** on payroll taxes and labor market outcomes in Sweden  
- **Anderson and Meyer / German payroll tax incidence papers** — the cited Anderson et al. paper is in this space  
- **Chetty, Looney, and Kroft (2009)** on salience, though this is a looser connection  
- Broader cross-country payroll tax/wedge work, possibly including **Lehmann et al.** and comparative European tax reform papers

### How should the paper position itself relative to those neighbors?

It should **not attack** them and should stop pretending it is a stronger version of them. It should say:

- Those papers estimate **economic incidence and real adjustments** under payroll tax changes.
- This paper studies **administrative reassignment of statutory incidence** under a near-pure relabeling, in a setting with direct institutional coordination.
- Therefore it is **complementary**, not superior: it speaks to how far statutory form can move independently of economic substance.

That is a cleaner lane.

### Is the paper positioned too narrowly or too broadly?

Oddly, both.

- **Too broadly** in claiming to resolve a foundational incidence proposition.
- **Too narrowly** in execution, because the outcome is labor-cost composition in one country over a short horizon.

The result is a mismatch between ambition and evidentiary object.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?

It should speak more directly to:

- **Tax salience and statutory labeling**  
  Not just Chetty in a cursory way, but the broader idea that who is nominally charged can matter for perception, bargaining, and politics even if not for equilibrium incidence.

- **Wage-setting and labor market institutions**  
  Since the reform came with a gross-wage mandate and a simultaneous minimum wage increase, the natural literature is on institutions and wage pass-through, not just canonical tax incidence.

- **Public economics of remittance and enforcement**  
  There is a broader literature on who remits taxes versus who bears them. The distinction between remittance, statutory assignment, and incidence is exactly where this paper should live.

- **Political economy / fiscal illusion**  
  The reform’s motivation and optics matter. If firms’ apparent tax burden falls and employees’ visible deductions rise, that may have political or behavioral consequences even if total burden does not change much.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?

Not yet. The most impactful conversation is not “another payroll tax incidence test.” It is: **What does statutory tax reassignment actually do in a world of institutions, salience, and political presentation?** That is a more original conversation, and one this paper is better suited to join.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Textbook public finance says statutory incidence should not matter for economic incidence; many policy debates nevertheless focus heavily on whether employers or employees “pay” payroll taxes.

### Tension

Romania offers a dramatic case: nearly all employer-side payroll taxes were shifted to employees overnight. But this was not a clean market experiment; it came with a wage-adjustment mandate and other institutional changes. So the real puzzle is whether this event reveals economic incidence, mere relabeling, or something deeper about institutions and tax presentation.

### Resolution

The paper finds an immediate, persistent shift in the observed composition of labor costs: employer non-wage costs collapse, wages rise, and the non-wage share falls sharply. In other words, statutory reassignment strongly changes how compensation is recorded, while not obviously changing the underlying total wedge.

### Implications

The current paper says the implication is “textbook incidence is confirmed.” I do not buy that as the central implication. The stronger implication is: **statutory payroll tax assignments can be dramatically re-engineered on paper with surprisingly little change in economic substance, especially when institutions coordinate the transition.** That matters for policy communication, tax harmonization debates, and how researchers interpret employer versus employee contribution rates.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has a serviceable arc, but not yet a convincing one. Right now it feels like a collection of big coefficient estimates attached to a too-familiar textbook proposition. The actual story is more nuanced and more interesting than the current packaging allows.

### What story should it be telling?

It should be telling a story about the gap between **statutory form and economic substance**. Romania is an unusually vivid demonstration that the employer/employee split of payroll taxation may be largely a matter of administrative presentation, not real labor-cost economics—especially under coordinated institutional implementation. That story is broader, more policy-relevant, and less vulnerable to the obvious critique that the paper is proving something mechanical.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party?

“Romania shifted almost the entire payroll tax from employers to employees overnight, and official labor-cost data show the employer side of compensation collapsed while gross wages jumped almost immediately.”

That is a good opener. People will lean in initially because the reform is extreme and unusual.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

They lean in for the reform. They reach for their phones if the conclusion is just “and therefore statutory incidence is irrelevant,” because many will think: “Well yes, plus there was a mandate, so what exactly did we learn?”

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Almost certainly: **“Did anything real happen—employment, net pay, bargaining, informality, worker perceptions, firm behavior—or is this just accounting relabeling?”**

That is the question the paper currently cannot answer, and it is the question standing between this being an interesting note and an AER paper.

### If findings are modest or mechanical, is that itself interesting?

Here the result is not null, but it is close to mechanical. Mechanical results can still be interesting if they overturn common policy rhetoric. The paper could make that case better: policymakers often talk as if shifting payroll taxes from employers to employees changes firms’ costs or workers’ burdens in a deep sense. Showing that it mostly changed the payslip and official decomposition is useful. But to carry an AER-level paper, the authors need to demonstrate why that insight matters beyond confirming a stylized fact.

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

1. **Front-load the real contribution**
   The introduction should state much earlier that this is fundamentally about **statutory reassignment under institutional enforcement**, not a pure market-incidence experiment.

2. **Trim the self-congratulatory “extreme test” language**
   Phrases like “the most extreme test in modern economic history” and repeated emphasis on gigantic effect sizes read as compensating for limited conceptual reach. The extremeness of the reform is useful; the rhetoric is overdone.

3. **Move some inference/technical detail out of the introduction**
   The introduction currently spends too much space on identification and permutation details before the reader is convinced the question matters. Referees can deal with that. The first pages need to sell the problem.

4. **Clarify the object of interest**
   The paper toggles between wages, non-wage costs, labor-cost composition, incidence theory, and policy harmonization. Pick one central object. It should probably be the **composition of compensation under statutory reassignment**.

5. **Do not bury the conceptual caveat**
   The wage mandate is the central interpretive fact, not a limitation to be dealt with later. It belongs near the front, because it defines the paper.

6. **Shorten the institutional background**
   Keep what matters for interpretation: tax rates, mandate, minimum wage increase, political purpose. Cut anything else.

7. **Rework the conclusion**
   The current conclusion mostly summarizes and overstates. It should instead do more conceptual work: distinguish remittance, statutory incidence, administrative incidence, and economic incidence; explain what Romania can and cannot tell us.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

At present, this is **farther from AER than the authors seem to think**.

### What is the main gap?

Mostly a **framing problem**, but also a **scope problem**.

- **Framing problem:** The paper claims to test a grand proposition about incidence, but the evidence is better suited to a narrower and different claim about statutory relabeling and administrative implementation.
- **Scope problem:** The outcomes are too close to the reform’s accounting mechanics. There is not enough on real behavior, perceptions, or broader implications.
- **Novelty problem:** Payroll tax incidence is a mature literature. “We also find statutory assignment doesn’t matter” is not enough, especially in a case where statutory reassignment was accompanied by a mandate.
- **Ambition problem:** The paper is competent, but conceptually safe. It takes a dramatic reform and asks a relatively small question of it.

### What would excite the top 10 people in this field?

One of two papers:

1. **A broader paper on statutory reassignment, salience, and real outcomes**  
   Same Romanian event, but linked to employment, worker net pay, firm-level responses, informality, bargaining, or political perceptions.

2. **A conceptual paper on statutory vs economic incidence across multiple reforms**  
   Romania as the extreme case, embedded in a broader comparative analysis showing when relabeling matters and when it does not.

### Single most impactful piece of advice

**Stop selling this as a clean confirmation of textbook tax incidence, and instead recast it around the distinction between statutory labeling and economic substance—then add at least one genuinely non-accounting outcome that shows why that distinction matters.**

That is the one change that could most improve the paper’s trajectory.
