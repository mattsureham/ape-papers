## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple, important question: when Europe coordinated the largest-ever restriction on corporate interest deductibility, did the aggregate corporate sector actually become less debt-reliant? Using cross-country variation in how tightly EU member states implemented ATAD, the paper’s headline claim is no: despite a major coordinated reform aimed at reducing the tax bias toward debt, economy-wide debt measures barely moved.

Why should a busy economist care? Because the paper is not really about one EU directive; it is about whether a textbook tax distortion that is easy to detect at the firm level can in fact be corrected at the macro level through coordinated policy. If the answer is no, that potentially changes how we think about tax coordination, corporate finance distortions, and the gap between micro compliance responses and aggregate outcomes.

**Does the paper itself articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Mostly yes. The introduction is better than average and gets to the point quickly. The first two paragraphs already contain the right ingredients: a first-order distortion, a major policy intervention, and a high-stakes test. That said, the current version is still a bit too “public finance seminar” and not enough “big economics question.” It starts from debt bias as a known literature and only later gets to the more interesting issue: whether coordinated tax reform can change macro outcomes rather than just firm-level behavior.

**What the first two paragraphs should say instead:**

> Corporate tax systems favor debt over equity, and decades of firm-level evidence show that firms respond to this incentive. But a deeper question remains unresolved: can policy actually reduce the aggregate debt bias of an economy, or do firms simply reorganize around the rule while total leverage barely changes?
>
> The EU’s Anti-Tax Avoidance Directive provides an unusually sharp test. Beginning in 2019, member states were required to cap deductible interest at 30 percent of EBITDA, making ATAD the largest coordinated attempt to curb debt bias in history. This paper asks whether that reform changed the financing structure of Europe’s nonfinancial corporate sector at the country level. The answer is no large aggregate effect: despite coordinated reform, debt-related aggregate outcomes remain essentially flat.

That version puts the world question first.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution in one sentence:**  
The paper’s contribution is to show that the EU’s coordinated cap on interest deductibility appears to have had little or no detectable effect on the aggregate financing structure of nonfinancial corporations across member states.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?
Only partly. The paper distinguishes itself from the firm-level tax/leverage literature by emphasizing aggregate national accounts and from ATAD papers by emphasizing macro rather than firm-level outcomes. That is a real distinction. But it is not yet differentiated sharply enough in terms of **what we learn that those papers could not tell us**.

Right now the contribution risks sounding like: “existing papers study firm-level effects; I study country-level effects and find a null.” That is not enough for AER. The paper needs to argue more forcefully that this is not just a change in level of aggregation, but a different scientific question: whether micro tax incentives aggregate into macro financial structure once equilibrium substitution and cross-border reallocation are allowed.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or filling a gap in a LITERATURE?
It is mixed, but currently too often framed as a literature gap: “first aggregate study,” “firm-level studies use Orbis,” etc. That is weaker. The stronger framing is a world question:

- Do coordinated anti-debt-bias reforms change macro leverage?
- Does tax coordination solve distortions that unilateral reforms cannot?
- Can firm-level tax responses fail to translate into aggregate outcomes?

That is the framing to lean into.

### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what’s new?
A smart economist could probably say: “It studies ATAD using aggregate EU data and finds no big effect on leverage.” That is understandable, but it is still perilously close to “another DiD paper about an EU reform.” The paper is not yet memorable enough.

The memorable version is: **“One of the biggest coordinated anti-avoidance reforms in the world changed the rules but not the aggregate balance sheet.”** That is a stronger identity.

### What would make this contribution bigger?
Several possibilities:

1. **Different outcome framing:**  
   Move from “interest/GOS ratio, debt composition, leverage” to the broader concept of **macro-financial fragility**. If the policy motivation is not simply tax compliance but reducing debt bias and fragility, then outcomes like refinancing exposure, debt service burden during rate hikes, insolvency rates, or cyclicality of investment would make the contribution more consequential.

2. **Mechanism evidence:**  
   The most important missing strategic element is evidence for the “phantom correction” idea. Right now it is a phrase, not yet a demonstrated mechanism. If the paper could show that firm-level tax-planning margins changed while aggregate leverage did not, it would become much more interesting. As written, the mechanism is speculative.

3. **Sharper comparison to unilateral reforms:**  
   The paper hints that coordinated reform should succeed where unilateral reforms failed. That comparison could be much more explicit. If earlier country-specific reforms produced detectable firm-level leverage changes but coordinated EU-wide reform did not move aggregate debt, that contrast is a much bigger point.

4. **Broader policy implication:**  
   The paper currently jumps to ACE in the discussion. That could be promising, but only if the paper is reframed as comparing two conceptual approaches: penalizing debt versus subsidizing equity. Otherwise it feels bolted on.

### How should the paper position itself relative to those neighbors?
**Build on them, not attack them.**  
The right message is not that the firm-level literature is wrong. It is that firm-level responsiveness is not the same thing as aggregate correction. The paper should present itself as the missing macro counterpart to a literature that has convincingly established micro elasticities.

A strong positioning formula would be:

- Prior work shows firms react to interest deductibility.
- Prior work also shows unilateral reforms can be arbitraged around.
- ATAD is the cleanest large-scale test of whether coordinated reform can change aggregate leverage.
- It appears not to.

That is a natural progression, not a fight.

### Is the paper currently positioned too narrowly or too broadly?
Currently it is **a bit too narrowly positioned** as an ATAD evaluation paper, with some broad claims sprinkled in. For AER, it needs to be the reverse: a broad economics paper using ATAD as the setting.

Right audience should be:
- public finance,
- corporate finance,
- international taxation,
- macro-finance/political economy of regulation.

At present, the introduction is still mostly speaking to public finance/tax specialists.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of or under-engaged with?
Two areas feel underdeveloped:

1. **Macro-finance / financial stability literature.**  
   If the motivating concern is aggregate debt bias and fragility, the paper should speak more directly to macro-financial stability, not just capital structure tax papers.

2. **Policy evaluation / micro-macro disconnect literature.**  
   There is a general economics theme here: policies can induce micro reoptimization without changing aggregate quantities. That conversation could include labor, trade, IO, and public finance analogies. The paper does not need to review those literatures, but the framing could borrow that logic.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?
Not quite. The paper is currently having the conversation: “What was ATAD’s effect?”  
The more important conversation is: **“When do major coordinated policies fail to move aggregate behavior despite changing incentives?”**

That is the unexpected literature bridge that could raise its ceiling.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Before this paper, economists believe:
- the tax code favors debt over equity;
- firms respond to those incentives;
- unilateral reforms are often undermined by cross-border shifting;
- therefore coordinated reform should, in principle, be more effective.

### Tension
The unresolved puzzle is whether eliminating avoidance opportunities and coordinating across countries is enough to change the aggregate debt-equity mix, or whether the distortion is too embedded in financing practices, legal structures, and general equilibrium responses.

### Resolution
ATAD, despite being large and coordinated, does not appear to generate a detectable aggregate reduction in interest burden, debt composition, or leverage.

### Implications
That would imply one of two important things:
- either coordinated interest-limitation rules mainly affect margins too small to matter for aggregate leverage,
- or firms offset the reform through reallocation/restructuring so that the intended macro correction never arrives.

That matters for tax policy design and for how economists translate micro tax elasticities into macro welfare claims.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
**Serviceable, but not fully disciplined.**  
The paper has the ingredients of a strong story, but the story is diluted by too much method/result narration and not enough conceptual tension. The phrase “phantom correction” is actually the paper’s best narrative asset. The paper should commit to it much earlier and define it clearly: a reform that changes statutory incentives, and perhaps some compliance behavior, without altering aggregate balance-sheet outcomes.

Right now there is also some internal tension in the storytelling:
- The main finding is a clean aggregate null.
- But the discussion then introduces a marginally significant subgroup result for the 3M countries.
- Then it speculates linear dose may be wrong.
- Then it pivots to ACE.

This makes the paper feel a bit like a collection of sensible observations rather than a tightly controlled narrative.

**The story it should be telling:**  
“Europe coordinated the canonical fix to debt bias. If tax coordination can solve this distortion, we should see it here. We do not. The natural interpretation is not that firms ignore tax rules, but that aggregate debt bias is harder to move than micro evidence suggests.”

That is a coherent AER-style arc.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party?
“I’d lead with: Europe implemented the largest coordinated cap on corporate interest deductions ever, across 27 countries, and the aggregate corporate sector’s debt reliance barely budged.”

That is the fact.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
**Lean in initially**, because the policy is large and the result is counterintuitive relative to a large micro literature. But the interest will evaporate quickly if the follow-up is merely “country-level DiD with nulls on a few ratios.” The paper’s job is to make the audience feel that the null is deeply informative, not just statistically clean.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Likely one of these:
- “So are firms adjusting in ways that don’t show up in aggregate leverage?”
- “Does this mean firm-level tax elasticities don’t aggregate?”
- “Is the reform too weak, or is debt bias just not tractable through deductibility caps?”
- “What should policymakers do instead?”

Those are excellent follow-up questions. The paper should orient itself around answering at least one of them better.

### Are the null findings interesting?
Yes, potentially very much so. But null papers only work when:
1. the intervention is unquestionably important,
2. the design is informative enough to rule out economically meaningful effects,
3. the interpretation is substantive rather than defensive.

This paper has (1), partly has (2), and is trying for (3). The line about ruling out large aggregate effects is useful. The paper is strongest when it says, in effect, **“the reform did not produce even a moderate aggregate correction.”** It is weakest when it sounds like, **“there might still be lots of small effects, and maybe the timing was short, and maybe the dose was mismeasured.”** Too much hedging undermines the strategic force of the null.

AER can publish nulls, but only if the paper owns the null as an answer to a major question.

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

### What would make it read better?

1. **Front-load the core finding earlier.**  
   The current introduction is solid, but the best sentence in the paper is conceptually this: *the largest coordinated anti-debt-bias reform did not change aggregate corporate financing.* That should appear even earlier and more starkly.

2. **Shorten the literature parade in paragraph 1.**  
   Opening with Modigliani-Miller / Myers / Desai / Graham etc. is standard but generic. The introduction can lose some literature scaffolding and gain conceptual clarity.

3. **Move some inferential detail out of the introduction.**  
   The first 2–3 pages contain too many details on bootstrap, leave-one-out, MDE, etc. Those belong later. For an editor or broad reader, that material clutters the story.

4. **Be selective about robustness in the main text.**  
   The robustness section is too prominent for a paper whose challenge is strategic significance, not credibility signaling. The wild bootstrap and placebo are enough in the main text. Leave-one-out and some other variants can stay in the appendix.

5. **Elevate implications; demote taxonomy.**  
   The institutional section is competent but too long relative to the payoff. More space should go to what it means that coordinated reform failed to move aggregates.

6. **Fix the discussion section’s drift.**  
   The “3M anomaly” discussion is dangerous strategically. It muddies a clean central message with a weak subgroup result and invites readers to think the null is an artifact of a bad dose measure. If the paper’s main claim is “no large aggregate effect,” don’t spend valuable narrative capital undercutting it unless that heterogeneity is central and persuasive.

7. **Conclusion should do more than summarize.**  
   The conclusion is decent, but it should end on a sharper conceptual lesson: micro distortions are not automatically macro margins, and coordinated tax reform may be necessary but insufficient to change aggregate financial structure.

### Are results buried that should be in the main text?
The most interesting buried idea is not a statistical result but a conceptual one: **the disconnect between firm-level policy compliance and aggregate balance-sheet outcomes.** If there is any empirical evidence that supports that mechanism, it should be brought into the main text.

### Is the paper front-loaded with the good stuff?
Reasonably, but not enough. The “phantom correction” concept is the good stuff. It should arrive on page 1, not as a slogan later.

At present, the gap is mostly **framing plus ambition**, with some **scope** concerns.

### Framing problem?
Yes. The science may be adequate for what it is, but the paper is still framed too much as an evaluation of an EU directive and too little as an answer to a major economics question about the micro-macro translation of tax policy.

### Scope problem?
Yes. Aggregate debt ratios alone may be too narrow for the claim the paper wants to make. If the message is about debt bias and macro fragility, the paper should ideally connect to broader macro consequences or sharper mechanism evidence. Right now it is a fairly narrow set of outcomes supporting a broad conclusion.

### Novelty problem?
Moderate. The setting is novel enough, and the aggregate angle is genuinely distinct, but the underlying empirical move is not intrinsically novel. To clear the AER bar, the paper needs the idea to feel larger than the method.

### Ambition problem?
Yes. The paper is careful and competent, but somewhat safe. It wants credit for being the “first aggregate study,” which is not enough. It should aspire to say something stronger: that coordinated tax reform can leave macro allocations unchanged even when tax incentives change materially.

### Single most impactful advice
**Reframe the paper around the micro-to-macro puzzle—why a major coordinated reform that should have reduced debt bias failed to move aggregate corporate balance sheets—and organize the entire introduction, results, and discussion around that question rather than around ATAD as a policy evaluation exercise.**

That is the one change that would most improve its odds.
