**Idea Fidelity**

The paper follows the original idea closely. It links the SNAP Retailer Historical Database with CDC natality data, targets birth outcomes, and uses corporate chain bankruptcies as instruments for supermarket closures. The main innovation — a reverse-test of Hoynes et al. (2011) via supermarket exits — is faithfully pursued. However, the treatment aggregation to the state-year level and the decision to rely on a handful of chain bankruptcies substantially diverges from the finer-grained, neighborhood-level channel emphasized in the manifest’s motivation. The original manifesto anticipated using the full spatial richness of the SNAP data (and the biologically tight nine-month maternal window) to pin down causal impacts; the actual paper, by contrast, works only with state-year aggregates and ends up with a weak instrument. The question of whether the paper “missed key elements” therefore depends on whether state-level inference can credibly capture the intended channel. As discussed below, the aggregation and weak instrument choices threaten that credibility.

**Summary**

This paper investigates whether SNAP-authorized supermarket closures, driven by large chain bankruptcies, worsen state-level birth outcomes using a panel of 51 state units over 2016–2023. After linking the USDA SNAP Retailer Historical Database with CDC natality microdata, the author estimates state-year regressions (OLS and instrumented by pre-existing chain exposure to bankruptcy) and finds a precisely estimated null effect on low birth weight, preterm birth, and gestational diabetes. The paper concludes that supermarket-to-birth-outcome transmission is either absent or confined to very localized geographies that the state-level aggregation cannot detect.

**Essential Points**

1. **Credibility of the Instrument and First Stage.** The chain-bankruptcy instrument fails to explain sufficient variation in state exit rates (first-stage F-statistic = 1.4). Even if the bankruptcy stories are plausibly exogenous, the IV results are uninformative. The paper should either find stronger, more localized instruments or abandon the IV approach and be transparent that identification relies on potentially endogenous OLS variation. As it stands, reporting IV estimates alongside such a weak first stage risks overstating the credibility of the null.

2. **Aggregation and Relevance to the Research Question.** The central biological argument hinges on pregnant women losing proximate access to fresh food. Yet the estimation is conducted at the state-year level, averaging over very heterogeneous geographies. If the harmful channel is localized (as all prior literature suggests), state-level averages will almost mechanically deliver nulls, regardless of data quality. The paper must demonstrate that the treatment variation (state exit rates) meaningfully captures the intended causal pathway — for example, by showing that exit rates correlate with access changes in high-risk areas or by conducting analyses at finer geographic levels. Without such evidence, the null result may simply reflect aggregation bias, not absence of effect.

3. **Incomplete Event-Study and Hypothesis Testing.** The narrative emphasizes pre-trends and heterogeneous timing, yet the event-study table (Table 2) lacks actual coefficients, standard errors, or visual aids, and the paper reports no formal test of pre-trend equality. Similarly, the heterogeneity section reports an isolated significant estimate for low Medicaid states with no discussion of multiple testing or interpretation. The authors must present complete event-study estimates (with coefficients, CIs, and preferably figures) and interpret heterogeneity results in light of multiple comparisons or test power. Otherwise, key identification claims (parallel trends, mechanism heterogeneity) remain unsubstantiated.

Given that the paper’s conclusions rest on weak instruments, state-aggregated treatment, and partially reported diagnostics, these issues are “essential.” If the authors cannot convincingly address them, the paper likely should be rejected.

**Suggestions**

1. **Revisit the Level of Analysis.** The core theoretical mechanism — that pregnant women lose quick access to affordable fresh produce — operates at sub-state geographic units (neighborhoods, counties, or even census tracts). The CDC public-use data suppresses such detail, but the SNAP retailer data can potentially be aggregated to the county level and matched with county-year vital statistics (available through CDC WONDER for key outcomes). If statewide birth data are the only option, consider weighting exit rates by population share or constructing a “closure exposure index” that emphasizes counties where the chain closures comprised a large share of local supermarkets. At minimum, provide a convincing argument (with descriptive evidence) that variation in state-level exit rates is not just noise but meaningfully alters access for sizable subpopulations.

2. **Strengthen or Reframe Identification.** Since the chain-bankruptcy instrument is weak, explore alternative instruments or quasi-experimental variation that can credibly predict closures. Possibilities include:
   - **Differential exposure based on pre-existing market share.** Interact state-level chain presence with bankruptcy timing, but use it in a diff-in-diff (event-study) framework rather than IV, highlighting that the closure shock is plausibly exogenous for states with sudden bankruptcy exits.
   - **Use store-level closures as treatment.** Aggregate births around the timing of each individual store exit (place vs. time) and exploit the fact that chain bankruptcies forced uniform store closure dates, allowing for a stacked difference-in-differences design.
   - **Instrument with other exogenous shocks.** For example, use changes in federal bankruptcy policy or pension fund restructurings that affected chain liquidity but not local health; alternatively, use the intensity of bankruptcy-related store closures relative to total supermarket counts in neighboring states as an instrument (if variation exists).
   - If no stronger instrument is available, be explicit that inference relies on OLS and that the IV results are reported only for transparency but not used for conclusions.

3. **Expand Pre-trend and Robustness Presentation.** The paper should fully report the event-study coefficients (Table 2 currently only lists the reference period). Include the actual estimates and, ideally, a figure showing the pre/post coefficients with confidence intervals. This will allow readers to assess parallel trends and the dynamics of the effect. Additionally, provide robustness tables for the placebo (C-section) and heterogeneity analyses to show whether results persist under alternative standard error adjustments (e.g., wild cluster bootstrap, which is already mentioned but not tabulated). For the heterogeneity section, clarify why the low-Medicaid significant coefficient (0.0017, p<0.05) does not contradict the null conclusion — is it economically negligible, spurious, or pointing to an omitted mechanism?

4. **Address Alternative Channels.** The exclusion restriction assumes that chain bankruptcies affect birth outcomes only through supermarket closures. Yet such bankruptcies may coincide with broader economic distress (e.g., wage losses for laid-off grocery workers, credit shocks to suppliers) that could independently affect maternal health. Provide evidence that these shocks do not confound the estimates — for example, by showing that general economic indicators (state unemployment, consumer sentiment, retail employment) do not systematically change around the bankruptcy events after controlling for year fixed effects. A falsification using outcomes plausibly unaffected by nutritional access but potentially affected by labor market shocks (such as maternal smoking rates, if available) could strengthen the claim.

5. **Clarify Interpretation of the Null.** The Discussion section proposes three mechanisms for the null (substitution, online delivery, aggregation bias). It would be helpful to empirically assess at least one of these using available data. For instance, show that states with higher e-commerce penetration or greater Walmart/Aldi presence have lower estimated effects, or that states experiencing ZIP-code-level closures (if data permit) have true reductions in supermarket density. If such evidence is unavailable, make explicit that the null is consistent with multiple mechanisms and that further research (e.g., using restricted county-level natality files) is needed. This will temper overconfidence in the null and better situate the contribution.

6. **Report Treatment Intensity in Context.** The paper states there are ~35,000 supermarket exits over 20 years, but the state-year summary table suggests about 64 exits per year. It would help to contextualize how big this treatment variation is relative to overall supermarket density. For example, what share of a state’s supermarkets closed due to the identified bankruptcies versus other reasons? How much does the “exit rate” move within a typical state over the sample period? Providing histograms or distributional plots would help readers gauge the magnitude of the shock and why the state-level effect might be hard to detect.

7. **Augment the Placebo Suite.** C-section rate is a reasonable placebo, but consider adding additional outcomes that should not respond to supermarket access (e.g., maternal smoking during pregnancy, neonatal mortality not tied to nutrition) to bolster confidence in the null. If no change is observed across multiple unrelated outcomes, the exclusion restriction gains credibility; if some placebo does move, this would signal residual confounding.

8. **Engage with the Null Literature.** The paper rightly references Allcott et al. (2019) and Hoynes et al. (2011). It could further discuss other null findings in the food access and health literature (e.g., work on small grocery store openings, mobile produce markets) to position the contribution more squarely within debates on the external validity of supply-driven interventions.

By addressing these points, the paper can more convincingly argue that supermarket closures (as measured) do not worsen birth outcomes. At present, the combination of weak instruments, aggregation issues, and incomplete diagnostics undermines the credibility of the null, but the topic remains important and deserves careful empirical treatment.
