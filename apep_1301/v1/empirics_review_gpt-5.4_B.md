## 1. Idea Fidelity

The paper pursues the broad research question in the manifest—whether SNAP-authorized supermarket exits worsen birth outcomes—but it departs from the original design in ways that substantially weaken the intended contribution. Most importantly, the manifest proposed a county-level design using 609 counties covering most U.S. births, with treatment defined by county supermarket exits and an IV based on corporate chain bankruptcies. The paper instead collapses the analysis to a state-year panel with only 408 observations. Given that the mechanism is explicitly about proximate food access during pregnancy, this shift from county to state is not a minor implementation detail; it changes the estimand and greatly increases the risk of attenuation from spatial aggregation.

The paper also omits or underdevelops several key elements of the manifest. The original plan emphasized outcomes including low birth weight, preterm birth, gestational diabetes, and stratification by Medicaid versus private payment. In the paper, gestational diabetes disappears from the results, and heterogeneity is done only by splitting states according to Medicaid birth share rather than by payer-specific birth outcomes. Similarly, the event-study component is not really implemented: the event-study table is effectively empty, so the reader cannot assess the pre-trend claim. Finally, the manifest pitched chain bankruptcies as the core identification strategy, but the realized IV is weak (first-stage F = 1.4), leaving the paper without the causal design that was supposed to distinguish it.

## 2. Summary

This paper links the SNAP Retailer Historical Database to natality data and studies whether supermarket closures affect birth outcomes. Using state-year variation from 2016–2023, it finds no detectable relationship between supermarket exit rates and low birth weight, preterm birth, or related outcomes, and argues that any true effects must be hyper-local rather than visible at the state level.

The topic is important and the null result could be informative. However, in its current form the paper does not provide credible causal evidence on the policy question it raises, primarily because the analysis is conducted at too aggregated a level and the proposed instrumental-variables strategy fails empirically.

## 3. Essential Points

1. **The paper does not identify the causal effect of local food-access loss on birth outcomes.**  
   The mechanism is local, but the analysis is at the state-year level. A supermarket closure that matters for a small set of neighborhoods will be almost invisible in state aggregates of both treatment and outcomes. This creates a serious mismatch between theory and empirics. As a result, the paper currently supports at most the narrow statement that state-level supermarket exit rates are not strongly correlated with state-level birth outcomes—not the stronger causal conclusion in the title and abstract.

2. **The IV strategy fails, and the paper should not lean on it as causal evidence.**  
   A first-stage F-statistic of 1.4 is fatal for the proposed IV contribution. The abstract and introduction still frame the paper as using chain bankruptcies to obtain causal estimates, but the results section itself shows that this strategy does not work in the realized sample. With such a weak first stage, the IV estimates are not informative, and the paper’s central claims should be rewritten accordingly unless a much stronger design is implemented.

3. **Several core empirical claims are unsupported or incomplete.**  
   The event-study evidence is not actually shown; gestational diabetes is mentioned but not reported; the placebo outcome is not as clean as claimed; and the heterogeneity analysis does not match the proposed mechanism. These issues make it difficult to evaluate either identification or interpretation. If substantially more evidence cannot be provided, I would recommend rejection rather than revision because too much of the paper’s causal architecture is currently missing.

## 4. Suggestions

The paper asks a good question, uses promising data, and is admirably transparent about the weakness of the IV. But to become a publishable short empirical paper, it needs to be reframed much more carefully and, ideally, re-estimated at a more appropriate level of geography.

**First, the paper should be rewritten to match what the data can actually support.** Right now the title, abstract, and introduction promise a causal study of supermarket closures and birth outcomes, but the empirical design mostly delivers a descriptive state-level null. I would strongly recommend toning down the language throughout. For example, “The Vanishing Aisle That Didn’t Matter” overstates the evidence. A more accurate framing would be that the paper finds no detectable effects in state-level aggregates and therefore cannot rule out meaningful local effects. That is still a potentially useful result, but it is not the same as showing that supermarket closures do not matter for maternal and infant health.

**Second, if at all possible, return to the county-level design in the manifest.** This is the single most important improvement. The manifest itself recognized the key issue: food access is local. Counties are not perfect, but they are far closer to the relevant margin than states. Moving from 51 states to 609 counties would also increase statistical power and produce variation that better matches the biological timing story. Even if the IV remains imperfect, a county-level event-study or DiD around major chain exits would be far more informative than the current state-level panel. If county-level natality outcomes are unavailable in the public-use files, the paper should either obtain restricted-access data or clearly reposition itself as a study of aggregate, not local, effects.

**Third, the treatment variable should be more tightly tied to actual exposure during pregnancy.** “Annual exits per 1,000 active supermarkets” is a fairly abstract state-level intensity measure. It would be more compelling to construct measures that better correspond to the mechanism: number of exits in the county, exits per births, share of population exposed to a closure, or change in supermarket access within the pregnancy window. If the exact month of store exit is observed, that timing can be exploited relative to conception or birth month rather than annual averages. Even an annual county-level measure would be a substantial improvement over the current state-level rate.

**Fourth, the event-study must be fully implemented or removed.** At present, the event-study table does not report coefficients, so the paper asks readers to trust a key identifying assumption without evidence. A complete event-study figure would be better than a table, especially in an AER: Insights-style paper. Show the dynamic path for low birth weight and preterm birth with confidence intervals, and make clear what defines treatment timing. Also, because treatment is staggered and likely heterogeneous, I would encourage using a modern event-study estimator rather than a simple two-way fixed effects setup if treatment timing differs across places.

**Fifth, the heterogeneity analysis should more directly test the mechanism.** Splitting states by median Medicaid share is a very blunt approach. The manifest’s original idea—to stratify births by payment source (Medicaid versus private)—is much stronger. If natality data allow payer-specific outcomes by geography-year, use them. That would let the paper ask whether closure exposure affects the population most likely to be liquidity constrained and dependent on nearby SNAP-authorized stores. Similarly, if maternal education, race/ethnicity, or parity can be used in aggregated cells, these dimensions may provide much sharper tests than state-level splits.

**Sixth, report the omitted outcomes and reconcile the text with the tables.** Gestational diabetes appears in the abstract and motivation but not in the results. Either include it fully, with explanation of timing and coding, or remove it from the framing. More generally, there are a number of places where the prose is stronger than the evidence: the paper says the placebo test supports the exclusion restriction, but a null on C-section rates does little to validate the bankruptcy instrument. Likewise, the paper says the null is “well-powered,” but the observed design is only well-powered for state-level average effects, not for the local effects actually of interest. Tightening the claims to match the evidence would materially improve credibility.

**Seventh, think more carefully about what the chain-bankruptcy instrument identifies even if it were strong.** The exclusion restriction is not automatic. Chain bankruptcies may coincide with broader regional retail distress, labor-market shocks, changes in pharmacy access, or neighborhood decline, especially if the chains are regionally concentrated. A more convincing design would separate chain-specific exposure from general retail trends and show reduced-form effects first. I would also encourage reporting the first stage graphically, chain by chain, and showing what fraction of total supermarket exits is actually explained by these bankruptcies. At present, the paper’s own results suggest the answer is “not much.”

**Eighth, the placebo strategy should be strengthened.** C-section rates are not an ideal placebo because they can respond to changes in maternal risk, provider behavior, and hospital practice patterns in ways not obviously orthogonal to economic shocks. Better placebo outcomes would be those less plausibly tied to nutrition but measured in the same data—perhaps sex ratio at birth, some delivery characteristics, or outcomes for groups less exposed to SNAP-related food access constraints. Another useful placebo is a pre-period “pseudo-bankruptcy exposure” test on the treatment variable itself.

**Ninth, contextualize the null more carefully relative to the literature.** The comparison to Allcott et al. is interesting but currently too direct. Allcott et al. study food access and purchases in a very different empirical environment and at a much more local margin. Your null at the state level does not necessarily “extend” their result to health; it may simply reflect aggregation. Likewise, the comparison to Hoynes, Schanzenbach, and Almond is conceptually appealing, but SNAP benefit introduction and supermarket closure are not symmetric treatments. One changes purchasing power; the other changes retail geography. The paper would benefit from a more restrained statement of what the null does and does not imply for the nutrition-health production function.

**Tenth, improve the presentation of summary statistics and scale.** Some of the reported variation looks suspiciously small for state-year birth outcomes (for example, the standard deviations of low birth weight and Medicaid share), and the paper should help readers understand whether these are within-state or pooled SDs and whether outcomes are percentages or fraction points. Also, the units in the tables should be cleaned up: “(\%\%)” is distracting, and the economic magnitudes should be discussed in interpretable terms. For example, what does a one-standard-deviation increase in exit rates correspond to in actual store closures? How large an effect would the design be able to detect?

Finally, if the authors cannot move to a finer geography, there is still a plausible short-paper contribution—but it is a different one. The paper could become a careful demonstration that publicly available state-year natality data are too aggregated to detect plausibly local food-access shocks, using chain bankruptcies as a case study. That would be a methods-and-measurement contribution rather than a causal policy paper. In its current form, the paper sits awkwardly between those two goals. Clarifying which paper it wants to be would significantly improve it.
