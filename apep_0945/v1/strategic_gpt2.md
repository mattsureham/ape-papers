## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper studies whether a president can use executive orders to durably reshape the regulatory state. Using Trump’s EO 13771 “two-for-one” regulatory budget and Biden’s subsequent rescission, it argues that temporary executive deregulation did not simply reduce rulemaking; it shifted agency effort toward deregulatory actions and may have left a persistent shortfall in new protective rules even after the order was lifted.

Why should a busy economist care? Because this is really a paper about state capacity and reversibility: can a president create long-lasting policy change without legislation, just by changing the production technology of regulation?

### Does the paper articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Not quite. The current introduction is competent, but it opens as a paper about “whether the executive can alter the pace and composition of regulation” and only later arrives at the more interesting claim: asymmetry of reversal. That asymmetry is the title-worthy idea, but it is not the lead idea. The first two paragraphs should foreground the irreversibility question immediately.

### The pitch the paper should have

A stronger opening would say something like:

> Presidents increasingly try to govern through the administrative state, but we know much less about whether executive interventions into rulemaking are reversible. When a president imposes a deregulatory constraint on agencies, does a successor simply switch the machine back on, or does temporary deregulation leave a persistent scar on the state’s capacity to produce new rules?
>
> This paper studies that question using Trump’s EO 13771 “two-for-one” regulatory budget and Biden’s 2021 rescission. Using the universe of federal rulemaking dockets, I show that the order did not straightforwardly shrink rulemaking. Instead, it reallocated activity toward deregulatory final rules while suppressing new proposed rules at the agencies most exposed to the constraint; after rescission, proposed rulemaking did not fully rebound. The broader implication is that executive deregulation may operate as a ratchet: easy to impose, harder to undo.

That is the AER-relevant version of the paper. It is about reversibility of state action, not just one executive order.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### Contribution in one sentence

The paper’s contribution is to argue that executive regulatory budgets change the composition of agency rulemaking and may create asymmetric, only partially reversible reductions in new rule production at the most exposed agencies.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest papers?

Only partially. The paper says “first causal cross-agency estimates,” which is a fine line for a field journal but not enough for AER. “First causal estimate” is not a contribution unless the reader also understands what larger substantive question is being answered. Right now, the differentiation from descriptive work on EO 13771 is clearer than the differentiation from broader work on presidential administration, bureaucratic capacity, and regulatory politics.

The closest distinction the paper wants is:

- prior work describes EO 13771 or debates whether it reduced regulations;
- this paper says the real effect was compositional, not volumetric;
- and the more ambitious claim is that the effect persisted after rescission.

That is potentially strong, but the paper currently presents three claims of unequal strength:
1. total rulemaking rose,
2. composition shifted toward deregulatory finalization,
3. rescission did not fully undo the effect.

The first two seem to be the empirically strongest and most novel package. The third is the most interesting, but in the draft it still reads as suggestive rather than decisive. That mismatch weakens the framing.

### Is the contribution framed as a question about the world or a gap in a literature?

It is mixed. The introduction starts with a world question, which is good, but quickly slips into literature-fill language (“first causal cross-agency estimates,” “contributes to several literatures”). For AER, the contribution should be framed more firmly as a world question:

- Can presidents durably redirect regulatory production using executive authority alone?
- Are changes in administrative capacity reversible when partisan control changes?

That is much stronger than “there is little causal evidence on EO 13771.”

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?

At present, maybe, but not cleanly. A smart reader would probably say: “It’s a DiD paper on Trump’s two-for-one rule using agency exposure.” That is not enough. You want them to say: “It shows that temporary executive deregulation can act like a ratchet: it shifts agencies toward deregulatory output and may suppress new rule creation even after the policy is rescinded.”

Right now the design is clearer than the idea.

### What would make the contribution bigger?

Most importantly: make the paper more directly about composition and reversibility rather than counts.

Specific ways to make it bigger:

1. **Use the deregulation/pro-regulation margin more directly.**  
   The paper itself admits that the EO 13771 designation field exists for 2017–2020. That sounds like the paper’s most natural main outcome, not a future extension. If the key claim is that agencies shifted toward deregulatory actions, the paper should show that directly rather than infer it from total rules rising and NPRMs falling.

2. **Elevate the stock, not just the flow.**  
   A bigger paper would connect monthly rulemaking flows to the evolution of the regulatory stock or policy content. Did high-exposure agencies end the Trump period with fewer pending protective rules, fewer major new obligations, or a more negative mix of regulatory actions? AER readers want to know what changed in the world, not just document counts.

3. **Clarify the “ratchet” mechanism.**  
   If the paper wants to own the word “ratchet,” it needs either stronger evidence on persistence or a more modest framing. As written, “ratchet” is a very strong title claim resting on suggestive post-rescission patterns. That is dangerous strategically. The paper is stronger as “executive deregulation redirects regulatory production, with suggestive persistence” unless it can really nail asymmetry.

4. **Connect to welfare-relevant domains.**  
   The paper would feel bigger if the reader saw what kinds of rules were displaced: environmental, workplace safety, health, financial protection. “Fewer NPRMs” is abstract. “Temporary regulatory budgets delayed new air pollution, workplace safety, or drug oversight proposals” is a different order of importance.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Presidents increasingly use executive action to influence the regulatory state. Agencies produce rules continuously, but it is unclear whether executive interventions simply re-time this production or permanently alter what agencies can do.

### Tension

EO 13771 was intended as a deregulatory tool, but there are two competing possibilities:
- it may have simply reduced rulemaking while in force, with rescission restoring the old equilibrium;
- or it may have changed the composition of agency effort and damaged the pipeline for new rules in a way that outlasted the order itself.

That is the paper’s tension. It is a good one.

### Resolution

The paper finds that the order appears to have increased total rulemaking among the most exposed agencies because agencies pursued deregulatory final rules, while new proposed rules declined and did not fully rebound after rescission.

### Implications

Executive deregulation may not work by shrinking activity overall; it may work by redirecting the administrative state and leaving persistent deficits in future protective rulemaking. That matters for how economists think about executive power, policy reversibility, and the real durability of partisan administrative change.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the ingredients, but the arc is not fully disciplined. Right now it feels like two papers awkwardly combined:

1. a paper on the surprising compositional effect of a regulatory budget;
2. a paper on the ratchet-like persistence of executive deregulation.

The first is better supported. The second is more exciting but less secure. The draft tries to give them equal weight, which makes the narrative wobble.

### What story should it be telling?

The cleanest story is:

- A deregulation order did **not** reduce regulatory activity in the simple way people think.
- Instead, it changed the **composition** of what agencies produced.
- That compositional shock may have **persistent consequences** because the pipeline for new rules is slower to rebuild than the machinery for repeal.

That is the right narrative hierarchy:
1. compositional redirection = core result,
2. asymmetric reversal = broader interpretation.

At present the title and framing imply the reverse hierarchy.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?

I would say:

> Trump’s two-for-one deregulation order may actually have increased total rulemaking at the agencies it hit hardest—because it pushed them to produce more deregulatory rules—while leaving fewer new protective rules in the pipeline even after Biden repealed it.

That is a good dinner-party fact. People will lean in.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

Lean in—if you say it that way. If you say “I estimate a difference-in-differences using Regulations.gov dockets,” phones come out immediately.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Likely one of these:

1. “Do you directly observe that the added activity was deregulation rather than ordinary rulemaking?”
2. “Was the post-2021 persistence really capacity loss, or just agencies spending time undoing Trump-era rules?”
3. “Did this matter in substantively important domains, or is it mostly bureaucratic paperwork?”
4. “How general is this beyond this one executive order?”

These are revealing. The fact that these are the immediate follow-ups tells you exactly what the paper needs to foreground.

### If findings are modest or null, is that okay?

The post-rescission result is modest/suggestive, and the paper does not yet fully make the case for why that modesty is still interesting. The null/modest component is only interesting if framed as evidence about asymmetry in state rebuilding. Without that framing, it risks feeling like an underpowered attempt to prove persistence.

So: the paper should not oversell the ratchet. It should make clearer that the major contribution is the compositional effect, with persistence as an important but still provisional implication.

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

### What would make it read better?

1. **Rewrite the introduction around one central claim.**  
   The first two paragraphs should introduce reversibility and composition immediately. The current version takes too long to tell the reader why this is more than a narrow regulatory paper.

2. **Move the design down; move the core finding up.**  
   The introduction gets into treatment intensity and agency examples too early. AER readers want the fact first, design second.

3. **Front-load the surprising result.**  
   “The regulatory budget increased total rulemaking at exposed agencies because it rewarded deregulatory finalizations” should appear before the empirical strategy paragraph, not after.

4. **Demote routine threats/robustness language in the introduction.**  
   Event studies, placebo tests, and dropped-agency checks do not belong in the intro at this level of prominence. That is referee-facing writing, not editor/reader-facing writing.

5. **Promote the mechanism evidence if it exists.**  
   If the designation field can directly show regulatory vs deregulatory output, that belongs in the main text, likely as a headline figure or main table.

6. **Tighten the discussion section.**  
   The discussion currently mixes mechanism, welfare, limitations, and future work. It should be sharper:
   - first paragraph: what changed in administrative behavior;
   - second: what this implies for reversibility and state capacity;
   - third: what remains uncertain.

7. **Conclusion should do more than summarize.**  
   Right now it mostly restates findings. It should end on a broader implication: executive authority may be especially powerful when it changes institutional pipelines rather than statutory policy.

### Is the paper front-loaded with the good stuff?

Moderately, but not enough. The “good stuff” is in the abstract and some of the results section. The introduction is still too much like a standard applied paper. This paper needs more of a top-journal reveal structure.

### Are there buried results that should be in the main results?

Yes: by the paper’s own logic, any direct evidence on EO 13771 designations—deregulatory vs regulatory actions—should be central. It is odd that the draft treats this as future work. If available, it belongs in the main text and may be the single most important missing element for the story.

### Is the conclusion adding value?

A little, but not much. It summarizes. It does not fully cash out the larger idea that temporary executive constraints may have durable institutional effects. That should be the concluding punchline.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

### What is the gap?

Mostly a combination of **framing problem** and **scope problem**, with some **ambition problem**.

- **Framing problem:** The paper’s deepest idea—irreversibility in administrative capacity—is not yet the organizing principle of the paper.
- **Scope problem:** The evidence is still too tied to rule counts and too indirect on composition and persistence.
- **Ambition problem:** The paper is currently satisfied with being “the first causal cross-agency estimate.” That is not an AER ambition level. It needs to answer a bigger question about the world.

I would not call it mainly a novelty problem. The setting is interesting and timely. The problem is that the paper has not yet fully translated the setting into a general insight economists will care about.

### Single most impactful piece of advice

**Reframe the paper around the claim that executive action changes the composition and reversibility of state capacity—and then show that composition directly, not indirectly through rule counts.**

If they could only change one thing, it should be that.

Concretely: the paper should stop being “a DiD on EO 13771” and become “a paper on whether executive deregulation creates an administrative ratchet.” But to earn that framing, it needs direct evidence on deregulation/pro-regulation composition and a more disciplined treatment of the persistence claim.
