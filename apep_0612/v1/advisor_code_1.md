**Idea Fidelity**

The paper substantially diverges from the original idea manifest. The manifest envisaged exploiting quasi-random case assignment to compare lenient and strict judges within the same court, using the Ash-DGY leave-one-out instrument to isolate the causal effect of individual asylum decisions on local crime. The current manuscript instead collapses the analysis to a state-level cross-section: the treatment is the state-average asylum grant rate (2019–2024), the instrument is a caseload-weighted average career grant rate of judges in that state, and the outcome is the average homicide rate over the same years. This approach sacrifices the within-court quasi-random variation that underpins the judge-IV strategy in the manifest. While the paper acknowledges the limitation and frames the exercise as a first step, it therefore does not pursue the core identification approach promised in the idea manifest. Key elements—within-court random assignment, panel variation exploiting judge turnover, and the case-level IV structure—are absent.

**Summary**

The authors assemble judge-level career grant rates from OpenImmigration.us and merge them with state-level homicide data from the CDC to study whether exogenous variation in asylum grant rates affects local crime. Using a cross-sectional 2SLS design, with the “judge leniency index” instrumenting the observed grant rate, they find no stable relationship between marginal asylum policy and homicide once demographic controls and region fixed effects are included. The paper documents a strong first stage but cautions that judge composition is correlated with state demographics, suggesting that more refined within-court variation is required for stronger causal claims.

**Essential Points**

1. **Identification strategy is not credible in its current form.** By aggregating judge leniency and crime to the state level, the paper relies on variation in long-run judicial composition across states—variation that the balance tests show is systematically correlated with poverty, income, and immigrant shares. The exclusion restriction therefore fails unless one assumes perfect control for these observables and linearity, which is implausible. The original judge-IV design (within-court random assignment) is what makes the strategy credible; collapsing to a state cross-section loses that support and renders the IV indistinguishable from OLS. Unless the authors can exploit within-court variation (e.g., judge turnover events or a panel structure that compares the same court over time), the causal interpretation remains tenuous.

2. **Temporal mismatch and outcome measurement weaken inference.** The treatment (asylum grant rate) is measured as a career average of judges, while the outcome aggregates homicide rates from 2019–2024. This conflates historic judicial behavior with contemporary crime fluctuations, obscuring the mechanism: the causal effect of marginal asylum decisions should operate contemporaneously (e.g., through recent arrivals gaining legal status). The current design cannot separate long-standing judge composition from recent decision flows, so it is unclear whether the (null) finding reflects a true lack of crime response or simply temporal aggregation. A clearer temporal linkage (e.g., linking asylum decisions in year \(t\) to crime in year \(t+1\) at the court or county level) is necessary.

3. **Sample size and external validity concerns limit informativeness.** Working with only 29 states (those with both courts and CDC data) and a cross-sectional setup provides limited variation, making the results sensitive to outliers. The nearly perfect first-stage coefficient (close to one) also indicates that the instrument is mechanically extremely similar to the endogenous regressor, offering little additional leverage beyond a single regressed variable. Without richer panel variation or within-court comparisons, it is difficult to learn about the marginal effect of asylum decisions, especially since the statewide treatment intensity (50k grants/year) is diluted across large populations, raising questions about statistical power and substantive significance.

Given these issues, the paper needs substantial revision or a redesign before publication. If the authors cannot obtain within-court time-series variation, the causal claim should be substantially weakened.

**Suggestions**

1. **Return to the case- or court-level judge-IV design.** The manifest promised a leave-one-out IV using quasi-random assignment to judges within the same courthouse. The authors should implement this structure directly: use panel data at the court or judge level, exploit rotation-based random assignment, and instrument individual-level asylum outcomes with each judge’s leniency excluding the focal case. Aggregating to the state level loses the most credible variation. If data limitations prevent this, the paper should clearly frame the contribution as descriptive evidence on state-level correlations between judge composition and crime, not as causal IV evidence.

2. **Exploit temporal variation and judge turnover.** As noted, compressing judges into cross-sectional states introduces contamination from non-random assignments. A more promising strategy would be to track judge-level grant rates over time within courts and identify abrupt changes when a lenient judge replaces a strict one (or vice versa). Difference-in-differences or event-study designs that compare crime trends before and after such turnovers—possibly at the county or metropolitan area level—would strengthen the identification. Court-year panels (with year fixed effects and court fixed effects) could also leverage variation in case loads and outcomes over time.

3. **Align treatment and outcome timing.** If the goal is to estimate the effect of marginal asylum grants on crime, the treatment should be contemporaneous with the outcome. Consider constructing annual or quarterly asylum grant counts (or rates) per court or county, derived from actual decisions rather than career averages. Then link those to crime in the same period (or with plausible lags) to capture how newly admitted asylum seekers might affect local safety. This approach would also allow you to control for other time-varying factors at the locality level (e.g., policing budgets, unemployment shocks) and improve power.

4. **Clarify the mechanism and scale of the treatment.** The paper’s main finding is a null, yet it is unclear whether this is because the marginal asylum decision simply has no effect or because the treatment intensity is too low to move crime at the state level. Please provide calculations showing the share of overall population or victimization potentially affected by asylum decisions in each state, and use those to interpret the magnitude of the estimates. If the treatment is dilute, consider focusing on more concentrated settings—cities or counties with large asylum case volumes—where the marginal effect might be detectable.

5. **Reassess the instrument’s exclusion validity.** State-level judge leniency clearly correlates with observable demographics (Table 4). This suggests omitted variable bias is a real threat. Consider using higher-dimensional controls (e.g., education, policing expenditures, urbanization) and/or leverage instrumental variables that vary at finer geographic or temporal scales (e.g., judge assignment within court). Alternatively, move toward a mechanism-based placebo strategy: show that leniency predicts outcomes tied to the judge allocation process but not to unrelated outcomes (beyond the suicide placebo already provided). Ultimately, the paper should motivate why the residual variation in judge leniency is exogenous after conditioning on observables.

6. **Discuss substantive implications conditional on design limitations.** While the null finding replicates existing literature on immigration and crime, the policymaker takeaways hinge on the causal interpretation. Given the identification issues, the concluding discussion should more openly acknowledge that the result pertains to state-level associations and that a stronger design is needed before drawing firm policy conclusions. Suggest avenues for future research that could address the current limitations, such as linking EOIR docket data with local crime metrics or working with EOIR to observe judge assignments in detail.

By addressing these points—especially by restoring within-court quasi-random variation and aligning treatment and outcome timing—the authors can turn the attractive idea of “judge leniency and crime” into a credible empirical contribution.
