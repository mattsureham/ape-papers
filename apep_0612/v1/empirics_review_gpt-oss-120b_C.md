**1. Idea Fidelity**  
The paper follows the manifest closely.  It uses the quasi‑random assignment of immigration cases to judges with wildly different grant rates, constructs a leave‑one‑out “judge‑leniency” index, and instruments state‑level asylum‑grant rates to estimate the causal effect on local crime.  All of the data sources listed in the idea (OpenImmigration.us judge‑court files, CDC homicide counts, ACS demographics) are employed, and the same identification strategy (Kling‑style IV with within‑court variation) is implemented.  The only minor departure is that the original idea called for a panel exploiting judge turnover; the current manuscript settles for a cross‑section supplemented by a short state‑year panel (2019‑2024).  This limitation is acknowledged in the text, so the paper remains faithful to the core idea.

---

**2. Summary**  
The authors exploit enormous within‑court variation in immigration‑judge grant rates (1.5 %–49.4 %) as an instrumental variable for the state‑level asylum‑grant rate.  Using a 2SLS design they find that a one‑percentage‑point increase in the instrumented grant rate changes the homicide rate by essentially zero (point estimate ≈ 0.02 per 100 k, 95 % CI [‑0.25, 0.29]), suggesting that marginal asylum decisions do not affect local violent crime.  The result is robust to several specifications and placebo outcomes, but the identification hinges on the cross‑sectional exclusion restriction, which is only partially defended.

---

**3. Essential Points**  

1. **Exclusion restriction is weak in the cross‑section** – The balance tests (Table 7) show that judge leniency is positively correlated with poverty‑rate (negative), foreign‑born share, and median income.  These variables also affect homicide rates, so conditioning on them is necessary but does not guarantee that the remaining variation in the instrument is exogenous.  Because the instrument is aggregated at the state level, it inherits all the systematic geographic sorting of judges (e.g., more lenient judges tend to be placed in wealthier, lower‑crime states).  Consequently, the IV may still pick up omitted‑variable bias.  The authors should either (a) exploit within‑state, within‑court variation over time (e.g., judge turnover) or (b) construct a more credible “leave‑one‑out‑court‑year” instrument that varies within a state.

2. **Treatment intensity is too dilute for a state‑level outcome** – Asylum recipients are a tiny fraction of any state’s population (≈ 0.02 % of the total).  Even a large increase in the grant rate translates into at most a few dozen additional legal residents per state per year, making a detectable impact on the aggregate homicide rate implausible.  The paper should report the implied change in the number of asylum recipients per 100 k population for a one‑point grant‑rate increase, and present power calculations that demonstrate whether the null can be distinguished from a substantively meaningful effect.

3. **Standard errors and clustering are insufficient** – All regressions cluster only at the state level (or, in the panel, also at the state level).  The instrumental variable is constructed from judge‑level data that are nested within courts, which are themselves nested within states.  Ignoring this two‑level hierarchy can under‑state the variance of the IV estimator.  A two‑way cluster (state × year and judge) or, more appropriately, a wild‑cluster bootstrap at the judge‑court level would provide more reliable inference.

---

**4. Suggestions**  

1. **Strengthen the identification strategy**  
   - **Panel with judge turnover:** Identify events when a judge leaves a court and is replaced by a judge with a markedly different grant rate.  Use a difference‑in‑differences‑in‑IV design (judge‑turnover * time) to isolate within‑state changes in grant rates that are plausibly exogenous.  
   - **Leave‑one‑out‑court‑year instrument:** Construct the instrument for each state‑year by averaging the grant rates of all judges **except** those serving in that state that year.  This leverages the same logic as Kling (2006) but preserves within‑state variation over time, reducing correlation with time‑invariant state characteristics.  
   - **Place‑based falsification:** Test whether the judge‑leniency index predicts outcomes that should be unaffected by asylum decisions but are also geographically patterned (e.g., property tax rates, school expenditures).  A finding of significance would further call the exclusion restriction into question.

2. **Present the economic magnitude more transparently**  
   - Compute the *average* number of additional asylum recipients generated by a one‑percentage‑point increase in the grant rate (e.g., 0.01 × total asylum applications in the state).  Translate the estimated effect on homicide into “lives saved/added per 10 000 additional asylum recipients.”  This will help readers assess whether the null is substantively meaningful.  
   - Include a power analysis (Monte‑Carlo or analytic) showing the minimum detectable effect given the sample size, instrument strength, and clustering.  If the detectable effect is larger than any policy‑relevant magnitude, the paper should qualify the null accordingly.

3. **Improve inference**  
   - Adopt two‑way clustering (state × year and judge‑court) or implement a wild‑cluster bootstrap at the judge level, following Cameron, Gelbach, and Miller (2008).  Report how standard errors change; if they increase substantially, the statistical significance of the reduced‑form and IV estimates may be altered.  
   - Report the Kleibergen‑Paap rk Wald F‑statistic for the IV in the panel specification (especially important when using a multi‑instrument, multi‑period setup).  

4. **Address the dilution problem directly**  
   - Consider alternative outcomes that are more sensitive to a small influx of legal immigrants, such as the rate of violent crimes *committed by* recent immigrants (if such data are available via the NIBRS offender ethnicity field) or the share of crimes involving undocumented status.  Even if the data are noisy, a robustness check with a more “proximate” outcome would strengthen the argument that a null on aggregate homicide is not merely a power issue.  
   - Alternatively, aggregate to a finer geographic level (county or metropolitan statistical area) where the proportion of asylum recipients is larger, and run the same IV.  This may increase statistical power and reduce the dilution problem.

5. **Clarify the role of controls and fixed effects**  
   - In Table 4 (main results) the coefficient on the instrumented grant rate flips sign and significance when region FE are added.  Provide a brief discussion of why region FE matter so much (e.g., they soak up much of the variation in judge leniency).  A decomposition of the variation explained by each set of controls would be helpful.  
   - Test for non‑linearities in the first stage (e.g., bins of judge leniency) to ensure the monotonicity assumption holds across the full range of grant rates.

6. **Data and replication**  
   - The paper relies on a small subset of states (N = 29) due to missing judge data.  Provide a supplemental table showing which states are excluded and why, and discuss whether their exclusion could bias the results (e.g., are they systematically more restrictive or violent?).  
   - Release the compiled dataset (judge‑leniency index, grant rates, crime outcomes) and the Stata/R code used for the IV; this will facilitate replication and allow others to explore alternative specifications.

7. **Minor presentation issues**  
   - The terminology “Judge Leniency Index” and “state‑grant‑rate” are sometimes used interchangeably; a clear definition early on would avoid confusion.  
   - Table captions should specify whether standard errors are clustered and at what level.  
   - The abstract mentions “CDC homicide data (2019‑2024) for 29 states” – clarify that the analysis is at the *state* level and that the sample is limited to states with at least one immigration court and complete data.  
   - The discussion could benefit from a brief note on the policy relevance of “marginal asylum decisions” (e.g., how many people are affected each year) to situate the null in a real‑world context.

---

**Overall Assessment**  
The paper tackles an innovative question and exploits a compelling source of quasi‑random variation.  The data collection is impressive, and the null finding is presented clearly.  However, the current cross‑sectional IV suffers from a weak exclusion restriction and an essentially underpowered setting given the tiny treatment share.  Strengthening the design (panel with judge turnover or within‑state variation), improving inference, and more explicitly addressing the dilution issue would transform this from an interesting exploratory exercise into a rigorous contribution.  With those revisions, the manuscript would merit publication in *AER: Insights*; in its present form, I recommend **major revision**.
