## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple, policy-relevant question: when the U.S. abruptly dismantles a major foreign-aid bureaucracy, who in the United States actually bears the domestic employment cost? Using the 2025 termination of most USAID contracts, the paper argues that the labor-market consequences were real but highly concentrated: they showed up mainly as a hiring freeze in professional services, and almost entirely in the Washington, D.C. metro area rather than across the country.

A busy economist should care because this is not really a paper about aid per se; it is a paper about the domestic incidence of government procurement, the local consequences of administrative retrenchment, and the geography of politically salient federal spending.

**Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Partly, but not well enough. The opening has a good instinct—“foreign aid has domestic employment consequences”—but the paper takes too long to reveal the genuinely interesting punchline, which is **concentration rather than breadth**. Right now the first paragraphs make it sound like a standard local-labor-market treatment paper on a procurement shock. The thing that makes this paper potentially memorable is that the domestic impact of a politically explosive national policy was **narrow, local, and mostly Washington-specific**.

### The pitch the paper should have

> In early 2025, the U.S. government effectively dismantled USAID, terminating most of its contracts in one of the sharpest procurement retrenchments in recent history. This paper asks a basic but previously unmeasured question: when foreign aid is cut, where do the domestic jobs disappear?
>
> Linking county exposure to USAID prime contractors with local labor-market outcomes, I show that the domestic employment effects were not nationally diffuse but overwhelmingly concentrated in the Washington, D.C. contractor ecosystem. Employment fell through missing hires rather than mass layoffs, with limited evidence of broader national labor-market fallout outside the capital region. The paper’s core contribution is thus to identify the domestic geographic incidence of foreign-aid procurement—and to show how politically salient federal spending can create highly localized place-based dependence.

That is the AER-facing version. It gives the question, the fact, and the broader stake immediately.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:**  
The paper shows that the abrupt termination of USAID contracts generated a local hiring freeze and modest downstream spillovers, but that the domestic employment incidence of foreign-aid procurement was concentrated almost entirely in the Washington contractor cluster rather than spread across the United States.

1. **A foreign-aid procurement setting rather than conventional domestic spending**
2. **A retrenchment / dismantling shock rather than expansion**
3. **A domestic-incidence question**
4. **A result of extreme geographic concentration**

Right now only (1) and (2) are explicit; (3) and especially (4) should be doing much more work in differentiating the paper.

- Governments often justify or attack spending programs based on who benefits domestically.
- We know surprisingly little about the domestic geographic incidence of foreign-aid procurement.

That is a world question with general relevance.

That is sharper and more memorable.

### What would make this contribution bigger?
Several possibilities:

- **Reframe the object of interest as geographic incidence rather than treatment effect.** The concentration result is more interesting than the average effect.

- **Expand the incidence frame beyond employment.** Earnings, establishment dynamics, vacancy postings, occupational composition, or contractor-specific adjustment would make the story feel larger and more structural.
- **Compare recipient-location versus place-of-performance incidence conceptually.** The paper’s choice is sensible, but the broader implication is that administrative spending may look geographically diffuse in political rhetoric while economically concentrating at headquarters nodes.

If the authors could only make one “bigger” move, it would be: **turn the paper from “effect of USAID cuts on local employment” into “where the domestic beneficiaries of foreign aid actually are.”**

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Before this paper, the natural public impression is that foreign aid primarily affects foreign recipients, while any domestic effects are either diffuse or politically symbolic. Economists know procurement can matter locally, but the domestic footprint of foreign-aid contracting is poorly measured.

### Tension
A huge federal policy retrenchment occurred. If foreign aid dollars pass through U.S. contractors, then terminating aid contracts might impose meaningful domestic labor-market costs. But it is unclear whether those costs are spread broadly across the country or concentrated in a few contractor hubs.

### Resolution
The paper finds a negative employment effect in professional services and retail-consumption spillovers, operating through reduced hiring rather than increased separations. But the central result is that the effect is overwhelmingly concentrated in the D.C. metro area; outside that cluster, there is little detectable domestic employment fallout.

### Implications
This changes how we think about the domestic political economy of foreign aid and, more broadly, about federal procurement: politically salient national programs may generate highly localized economic dependence. Administrative retrenchment may bite through missing hires rather than visible layoffs, and the places that bear the cost may be much narrower than the politics suggests.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
It has the ingredients, but the arc is underdeveloped and slightly conflicted.

At present, the paper starts by promising a broad national domestic-employment story, then discovers that the effect is really a D.C.-specific story, and then treats that as almost a caveat. In fact, that is the story. The “outside DMV it vanishes” result should not feel like an awkward limitation discovered in Table 1 column 5; it should be the paper’s organizing revelation.

So yes: the paper currently risks feeling like **a collection of results looking for a story**, because it presents:
- main employment effect,
- hiring effect,
- sectoral spillovers,
- DMV exclusion,
- placebo weirdness,

without clearly telling the reader which result is the centerpiece.

### What story should it be telling?
This one:

1. **A nationally dramatic policy shock occurred.**
2. **Everyone assumed its domestic footprint might be broad.**
3. **In fact, the footprint was narrow and clustered.**
4. **Within that cluster, the shock looked like a hiring freeze with local consumption spillovers.**
5. **Therefore, the domestic incidence of foreign-aid procurement is much more spatially concentrated than its politics.**

That is a real narrative.

For AER positioning, the more interesting claim is that **the domestic constituency created by foreign-aid procurement is much smaller and more geographically concentrated than many people would assume**.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

Right now the gap is mostly a **framing and ambition problem**, with some **scope** issues.

### Framing problem
The paper undersells its best idea. It is written like a competent event-study paper on a narrow policy shock. It should be written like a paper about the hidden domestic geography of foreign-aid procurement and the place-based incidence of administrative retrenchment.

### Scope problem
The analysis feels a bit thin for AER if the ultimate result is “effect in one sector, one region, one mechanism.” For a top journal, the paper likely needs either:
- more direct evidence on the contractor geography and why the incidence is so concentrated,
- richer mechanism evidence,
- or a broader comparative payoff showing this is a general feature of federal procurement.

### Novelty problem
The setting is novel enough, but the empirical language makes it sound less novel than it is. “Shift-share DiD on local employment” is not a contribution. “Foreign aid’s domestic footprint is a narrow beltway labor market, not a national jobs program” is.

### Ambition problem
The current paper is careful but somewhat safe. It reports effects but does not fully cash out the bigger conceptual implications:
- Who are the domestic beneficiaries of globally framed spending?
- How concentrated are they?
- What does that imply for political support, retrenchment, and regional dependence?

That is the ambitious version.

### Single most impactful advice
**Rebuild the paper around the concentration result: this is not a paper showing that USAID cuts reduced employment; it is a paper showing that the domestic economic incidence of foreign-aid procurement is overwhelmingly concentrated in the Washington contractor cluster.**

If they make that one change seriously—not cosmetically, but in title, introduction, result order, and discussion—the paper becomes much more distinctive.
