## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple and potentially interesting question: when the U.S. abruptly dismantles a major foreign-aid procurement apparatus, who in the United States bears the domestic labor-market cost? Using the 2025 termination of most USAID contracts, the paper argues that the shock reduced professional-services employment through a hiring freeze, but that the effect was overwhelmingly concentrated in the Washington, D.C. metro area rather than spread across the country.

Why should a busy economist care? Because the paper sits at the intersection of procurement, place-based labor demand, and the political economy of foreign aid: it tries to show that “foreign aid” is also a domestic spending program, but one whose U.S. incidence is extremely geographically concentrated.

Does the paper articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs? Not quite. The opening question is decent, but the introduction initially sounds like a broad national story about the domestic employment effects of foreign aid procurement, while the paper’s own most interesting result is nearly the opposite: there is not much of a diffuse national labor-market story; this is mostly a D.C.-area agglomeration story. The introduction takes too long to reveal that the headline is concentration, not simply job loss.

### The pitch the paper should have

“Foreign aid is commonly debated as money sent abroad, but much of it is first spent through U.S.-based contractors. When the U.S. terminated most USAID contracts in 2025, did that produce meaningful job losses across American communities—or were the domestic effects concentrated in the small set of places where the aid industry is actually located? Using county-level variation in preexisting contractor exposure, I show that the labor-market effects were real but highly localized: the shock generated a hiring freeze in professional services and spillovers to local consumption, almost entirely within the Washington, D.C. contractor cluster. The paper’s broader point is that the domestic incidence of globally oriented government spending can be far narrower, and more place-based, than national political rhetoric suggests.”

That is a sharper and more honest AER-style pitch than “first causal estimate of domestic employment effects of foreign aid procurement.”

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper’s contribution is to show that the domestic employment incidence of USAID procurement cuts was not broadly national but highly concentrated in the D.C. contractor cluster, operating mainly through reduced hiring rather than separations.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest papers?

Only partly. The paper gestures toward three literatures—fiscal multipliers, foreign aid, and administrative shocks—but it does not yet clearly separate itself from nearby work on local government spending multipliers and spatial incidence of federal procurement. Right now the contribution risks reading as: “another local labor-demand shock paper, this time using USAID.” That is not enough.

The real differentiator is narrower and more interesting:
1. **The spending category is globally targeted but domestically intermediated.**
2. **The local incidence is highly concentrated in a specialized contractor cluster.**
3. **The labor-market adjustment occurs through hiring freezes, not layoffs.**

Those three elements together are the paper’s identity. The introduction currently underplays that bundle.

### World question or literature gap?

The paper starts with a world question, which is good: what happened to American workers when USAID contracts were terminated? But it then lapses into “this contributes to three literatures,” with the first contribution framed as a category extension—another spending multiplier setting, another shock. That weakens it. The strongest framing is about the world: **When globally oriented state capacity is dismantled, where do domestic economic costs appear, and through what margins do they adjust?**

That is much stronger than “first causal estimate in this setting.”

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?

Not cleanly. At present they might say: “It’s a DiD on USAID contract terminations and county employment.” That is not good enough. You want them to say: “It shows that foreign aid has a domestic footprint, but almost all of that footprint sits in the D.C. aid-contractor cluster, and the adjustment happens through missing hires.”

### What would make the contribution bigger?

Specific possibilities:

- **Lean much harder into geography and agglomeration.** The biggest result is not the average treatment effect; it is that the effect disappears outside DMV. That should become the centerpiece. The paper should ask why the aid industry is spatially concentrated and what that means for the domestic political economy of foreign aid.
- **Upgrade the mechanism from ‘hiring freeze’ to a broader labor-market adjustment margin.** If possible, show occupational composition, wage adjustment, vacancies, or worker reallocation. “Hires down, separations flat” is suggestive but still thin as a mechanism.
- **Make the comparison more conceptually ambitious.** Compare USAID procurement to other forms of federal spending: defense procurement, federal civilian employment, or other internationally oriented agencies. The paper would become much larger if it asked whether foreign-aid procurement is unusually clustered relative to other government spending categories.
- **Push the political-economy framing.** If domestic beneficiaries are unusually concentrated in politically atypical places, that says something broader about why aid has weak domestic coalitions. That could be a real AER conversation if developed carefully.
- **Broaden outcomes to show local equilibrium effects.** Retail spillovers are a start, but a bigger paper would speak to local earnings, business activity, migration, commercial real estate, or worker transitions. Right now the spillover evidence feels like a side result rather than a broader statement.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

### Closest neighbors

The closest conversation is probably with papers on local fiscal multipliers and geographic incidence of government spending, not the classic foreign-aid effectiveness literature. Likely neighbors include:

- **Moretti (2010)** on local multipliers and tradable/nontradable spillovers.
- **Shoag (2010)** on government spending and local labor markets.
- Possibly **Greenstone, Mas, and Nguyen-style place-based labor demand papers**, depending on framing.
- On procurement specifically, the paper should probably be talking to work on **defense procurement**, **federal contracting**, and **government-dependent local economies** more directly than it currently does.
- If it wants the political-economy angle, it should probably engage work on **concentrated benefits and diffuse politics**, and perhaps state capacity / bureaucracy literatures.

### How should it position itself relative to neighbors?

Mostly **build on and redirect**, not attack. The right posture is:

- Existing multiplier papers show that geographically concentrated government spending can move local labor markets.
- This paper adds a distinctive case where the spending is politically discussed as foreign, but economically intermediated through a domestic, highly clustered contractor ecosystem.
- The surprise is not merely “procurement matters”; it is **where** it matters and **how narrow** that geography is.

The paper should not overclaim that it transforms the foreign-aid literature. That is too far from where the empirical action is.

### Is the paper positioned too narrowly or too broadly?

Oddly, both.

- **Too broadly** when it claims to contribute to foreign aid effectiveness in a major way. Documenting domestic contractor employment incidence is not really an answer to Burnside-Dollar/Easterly/Deaton style questions.
- **Too narrowly** when it reads like a single-agency shock paper with one treatment and a few sectoral outcomes.

It needs a cleaner lane: **local incidence and political economy of globally oriented procurement**.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?

It seems underconnected to:
- **Federal procurement / defense spending / government contracting** literatures.
- **Spatial concentration / agglomeration / industry clusters** literatures.
- **Political economy of distributive politics and concentrated interests**.
- Potentially **public administration / state capacity / bureaucracy** work, especially if the paper wants to say something broader about dismantling agencies.

The foreign aid effectiveness citations feel ornamental. They are not the paper’s true interlocutors.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?

Not yet. The best conversation is not “does foreign aid have domestic costs?” in the abstract. The best conversation is: **what is the domestic spatial incidence of outward-facing government functions, and how does that shape the politics of those functions?** That is a more original and more interesting framing.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Before this paper, most economists would think of USAID as a foreign-facing program whose domestic effects, if any, are secondary and probably diffuse.

### Tension

But foreign aid is implemented through domestic contractors. So a major aid cut may actually be a domestic labor-demand shock. The key puzzle is whether those domestic costs are spread across many American places or concentrated in a narrow contractor cluster.

### Resolution

The paper finds a measurable decline in professional-services employment and hiring in exposed counties, but then reveals that the entire effect is essentially a Washington-area phenomenon.

### Implications

The domestic beneficiaries of foreign aid procurement are much more geographically concentrated than the politics surrounding aid would suggest; therefore, the domestic coalition for foreign aid may be narrower, more elite, and less politically broad-based than conventional rhetoric implies.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the ingredients, but the arc is not fully controlled. Right now it reads like this:

1. big national shock,
2. employment falls,
3. hires fall,
4. some sectoral spillovers,
5. oh, by the way, everything disappears outside DMV.

That ordering is backwards for storytelling. The DMV concentration result is not a qualification tucked into section 4.4; it is the paper’s central revelation. Without it, the paper is standard. With it, the paper has a distinctive story.

### What story should it be telling?

The story should be:

- **Setup:** Foreign aid looks foreign.
- **Tension:** In practice it supports a domestic contractor ecosystem; maybe dismantling it has broad domestic fallout.
- **Resolution:** The domestic fallout exists, but it is overwhelmingly concentrated in one specialized metro cluster and works through foregone hiring.
- **Implications:** This sharp spatial concentration helps explain both the economics and politics of aid: real domestic beneficiaries exist, but they are too geographically narrow to create a mass political constituency.

That is a coherent AER-worthy narrative idea. The current draft only partially tells it.

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party?

I would lead with: **“Cutting USAID did not create broad job losses across America; it mostly hit the D.C. area’s aid-contractor labor market, and mainly by preventing hires rather than triggering layoffs.”**

That is the memorable fact.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

Economists would lean in modestly—more than for a routine DiD, less than for a truly field-defining result. The lean-in moment comes from the **concentration** result, not the coefficient size. “Foreign aid has a domestic footprint, but it is basically an agglomeration in Washington” is an interesting sentence.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Probably one of these:
- “Is this just a D.C. federal-contracting story rather than a foreign-aid story?”
- “How different is USAID from defense or other procurement?”
- “Does this tell us anything general about the domestic political economy of outward-facing government spending?”
- “If the effects vanish outside DMV, is the right paper about agglomeration and contractor ecosystems rather than aid?”

Those are exactly the questions the paper should be trying to answer more directly.

### If findings are modest, is that okay?

Yes—provided the paper embraces the modesty as the point. The null outside DMV is interesting. In fact, it is more interesting than a diffuse small effect. But the paper must stop treating that result as a limitation and instead present it as the central substantive discovery.

At present, the paper half does this. It says the concentration “transforms the policy interpretation,” which is right. It should go further: **the absence of broad national employment effects is itself the finding that changes how we think about the domestic incidence of aid.**

Not enough. The good stuff is:

2. the margin is hiring, not layoffs;

Those should be on page 1, not revealed gradually.

### Is the conclusion adding value?

Some, but not enough. The best sentence in the paper is the one about geographic mismatch between who benefits from aid procurement and who votes on aid policy. That belongs at the center of the paper, not near the end.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

At the moment, the gap is mainly a mix of **framing problem** and **ambition problem**, with a bit of **scope problem**.

- **Framing problem:** The paper is still selling itself as a clever estimate of the domestic employment effects of USAID cuts. That is not quite enough. The sharper paper is about the domestic spatial incidence and political economy of globally oriented procurement.
- **Ambition problem:** The paper is competent but safe. It documents one shock, one main sector, a hires/separations decomposition, and a couple spillovers. For AER, the paper needs to make a broader claim that matters beyond USAID.
- **Scope problem:** Once the non-DMV null appears, the paper becomes really about a contractor cluster. That is potentially interesting, but then the paper needs to lean into cluster economics, procurement concentration, or political economy—not remain a narrow policy note.

### Be honest: how far is it from an AER paper?

In current form, fairly far. Not because the topic is uninteresting, but because the paper has not yet converted a topical shock into a general economic insight. Top-field readers will ask: what do I learn here beyond “D.C. contractors were exposed to USAID cuts”? The current draft does not fully answer that.

### The single most impactful piece of advice

**Reframe the paper around geographic concentration and domestic political economy—make the headline finding that foreign-aid procurement creates a highly localized U.S. contractor cluster, so dismantling aid is a place-based labor shock in Washington rather than a broad national employment event.**

If the author only changes one thing, it should be that.
