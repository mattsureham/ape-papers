## 1. Idea Fidelity

The paper broadly pursues the original manifest: it uses French DVF transaction data, school catchment polygons, and a spatial RDD at REP/non-REP collège sector boundaries to study housing price capitalization. It also follows the intended question of whether the net capitalization of REP/REP+ designation is negative, consistent with stigma.

That said, the paper misses a key element of the original idea’s ambition: the manifest framed the design as identifying the net capitalization of *policy designation* at school-sector boundaries, with explicit attention to the possibility of a fuzzy design due to derogations/private schooling and to separating resource effects from stigma. The paper ultimately estimates a cross-sectional boundary discontinuity in prices long after the 2015 reform, but then interprets this as a “stigma” effect. That is a much stronger claim than the design can support. In practice, the empirical design identifies the capitalization of being assigned to a REP catchment bundle, not the causal effect of the public label per se, and not cleanly the resource/stigma decomposition envisioned in the manifest. The paper also does not use actual attendance, private school take-up, or reform timing, despite the manifest highlighting these as central to interpretation.

## 2. Summary

This paper studies whether French priority education designation (REP/REP+) is capitalized into nearby housing prices. Using DVF universe property transactions matched to collège catchment boundaries, the author estimates a spatial regression discontinuity comparing properties on opposite sides of REP/non-REP boundaries and finds a 2–4 percent lower price on the REP side.

The topic is important and potentially well suited to the AER: Insights format: France provides rich administrative data, many treatment boundaries, and a policy setting where enhanced school resources coexist with a possibly stigmatizing label. However, in its current form the paper overstates what the design identifies, and the main interpretation is not yet credible.

## 3. Essential Points

1. **The design does not identify “stigma” as opposed to pre-existing neighborhood differences or the overall school-assignment bundle.**  
   The central interpretation is that a negative discontinuity means “the labeling channel dominates the resource channel.” But with only post-2020 cross-sectional transactions, the paper cannot distinguish stigma from: lower peer composition, worse neighborhood amenities, crime, different school quality unrelated to REP resources, or pre-existing socioeconomic sorting that informed both REP designation and boundary placement. A boundary RDD can identify a local discontinuity in assigned school sector, but not the mechanism behind it. To make a stigma claim, the paper needs either (i) timing variation around designation/reform, e.g. a difference-in-discontinuities or event-study design around 2015 (and perhaps 2017 for REP+ primary-school resource changes, if relevant), or (ii) direct evidence that perceived label salience changed while underlying neighborhood quality did not. Without this, the paper should be reframed as estimating the capitalization of REP assignment, not stigma.

2. **The continuity assumption is not persuasive in this setting, and the current diagnostics are insufficient.**  
   The major threat is that catchment boundaries may coincide with genuine neighborhood discontinuities. The paper acknowledges some covariate imbalance, but the problem is deeper: school sector boundaries are administrative objects drawn in response to geography, transport, capacity, and population composition. In urban France they may track roads, rail lines, social-housing edges, commune/quartier boundaries, or historically segregated neighborhoods. Boundary-segment fixed effects do not solve this; they absorb level differences across segments, but not within-segment discontinuities exactly at the border. Likewise, the McCrary test on transactions is not very informative here: smooth density of transactions does not rule out sorting in the housing stock or persistent composition differences in neighborhoods. The paper needs much stronger validation: balance in pre-determined census covariates, land use, building age, housing type, social housing share, income/unemployment proxies, and tests excluding boundaries that overlap major roads, rivers, rail lines, or commune borders. As written, the identification strategy is too weak for the paper’s claims.

3. **There are serious internal inconsistencies in the estimates and interpretation that must be fixed before the results can be evaluated.**  
   Several tables appear to reverse signs or mix estimands. In Table 1/Panel A the coefficient is positive, but the text interprets this as a REP penalty because prices are “higher on the non-REP side”; this needs precise definition of treatment coding and estimand. Table 4 is especially problematic: the pooled estimate is positive, the REP-only estimate is negative, and the REP+ estimate is implausibly large at 23.7 percent, yet all are described as discounts. These are not minor presentation issues; they make it impossible to know what is being estimated. The paper also inconsistently states the apartment share is higher on the REP side in the text but lower in the summary statistics table. Until the coding, signs, sample construction, and interpretation are fully reconciled, the empirical findings are not reliable.

## 4. Suggestions

This is a promising paper idea, and I think it could become a useful contribution if the authors narrow the claim and strengthen the design. My suggestions below are intended in that spirit.

First, I would strongly encourage a **substantial reframing of the paper’s research question**. In the current version, the paper asks whether priority education “stigmatizes” neighborhoods, but the design can only credibly address whether housing markets discount residence in a REP catchment relative to an adjacent non-REP catchment. That is still an interesting result. In fact, a cleaner and more defensible title would emphasize “housing price capitalization of assignment to priority education sectors” rather than “stigma.” If the authors want to keep the stigma language, it should be presented as one interpretation among several, not as the causal mechanism established by the evidence.

Second, the paper would benefit enormously from exploiting **policy timing**, which is currently left on the table despite being central to the institutional story. The 2015 reform is the natural source of identification if the question is about designation. A much stronger design would compare the same boundary areas before and after REP/REP+ redesignation, ideally using transactions from several years before and after 2015. Even if DVF coverage is imperfect pre-2019 in some public versions, the authors should explore what is feasible. A difference-in-discontinuities design—comparing price jumps at boundaries that become REP/non-REP after the reform relative to stable boundaries—would go much further toward identifying capitalization of designation rather than long-standing neighborhood disadvantage. If a full pre/post design is impossible, the paper should say so clearly and moderate the claims accordingly.

Third, I recommend a much more systematic **boundary validation exercise**. The authors should characterize the boundaries themselves: what fraction coincide with commune borders, IRIS boundaries, major roads, rivers, rail tracks, or visible urban barriers? A useful table would report results after progressively excluding such boundaries. Similarly, map figures would be very helpful: show several representative boundaries, both those with small estimated discontinuities and those with very large ones. The current REP+ estimate is so large that it almost certainly reflects boundaries placed along major socioeconomic divides. Visual evidence could make that much clearer.

Relatedly, the paper should test continuity in **predetermined neighborhood covariates**, not just transaction-level housing characteristics. Commune- or IRIS-level census variables from INSEE—income, unemployment, education, share social housing, immigrant share, family structure, population density, dwelling age, tenure mix—would be highly informative. Even though these are measured at coarser geography, they can still be assigned locally near boundaries and tested for jumps. If there are discontinuities in these variables at the same borders, the identifying assumption is undermined. If not, confidence in the design would rise materially.

Fourth, the empirical implementation should more closely match the structure of a **spatial RDD with many boundaries**. Pooling all properties by signed distance to the nearest boundary is not obviously appropriate when boundaries differ substantially in urban form, market thickness, and treatment intensity. The boundary-segment fixed-effects specification is a good start, but I would push further. For example:
- estimate boundary-specific discontinuities and report their distribution;
- weight boundaries more transparently, perhaps by effective sample size or inverse variance;
- show whether results are driven by a handful of large metropolitan areas, especially Seine-Saint-Denis and Marseille;
- report leave-one-département-out or leave-one-metro-area-out estimates;
- cluster at a higher spatial level where appropriate, or use randomization-inference style checks across boundaries.

Fifth, the paper should engage more directly with the **fuzzy assignment issue** mentioned in the manifest. If roughly 10 percent of households obtain derogations and private schooling is common, the treatment is not simply “school actually attended,” but rather residence in a sector with a different default assignment. That is still a meaningful treatment for housing markets—buyers care about default public assignment—but the paper should say this carefully. If data on private-school availability or local private enrollment rates are available, the authors could test whether capitalization is weaker where opting out is easier. That would both sharpen interpretation and connect nicely to the French context and the Fack-Grenet literature.

Sixth, I would recommend a clearer distinction between **REP and REP+** in both institutions and interpretation. As written, the paper bundles them in the main specification even though the policy packages differ and the geography of REP+ is much more extreme. Since the REP+ estimate is implausibly large and likely contaminated by boundary confounding, one possibility is to make REP-only the main analysis and treat REP+ as a separate, exploratory exercise. Another is to compare REP to non-REP and REP+ to REP, which may be more policy-relevant if both sides are disadvantaged but resource intensity differs. In its current form, the REP+/non-REP contrast is probably too confounded to support strong claims.

Seventh, the paper needs a careful **audit of coding and exposition**. The sign conventions should be harmonized throughout: define exactly whether the coefficient is the effect of being on the REP side or the non-REP side, and ensure every table and sentence follows the same convention. Likewise, the discrepancy in apartment shares and the odd mixture of positive and negative coefficients must be fixed. I would also suggest simplifying the main table structure: one main table with a clearly defined estimand, then robustness tables with the same sign convention. Right now, the presentation obscures rather than clarifies the result.

Eighth, I think the authors should reconsider the use and interpretation of the **McCrary density test**. In this context, smooth transaction density near the boundary does not say much about whether the housing stock or neighborhood composition changes discretely at the boundary. It is fine as a supplementary diagnostic, but it should not be presented as major evidence for identification. More probative would be tests on the characteristics of the stock of dwellings, cadastral parcels, building footprints, and neighborhood demographics.

Ninth, the paper could be strengthened by exploiting the richness of the DVF data more fully. For example:
- use price per square meter as an outcome in addition to log total price;
- include finer controls or fixed effects for property type and perhaps municipality-by-quarter;
- if repeated sales can be identified, estimate a repeated-sales specification to absorb time-invariant property quality;
- show whether effects are stronger for family-sized dwellings, where school assignment should matter more, than for studios or investor-oriented apartments;
- test whether effects vary with distance to private schools or with measures of school competition.

Finally, I encourage the authors to tone down some of the more sweeping claims in the discussion. A statement such as “the label dominates the resource channel” is too strong for the current evidence. A more measured conclusion would be: *residence in a REP catchment is associated with lower housing prices at nearby administrative boundaries, suggesting that any resource capitalization does not offset the negative bundle of signals and amenities associated with those catchments.* That would still be interesting and policy relevant, while remaining closer to what the design can sustain.

Overall, the paper is built around a very good question and a potentially valuable data/design combination. But for publication in a journal like AER: Insights, the identification argument must be much tighter, and the interpretation must be brought into line with the empirical design.
