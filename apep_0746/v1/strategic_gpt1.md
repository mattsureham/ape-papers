## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether being assigned to a publicly labeled “priority education” school zone in France raises or lowers nearby housing prices. Using property transactions near school catchment boundaries, it finds that homes on the REP side of the boundary sell for about 2–4 percent less, suggesting that the market values attached to the designation are negative on net, despite the extra school resources those zones receive.

Why should a busy economist care? Because this is not just a France school-finance paper. It speaks to a broader question in public economics: when governments target resources by publicly labeling places or people as disadvantaged, does the label itself create offsetting costs?

**Does the paper articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Mostly, but not quite. The opening is strong and readable, but the pitch gets muddied by moving too quickly from “negative net capitalization” to “stigma.” The paper’s actual finding is cleaner and more credible than its interpretation: **designation is negatively capitalized on net**. The paper should lead with that. “Stigma” can be one mechanism, but it cannot be the headline claim if the paper cannot separate label effects from peer composition and neighborhood signals.

**The pitch the paper should have:**

> Governments often target extra resources to disadvantaged places, but doing so usually requires publicly identifying those places. This paper asks whether that public designation raises or lowers local housing demand. Using French school catchment boundaries, I show that homes assigned to “priority education” zones sell for 2–4 percent less than homes just across the boundary, even though priority schools receive substantially more resources. The central fact is that the market capitalization of targeted school designation is negative on net, revealing a potentially important hidden cost of place-based targeting.

That is the AER-relevant pitch. It is about the economics of targeting, labeling, and capitalization—not just “a spatial RD about school zones in France.”

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:**  
The paper provides large-scale boundary-based evidence that assignment to a targeted, publicly designated education zone is negatively capitalized into housing prices, implying that the net market value of the designation-resource bundle is negative.

### Is this clearly differentiated from the closest papers?
Not yet sharply enough.

The paper is clearly adjacent to the school-quality capitalization literature, but it needs to explain more forcefully why this is **not** just another school-boundary house-price paper. The key differentiator is:

- classic papers estimate capitalization of **school quality or school assignment bundles**;
- this paper studies capitalization of **policy designation under compensatory funding**, where the treated side gets more resources but carries an adverse public signal.

That is a real distinction. But the introduction keeps slipping into “this isolates stigma from quality,” which is too strong and invites resistance. It does not isolate stigma; it identifies negative net capitalization of a designation that comes with resources.

### WORLD question or LITERATURE gap?
At its best, this is framed as a question about the world:

- Do targeted place-based policies impose hidden market penalties because of the way beneficiaries are labeled?

That is good. But the paper sometimes retreats into weaker literature-gap framing:

- “extends school boundary capitalization literature to France”
- “adds to growing use of French administrative data”

Those are not top-journal contribution statements. They are supportive context, not the main point.

### Could a smart economist explain what is new?
Right now, they would probably say:

> “It’s a school-boundary housing-capitalization paper in France showing lower prices in priority zones.”

That is not enough.

What you want them to say is:

> “It shows that when governments target extra school resources by publicly labeling neighborhoods as disadvantaged, the label/resource bundle is negatively capitalized in housing markets. So targeting may have hidden equilibrium costs.”

That is much stronger.

### What would make this contribution bigger?
Several possibilities:

1. **Separate label from underlying disadvantage more directly.**  
   The paper itself admits this is the key limitation. A redesignation event, difference-in-discontinuities, or timing-based analysis would make the contribution much bigger because it would move from “negative net capitalization of assignment” toward “effect of public designation per se.”

2. **Connect to broader targeting design.**  
   The paper would feel bigger if it showed that the French case is an example of a more general problem: means-tested or place-based targeting can create informational externalities via public labels. A formal conceptual framing or simple model would help.

3. **Show what exactly markets are pricing.**  
   A mechanism section distinguishing school resource improvements, peer composition, and neighborhood signal would greatly enlarge the paper’s intellectual scope. Even descriptive evidence on amenities, crime, school outcomes, or demographics near boundaries could sharpen the claim.

4. **Shift the headline away from “school quality” toward “policy design.”**  
   That alone would make the paper feel less like a specialized urban/education design paper and more like a public economics paper about the unintended consequences of targeting.

On the policy side, it should also speak to place-based policy and stigma/labeling literatures, though those are less crisply anchored here. Depending on what the authors can support, relevant adjacent conversations may include:

- place-based policy evaluations such as **Busso, Gregory, and Kline (2013)** or related enterprise-zone work
- housing-market capitalization of local disamenities/signals
- statistical discrimination and labeling in markets

### How should the paper position itself?
**Build on, not attack.**

The right positioning is:

- Black/Gibbons/Bayer show that school assignment boundaries matter for housing prices.
- This paper brings that logic to a different object: not school effectiveness per se, but the capitalization of a targeted policy designation that bundles extra resources with an adverse public signal.

That is a clean extension with a conceptual twist. No need to claim that prior literature “cannot separate stigma from quality” unless the paper really can.

### Too narrow or too broad?
Currently, it is oddly both.

- **Too narrow** in the empirical self-presentation: a French REP boundary paper.
- **Too broad** in the interpretive leap: “stigma” and “hidden cost of place-based policies” are asserted more strongly than the design can yet carry.

The paper needs a tighter middle position:
- this is a **school-boundary capitalization paper with implications for the design of targeted policies**.

### What literature does it seem unaware of?
Two gaps:

1. **Targeting and stigma / labeling in public economics.**  
   The paper needs a more serious engagement with the economics of means-testing, category-based targeting, and public labels. Even if the exact settings differ, the conceptual kinship matters.

2. **Urban equilibrium / neighborhood signal literature.**  
   Housing markets capitalize bundles. The paper should more explicitly acknowledge that a school-zone designation may proxy for peers, future neighborhood trajectory, public service quality, or reputation. That would make the framing more honest and more connected to urban economics.

### Is it having the right conversation?
Not fully. Right now it is mostly in conversation with school-capitalization papers. That is necessary but not sufficient.

The more impactful conversation is:
- **How should governments target resources when targeting itself creates informational and equilibrium costs?**

That is the conversation that could make the paper matter beyond education and France.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Governments target extra resources to disadvantaged schools and neighborhoods. In principle, extra resources should make assigned areas more attractive. France is a particularly striking case because the resource differences are large and publicly salient.

### Tension
Targeting requires public designation. That designation may act as a negative signal about the neighborhood or the peer group, potentially offsetting or overwhelming the value of the extra resources. So the central tension is: **does targeted support help places, or does the public label attached to targeting depress demand?**

### Resolution
Housing prices are lower on the priority-education side of school catchment boundaries—about 2–4 percent lower on net.

### Implications
Targeted policies may impose hidden capitalization costs. Policymakers should recognize that a program’s resource transfer is not its full incidence; public designation can alter market perceptions and local asset values.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
It has the bones of one, but it is not fully disciplined. The paper alternates between three stories:

1. a school-boundary housing capitalization paper,
2. a stigma/labeling paper,
3. a French administrative data and spatial RD paper.

The result is a slightly unstable narrative.

**The story it should be telling:**  
This is a paper about the **equilibrium cost of publicly targeted aid**. The school-boundary design is the empirical vehicle, not the story itself. “France” is the setting, not the contribution. “Spatial RD” is the tool, not the headline.

Right now there are also signs of a results-first paper searching for a bigger claim. The REP+/REP split is especially damaging narratively: a 23.7 percent effect that the paper itself says is probably not interpretable as causal makes the whole story feel less settled. For positioning purposes, that result should be de-emphasized heavily or moved out of center stage.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?
I would say:

> “In France, homes assigned to priority-education zones sell for less than homes just across the school-boundary line, even though those zones receive more school resources.”

That is a good fact. It has tension built in.

### Would people lean in?
Yes—initially. The contrast between “more resources” and “lower prices” is inherently interesting.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Immediately:

> “Is that really the label, or is it just that REP zones are worse neighborhoods on dimensions the boundary design doesn’t wash out?”

That is the core issue. Not an econometric quibble—an interpretation and positioning issue.

If the paper can answer that better, the audience leans in further. If not, they start to downgrade it from “important result” to “interesting but unsurprising reduced-form fact.”

### Is the finding interesting if interpreted modestly?
Yes. Even without isolating pure stigma, the finding that the **net capitalization** of a compensatory school designation is negative is still interesting. The paper should lean into that modest but important interpretation. It does not need to oversell pure stigma to be publishable somewhere good.

But for AER-level excitement, it needs either:
- stronger separation of mechanisms, or
- much sharper framing around the economics of targeting and labeling.
