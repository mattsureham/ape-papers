## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a big policy question: when states confront insurgency in poor peripheral regions, can a strategy that combines development spending with security administration generate economic gains? Using India’s Integrated Action Plan for Naxal-affected districts, the paper argues that targeted block grants delivered through a joint civil-security structure were followed by faster growth in district nightlights, especially in more tribal and underserved places.

A busy economist should care because this is not just another place-based policy evaluation: it speaks to whether the state can build legitimacy and growth in conflict zones through “guns and roads” rather than through repression or development alone.

**Does the paper itself articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Not quite. The current opening is competent, but too generic and slightly literature-first. It takes a few paragraphs before the reader understands the real hook: a major government tried to operationalize the idea that security and development are complements, and this paper studies whether that strategy actually changed local economic activity. The introduction should get to that immediately and frame the paper around a real-world question, not around the existence of an unevaluated program.

**The pitch the paper should have:**

> Governments fighting insurgency often face a core strategic choice: do they try to pacify contested regions with force, with development, or with both at once? India’s Integrated Action Plan is a rare large-scale attempt to do both simultaneously, channeling discretionary development funds to Maoist-affected districts through a committee jointly run by civilian administrators and the police.  
>   
> This paper asks whether that combined state-building strategy translated into local economic gains. Using district-level nightlights over two decades, I show that IAP districts experienced faster post-2010 growth in economic activity than comparable districts, with especially large gains in high-tribal-share areas. The broader contribution is to the economics of conflict and state capacity: it provides evidence on whether bundling development with security can change economic trajectories in places where the state is weakest.

That version gives the reader the question, the policy experiment, the finding, and the broader stakes almost immediately.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Insurgency-affected regions are often poor, remote, and weakly governed. Governments must decide whether to respond through force, development, or some mix of the two. India chose the mixed strategy via the IAP.

### Tension
We have many theories saying security and development may complement each other, but little evidence on whether bundled security-development programs actually improve local economic conditions. Moreover, these places are selected precisely because they are poor, conflict-affected, and on unusual trajectories, making it difficult to know whether post-program gains reflect policy or pre-existing convergence.

### Resolution
The paper finds that IAP districts show faster post-2010 growth in nightlights, with smaller but still positive effects once one accounts for their prior upward trajectory. Gains appear especially large in more tribal districts.

### Implications
The implication is potentially important: integrated state-building interventions may produce economic improvements in contested territories, especially in highly marginalized regions. More broadly, the paper suggests that development policy in conflict zones may be more effective when embedded in a governance structure that coordinates with security administration.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
It has the ingredients, but the story is still underpowered. At times it reads like a collection of regression specifications organized around the pre-trends problem, rather than a paper with a strong conceptual spine.

The strongest story available is:

1. **States need a strategy for governing insurgent territories.**
2. **India tried a distinct institutional model: development administered jointly with security.**
3. **The key empirical question is whether that model changed economic trajectories.**
4. **The answer is yes, but more modestly than a naive comparison suggests, and especially in the most marginalized districts.**
5. **This informs how economists think about state-building, not just place-based grants.**

Right now, the paper’s center of gravity is “look, the DiD shrinks once I add trends.” That is not the right main character. The main character should be the state-building question.

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?
I’d say: **India tried to fight Maoist insurgency by giving local districts flexible development funds overseen jointly by civil administrators and the police, and those districts appear to have grown faster afterward—especially the more tribal ones.**

That is a reasonably good lead, because it combines a striking institutional fact with a substantive result.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
They would lean in at first because the policy object is unusual and important. But they may drift if the next sentence is just “measured by nightlights in a district-level DiD.” The interest is in the idea of integrated state-building; the paper must not let that idea collapse into a familiar reduced-form template.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Almost certainly: **“Is this really development, or just more electrification / military presence / measurement artifact?”**  
And second: **“What exactly was special about the security-development integration?”**

Those are strategic questions, not referee questions. The current draft does not have a satisfying answer because the evidence is too concentrated on a single proxy outcome and the mechanism discussion is thin.

### If findings are modest, is that still interesting?
Yes, potentially. A modest positive result is interesting here if framed correctly: conflict-zone state-building is hard, the program was not huge, and the fact that even a relatively modest grant appears to shift local economic activity in marginalized districts is meaningful.

But the paper has to **own the modesty** rather than apologizing for it. The current draft sometimes sounds like it discovered a big effect, then had to back off because of pre-trends. Better to say: in an exceptionally difficult environment, even a modest acceleration is policy-relevant.
