## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether a government can spur economic development in insurgency-affected areas by pairing development spending with the security state. Using India’s Integrated Action Plan in Naxal-affected districts, it argues that districts receiving this bundled intervention saw faster growth in nighttime lights, especially in tribal-majority places, suggesting that “guns and roads” may be complements rather than substitutes in state-building.

Why should a busy economist care? Because this is a first-order question about how states govern contested territory: whether development policy can work where state capacity is weak and violence is salient. That question sits at the intersection of development, political economy, conflict, and place-based policy.

**Does the paper itself articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Not quite. The current opening is competent, but it is too program-description-heavy too early and too literature-gap-heavy. The paper’s best hook is not “there is no rigorous evaluation of the IAP.” The hook is: **states around the world use development spending as part of counterinsurgency, but we know surprisingly little about whether such bundled state-building actually produces economic gains in contested regions.**

The introduction should lead with the world question, then present India’s IAP as an unusually clean and important test case.

### The pitch the paper should have

> Governments fighting insurgencies rarely choose between guns and development; in practice they deploy both. Yet economists still know little about whether this bundled strategy actually improves economic conditions in contested territory, or whether development spending in conflict zones is too distorted, too insecure, or too politicized to deliver growth.  
>   
> This paper studies India’s Integrated Action Plan, which sent flexible block grants to some of the country’s most insurgency-affected tribal districts under joint civilian-security oversight. Using district-level nightlights, I ask whether these districts experienced faster economic growth after designation. The core finding is that they did—especially in high-tribal-share districts—suggesting that coordinated security-development interventions can raise economic activity in marginalized conflict areas, though the magnitude is substantially smaller once one accounts for pre-existing convergence.

That is sharper, more world-facing, and more memorable.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:**  
The paper provides evidence that a bundled security-and-development place-based program in India’s Naxal-affected districts increased local economic activity, with especially large gains in tribal-majority districts.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?
Only partially. The paper names relevant literatures, but the differentiation is still muddy. Right now the reader may come away with: “This is a nightlights DiD on a conflict-area grant program.” That is not enough.

The paper needs to distinguish itself more clearly along one of these margins:

1. **Bundled security + development under joint governance** rather than aid alone, roads alone, or anti-violence policy alone.
2. **Economic development outcomes** rather than violence outcomes.
3. **State-building in marginalized internal frontiers** rather than generic place-based policy.
4. **Heterogeneous effects in tribal, weak-state regions**, which may be the most interesting substantive angle.

At present, the contribution is framed partly as “no one has evaluated this program,” which is true but not by itself AER-relevant. AER wants the answer to a broad question, not the filling of a local hole.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or filling a gap in a LITERATURE?
It starts with a world question, which is good, but then slips too quickly into “there is no rigorous causal evaluation of the IAP.” That is weaker. The paper should relentlessly frame itself as answering:

- Can development policy work in insurgency zones?
- Are security and development complements in state-building?
- Where are the returns to place-based investment highest: in the most disconnected, conflict-prone regions?

Those are world questions. The paper should subordinate the “no one has done IAP before” point.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?
Right now, maybe—but not confidently. They might say: “It’s a DiD on an Indian anti-Naxal development program using nightlights.” That is not enough.

The introduction should leave them saying something like:  
**“It shows that when the state couples development grants with security-adjacent governance in insurgent districts, economic activity rises, especially in tribal frontier regions.”**

That is a contribution. “Another DiD paper about X” is the current risk.

### What would make this contribution bigger?
Several possibilities:

- **Mechanism:** The paper currently cannot say whether the gains came from roads, electrification, administrative penetration, violence reduction, or fiscal flexibility. Even descriptive evidence on project composition, road-building, electrification, or public goods would make the story much larger.
- **Outcome expansion:** Nightlights alone makes this feel thin. Adding outcomes on roads, market access, public service delivery, migration, employment structure, or even conflict intensity would transform the ambition.
- **Comparison framing:** The strongest comparison is not IAP vs non-IAP in a generic sense, but **bundled counterinsurgency development vs ordinary development spending**. If the author can show that IAP-type districts outperformed districts receiving other schemes, or that the joint security-development governance structure mattered, the paper becomes much more original.
- **Political economy angle:** If the paper could connect the gains to state reach in historically excluded tribal regions, it would speak to much bigger debates about state capacity and inclusion.

The single biggest “bigger contribution” lever is: **move beyond nightlights as the only substantive payoff.**

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
States confronting insurgency often invest in both coercion and development, especially in peripheral regions where underdevelopment and weak state presence coincide.

### Tension
We do not know whether such bundled interventions actually improve local economic conditions. Development spending in conflict zones may fail because insecurity prevents implementation, elites capture resources, or aid is redirected toward control rather than growth. On the other hand, security may make development more productive, and development may make security more effective.

### Resolution
The paper finds that India’s IAP is associated with faster growth in district nightlights, with larger effects in high-tribal-share districts, though the magnitude is much smaller once one accounts for pre-existing convergence.

### Implications
The result suggests that integrated state-building interventions can yield economic gains in contested regions, particularly in highly marginalized areas. For economists, this bears on conflict policy, place-based development, and the returns to public investment where the state is weakest.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
It has the pieces, but the arc is not yet fully disciplined. The paper currently has a bit of a **results-first econometrics story** rather than a **substantive political economy story**.

The actual story should be:

1. These districts are places of weak state presence and chronic insurgency.
2. The state tried an unusual institutional response: flexible development spending tied to the security apparatus.
3. The key question is whether that strategy produced development rather than just administrative theater.
4. It appears to have done so, especially where deprivation was deepest.

That is a coherent narrative. Right now the paper instead spends too much narrative energy on specification progression and pre-trends. Necessary, yes—but editorially, that cannot be the story.

So: **not a collection of results, but still too close to one.** The paper needs to foreground the state-building question and let the econometric caveats support, rather than define, the narrative.

That is the best version of the fact.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
Some would lean in—especially development, political economy, and conflict economists—but not yet all. Why? Because the finding is interesting, but the current paper makes it sound narrower and more provisional than it needs to.

If presented well, the hook is strong: **Does development work in insurgency zones when paired with coercive state capacity?** That is a real lean-in question.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Almost immediately:

- “Is this actually development, or just electrification / more lights?”
- “Did violence fall too?”
- “What was the mechanism—roads, policing, or administrative reach?”
- “Why are effects larger in tribal districts?”
- “How unique is IAP relative to ordinary Indian spending?”

Those follow-up questions reveal both the paper’s promise and its current limitation. The paper generates exactly the right curiosity, but it cannot answer enough of it yet.

### If findings are modest: is the modest result interesting?
Yes, if framed properly. The preferred effect is not huge, but in these settings a moderate positive effect is inherently interesting. The key is that the paper should say:

- This is not a miracle.
- The naive estimate is overstated.
- Even after disciplining the comparison, there is still evidence of acceleration.
- In fragile, low-capacity conflict districts, a moderate positive effect is substantively meaningful.

That is a credible and interesting message. It should not read like “we still got significance after adding trends.” It should read like **“the true effect is smaller than naive comparisons suggest, but still consequential in one of the hardest environments for policy delivery.”**

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

### What would make it read better?

#### 1. Front-load the substantive insight
The current introduction takes too long to crystallize the main message. By paragraph two or three, the reader should already know:

- what IAP is,
- why it matters,
- what the main finding is,
- and why the tribal heterogeneity matters.

#### 2. Compress the measurement and estimator boilerplate
The paper over-explains standard DiD machinery relative to the importance of the substantive question. For an AER audience, this is deadweight in the main text. The pre-trend issue is important, but the exposition can be tighter.

#### 3. Reorganize around questions, not specifications
The results section now reads as Column 1, Column 2, Column 3, Column 4. That is natural but not compelling. Better:

- **Did IAP districts grow faster?**
- **How much of that is pre-existing convergence?**
- **Where were the gains largest?**
- **What does that imply about the program’s mechanism or target population?**

That is much more readable.

#### 4. Bring the heterogeneity forward
The tribal-share result is probably the most memorable substantive finding after the headline estimate. It should not feel like an add-on. If the paper’s real claim is that integrated state-building matters most in historically excluded frontier districts, then this belongs near the center of the argument.

#### 5. Reduce the emphasis on “nightlights literature”
Nightlights are a tool, not a contribution. Do not sell measurement as one of three coequal literatures.

#### 6. Move some inferential detail out of the main text
The discussion of clustering, placebo implementation detail, and some specification caveats can be tighter in the body and more fully elaborated in appendix if needed. Right now the reader sees too much scaffolding.

#### 7. Strengthen the conclusion
The current conclusion mostly summarizes. It should instead answer:  
**What should economists update on?**  
For example:

- Development spending can produce growth even in insurgent districts.
- Joint civilian-security governance may matter.
- Returns may be highest in marginalized tribal peripheries.
- But the policy is not a silver bullet, and naive before-after comparisons exaggerate the gains.

That would add value.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The gap is mostly a combination of **scope problem** and **framing problem**, with some **ambition problem**.

### Framing problem
The paper has a potentially AER-worthy question but does not yet tell it at sufficient altitude. It is still written too much like a careful policy evaluation and not enough like a paper about how states build development in contested territory.

### Scope problem
One outcome—nightlights—is too thin for the size of the question. For AER, the paper likely needs more evidence on what changed on the ground: roads, electrification, service access, market integration, conflict, or some clear mechanism. Without that, the paper remains suggestive.

### Ambition problem
The current paper is competent and self-aware, but safe. It is careful in exactly the way a good field-journal paper is careful. AER needs a bigger conceptual payoff.

### Novelty problem
The novelty is real but not yet fully monetized. “First evaluation of IAP” is not enough. “Evidence that integrated security-development policy can raise economic activity in marginalized insurgency zones” is much better. Still, to make that novelty fully persuasive, the paper needs a stronger bridge from this case to a general proposition about state-building.

### Single most impactful piece of advice
**Reframe the paper around the broader question of whether integrated state-building works in contested territory, and add at least one concrete mechanism or non-nightlights outcome that shows what actually changed on the ground.**

If they can only change one thing, that is it. If the story remains “nightlights went up after a conflict-area grant program,” this is likely below AER. If it becomes “bundled security-development policy expanded economic activity and state reach in historically excluded insurgent districts, especially where deprivation was deepest,” with supporting evidence beyond luminosity, then it starts to look like an AER conversation.
