**Summary**

The paper studies the Integrated Action Plan—India’s combined security-and-development program launched in 2010 for 60 Maoist-affected tribal districts—and estimates its impact on satellite nightlights between 1994 and 2013. Using a TWFE DiD with district fixed effects and, crucially, district-specific linear trends, the preferred specification attributes a 17% increase in nightlights post-treatment, with larger effects in high Scheduled Tribe-share districts. The analysis highlights substantial pre-treatment convergence, which the author interprets as biasing naive DiD estimates upward and motivates the conservative trend-augmented specification.

**Essential Points**

1. **Identification is incomplete without the promised designs.** The manifest committed to two complementary strategies: a boundary DiD comparing villages just across IAP district borders and an RDD exploiting observable selection cutoffs. The paper relies solely on aggregate district-level TWFE regressions with trends, leaving the boundary and RDD plans unrealized. Without exploiting (a) close-border comparisons and (b) the selection rule, the stronger causal claims implied by the manifest cannot be delivered. Please implement at least one of those strategies (preferably both, if feasible) or explain clearly why they could not be executed. Otherwise, the evaluation of the IAP’s causal effect remains partial.

2. **Parallel-trends violation remains unresolved.** The event study shows systematic convergence across many pre-period years, and the placebo at 2005 produces a large fake effect, which rightly raises concern. Yet the reliance on district-specific linear trends assumes that the true counterfactual follows a linear path—a strong assumption given the nonlinear pre-trend pattern (e.g., fluctuations around –0.1 to –0.4). The paper needs to justify the linearity assumption more thoroughly (e.g., by showing goodness-of-fit or bounding the influence of flexible trends) or consider alternative approaches (e.g., synthetic control, matching on trends, or local projections). Without that, the preferred estimate may still be driven by misspecified counterfactual dynamics.

3. **Mechanisms and policy content remain vague.** While the IAP is described institutionally, the paper provides no insight into what was actually spent (security vs. roads vs. electricity) or how funds were absorbed. The interpretation that nightlights reflect development acceleration is plausible but weak without linking to intermediate investments or service delivery. Are there any data (even anecdotal or administrative) on expenditure composition, project shares, or complementary initiatives? Do districts differ in how the joint collector/police committee operated? The paper needs to either incorporate such evidence or temper claims about policy implications accordingly.

**Suggestions**

1. **Implement the boundary DiD or RDD (or both) if possible.** The manifest highlighted a sharp designation and the potential to compare IAP villages to those just outside the treated districts. You already have district-level nightlights and can likely map villages within a 25 km buffer; even if finer nightlights are unavailable, district-level spillovers could be proxied by using only border districts or adjacent districts. For the RDD, reconstruct the selection rule using observable criteria (LWE incidents, tribal share thresholds). Even a “fuzzy” RDD exploiting a continuous measure (e.g., tribal share or violence score) around the cutoff would significantly bolster causal claims. If these were infeasible, a short explanation should be added, perhaps in an appendix.

2. **Examine flexible trend specifications or alternative counterfactuals.** Since linear trends may not capture the observed nonlinearity, consider estimating models that allow for quadratic trends, splines, or use pre-treatment averages (e.g., taking districts’ fitted values from a pre-period regression and extrapolating). Another option is to match treated and control districts on pre-treatment growth paths and re-estimate the DiD on that subset. You might also explore synthetic control-style counterfactuals for groups of treated districts, or reestimate event studies with longer leads/lags to see if the positive post-treatment kink is robust. These robustness checks would better justify the choice of trends and strengthen confidence in the 17% estimate.

3. **Explore mechanisms and complementarities.** To understand whether “guns or roads” (or both) drove the effect, you can use any available administrative data on IAP-funded projects—even if imperfect. For instance, PMGSY road projects, schools built, or electrification schemes across districts might serve as proxies for development investment. Alternatively, exploit survey data (NSS, NFHS) to show improved public goods or reductions in conflict intensity correlating with the treatment. If granular spending data are unavailable, consider relating nightlight gains to baseline deprivation: do districts with worse roads benefit more? You already split by ST share; expanding heterogeneity to baseline connectivity or previous conflict intensity could give more insight into mechanisms.

4. **Clarify the interpretation of nightlights and address alternative explanations.** Nighttime luminosity may respond to changes other than economic development (e.g., intensified security lighting, electrification projects funded by the IAP, or even conflict dynamics). Discuss whether the IAP could have increased lights through security investments (e.g., more patrol camps) rather than pure economic growth. If possible, check whether carrying capacities (e.g., rural electrification progress, road density) improved differentially to support the development interpretation. This would also tie into policy relevance, as the paper’s narrative hinges on combined security-development spending.

5. **Expand on the later expansion and spillovers.** The paper briefly notes that the IAP later expanded, but it would help to investigate whether expansion indirectly affected control districts (anticipation, competition for funds, or displaced insurgent activity). A falsification test restricting the post-period to 2011–2012 (before most expansions) or excluding districts that later received the program could test for spillovers. Alternatively, a placebo DiD using districts that eventually became treated but were not in 2010 might show whether secular trends are similar.

6. **Improve the presentation of heterogeneity.** The tribal-share split is informative, but a continuous interaction (e.g., IAP × ST share) with appropriate controls could show whether the effect scales smoothly with deprivation. You might also interact treatment with baseline nightlight levels, literacy, or road density to see if returns diminish with prior development. Including such regressions in either the main text or appendix would deepen the policy discussion.

By addressing these points, the paper would deliver on its manifest promise, increase confidence in the causal claim, and enrich the understanding of how the IAP affected the Naxal corridor.
