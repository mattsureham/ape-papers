The paper asks whether locally implemented climate regulations polarize politics even when they are nationally popular, using France’s low-emission zones (ZFEs) as a case study. It links constituency-level election outcomes (2002–2024) to the spatial rollout of ZFEs and argues that “local conflict” around ZFEs largely reflects a pre-existing urban–rural cleavage rather than policy-induced polarization—while far-right vote shares *fall* after ZFE activation.

**What the first two paragraphs should say instead (the pitch the paper should have).**

> Linking twenty years of constituency election outcomes to the staggered geography of ZFE implementation, I show that the places targeted by ZFEs were already politically distinctive long before the policy existed: metropolitan constituencies have more fragmented party systems and lower far-right support. ## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution in one sentence.**  
The paper documents that France’s low-emission zones do not increase electoral fragmentation and are associated with lower far-right voting, while showing that the apparent “local polarization” around climate policy largely reflects pre-existing urban–rural political structure rather than policy-induced division.

**Is it clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?**  
Not yet. The intro cites relevant neighbors (Yellow Vests/carbon tax backlash; local energy infrastructure and elections; far-right drivers), but the differentiation reads like “this is another policy/elections paper, but in France and with ZFEs.” The sharper differentiation is: (i) multi-election, spatially targeted climate regulation; (ii) *political geography* as the main object (scale mismatch), not merely “policy effect on vote share”; and (iii) a message about interpretive errors when treated places are structurally different (urban cores).

**World-question vs literature-gap framing.**  
It’s currently split. The “world” question is strong—why climate policy can be nationally popular yet locally combustible—but the paper repeatedly retreats to “and this also illustrates modern DiD.” For AER positioning, the causal-methods point should be subordinate: *the world fact* is the scale mismatch and what it implies for designing/communicating the green transition.

**Can a smart economist summarize what’s new after reading the introduction?**  
They could say: “It’s a DiD on ZFEs and voting; ENP is null; far-right falls; big pre-trends.” That’s close to “another DiD paper about X.” What they won’t reliably say—unless the author pushes it harder—is: “This is fundamentally about how to interpret geographic backlash narratives in climate politics; the ‘conflict’ is mostly selection into treated geography, not polarization caused by the policy.”

**What would make the contribution bigger (specific suggestions).**
1. **Outcome upgrade:** ENP is a somewhat indirect proxy for “conflict.” A bigger outcome would be *direct measures of local conflict* plausibly tied to ZFEs: protests, petitioning, municipal rollbacks/delays, local council voting, litigation, enforcement intensity, or traffic-fine incidence. Even one strong, policy-proximate conflict outcome would make the “scale mismatch” claim feel real rather than rhetorical.
2. **Mechanism discipline:** The far-right decline is intriguing but currently hangs on a menu of speculative channels. AER-level contribution would benefit from committing to (and organizing around) one mechanism that is inherently “scale mismatch”: e.g., **within-metro distributional conflict (core vs peri-urban commuters)** vs **political sorting/party supply**.
3. **Comparative leverage:** France-only can work, but the general lesson would land harder with a comparison to another country’s LEZs or to another French spatial policy with similar geography (e.g., congestion charging, transit expansions, housing regulation), showing the “geography masquerading as backlash” pattern is not unique to ZFEs.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

**Setup (world before this paper).**  
Climate policies can be nationally legislated yet locally resisted; commentators interpret local resistance as evidence of polarization/backlash that threatens decarbonization.

**Tension (puzzle/gap).**  
Is local conflict actually caused by climate policy, or does climate policy simply land in places that are already politically distinct (urban vs non-urban), making correlation look like polarization?

**Resolution (what it finds).**  
ZFE areas were politically different long before ZFEs existed; once you account for that, there’s no detectable increase in party fragmentation and far-right vote share declines after activation; national climate bills pass comfortably.

**Implications (why beliefs/behavior should change).**  
Policymakers should not assume that place-based environmental regulation mechanically produces polarization; analysts should treat “backlash” narratives with skepticism when treated places are structurally distinct; political constraints may be more about geographic incidence and coalition geography than about “climate becoming a culture war issue” per se.

**Evaluation of arc.**  
The arc is present but not fully earned because “local conflict intensifies” is asserted more than demonstrated (electoral fragmentation is framed as conflict, but many readers won’t buy ENP-as-conflict without additional evidence). The paper is closest to a strong narrative when it says: **“climate policy activates a cleavage rather than creating it”**—but then it needs to show activation with something closer to conflict behavior (or be more modest and say “electoral polarization does not increase”).

**If it’s a collection of results looking for a story, what story should it tell?**  
Tell a single story: **“The geography of climate policy is the politics of climate policy.”** ZFEs are a case where national-level coalition building is easy because costs are geographically concentrated; local politics look contentious because the implementing jurisdictions are already distinctive; and the electoral response is not the feared far-right backlash.
