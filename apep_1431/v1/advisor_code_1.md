**Idea Fidelity**  
The paper largely pursues the manifest’s core ambition of documenting anticipatory bunching around the 2025 DMTO increase using the DVF register. It retains the focus on transaction timing, compositional shifts, and a staggered-difference-in-difference (DiD) logic across departments. However, it departs from the manifest in two important ways. First, instead of exploiting the administrative decision of the 73 departments that adopted the increase and the ~20 that did not, the paper infers “treatment” from retrospective jump magnitudes, yielding only 25 treated units and 51 controls. This self-selection of the treatment sample risks conflating the policy effect with idiosyncratic March anomalies. Second, the manifest emphasized exploiting the first-time-buyer exemption (250,000 EUR) to build an RDD component; the submitted version drops that angle entirely and offers no discussion of the exemption. These omissions weaken the promised identification richness.

**Summary**  
Using the DVF registry from 2021–2025, the paper argues that the April 2025 DMTO increase generated a statistically significant March surge in residential transactions (23 percent) and a post-reform hangover (38 percent). The surge is described as a “composition illusion,” driven by high-value buyers retiming purchases to avoid the higher tax, while lower-value markets show weaker volume responses. Placebo exercises, non-residential checks, and quintile heterogeneity analyses serve to bolster belief in a causal tax-driven effect.

**Essential Points**  
1. **Endogenous treatment classification undermines causal claims.** The paper classifies treated departments based on their own March 2025 jumps (excess ratios ≥1.15) rather than using the exogenous administrative choice of whether a department raised the DMTO. This circularity means the “treated” group is defined by the outcome of interest, making the DiD’s parallel-trends assumption implausible. You must obtain the actual list of adopters (73 departments) and non-adopters (~20) and re-estimate the effects using that assignment (or, if unavailable, provide strong justification/validation that your anomaly-based classification proxies the true policy adoption without mechanical bias).

2. **Evidence for the “composition illusion” is indirect and disconnected from the DiD framework.** The national-level March/February value spike (21.8 percent) is compelling, but it is presented separately from the DiD whose coefficients on average value are zero. The heterogeneity tables show changes across quintiles, but there is limited direct evidence that the march surge is disproportionately driven by high-value buyers (e.g., quantile regressions, transaction-level movements, or buyer characteristics). Include transaction-level analyses (price quantiles, model with individual-level value bins) within the DiD to document that the treatment effect is larger at higher transaction values, or at least exploit the DVF’s price variable more fully.

3. **Pre-trends and auxiliary validity checks are insufficiently explored.** While the paper mentions 48 pre-reform months and two placebo dates, the main text lacks event-study plots or formal tests of equal trends prior to March 2025. The potential non-parallelism between richer (treated) and poorer (control) departments—already evident in Table 1 baseline differences—could drive the estimated 2025 surge. Please present event-study coefficients spanning multiple pre-treatment months, and consider matching or weighting (e.g., synthetic control or exact matching on baseline transaction volumes) to guard against differential seasonality or upward trends in treated municipalities.

**Suggestions**  
- **Clarify and secure the treatment variable.** Prioritize acquiring the definitive list of departments that formally adopted the April DMTO increase. If the list is public (e.g., via departmental resolutions or press releases), cite it explicitly. If not available, justify the threshold-based classification rigorously: demonstrate that the March surge is absent in all departments known *not* to have adopted the increase and that no other policy or data anomaly coincides with those departments. Additionally, consider conducting sensitivity analyses that vary the 1.15 threshold, or use continuous measures of the March/February jump to predict the DMTO adoption probability, so readers can assess robustness to the classification rule.

- **Strengthen the composition argument with more granular analyses.** The notion that high-value buyers drove the surge can be better supported. For example:
  - Exploit DVF transaction-level data to estimate the March vs. February distribution of transaction values within treated departments. Show whether the upper tail moves more than the median.
  - Estimate DiDs separately for transactions above different thresholds (e.g., >300k, >500k, top 10 percent) to demonstrate size-dependence.
  - Use quantile regressions or non-linear models to capture whether the treatment effect grows with price.
  - If possible, combine price bins with department heterogeneity (e.g., high-value department × high-value transaction) to reveal interaction effects.

- **Revisit the reported coefficient interpretation and magnitude.** The DiD uses log counts, but the within R² in Table 2 is close to zero, suggesting that the model identifies effects almost entirely from between-unit variation. Consider re-specifying the model at a finer level (e.g., week × department) to exploit more variation around the deadline, or use Poisson/negative binomial counts if heteroskedasticity is a concern. At a minimum, explain why the within R² is so low and whether that affects the coherence of the estimated effects.

- **Improve pre-trend and placebo diagnostics.** The robustness table (Table 4) shows placebo coefficients, but event-study graphs are standard in bunching studies and would reassure readers about parallel trends. Plot the treatment effect relative to the February baseline for several months (say September 2024 to April 2025), with confidence intervals. Additionally, consider falsification tests on outcomes that should not be affected by the reform, such as new-construction transactions (which are subject to VAT) or real estate transactions in DOM-TOM, to reinforce the causal narrative.

- **Explicitly discuss potential confounders and alternative explanations.** Department fixed effects do not control for time-varying shocks (e.g., local economic news, mortgage rate changes, supply shocks) that might differentially affect high-value departments. If data permit, control for observable local macro indicators (unemployment, credit conditions, or prior month volume) or include department-specific trends. Even a discussion explaining why such factors are unlikely to coincide with the March surge would strengthen the argument.

- **Leverage the promised RDD from the first-time buyer exemption.** The manifest highlighted the optional exemption for purchases under €250,000 effective June 1. Although the paper currently focuses on the April spike, pursuing the RDD component would add substantial value. If the exemption was implemented in certain departments (or across all), analyze the distribution of transactions around €250k before and after June 1. Even if the exemption is not the main focus, referencing it in the paper could open opportunities for future extensions.

- **Address potential reporting or data-timing issues around April 2025.** The paper mentions that the April hangover estimate may be an upper bound due to reporting lags. Consider quantifying these lags (e.g., compare April with May counts or use notaire processing delays) or conducting robustness checks that trim April and only rely on March/February differences, to see if conclusions hold without the April coefficient.

- **Clarify data exclusions and sample construction.** Table 1 and the text have slightly inconsistent department counts (e.g., paper mentions 93 departments in data, but Table 4 uses 76). Provide a clear flowchart or appendix describing how the sample of departments was selected, which ones were excluded (why), and how missing months or extreme values were handled. This documentation will enhance replicability and make the classification scheme more transparent.

- **Discuss policy implications quantitatively.** The claim that forecasters will overestimate revenue or that the surge constitutes a “composition illusion” would benefit from back-of-the-envelope calculations—e.g., estimate the revenue lost due to the hangover, or simulate how much of the March volume would have occurred absent retiming. This would help readers gauge the real-world magnitude of the market distortion.

In sum, the paper makes a promising contribution, but it needs to anchor the treatment assignment in administrative reality, deepen the compositional analysis, and enhance the identification checks. Addressing these suggestions will substantially strengthen the causal story and elevate the policy relevance.
