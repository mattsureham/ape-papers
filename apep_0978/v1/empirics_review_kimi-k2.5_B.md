**Report on "The Subsidy Mirage: Japan's Solar Feed-in Tariff and the Structural Decline of Farmland"**

**1. Idea Fidelity**

The paper adheres to the core empirical architecture outlined in the manifest—a continuous difference-in-differences design exploiting the interaction of nationally declining FIT rates with prefectural upland-field shares—and successfully implements the proposed panel structure (47 prefectures, 2005–2022). However, it critically deviates from the specified outcome variable. The manifest explicitly identified “MAFF agricultural land diversion” (flow data, ~16,000 ha/year) as the primary outcome, whereas the paper analyzes cultivated land *stocks* (hectares of total cultivated area). This substitution fundamentally alters the research question: the paper tests whether subsidies reduced aggregate farmland stocks rather than whether they accelerated farmland-to-solar *conversion*. Because stocks decline through abandonment, afforestation, and urbanization—not merely solar development—the current design cannot directly refute the political narrative it targets. Additionally, the manifest proposed the 2017 FIT amendment as a “within-regime break” for identification; the paper mentions this amendment only in a robustness window rather than exploiting it as a separate source of variation.

**2. Summary**

This paper employs a continuous difference-in-differences design to test whether Japan’s solar feed-in tariff caused farmland loss by exploiting variation in national tariff rates and prefectural upland-field suitability. Despite finding a robust statistical association between FIT intensity and declining cultivated land, the paper demonstrates through mechanism-matched placebo tests and sensitivity analyses that this relationship reflects pre-existing structural trends—urbanization and farmer aging—rather than causal solar conversion, offering a novel null result on the land-use consequences of renewable energy subsidies.

**3. Essential Points**

* **Mismatched Outcome Variable.** The analysis relies on cultivated land stocks rather than the diversion flows specified in the research design, conflating solar conversion with abandonment and urbanization. While the paper convincingly shows that the FIT did not drive *aggregate* land loss, it cannot rule out localized solar conversion that was offset by other margins, or distinguish conversion from land-use change that occurred without solar development. To support the claim that the “subsidy mirage” is structural rather than energetic, the analysis requires either diversion flow data or direct measures of solar installations by prefecture.

* **Agrivoltaic Measurement Error.** The 2013 MAFF directive explicitly permitted “agrivoltaic” dual-use systems, where solar panels are installed above crops. If such land remains classified as “cultivated” in the MAFF survey (a likely coding given that farming continues beneath the panels), the outcome variable is right-censored for the primary margin of solar expansion. The paper does not clarify whether agrivoltaic installations are excluded from cultivated land counts. If they are included, the null result is mechanically biased, as the very policy mechanism studied (the 2013 directive enabling agrivoltaics) renders the outcome incapable of detecting conversion.

* **External Validity and First-Stage Validation.** The baseline association vanishes when weighting by cultivated area or including prefecture-specific trends, suggesting the result is driven by small, urbanizing prefectures with minimal agricultural significance or solar potential. Before concluding that solar subsidies caused no conversion, the paper must demonstrate that high-upland prefectures actually experienced differential solar deployment (a first-stage). Without validating that the treatment intensity correlates with solar capacity additions, the null result may reflect inadequate statistical power or misspecification at the intensive margin rather than absence of treatment effects.

**4. Suggestions**

**Validate the First Stage.** The paper assumes that high-upland prefectures were more exposed to solar conversion, but it does not demonstrate that these prefectures actually installed more solar capacity. I recommend obtaining METI data on solar PV capacity additions by prefecture (available from the Agency for Natural Resources and Energy) to test whether $UplandShare$ predicts solar deployment. If high-upland prefectures did not install more solar, the “mechanism-matched placebo” comparing paddy and upland fields is uninformative, as there is no first-stage to invalidate. If they did install more solar but cultivated land did not decline, this supports the agrivoltaic dual-use interpretation—that solar was added to existing farmland without removing it from cultivation—rather than the “structural decline” interpretation.

**Clarify Land Classification Rules.** The paper must explicitly address how agrivoltaic systems are treated in the MAFF Cultivated Land Survey. If dual-use land is coded as cultivated (consistent with the statutory definition of farmland under the Agricultural Land Act), the outcome variable cannot detect the primary form of solar expansion permitted after 2013. The authors should consult MAFF documentation or survey methodology to determine whether “cultivated land” includes agrivoltaic acreage. If inclusion is likely, the paper should acknowledge this as a lower-bound estimate and, if possible, use alternative data (such as the diversion data mentioned in the manifest) to bound the true conversion effect.

**Exploit the 2017 Amendment.** The manifest identified the 2017 shift from fixed tariffs to competitive auctions as a “within-regime break.” I suggest using this as a separate event study or split-sample test. If solar conversion drives land loss, the effect should weaken or reverse after 2017 when effective subsidies fell for large projects. Currently, the paper only truncates the sample at 2016; instead, interact a post-2017 indicator with the treatment intensity to test for a break in the slope. This would provide sharper identification than the gradual pre-trend tests, which are borderline significant ($p = 0.15$) and show a monotonic approach toward zero that raises concerns about differential trends.

**Address Weighting and Representativeness.** The reversal of results under area-weighting is troubling. The unweighted estimator gives excessive influence to Tokyo and Okinawa (small, urbanizing prefectures with extreme upland shares), while downweighting Hokkaido and Niigata, where most Japanese agriculture—and presumably most solar conversion—occurs. I recommend reporting both weighted and unweighted estimates as main results, and conducting heterogeneity analysis by prefecture characteristics (e.g., above/below median cultivated area, solar irradiance, or grid connectivity). If the null result holds only in small prefectures but reverses in large agricultural ones, the “subsidy mirage” conclusion requires significant qualification.

**Strengthen the Placebo Logic.** The paddy-field placebo test assumes that conversion costs are the binding constraint on solar siting. However, paddy fields in high-upland prefectures may face different irrigation costs, zoning pressure, or urban spillovers than paddy in rice-dominated prefectures. I suggest adding placebo tests using land categories entirely irrelevant to solar (e.g., forest land or already-developed urban area) to test whether the upland-share interaction simply captures general urbanization trends. Additionally, clarify the institutional detail: are paddy fields legally protected from conversion even in high-upland prefectures, or does the 2013 directive create a loophole? If legal protections bind equally, observing no differential paddy decline would actually support the solar mechanism; the fact that paddy declines *more* suggests the interaction captures other forces (e.g., substitution away from rice in diversified prefectures).

**Inference with Few Clusters.** With only 47 prefectures and a continuous treatment, conventional cluster-robust standard errors may suffer from size distortions. I recommend reporting $p$-values from a wild cluster bootstrap (Cameron, Gelbach, and Miller 2008) or the randomization inference already conducted (which is reassuring at $p = 0.014$) as the preferred inference method in the main text. Additionally, given that Tokyo and Okinawa are clear outliers in the upland-share distribution, report “leave-two-out” results excluding both simultaneously to ensure the result is not driven by these leverage points.

**Reframe the Conclusion.** The abstract and conclusion state definitively that “Japan’s agricultural land loss is structural... not a byproduct of solar policy.” Given the data limitations—particularly the use of stock rather than flow data and the ambiguity around agrivoltaic classification—I recommend softening this to: “We find no evidence that solar subsidies accelerated aggregate farmland loss at the prefecture level.” This more cautious framing accurately reflects the paper’s contribution (a credible null result on stock changes) without overclaiming on the underlying mechanism of conversion.
