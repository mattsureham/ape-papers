# Section-by-Section Review

## The Opening
**Verdict:** **Slow start; needs more "Shleifer" punch.**

The opening sentence is a classic "question" lead: *"Do government labels intended to help disadvantaged schools instead stigmatize the neighborhoods they serve?"* It’s functional, but it lacks a concrete hook. 
*   **The Shleifer Fix:** Open with the staggering raw fact hidden in your summary stats. 
*   **Suggested Rewrite:** "In France, homes located near 'priority' schools sell for 21 percent less than those near standard schools. This gap suggests a massive 'stigma tax' on disadvantaged neighborhoods, yet it remains unclear whether the label itself drives the discount or if it simply marks existing poverty."

## Introduction
**Verdict:** **Solid but over-reliant on literature-listing.**

Paragraph 2 is a "shopping list" of citations. Shleifer weaves literature into the narrative of the *idea*, not a list of names. You describe the "what we find" well, but the transition to the RDD methodology (Para 3) is a bit dry.
*   **Katz Sensibility:** Make us feel the consequences earlier. Instead of "capitalizing into property values," try "school labels shift the wealth of every family on the block."
*   **Clarity Check:** You mention "equidistance loci" (page 2). While precise, "midpoint between schools" is more readable for the first page.

## Background / Institutional Context

Section 2.4 (*Resources in REP/REP+ Schools*) is excellent. You take a dry policy and make it concrete: *"class sizes in targeted grades fell from roughly 24 students to 12."* This allows the reader to *see* the classrooms. It justifies why a homebuyer might actually *want* to live there (the "resource channel").

## Data

*   **Improvement:** The discussion of "flat-Earth projection" (Page 11) is a technical distraction. Move the specific geometry to a footnote or the appendix to keep the prose flowing.

## Empirical Strategy

You follow the Shleifer rule of explaining the logic before the math. Equation (2) is a beautiful example of Shleifer-esque distillation: decomposing the effect into "Resource" (+) and "Stigma" (-). It makes the rest of the paper feel inevitable.

## Results

The discussion of Table 2 (Pages 17-18) is the strongest part of the paper. You don't just say "Column 5 is zero." You say: *"Within municipalities... the price gradient is exactly zero."* 
*   **The "Katz" touch:** You explain *what we learned*: "The REP label appears to be neither strongly beneficial... nor strongly harmful... a genuine null result." This is high-level academic prose.
