**SUBJECT:** Strategic Positioning of "The Stigma of Priority: Education Priority Zone Labels and Housing Prices in France"

---

### 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper investigates whether government labels intended to aid disadvantaged schools (France’s REP program) inadvertently stigmatize neighborhoods and depress property values. Using 1.7 million geocoded transactions and a boundary discontinuity design, the author finds that the apparent "stigma tax" is actually a statistical artifact of geographic sorting: once commune-level fixed effects are applied, the price gradient vanishes entirely. This suggests that place-based policy labels do not independently distort markets, and that private schools act as a critical "escape valve" that neutralizes residential sorting incentives.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this pitch excellently. The first two paragraphs clearly define the tension between the "resource channel" (positive) and the "stigma channel" (negative). However, to be more AER-ready, the pitch should emphasize the *theoretical* implication: that government signals are often informationally redundant in equilibrium. 

**Revised Pitch for the Paper:** 
"Do state-sponsored signals of neighborhood disadvantage create independent market distortions, or are they merely mirrors of existing equilibrium sorting? I show that France’s 'priority' school labels carry no independent price penalty once local sorting is accounted for, implying that such policy designations are informationally redundant to homebuyers. This findings suggests that the welfare costs of 'stigma' in place-based policies may be overstated, as private school competition provides an institutional escape valve that decouples residential choice from school assignment."

---

### 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution:** The paper identifies that government-mandated labels of school disadvantage do not impose an independent "stigma tax" on housing prices, as the observed price gaps are entirely explained by municipal-level sorting and are neutralized by private school availability.

- **Differentiation:** It differentiates itself well from Black (1999) and Fack & Grenet (2010) by moving from "performance signals" (test scores) to "policy signals" (labels) and scaling the analysis to a national level.
- **Framing:** It is currently framed as answering a question about a specific French policy. It should be reframed as a broader question about the **Information Environment of Urban Equilibrium**.
- **Clarity:** A smart economist would get it: "It’s a paper showing that 'stigma' labels don't matter because people already know which neighborhoods are poor."
- **Bigger Contribution:** To make it bigger, the author should focus on the **signaling value of the state**. Why does the market ignore the government's label? Is it because the government is late to the party, or because the resources provided cancel out the stigma? The "exactly zero" result in Column 5 of Table 2 is the "smoking gun" that needs more theoretical weight.

---

### 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

- **Closest Neighbors:** Black (1999) on school capitalization; Bénabou et al. (2009) on ZEP outcomes; Fack and Grenet (2010) on Paris school boundaries; Figlio and Lucas (2004) on school report cards.
- **Positioning:** The paper currently "builds on" these neighbors. It should "synthesize" them into a broader argument about Tiebout sorting under imperfect information.
- **Missing Literature:** The paper should speak to the **Social Signaling** literature (e.g., Spence) and **Place-Based Policies** (e.g., Glaeser and Gottlieb). It needs to connect to the literature on "Labeling Effects" in sociology/economics to explain why the *name* of a policy matters.
- **Conversation:** It is currently having an "Education/Urban" conversation. It should attempt a "Public/Information" conversation.

---

### 4. NARRATIVE ARC

- **Setup:** Governments label poor neighborhoods to help them.
- **Tension:** This label might be a "kiss of death" (stigma) that lowers property values and destroys the tax base.
- **Resolution:** The stigma is an illusion; the market has already priced in the neighborhood quality, and the label adds no new information (except in Paris).
- **Implications:** Policymakers don't need to fear the "stigma tax," but they should recognize that labels are redundant in the face of private school competition.

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong but the "resolution" is a null result. The author needs to lean harder into the "Paris vs. Provinces" and "Private School" results to prevent the paper from feeling like "nothing happened."

---

### 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

- **Lead Fact:** "When the French government labels a school as a 'priority zone' for the poor, it has zero effect on the price of the houses across the street."
- **Reaction:** People will lean in for the *mechanism* (private schools) and the *exception* (Paris).
- **Follow-up:** "Does this mean the policy resources are useless, or just that the market already knew the neighborhood was struggling?"
- **The Null:** The null is interesting here because the "stigma tax" is a common fear in urban policy. Proving it's zero (conditional on sorting) is a high-value contribution.

### 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The gap is **Theoretical Ambition**. Currently, it feels like a very high-quality "Policy Evaluation" (as the author's affiliation suggests). To be an AER paper, it needs to be a "Market Equilibrium" paper. 

**The single most impactful piece of advice:**
Shift the focus from "Evaluating the REP program" to "Testing the Information Redundancy of Government Signals." The finding that the label matters in Paris but reverses elsewhere is a major clue about how information is processed in high-density vs. low-density markets. Explaining *why* the market ignores the signal in some places but not others (beyond just "sorting") would elevate this.
