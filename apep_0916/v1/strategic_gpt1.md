## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a big historical and economic question: when banking systems collapse, do they permanently damage workers’ long-run earning trajectories, or mainly destroy household balance sheets and force geographic reallocation? Using linked U.S. Census records spanning 1920 to 1940 and variation in pre-Depression banking fragility from unit banking laws, the paper argues that the Great Depression’s banking collapse did not leave lasting scars on occupational earnings paths, but did increase homeownership loss and displacement.

A busy economist should care because this is, at least in aspiration, not really a paper about the 1930s. It is a paper about the incidence of financial crises: do credit collapses permanently lower human capital returns, or do they mostly hit wealth and location while labor markets eventually reabsorb workers?

**Does the paper articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Mostly, but not optimally. The current opening is competent and readable, but it slides too quickly into “first individual-level evidence” and “largest linked panel” mode. That makes the contribution sound like a data exercise rather than an answer to a first-order economic question. The best version of the pitch is not “we bring microdata to a famous macro episode.” It is “we show that one of the canonical financial collapses in history appears to have scarred assets and locations more than long-run earnings capacity.”

### The pitch the paper should have

**Paragraph 1:**  
Financial crises are widely believed to leave long-run scars on workers, not just temporary recessions: they destroy credit, shut down firms, disrupt careers, and may permanently lower lifetime earnings. The Great Depression is the canonical case. But despite the centrality of that episode to how economists think about finance and real activity, we still do not know whether banking collapse permanently damaged the workers who lived through it, or whether its durable effects fell instead on wealth, housing, and location.

**Paragraph 2:**  
This paper brings individual-level evidence to that question by linking 8.45 million men across the 1920, 1930, and 1940 U.S. Censuses and exploiting preexisting differences in banking fragility created by state unit banking laws. The central result is stark: areas more exposed to fragile banking systems saw no detectable long-run decline in occupational income or 1940 wages, but they did see more homeownership loss and geographic displacement. The Depression’s banking collapse appears to have destroyed balance sheets and reshuffled people across places, without permanently downgrading their earnings trajectories.

That is the AER version of the paper. Right now the intro is close, but still too much “here is my design” and not enough “here is the belief about the world I am overturning.”

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:**  
The paper claims that exposure to Depression-era banking fragility had little or no lasting effect on individual occupational earnings trajectories, but did increase household wealth loss and geographic displacement.

That is a clean contribution. The problem is not absence of a contribution; it is **scale and differentiation**.

### Is it clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?
Only partially.

The paper differentiates itself mainly on:
1. **Level of observation**: individual rather than aggregate/firm-level.
2. **Historical episode**: Great Depression banking collapse.
3. **Outcome mix**: occupational trajectories plus migration/homeownership.

That is not enough by itself. “Micro evidence on an old macro question” is useful, but for AER the paper must make unmistakably clear why the answer is surprising relative to what the literature would have led us to expect.

The closest comparator inside the paper is Ziebarth; outside that, the natural neighbors are the Depression banking literature, the branch banking/growth literature, and the literature on scarring from downturns and crises. The paper needs to say more sharply: **existing work implies lasting real effects; we show those effects may not run through long-run earnings capacity at the individual level.**

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or filling a gap in a LITERATURE?
It starts with the world, which is good, but quickly reverts to “first individual-level evidence,” “largest linked panel,” and “contributes to three literatures.” That is weaker. AER papers should sound like they are adjudicating a substantive proposition about how the economy works.

The stronger framing is:

- **World question:** When financial systems fail, what gets permanently scarred—workers’ earnings capacity, or households’ balance sheets and locations?
- **Not:** There is no paper linking 8.45 million men to study unit banking.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?
They could get there, but too many would summarize it as:  
“It's a big linked-census DiD-ish paper on Depression-era banking fragility that finds mostly null effects on occupational mobility.”

That is not enough. The introduction needs to force the takeaway:

> “The surprising result is that one of history’s largest banking collapses appears not to have durably harmed workers’ occupational earnings paths, even though it did reduce asset retention and increase displacement.”

That is memorable. “Another DiD paper about unit banking” is not.

### What would make this contribution bigger?
Several possibilities, in descending order of payoff:

1. **Make wealth/place the coequal center of the paper, not a side note.**  
   Right now the paper is organized as a null on occupational mobility with small auxiliary results on homeownership and migration. That is strategically backwards. The interesting claim is comparative incidence: **financial collapse hit assets and location more than earnings capacity**. That is bigger than “we find a null.”

2. **Sharpen the contrast between short-run disruption and long-run recovery.**  
   If the paper can show temporary disruption by 1930 with recovery by 1940, the story becomes much more compelling: crises dislocate but do not permanently scar careers. Even without changing the empirical core, the framing should emphasize dynamics rather than a flat null.

3. **Use a stronger welfare-relevant outcome architecture.**  
   Occupational score is not vivid enough to carry the paper. If there are credible measures of wage income, nonemployment, labor force attachment, migration distance, housing tenure, family formation, or urban transition, the paper becomes much more consequential. AER readers need to see people’s lives, not just OCCSCORE.

4. **Lean into heterogeneity where theory predicts scarring.**  
   If young workers, farmers, low-baseline-wealth households, or residents of high-failure counties still show little persistent earnings loss, that is a much stronger challenge to conventional wisdom than a pooled average null.

5. **Reframe from “Great Depression” to “what financial crises do to households.”**  
   The historical setting is a vehicle. The claim should be about crisis transmission more generally.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

### Closest neighbors
The paper’s closest neighbors are probably:

1. **Bernanke (1983)** — nonmonetary effects of the financial crisis in propagating the Depression.  
2. **Calomiris and Mason (2003)** — fundamentals and bank distress during the Depression.  
3. **Mitchener and Richardson / Wheelock / Calomiris-related banking history work** — branch banking restrictions, bank fragility, and failures.  
4. **Jayaratne and Strahan (1996)** — branch banking and growth.  
5. **Ziebarth (2013)** — local labor market/individual consequences of Depression-era banking distress.  
And on the broader scarring side:
6. **Chodorow-Reich (2014)**, **Mian and Sufi (2014/2015)** — credit shocks and employment/household outcomes.  
7. **Hornbeck (2012)** — displacement and long-run adjustment in a historical shock setting.

### How should it position itself relative to those neighbors?
**Build on and revise**, not attack.

- Relative to **Bernanke** and the macro-Depression literature:  
  “Aggregate nonmonetary effects need not imply permanent individual earnings scarring.”
  
- Relative to **branch banking / finance-growth** papers:  
  “Bank structure mattered, but perhaps more through wealth preservation and spatial allocation than through long-run occupational rank.”

- Relative to **modern crisis/scarring** work:  
  “Credit shocks can have severe real effects without permanently downgrading occupational trajectories over long horizons.”

This should be framed as a **decomposition of the real effects of financial collapse**, not as a contradiction for its own sake.

### Is it currently positioned too narrowly or too broadly?
Oddly, both.

- **Too narrowly** in the empirical setup: unit banking laws × agricultural share is a niche historical design that could trap the paper in economic history.
- **Too broadly** in the literature claims: it says it contributes to macro history, finance-growth, and scarring literatures, but without a single unifying thesis.

The paper needs one central conversation:
> **What is the durable micro incidence of financial crises?**

Then the historical and banking literatures become support, not the main stage.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?
It needs stronger engagement with:

- **Labor market scarring** literature beyond recession exposure narrowly defined.
- **Household finance / balance sheet recessions**.
- **Spatial reallocation / migration as adjustment**.
- Possibly **intergenerational or life-cycle recovery** literatures if the authors want to say something about long-run trajectories.

The current references are competent but feel somewhat siloed into Depression banking history plus a few broad finance papers.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?
Not quite. Right now it is having the conversation:
> “Did unit banking matter for occupational outcomes in the Great Depression?”

The better conversation is:
> “When credit collapses, are the durable losses borne in labor income, household wealth, or geography?”

That second conversation is much closer to AER territory.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
The pre-paper world is one in which economists believe the Great Depression’s banking collapse had major real effects, and many readers would loosely infer that such effects included long-run damage to individual careers and earnings.

### Tension
We have strong aggregate evidence and vivid historical narrative, but almost no direct evidence on the long-run trajectories of individuals exposed to banking fragility. The unresolved question is whether financial collapse permanently lowers workers’ earning capacity, or instead primarily destroys wealth and triggers relocation.

### Resolution
The paper finds no detectable long-run effect on occupational income trajectories or 1940 wages, but does find evidence of greater homeownership loss and migration/displacement.

### Implications
This suggests a different incidence of financial crisis than the conventional narrative implies: banking collapse may create large aggregate and household disruptions without permanently lowering workers’ occupational standing. Recovery can occur through reallocation rather than restoration in place.

### Does the paper have a clear arc?
It has the pieces of one, but the current draft still feels somewhat like **a null result followed by a set of side outcomes**. The story is there, but it is not fully claimed.

The paper should tell this story:

1. Economists believe banking collapse causes lasting real harm.
2. The unresolved question is where that lasting harm resides.
3. We show it resides more in **wealth and place** than in **long-run earnings capacity**.
4. Therefore, financial crises may be persistent in welfare terms without showing up as permanent occupational downgrading.

That is a real narrative arc. Right now the paper undersells the “wealth and place” resolution and overemphasizes the precision of the null.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?
I would say:

> “Even in the Great Depression, living in places with more fragile banking systems doesn’t seem to have permanently lowered workers’ long-run occupational earnings paths—but it did push more people out of homeownership and out of place.”

That is the hook.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
Some would lean in, because the finding cuts against a strong intuition. But many would immediately ask whether this is just a measurement issue with occupational scores, or whether survivors/movers are masking the effect. That means the paper cannot rely on “precise null” alone. The comparative result—**earnings resilience alongside asset/place scarring**—is what keeps people engaged.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Probably one of these:

1. “So where did the losses go if not wages?”
2. “Was the effect temporary rather than permanent?”
3. “Is this occupational score too coarse to see real income losses?”
4. “Is migration the adjustment margin that saved earnings?”

That is useful guidance. The paper should be written to answer those questions proactively.

### If the findings are null or modest: is the null itself interesting?
Yes, but only under a stronger framing. A null result is interesting here because:
- the shock is historically massive,
- the priors are that persistent scarring should exist,
- the estimates are precise,
- and there is an alternative margin where effects do show up.

Without that last point, the paper risks feeling like a failed attempt to find an effect. With it, the paper becomes an incidence paper: **financial collapse scarred balance sheets and locations, not long-run occupational ranking**. That is much better.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The gap is mainly a mix of **framing problem** and **ambition problem**.

### Framing problem
The science may be serviceable, but the paper currently presents itself as:
- a historical banking paper,
- with a large linked dataset,
- that finds a precise null on occupational scores.

That is not enough.

It needs to present itself as:
- a paper on the **micro incidence of financial crises**, using the Great Depression as the sharpest test case,
- showing a substantive and surprising distinction between **earnings resilience** and **wealth/place scarring**.

### Ambition problem
The paper is competent but safe. It does not yet fully exploit the scale of the data or the breadth of the question. AER-level ambition would require the reader to feel that the paper changes how we think about crisis transmission. Right now it changes how we think about one historical episode, but only a bit.

### Novelty problem
Moderate. The historical setting and scale are novel, but the basic ingredients—banking fragility, linked census data, long-run outcomes—sit in already active literatures. To clear the bar, the paper must persuade the reader that the answer is genuinely new and belief-revising, not just a bigger sample with a null.

### Scope problem
Also moderate. The outcomes are still too narrow relative to the question the paper wants to answer. If the durable harm is really to wealth and geography, that part of the paper needs to be expanded conceptually and empirically, not left as a secondary result.

### Single most impactful advice
**Reframe the paper around a comparative claim—financial collapse permanently scarred balance sheets and locations more than long-run earnings capacity—rather than around a precise null on occupational mobility.**

That one change would improve the intro, literature review, results organization, and conclusion all at once.
