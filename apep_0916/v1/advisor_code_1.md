**Idea Fidelity**

The paper faithfully pursues the manifest’s core ambition: leveraging the MLP 1920–1930–1940 linked panel to assess whether Depression-era bank failures scuffed individual careers. It retains the stated data sources (MLP, agricultural shares, unit banking classification) and reproduces the interaction-based identification strategy between unit banking states and agricultural exposure. However, the manifest promised an instrumental-variable/Bartik-style strategy and a three-decade long-difference design that explicitly uses the pre-Depression 1920 baseline and instruments county-level failure exposure; the paper ends up estimating a cross-sectional long-difference regression without the instrument or explicit IV/Bartik implementation. The promised panel leverage (e.g., identification through within-individual changes with variation in 1920 baseline controls) is somewhat implicit but appears in the specification, so most of the key elements are present, except for the more elaborate IV/Bartik strategy mentioned in the manifest.

**Summary**

This paper links 8.45 million men across the 1920, 1930, and 1940 censuses to assess whether Depression-era bank fragility—proxied by an interaction of state unit banking laws and county agricultural exposure—caused lasting occupational income scarring. The main result is a well-powered null on occupational-income trajectories, accompanied by positive evidence of scarring on wealth-related and geographic outcomes such as homeownership loss and migration. The null result is argued to reflect labor-market resilience despite severe banking disruption, shifting the narrative from persistent career damage to displacement in wealth and place.

**Essential Points**

1. **Identification strategy remains too reduced-form.** The manifest highlights the role of county-level bank failure exposure instrumented by unit banking laws, yet the paper never implements the IV/Bartik-style approach or introduces actual bank-failure data at the county level. Instead, it relies on a simple interaction between unit banking status and agricultural share, without exploiting within-state variation in failure intensity or a first-stage linking regulatory variation to bank failures. The authors should either (a) explicitly use county-level bank failure rates as outcomes/treatments and instrument them with unit banking × agriculture, (b) provide direct evidence that the interaction is plausibly exogenous beyond the assumed absence of differential trends, or (c) clearly justify why the more ambitious IV strategy was infeasible. Without this, concerns about omitted variable bias (e.g., agricultural structural change, policy differences, migration patterns) remain.

2. **Threats to the parallel trends assumption are only partially addressed.** While the paper mentions a 1920–1930 placebo decomposition and region fixed effects, the core interaction may still capture differential structural transformation (e.g., agricultural-to-manufacturing shifts) that interact with both agriculture share and pre-existing unit banking status. The 1920–1930 test is reassuring, but the paper should demonstrate more explicitly that any pre-existing trends do not predict the interaction (e.g., via event-study-style leads/lags or county-specific linear trends). Without this, the null estimate could simply reflect offsetting biases rather than a causal zero effect.

3. **Occupational income score may not capture individual welfare, yet alternatives are underused.** The paper acknowledges the limitations of OCCSCORE but does not exploit available wage data from 1940 or other outcomes (e.g., 1930 occupations, earnings, firm-level information) to triangulate the null on occupational trajectories. Given the reliance on this single long-difference measure, it is crucial to (a) show robustness using 1930 outcomes (e.g., mid-point occupation or wage cross-section), (b) use 1940 wage income as an outcome for the subset with earlier proxies, or (c) supplement with alternative individual-level indicators (e.g., employment status, industry switches). This would strengthen confidence that the “null” is not due to measurement noise or misalignment between the scoring metric and true earnings capacity.

**Suggestions**

1. **Implement the promised IV/Bartik strategy or clearly describe its infeasibility.** If county-level bank failure data exist (as suggested by the manifest), the authors should construct a first-stage linking unit banking laws to failure rates (perhaps times agricultural share) and estimate the causal effect of failure exposure on outcomes. This would strengthen identification beyond the simple interaction. If data quality or coverage preclude this, explain the limitation and show that the interaction still has strong predictive power for failures (e.g., regress bank suspensions on UnitBanking × AgShare). Provide first-stage statistics to reassure readers that the interaction captures the institutional shock rather than secular differences.

2. **Add event-study or trend-analyses to bolster parallel trends.** The pre-Depression placebo (1920–1930) is useful, but the authors could adopt a more granular approach: estimate the interaction’s effect on multiple pre-treatment outcomes (e.g., occupational changes 1910–1920 if data allow, or other socioeconomic indicators in 1920) or fit county-level trends in agricultural output or migration rates before 1929. Alternatively, including county-by-decade fixed effects (or county-specific year trends) would absorb differential structural change and make the identification rely more directly on the 1929–1933 shock. If that is infeasible, at least show plots of log occupational trajectories by unit-banking × agriculture terciles to illustrate parallelism.

3. **Triangulate with additional outcomes and intermediate variables.** Extend beyond OCCSCORE to construct comparable metrics using 1930 occupational profiles or 1940 wage income (albeit available only post-treatment). For example, regress 1940 INCWAGE on the interaction, controlling for 1930 occupation (as a proxy for pretreatment sest). Similarly, explore whether the interaction predicts transitions between occupations (e.g., farm to non-farm) or continued employment in the same industry. This would reinforce the argument that occupational standing was resilient even if the income score is noisy. For the partial wage data, consider using a difference-in-differences setup within 1940 cross-section, instrumenting wage differences with pre-1929 bank fragility to assess causal links.

4. **Elaborate on mechanisms behind wealth/place scarring.** The paper hints that wealth shocks drove homeownership loss and migration, but offers little detail. If county-level bank failure counts are accessible, show their correlation with these alternative outcomes to link the financial shock to displacement. Alternatively, examine whether individuals experiencing homeownership loss are those with histories of banking proximity (e.g., residing in counties with more banks per capita) and whether the interaction predicts increased inter-county migration intensity (e.g., bigger distances). This would highlight how financial distress specified by the institution ties into the wealth/place findings, rounding out the narrative.

5. **Discuss potential sample selection from the MLP linking procedure.** The appendix notes that stayers may be easier to link; this selection could bias results if both linking success and treatment exposure correlate with outcomes. Provide descriptive stats on linkage rates across unit banking × agriculture categories or include inverse-probability weights to check robustness. Alternatively, show that the main results hold when restricting to high-linkability strata (e.g., those with unique names or minimal mobility). This would help ensure the 8.45 million sample is representative of the underlying population and not stressing the null.

6. **Clarify standard-error treatment given clustering level.** The clustering at the state level (49 clusters) is appropriate, but the interaction of state-level unit banking with county-level ag share induces limited within-state variation. Consider using wild-bootstrap p-values to ensure inference is not overstating precision, especially in robustness checks. Also, present results with county-level fixed effects where feasible (e.g., comparisons within states) to show that the null is not driven by between-county differences.

7. **Present descriptive evidence on bank failure exposure.** Even if the main regression relies on the interaction, supplying a table or figure illustrating how bank failure rates vary with unit banking laws and agricultural intensity would help lay readers understand the first-stage. For instance, plot average suspensions per capita against ag share separately for unit vs. branch states. This would visually anchor the causal story and justify the use of the chosen interaction.

8. **Consider alternative treatment windows or event timing.** The 1920–1940 window is long, but the key shock occurred 1929–1933. Investigate whether shorter windows (e.g., 1930–1940 change in OCCSCORE) show similar nulls or whether there is a transient dip that recovers by 1940. Such analysis would reveal whether “no scarring” is a long-run outcome or if there were temporary setbacks smoothed out over time.

By addressing these points, the paper would significantly strengthen its causal claims and make the null finding more compelling within the rich historical context.
