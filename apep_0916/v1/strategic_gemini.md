### 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether the massive banking collapse of the Great Depression left permanent scars on the career trajectories of individuals. Using a newly constructed 20-year linked census panel of 8.45 million men, it exploits the "natural experiment" of unit banking laws—which made banks in agricultural areas hyper-vulnerable to failure—to show a surprising, well-powered null result: while the crisis destroyed wealth and forced migration, it did not permanently lower an individual's occupational earning capacity. It suggests the American labor market was far more resilient to the 20th century's greatest credit shock than the "scarring" narrative implies.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this pitch exceptionally well in the first two paragraphs. It immediately hooks the reader by pitting a "conventional narrative" (Friedman & Schwartz; Bernanke) against new, individual-level evidence. It clearly states the data, the identification strategy, and the provocative finding. 

### 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:** The paper provides the first large-scale individual-level evidence that Depression-era banking fragility caused significant wealth loss and displacement but failed to produce long-term occupational scarring.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It is well-differentiated. While Ziebarth (2013) is the closest neighbor, this paper scales the analysis from a single Fed district border to the entire nation, shifting the contribution from a "local case study" to a "general equilibrium" fact.
*   **Question vs. Gap:** It answers a question about the WORLD (Does financial collapse ruin careers?). This is much stronger than merely "filling a gap" in the linked-census literature.
*   **What would make it bigger?** To move from "very good" to "undeniable AER," the author needs to strengthen the *mechanism* for the null. Why did the labor market absorb the shock? Is it because skills were general, or because the New Deal's "alphabet soup" of agencies effectively acted as a buyer of last resort for displaced labor? A more explicit test of the "New Deal as a stabilizer" hypothesis would elevate the paper.

### 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

*   **Closest Neighbors:** Bernanke (1983) on non-monetary effects; Calomiris & Mason (2003) on bank failures; Hornbeck (2012) on Dust Bowl displacement; and the growing "scarring" literature (e.g., Yagan 2019, though in a different era).
*   **Positioning:** The paper wisely positions itself as a friendly "overturner" of the Bernanke/Friedman/Schwartz narrative. It doesn't say they were wrong about the macroeconomy; it says they were incomplete about the *individual*.
*   **Conversation:** It is currently having a "Macro History" conversation. It should lean harder into the "Labor/Public" conversation regarding **social insurance**. The fact that careers survived despite the absence of a modern safety net is a profound finding that speaks to current debates about the necessity of "job shields" vs. "wealth shields."

### 4. NARRATIVE ARC

*   **Setup:** The Great Depression as the ultimate "scarring" event.
*   **Tension:** We’ve assumed for 90 years that it broke careers, but we’ve never actually tracked the people.
*   **Resolution:** The "well-powered null." People lost their houses and moved, but they found new jobs at the same skill level.
*   **Implications:** Capital is fragile; human capital (or at least labor market slotting) is remarkably robust.

**Evaluation:** The narrative arc is very strong. It follows a classic "myth-busting" structure that editors love.

### 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

**The Dinner Party Fact:** "During the Great Depression, living in a county where the banking system completely imploded didn't actually lower your income 10 years later—it just meant you were more likely to lose your house and move."

**Response:** People will lean in. The "Resilient Career" is a counter-intuitive finding. The follow-up question will be: "Wait, so was the Depression actually *not that bad* for the average worker?" The author needs to be very careful to clarify that the *wealth* destruction was real and painful, even if the *occupational* score stayed flat.

### 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

*   **The Wage Confirmation (Section 5.2):** This is vital. In historical papers, reviewers always complain that "Occupational Score" is a blunt instrument. Moving the 1940 wage result earlier or making it a more central pillar of the "Main Results" would preempt the most obvious referee critique.
*   **Selection Bias (Section 6):** The discussion of MLP linking bias is tucked away in limitations. This needs a more robust "correction" or "sensitivity analysis" (e.g., the Charlie-style bounding exercises) to be taken seriously at the AER level. If the "most scarred" people are the ones who disappeared from the sample, the null is a mechanical artifact.

### 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The gap is **ambition regarding the "Why."** A well-powered null is a "fact," but the AER wants a "theory." 

**Single most impactful piece of advice:** The author must move beyond the "reduced-form null" and provide a more rigorous exploration of *why* the labor market was resilient. Specifically, they should test if this resilience was higher in areas with more "New Deal" spending (using Fishback's data) or in areas with more diverse industrial bases. If the author can show that "Policy X" (like the WPA) is what prevented the scarring, this becomes a foundational paper on the history of the American safety net.
