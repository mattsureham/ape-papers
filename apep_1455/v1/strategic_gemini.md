## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
This paper evaluates New Zealand’s 2022 Medium Density Residential Standards (MDRS)—the most aggressive national upzoning mandate in the Anglophone world—to see if removing regulatory barriers actually triggers the construction of "missing middle" housing (townhomes/apartments). Using a regional difference-in-differences design, the author finds a precise null result on housing composition, suggesting that even "as-of-right" zoning is insufficient to shift the housing mix when binding constraints exist in financing and construction costs.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this well, but the first two paragraphs are a bit "policy-heavy." They focus on the specific NZ law rather than the fundamental economic puzzle. 

**The Pitch the Paper Should Have:**
"Economists generally agree that restrictive zoning limits housing supply, but a secondary, more stubborn hypothesis remains: that even if we deregulate, the 'missing middle' of housing will not appear because of structural supply-side barriers. This paper exploits a unique national experiment in New Zealand—mandated 'as-of-right' triplex zoning across all major cities—to provide the first precise evidence that eliminating planning consents alone fails to shift the composition of new housing. The results imply that the housing 'missing middle' is not a regulatory artifact, but a structural market failure that zoning reform alone cannot solve."

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**Contribution:** The paper provides a precise null result showing that aggressive "as-of-right" upzoning does not shift the composition of new housing toward multi-unit dwellings in the short-to-medium term.

*   **Differentiation:** It differentiates itself from Greenaway-McGrevy (2023) by focusing on the *national* mandate (MDRS) rather than just Auckland, and from Been et al. (2023) by shifting the focus from *total supply* to *compositional mix*.
*   **Framing:** Currently, it leans toward "filling a gap in the upzoning literature." To be AER-quality, it needs to frame itself as a fundamental test of **Supply-Side Theory**: Are regulations the primary "chokepoint" of urban form?
*   **Clarity:** A smart economist would say: "It's a precise null on the NZ triplex reform." It’s clear, but risks being seen as "another DiD" without a more robust exploration of *why* it failed.
*   **Bigger Contribution:** To make this "AER big," the author needs to move beyond the null and prove the mechanism. If it’s not zoning, is it the interest rate? Is it the lack of specialized labor? Data on construction costs or developer profits per square meter would elevate this.

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
*   **Neighbors:** Glaeser & Gyourko (2018) on housing supply constraints; Hsieh & Moretti (2019) on the macro effects of housing constraints; and the recent Auckland/Minneapolis upzoning papers (Greenaway-McGrevy; Been et al.).
*   **Positioning:** It should position itself as the "sobering reality check" to the optimistic supply-side literature. It isn't attacking the idea that zoning matters; it's arguing that zoning is a *necessary but insufficient* condition.
*   **Missing Conversations:** The paper needs to speak to the **Industrial Organization of Construction**. If the "missing middle" didn't happen, is it because the developer market is too concentrated in standalone-home builders? (Relate to Murphy, 2018).

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC
*   **Setup:** Cities are expensive; everyone blames zoning.
*   **Tension:** New Zealand does the "impossible" and bans restrictive zoning overnight. If the theory is right, we should see a townhouse boom.
*   **Resolution:** Nothing happens to the housing mix. In fact, multi-unit levels might have dropped faster than houses.
*   **Implications:** The "missing middle" is a deep structural problem, not just a legal one.

**Evaluation:** The arc is currently a "collection of results." To improve, the author should lean into the **paradox**: Why did the most aggressive reform in the world result in zero change? The "Construction Cycle" threat (page 4) should be moved from a "threat to validity" to a "core part of the story" regarding how macro-factors override micro-deregulation.

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
At a dinner party, you lead with: **"New Zealand legalized triplexes on every lot in its major cities, and four years later, not a single percentage point of the housing mix had changed."** 
Economists will lean in. The follow-up will be: "Was it because interest rates spiked at the same time?"
The paper’s finding is an "interesting null." It challenges the current zeitgeist that "just upzone it" is a panacea.

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
*   **Front-loading:** The "log multi-unit levels" result (Column 4, Table 2) is actually *more* interesting than the share. It shows that multi-units are *more* sensitive to downturns than houses. This should be a headline result, not a side note.
*   **Appendix:** The "Standardized Effect Sizes" (Table 6) are overkill for a 15-region study. Keep it simple.
*   **Mechanisms:** Section 6 (Discussion) is currently pure speculation. Even descriptive data on interest rates or material costs during this period would make this section feel like "science" rather than a "conclusion."

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
**The Gap:** The paper is currently a very clean, very "safe" policy evaluation. It lacks the **theoretical ambition** and **mechanical depth** of an AER paper.

An AER-worthy version of this paper would explain *why* the supply-side elasticity for multi-unit housing is so much lower than for standalone houses during a credit crunch.
