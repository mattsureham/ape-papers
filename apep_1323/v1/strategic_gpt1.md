## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether policies that push economies away from cash also shrink the brick-and-mortar banking infrastructure that many households still rely on. Using Nigeria’s 2012 cash-transaction surcharge policy, the paper argues that the move toward digital payments coincided with a sizable relative decline in bank branches, raising the possibility that “cashless” reforms can come with an overlooked access cost.

Why should a busy economist care? Because the broader policy question is important and general: when governments digitize finance, do they expand inclusion or simply change its form, potentially leaving cash-reliant and marginal consumers behind?

**Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Not quite. The current opening starts with a descriptive fact pattern and then moves quickly into institutional detail. It takes too long to get to the real question, which is not “what happened to branches in Nigeria?” but “can digitalization policies unintentionally reduce physical access to finance?” That is the AER-relevant question.

**What the first two paragraphs should say instead:**  
A stronger opening would lead with the broader economic tension:

> Around the world, governments and central banks are trying to reduce the use of cash and accelerate digital payments. The standard case for these policies is that they modernize finance, lower transaction costs, and expand inclusion. But there is a neglected possibility: if cash becomes less central, banks may retrench their physical branch networks, weakening access for the households and firms least able to substitute into digital channels.
>
> This paper studies that possibility using Nigeria’s 2012 “Cash-less Nigeria” policy, one of the earliest large-scale cash-penalty regimes in a low-income setting. I show that after the policy, Nigeria experienced a sharp relative decline in bank branch density, with no corresponding rise in ATM density. The broader implication is that financial digitalization may reorganize access rather than simply expand it.

That is the pitch the paper should have.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

The paper needs to be sharper about what exactly is new:

It oscillates between both, but too often slips into gap-filling mode. “The literature has not addressed physical infrastructure consequences” is weaker than “policymakers may be inducing a hidden substitution away from physical access.”

- What happens to physical access when governments penalize cash?

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Governments increasingly promote digital payments and discourage cash; these reforms are generally sold as modernizing and inclusion-enhancing.

### Tension
Physical bank branches still matter for onboarding, trust, and access—especially in low-income environments—so a move away from cash may have an unintended downside if it leads banks to close branches before digital alternatives fully substitute.

### Resolution
Nigeria’s cash-penalty regime coincided with a large relative decline in bank branch density, with no corresponding change in ATM density.

### Implications
Digitalization policy may reshape access in ways that standard success metrics miss. Policymakers should not assume that greater electronic-payment usage necessarily means broader access to finance.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
It has the bones of one, but the execution is uneven. Too much of the paper reads like a cautious empirical note organized around estimators and caveats rather than a coherent economic narrative. The author repeatedly steps on the punchline by emphasizing limitations before the reader has absorbed why the question matters.

At present, it is somewhat a **collection of results looking for a story**.

### What story should it be telling?
The story should be:

1. Physical access to banking still matters in developing economies.
2. Digital-finance policy changes the economics of serving customers.
3. Nigeria provides an early, policy-driven case of that transition.
4. The observed branch contraction suggests a real access tradeoff that the literature and policy debate underappreciate.
5. Therefore, evaluating digital-finance reforms requires tracking both digital uptake and physical retrenchment.

That is a proper narrative arc. The current draft is close, but it keeps drifting into “I found an interesting pattern, but maybe it’s not causal, and data are limited.” That may be intellectually honest, but strategically it weakens the paper’s identity.

The paper can survive with suggestive identification if it is positioned as:

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

Be honest: in current form, this is **not an AER paper**.

### What is the main gap?

Primarily a **scope/ambition problem**, with some **framing problem** layered on top.

- **Framing problem:** The paper has not yet found the strongest version of its own question.
- **Scope problem:** The evidence is too narrow for the size of the claims it wants to make.
- **Novelty problem:** Branch decline following digitization is intuitively plausible; the paper needs to show why this is a major new fact rather than an unsurprising byproduct of modernization.
- **Ambition problem:** The paper is content to document a single-country pattern when the real contribution would require saying something broader about access, substitution, or welfare.

### What is the gap between this paper and one that would excite the top 10 people in the field?
A paper that would excite the field would likely do at least one of the following:

1. **Show the substitution margin directly**  
   Branches down, but what rises instead—agent networks, POS density, account usage, mobile money, digital transfers, rural transaction access?

2. **Show who is harmed or helped**  
   Is the transition regressive? Does it widen rural-urban gaps? Does onboarding fall? Do low-literacy or low-connectivity populations lose?

3. **Generalize beyond Nigeria**  
   Put Nigeria into a broader comparative frame of cash penalties/digitalization episodes.

4. **Offer a more fundamental economic claim**  
   For example: digital-finance policy changes the industrial organization of banking access.

Right now, the paper identifies a suggestive correlation and a good question. That is not enough for AER.

### Single most impactful advice
**Reframe the paper around the broader economic tradeoff—digitalization versus physical access—and add evidence on whether lost branches were replaced by other access channels or left a true access gap.**

If they can only change one thing, that is it.
