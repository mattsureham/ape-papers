## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether a government policy designed to push consumers away from cash and toward digital payments also hastened the retreat of brick-and-mortar banking. Using cross-country comparisons around Nigeria’s 2012 “cashless” policy, the paper documents that Nigeria’s bank branch density fell relative to peer countries, while ATM density did not rise commensurately. A busy economist should care because the transition to digital finance may have a hidden distributional cost: policies that modernize payments may also reduce physical access to the formal financial system.

The paper does **not** currently articulate this pitch as clearly as it should in the first two paragraphs. The opening starts with a descriptive puzzle about branch decline, but it does not immediately elevate the question to a broader economics audience: when governments tax cash, do they change the organization of financial intermediation, and with what consequences for inclusion? Right now the introduction reads like a country case study; it needs to read like a paper about a general equilibrium tension in financial development.

### The pitch the paper should have

“Governments around the world are trying to move economies away from cash and into digital payments. But when policymakers make cash more expensive, they may not simply change how people pay—they may also change how banks choose to serve customers, potentially accelerating the withdrawal of physical banking infrastructure. This paper studies Nigeria’s 2012 cash-penalty regime, one of the earliest large-scale policies of its kind in Africa, and shows that the shift toward cashless payments coincided with a sharp relative decline in bank branches, without offsetting growth in ATMs. The broader question is whether policies that promote digital finance can inadvertently reduce physical access to formal banking, especially in low-income settings where digital and physical channels are not close substitutes.”

That is the story. The current intro is too Nigeria-forward and too result-forward, and not enough question-forward.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:** The paper documents that Nigeria’s 2012 cash-penalty policy coincided with a large relative decline in bank branch density, suggesting that policies promoting digital payments may accelerate the withdrawal of physical banking infrastructure.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest papers?
Only partially. The manuscript distinguishes itself from papers on digital payment adoption in Africa and from Nigeria-specific descriptive work on the cashless policy, but the differentiation is still thin. The reader understands that “no one has looked at branches in Nigeria,” but not yet why that omission matters for the larger literature on finance, technology adoption, and inclusion.

The contribution is currently differentiated mainly by:
1. **Setting**: Nigeria.
2. **Policy**: a cash-penalty regime.
3. **Outcome**: branch density rather than payment usage.

That is not enough for AER unless it is framed as answering a broader question.

### World question or literature-gap question?
At present it is split between the two, and too often it sounds like a literature-gap paper: “the existing literature has not addressed physical infrastructure consequences.” Stronger framing would make it a **world question**:

- When states push economies toward digital payments, what happens to the supply side of banking?
- Does digitalization complement or substitute for physical access points?
- Are there hidden inclusion costs to cash-reduction policies in developing economies?

That framing is much stronger than “this outcome has not yet been studied for Nigeria.”

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the intro?
Not cleanly. Right now they would probably say: “It’s a DiD on Nigeria’s cashless policy and bank branches.” That is not enough. They should instead say: “It shows that anti-cash policy may reorganize the banking sector by shrinking physical access points, which complicates the standard story that digital finance unambiguously expands inclusion.”

### What would make this contribution bigger?
Several possibilities:

- **Link branch decline to inclusion-relevant outcomes.** The paper currently stops at infrastructure. A bigger paper would connect branch retrenchment to account ownership, rural access, deposit penetration, firm banking access, or household usage of formal finance.
- **Show substitution, not just disappearance.** If the argument is that branches are replaced by digital channels, the paper needs evidence on whether digital channels actually filled the gap. Without that, the contribution is “branches fell,” which is narrower and more descriptive.
- **Exploit within-Nigeria heterogeneity.** The introduction admits this was the original design. That is likely the version with real upside. A cross-country panel with one treated unit is too blunt an instrument for the ambition of the claim.
- **Reframe around organization of banking, not just branch counts.** The big question is whether payment modernization changes the production technology of retail banking in low-income settings.
- **Use a sharper comparison class.** Compare cash-penalty regimes to other digital-finance expansions that did not tax cash, or compare Nigeria to countries with similar oil exposure but different payment policies. That would make the story less “Nigeria had a lot going on” and more “cash penalties matter specifically.”

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Many governments want to reduce reliance on cash and expand digital payments. The standard view is that this promotes efficiency, formalization, and financial inclusion.

### Tension
But digitalization may not simply add new channels; it may induce banks to scale back physical infrastructure. In settings where branches still matter for onboarding, trust, and access, this creates a tension: policies meant to broaden access may also remove one of its key delivery mechanisms.

### Resolution
Nigeria’s 2012 cash-penalty policy coincided with a substantial relative decline in bank branches, with no corresponding rise in ATM density.

### Implications
Digital-finance policies may reorganize the supply side of banking in ways that matter for inclusion, distribution, and spatial access. Policymakers should not assume that more digital payments automatically means broader financial access.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
Only weakly. The ingredients are all there, but the paper is still too much a **collection of empirical patterns plus caveats** rather than a fully shaped story.

The current narrative problem is that the paper spends a lot of time oscillating between:
- “This is an important policy effect,” and
- “This may just be recession.”

The candor is admirable, but narratively it leaves the paper stranded. The paper should instead be organized around a more defensible and still interesting claim:

> Nigeria’s anti-cash transition coincided with a major retreat in physical banking infrastructure; whether caused by the policy directly or by a broader reorganization of banking during the digital transition, the episode reveals a neglected tension between payment modernization and physical access.

That is a stronger story because it does not overclaim causality, but it still says something important about the world.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?
“I’d lead with: one of Africa’s earliest major anti-cash policies was followed by a roughly 30 percent relative decline in bank branch density, with no corresponding increase in ATMs.”

That is the headline fact.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
Some would lean in—especially development, banking, and fintech economists—but many would reach for their phones unless the presenter immediately broadens the framing. “Nigeria branch counts fell” is not by itself a top-journal fact. “Digital-payment policy may shrink physical banking access in places where branches still matter” is much better.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Almost immediately: **“Did people lose access, or were branches simply replaced by better digital channels?”**

That is the crucial unanswered question. The paper currently cannot answer it, and that is the central reason it feels incomplete as an AER paper.

### If findings are modest or partly null
The null ATM result is not the problem; in fact it helps the story by showing no obvious replacement in traditional physical infrastructure. But the paper needs to make clearer that this is not just “one negative result and one null result.” The value is in highlighting a potentially important **recomposition** of financial access that standard cross-country measures may not capture.

Still, the paper has to do more to persuade the reader that learning this is substantively important, not just a failed attempt to show digital expansion.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

Be honest: in its current form, this is **not** an AER paper.

### What is the gap?
Primarily:
- **Scope problem**
- **Ambition problem**
- Secondarily, **framing problem**

It is not mainly a prose issue. The paper’s current evidentiary scope is too narrow for the breadth of the claims it wants to make. A single-country, cross-country-comparison paper with one salient but hard-to-interpret reduced-form fact is not enough for AER unless the fact is truly astonishing or the mechanism and implications are nailed down. Here, the result is interesting but not yet decisive enough, and the paper cannot tell us whether branch decline represented harm, substitution, or broader restructuring.

### Is it a novelty problem?
Partly. The exact setting/outcome combination is new enough, but the deeper question is not yet pushed far enough beyond “another policy evaluation of digital finance.” Novelty at AER level comes from changing how the field thinks about digitalization and access, not from being first to study branch counts in Nigeria.

### Single most impactful advice
**If the author could change only one thing: re-center the paper on the broader question of whether digital-payment policy changes the supply of financial access, and bring evidence that speaks to substitution versus loss of access.**

That is the pivot. Without that, the paper remains an interesting descriptive policy note. With that, it could become part of a much bigger conversation.

If more bluntly stated: **the paper needs one more empirical leg**—something on who loses, what replaces branches, or how access changes—not just that branches decline.
