This is an interesting idea with a potentially strong hook, but in its current form the paper is selling a result that it simultaneously tells the reader not to believe. That is a strategic problem more than an econometric one. The core instinct — regulatory budgets may change classification behavior rather than total regulatory activity — is AER-relevant. ## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks how bureaucracies respond when governments impose a hard quantitative constraint on regulation. Studying Trump’s EO 13771 “two-for-one” regulatory budget, it argues that agencies did not mainly reduce the amount of rulemaking; instead, they shifted the composition of rulemaking away from actions likely to trigger the constraint, suggesting that regulatory budgets can reshape the form of state action more than its level.

Why should a busy economist care? Because this is not really a paper about one executive order. It is about a general issue in public economics and political economy: when governments regulate through budgets, caps, and performance metrics, do organizations do less of the targeted activity, or do they relabel and reorganize what they do?

### Does the paper articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Not quite. The current introduction is pretty good by field-journal standards, but it still reads too much like “first paper on EO 13771” and not enough like “general lesson about constrained bureaucracy.” It also overshoots by claiming “first causal evidence” and then quickly concedes that the headline result is fragile. The first two paragraphs should not lead with novelty claims that the rest of the introduction softens.

### What the first two paragraphs should say instead

Something like:

> Governments increasingly try to control bureaucracy using quantitative constraints: spending caps, staffing ceilings, performance targets, and, in regulation, “one-in-two-out” regulatory budgets. But such constraints need not reduce activity one-for-one. Bureaucracies may instead adapt by changing what they classify, prioritize, and route through different procedural channels. Whether regulatory budgets reduce regulation itself or mainly reallocate it across categories is an important and largely unanswered question for public economics and administrative law.
>
> This paper studies the most consequential U.S. attempt to impose such a constraint: Executive Order 13771, which required executive agencies to offset new regulatory actions with repeals and maintain net-zero regulatory costs. Using cross-agency differences in how exposed agencies were to the order, I show that the main response appears to have been compositional rather than volumetric: more exposed agencies shifted away from economically significant rules, while total rulemaking changed less. The broader implication is that regulatory budgets may change the type of regulation governments produce more than the amount they produce.

That is the pitch the paper should own.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### Contribution in one sentence

The paper’s contribution is to argue that binding regulatory budgets induce bureaucratic adaptation on the composition/classification margin — changing which rules agencies issue and how they label them — rather than primarily reducing total rulemaking.

Only partially. The paper names several broad literatures, but the differentiation is still fuzzy. Right now the contribution can too easily be paraphrased as “a DiD paper on Trump-era deregulation using Federal Register data.” That is not enough. The author needs to distinguish the paper from at least four nearby genres:

1. **Papers on the quantity of regulation**  
   The paper must say: those papers ask whether regulation rises or falls; I ask whether binding constraints alter the mix of actions and the use of regulatory categories.

2. **Papers on presidential control of agencies**  
   The paper must say: those papers focus on whether presidents can steer bureaucracy; I show one particular technology of control — a regulatory budget — may work by changing administratively salient classifications.

3. **Papers on gaming/public-sector performance metrics**

4. **Administrative-law or policy commentary on EO 13771**

The paper is strongest when framed as a world question: **How do agencies adapt when the state imposes a regulatory budget?**

AER wants a paper about the world.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Very specifically:

- **Different outcome variable:** The paper would be much bigger if it could show not just fewer “significant” designations, but that substantively similar rules were rerouted into less costly procedural categories, or that cost estimates bunched just below thresholds. ## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Governments want to restrain the administrative state. Regulatory budgets and “one-in-two-out” rules seem like a direct way to reduce regulatory burden by forcing agencies to economize on costly rules.

### Tension

But when control is imposed through measurable categories — “significant” rules, countable actions, booked costs — agencies may adjust on those same measured margins rather than on the underlying activity. So the puzzle is whether regulatory budgets reduce regulation or just reclassify it.

### Resolution

The paper’s evidence suggests the main response was compositional: more exposed agencies appear to have issued fewer rules designated as significant, while total rulemaking changed less, and this pattern persisted after rescission.

### Implications

If true, that means regulatory budgets are not neutral accounting devices; they shape the internal allocation and presentation of bureaucratic output. More broadly, performance constraints in government can change categories before they change conduct.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the pieces of one, but not a fully disciplined arc. Right now it is halfway between:
- a paper about one policy episode, and
- a paper about strategic bureaucratic adaptation.

The latter is the real story. The former is just the setting.

There is also a tonal problem: the paper repeatedly says the most interesting result is “suggestive,” “fragile,” and “requires confirmation.” That may be intellectually honest, but narratively it leaves the story unresolved. If the author wants to keep that caution — and they probably should — then the paper must redefine the object of contribution. Instead of “we establish a reclassification ratchet,” it should be “we present evidence consistent with a previously underappreciated margin of bureaucratic response, and show why aggregate rule counts are a misleading scorecard.”

That is a real narrative even if the estimates are modest.

Immediately: **Did agencies actually regulate less, or did they just relabel similar actions?**

### If the findings are modest, is that okay?

Yes, but only if the paper embraces a modest-but-important message. Null or imprecise results can matter when they overturn a strong policy presumption. Here the relevant presumption is: a hard regulatory budget should reduce total regulatory output. Evidence that the action may instead be on classification/composition is genuinely useful.

But the paper must stop trying to sound definitive and instead sound insightful. Right now it tries to have it both ways: “the primary response was compositional,” followed by “the result does not survive the bootstrap.” As written, that undermines trust in the storytelling.

The right move is:
- make the **question** big,
- make the **finding** disciplined and careful,
- make the **implication** conceptual.
