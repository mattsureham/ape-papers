## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple and potentially important question: when the Trump administration imposed a “regulatory budget” on federal agencies via EO 13771, did agencies actually regulate less, or did they instead change the kinds of rules they labeled and advanced? Using cross-agency differences in how exposed agencies were to the order, the paper’s headline claim is that the policy mostly changed the composition of rulemaking—especially the share designated “significant”—more than total rulemaking volume.

A busy economist should care because this is not really a paper about one executive order; it is a paper about whether bureaucracies respond to hard administrative constraints by changing real output or by reallocating activity across categories. That is a first-order question for state capacity, performance measurement, and the design of regulatory institutions.

**Does the paper articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Not quite. The current introduction gets reasonably close, but it overcommits too early to “first causal evidence,” then immediately walks the reader into a narrow institutional setup. It does not sufficiently foreground the broader economic question: when governments put budgets or quotas on regulation, what margin adjusts? Nor does it clearly separate the big-world question from the empirical implementation.

**What the first two paragraphs should say instead:**

> Governments around the world increasingly try to control regulation with numerical rules: caps on aggregate regulatory cost, “one-in-two-out” requirements, and procedural thresholds that determine which rules receive scrutiny. The key policy question is not only whether these systems reduce regulation, but how bureaucracies adapt to them. Do agencies make fewer rules, repeal more old ones, or shift activity into categories that avoid the binding constraint?
>
> This paper studies that question using the most ambitious U.S. test case: Executive Order 13771, which required executive agencies to offset new regulatory actions and keep net regulatory costs at zero. I show that the main response appears to have been compositional rather than volumetric: agencies more exposed to the order shifted away from rules designated “significant,” while total rulemaking changed much less. The broader implication is that regulatory budgets may reshape the classification and channel of regulation more than the amount of regulation itself.

That is the pitch. The current draft has the pieces, but it needs to lead with the general phenomenon—**bureaucratic adaptation to quantitative constraints**—not the executive-order trivia.

Several possibilities:

1. **Move from classification to substance.**

If the paper could show that reclassification was accompanied by similar text length, legal scope, economic importance, OIRA review behavior, or ex post outcomes, the contribution becomes much larger. 4. **Show substitution across channels.**

- Build on gaming/measurement literatures by showing a new adaptation margin in rulemaking.

Oddly, both.

It needs a tighter center of gravity: **bureaucratic adaptation to threshold-based regulatory constraints**.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?
It seems under-engaged with:

- empirical work on rulemaking institutions and OIRA review behavior,
- state capacity / bureaucratic behavior literatures in political economy.

It overreaches into some literatures that are not doing much work for it, especially broad macro-regulation citations.

Not yet. The most impactful framing is probably **not** “this contributes to the literature on regulatory design” in the abstract. It is:

That lets it talk to public economics, political economy, organizational economics, and administrative law all at once.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Governments want to control regulatory growth. One increasingly popular tool is the regulatory budget: numerical caps, offset requirements, and significance thresholds that force agencies to trade off actions.

### Tension
But bureaucracies facing hard constraints may adapt strategically. The central uncertainty is whether such policies reduce real regulatory activity or simply push agencies toward less constrained categories and labels. EO 13771 is an ideal test because it was unusually aggressive and its bindingness varied across agencies.

### Resolution
The paper’s answer is that the main adjustment seems to have been compositional: more exposed agencies appear to have reduced the share of rules designated significant, while total rulemaking volume changed modestly at most. The pattern persists after rescission, suggesting an institutional ratchet.

### Implications
If true, regulatory budgets may not reduce regulation in the straightforward way advocates imagine. Instead, they may re-channel regulation into forms that are less visible to the metric being targeted. This matters for policy design in the U.S. and elsewhere.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
It has the ingredients, but the arc is weakened by two problems.

1. **The paper tells the reader too aggressively that the evidence is weak before the story has landed.**  
   I appreciate the honesty, but strategically the introduction reads like a paper arguing itself out of contention. The first few pages repeatedly emphasize imprecision, fragile inference, and caveats. Those all belong in the paper, but not at the expense of the narrative spine.

2. **The “ratchet” story outruns the evidence as presented.**  
   The paper wants a setup-tension-resolution-implications arc ending in “persistent reclassification ratchet.” But the evidence is really more modest: non-reversal in the post period. That can support persistence, but “ratchet” sounds stronger, more structural, and more mechanism-specific than the paper can currently deliver.

So yes, there is a potential story, but the paper is still somewhat a **collection of suggestive results looking for a sharper conceptual frame**.

### What story should it be telling?
Not “Trump’s EO 13771 caused X.”  
Instead:

> “When governments regulate via budgets and thresholds, agencies adapt on the margin that the metric leaves open. In this case, the open margin was classification.”

That is the story.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?
“I think the Trump two-for-one regulatory budget may not have reduced rulemaking much; instead, agencies more exposed to it seem to have shifted away from calling their rules ‘significant.’”

That is the memorable fact.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
Some would lean in—especially public economists, political economists, and anyone interested in state capacity or measurement. But many would immediately ask whether this is just relabeling or whether substantive regulation actually changed. And that is the right question.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
“Did agencies actually change the substance or cost of regulation, or just manipulate classification?”

That is the core strategic issue. If the paper cannot answer that, it at least needs to make that uncertainty central and explain why the classification margin itself is intrinsically important.

### If findings are null or modest, is that itself interesting?
Potentially yes. “The most aggressive regulatory budget in U.S. history did not obviously reduce total rulemaking” is itself interesting. But the paper does not yet sell that null effectively. Right now the modest effects read less like a meaningful lesson and more like a paper apologizing for not finding cleaner estimates.

To make the modestness interesting, the paper needs to say:

- This was a very high-profile, politically salient, administratively costly intervention.
- If even this policy mainly changed categories rather than aggregate output, that is informative about the limits of metric-based control over bureaucracies.
- Null effects on volume are substantively important because advocates sold the policy as a major deregulatory tool.

In other words, the null must be reframed as a finding about the **limits of command-and-control over administrative output**.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

Bluntly: in its current form, this is **not yet an AER paper**. The gap is not mainly executional polish. It is substantive positioning and ambition.

### What is the main gap?

**Mostly a scope/novelty/ambition problem, with some framing issues layered on top.**

- **Framing problem:** The paper’s real idea is better than its current presentation. It should be framed as evidence on bureaucratic adaptation to threshold-based constraints, not just “EO 13771 did/didn’t work.”
- **Scope problem:** The outcomes are too close to the targeted classification system. That makes the paper feel inward-looking. It needs evidence on whether classification changes mapped into anything substantively important.
- **Novelty problem:** The broad intuition—organizations game metrics—is already well known. To clear the AER bar, the paper needs to show something that materially changes how economists think about regulatory governance, not just confirm a familiar adaptation story in one domain.
- **Ambition problem:** The paper is careful but cautious to a fault. It reads like a competent, well-designed field-journal paper rather than a paper trying to reshape a conversation.

### What is the single most impactful advice?
**Show that the apparent reclassification response changed substantively meaningful regulatory output, not just labels.**

If the author could only change one thing, that is it. Everything else is secondary. If the paper can demonstrate that agencies shifted below the “significant” threshold while leaving traces of similar underlying economic importance—or, conversely, that substantive burden really fell—then the paper becomes about the design failure or success of regulatory budgets. Without that, it remains a suggestive paper about classification behavior.
