**Summary**  
The paper studies Taiwan’s 2010 reform that replaced generous, sector-specific R&D tax credits with a universal 15% credit. Using USPTO patent filings aggregated at the USPC class-year level and a difference-in-differences strategy, the author finds that treated (formerly favored) technology classes did not reduce patenting; if anything, treated classes—particularly semiconductors—show modestly higher counts post-reform, while patent quality proxies remain unchanged. Placebo tests with Israel and South Korea and robustness checks support the null/positive finding, which the author interprets through an “inframarginal subsidy” lens.

**Essential Points**

1. **Clarify treatment classification and ensure comparability of control classes.** The 22 “treated” USPC classes span several strategic sectors, but the paper lacks detail on how these classes map to SUI industries and why the remaining 28 topo classes constitute a valid counterfactual. If treated and control classes differ in underlying growth trends, technology spillovers, or exposure to global demand shocks, the DiD estimate may conflate the tax reform with compositional shifts in Taiwan’s patenting portfolio. Provide a clear mapping (ideally in an appendix table) and demonstrate that treated and control classes were on similar trajectories pre-2010 beyond the aggregated event-study coefficients. An option is to match treated classes to a subset of control classes with similar pre-period trends or to include class-specific linear or quadratic trends.

2. **Strengthen the parallel trends evidence and robustness to differential dynamics.** The text asserts “flat pre-trends,” but the event study is summarized only qualitatively. Publishing the event-study graph with confidence bands (and ideally coefficients) would allow readers to judge whether the parallel trends assumption holds, especially since innovation can evolve in technology-specific ways. Also consider estimating the DiD with leads and lags or including “fake” treatment years to show stability. Without visual/quantitative evidence, the central identification assumption remains under-supported.

3. **Address potential contemporaneous policy or macro developments and alternative mechanisms.** The IIA reform coincided with broader shifts: the global financial crisis aftermath, Taiwan’s integration into global value chains, and possible expansion of other subsidies (e.g., direct grants, infrastructure investments). The paper mentions the crisis but relies on year fixed effects to absorb it; however, if, say, semiconductor demand rebounded or multinationals made complementary investments in 2010, the treated classes’ patenting could rise for reasons unrelated to the tax reform. A more convincing interpretation would either control for observable sectoral demand shocks (e.g., exports by sector) or argue why such shocks would affect treated and control classes equally. At a minimum, discuss other policy changes during 2010 that might confound the effect and, if data allow, control for class-specific measures of global demand or firm entry/exit.

**Suggestions**

- **Expand the event-study exposition.** Graph the coefficients for each relative year, include confidence bands, and discuss any suggestive dynamics around 2010. This will anchor the parallel-trends claim and help readers see whether the positive post-treatment coefficients result from a one-time jump, pre-trends, or persistent divergence.

- **Disaggregate treatment by firm type or patenter.** Because the hypothesis hinges on frontier firms being inframarginal, it would be informative to see whether the positive effect concentrates among large exporters (e.g., TSMC, UMC). If assignee-level data are available, consider splitting patents by firm size or export orientation, or by whether the firm previously received SUI credits. Even an appendix table showing that the top few Taiwanese assignees dominated treated classes and maintained high patent counts post-2010 would strengthen the mechanism.

- **Examine intensity margins (claims, citations) conditional on counts.** The paper reports mean claims and citation averages but does not condition these on changes in patent counts. Consider a triple-difference where the unit is patent, interact treatment with time, and control for firm fixed effects if possible. Alternatively, examine the distribution of citations or claims to see if the composition of patents changed (e.g., fewer low-quality patents replacing high-quality ones). This would test the “substitution” interpretation mentioned in the discussion.

- **Document the path from tax credit to patenting more rigorously.** Patenting is a noisy proxy for R&D, and firms may adjust inputs other than patentable outputs (e.g., process innovation, trade secrets). Incorporate additional outcomes if data permit (e.g., number of USPTO continuations, patent families, citations to industry-leading patents) or at least discuss the potential bias from using patent counts and how it influences interpretation (especially since the effect is positive, which could reflect sectoral expansion rather than R&D intensity). 

- **Consider alternative control groups beyond USPC classes.** While the static control classes appear reasonable, exploring robustness to alternative control definitions—such as classes belonging to nearby technology clusters or classes with similar pre-2009 citation structures but not designated by SUI—would demonstrate that the result is not sensitive to class selection.

- **Provide more context on the credit reform’s implementation timeline and anticipatory behavior.** Did firms know about the sunset well in advance (since it was legislated) and thus adjust behavior before 2010? If so, pre-treatment dynamics might be contaminated. If data on R&D expenditures or tax filings exist, even descriptive, add them to show whether firms accelerated investment before 2010 or whether the reform was unexpected. This would also inform whether the “phantom credit” operates through expectations.

- **Explain standard-error clustering and degrees of freedom concerns.** With 22 treated classes and 28 controls, clustering at the class level yields relatively few clusters. Discuss the implications (e.g., potential over-rejection) and, if feasible, show results with wild-cluster bootstrap or two-way clustering (class × year) to reassure readers. Similarly, mention whether the Poisson QMLE uses robust SEs and whether overdispersion affects inference.

- **Clarify placebo-country DDD specification.** The triple difference adds inter-country identifiers, but the text focuses on Israel and Korea without fully explaining why these countries are the best comparators. Provide summary statistics showing that Israel and Korea had similar patenting profiles pre-2010 (class distribution, growth rates). Also specify whether the DDD is balanced (equal weights) or whether Taiwan’s larger sample dominates the estimate; if unbalanced, re-weighting or sample restriction might be necessary.

- **Discuss external validity carefully.** The conclusion extrapolates to CHIPS and EU subsidies, but Taiwan’s sectoral structure, firm composition, and institutional environment differ. Temper these statements by acknowledging that frontier firms in other countries may react differently, that non-patent innovation is beyond the scope, and that targeted subsidies might matter more for emerging sectors where the credit could shift behavior.

Overall, the paper is well structured and tackles an important question with a plausible empirical design. Addressing these points—especially clarifying treatment definition, strengthening parallel-trend evidence, and contextualizing alternative explanations—will make the inference more convincing and the contribution sharper.
