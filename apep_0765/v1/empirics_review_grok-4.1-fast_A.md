### 1. Idea Fidelity
The paper deviates substantially from the original idea manifest. The manifest proposed tract-level analysis linking SNAP retailer exits to HMDA data over 2007-2024, using staggered difference-in-differences (DiD) with county + year fixed effects (FE), augmented by an instrumental variable (IV) based on corporate chain bankruptcies (e.g., A&P, Tops). Treatment was defined as tracts losing a SNAP supermarket, with outcomes including log(originations), denial rates, log(median loan amount), and FHA/VA share, in a panel of ~70,000 tracts × 15+ years and ~5,000-10,000 exits.

The executed paper shifts to county-level aggregation (3,186 counties × 2018-2023), defines treatment as a county's *first* SNAP supermarket deauthorization (binary post-treatment indicator), and employs two-way fixed effects (TWFE) without staggered timing, event studies, or the promised IV. It uses a shorter HMDA period (2018-2023 via CFPB), omits NaNDA/ICPSR data, and ignores tract geocoding despite both datasets supporting it. These changes weaken spatial precision for "neighborhood" effects and abandon the manifest's core identification (staggered DiD + IV), producing a less credible test of the research question.

### 2. Summary
This paper tests whether SNAP supermarket closures signal neighborhood decline to mortgage lenders, using county-year TWFE DiD on HMDA home-purchase outcomes (originations, denial rates, loan amounts, FHA shares) merged to USDA SNAP retailer data (2018-2023). It finds precisely estimated null effects across outcomes, robust to state × year FE, and interprets this as evidence that food desert formation does not propagate through credit markets. The contribution is a clean closure of a novel channel linking retail exits to housing finance, though at coarser county geography than ideal for neighborhood effects.

### 3. Essential Points
The paper has three critical flaws that must be fixed for AER: Insights consideration; failure to address them warrants rejection.

1. **Geography mismatch undermines the research question.** The manifest and introduction frame this as a *neighborhood* effect (citing tract-level studies like Kolea 2021 on property values), but analysis aggregates to counties (average population ~100,000; land area ~1,000 sq mi). A supermarket exit in one tract (or urban corner) cannot credibly affect county-wide lending, diluting power and mismeasuring local spillovers. Authors must redo at tract-level (feasible per manifest's "smoke test" and both datasets' geocoding), or explicitly justify/motivate county (e.g., via spatial falsification tests showing no intra-county spillovers).

2. **Identification lacks credibility without parallel trends validation or IV.** TWFE on *first* county exit assumes never-treated counties (41%) are valid controls, but treatment is non-random: counties with supermarkets are systematically larger/urban/richer (Table 1 implies selection via orig=793 mean). No event study, dynamic leads/lags, or pre-trends plot validates parallel trends; the "support" paragraph invokes store-level decisions but ignores county economic sorting. The promised chain bankruptcy IV (exogenous shocks) is absent—implement it at tract/county level to address endogeneity (e.g., exits correlate with local downturns). Sun-Abraham or Callaway-Sant'Anna estimators are also essential for any future staggered setup.

3. **Limited scope and power hide heterogeneity.** HMDA covers only 2018-2023 (post-GFC boom + COVID distortions), ignoring 2007-2017 bulk CSVs or NaNDA (manifest's 1981+ aggregates). With ~59% ever-treated and binary post, power is strained (e.g., log(orig) SD=1.81 rules out ±1.7% precisely, but economic priors suggest smaller/local effects). No sub-samples (urban/rural, low-income tracts) or interactions (e.g., with baseline food access) explore why null. Extend timeframe and stratify, or compute minimum detectable effects (MDEs) by subgroup to confirm informativeness.

### 4. Suggestions
While the null is cleanly presented and policy-relevant (ruling out credit feedbacks for food desert interventions), the paper would strengthen considerably with these non-essential refinements, elevating it toward AER: Insights standards.

**Data and Measurement Improvements.** Tract-level execution is low-hanging fruit: SNAP's 703K retailers geocode to tracts (manifest's Shannon repo), and HMDA's census_tract field enables direct merges without "no aggregation penalty." Recode treatment as tract-year supermarket exits (staggered, ~5-10K events), controls as similar non-exiting tracts (e.g., matched on pre-trends via entropy balancing). Add covariates like tract income, % minority, or Zillow ZHVI for balance checks (Table 1 could expand to treated/control diffs). For SNAP, clarify "supermarket-class" (Large Grocery/Supermarket/Super Store)—sensitivity to excluding supercenters (e.g., Walmart) would address composition shifts (noted in background). Incorporate 2007-2017 HMDA CSVs + NaNDA for 2007-2024 panel, enabling pre-GFC baselines and longer pre-trends.

**Empirical Strategy Enhancements.** Replace TWFE with an event study: \( Y_{it} = \sum_{k=-5}^{5} \beta_k Treat_i \times 1\{t = g_i + k\} + \alpha_i + \gamma_t \), plotting \(\beta_k\) (k=0 normalized) to visualize dynamics—essential for staggered adoption (59% treated over 6 years). At tract-level, add tract pair FEs for supermarkets (à la Qian 2023). Implement IV: instrument tract/county exits with chain bankruptcies (A&P 2010/2015, Tops 2018/2021, etc.), using Barnes 2SLS stages; first-stage from USDA data should be strong (~10-20% affected retailers). Robustness: (i) alternative controls (e.g., synthetic controls via tract nearest neighbors); (ii) spatial autocorrelation (Moran tests, Conley SEs); (iii) falsification on non-supermarkets (e.g., convenience exits).

**Heterogeneity and Mechanisms.** The discussion's three explanations (fundamentals, small effects, temporality) are speculative—test them. Interact treatment with baseline tract traits: e.g., high food desert index (USDA), low income (<80% AMI), or % Black (<50% white). Expect larger nulls/negatives in vulnerable areas if robust. Mechanism tests: (i) split denial rates by reason (HMDA fields: debt-to-income, credit history vs. "property/location"); (ii) regress on appraisal gaps if LARGER HMDA flags available (2020+); (iii) link to ZTRAX/Zillow for comp sales effects post-exit. Dose-response is good (Table 5A)—extend to # exits or share of tract grocers lost.

**Presentation and Extensions.** AER: Insights favors brevity—trim institutional background (merge to intro), expand sumstats (add treated/control Table 1 diffs, pre-trends Figure 1). Add Figure 2: maps of treatment timing/shares by metro size. Conclusion overclaims "robust to food infrastructure loss"—nuance to "SNAP supermarket exits." Bigger picture: link to neighborhood cascades (manifest's "Crime & Neighborhood Cascade"); append crime/property value falsifications (e.g., merge FBI UCR, Zillow) showing expected effects there, nullifying credit. Policy: quantify bounds (e.g., CIs imply denial <0.6pp = <$1B/year lost originations). Standardized effects (App. Table 6) are nice—promote to main text. Finally, disclose APEP automation fully (e.g., code repo verification).

These changes would make a compelling, novel null: tract-level precision + IV + dynamics credibly close the mortgage channel, with policy punch for FRESH-like programs. Current version is promising but provisional due to county coarseness.
