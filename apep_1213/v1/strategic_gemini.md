### 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper uses a massive bank fraud in Moldova (the "stolen billion") as a natural experiment to study how the destruction of credit supply affects the real economy in thin markets. It exploits a unique historical instrument—the Soviet-era placement of bank branches—to show that regions dependent on state-linked banks suffered permanent, decade-long employment losses. The paper argues that credit-starved firms don't necessarily die; they become "zombies" that survive but stop hiring, creating a persistent "inheritance trap" for transition economies.

**Evaluation:** The paper does a decent job of framing the "stolen billion" in the first paragraph, but it gets bogged down in the raion-level mechanics too quickly.
**The pitch it should have:** 
"When a dominant state bank collapses due to internal fraud, does the private sector eventually fill the void, or does the historical legacy of the bank's footprint create a permanent trap? By exploiting the Soviet-era administrative placement of Moldova’s branch network, this paper identifies a 'zombie firm' channel where credit shocks do not cause firm exit but rather a decade-long stagnation in hiring. These findings suggest that in developing economies, institutional 'legacies' of central planning can amplify modern financial shocks into permanent shifts in the geographic distribution of economic activity."

---

### 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution:** The paper identifies a "zombie firm" mechanism where credit supply shocks lead to persistent labor market contraction without firm exit, driven by geographically "locked-in" institutional legacies.

- **Differentiation:** It differentiates itself from Chodorow-Reich (2014) and Huber (2018) by focusing on a *pure supply shock* (fraud) in a *thin market* (transition economy) with a *longer horizon* (10 years).
- **World vs. Literature:** It currently sits somewhere in between. The "Soviet Inheritance Trap" framing is a strong "World" hook, but the empirical section leans heavily on filling a gap in the credit-shock literature.
- **"Just another DiD?":** A cynical reader would call this "A DiD on a small country with few clusters." To beat this, the paper needs to emphasize the **persistence**. Most banking papers look at 2-3 years; this looks at 10.
- **Bigger Contribution:** The contribution would be bigger if it moved beyond employment and looked at **productivity** or **reallocation**. If firms aren't exiting, is the "zombie" status misallocating capital away from more productive entrants in other raions?

---

### 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

- **Neighbors:** La Porta et al. (2002) on state banks; Chodorow-Reich (2014) on credit shocks; Acemoglu et al. (2011) on institutional persistence.
- **Positioning:** It should **synthesize** the banking literature with the "path dependency/persistence" literature. It’s currently playing too much in the banking sandbox.
- **Missed Conversation:** The paper is surprisingly quiet on the **Misallocation** literature (Hsieh & Klenow). If firms survive but don't grow, this is a classic misallocation story. Connecting to the "Growth" or "Development" literatures would broaden the AER appeal.
- **Narrowness:** It risks being seen as a "Moldova paper." It needs to frame Moldova as a *representative laboratory* for all post-Soviet/developing states with centralized banking legacies.

---

### 4. NARRATIVE ARC

- **Setup:** Soviet planners placed banks for coverage, not profit, creating a map of financial dependence.
- **Tension:** A massive fraud wipes out this network. Does the market "correct" this, or does the 50-year-old map dictate the recovery?
- **Resolution:** The market fails to correct it. Firms in dependent raions shrink and stay small for a decade. 
- **Implications:** Financial development isn't just about aggregate liquidity; it’s about the geographic "plumbing." If the plumbing is a legacy of a dead empire, the economy is fragile.

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong. The "zombie firm" finding is the crucial "twist" that prevents this from being a boring "credit is good for jobs" paper.

---

### 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

- **Lead Fact:** "Ten years after the banks were robbed, the towns that relied on those Soviet branches still haven't recovered their lost jobs."
- **Reaction:** People will lean in because of the 10-year persistence and the "stolen billion" hook.
- **Follow-up:** "Why didn't other banks just open branches there if there was demand?" This is the question the paper *must* answer better in the discussion.

---

### 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

- **Front-loading:** The "Soviet Inheritance" history is the most "AER-y" part. It should be moved earlier or integrated more tightly into the intro.
- **Data Section:** The admission that branch-level data isn't machine-readable is a "referee-bait" weakness. The author should find a way to get that data or at least a subset of it to validate the proxy.
- **The "Zombie" Evidence:** This is currently "inferential." The author needs to dig deeper into the "number of enterprises" result. Is there a way to look at firm *age* or *size* distributions to prove these are actually old firms surviving?

---

### 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The gap is **Ambition**. Currently, it feels like a very high-quality *Journal of Money, Credit and Banking* paper. To make it AER, it needs to be a paper about **Institutional Path Dependency**.

**The Single Most Impactful Advice:** Shift the framing from "the effects of a banking crisis in Moldova" to "how the geographic footprints of planned economies create permanent vulnerabilities to modern financial shocks." 

If the author can prove that the *lack of exit* (the zombie channel) is the reason for the *lack of recovery*, they have a top-tier story about why some regions stay poor forever.
