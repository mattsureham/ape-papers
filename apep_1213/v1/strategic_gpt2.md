## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper studies whether the collapse of a dominant bank can cause long-lasting damage to local labor markets when firms have few alternative sources of credit. Using Moldova’s 2014 “stolen billion” banking fraud, the paper argues that places more dependent on the failed Soviet-inherited branch network suffered persistent employment losses, with little firm exit, suggesting local firms survived but stopped growing.

A busy economist should care because the paper is trying to say something broader than “Moldova had a banking scandal”: when financial intermediation is geographically concentrated, a bank failure can become a place-based labor market shock with decade-long effects. That is potentially important for banking, development, regional economics, and transition economies.

### Does the paper articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Not quite. The current introduction is reasonably readable, but it takes a few paragraphs to get to the real point, and even then the pitch wobbles between three different stories:

1. a banking-crisis paper,
2. a Soviet-legacy/institutional-historical paper,
3. a zombie-firm paper.

Those are related, but the paper has not decided which is the headline. Right now the first two paragraphs emphasize the scandal and the branch network, but the actual claim of broad interest is about **the long-run real consequences of concentrated local credit supply destruction**. That needs to be front and center immediately.

### What the first two paragraphs should say instead

A stronger opening would say something like:

> What happens to local employment when a dominant bank suddenly disappears in places with no close substitutes? This question matters well beyond banking crises: in thin financial markets, the collapse of a single intermediary can cut off firms from working capital and investment finance, potentially turning a financial scandal into a persistent regional labor market shock.
>
> This paper studies that question using Moldova’s 2014 “stolen billion” fraud, which brought down three banks that accounted for much of the country’s branch network. Because the largest bank inherited a Soviet-era geographic footprint that left some districts heavily reliant on it and others less exposed, the episode generates sharp cross-regional variation in the destruction of local credit supply. I show that more exposed districts suffered large, persistent employment losses for a decade, while enterprise counts changed little, consistent with firms surviving but shrinking rather than exiting.

That is the pitch the paper should own.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### Contribution in one sentence

The paper’s contribution is to argue that the collapse of a geographically dominant, state-linked bank caused persistent local employment losses in bank-dependent regions, with effects operating more through firm contraction than firm exit.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest papers?

Only partially. The paper cites standard “real effects of credit supply shocks” papers, but the differentiation is still too generic: “cleaner shock,” “fraud not fundamentals,” “Soviet legacy provides exposure.” That is not yet sharp enough.

The paper needs to distinguish itself along dimensions that readers will remember:

- **What is new substantively?** Persistent local employment effects a decade later.
- **What is new conceptually?** Concentrated local bank dependence transforms bank failure into a place-based labor market shock.
- **What is new empirically?** Geographic exposure shaped by inherited state-bank branch networks in a transition economy.
- **What is new mechanistically?** Surviving firms contract rather than exit.

Right now the introduction lists these pieces, but does not rank them. The result is that the paper sounds like “another regional credit-supply paper, but in Moldova.”

### Is the contribution framed as a question about the WORLD or as filling a gap in a LITERATURE?

Mostly the former, which is good, but the paper repeatedly slips back into literature-gap language. Its strongest framing is world-facing:

- What happens when dominant local lenders fail?
- Do thin banking markets create persistent local scarring?
- Can historical institutional geography shape exposure to modern financial shocks?

That is the right mode. It should lean harder into that and reduce the “this contributes to two literatures” boilerplate.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?

Not cleanly enough. I think many readers would summarize it as:

> “It’s a DiD on a bank collapse in Moldova using local bank dependence.”

That is not enough for AER-level memorability.

What they should be able to say is:

> “It shows that when a dominant local lender collapses, the effect is not mainly mass firm death but long-term shrinkage of surviving firms, and that historical state-bank geography predicts where the labor market damage persists.”

That is much stronger.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Specific ways to enlarge the contribution:

1. **Make the place-based labor market angle the headline.**  
   Right now employment is treated as one outcome among several. It should be the core object. If possible, deepen the labor market implications: wage bill, sectoral employment composition, worker reallocation, formal/informal substitution, or migration. Without this, the paper risks reading like a finance paper with one reduced-form employment outcome.

2. **Strengthen the “firm contraction, not exit” mechanism.**  
   This is potentially the paper’s most distinctive result, but it is currently inferential and underdeveloped. If there is any way to show changes in firm size distribution, average employment per firm, or growth of incumbent firms versus entry/exit margins, that would materially raise the paper’s ambition.

3. **Push beyond Moldova as a case study.**  
   The current “Soviet inheritance trap” language gestures at a broader idea, but the paper does little to substantiate external relevance. 4. **Clarify whether the contribution is about fraud, state ownership, or local banking concentration.**

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Before this paper: economists know bank credit shocks can affect firms and employment, but much of the evidence comes from richer countries or episodes where supply and demand are entangled, and less is known about whether such shocks create persistent regional labor-market scars in thin banking markets.

### Tension

The puzzle is whether the collapse of a major bank mainly causes temporary disruption, firm exit, or long-lasting local contraction—and whether historical financial geography determines who gets hit hardest. Moldova provides an extreme shock, but the paper must convince readers that this is more than an idiosyncratic scandal.

### Resolution

The paper finds that more bank-dependent raions experience substantial and persistent employment losses after the banking collapse, while enterprise counts barely change. The implied story is that firms survive but contract.

### Implications

The implication is that dominant-bank failure can generate persistent place-based economic damage, particularly where financial alternatives are thin. Historical branch-network legacies can shape present-day vulnerability. Policy should care not only about depositors and bank stability, but also about local substitution capacity in credit markets.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

Serviceable, but not fully coherent. At the moment it feels like a credible empirical paper with several candidate stories competing for dominance.

The main narrative problem is that the paper’s title and abstract promise a dramatic historical-institutional story—“The Soviet Inheritance Trap”—while the actual evidence is more modestly about regional exposure to a bank collapse proxied by financial-enterprise density. The narrative outruns the measurement.

So the paper currently risks being:
- **too dramatic in language,**
- **too modest in empirical object,**
- and therefore slightly mismatched.

### What story should it be telling?

It should tell this story:

1. Moldova’s bank fraud caused a sudden collapse of a dominant lender.
2. Because local banking markets were thin and unevenly concentrated, some districts lost much more credit access than others.
3. Those more exposed districts suffered persistent employment losses.
4. The absence of firm-exit effects suggests the damage came through suppressed growth of surviving firms.
5. The broader lesson is about the labor-market consequences of concentrated local finance, not just about Moldova.

That story is clear, plausible, and worth telling.

The “Soviet inheritance trap” can remain as a subtitle-like motif, but it should not bear more conceptual weight than the evidence can support.

Some would lean in, especially labor/finance/development economists. But many would still need convincing that this is not just a colorful case study from a small country with a noisy treatment proxy.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Probably one of these:

- “Is this really about credit supply, or just about poorer rural places?”
- “Why didn’t firms exit?”
- “How do we know the branch-network story is doing real work if the treatment is financial-enterprise density rather than actual bank branches?”
- “What should I learn from Moldova for banking systems elsewhere?”

Those are strategic questions, not referee-level econometrics questions. The paper needs to answer them in its framing.

### If the findings are modest: is the modesty interesting?

The employment effect is not modest; it is reasonably large. The null on enterprise counts is interesting, but only if the paper leans into why that matters. Right now the “zombie firm” interpretation is intuitive but still thinly evidenced. Without richer mechanism, it could sound like a post hoc gloss on a null result.

The paper should make the case that learning “credit shocks suppress incumbent firm growth more than they induce exit” matters because it changes how we think about adjustment and policy. That is valuable if made more convincingly.
