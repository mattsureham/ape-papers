## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper examines how China’s 2018 "National Sword" policy—which effectively banned US recyclable exports—disrupted the domestic US recycling labor market. It uses county-level variation in pre-shock waste sector intensity to show that the loss of the world’s primary buyer caused a 4–14% decline in sector employment and a significant contraction in job creation.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this well in the first paragraph, but it frames the "so what" too narrowly around the "gap" in environmental science literature. 

**The pitch the paper *should* have:**
"Economists typically study trade shocks as 'import competition' (the China Shock). This paper identifies the mirror-image: an 'export-destination shock' where a unilateral foreign regulatory change destroyed the economic viability of a domestic green industry. By exploiting the collapse of the $2.7 billion waste trade, I show that the 'circular economy' is not a closed loop but a global supply chain whose domestic labor demand is precariously dependent on foreign environmental policy."

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:** The paper provides the first causal evidence that trade-dependent environmental infrastructure is vulnerable to "export-access" shocks, resulting in significant domestic labor market contraction when foreign demand is unilaterally withdrawn.

- **Differentiation:** It differentiates itself from the "China Shock" (Autor et al.) by flipping the sign—it’s not imports hurting manufacturing, but the loss of an export market hurting services/processing.
- **Framing:** It is currently framed as "filling a gap" in the National Sword literature. To get into the AER, it must be framed as a fundamental inquiry into the **fragility of the green economy's global supply chain.**
- **Clarity:** A smart economist would say: "It's the China Shock for garbage." 
- **Bigger Contribution:** To make it bigger, the author needs to explore the **incidence** of the shock. Who lost the jobs? If average earnings went up (as Table 2 suggests), were the most vulnerable low-skilled workers permanently displaced into unemployment or did they transition to other sectors? That transition (or lack thereof) is the "AER-level" question.

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

- **Closest Neighbors:** Autor, Dorn, and Hanson (2013) on trade shocks; Kellenberg (2012) on waste trade; and the literature on "Reverse Pollution Havens."
- **Positioning:** It should stop "complementing" environmental science and start "challenging" the trade literature. Most trade shock papers assume the shock is a surge in competition. This is a "refusal to buy" shock. 
- **Missing Literature:** The paper is thin on the **Urban/Regional Economics** of waste. It treats counties as islands, but waste is transported across county lines. It should speak to the "Spatial Economics" of the waste industry.
- **Right Conversation?** It’s currently in the "Waste Management" conversation. It needs to move into the **"Resilience of Global Value Chains"** conversation.

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

- **Setup:** For 20 years, the US "recycled" by simply shipping trash to China.
- **Tension:** China suddenly decided they didn't want our "foreign garbage." The economic floor of the US recycling industry (positive scrap value) turned into a ceiling (disposal costs).
- **Resolution:** Employment didn't just slow down; it collapsed relative to its high-growth trajectory, revealing that the US "green" boom was an artificial byproduct of Chinese industrial demand.
- **Implications:** You can't mandate a circular economy at the curb if you don't have a buyer at the end of the pipe.

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong. The "tension" is particularly compelling because it involves a $191M market falling to $1M (a 99.9% collapse), which is a "pure" shock rarely seen in economics.

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

**The Dinner Party Fact:** "When China stopped buying our plastic, the US recycling industry didn't just pivot—it shed 14% of its workforce and started dumping the stuff in landfills."

**Reaction:** People will lean in. They care about recycling and they care about China. 

**Follow-up Question:** "If we aren't shipping it to China, and we aren't hiring people to process it here, where is the plastic going?" (The paper hints at land-filling, but proving the **environmental-economic trade-off** would be the clincher).

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

- **Front-load the Event Study:** Table 4 (the event study) is the "money" result. The pre-trend growth premium followed by the sharp break is a beautiful visual that should be Figure 1, not Table 4.
- **Mechanism Section:** Section 5 (Firm dynamics) is too brief. If firm job gains fell and separations fell (the "chilled" labor market), this suggests a "wait and see" tragedy. This deserves more than a paragraph.
- **Appendix:** The "Standardized Effect Sizes" (Table 5) feel like a distraction from the narrative. Move them to an online appendix.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

**The Gap:** Currently, it’s a very good "Applied Micro" paper about a specific policy. To be an AER paper, it needs to be a paper about **the unintended consequences of the Global Green Transition.**

**Single Biggest Improvement:** Trace the **spillover effects** on the public sector. If private recycling firms contracted, did municipal budgets for waste management explode? If the author can link the "National Sword" shock to a fiscal crisis in high-exposure counties (higher taxes or cut services to pay for landfilling), the paper becomes a general interest masterpiece.
