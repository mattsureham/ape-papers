## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper studies what happened to U.S. recycling-sector jobs when China’s 2018 National Sword policy abruptly shut down the main export market for American recyclable waste. The big question is whether environmental infrastructure that looked “domestic” was in fact propped up by foreign demand—and whether the loss of a single buyer can generate a local labor-market shock analogous to the China import shock, but in reverse.

The core idea is interesting and potentially AER-relevant. A busy economist should care because it links trade dependence, environmental policy, and local labor markets in a clean, intuitive setting: when China stopped buying American waste, did U.S. recycling capacity shrink, and what does that reveal about the economic foundations of the circular economy?

### Does the paper articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Not quite. The opening is vivid, but the paper gets to the main economic question too slowly and frames it too much as “no one has estimated this causal effect yet.” That is a literature-gap pitch, not a world-question pitch. The first two paragraphs should much more directly state the broader question: how fragile are domestic industries that depend on a single foreign outlet, and what does the recycling case teach us about the economics of environmental policy?

### The pitch the paper should have

For decades, U.S. recycling was sustained by an implicit bargain with China: American municipalities collected and sorted waste, and China absorbed the output. When China’s National Sword policy abruptly shut that market in 2018, it created a sharp test of whether the U.S. recycling system was a viable domestic industry or a trade-dependent equilibrium resting on a single foreign buyer.

This paper shows that counties more exposed to waste management experienced a meaningful decline in recycling-sector employment after National Sword. The broader lesson is that environmental infrastructure can be highly vulnerable to trade shocks on the demand side: the “circular economy” in practice depended not just on collection and sorting, but on reliable downstream demand from abroad.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper’s contribution is to show that China’s National Sword import ban caused a contraction in U.S. waste-management employment, revealing that American recycling infrastructure was economically dependent on foreign demand and vulnerable to buyer-specific trade shocks.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?

Only partially. The paper differentiates itself from the environmental science literature by emphasizing labor-market effects, and from the trade-shock literature by stressing the loss of an export market rather than import competition. That is useful, but the differentiation is still somewhat thin. Right now the paper risks sounding like: “Here is another local-labor-market DiD, this time in recycling.”

The more powerful differentiation would be:

1. **This is not just a recycling paper.** It is a paper about the fragility of domestic sectors built around foreign downstream absorption.
2. **This is not just another trade-shock paper.** It studies a buyer-side shock to an environmentally regulated service sector.
3. **This is not just documenting job loss.** It says something about whether circular-economy systems are economically self-sustaining absent subsidized or guaranteed end-market demand.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a gap in a literature?

Too much as filling a literature gap. “No study has estimated the causal labor market effects...” is rarely the strongest lead. The stronger question is about the world: **Was U.S. recycling a real domestic industry, or a domestic collection system supported by export demand to China?**

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?

Somewhat, but not crisply enough. Right now they might say: “It’s a DiD on how National Sword affected waste-management employment.” That is accurate but not memorable. The paper needs a more portable insight.

What you want them to say is:  
**“It shows that the U.S. recycling sector was effectively built on access to Chinese demand, so when that buyer disappeared, local recycling employment fell. It’s a reverse trade-shock paper with implications for circular-economy policy.”**

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Most importantly: **connect labor-market effects to the broader economics of recycling-market viability.** Specific ways:

- **A different framing:** from “labor-market effects of National Sword” to “what National Sword revealed about the economics of U.S. recycling.”
- **Different outcomes:** municipal recycling program closures, landfill diversion, domestic processing investment, prices for recyclables, or local disposal behavior would make the paper feel much more consequential. Employment alone is narrower than the framing promises.
- **Mechanism:** distinguish collection, sorting, brokerage, and processing if possible. Right now “NAICS 562” is broad; a more granular mechanism would help show exactly what part of the recycling chain was trade-dependent.
- **Comparison:** directly compare this shock to other export-market collapses or to import-competition logic in a more conceptual way, not just rhetorically.
- **Generalization:** show that this is a case study of a broader phenomenon—local industries exposed to a single foreign buyer, especially in environmentally regulated markets.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Before National Sword, the U.S. recycling system operated in a world where China absorbed enormous quantities of recyclable waste, making municipal and private recycling economically workable even with contamination and low-quality sorting.

### Tension

Was this system genuinely domestic and sustainable, or was it a fragile equilibrium dependent on one foreign buyer? When China shut the door, would the effect be merely a redirection of trade flows, or a real contraction in U.S. recycling activity and employment?

### Resolution

More exposed counties saw declines in waste-management employment and related labor-market flows, suggesting the shock meaningfully contracted the sector rather than simply rerouting activity without local consequences.

### Implications

Environmental infrastructure can be deeply trade-dependent. Circular-economy policy cannot focus only on collection and sorting; it must also solve the problem of downstream demand for recycled materials.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the ingredients, but the arc is not yet fully disciplined. At times it reads like a set of regression outputs wrapped in a dramatic introduction. The strongest story is there, but the paper keeps slipping back into narrower empirical-report language.

It should be telling one crisp story:

> The U.S. thought it had a recycling industry; in fact it had a waste-export equilibrium. National Sword exposed that distinction, and the labor-market contraction is evidence that the domestic system was not commercially viable on its own.

That is the story. Everything else should serve it.

**“When China stopped buying U.S. recyclable waste, places more exposed to the recycling sector lost recycling jobs—suggesting that the U.S. recycling system was economically dependent on Chinese demand.”**

Or even more sharply:  
**“National Sword revealed that American recycling was not just environmental infrastructure; it was an export-dependent industry.”**

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

Some would lean in—especially trade, environmental, and urban/public economists—because the setting is vivid and unusual. But many will ask, fairly quickly: **is the result bigger than this specific niche?** If the answer remains just “there were fewer jobs in waste management,” attention will fade.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Almost certainly one of these:

1. **Did the waste get landfilled instead?**
2. **Did municipalities shut down recycling programs?**
3. **Was there domestic capacity expansion elsewhere that offset the job loss?**
4. **Is this really about trade dependence, or just about one small sector?**
5. **What does this imply for current circular-economy policy?**

The paper currently does not fully answer the most important follow-up questions. That is a strategic weakness.

### If the findings are modest, is the modesty itself interesting?

Yes, potentially. If the preferred estimate is closer to the smaller number, that could still be interesting if framed correctly: National Sword did not destroy a huge sector, but it revealed that the sector’s economics were precarious and adjustment was contractionary rather than transformational. The null-to-modest angle would matter if tied to the broader claim that **policy changed trade flows much more dramatically than it changed domestic green industrial capacity**.

But the current draft does not fully make that case. It sometimes sounds as though the smaller estimate is a consolation prize rather than part of the substantive lesson.
