## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
This paper asks whether the "Brussels Effect"—the global reach of EU regulation—significantly impacts the US labor market through the GDPR’s extraterritorial compliance requirements. Using a triple-difference design, it finds that while US Information-sector employment fell nationally following GDPR, this decline was not concentrated in states with high EU trade exposure, suggesting that the "compliance tax" transmits through firm-level global standards rather than regional trade channels. Economists should care because it provides the first empirical test of how foreign-origin digital regulation restructures domestic labor demand, a question of increasing urgency as the EU rolls out the AI Act and DMA.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this well in the second paragraph, but the first paragraph is a bit too "journalistic" (citing LinkedIn stats). 
**Proposed Revision for Paragraph 1:** "Does the extraterritorial reach of European regulation—the so-called 'Brussels Effect'—reshape labor demand in the United States? While the EU’s General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) imposes significant compliance costs on any firm serving European users, it remains unclear whether these costs transmit to foreign labor markets through bilateral trade flows or via uniform firm-wide shifts in data governance. This paper provides the first causal evidence of the GDPR’s impact on US employment and hiring, distinguishing between trade-based and firm-based transmission mechanisms."

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**Contribution:** The paper identifies that the labor-market transmission of the Brussels Effect operates through national firm-level compliance rather than state-level trade geography.

- **Differentiation:** It moves beyond the "EU-only" focus of *Goldberg et al. (2024)* and *Janssen et al. (2022)* and provides an empirical backbone to *Bradford’s (2020)* theoretical work on regulatory export.
- **World vs. Literature:** It frames itself as answering a question about the **WORLD** (the transmission of global regulatory shocks), which is an AER-level framing.
- **Differentiator:** A smart economist would see this as more than "another DiD." It is a test of **spatial transmission mechanisms** for intangible regulatory shocks.
- **Bigger Contribution:** To make this truly "Big," the author needs to bridge the gap between the 7.7% national decline (DD) and the null geographic effect (DDD). If the 7.7% is secular, the paper is just a precise null. If the author can link the 7.7% more tightly to GDPR (perhaps using firm-level data on EU digital exposure vs. merchandise exports), it becomes a blockbuster.

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
- **Neighbors:** *Goldberg et al. (2024)* (GDPR costs); *Bradford (2020)* (Brussels Effect); *Autor et al. (2007)* (Labor demand/Regulation).
- **Positioning:** It should **synthesize** the Brussels Effect theory with the Labor Demand literature.
- **Missing Conversations:** The paper is currently a bit light on the **International Trade** literature (e.g., Helpman, Melitz) regarding how multinational firms internalize global versus local costs. It should speak to the "pollution haven" or "regulatory race to the bottom/top" literatures.

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC
- **Setup:** The EU regulates the world; US firms scramble to comply.
- **Tension:** Does this cost hit the US economy where trade is densest (geographic channel), or does it hit everywhere because firms standardize (firm-level channel)?
- **Resolution:** A precise null on the geographic channel.
- **Implications:** Future EU acts (AI, DMA) will likely have diffuse, national impacts on the US labor market that are invisible to regional trade-exposure metrics.

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong. It turns a "null result" into a "mechanistic discovery."

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
At a dinner party, I’d lead with: *"The GDPR didn't just affect Europe; it coincided with a 7% drop in US tech hiring, but surprisingly, it hit Mississippi just as hard as Connecticut, regardless of who actually trades with the EU."*
- **Reaction:** People lean in on the 7% but might skeptically ask, *"Is that just the tech bubble or secular trends?"*
- **Follow-up:** *"How do you know it was GDPR if it didn't track with trade exposure?"* This is the paper's weakest point—the reliance on the national DD to suggest a GDPR effect while the DDD shows no geographic variation.

---

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
- **Front-loading:** The distinction between merchandise exports and digital exposure (page 11) is crucial and should be moved into the **Introduction** or **Institutional Background**. It justifies why the null matters.
- **Appendix:** The "3-digit NAICS placebos" are actually very strong evidence and should be moved from the appendix to the main **Results** section.
- **Conclusion:** It is currently a bit repetitive. It should lean harder into the policy implications for the **EU AI Act**.

---

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
**The Gap:** Currently, the paper finds a national decline that it *suggests* is GDPR, and a geographic null it *proves* is robust. The 7.7% national decline lacks a "smoking gun" connecting it to GDPR. 

**Ambition Problem:** The paper uses "merchandise exports" as the geographic treatment. In the digital age, this is a "noisy proxy" (as the author admits). 

**Single Biggest Improvement:** The author should supplement the state-level merchandise export data with a measure of **digital service intensity** or **firm-level EU revenue exposure** (perhaps from Compustat or website traffic data) to see if the "geographic null" persists when using a more relevant digital-exposure metric. If the null holds against a *better* proxy, the "firm-level standardization" story becomes undeniable.
