# Research Ideas

## Idea 1: Does Rent Control Destroy Property Value? Staggered Evidence from France's Encadrement des Loyers
**Policy:** France reintroduced rent ceilings (encadrement des loyers) in a staggered rollout: Paris (July 2019), Lille (March 2020), Plaine Commune (June 2021), Est Ensemble (December 2021), Lyon-Villeurbanne (November 2021), Montpellier (July 2022), Bordeaux (July 2022), Pays Basque (2023), Grenoble (2024+). Each city adopted at a specific date, creating 10+ treated units with different treatment timing.
**Outcome:** Property sale prices from DVF (Demandes de Valeurs Foncières), France's universe of property transactions since 2014. Approximately 5-6 million transactions per year covering every commune in metropolitan France (except Alsace-Moselle). Contains transaction price, property type, surface area, number of rooms, commune code, and cadastral parcel. Data is open-access on data.gouv.fr.
**Identification:** Triple-difference exploiting three margins: (1) controlled vs. uncontrolled cities (staggered DiD using CS-DiD); (2) pre vs. post rent control adoption; (3) investment-type properties (small apartments, studios, 1-2 rooms in central areas — high rental probability) vs. owner-occupier-type properties (houses, large apartments — low rental probability). The third difference nets out any city-level shock that affects all property types equally. The theory is sharp: rent control reduces expected future rental income, lowering the net present value of investment properties, while owner-occupiers who plan to live in the property are unaffected. Additional mechanism tests include: (a) comparing price effects across property sizes within apartments (studios most affected), (b) heterogeneity by neighborhood rent tension (binding vs. non-binding ceilings), (c) transaction volume effects (if investors exit, fewer investment-type sales). Built-in placebos: commercial properties in controlled cities (not covered by residential rent control); properties in surrounding suburbs just outside the regulatory perimeter; owner-occupied houses in controlled cities.
**Why it's novel:** Morin et al. (2025, JHE) study the effect of Paris rent control on RENTS, finding 5.2% moderation. We study the effect on property SALE PRICES — the capitalization channel. Autor, Palmer, Pathak (2014, JPE) studied property price effects of rent DECONTROL in Boston; we study the mirror image (introduction). Diamond et al. (2019, JPE) showed San Francisco rent control reduced rental supply; we test whether French rent control is priced into property markets. The staggered multi-city design with 10+ adoption events is uniquely French and provides statistical power that single-city studies cannot.
**Feasibility check:** DVF is confirmed available on data.gouv.fr (bulk CSV, ~5GB/year). Treatment dates confirmed for all major cities. Pre-periods: 5 years for Paris (2014-2019), up to 8 years for later adopters. Post-periods: up to 6 years for Paris (2019-2025). Control cities: all French cities 100k+ population without rent control. Property type classification feasible from DVF fields (type_local, nombre_pieces_principales). N > 20 million transactions. Excludes Alsace-Moselle (different land registry system — not a problem, no rent-controlled cities there).

## Idea 2: Unshackling Growth? France's Loi Pacte and the 50-Employee Regulatory Threshold
**Policy:** France's Loi Pacte (May 2019, effective January 2020) raised many regulatory thresholds from 50 to 250 employees and introduced a 5-year grace period for firms crossing any threshold. Before 2020, crossing 50 employees immediately triggered mandatory profit-sharing, works councils, union representation, and other obligations — the famous "50-employee curse" documented by Garicano, Lelarge, and Van Reenen (2016, AER). The reform was designed to eliminate the bunching at 49 employees that their paper documented.
**Outcome:** ACOSS/URSSAF open data on establishment counts by size bracket (1-4, 5-9, 10-19, 20-49, 50-99, 100-249, 250+), by département and major sector, annually since 2006. Available on open.urssaf.fr and data.gouv.fr. This gives the distribution of firms around the critical thresholds for 14 pre-reform years and 5+ post-reform years across 96 départements.
**Identification:** Difference-in-discontinuities. Compare the dynamics at the 49/50 boundary (treated: obligations moved to 250, 5-year grace added) against the 99/100 boundary (control: no regulatory threshold moved). The outcome is the ratio of establishments in the 50-99 bracket to those in the 20-49 bracket — a measure of "flow through" the former barrier. If the threshold was binding, this ratio should increase post-reform at the 49/50 boundary but not at control boundaries. Additional tests: sector heterogeneity (sectors with more binding obligations at 50 should show larger effects); 249/250 boundary as a second test (obligations moved TO this threshold — should show reverse pattern). COVID confound addressed by the diff-in-disc: COVID affects all boundaries equally, so the difference removes the common shock.
**Why it's novel:** Garicano et al. (2016, AER) documented the bunching problem but could not test causality — correlation between regulation and bunching doesn't prove the regulation caused the bunching. The Loi Pacte is the natural "reverse experiment": if removing the regulatory bite at 50 causes de-bunching, we have causal evidence that regulation was the binding constraint. Recent work by Carbonnier (2022, AMSE WP) finds under-reporting at the threshold; we test whether the reform affects actual employment growth, not just reporting.
**Feasibility check:** ACOSS data confirmed available (open.urssaf.fr, updated annually). 2006-2024 panel: 18 years × 96 départements × 6 sectors × 7+ size brackets. Size brackets exactly straddle the 50-employee threshold (20-49 vs 50-99). Pre-period: 13 years (2006-2018). Post-period: 5 years (2020-2024, excluding 2019 as transition year). Control boundary: 99/100 (within same dataset). Low RAM compatible: aggregate panel, small file size.

## Idea 3: The Price of Exclusion — Do Housing Quotas Break Residential Segregation? Evidence from France's Loi SRU
**Policy:** France's Loi SRU (2000, strengthened 2013) requires municipalities with 3,500+ inhabitants (1,500+ in Île-de-France) in urban areas of 50,000+ to maintain at least 25% social housing. Non-compliant municipalities pay annual penalties proportional to their deficit. The 2013 Loi Duflot quintupled maximum penalties for repeat offenders and raised the target from 20% to 25%. Municipalities between 20-25% social housing went from compliant to non-compliant overnight.
**Outcome:** DVF property transactions (2014-2024) for private housing prices around social housing construction sites. RPLS (Répertoire des Logements Sociaux) for social housing inventory. SRU bilan triennal published by the Ministry of Housing listing each municipality's compliance status and construction.
**Identification:** Dose-response DiD using the 2013 penalty reform. Treatment intensity = municipality's gap below 25% target × population (determines penalty amount). Control: municipalities already above 25%. Pre-period: limited (DVF starts 2014, reform in 2013). Mechanism: test whether municipalities actually built more social housing (compliance channel) and whether private property prices responded (capitalization channel). Built-in placebo: municipalities comfortably above the 25% threshold should show no change in construction or property prices.
**Why it's novel:** Gobillon and Vignolles (2016, JUE) evaluate the original 2000 SRU law's effect on social housing construction. Nobody has studied the 2013 penalty quintupling and its effect on both compliance AND property markets. The "price of exclusion" framing — what wealthy communes pay to remain exclusive — is a novel conceptual object.
**Feasibility check:** DVF available but pre-period is very thin (reform January 2013, DVF starts April 2014 — at most a few months of clean pre-treatment). SRU bilan data likely available on data.gouv.fr but machine-readable format uncertain. This is the riskiest of the three ideas due to data timing. The 2017 SRU extension (which brought NEW municipalities under coverage) could provide a cleaner natural experiment with adequate pre-period, but requires verification of which municipalities were newly covered.
