## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
The paper investigates how government-imposed price ceilings on housing subsidies (France’s PTZ) act as focal points for developers. Using a massive transaction dataset, it shows that developers bunch prices exactly at these caps to capture the subsidy as revenue, and it uses a 2024 policy reclassification to prove this bunching "migrates" when the caps move. Economists should care because it provides a precise mechanism for why homeownership subsidies often fail to lower costs for buyers, instead padding producer margins.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this well, but the first two paragraphs are a bit "technical-first." 
**The pitch it should have:** 
"Does capping the price of subsidized homes protect buyers or provide a target for sellers? This paper documents a 'subsidy cap trap' in the French housing market, where developers strategically price new builds exactly at the eligibility ceiling. By exploiting a sudden geographic reclassification of these ceilings, I show that these price points are not driven by market fundamentals but by strategic capture, shifting the benefit of the subsidy from the household to the firm."

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**Contribution:** This paper provides the first causal evidence of "difference-in-bunching" migration at housing subsidy price ceilings to document developer-side capture.

- **Differentiation:** It is well-differentiated. While the "tax notch" literature (Kleven, Saez) is mature, applying it to third-party (developer) responses in a housing subsidy context is fresh.
- **Framing:** It is currently framed as answering a question about the **WORLD** (incidence of subsidies), which is a strength.
- **Explainability:** A smart economist could explain this in one sentence: "Developers treat the subsidy eligibility cap as a price floor."
- **Bigger Contribution:** To move from "very good" to "AER-level," the paper needs to move beyond documenting the bunching and quantify the **welfare loss or quality degradation.** If a developer prices at the cap, are they reducing the square footage of the apartment to make the margin work? That "quality adjustment" is the hidden cost of the trap.

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
- **Neighbors:** Kleven (2016) on bunching; Best & Kleven (2018) on housing notches; Mian & Sufi (2009) on credit expansion; and Fack (2006) on French housing benefits.
- **Strategy:** It should **synthesize** the bunching literature with the incidence literature. It currently "speaks" to bunching researchers, but it should "attack" the design of affordable housing policies.
- **Missing Conversation:** It could benefit from connecting to the **Industrial Organization (IO)** literature on focal point pricing and menu costs. Why is the bunching so "sticky" and "sharp"?
- **Conversation Fit:** It is having the right conversation, but it needs to broaden from "this happened in France" to "this is a fundamental flaw in threshold-based subsidy design."

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC
- **Setup:** Governments use price caps to ensure subsidies help the poor/middle class.
- **Tension:** These caps create a "notch" where pricing $1 above the cap loses the buyer thousands in subsidy, giving developers massive market power at that specific price point.
- **Resolution:** Developers bunch at the cap; when the government moves the cap, the developers move their prices immediately.
- **Implications:** Subsidies are being captured by supply-side actors; policy should move toward sliding scales rather than hard caps.

**Evaluation:** The narrative is strong. However, the "Resolution" is slightly weakened by the underpowered VEFA-only triple-difference (page 10). The author needs to lean harder into the "migration" as a proof of concept, even if the N is small.

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
- **Lead Fact:** "When the French government raised the price cap for subsidies by €37,000, new-build prices in those towns jumped to the new cap almost instantly, while resale prices didn't budge."
- **Reaction:** Lean in. It’s a clean, intuitive "smoking gun" for corporate capture of public funds.
- **Follow-up:** "Does the buyer get a better house for that higher price, or just the same house at a higher margin?" (This is the question the paper currently struggles to answer).

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The gap between this and a "Top 5" publication is **ambition regarding the mechanism.** 

Currently, the paper proves *that* it happens. An AER paper should try to show *how* it happens. Do developers change the mix of apartments (building more 2-bedroom units to hit the modal cap)? Do they reduce finish quality? 

**Single most impactful piece of advice:** Use the "surface area" data mentioned in the Data Appendix to test if **price per square meter** stays constant or if developers are "down-sizing" units to fit the cap. If you can show that the "Subsidy Cap Trap" leads to smaller homes for the same price, you have a landmark paper on the distortionary effects of housing policy.
