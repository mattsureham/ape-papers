**Idea Fidelity**  
The submitted paper departs substantially from the original idea manifest. The manifest promised a factory-level “Accord vs. Alliance vs. control” triple difference-in-differences using International Accord inspection data, BGMEA factory registers, and export survival, with treatment assignment driven by pre-2013 buyer nationality. The paper instead analyzes destination-level bilateral exports (Bangladesh to EU vs. US vs. other partners) using COMTRADE data and a destination-product-time triple diff. None of the factory-level outcomes (remediation completion, audit compliance, export survival), data sources (Accord inspection database, BGMEA registry), or the factory-level identification strategy described in the manifest are present. As a result, the paper studies a different unit of observation (country pairs rather than factories), a different causal channel (destination/regime instead of factory-level enforcement), and has an entirely different empirical approach. The overall research question—"does binding versus voluntary private governance affect factory-level safety compliance and worker outcomes?"—is not the question the paper answers. Therefore the manuscript fails to follow the manifest’s core plan.

**Summary**  
The paper documents that, following Rana Plaza, Bangladesh apparel exports to Accord destinations (EU countries) remained stable while exports to Alliance destinations (the United States) fell significantly, using a destination-product-time triple difference-in-differences specification. The author interprets this as evidence that the binding Accord commitment sustained sourcing relationships whereas the voluntary Alliance did not, arguing that the asymmetry provides a revealed-preference estimate of the value of enforcement commitment in private governance. Robustness checks (alternative post periods, excluding competitor countries, non-apparel placebo) support the negative Alliance coefficient, but pre-trend tests show Alliance destinations were already experiencing a downward trend before 2013.

**Essential Points**

1. **Unit of analysis and identification mismatch.** The key claims in the introduction and discussion are about factory-level governance and enforcement—the paper repeatedly references factory remediation, compliance, and survival. Yet the empirical strategy operates at the destination-country level using bilateral export data. The policy mechanism proposed in the manifest (Accord vs. Alliance factory certification driven by buyers) is therefore not identified. The current specification implicitly assumes that all exports to EU countries are governed by Accord signatories and all US exports by Alliance signatories, but export destination is a very noisy proxy for factory-level governance. Without factory-level data linking exporters to regimes, the paper cannot credibly attribute observed export dynamics to the enforcement regime rather than to other destination-specific shocks or sourcing policies. The authors must either obtain factory-level data as promised or recast their claims to match what can be identified with the destination-level triple diff, including a transparent discussion of the correspondence between destinations and governance regimes.

2. **Threats to causal identification from destination heterogeneity.** The triple difference relies on the assumption that EU and US destinations would have followed parallel apparel trends relative to control destinations absent Rana Plaza. However, EU and US demand structures, trade policies (e.g., duty-free schemes beyond EBA), and macroeconomic exposure differ substantially—moreover, the paper’s own event study shows a significant Alliance pre-trend (Alliance apparel exports were already declining relative to control before 2013). This undermines causal interpretation because the Alliance coefficient may largely capture pre-existing sourcing shifts unrelated to private governance enforcement. The manuscript acknowledges this but then proceeds to interpret the coefficient as evidence of voluntary governance failure. A more credible strategy would require additional controls or alternative specifications (e.g., matching on pre-trends, synthetic control for the US destination, or instrumenting regime assignment) to isolate the governance effect. Without that, the causal story remains weak and the headline conclusion (“binding enforcement prevented collapse, whereas voluntarism allowed exit”) is not fully supported.

3. **Overstated inference about Accord effectiveness.** The null result for Accord in the triple diff is interpreted as “binding governance preserved trade.” However, this inference is tenuous because the Accord coefficient is statistically insignificant and the point estimate is positive but small, while basic partner-level specifications yield mixed signs depending on fixed effects. Without a clear positive effect or placebo demonstrating that EU exports would have declined in the absence of the Accord, the claim that the Accord “created stable sourcing relationships” is not established. The analysis would benefit from direct tests (e.g., comparing Accord destinations to a synthetic control composed of similar high-trade partners without Accord brands) and from discussing alternative explanations for EU stability, such as tariff preferences, stronger buyer diversification strategies, or simultaneous policy responses.

**Suggestions**

1. **Reframe the empirical strategy to match the data or acquire the promised factory-level data.** If factory-level Accord/Alliance assignment and remediation data exist (as described in the manifest), the empirical strategy should return to that design: a factory-level triple difference with factory fixed effects, treatment defined by European vs. North American buyer composition, and outcomes such as remediation completion and survival. If that data is not available, the paper should clearly state that it studies destination-level export dynamics as a proxy and adjust the framing accordingly. In either case, include a detailed discussion (with tables or figures) showing how destination-country classification maps onto regime participation—e.g., share of Accord/Alliance factories exporting to each destination, buyer headquarters distribution, or brand-level sourcing data. Without this, the regime designation remains an unverified assumption.

2. **Strengthen the triple diff by addressing pre-trends and alternative counterfactuals.** The pre-trend issue is central; the Alliance coefficient likely captures existing US trade contraction. Consider estimating the model using a pre-trend-adjusted sample (e.g., de-meaned by linear trend), introducing partner-specific linear time trends, or constructing a synthetic counterfactual for the US using a weighted combination of other partners. Alternatively, focus on nearer-term deviations by exploiting higher-frequency data (if available) or performing a discontinuity around 2013 for Accord countries. Additionally, explore whether other high-income destinations (Canada, Australia, Japan) experienced similar declines—to the extent they did not, that would strengthen the governance interpretation. Provide a deeper discussion of the pre-existing “China Plus One” diversification and how it interacts with governance regimes.

3. **Include more direct tests of the mechanism.** The policy story hinges on binding commitment preserving sourcing relationships by forcing brands to stay. The current analysis merely shows differential exports by destination. To bolster the mechanism, consider incorporating brand-level or factory-level data on order retention, remediation progress, or buyer responses. If such data remain unavailable, use indirect proxies: for example, compare the export dynamics of Accord countries to Accord-brand-only merchandise or examine whether Accord exports were concentrated in Accord-certified factories (by matching industry-level trade with certification lists). Another approach is to exploit the dissolution of the Alliance in 2018—if Alliance exports accelerated after disbandment because brands had already left, that would help disentangle governance from secular trends.

4. **Clarify the control group and product definitions.** Classifying 155 diverse countries as a single control regime risks averaging over heterogeneous trajectories. Some of these countries may have had their own private governance agreements or situated in shifting trade networks. Consider breaking down the control group (e.g., high-income vs. low-income, regional clusters) or re-weighting by pre-2013 export shares to ensure the triple diff is not driven by extreme observations. Likewise, the choice of non-apparel control products (fish, cotton, footwear) is reasonable but limited. Justify why these products are unaffected by the governance regimes and show that results are robust to including other industries (e.g., leather goods). Providing additional tables or figures showing the raw time paths for apparel and non-apparel exports across regimes would help readers assess the plausibility of the identifying assumption.

5. **Revisit policy claims in light of the empirical scope.** The concluding policy statements imply that the findings generalize to current due-diligence debates and that binding private governance “outperforms” voluntarism. Given the data limitations and the destination-level unit of analysis, temper these claims: emphasize that the paper documents a correlation between destination regime and export stability, not a definitive causal mechanism. If the factory-level governance data is eventually used, tie the conclusions back to that level. Otherwise, acknowledge that factors beyond enforcement architecture (e.g., trade preferences, brand sourcing strategies) may explain the observed pattern and that extending the analysis to other contexts would be needed to generalize.

6. **Improve transparency of data construction and robustness.** The appendix provides helpful data provenance, but the main text should include more explicit description of how the destination regime classification was operationalized. Provide the raw export shares by regime and year, and consider including a map or figure showing how Eure destinations and the US compare over time. For robustness, present regressions with alternative clustering (e.g., two-way clustering by partner and product) or standard errors addressing serial correlation given the panel nature. Also, explain why 2014 is excluded (reporting gap) and discuss whether the results are sensitive to including imputed values for that year.

By aligning the empirical strategy with available data, thoroughly interrogating the pre-trend and control-group assumptions, and clarifying the mechanism, the paper can make a stronger and more credible contribution to the literature on private governance and supply chain regulation.
