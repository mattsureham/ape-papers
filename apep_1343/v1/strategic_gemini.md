## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether the design of private supply-chain regulation—specifically, legally binding commitments versus voluntary self-regulation—matters for trade resilience after a catastrophic industrial accident. Using the 2013 Rana Plaza collapse as a natural experiment, the author compares the European-led "Accord" (binding) with the North American "Alliance" (voluntary) and finds that while raw trade data suggests the binding regime "saved" exports, the effect is entirely explained by pre-existing sourcing trends.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this pitch quite well in the first paragraph. However, it currently leads with the tragedy of Rana Plaza rather than the economic puzzle of contract design. 

**Revised Pitch:**
*Economic theory suggests that binding contracts should outperform voluntary arrangements when the temptation to exit is high. Following the Rana Plaza disaster, the global apparel industry split into two competing governance experiments: one legally binding and one voluntary. This paper shows that while these regimes appear to have divergent effects on trade flows, these differences disappear once pre-existing sourcing trajectories are accounted for, suggesting that aggregate trade data is too coarse a tool to evaluate the efficacy of private governance.*

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:** The paper provides a well-identified null result showing that destination-level trade flows cannot distinguish between binding and voluntary private governance regimes due to dominant pre-existing sourcing trends.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It is well-differentiated from Bossavie et al. (2023) by adding the destination-regime variation.
*   **Framing:** It is framed as a question about the world (Do governance designs matter?) but concludes as a methodological caution (Trade data is too coarse).
*   **Clarity:** A smart economist would say: "It's a paper that debunked the 'Accord' advantage by showing it was just a pre-trend."
*   **Making it bigger:** To make this an AER-level contribution, the author needs to go beyond "this data doesn't work." They need to provide evidence of *why* the US was diversifying away (the "China Plus One" mentioned on page 9) and perhaps use a second dataset (e.g., firm-level or factory-level) to show where the "missing" trade went.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

*   **Neighbors:** Locke (2013), Distelhorst et al. (2017), Bossavie et al. (2023), and the Hart & Moore (1988) theory of contracts.
*   **Positioning:** Currently, it builds on the literature but ends by throwing cold water on it. It should position itself more aggressively as a "critique of aggregate evaluations" in supply chain literature.
*   **Missing Conversations:** The paper would benefit from connecting to the **Trade and Institutions** literature (e.g., Levchenko, 2007) regarding how institutional quality (or private substitutes for it) determines comparative advantage.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

*   **Setup:** Global outrage after Rana Plaza leads to two different institutional fixes.
*   **Tension:** Does the "binding" nature of the Accord actually keep brands in the country, or is it just cheap talk?
*   **Resolution:** At first glance, it works. Upon closer inspection (the "twist"), it’s all pre-trends. The US was leaving anyway; the EU was staying anyway.
*   **Implications:** Don't use Comtrade data to judge these regimes; we need more granular data.

**Evaluation:** The narrative arc is very strong. It follows a "sleuth" structure—uncovering a result and then debunking it. This is a classic AER style for a "Comment" or a short "Article."

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

**The Dinner Party Fact:** "Did you know that the famous 'Accord' that supposedly saved Bangladesh's garment industry after Rana Plaza actually had no measurable effect on trade once you look at the fact that the US was already quitting Bangladesh years before the fire?"

**Reaction:** People will lean in. It challenges a "feel-good" narrative in ESG and international development.

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

*   **Front-loading:** The paper is well-structured. The "reveal" of the null result in the abstract and intro is honest and keeps the reader from feeling cheated.
*   **Visuals:** The paper needs an Event Study plot. Table 3 is hard to digest. A chart showing the divergent trends of the EU vs. US *before* 2013 would be the "money shot."
*   **Appendix:** The standardized effect sizes (Table 5) are a bit overkill for the main text; move them to the appendix.

---

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The gap is **Ambition**. Currently, the paper is a "Corrective Note." To be a full AER article, it needs to solve the problem it identifies. 

**Single Biggest Improvement:** Use the Comtrade data to show where the US diverted its trade (e.g., Vietnam, Ethiopia) and show that this diversion was *not* happening for Accord-signatory countries. If the author can show that "Voluntary" regimes allow for "flight" to specific lower-cost competitors while "Binding" regimes create "stickiness" at the country level (even if the pre-trend makes the point estimate fuzzy), the paper becomes a major statement on the **political economy of sourcing**.
