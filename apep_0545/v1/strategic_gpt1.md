## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether media attention to regulatory problems and regulatory burden changes the pace of federal rulemaking. Using agency-quarter data from 2015–2024, it argues that negative media coverage of regulatory burden is associated with **more**, not less, significant rulemaking—except during the Trump administration’s EO 13771 period, when that relationship turns negative.

A busy economist should care because the paper is trying to say something broader than rule counts: whether information flowing through the media pushes the administrative state toward more regulation, less regulation, or simply different forms of political mobilization. If true, that bears on classic questions in political economy about salience, interest-group organization, and executive control of bureaucracy.

### Does the paper articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Not quite. The opening is reasonably lively, but it spends too much time on the Trump executive order before clearly stating the paper’s central empirical surprise. The reader does not get the sharpest version of the question soon enough: **when media coverage emphasizes regulatory costs, does that actually generate deregulation—or does it instead stimulate more rulemaking?**

The first two paragraphs should do less scene-setting and more economic positioning. Right now the introduction opens with a policy episode and then gestures at “the regulatory ratchet.” What it should do is immediately establish the expected benchmark, the empirical puzzle, and the broader implication.

### The pitch the paper should have

> Regulation is often thought to ratchet upward because salient accidents generate demands for new rules, while complaints about regulatory burden generate too little pressure for repeal. This paper asks whether that logic is visible in actual federal rulemaking: when media coverage emphasizes safety incidents or regulatory burden in a sector, do agencies issue more or fewer important rules?
>
> Using a panel of major federal agencies from 2015–2024, we find the opposite of the simple deregulatory story. Negative sector-specific “burden” coverage predicts **more** significant rulemaking in normal periods, but this relationship turns negative during the Trump administration’s EO 13771 regime. The broader implication is that media attention to regulatory costs may usually mobilize organized stakeholders into the rulemaking process rather than shrink the administrative state—unless executive institutions force agencies to convert that attention into deregulation.

That is the AER-facing story. It is cleaner, more world-facing, and gets to the paradox immediately.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper’s contribution is to show that sector-specific negative media coverage of regulatory burden predicts **more** federal rulemaking across agencies in normal periods, with that relationship reversing only under a strong executive deregulatory mandate.

### Is this clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?

Only partially. The paper cites broad literatures—media effects, regulation, administrative law, executive control—but does not sharply distinguish itself from the closest empirical neighbors. The contribution currently risks sounding like: “we apply media-salience logic to rulemaking with GDELT data.” That is not enough. The distinctive claim is not the data source; it is the **sign reversal** relative to naive theory and its conditionality on executive institutions.

The paper should be much more explicit that it is not merely showing “media matters for regulation,” but rather:

1. incident salience does **not** robustly predict more significant rules;
2. burden salience predicts the **opposite** of conventional deregulation logic;
3. executive directives can flip the sign.

That triptych is the real contribution.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a gap in a literature?

It is mixed, but too often framed as filling a literature gap (“first systematic panel evidence...”). That is weaker. The stronger framing is world-facing:

- How does the administrative state respond to media pressure?
- Why is deregulation hard even when regulatory costs become salient?
- When can executive power reverse ordinary ratchet dynamics?

Those are real-world political economy questions. The paper should lean into them.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?

Not cleanly. Right now they might say: “It’s a panel paper linking GDELT tone to agency rulemaking, with a Trump interaction.” That is not enough. They should instead be able to say:

> “It shows that media complaints about regulatory burden usually correlate with more rulemaking, not less, suggesting stakeholder mobilization through the administrative process, and only a strong executive constraint flips that relationship.”

That is memorable. The paper currently contains that idea, but it is not sufficiently front-and-center.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Several possibilities:

1. **Move from rule counts to substantive content.**  
   The biggest vulnerability in strategic terms is that “more rulemaking” can mean many things. If the paper could distinguish between rules that increase burdens, rules that revise existing burdens, deregulatory actions, procedural rules, and economically meaningful actions, the contribution becomes much larger. Right now “counts” are a somewhat blunt endpoint.

2. **Directly observe the proposed mechanism.**  
   If the story is industry mobilization through comments, then comment volume, commenter composition, petitions, trade-association activity, or OIRA meetings would dramatically enlarge the paper. Without that, the mechanism is suggestive rather than field-defining.

3. **Make the comparison more structural.**  
   The paper could compare sectors where organized regulated interests are especially strong versus sectors where diffuse consumer/safety interests are stronger. That would turn a broad pattern into a sharper political economy test.

4. **Reframe around executive control of bureaucracy.**  
   The most AER-relevant version may not be “media and rulemaking” but “how executive institutions mediate media-driven pressure on bureaucracies.” The Trump reversal is the most distinctive empirical feature; the paper should treat it not as one heterogeneity table, but as the central institutional lever.

If the author could only enlarge one margin, it would be mechanism via comment-process mobilization or another direct administrative channel.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

The world before this paper: regulation is often viewed as sticky and expansionary; salient harms are thought to produce new rules, while complaints about compliance costs are thought to create pressure for deregulation. Media coverage is a plausible source of salience, but we know much less about how media attention feeds into administrative rulemaking.

### Tension

The puzzle is that two intuitive media channels imply opposite predictions: incident coverage should push agencies toward more regulation, while burden coverage should push toward less. But it is not clear which channel dominates in practice, and the administrative process may transform media pressure in unexpected ways.

### Resolution

The paper finds that burden coverage is positively associated with significant rulemaking in ordinary periods, while incident coverage does little or even predicts less proposed rulemaking. During the Trump/EO 13771 period, the burden relationship flips negative.

### Implications

The implication is that media pressure about regulatory costs does not naturally produce deregulation. Instead, it may activate organized participation in the administrative process, increasing rulemaking unless executive institutions force agencies onto a deregulatory path.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the ingredients, but the arc is not fully disciplined. Right now it sometimes reads like a collection of regressions organized around an appealing phrase (“media ratchet”) rather than a clean story. The strongest story is:

1. There are two kinds of media salience.
2. Conventional intuition predicts opposite responses.
3. The burden response goes the “wrong” way.
4. That wrong-way response disappears under an unusually strong executive mandate.
5. Therefore, the translation from public information to administrative action is mediated by organized interests and institutional control.

That should be the spine of the paper. Every section should serve it.

The weakest parts of the narrative are where the paper overstates mechanism without observing it, and where it drifts into broad claims about “the regulatory ratchet” generally. It needs more restraint and more discipline.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?

I would lead with:

> When news coverage emphasizes regulatory burden in a sector, agencies usually issue more—not fewer—significant rules; only under Trump’s two-for-one order does that relationship reverse.

That is the memorable fact.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

Economists would lean in, at least initially, because the sign is surprising. The Trump reversal adds a second hook. There is a real dinner-party fact here.

But they would quickly ask whether “burden coverage” is really burden coverage, and whether “more rulemaking” means more regulation, more procedural churn, or more revisions. Those are strategic vulnerabilities, not just identification ones.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Most likely:

1. “Are you sure your burden variable isn’t just picking up bad news in the sector?”
2. “Does more rulemaking mean more restrictive regulation, or just more administrative activity?”
3. “Can you show the comment-process mechanism directly?”
4. “Why is Trump the only period where this flips—was it EO 13771 specifically or just administration-wide selection?”

Those are exactly the questions the paper should anticipate in framing.

### If findings are modest or null

The null on incident coverage is potentially interesting, but the paper does not yet make the strongest case for why. A finding that salient accidents do not translate into more major rulemaking is worth knowing, but only if framed as evidence about the mismatch between public salience and administrative procedure. Right now it reads more like “we expected something and did not find it.” That is not enough.

The positive burden result is the real star; the null incident result is supporting cast.
