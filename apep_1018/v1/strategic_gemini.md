## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
This paper asks why firms comply with mandatory reporting laws when the probability of detection for non-compliance is low. Using a decade of OSHA severe injury data, it finds that an employer's propensity to report an injury co-moves strongly with reporting by other firms in the same state, a phenomenon the author calls a "compliance shadow" cast by state-level regulatory attention shocks.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this pitch reasonably well by page 2, but the first two paragraphs are a bit bogged down in institutional detail. 
*   **The Pitch it should have:** "Mandatory self-reporting is the bedrock of modern regulation, yet in settings like workplace safety, over 50% of incidents go unreported. This paper exploits a new 'universe' of severe injury data to show that reporting is not an independent firm-level decision but is driven by state-level 'compliance shadows'—temporary surges in reporting triggered by spikes in local regulatory salience. These results suggest that the deterrent effect of a single OSHA action is significantly larger than previously measured, as it ripples across unrelated industries within the same geographic jurisdiction."

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**Contribution:** The paper documents that regulatory compliance spillovers are driven by geographic "salience" shocks rather than industry-specific information contagion.

*   **Differentiation:** It moves beyond Johnson (2020) and Levine (2012) by focusing on the **extensive margin** of reporting itself, rather than just the reduction of injuries.
*   **World vs. Literature:** It frames itself as answering a question about the WORLD (why do firms report?), which is a strength. 
*   **Smart Economist Test:** A reader would say, "It’s a paper showing that when OSHA gets loud in a state, everyone starts reporting their accidents, even if they aren't in the industry being targeted."
*   **Making it bigger:** To truly clear the AER bar, the author needs to pin down the **exact nature of the shock**. "Regulatory attention" is a bit of a black box. Linking the SIR data to local news coverage of OSHA or changes in state-level budgets/leadership would turn a "co-movement" paper into a "mechanism" paper.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
*   **Closest Neighbors:** Johnson (2020, AER) on shaming; Scholz and Wei (1997, APSR) on federalist enforcement; Manski (1993) on peer effects.
*   **Positioning:** The paper correctly attempts to pivot away from "peer effects" (Manski) once the placebo tests fail, which is intellectually honest. 
*   **Missing Conversations:** The paper should speak more to the **Economics of Attention** (Gabaix, etc.). If firms have limited bandwidth, they only comply when the regulator is "top of mind."
*   **Right Conversation?** It currently sits in Labor/Public. It might find more "alpha" by connecting to the **Accounting/Tax Compliance** literature, where the "reporting vs. reality" distinction is the central tension.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC
*   **Setup:** Regulatory agencies rely on self-reporting, but firms hide bad news.
*   **Tension:** Does a firm's decision to "come clean" depend on what its peers are doing? 
*   **Resolution:** Yes, but surprisingly, it’s about *geography*, not *industry*. A construction accident in Miami makes a hospital in Miami more likely to report an injury, but doesn't affect a construction site in Atlanta.
*   **Implications:** OSHA should focus on "high-visibility" enforcement to maximize these geographic spillovers.

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong. The "twist" in Section 5.3 (that the placebo fails, leading to the "compliance shadow" theory) is the most compelling part of the paper.

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
*   **Dinner Party Fact:** "When an employer in your city gets busted by OSHA, you're 10% more likely to report your own accidents next month, even if you're in a completely different business."
*   **Reaction:** Lean in. It suggests a "scared straight" effect that is local and visceral.
*   **Follow-up:** "How do the firms even know? Is it in the local paper, or do the managers all talk at the same bars?" (The paper needs to answer this).

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
*   **Front-loading:** The "Identification Tests" in 5.3 are actually the core of the story. They should be moved earlier or integrated into the main results to prevent the reader from thinking this is a standard (and potentially flawed) peer-effects paper.
*   **Appendix:** Table 6 (Standardized Effect Sizes) is a bit "meta." Move the core intuition of those magnitudes into the Discussion.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The paper is currently a very high-quality "fact-finding" mission. To make it an AER paper, it needs **Ambition**. 

**Single most impactful advice:** Find a source of exogenous variation for the "regulatory shock." Currently, the paper uses "peer reporting" as a proxy for a shock. If the author could find a truly exogenous shock—like a specific budget windfall in certain OSHA area offices or a randomized enforcement pilot—the "Compliance Shadow" would move from a correlation to a fundamental law of regulatory economics.
