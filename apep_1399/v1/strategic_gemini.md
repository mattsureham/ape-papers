## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
This paper evaluates the impact of Radon-Resistant New Construction (RRNC) building codes on cancer mortality using a staggered difference-in-differences design. While radon is the second leading cause of lung cancer, the study finds a precisely estimated null effect, which the author attributes to the biological latency of cancer and slow housing stock turnover. It serves as a cautionary tale for the evaluation of long-horizon health policies.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this clearly, but it is too "defensive" about the null in the first two paragraphs. It reads like an apology for why the result isn't there.
**Better Pitch:** "Public health regulations often target diseases with decadal latency periods, yet the empirical literature focuses almost exclusively on contemporaneous or short-run outcomes. Using the staggered adoption of radon building codes, I show that even for a major carcinogen, standard policy evaluation windows fail to capture health benefits. This paper establishes the 'temporal mismatch' in regulatory evaluation, providing a benchmark for the time horizons required to realize returns on infrastructure-based health interventions."

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**Contribution:** The paper provides the first quasi-experimental estimate of the population health impacts of residential radon building codes, documenting that such benefits are undetectable within the first two decades of adoption.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It is well-differentiated from the engineering/medical literature (which focuses on dose-response) and the building code literature (which focuses on costs or energy, e.g., Ito 2014; Levitt & Syverson 2006).
*   **Framing:** Currently, it's framed as a "gap in the literature" about radon. It needs to be framed as a question about **Policy Evaluation Time Horizons.**
*   **The "DiD" Trap:** In its current form, a smart economist might dismiss it as "another DiD paper with a null."
*   **Bigger Contribution:** To make this AER-worthy, the author needs to focus on the **cost-benefit mismatch**. They should use their null and the MDEs to calculate a "break-even" latency period. At what year of code-existence does the NPV of the $500 construction cost become positive? That turns a null result into a prospective policy tool.

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
*   **Neighbors:** Chay & Greenstone (2003) on infant mortality; Deryugina et al. (2019) on elderly mortality; Almond et al. (2009) on radioactive exposure.
*   **Strategy:** It shouldn't "attack." It should **complement** the "short-run" health literature by serving as the "long-run" counterpoint.
*   **Unaware of:** The paper should speak more to the **real estate/urban economics** literature regarding housing durability and depreciation. If the house lasts 80 years but the policy is evaluated at 15, the paper is actually about the *option value* of healthy infrastructure.
*   **The Right Conversation:** The author is currently talking to environmental health economists. They should also be talking to **public finance** economists about the discounting of future health benefits in regulatory impact analyses.

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC
*   **Setup:** Radon is a massive, geologically determined killer.
*   **Tension:** We spend millions on building codes, but do they actually move the needle on mortality?
*   **Resolution:** Within a 20-year window, they do not.
*   **Implications:** Policy evaluation cycles (and tenure clocks) are too short for infrastructure-based health interventions.

**Evaluation:** The arc is "serviceable" but lacks a climax because the results are flat. The "resolution" needs to be shifted from "we found nothing" to "we found that the necessary window for evaluation is X, which current datasets Y cannot yet provide."

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
*   **Dinner Party Fact:** "We mandate $500 radon kits on every new house in 11 states, but 20 years later, it hasn't saved a single detectable life."
*   **Reaction:** Lean in—everyone hates building costs. But then the follow-up is: "Well, obviously, because cancer takes 20 years to grow. Why did you write the paper now?"
*   **The Answer:** This is where the paper needs to be bulletproof. The "so what" isn't the null; the "so what" is the **quantified power failure of standard empirical windows.**

---

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
*   **Front-load the MDE/Back-of-envelope:** Move the power considerations (Section 4.2) and the cost-benefit (5.6) earlier. The reader needs to know *why* the null was expected before they see the tables.
*   **Appendix:** The GRP (geology) construction is thorough but dry; keep it brief in the text.
*   **Conclusion:** It’s currently a summary. It needs to be a "Call to Action" for researchers to use the "first stage" (indoor air testing data) as a proxy when mortality is too far away.

---

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The gap is **ambition.** Currently, it's a very competent evaluation of a specific code. For the AER, it needs to be a paper about the **Economics of Latency.**

**Single most impactful advice:** Stop trying to find the mortality effect and instead **model the "First Stage" and the "Projection."** If the author can find data on actual indoor radon levels in treated vs. untreated homes (the "first stage"), they can use the epidemiological dose-response to show that while mortality is a null *today*, the codes are "printing" future life-years that will show up in 2040. This turns a "failed" mortality study into a "successful" policy valuation study.
