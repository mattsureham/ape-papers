**Idea Fidelity**

The paper largely pursues the vision laid out in the manifest: it studies the staggered adoption of Radon-Resistant New Construction (RRNC) codes across U.S. states, layers in geological radon potential, and estimates effects using a difference-in-differences framework augmented by Callaway–Sant’Anna to address treatment-timing heterogeneity. That said, several departures are worth mentioning. First, the manifest proposed exploiting county-level variation in geological radon potential as a “dose” interaction, whereas the paper aggregates geology to the state level (high-GRP share exceeding 50%) and interacts RRNC adoption with this binary indicator. Second, the manifest emphasized lung cancer mortality, ideally at the county level, while the paper uses state-level total cancer mortality because of data constraints. These choices attenuate both the treatment variation and the signal density relative to the original proposal. The paper does engage with the long latency and stock-turnover arguments from the manifest, so the core research question is preserved, albeit at a more aggregated and conservative level of analysis.

**Summary**

The paper investigates whether state-level adoption of RRNC building codes lowered cancer mortality between 1999 and 2017. Exploiting staggered adoption and geological radon potential through both TWFE and Callaway–Sant’Anna estimators, the author finds a precisely estimated null effect on age-adjusted cancer death rates, even after allowing for higher “doses” in high-GRP states. The paper interprets the null as consistent with long biological latency, slow housing turnover, and the limited share of the population living in new, code-compliant homes within the sample period.

**Essential Points**

1. **Outcome choice attenuates identification and stretches interpretation.** Using all-cause cancer mortality dilutes the signal from radon-related lung cancer and introduces substantial attenuation bias (downward bias toward zero), as acknowledged in the text. Because lung cancer accounts for only ~25% of cancer deaths, the estimated treatment effect is likely to be one-quarter of any true effect on lung cancer. This makes it extremely difficult to assess whether the null is due to an ineffective policy or simply an improperly measured outcome. Without a lung-specific outcome, the identification strategy cannot fully test the causal link the paper seeks to investigate. The reviewer recommends that the authors either gain access to restricted-use lung cancer mortality data (preferably at the state or county-year level) or explicitly model the attenuation (e.g., via bounding) in the current setup.

2. **Geological variation is underutilized, raising concerns about dose variation and parallel trends.** The manifest’s key innovation was a “geology × policy” dose interaction, but the paper reduces this to a state-level binary classification (HighGRP share > 0.5) and a simple interaction. This coarse grouping may absorb much of the inherent variation in geological radon potential, especially given that RRNC states are disproportionately high-GRP. As a result, the comparison of high vs. low states might conflate policy response with unconditional differences in radon exposure and health behaviors. The paper does not exploit within-state (county-level) variation, nor does it test the interaction in a continuous, more granular way. This raises questions about whether the identification strategy is genuinely capturing variation in geological risk or just comparing inherently different states. A finer-grained measure (e.g., weighted continuous GRP share interacting with treatment) and presentation of parallel-trend tests by dose group would bolster credibility.

3. **Statistical power constraints need clearer framing and alternative estimands.** The paper candidly performs a power analysis and concludes that the design cannot detect effects smaller than ≈4.5 deaths per 100,000 because treatment affects only new construction and the housing stock turns over slowly. While the calculation is honest, it leaves the reader wondering whether any meaningful learning is possible given these constraints. Relying solely on TWFE point estimates in a setting with a small number of early-treated states exacerbates the risk that noise overwhelms signal. The reviewer suggests that the authors either pivot to estimating intermediate outcomes that respond sooner (e.g., radon test outcomes, construction permits, percentage of homes built after treatment, if available) or at least frame the estimand as a bounds exercise that quantifies how much benefit would be compatible with the observed null. Otherwise, the empirical strategy may be too underpowered to inform the policy debate in the stated timeframe.

If these concerns cannot be addressed satisfactorily, the paper should be reconsidered, as the credibility of the null hinges on measurement precision and dose variation.

**Suggestions**

1. **Improve outcome measurement and clarity on attenuation.** As a first step, strongly consider accessing cause-specific mortality (lung cancer) through restricted-use CDC WONDER or SEER data, even if it means restricting the sample or pooling multiple years at once to maintain confidentiality. If that is infeasible, provide a formal attenuation analysis: derive the implied effect on lung cancer mortality given the observed null on all-cancer mortality, incorporating the fraction of cancer deaths that are lung cancer and plausible treatment effects on lung cancer. This would transform the null into a bound (e.g., “we can rule out reductions greater than X in lung cancer mortality”) rather than an unenlightening zero. Additionally, consider whether alternative outcomes such as radon testing rates, the share of housing stock built after the code, or even building-permit data can be used as intermediary responses.

2. **Exploit within-state heterogeneity in geological radon exposure.** The manifest envisioned a continuous geology × policy interaction at the county level; the current paper stops at the state level. One way to get closer to the original idea is to construct a county-level dataset for high-GRP share and merge it with state-level RRNC adoption, allowing estimation of a county-level DD with treatment intensity given by geological risk. Even if mortality outcomes are only available at the state level, you could aggregate treatment intensity (e.g., proportion of a state’s population living in high-GRP counties) and present more granular variation (e.g., quartiles of GRP share). Provide more diagnostics (event studies, pre-trend tests) separately for high-GRP and low-GRP subsets to demonstrate that parallel trends hold conditional on geology.

3. **Reframe the identification narrative around treatment intensity and dosages.** The manuscript currently treats high-GRP states as a simple indicator. It would strengthen the argument to frame the identification as a continuous “bedrock dose”: policy is the same across states, but the exposure reduction depends on geology. Present specifications where “dose” equals the share of population in high-GRP counties, the average county GRP, or even the average indoor radon measurement if available. Show that the treatment effect scales with these measures (even if imprecisely estimated) to reinforce the plausibility of the mechanism. If the interaction remains insignificant, show confidence intervals for the implied marginal effect per percentage point of high-GRP share to help readers understand the precision on the dose-response slope.

4. **Deal more explicitly with spillovers and SUTVA concerns.** The paper argues that policies in one state cannot affect another because radon is localized. However, high-GRP states also tend to border other high-GRP states in the East and Midwest, and there might be regional awareness campaigns or builder practices that cross borders. The authors should consider spatial clustery standard errors, including neighboring-state controls, or at least show that results are robust when omitting geographically adjacent non-treated states (to rule out spillovers). Additionally, since some states apply codes only to EPA Zone 1 counties, consider using zone-level compliance intensity where possible to ensure that treated and untreated subpopulations within the same state (or adjacent counties) are appropriately accounted for.

5. **Explore intermediate outcomes and first-stage evidence.** The null on mortality is plausible due to latency, but readers will want assurance that the “first stage” (RRNC codes → reduced radon in new homes) is plausible. If indoor radon testing data from the EPA is obtainable by state/year, even if noisy, it could be used to show immediate reductions in measured radon levels or increased mitigation activity in treated states. Alternatively, state-level data on building permits broken down by construction year could reveal how quickly RRNC-compliant housing accumulates. Linking these intermediate outcomes to the code would demonstrate that the policy works mechanically even if health impacts are still distant.

6. **Clarify the role of the placebo outcomes and reinforce power discussion.** The placebo regressions show noisy coefficients, but several are larger in magnitude than the cancer coefficient (e.g., Post-RRNC effect on heart disease). Consider presenting standardized effect sizes or equivalently scaled coefficients to help readers compare. Also, elaborate on why placebo effects (especially if they are sizable) do or do not undermine the validity of the identification. The permutation test and Bacon decomposition are helpful; make sure their implications are clearly interpreted (e.g., “the distribution of permuted estimates peaks near zero, so our estimate is not unusual; thus, we cannot reject the null even if we cannot definitively rule out a modest effect”).

7. **Provide clearer policy takeaway with long-term projection.** The Discussion and Conclusion section can be tightened by linking the empirical findings to simulation-based projections: if the housing stock turnover is 2% annually, what share of homes will be code-compliant in 2040 or 2050? Use the epidemiological dose-response to translate that into expected lung cancer reductions, even if speculative. This will help policymakers understand that the null is a short-run phenomenon and that the policy could still be valuable in the long run. Quantify the implied cost per life-year saved under different horizon assumptions to place the null in a cost-benefit framework.

In summary, the paper tackles an important policy question with a novel idea, but its empirical credibility would be strengthened by tighter outcome measurement, more granular utilization of geological variation, a clearer articulation of treatment intensity, and additional intermediate outcomes or projections to make the null informative rather than inconclusive.
