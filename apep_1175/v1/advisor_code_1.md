**Summary**

The paper evaluates whether the 2010 OxyContin abuse-deterrent reformulation causally affected county-level labor market outcomes through a prescription-to-illicit opioid transition. Using pre-reform OxyContin brand share from ARCOS as a continuous treatment in a staggered DID with county and year fixed effects (and controls for total opioid volume), it finds no negative effects on employment, earnings, separations, or hires; if anything, the estimates are small and positive. Age-group heterogeneity shows similar coefficients for elderly workers, suggesting that the reformulation instrument captures non-opioid economic trends, calling into question its validity for labor outcomes even though it is well-validated for mortality.

**Essential Points**

1. **Identification justification in the context of labor outcomes is below the bar.** The paper correctly notes that the instrument is supply-side and potentially correlated with other economic features, but it stops short of systematically testing or mitigating those concerns. The main null result rests on the assumption that pre-reform OxyContin share is as good as randomly assigned once total opioids per capita are controlled for. Yet the observed positive estimates for all age groups (including elderly workers) raise suspicion that the instrument proxies for other county trajectories. The paper should strengthen this section by (a) providing more evidence on the sources of variation in brand share (e.g., Purdue marketing territories, distributor networks) to support orthogonality to labor shocks, (b) testing whether pre-reform brand share predicts other non-opioid outcomes, or (c) exploiting additional covariates to plausibly rule out confounding. Without such evidence, the core identification argument remains unconvincing.

2. **The dynamic event study is mentioned but not shown, limiting the parallel-trends claim.** The paper states that pre-trend coefficients hover near zero but does not present the corresponding figure or table; instead, it relies on supplementary data “available upon request.” For an IV/In-DD analysis centered on a new outcome, it is essential to display the event study to convince readers that there is no pre-existing differential trend correlated with OxyContin share. Please include the event-study plot with confidence intervals in the main text or appendix and report the actual coefficients and standard errors. The lack of transparency reduces confidence in the exogeneity claim.

3. **Interpretation of the null and instrument “boundary” is interesting but overstated without additional checks.** The paper concludes that the reformulation instrument fails for labor outcomes because the treatment does not affect the workforce or because elderly effects mirror prime-age ones. However, alternative explanations—e.g., measurement error in QWI (missing informal employment) or offsetting supply and demand effects—are not explored. To strengthen the conclusion, the authors should (a) analyze other opioid-sensitive outcomes (e.g., disability claims, Medicaid enrollment) to confirm that the instrument indeed correlates with adverse economic phenomena, (b) assess whether there is heterogeneity by labor force attachment (e.g., counties with higher pre-reform employment rates), or (c) examine placebo outcomes that should not respond to the reformulation if the instrument is valid. Without these additional diagnostics, the claim that the instrument violates the exclusion restriction for labor markets remains plausible but not fully demonstrated.

**Suggestions**

1. **Deepen the instrument narrative.** Section 3.2 lists the theoretical source of variation (marketing territories, distributor deals, prescriber preferences) but lacks empirical support. Consider documenting that OxyContin brand share is only weakly correlated with baseline labor market or demographic controls once total oxycodone per capita is included. A simple regression of pre-reform employment growth on brand share plus controls could help. Additionally, you could exploit Purdue-specific data, if available, or salesforce deployment memos to show that brand share variation principally reflects marketing strategy rather than local economic conditions. Doing so would clarify whether the observed null is due to the instrument failing or because the transition simply had no labor market impact.

2. **Provide the full event-study and placebo analysis.** Include the event study plotted in an appendix figure with confidence intervals. Report the pre-reform coefficients numerically to show parallel trends. Furthermore, presenting placebo tests—e.g., using falsified reformulation dates (2008) or outcomes that should be unaffected (e.g., county-level school enrollment)—would demonstrate whether the instrument picks up general county trends. Such placebos can also help explain why elderly employment correlates with brand share if there are systematic non-opioid drivers.

3. **Explore treatment intensity heterogeneity.** If the instrument fails for the entire sample, it may still deliver insights for subgroups of counties where the pre-reform brand share is less likely to reflect other economic traits. For instance, restricting to counties with similar pre-reform employment trends or similar access to health infrastructure may isolate a subgroup in which the instrument better satisfies the exclusion restriction. Alternatively, interacting the treatment with measures of opioid vulnerability (e.g., baseline overdose death rates) could reveal whether counties more exposed to misuse respond differently. These analyses can demonstrate whether the null is uniform or concentrated in particular contexts, refining the paper’s argument about the instrument boundary.

4. **Clarify the causal estimand and mechanism.** Given that the reformulation was a shock to the composition rather than the level of opioid supply, spell out more clearly what the DID coefficient recovers. Is it the labor market effect of the ratio of OxyContin to other oxycodone? Or is it interpreted as a latent intensity of illicit substitution? Explicitly articulating the causal pathway (e.g., “the instrument shifts the probability that an opioid user switches to heroin”) can guide readers in understanding why the labor effects would be expected, and why their absence is informative.

5. **Discuss statistical power and confidence intervals in more depth.** The paper notes that the employment coefficient is small and statistically insignificant, but the 95% CI spans values that are not necessarily policy-irrelevant. Presenting minimum detectable effects or equivalence test results would help quantify how large an effect the study can rule out. Similarly, for the earnings and flow outcomes, report whether the null can reject effect sizes of policy relevance. This helps readers evaluate whether the null speaks to absence of an effect or limited power.

6. **Link implications back to policy more tightly.** The discussion currently generalizes to the principle that instrument validity may vary across domains. Consider grounding the policy takeaway more specifically: what does the null say about whether reducing prescription opioid supply (versus addressing illicit opioids) is likely to improve employment? If the reformulation primarily affected individuals already detached from the labor force, then interventions targeting earlier-stage misuse (pre-2010) might be more labor-relevant. Exploit the empirical findings to distinguish these mechanisms, thus giving policymakers clearer guidance.

7. **Report first-stage diagnostics even if the instrument is no longer used for 2SLS.** Since the original idea centered on using OxyContin share as an IV for the heroin transition into labor outcomes, it would be informative to report first-stage estimates (heroin deaths or some proxy as the endogenous variable). Even if you ultimately argue the instrument fails the exclusion restriction, presenting the first stage and the implied LATE will help readers assess the plausibility of the causal channel. If you can show that the first stage is strong (as prior work indicates) while the reduced form is null, that reinforces the argument that the instrument isolates the wrong margin for labor consequences.

8. **Strengthen robustness appendix with additional diagnostics.** While the robustness checks included are useful, consider also reporting results with alternative weighting schemes (e.g., inverse-variance), adding a control for lagged employment, and testing sensitivity to excluding certain states heavily involved in the opioid crisis (e.g., Appalachian states). These can help rule out the possibility that a handful of influential counties drive the positive associations in unweighted models.

By incorporating these suggestions, the paper will provide a more compelling and transparent assessment of whether reformulation exposure impacted labor markets and whether the instrument is valid for economic outcomes.
