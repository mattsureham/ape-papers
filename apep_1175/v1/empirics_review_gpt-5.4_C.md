## 1. **Idea Fidelity**

The paper only partially pursues the original idea in the manifest. The manifest proposed an **IV design** in which pre-reform county OxyContin brand share instruments for the **prescription-to-illicit transition intensity**, with a first stage in overdose/heroin substitution outcomes and a second stage on labor market outcomes. The paper instead estimates a **reduced-form continuous-treatment DiD** of labor outcomes on pre-reform OxyContin share interacted with post-2010, and then interprets null/positive reduced-form estimates and age-pattern “placebos” as evidence that the instrument is invalid for labor market inference.

That shift is not illegitimate—in fact it may be the right empirical conclusion—but it is a substantial departure from the original research design. In particular, the paper does **not** show the first stage in this sample, does **not** estimate 2SLS, and therefore does not directly answer the manifest’s stated causal question: what is the effect of reformulation-induced illicit opioid transition on employment and earnings? Instead, it answers a different and narrower question: whether the reduced-form exposure measure predicts labor market deterioration. If that is the intended contribution, the paper should state this much more clearly and stop framing itself as applying “the reformulation instrument” to identify labor-market effects.

A second fidelity issue is data/sample execution. The manifest emphasized near-universe ARCOS/QWI coverage and roughly 3,056 counties; the paper ends with **2,619 counties** and only 43 state clusters. That may be fine, but the attrition is large enough that the paper needs a transparent accounting of what was lost and why. Right now it is hard to know whether sample construction itself is shaping the result.

## 2. **Summary**

This paper studies whether counties more exposed to the 2010 OxyContin abuse-deterrent reformulation experienced weaker labor market outcomes after 2010. Using county-level ARCOS-based pre-reform OxyContin brand share interacted with a post indicator, the authors find small, imprecise, and if anything positive coefficients for employment, earnings, hires, and separations; they interpret similar patterns for older and elderly workers as evidence that the OxyContin-share measure is not a valid source of opioid-specific labor-market variation.

The basic empirical result is clear: there is no convincing reduced-form evidence here of county-level labor market scarring from reformulation exposure. The more ambitious claim—that this demonstrates failure of the exclusion restriction for labor outcomes—is plausible, but not yet established as cleanly as the paper suggests.

## 3. **Essential Points**

**1. The paper must show the first stage and decide whether it is an IV paper or a reduced-form paper.**  
At present, the paper repeatedly discusses “the instrument” and draws conclusions about IV validity, but it never estimates the first stage in the actual analysis sample. That is a major omission. You need, at minimum, to show that pre-reform OxyContin share strongly predicts the post-2010 illicit transition in your county sample—e.g., heroin deaths, opioid deaths with a heroin/fentanyl composition shift, or another defensible transition measure. If the first stage is weak or absent in your restricted sample, then the contribution is simply a null reduced-form finding. If the first stage is strong, then show 2SLS and let readers compare reduced form, first stage, and implied labor-market LATE. Right now the paper sits uncomfortably between designs.

**2. The identifying assumption is weaker in prose than in econometric reality, and the control strategy is not sufficient for post-recession differential trends.**  
The key threat is obvious: counties with high OxyContin brand share may have different healthcare sectors, age structures, rurality, disability dependence, or Great Recession recovery paths. The age-placebo pattern is suggestive, but it is not a substitute for a more demanding specification. The current model has county FE, year FE, and total oxycodone × year, but no region × year effects, no pre-period trend adjustments, no baseline controls interacted with post, and no convincing event-study figure. Given the timing—2010, right after the recession—you need to do much more to separate reformulation exposure from differential recovery. As written, I do not think the exclusion/parallel-trends argument is persuasive either for validity or invalidity.

**3. The inference and interpretation need tightening.**  
State-clustered standard errors with 43 clusters may be acceptable, but the paper leans too hard on significance/non-significance, especially in robustness exercises. “Two-way clustering by state and year” is not standardly described as “state-by-year,” and with only 15 years that procedure may not solve the finite-cluster problem you face. More importantly, the paper overstates what is ruled out. A 95 percent interval of roughly [-0.24, 0.73] on a coefficient scaled to a unit change in OxyShare is not especially tight; when translated to realistic exposure differences it rules out only moderately sized county-level effects. The magnitudes are plausibly small at county level, but the paper should be more modest about precision and about “rejecting substantial labor market damage.”

## 4. **Suggestions**

The paper is asking a worthwhile question, and I think the most promising path is to sharpen the contribution rather than broaden it. My main advice is: **either become a proper IV paper, or explicitly become a reduced-form paper about the limits of the reformulation design for labor-market outcomes.** Right now it tries to do both and therefore undersatisfies each.

First, I strongly recommend adding a compact **validation section**. Show in your exact county sample that higher pre-reform OxyContin share predicts the outcomes established in Alpert et al. and Evans et al.—for example heroin mortality, synthetic-opioid mortality in later years, or overdose composition shifts. Even one clean figure and one table would matter a lot. If the first stage is there, then estimate 2SLS with transition intensity as the endogenous regressor. If the first stage is not there in your sample, that itself is important and should be foregrounded: “the canonical first stage does not survive our county restrictions/QWI merge,” which would substantially change the interpretation.

Second, the event-study evidence needs to be shown, not described. In this setting, the omitted figure is central. I would want to see annual coefficients with confidence bands for prime-age employment and at least one placebo group. If the pre-trends are truly flat, that helps. If there is drift beginning in 2008 or 2009, that would support your current interpretation that OxyShare proxies for differential recovery patterns. Because 2010 is a messy macro period, I would also suggest specifications with **division-by-year fixed effects** or at least census-region-by-year fixed effects. If the positive coefficients disappear under those controls, that would be informative.

Third, I would probe much harder whether the positive “placebo” age patterns really imply invalidity. Elderly employment is a useful check, but it is not a perfect placebo. Counties with more favorable post-2010 recoveries could increase employment broadly, including among older workers; that is your own alternative story. So the age heterogeneity is not itself evidence against opioid effects unless you formally test whether effects are significantly different across groups and unless you benchmark expected exposure by age. A better placebo would be outcomes and sectors less plausibly affected by illicit opioid use: for example, **female 65+ employment**, public-sector employment if available elsewhere, or perhaps age-industry cells where opioid misuse is historically rare. Relatedly, if opioids affect labor supply most in goods-producing sectors, you should test sectoral heterogeneity rather than only age heterogeneity.

Fourth, the magnitudes need a more disciplined discussion. A coefficient of 0.24 on OxyShare × Post sounds large until one remembers the relevant policy variation is perhaps a 10–15 percentage point difference in OxyShare across counties, implying effects around 2–4 log points. That is not absurdly small, but nor is it negligible in a county-level panel. The paper should benchmark this against known county-level shocks and against the mortality literature. For example: if a one-SD increase in OxyShare raises heroin mortality by X, what labor-market effect would one reasonably expect if the opioid-labor elasticity from prior work held? This kind of calibration would help readers decide whether your estimates are economically informative or simply too noisy.

Fifth, I would revisit the outcome construction. QWI is a strong source, but annual aggregation may wash out relevant short-run changes around 2010–2012, especially for hires and separations. Since the underlying data are quarterly, I would at least test quarterly specifications with seasonality controls. You may gain precision and better timing. Also, beginning-of-quarter employment and average monthly earnings are UI-covered outcomes; if the treated population is marginal to formal employment, that may be exactly why you find little. That point belongs earlier and more prominently in the paper, because it is not merely a caveat—it is part of the interpretation.

Sixth, the paper would benefit from a more serious treatment of weighting. The unweighted estimate becomes positive and significant; that is potentially interesting, but the current interpretation (“captures economic trends”) is too quick. Unweighted estimates re-center the estimand on small counties, which may differ mechanically in measurement error, industrial composition, overdose dynamics, and ARCOS share volatility. Please show how OxyShare variance differs by population, and consider binned scatterplots or partial residual plots by county size. It would also help to report results weighted by baseline employment, not just population, since the outcomes themselves are employment-based.

Seventh, on inference, I would supplement cluster-robust SEs with a **wild cluster bootstrap** at the state level. With 43 clusters this is feasible and would reassure readers, especially because several headline interpretations rest on marginal significance patterns in robustness and heterogeneity tables. I would also avoid the claim that the alternative clustering produces “slightly tighter standard errors, as expected”; that is not a reliable expectation and sounds mechanical rather than econometric.

Eighth, the paper should be more careful with language around exclusion restriction failure. What your evidence currently shows is that OxyShare predicts labor outcomes in ways not obviously concentrated in opioid-vulnerable groups, and that reduced-form labor effects are not negative. That is consistent with exclusion failure, but also with a weak or irrelevant labor-market first stage, aggregation bias at the county level, attenuation from noisy outcomes, or a treated population largely outside formal employment. Those are different substantive conclusions. I would present “instrument boundary” as a hypothesis supported by the evidence, not as something proven.

Finally, if this remains in AER: Insights format, the contribution should be stated in one sentence very early: **“We show that the canonical OxyContin reformulation exposure measure does not generate credible or economically meaningful county-level labor-market variation, despite its success in mortality applications.”** That is a potentially useful paper. But to make it convincing, you need to (i) validate the first stage in your sample, (ii) strengthen the trend diagnostics substantially, and (iii) moderate the strongest claims about exclusion and precision. As it stands, the paper has an interesting negative result, but it does not yet pin down whether the problem is invalid identification, insufficient power, or simply the absence of detectable county-level labor effects.
