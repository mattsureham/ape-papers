**Idea Fidelity**

The paper faithfully pursues the manifest’s core idea. It leverages the EU 2018 neonicotinoid ban, the sudden availability of Article 53 derogations in 11 countries for sugar beet, and the pollinator-dependence gradient from Klein et al. to implement the promised triple-difference design. The Eurostat crop-yield data, derogation classification, and robustness checks (placebos, event study, post-ECJ reversal) follow the manifest’s plan. One minor deviation: the manifest envisioned a possible heterogeneous effect “by crop pollinator dependence,” whereas the paper mostly reports aggregate DDD coefficients and relegates crop-group decompositions to later robustness sections. Still, the main empirical approach matches the research question.

**Summary**

The paper studies the yield effects of the EU’s 2018 neonicotinoid ban through a triple-difference estimator that interacts the post-ban period, crop pollinator dependence, and whether a country issued Article 53 emergency derogations. Using Eurostat crop yield data for 26 countries and 13 crops over 2000–2023, the paper finds no statistically significant yield impact either in the full DDD specification ($\hat\beta=-0.097$, SE=0.117) or across a battery of robustness tests, suggesting that removing neonicotinoids imposed no measurable crop yield penalty at the country level.

**Essential Points**

1. **Interpretation of the DDD Coefficient in Light of Continuous PDR and Derogation Practice:** The core identification relies on variation in the product of Post × PDR × Derogation status, yet the paper does not sufficiently link this interaction to the actual scope of derogations, which were confined to sugar beet. That limits the plausibility that pollinator dependence across all crops meaningfully loads onto this interaction. In the absence of crop-specific derogations (beyond sugar beet), the design effectively compares pollinator-dependent crops in derogation countries to the same crops in non-derogation countries, regardless of whether those crops were actually treated with neonicotinoids. The authors need to show—ideally with data—that derogation countries maintained wider neonicotinoid use on pollinator-dependent crops besides sugar beet during 2019–2022, or alternatively reformulate the empirical strategy to align more directly with the actual policy exposure. Without that link, the triple interaction risks conflating the general ban with unrelated yield trends, and the DDD estimate cannot be straightforwardly interpreted as the causal effect of neonicotinoid restriction on pollinator-dependent crops.

2. **Role of Sugared Beet and the Sufficient Variation in Treatment Exposure:** The analysis combines 13 crops, yet only sugar beet was explicitly granted derogations. The paper explores a sugar beet–only DD but does not use it to anchor the DDD’s interpretation. If derogations (and thus compliance with the ban) are concentrated in one crop, then most of the triple-difference variation comes from comparing pollinator-dependent oilseeds (rapeseed, sunflower) and cereals across countries that may or may not have granted derogations for sugar beet. Unless these countries also differed systematically in the use of neonicotinoids on those other crops, it is hard to see how the DDD identifies the intended effect. The authors should more clearly demonstrate that the credibility of the identification extends beyond sugar beet—either by documenting crop-level authorization data, pesticide sales by crop, or by restricting the sample to crops historically treated with neonicotinoids. Without evidence that pollinator-dependent crops other than sugar beet were treated differently across derogation status, the null result may simply reflect noise rather than informative variation.

3. **Power and Magnitude Considerations:** The paper emphasizes a null result but provides limited guidance on the smallest yield effect it can confidently rule out. The 95% CI of $[-0.33,0.13]$ on log yield translates into a roughly ±13% change, which is not small relative to the hypothesized 4–16% losses motivating the derogations. It would strengthen the contribution to calculate the minimum detectable effect (MDE) given the estimation sample and clustering, and to relate it to the magnitudes cited in the policy debates (e.g., the 4–16% ex ante projections). Without this, readers cannot tell whether the null is informative or simply underpowered. Addressing this by computing MDEs or translating the CI into more intuitive percentage yield changes would make the conclusion—“no measurable penalty” versus “cannot rule out moderate losses”—much clearer.

**Suggestions**

1. **Clarify the Exposure Mapping:** Because the triple difference mixes pollinator dependence with country-level derogation status, it’s essential to document how misclassification or heterogeneity across crops affects identification. Consider adding a table showing how many of the 13 crops were ever covered by derogations (even informally) in each country, or at least whether sugar beet was agriculturally important in derogation versus non-derogation countries. If crop-level data on neonicotinoid authorizations are unavailable, explain the rationale for assuming that derogation status is a valid proxy for continued neonicotinoid availability on all pollinator-dependent crops in those countries. Specifying this either in the institutional background or empirical strategy will help readers assess the plausibility of the key assumption.

2. **Leverage Additional Outcome Measures:** The Eurostat dataset also reports harvested area and production, which permit computing yield indirectly or analyzing production quantities directly. If, for example, derogation countries reallocated area away from sensitive crops post-ban, that could bias interpretation of yield-level nulls. A robustness check controlling for area, or examining total production per crop to see if output compensation occurred via area adjustments, would enrich the interpretation. Similarly, linking to pesticide sales or usage (if available) in Derogation versus non-D countries by crop would provide evidence about the mechanism the paper invokes in the Discussion.

3. **Expand the Mechanism Evidence:** The Discussion posits either substitution to alternatives (pyrethroids, cultural practices) or non-essentiality of neonicotinoids. These are plausible but speculative. Can the paper integrate more empirical support—for instance, by showing trends in pyrethroid sales or adoption of alternative seed treatments before and after 2018, ideally differentiated by derogation status? Alternatively, if such data are unavailable, consider a more cautious framing that presents substitution as one hypothesis rather than a conclusion. Adding a short section summarizing available evidence or pointing to descriptive statistics that corroborate the proposed mechanism would strengthen the paper’s policy relevance.

4. **Address the Role of 2023 Data and ECJ Reversal More Directly:** The manifest highlighted the ECJ ruling as a “built-in reversal.” While Model 5 disaggregates the post-ban period, its interpretation is currently cursory. Since derogations ended in 2023, one might expect a jump for sugar beet yields in derogation countries between 2022 and 2023 if derogations had been materially supporting yields. Presenting a focused event study or difference-in-difference around this reversal—perhaps using only sugar beet—could provide a sharper test and help distinguish between the substitution and “not essential” hypotheses. If data are noisy due to short post-reversal window, explain that clearly.

5. **Consider Normalizing the DDD by PDR Variation:** The Pollinator Penalty idea relies on a gradient from PDR=0 to 0.65. Yet the PDR distribution is heavily skewed toward zero because most crops are cereals/roots. The triple interaction’s interpretation might improve if PDR is rescaled (e.g., normalized to unit variance) or if the regression is run separately by PDR bins (low, medium, high). Presenting results that show the effect for high-PDR crops explicitly (rather than averaging in a continuous variable dominated by zeros) would help readers see whether the null holds precisely where pollinator services should matter most.

6. **Strengthen the Presentation of Robustness:** The leave-one-country-out results are mentioned in passing in the text and appendix but not shown in a figure or table. Including a short plot of the coefficient for each leave-out iteration, or otherwise summarizing the distribution, would visually reassure readers that the null is not driven by any single country. Likewise, the robustness table could be expanded to include the main coefficient’s significance level and 95% CI to make comparisons more transparent.

7. **Clarify Sample Construction and Potential Missing Data Issues:** The data appendix notes that some country–crop pairs have intermittent reporting and that the panel is unbalanced. Elaborate briefly whether missingness is correlated with PDR or derogation status. If crops with higher PDR are underreported in some countries, that could bias the triple-difference estimate. Providing a table of reporting frequency by crop group and by country type (derogation vs. non-derogation) would help allay such concerns.

8. **Situate the Null Within the Broader Literature on Pesticide Bans:** The Introduction appropriately cites simulation work and field trials. To sharpen the contribution, add a sentence or two contrasting the paper’s null finding with studies estimating yield effects in other contexts (e.g., bans of specific pesticides in non-EU countries). Emphasizing that this is one of the first large-scale policy evaluations with country-level data would make the “bigger picture” claim more compelling.

By addressing these points—particularly the mapping between derogation status and crop-level exposure, the interpretation of the null effect, and the description of mechanisms—the paper will present a more credible and informative empirical evaluation of the EU neonicotinoid ban.
