## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple and policy-relevant question: when a government fully deregulates domestic aviation, does competition finally reach the thin, underserved routes that reformers say they want to help? Using Argentina’s 2024 aviation liberalization, the paper argues that the answer is largely no: deregulation increased reshuffling and reallocation, but did not bring meaningful service expansion to previously monopoly routes, and may even have facilitated exit from them.

A busy economist should care because this is potentially a clean test of a broad proposition that extends far beyond airlines: removing legal barriers to entry is not enough if the real constraint is market size. If true, the paper speaks to competition policy, market design in thin markets, infrastructure access, and the limits of deregulation in developing economies.

### Does the paper articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Pretty well, but not maximally sharply. The current introduction is competent and readable, yet it still sounds like “another policy evaluation of a reform” rather than “a general lesson about when deregulation fails.” The first paragraphs lead with aviation deregulation as a topic; they should lead with the broader economic claim: in thin markets, monopoly may reflect demand fundamentals rather than policy barriers, so deregulation can disappoint even when implemented dramatically.

### The pitch the paper should have

Here is the pitch the paper should state in the first two paragraphs:

> Policymakers often assume that monopoly markets are underserved because regulation blocks entry. But in thin markets, monopoly may persist because demand is too weak to support competition, not because legal barriers are binding. Argentina’s 2024 aviation reform—an unusually sweeping and immediate deregulation of fares, routes, and cabotage—offers a rare test of this distinction.
>
> If deregulation works as its advocates claim, previously monopoly routes should see the largest gains in entry and service. Instead, I show that liberalization mainly reallocated capacity toward already attractive corridors and newly profitable connections, while thin monopoly routes saw little improvement and in some cases lost carriers. The broader lesson is that removing regulatory barriers does not create competition where underlying market size cannot support it.

That is an AER-style opening. It frames a world question first, then introduces the setting.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### Contribution in one sentence

The paper’s contribution is to show, in a sharp national deregulation episode, that eliminating legal barriers to airline entry did not expand service on previously monopoly domestic routes, implying that many such monopolies were commercially rather than regulatory determined.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3-4 papers?

Only partially. The paper cites the classic airline deregulation literature and some entry/IO papers, but the differentiation is still too generic. Right now the contribution reads as: “we study deregulation in Argentina rather than the U.S.” That is not enough for AER unless the paper makes very explicit what this case teaches that the classic literature could not.

The differentiating angle should be something like:

- classic U.S. deregulation papers show average gains from liberalization in a large, dense market;
- entry models in IO tell us thin markets may not sustain multiple firms;
- this paper provides reduced-form evidence from a sweeping real-world reform that directly tests whether monopoly routes in a thin-market developing-country setting were constrained by regulation or by demand.

That last clause is the real contribution. The paper gets at it, but does not hammer it hard enough.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or filling a gap in a LITERATURE?

Mostly both, but it leans too much toward literature-gap framing in the contribution paragraph. The stronger framing is a world question:

- Why do some monopoly markets remain monopoly after deregulation?
- When does entry reform fail because the binding constraint is not regulation but scale?

That is a stronger claim than “there is little evidence from developing-country aviation.”

### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain what’s new?

They could get there, but they might still summarize it as “a DiD on Argentina aviation deregulation.” That is the danger. The paper needs to make it easier for a reader to say:

> “It shows that deregulation doesn’t create competition in thin markets when monopoly is driven by demand fundamentals rather than entry barriers.”

That is memorable. “Competition illusion” is a decent phrase, but currently it risks sounding like branding rather than a conceptual contribution. The concept needs to be defined more rigorously: not all observed post-reform entry means underserved monopoly markets benefited.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Three specific ways:

1. **Reframe the core outcome around market access rather than just traffic.**  
   Passengers, seats, and flights are fine, but the most compelling outcome may be whether deregulation improved connectivity for peripheral communities: frequency reliability, city-pair access, travel time, or network reach. “Did thin places get connected?” is bigger than “did log passengers change?”

2. **Make the mechanism more economic and less descriptive.**  
   The current mechanism is basically “airlines chased profitability.” That is true but banal. The paper would be bigger if it showed more systematically that post-reform gains concentrate on routes with pre-existing demand fundamentals, airport capacity, or tourism/business traffic. In other words: not just that monopoly routes didn’t gain, but why they didn’t gain.

3. **Generalize beyond aviation explicitly.**  
   The paper has the ingredients to speak to a larger class of deregulation policies in thin markets—banking access, telecom, buses, hospitals, pharmacies, etc. If the author can articulate the general economic condition under which liberalization creates reallocation rather than access expansion, the contribution becomes much larger.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Deregulation is widely sold as a way to bring competition and service to underserved markets. The canonical airline evidence, especially from the U.S., supports this optimism.

### Tension

But underserved monopoly routes may not be underserved because regulators blocked entry. They may be monopoly routes because demand is too weak to sustain multiple carriers. If so, deregulation may increase flexibility without improving access where need is greatest.

### Resolution

Argentina’s sweeping 2024 aviation deregulation did not generate the expected gains on previously monopoly routes. Service did not increase meaningfully there, and carrier presence may even have fallen, while activity shifted toward more commercially attractive routes.

### Implications

Economists and policymakers should be more skeptical of “entry barrier” narratives in thin markets. Liberalization can reallocate supply rather than broaden access, so reforms may need complementary subsidies, service obligations, or infrastructure improvements if the objective is territorial inclusion.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has one, but it is not yet tight. The paper is closer to a coherent paper than to a random collection of results, but the narrative still gets diluted by too much method exposition and too little conceptual focus. The strongest story is not “here is a policy reform and some estimates.” The strongest story is:

> A dramatic deregulation tested whether monopoly routes were constrained by policy or by economics. The answer appears to be economics.

That story should organize the whole paper.

At present, the paper sometimes sounds like it has two stories:
1. a policy evaluation of Milei’s reform;
2. a broader claim about thin-market competition.

The second is the publishable one. The first is just the setting.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?

I would say:

> Argentina completely deregulated domestic aviation, and the routes with the most obvious room for new competition—the monopoly routes—did not gain service. Deregulation mostly reshuffled capacity toward routes that were already commercially attractive.

That is the lead.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

They would lean in—if presented this way. It is a clean, surprising proposition because it cuts against a common prior. But if presented as “I study Decree 599/2024 using a difference-in-differences design,” they will reach for their phones immediately.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Likely one of these:

- “So were those monopoly routes never viable in the first place?”
- “Is this about market thickness or about state-owned carrier retreat?”
- “Did consumers gain through lower fares on thick routes even if thin routes didn’t improve?”
- “Is this an aviation result or a general deregulation result?”

Those are good questions. The introduction should anticipate them.

### If the findings are null or modest, is that interesting?

Yes, potentially very interesting. But the paper needs to make much more of *why* a null on service expansion is economically important. The current framing does some of this, but it still treats the null as “the reform didn’t work as expected” rather than “this reveals that the common policy diagnosis was wrong.”

A null result becomes interesting when it overturns an important mechanism. Here the mechanism is: barriers to entry were not the main reason these routes lacked competition. That is valuable learning.
