## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple, policy-relevant question: when a government fully deregulates domestic aviation, does competition finally reach thin, underserved routes, or does service simply get reshuffled toward already-profitable corridors? Using Argentina’s 2024 aviation liberalization, the paper argues that removing regulatory barriers did not expand service on previously monopoly routes; instead, airlines reallocated toward dense markets and some thin routes actually lost carriers.

A busy economist should care because this is not really an airline paper; it is a paper about a broader economic claim that deregulation and entry liberalization do not automatically create competition where the underlying economics are weak. That is a first-order issue for industrial organization, regulation, regional development, and state-capacity debates in developing countries.

**Does the paper articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Mostly yes, but not optimally. The current opening is competent and readable, but it starts from “aviation deregulation” as a sectoral topic rather than from the broader economic question: when are monopolies created by regulation versus by thin demand? The paper’s best idea is not “Argentina deregulated airlines”; it is “many policymakers mistake regulatory barriers for the main reason underserved markets remain monopolized.” That should be the hook from line one.

### The pitch the paper should have

Here is the first-two-paragraph pitch the paper should deliver:

> Policymakers often assume that concentrated markets remain uncompetitive because regulation blocks entry. But in many thin markets, monopoly may persist not because entry is forbidden, but because demand is too weak to support it. Distinguishing between these two cases matters enormously: if concentration is structural rather than regulatory, deregulation may generate little new competition and may even accelerate exit from marginal markets.
>
> This paper studies an unusually clean test of that distinction: Argentina’s 2024 aviation reform, which simultaneously eliminated fare controls, opened domestic route access, and authorized foreign cabotage. If regulatory barriers were the main reason many domestic routes remained monopolized, those routes should have seen entry and traffic growth after the reform. Instead, I find that previously monopoly routes saw no improvement in service and lost carriers on net, while growth concentrated on thicker corridors. The broader lesson is that liberalization can create the appearance of competition without extending market access where demand is fundamentally thin.

That is the AER version of the story: not “aviation reform in Argentina,” but “what deregulation can and cannot do in structurally thin markets.”

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution in one sentence:**  
The paper shows that sweeping airline deregulation in Argentina did not bring competition or traffic growth to previously monopoly domestic routes, implying that many concentrated markets were constrained by thin demand rather than by regulation.

That is a real contribution. But right now it is only **somewhat clearly** differentiated from nearby work.

### Is it clearly differentiated from the closest papers?
Only partially. The paper cites classic airline deregulation and entry papers, but the introduction still risks sounding like “another policy evaluation of a liberalization episode.” The distinction it needs to sharpen is:

- not another paper showing deregulation changes fares/traffic on average;
- not another entry paper documenting that firms prefer dense markets;
- but a paper using a major reform to separate **regulatory barriers** from **structural barriers** as explanations for persistent concentration.

That is the conceptual contribution. The paper is closest to becoming interesting when it says monopoly routes may be monopolies “for demand reasons, not regulatory ones.” That line should be the centerpiece.

### World question or literature gap?
It is framed partly as a world question, partly as a literature-gap exercise. It should lean much harder into the world question.

The strong version is:
- “Why do underserved markets remain underserved after deregulation?”
- “When does liberalization fail to create competition?”
- “What kind of monopoly is policy actually confronting?”

The weaker version, which the paper currently drifts into, is:
- “There is little evidence from developing-country aviation markets.”

That is true, but it is not enough for AER. “No one has done this in Argentina” is not a top-journal contribution. “This episode reveals a general limit of deregulation in thin markets” is.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new?
Right now, a smart economist could probably say: “It’s a DiD on Argentine airline deregulation showing no gains on monopoly routes.” That is decent, but not memorable enough.

You want them instead to say:  
“Interesting paper: it shows that deregulation reveals whether monopoly was regulatory or structural. In Argentina, it was structural on thin routes.”

That is the portable idea.

### What would make the contribution bigger?
Several possibilities:

1. **Sharper welfare/access framing.**  
   Right now the outcomes are passengers, seats, flights, carriers. Those are sensible, but still feel sectoral. The paper gets bigger if it frames these as changes in **market access for peripheral communities**. Even a simple access metric—number of city-pairs reachable, connectivity to Buenos Aires or major hubs, travel-time access to the national network—would enlarge the stakes. If monopoly routes do not just fail to gain competition but become less connected, the paper gets much bigger.

2. **More explicit mechanism distinction: entry versus exit freedom.**  
   The paper has a good line that deregulation enabled exit as well as entry. That is potentially the most original mechanism in the paper and should be elevated. The story is not merely “entry did not happen”; it is “liberalization relaxed retention of socially valuable but commercially weak service.” That is much more interesting.

3. **A cleaner comparison to thick versus thin markets as a general theory test.**  
   The paper should explicitly say that the reform is a test of where policy barriers matter: thick markets should respond if regulation was binding; thin markets may not. This would move it from a country-case paper to a general proposition about market structure.

4. **A more ambitious framing around “misdiagnosing concentration.”**  
   The biggest version of this paper is not about aviation at all. It is about a policy mistake common in many sectors: treating all concentration as evidence of anti-competitive regulation rather than of market thickness constraints.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

### Closest neighbors
The nearest neighbors seem to be:

1. **Berry (1992)** on airline hub competition and entry determinants.
2. **Borenstein (1992)** and related post-deregulation U.S. airline evidence on fares and competition.
3. **Morrison and Winston (mid-1990s)** on airline deregulation and performance.
4. **Ciliberto and Tamer (2009/2010)** and broader empirical IO entry games literature.
5. Possibly **Bresnahan and Reiss (1991)** on entry thresholds in small markets.

One might also place it near:
- universal service / public service obligation literatures in transport;
- development and spatial economics papers on infrastructure and market access;
- political economy of deregulation in developing countries.

### How should the paper position itself relative to them?
**Build on and reinterpret**, not attack.

This paper is not overturning Berry or Borenstein. If anything, it is showing that a classic insight from industrial organization—entry requires sufficient market size—matters more than textbook deregulation rhetoric would suggest in a developing-country setting. So the paper should say:

- the U.S. deregulation literature documents large gains in a thick market;
- the IO entry literature predicts that thin markets may remain concentrated even after barriers fall;
- this paper provides a clean policy test of that logic in a setting where thin-route monopoly was widespread.

That triangulation is much stronger than simply saying “the literature is mostly U.S.-based.”

### Is it positioned too narrowly or too broadly?
Currently, it is **too narrowly positioned in aviation**, while making slightly over-broad rhetorical claims (“implications worldwide”) without doing the conceptual work to justify them.

It should broaden conceptually but remain disciplined empirically:
- empirically: one major aviation reform in Argentina;
- conceptually: a test of whether deregulation can create competition in structurally thin markets.

That is the right scale.

### What literature does it seem unaware of?
It should speak more to:

1. **Entry in small/thin markets** beyond airlines, especially Bresnahan-Reiss style market-size thresholds.
2. **Universal service / public service obligations / socially valuable but commercially unviable service.**
3. **Spatial economics / regional inequality / transport access.**
4. **Development-state or liberalization debates** where supply response depends on complementary market conditions.
5. Potentially the literature on **misallocation of policy tools**: when deregulation is the wrong remedy because the problem is not a policy wedge but low demand or missing complements.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?
Not yet fully. It is having an airline-policy conversation. It should be having a broader conversation about **the limits of deregulation as a competition policy tool in thin markets**.

That is the unexpected literature connection that could raise its impact.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Before this paper, the standard policy narrative is that removing barriers to entry should increase competition, lower prices, and improve service—especially where markets are concentrated. In aviation, this intuition is reinforced by the U.S. deregulation experience.

### Tension
But many concentrated markets may not be concentrated because of regulation. They may be concentrated because they are too thin to sustain multiple firms. So when a government liberalizes entry, what happens on the very routes policymakers most hope to help?

### Resolution
In Argentina’s sweeping 2024 aviation reform, previously monopoly routes do not gain traffic, seats, or frequencies relative to already competitive routes, and they lose carriers on net. Entry occurs, but mostly not where the unmet-policy promise lay.

### Implications
Deregulation is not enough when the core constraint is market thickness. Liberalization may produce visible competition in profitable corridors while leaving peripheral places no better off—or worse off. Complementary policies may be required if the objective is access rather than just formal openness.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
It has one, but it is not as tight as it could be. The paper is close to a coherent story, yet still reads at moments like a collection of regressions around an intuitively appealing case study. Two things weaken the arc:

1. The introduction spends too much time on institutional detail before fully crystallizing the conceptual puzzle.
2. The paper oscillates between two stories:
   - “deregulation failed on monopoly routes,” and
   - “continuous concentration predicts weaker outcomes.”
   
Those are related, but the paper should choose a primary story. My advice: the primary story should be **the failure of liberalization to improve thin monopoly markets**, with the continuous HHI results as supporting evidence that the phenomenon is graded rather than binary.

### What story should it be telling?
It should tell this story:

> Policymakers look at monopoly routes and infer blocked entry. Argentina removed the relevant barriers nearly all at once. The routes that should have benefited most did not. Why? Because those routes were not “protected monopolies”; they were “structural monopolies” sustained by weak demand and, in some cases, implicit cross-subsidy. Deregulation therefore exposed rather than solved the underlying problem.

That is a story with setup, puzzle, mechanism, and implication.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?
“I’d start with: Argentina fully deregulated domestic aviation, and the routes that were monopolies beforehand did not get more service—they actually lost carriers on net.”

That is crisp and surprising enough.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
They would **lean in briefly**, but whether they stay engaged depends on the second sentence. If the follow-up is “in Argentina, airlines reshuffled across routes,” some will mentally classify it as a niche transport paper. If the follow-up is “so this reform lets us separate regulatory from structural monopoly,” then you have the room.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Probably:
- “How do you know those routes were constrained by demand rather than by regulatory barriers?”
- or “Is this an aviation-specific result, or does it tell us something general about thin markets?”

Again, those are exactly the questions the framing should embrace.

### Are the findings null or modest?
The headline finding is partly null and partly negative. That is okay. The null is interesting **if and only if** the paper convincingly argues that the reform was a strong test of the deregulation hypothesis. It mostly does. But it should make the value of the null more explicit:

- This was not a marginal reform.
- It removed multiple barriers at once.
- The relevant markets were exactly the ones policymakers would point to as needing liberalization.
- Yet no meaningful service expansion appeared there.

That makes the null informative rather than disappointing.

At present, the “competition illusion” label helps, but it is a bit slogan-y. The paper must ensure that the phrase is earned by the conceptual argument, not just by rhetoric.

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

### What would make it read better?

1. **Front-load the core insight much more aggressively.**  
   The most important sentence in the paper is basically: *monopoly routes may be monopolies for demand reasons, not regulatory ones.* That should appear in the first paragraph, not as an aside.

2. **Shorten the empirical strategy section in the main text.**  
   For editorial positioning purposes, the paper spends too much valuable front-half real estate explaining why TWFE is okay, detailing placebo timing, etc. That is referee-facing material. The main text should reserve more space for the conceptual contribution and the economic interpretation.

3. **Move some defensive material to the appendix.**  
   The pre-trends prose, discussion of clustered inference, and several lines in robustness should be compressed or moved. The reader currently encounters too much econometric self-defense relative to narrative payoff.

4. **Elevate the entry/exit decomposition.**  
   The most vivid finding in the paper is not the coefficient table; it is that deregulation enabled **exit freedom** from thin routes. That section should appear earlier—possibly immediately after the headline result—and maybe even previewed in the introduction as the key mechanism.

5. **Clarify the relation between binary and continuous treatment results.**  
   Right now the discrepancy is explained, but it still risks confusing readers. The paper should state a hierarchy:
   - headline fact: monopoly routes did not improve;
   - broader pattern: more concentrated routes generally underperformed after liberalization.
   
   That makes the continuous treatment result a strengthening exercise rather than a separate story.

6. **Rework the conclusion.**  
   The conclusion is fine but somewhat generic. It should not just say “pair liberalization with subsidies/investment.” It should end on the sharper conceptual insight: **competition policy fails when policymakers misdiagnose structural thinness as regulatory exclusion.**

### Is the paper front-loaded with the good stuff?
Reasonably, but not enough. The abstract is actually stronger than the introduction. The intro should more quickly deliver the paper’s general lesson.

### Are important results buried?
Yes: the **exit-freedom** interpretation is buried relative to its importance. That is where the paper feels freshest.

### Is the conclusion adding value?
Some, but not enough. It summarizes rather than elevates. It should widen the aperture beyond aviation.
