## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper exploits a "big bang" deregulation in Argentina (Decree 599/2024) to test whether removing regulatory barriers actually brings competition to underserved, monopoly markets. Using high-frequency microdata, the author finds a "competition illusion": while aggregate traffic grew, deregulation actually led to a net *exit* of carriers from monopoly routes as incumbents were freed from cross-subsidization requirements. It’s a cautionary tale about the limits of liberalization in "thin" markets where demand, not regulation, is the binding constraint.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this well in the first two paragraphs, successfully pivoting from the "canonical" US 1978 experience to the realities of developing-country markets. However, the term "Competition Illusion" is slightly ambiguous in the pitch—it should explicitly state that the "illusion" is the belief that removing barriers *automatically* creates competition in thin markets.

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:** The paper demonstrates that in thin markets, aviation deregulation can lead to market abandonment rather than entry, because regulatory barriers were the only thing forcing service through cross-subsidization or mandates.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It differentiates well from Borenstein (1992) and the US literature by focusing on the "demand-side" failure in developing nations.
*   **World vs. Literature:** It frames itself as a question about the **world**—specifically the Milei reforms—which is a major strength. It feels timely and high-stakes.
*   **AER Quality?** A smart economist would say: "It’s a paper showing that deregulation killed cross-subsidies in Argentina, leaving peripheral towns worse off." This is a clear, "cocktail party" takeaway.
*   **Bigger Contribution:** To move from "very good" to "AER definitive," the paper needs to prove the **mechanism of cross-subsidization**. Are Aerolíneas Argentinas’ profits on the "trunk" routes actually dropping due to new LCC competition, thereby forcing them to cut the "monopoly" routes? Connecting the trunk-route price wars to the monopoly-route exits would be the "smoking gun."

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

*   **Closest Neighbors:** Borenstein (1992) on US deregulation; Bresnahan & Reiss (1991) on entry in thin markets; Cavallo & Cottani (2024) on Milei's macro reforms.
*   **Positioning:** The paper correctly builds on Bresnahan & Reiss. It should more aggressively **attack** the "Washington Consensus" view that entry barriers are the *sole* reason for lack of competition in the Global South.
*   **Missing Conversations:** The paper should speak to the **Public Economics/Political Economy** literature on "Universal Service Obligations" (USO). This isn't just an IO paper; it's a paper about how the state ensures service to the periphery. Mentioning the trade-off between efficiency and equity in network industries would broaden the audience.

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

*   **Setup:** Argentina’s aviation was a sclerotic, state-dominated monopoly with high barriers and cross-subsidies.
*   **Tension:** The Milei administration removed all barriers overnight. Textbook theory says the most underserved markets (the monopolies) should see the most entry.
*   **Resolution:** The opposite happened. Entry flooded the already-competitive routes; carriers fled the monopoly routes.
*   **Implications:** Deregulation is not a panacea; it can exacerbate regional inequality if not paired with targeted subsidies (e.g., Essential Air Service).

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong and "clean." It reads like a natural experiment with a surprising result.

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

**Lead fact:** "When Argentina opened its skies, the routes that most needed competition actually lost their only airlines."
**Reaction:** People lean in. It’s counter-intuitive to the "competition is always good" narrative.
**Follow-up:** "Why didn't the low-cost carriers (LCCs) want those routes if the incumbent left?"
**The Paper's Answer:** Because they are "thin" markets where even one carrier can't break even without a subsidy. The result is a highly valuable "informative null" on the power of deregulation.

---

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

*   **Front-loading:** The paper is well front-loaded.
*   **Robustness:** The "New route" (city-pair) result on page 8 is actually very interesting and currently feels like a side note. It suggests deregulation helped "breadth" (new connections) but not "depth" (competition on existing thin routes). This deserves more prominence.
*   **The Appendix:** The COVID exclusion is handled well, but I’d like to see more on **fares**. The abstract mentions "eliminating fare controls," but the results focus on "passengers/seats." If the author could find even proxy data for fares (web-scraped or CPI sub-indices), it would complete the welfare picture.

---

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The gap is **ambition regarding welfare and political economy.** Right now, it’s a very high-quality IO evaluation of a specific decree. To be an AER paper, it needs to address the **"Trilemma" of Infrastructure**: You can have (1) No fiscal cost to the state, (2) Low prices for consumers, and (3) Universal geographic coverage—but you can't have all three.

**The single most impactful piece of advice:** Quantify the "Implicit Subsidy." Use the data to estimate how much "trunk" route profit was previously required to keep one of these "monopoly" routes alive. This transforms the paper from a "did-it-work" evaluation into a theoretical contribution on the death of cross-subsidization in network industries.
