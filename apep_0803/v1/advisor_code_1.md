**Summary**

This paper studies whether Medicaid expansion under the ACA generated racially inclusive employment gains in the healthcare sector. Using state-level QWI race-ethnicity aggregates and staggered DiD (TWFE, Sun–Abraham, and a Black-vs-White triple-difference), the author finds that expansion increased Black healthcare employment by roughly 10 percent while leaving White employment flat, raising the Black share of healthcare jobs by about 0.5 percentage points. The bulk of the effect comes through new hiring rather than retention, and it is specific to healthcare relative to a retail placebo.

**Essential Points**

1. **Causal interpretation hinges on a credible control group and model for timing heterogeneity.** The paper relies on 11 never-expanders plus staggered late expanders, yet the treatment and control states differ sharply in pre-existing Black employment shares, uninsurance rates, and broader labor markets. While Sun–Abraham is noted, the paper neither shows the relative event-study dynamic nor conducts falsification tests beyond the linear pre-trend coefficient. Without more transparent evidence that late expanders are valid counterfactuals (e.g., excluding early/late cohorts or showing dynamic plots for never-treated), readers cannot assess whether the estimated 10 percent rise reflects Medicaid expansion or coincident trends.

2. **Aggregation to the state-year level masks the localization and mechanism promised by the manifest.** The idea emphasised county-level Medicaid density and the racial composition of local hiring, but the analysis aggregates to states and years, which conflates heterogeneous counties and cannot exploit the cross-sectional geographic overlap between uninsurance and Black workforce share. This broad aggregation also makes the mechanism — expansion demand hitting high-Black counties — speculative rather than empirically grounded.

3. **Inference is fragile given a small number of treated units and the imprecision reported, yet the paper focuses on point estimates.** Clustered SEs with 51 states may understate uncertainty, particularly for the DDD estimates (standard errors around 0.07). The only robustness checks are cohort restriction and a retail placebo, but there is no exploration of how sensitive results are to alternative specifications (e.g., state-specific trends, excluding outlier states, or alternative clustering). Without demonstrating robustness, the policy takeaway — that Medicaid expansion is an implicit racial inclusion policy — rests on thin statistical ground.

**Suggestions**

- **Expand the identification strategy to exploit the promised continuous treatment variation.** Use the county-level uninsurance rate (or pre-expansion Medicaid take-up) as a measure of Medicaid intensity, interacted with the expansion indicator, to test whether higher Medicaid density counties experienced larger racial employment shifts. This would directly tie the mechanism to the geographic overlap of uninsurance and Black labor supply, fulfilling the manifest’s promise and strengthening the causal story. If feasible, include county-level controls (demographic composition, baseline healthcare capacity) to ensure the interaction captures demand shocks rather than compositional differences.

- **Leverage more of the QWI granularity to bolster mechanisms and heterogeneity.** Instead of collapsing to the state-year level, consider county-quarter (or at least county-year) aggregates for key analyses, particularly the hiring-flow decomposition. This would allow you to: (i) control flexibly for local trends via county fixed effects or county-specific trends; (ii) interact Medicaid expansion timing with county-level Black share or uninsurance to test whether the effect is concentrated where the Black workforce is large; and (iii) add spatial clustering or neighboring-state controls to address spillovers. Even if some cells are suppressed, averaging across counties for which data are available should still yield insight.

- **Present event-study plots and additional placebo tests.** Graphical evidence of parallel pre-trends is especially important when using limited control states. Show the Sun–Abraham event study (or even a TWFE-like plot) separately for Black and White employment as well as their gap, covering 2001–2023. Add falsification tests such as applying the same DDD to odd outcomes (e.g., manufacturing employment by race) or shifting the expansion date forward to confirm that no “effect” shows up before actual expansion. This will reassure readers that the estimated racial employment gain is not driven by pre-existing diverging trends or unobserved policy changes.

- **Reconsider the standard errors and clustering strategy.** With 40 treated and 11 control states, the cluster count is small. Complement the state-clustered SEs with wild cluster bootstrap p-values (Cameron et al., 2008) or report Conley–Taber adjustments if you suspect few clusters. Alternatively, bootstrap the DDD estimate across states to show how stable the 9–10 percent estimate is. Transparency about the precision of the key estimate will temper readers’ expectations about policy magnitudes.

- **Deepen discussion of alternative mechanisms and gather suggestive evidence.** The current narrative emphasises local geographic overlap, but without empirical tests the mechanism remains speculative. Use available data to distinguish between demand-side (expansion-driven revenue, community health center hiring) versus supply-side (Black workers entering healthcare through public programs) explanations. For instance, if possible, examine whether the effect is stronger in counties with more safety-net hospitals, lower pre-expansion hospital employment, or higher community health center presence. Even if direct measures are unavailable, present correlational evidence linking the effect size to proxies such as county poverty levels or Medicaid enrollment growth.

- **Engage with alternative comparisons.** The manifest mentions comparing expansion states to late expanders as “clean controls.” Instead of pooling all control states, present separate results using only never-expanders, only early expanders, and only late expanders as counterfactuals. This will show whether the 10 percent effect is driven by certain groups (e.g., early-expanding states' rapid hiring) and help guard against bias from non-random timing.

- **Address potential measurement issues in the QWI.** Cell suppression in sparse counties could disproportionately affect Black employment tallies, biasing the share. Outline how you treat missing cells (e.g., imputation, aggregating across quarters) and consider sensitivity checks restricting to counties with complete data or weighting states by the proportion of population covered. If suppression is a concern, provide evidence that it does not bias the relative trends (e.g., by showing that the fraction of suppressed cells does not change post-expansion or by race).

- **Clarify the scope of the policy claim.** The abstract and conclusion portray Medicaid expansion as an “implicit racial inclusion mechanism,” but the evidence only shows relative Black employment gains across states that expanded versus those that did not. Discuss the distinction between relative share increases and absolute employment gains, and acknowledge that non-expansion states already had higher Black shares of healthcare employment pre-2014. This nuance will help prevent overgeneralization — the redistributive effect may come more from expansion states catching up rather than reverse.
