## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple but potentially important distributional question: when Medicaid expansion increases healthcare spending and staffing, who gets the new jobs? Using race-specific employment data, it argues that the ACA’s Medicaid expansions increased Black employment in healthcare substantially, while leaving White healthcare employment roughly unchanged, implying that a major social insurance reform also shifted the racial composition of local labor demand.

A busy economist should care because this reframes Medicaid expansion from a coverage question into a broader incidence question: not just who gets insured, but who benefits on the labor-market margin when public spending expands.

### Does the paper articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Reasonably well, but not as sharply as it should. The current opening is competent, but it still sounds a bit like “there is a literature on Medicaid expansion, and here is a race-specific extension.” That is too small for AER. The paper should open with a world question, not a literature gap: **when governments expand the welfare state, do the jobs created by that spending reach historically excluded workers, or do they flow through existing labor-market hierarchies?** Medicaid expansion is then the clean empirical setting for that larger question.

### The pitch the paper should have

> Public spending creates jobs, but economists know surprisingly little about who gets them. This paper studies whether one of the largest recent expansions of the U.S. safety net—ACA Medicaid expansion—changed not just insurance coverage and hospital finances, but the racial allocation of new healthcare employment. Using race-specific workforce data and staggered state adoption, I show that Medicaid expansion increased Black healthcare employment substantially while leaving White employment largely unchanged, suggesting that the labor-market incidence of public insurance expansion was strongly shaped by the geography of unmet medical demand and local labor supply.

That is a much stronger opening than “the racial distribution of employment effects has been invisible.”

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### Contribution in one sentence

The paper’s contribution is to show that Medicaid expansion’s healthcare employment effects were not racially neutral: the policy appears to have disproportionately increased Black employment in healthcare, primarily through hiring rather than retention.

### Is this clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?

Only partially. The paper says prior work studies insurance, uncompensated care, hospital finances, and aggregate employment, while this paper adds race-specific employment incidence. That is true, but the differentiation is still somewhat thin. Right now the contribution risks sounding like: “take a well-studied policy, slice the outcome by race, and estimate heterogeneity.” That is publishable somewhere; it is not yet obviously AER-level.

The author needs to be more explicit that the paper is not merely adding subgroup analysis but answering a first-order question about **the distribution of labor-market benefits from social spending**. The closest papers likely show that Medicaid expansion affects provider revenues or overall healthcare employment; this paper’s real novelty is about **incidence across workers**. That distinction needs much more force.

### World question or literature gap?

Currently framed too much as a literature gap. The paper repeatedly says the racial distribution has been “invisible” or “not answered.” That is fine as a sentence or two, but it should not be the backbone. The stronger frame is:

- Governments increasingly use social insurance expansions as place-based demand shocks.
- We do not know whether those shocks reduce or reproduce inequality in the labor market.
- Medicaid expansion provides a way to observe that incidence.

That is a world question. AER papers usually lead there.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new?

At present, maybe, but with some hesitation. They could say: “It’s a DiD paper showing Medicaid expansion increased Black healthcare employment more than White employment.” That is clear enough. But they might also say: “It’s another policy-incidence heterogeneity paper using QWI.” That is the danger.

The introduction does not yet make the reader feel that the finding changes how we think about the economic incidence of public spending.

### What would make the contribution bigger?

Several possibilities:

1. **Move from race-specific employment levels to the incidence of public spending more broadly.**  
   The paper should make explicit that this is about who captures the labor-demand effects of state spending expansions.

2. **Say more about where in healthcare the gains occurred.**  
   The limitation on occupation is a real strategic problem. A 10% increase in Black healthcare employment is much more interesting if one can say whether this was concentrated in home health aides, nursing assistants, clinics, hospitals, or higher-skill jobs. Right now the result invites the skeptical thought that the paper may be documenting low-wage margin expansion only.

3. **Sharpen the mechanism with geography.**  
   The paper’s geographic-overlap story is plausible and potentially interesting, but it remains asserted rather than made central. If the paper could show larger effects where pre-expansion uninsurance and Black labor supply overlap more strongly, the story would feel much bigger.

4. **Connect to labor-market inequality, not just Medicaid.**  
   The paper becomes larger if it says: social policy can alter racial inequality through labor demand in publicly exposed sectors.

If the author could only enlarge one dimension, it should be mechanism via geography and local labor demand.

It seems under-connected to at least three conversations:

1. **Incidence of public spending / fiscal policy on labor markets**  
   The paper should speak more directly to economists interested in who benefits from government demand shocks.

2. **Local labor demand and racial inequality**  
   There is a broader literature on whether demand shocks narrow or widen racial gaps depending on segregation, networks, and occupation structure. This paper should be talking to that literature.

The more impactful conversation is: **when the state expands financing in labor-intensive sectors, does that spending create inclusive employment growth?** That is more distinctive and more generalizable.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Before this paper, economists know Medicaid expansion increased insurance coverage and improved provider finances, with some evidence on aggregate employment effects in healthcare. But we do not know who captured those new employment opportunities.

### Tension

Public spending can either broaden opportunity or reinforce existing segmentation. Healthcare is both a massive employer of Black workers and a highly stratified sector. So when Medicaid expansion increased demand for care, did it create inclusive employment growth, or did existing racial hierarchies absorb the gains?

### Resolution

The paper finds that Medicaid expansion increased Black healthcare employment substantially, with little effect on White employment, and that the gains came through hiring rather than lower separations.

### Implications

If true, Medicaid expansion did more than insure patients: it altered the racial incidence of labor demand in healthcare. More broadly, social insurance expansions may affect inequality not only through transfers to consumers but through the composition of jobs created in beneficiary communities.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

A decent one, but it is still a bit underpowered. The paper has the pieces of a narrative, but they do not yet add up to a fully compelling story. At present it reads somewhat like a collection of sensible results:

- main race-specific employment effects,
- share effects,
- hires,
- placebo,
- pre-trends,
- discussion.

What is missing is a stronger central tension. The real story should not be “the literature hasn’t looked at race.” It should be:

> Medicaid expansion created a major sectoral demand shock in healthcare. The open question is whether such shocks generate inclusive employment growth or simply scale up existing workforce patterns. The answer here is unexpectedly asymmetric by race.

That gives the paper sharper stakes.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?

“I’d lead with: Medicaid expansion appears to have raised Black healthcare employment by about 10%, while White healthcare employment barely moved.”

That is a clean, memorable fact.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

Some would lean in. The result is intuitively interesting and politically salient. But top economists will immediately ask two things:

1. **What kind of jobs are these?**
2. **Why did the gains accrue to Black workers specifically?**

If the paper cannot answer those questions beyond broad speculation, attention may fade quickly.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Most likely: “Is this because Medicaid expansion increased demand in Black communities, or because healthcare employers changed their hiring behavior?”  
Second likely: “Are these good jobs, or just growth in lower-paid healthcare support work?”

Those are exactly the questions the paper should anticipate much earlier and more directly.

### If findings are modest or null

The findings are not null, but some supporting outcomes are modest and imprecise. The share effect is not huge. That is fine if the paper emphasizes that the economically striking result is in levels/hiring and frames the share shift as corroborating evidence, not the star.

One caution: the paper wants to sound dramatic, but some of its own ancillary evidence is only suggestive. It should avoid over-selling the precision of the broader inclusion claim.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

This is not primarily a “science is bad” problem from an editorial-positioning perspective. It is mainly a **framing and ambition problem**, with some **scope** limitations.

### What is the gap?

#### 1. Framing problem
The paper is currently framed as:
- Medicaid expansion has many effects;
- here is one more effect by race.

That is not enough.

It should be framed as:
- public spending creates jobs;
- economists rarely know which workers capture those jobs;
- this paper shows that the labor-demand effects of Medicaid expansion were racially asymmetric in a way that bears on inequality and the incidence of the welfare state.

That is a much bigger idea.

#### 2. Scope problem
The mechanism is underdeveloped. The paper has a plausible story about geographic overlap between the newly insured and Black labor supply, but little in the paper as presented turns that from conjecture into evidence. For AER, the paper needs at least one step further toward explaining why this happened.

#### 3. Novelty problem
There is some risk that the paper will be perceived as a subgroup extension of an already mature Medicaid literature. The author must actively fight that impression.

#### 4. Ambition problem
The paper is competent and tidy, but somewhat safe. It does not yet swing for a broader claim about the distributional incidence of social policy. AER papers usually do.

### Single most impactful piece of advice

**Reframe the paper away from “a race-specific extension of Medicaid employment effects” and toward “who captures the jobs created by social insurance expansions,” then organize the evidence around that broader incidence question with much stronger mechanism on why Black workers benefited disproportionately.**
