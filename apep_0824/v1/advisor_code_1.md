**Idea Fidelity**

The paper in its current form diverges substantially from the original manifest. The idea promised an analysis of Romania’s micro-enterprise graduation thresholds – exploiting the sequential reductions from €1 M to €100 K and leveraging firm-level API data to estimate revenue bunching at each threshold. Instead, the paper studies the 2016–2018 expansion (€65 K to €1 M) using Eurostat aggregate SBS data in a country-level difference-in-differences setup, focusing on enterprise counts and turnover levels. Key elements of the manifest – the firm-level API data, explicit bunching around thresholds, and the graduation mechanism – are absent. The identification strategy envisioned (a quasi–regression discontinuity/bunching design relying on precise thresholds) is replaced by a cross-country DiD that does not exploit the rich within-Romania variation. As a result, the paper does not pursue the manifest’s research design or identification strategy.

**Summary**

The paper studies Romania’s dramatic expansion of the micro-enterprise turnover-tax threshold by comparing Romania’s sector-level firm counts and turnover (2008–2020) to ten Central and Eastern European peers using a DiD framework. It finds no detectable increase in micro-firm counts after the reform, while average turnover per enterprise rises uniformly across all size classes, which the author attributes to broader economic growth rather than the tax policy. The main policy takeaway is that expanding simplified regimes may affect the intensive margin of existing firms without stimulating new entry.

**Essential Points**

1. **Identification is not credible given the chosen design.** The key estimating equation effectively compares Romania to peers before/after 2017 with a single treatment indicator, but Romania is the only treated country and the “treatment” aggregates several policy changes (2016, 2017, 2018) plus broader macro dynamics. The parallel-trends assumption is not convincingly demonstrated. No comprehensive event study results are shown in the main text, and the placebo tests reported (e.g., 2013 placebo) have wide confidence intervals, offering little reassurance. With only 11 clusters, clustering at the country level may understate uncertainty, especially given heterogeneity in pre-trends across sectors/countries. The reliance on sector-year-by-country fixed effects cannot substitute for a credible counterfactual when treatment timing and intensity vary and other contemporaneous shocks (e.g., Romania’s GDP surge) are likely correlated with the reform.

2. **The empirical proxy for firm creation is poorly matched to the policy.** The reform changes the turnover ceiling but does not alter the employee-size classification; yet the paper measures firm creation by the count of 0–9 employee firms in each sector-year. Firms that newly qualify may simply report higher revenue but not change employee counts, while new entrants may not fall into the micro class if they hire immediately. Moreover, the Micro-enterprise regime applies to revenue, not employment, so using the 0–9 class as the outcome introduces mechanical noise and misalignment with the policy treatment. Without firm-level data on revenue relative to the threshold or even a count of newly registered micro-enterprises, the “extensive margin” conclusion is difficult to substantiate.

3. **The paper ignores the rich within-country variation and the specified mechanism.** The idea manifest emphasized bunching around revenue thresholds through Romania’s firm-level API. The current analysis relies solely on aggregate Eurostat data, so it cannot speak to revenue bunching, graduation behavior, or the counterfactual of firms being pushed over the ceiling. The reform’s staggered nature (multiple threshold increases) is also ignored in the treatment indicator, which treats everything post-2017 as identical despite enormous heterogeneity in firm exposure. Without exploiting the threshold discontinuities, the paper misses the mechanism by which the policy should affect firm behavior and cannot separate the effect of the micro-enterprise reform from other contemporaneous changes in Romania.

**Suggestions**

1. **Realign the empirical strategy with the manifest’s focus on thresholds.** The strongest path forward is to obtain (or replicate) the firm-level revenue/employment data from the ANAF API mentioned in the manifest. With such data, the authors can estimate revenue bunching at each threshold or directly observe how firms respond to being above/below the ceiling. Regression discontinuity or bunching techniques would allow a more precise identification of the causal effect of the micro-enterprise eligibility changes, and they would align with the manifest’s original contribution. If confidential data remain unavailable, the manuscript should explicitly acknowledge that it departs from the manifest and clearly justify why aggregate SBS data are the best available proxy.

2. **Re-think the counterfactual and treatment variation.** If aggregate data remain the only empirical basis, consider richer treatment variation than a single post-2017 indicator. Romania’s threshold increased in stages (2016: €100 K, 2017: €500 K, 2018: €1 M), and the reform became mandatory in 2020. An event-study exploiting these multiple “treatment dates” could better capture dynamic effects and allow for pre-trend diagnostics. Furthermore, instead of comparing Romania to all CEE peers equally, consider constructing a synthetic control or a reweighted donor pool that more closely matches Romania’s pre-reform trajectory in micro-enterprise growth. This would bolster the parallel-trends assumption.

3. **Match outcomes to the policy mechanism.** Since the reform targets revenue eligibility, outcome variables should be as close to that margin as possible. Potential alternatives include: (a) analyzing the number of new firm registrations (available from Eurostat demography or national registers) to capture extensive-margin entry more directly; (b) focusing on average firm revenue within revenue bins if such data exist, or on turnover ratios; (c) using the share of firms reporting turnover near the old thresholds if finer bins exist. If the 0–9 employee class is retained, provide a stronger argument (with evidence) that firm size (by employees) is reliably correlated with turnover relative to the threshold. Otherwise, the paper risks conflating the policy effect with unrelated employment dynamics.

4. **Improve inference given clustering concerns.** With only 11 country clusters, ordinary cluster-robust standard errors may be unreliable. The paper notes this concern but provides no alternative. Apply recent methods such as wild bootstrap (Rademacher distribution) for clustered data with few clusters or use the Cameron, Gelbach, and Miller (2008) correction. Reporting these alternative standard errors will strengthen confidence that null findings are not driven by weak inference. Additionally, quantify the statistical power more fully: the current confidence intervals are relatively tight, but a formal power calculation (or minimum detectable effect) would help readers assess the informativeness of the null.

5. **Provide the event-study graphs and robustness details in the main text.** The appendix mentions pre-trend coefficients (e.g., +0.013, +0.041 at certain leads) but does not show the corresponding figure. Include the event-study plot in the main paper to visually demonstrate whether Romania diverges from peers before 2017. Similarly, the robustness checks (control group variations, sector heterogeneity) deserve fuller exposition rather than brief reporting. Showing how results change (or stay null) when including Western European countries or when stratifying by services versus manufacturing would help readers assess the stability of the findings.

6. **Discuss alternative channels for the intensive-margin effect.** The observed uniform turnover increase across all size classes may stem from concurrent macroeconomic growth rather than the reform, but the manuscript can do more to substantiate that claim. For instance, compare Romania’s GDP growth or export performance relative to peers, or control for country-year GDP levels in the regressions. Alternatively, consider interacting the treatment with sector-level export intensity or exposure to global value chains to explain why broad-based turnover rises occur. Without such controls, the null on firm creation may be confounded by omitted macro shocks.

7. **Clarify policy implications while acknowledging limitations.** The null result is interesting, but it is only persuasive if the analysis convincingly isolates the reform’s effect. In the current draft, alternative explanations (e.g., measurement error, other reforms, data limitations) could easily explain the lack of effect. Be explicit about what conclusions are supported by the data (e.g., “We cannot reject zero effect on micro-enterprise counts as measured by employee size”) rather than broader statements about “no new firms were created.” Also, briefly discuss how these findings align or conflict with other Romanian reforms during the sample.

Implementing some of these suggestions would make the paper more tightly focused on the original policy mechanism and strengthen confidence in the null finding.
