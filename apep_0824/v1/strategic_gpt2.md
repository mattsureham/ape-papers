## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple and policy-relevant question: if a government makes small-business taxation dramatically more generous, do more firms get created? Using Romania’s enormous expansion of its micro-enterprise tax regime and comparing Romania to other Central and Eastern European countries, the paper argues the answer is no: the policy appears not to increase firm entry, despite a very large tax subsidy.

A busy economist should care because simplified small-firm tax regimes are ubiquitous and are often sold as entrepreneurship policy. If even one of the largest such expansions in Europe did not move entry, that is potentially important for how we think about tax policy, entrepreneurship, and the margins on which firms respond.

### Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Reasonably well, but not optimally. The first paragraph is strong and concrete. The second paragraph moves too quickly into the design rather than sharpening the big question and why this case is unusually informative. The introduction currently sounds like “here is my DiD design” before it fully earns the reader’s attention with the substantive claim.

### What should the first two paragraphs say instead?

The paper should lead less with the method and more with the substantive puzzle:

> Governments around the world subsidize small firms through simplified tax regimes on the premise that lower tax and compliance burdens will spur entrepreneurship. But do such policies actually create new firms, or do they mainly transfer rents to firms that would exist anyway? This is a first-order question for tax policy and development policy alike, because small-business regimes are among the most common tools used to promote entry.
>
> Romania offers an unusually sharp test. Between 2013 and 2018, it expanded eligibility for its micro-enterprise turnover tax from EUR 65,000 to EUR 1,000,000 in revenue, bringing the vast majority of firms under a much lighter tax regime. If lowering small-firm taxes stimulates entry anywhere, this is a setting where one should see it. Yet comparing Romania to similar countries in the region, I find no increase in firm creation. The reform appears to have changed the tax treatment of firms far more than the number of firms.

That is the paper’s real pitch. The design should come after this.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### Contribution in one sentence

The paper’s contribution is to show that an exceptionally large expansion of a simplified small-business tax regime in Romania did not increase firm creation, suggesting that such tax policies may affect incumbent firms more than entrepreneurial entry.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest papers?

Only partly. The paper distinguishes itself from the bunching / threshold-response literature by saying: that literature studies behavioral responses around cutoffs, while this paper studies whether enlarging eligibility creates firms. That is a real distinction and probably the paper’s cleanest conceptual contribution.

But the differentiation is still somewhat thin because the paper does not yet convincingly map the closest neighboring literatures into separate buckets:
1. bunching and firm-size threshold papers,
2. business tax and firm creation / business density papers,
3. simplified tax regime / informality papers,
4. entrepreneurship constraints in developing and transition economies.

Right now the reader gets “this is not a bunching paper,” but not a strong enough sense of exactly what unanswered world-question remains after the existing literature.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a gap in a literature?

Mostly as a world question, which is good: do generous small-firm tax breaks create firms? That is much stronger than “the literature lacks evidence from Romania.” The paper should lean even harder into the world question and avoid sounding like a substitute for unavailable firm-level data.

### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what’s new?

Sort of, but not crisply enough. Right now they might say: “It’s a DiD paper showing Romania’s micro-firm tax expansion didn’t increase the number of firms.” That is clear enough, but it still risks sounding like “another policy-evaluation paper” rather than “a paper that changes how we think about entrepreneurship subsidies.”

The paper needs to make the novelty more conceptual:
- the standard defense of simplified regimes is entry;
- most existing evidence is about avoidance/bunching/intensive-margin distortions;
- this paper tests the central policy rationale directly and finds no support, even in an extreme case.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Three possibilities:

1. **A stronger “what margin responds?” framing.**  
   The paper should not just say “no new firms.” It should say: the main observable response to simplified tax regimes may be sorting, reporting, or rent capture by incumbents rather than net entry. That is a broader and more interesting claim.

2. **Better connection to entrepreneurial constraints.**  
   The paper hints that taxes are not the binding constraint; that should become central. If the paper can position itself as evidence on the hierarchy of constraints facing entrepreneurs, it becomes bigger than a tax-policy paper.

3. **A more compelling outcome architecture.**  
   Right now the main outcome is enterprise count in the 0–9 employee bin. That feels a bit mechanical and somewhat distant from the headline claim “created no new firms.” Strategically, the paper would be bigger if it could foreground outcomes that map more directly to entry and firm composition—business births, registrations, survival, reallocation across size classes, or formalization margins. Even if these are supplementary or imperfect, they would help the paper feel like it is truly about entrepreneurship rather than just one aggregate count series.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

### Closest neighbors

- **Guner, Ventura, and Xu (2008)** on size-dependent policies and misallocation.
- **Garicano, Lelarge, and Van Reenen (2016)** on firm size distortions and tax/social policy thresholds.
- **Djankov et al. (2010)** on corporate taxes and entrepreneurship/business density across countries.
- Potentially also **Bachas and Soto / Bachas et al.** on small taxpayer regimes, informality, and administrative simplification.

If I were handling this for AER, I would also expect the authors to know the public finance / development literature on **presumptive taxation, simplified regimes, and formalization**, not just bunching.

### How should the paper position itself relative to those neighbors?

**Build on them, don’t attack them.** The right move is:
- bunching papers show firms respond around thresholds;
- misallocation papers show such policies distort firm size and organization;
- tax-and-entry papers suggest taxes may matter for entrepreneurship in aggregate;
- this paper asks whether the policy rationale most often offered by governments—encouraging entry—actually shows up in a major real-world reform.

That is a useful bridge. The paper should present itself as testing the external validity of a common policy claim, not as “refuting” the existing literature.

### Is the paper currently positioned too narrowly or too broadly?

A bit too narrowly in data/method terms, and a bit too broadly in rhetorical terms.

- **Too narrow** because much of the introduction reads like a Romania + Eurostat + DiD application.
- **Too broad** because phrases like “simplified tax regimes may not stimulate entry” are broader than the evidence currently supports.

The sweet spot is: **“Romania offers an extreme and policy-relevant test of whether expanding simplified small-firm taxation stimulates net firm entry.”**

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?

It seems underconnected to:
- **development/public finance** work on presumptive taxation and small taxpayer regimes,
- **informality/formalization** literature,
- **entrepreneurship and business dynamism** literature,
- potentially **administrative burden / compliance-cost** literature.

The current references skew toward threshold and tax-distortion papers. That is necessary but not sufficient. This paper should also be in conversation with the literature on why firms formalize, why they enter, and what administrative simplification actually buys.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?

Not quite. Right now it is having the “firm-size threshold distortion” conversation. That is adjacent, but not the highest-payoff framing.

The better conversation is:
**What actually constrains entrepreneurship, and what kinds of tax policy can plausibly relax those constraints?**

That framing opens the paper to public finance, development, and macro/IO audiences. It also makes the null more meaningful.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Governments use simplified tax regimes for small firms to promote entrepreneurship and reduce compliance burdens. Romania implemented one of the most dramatic expansions of such a regime in Europe.

### Tension

There is lots of evidence that firms respond to thresholds, but much less evidence on whether making these regimes dramatically more generous actually creates new firms. The policy rhetoric says yes; the existing literature mostly documents other margins.

### Resolution

Romania’s massive threshold expansion appears not to increase the number of micro-enterprises relative to comparable countries. Any increase in average turnover seems to reflect broader macroeconomic catch-up rather than a policy-specific response.

### Implications

The common policy justification for small-firm tax privileges—stimulating entry—may be overstated. These regimes may primarily reallocate tax liabilities, affect reporting, or benefit incumbent firms rather than expand the firm base.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

Yes, more than many papers of this type. There is a real story here. But the arc weakens in the middle because the paper keeps slipping from “important question about entrepreneurship” into “here is a null DiD on a noisy proxy outcome.” That is a strategic problem, not necessarily a scientific one.

At moments the paper also feels like a collection of defensive empirical points:
- no effect on counts,
- placebo,
- turnover patterns,
- alternative control groups,
- heterogeneity,
- appendix business demography.

The story should be more disciplined:
1. this policy was explicitly sold as entrepreneurship policy;
2. this was an extreme test case;
3. the entry margin does not move;
4. the observable action, if any, is elsewhere;
5. therefore the policy rationale deserves skepticism.

That is the story. Everything else should serve it.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?

“Romania expanded eligibility for its micro-enterprise tax regime from €65,000 to €1 million in revenue—one of the biggest small-business tax subsidies in Europe—and it apparently created no new firms.”

That is a good dinner-party fact. People will understand it instantly.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

They would lean in initially. The reform is unusually large, and the headline is provocative.

But the next question comes quickly: **“Really? No firms? Then what did firms actually do?”** If the paper cannot answer that beyond “turnover went up a bit but so did everything else,” attention may fade. The headline gets the reader in; the lack of a richer response-margin story may limit impact.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Most likely:
- Did firms bunch, split, formalize, or relabel?
- Is this really about entry, or are you too far from the relevant margin with these aggregate data?
- What does this imply about the actual purpose of these regimes?

### Is the null itself interesting?

Yes, potentially very much so. But the paper must work harder to make it feel like an informative null rather than a failed attempt to detect something.

The right argument is not “we found nothing.” It is:
- this was a large and policy-salient intervention,
- if tax simplification were a first-order lever for entry, this is where we should expect to see it,
- we do not,
- therefore the margin policymakers care about is likely not the one they are moving.

That is a meaningful null. The paper is close to making that case, but it should push harder on why this is a genuinely probative setting.

4. **Integrate the turnover result more strategically.**  
   As written, the turnover section risks feeling like a side result. It should be reframed as an attempt to understand where the policy’s effects did and did not appear. If the only positive response is a broad-based increase across all size bins, say directly: “the observed intensive-margin changes do not line up with the policy’s target margin.”

5. **Trim robustness from the main text.**  
   Some of the robustness discussion reads like a referee memo. The alternative control groups and sector heterogeneity can be stated more briefly in the text and shown in appendix unless they are central to the story.

6. **Conclusion should do more than summarize.**  
   The conclusion currently restates the result capably, but it could go one step further by articulating the paper’s broader lesson: small-firm tax privileges are often justified as entry policy, but the evidence here suggests they are better understood as incumbent-firm policy unless paired with reforms that target actual entry barriers.

### Are there buried results that should be in the main text?

If the business demography data are credible and speak more directly to births/deaths, they probably belong in the main paper, even briefly. Strategically, anything closer to true entry helps the paper’s central claim. If that result is only in the appendix, the paper is underselling one of the few things that could make the story feel less proxy-driven.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

Bluntly: in current form, this is not yet an AER paper.

### What is the gap?

Mostly **scope and ambition**, with some **framing**.

- **Not mainly an identification problem** for present purposes.
- **Not mainly a writing problem**, though the writing could sharpen.
- The bigger issue is that the paper currently offers one clean, interesting null from fairly aggregated data in one country episode. That is publishable somewhere, but AER typically wants either:
  - a broader empirical reach,
  - a more decisive mechanism story,
  - a more general conceptual contribution,
  - or an undeniably first-order fact established with unusually compelling evidence.

Right now the paper has a nice first-order question and an intriguing extreme-case setting, but the evidence still feels too indirect and too narrow to fully carry the ambition of the claim.

### Is it a framing problem?

Partly. The story should be “what margins do small-firm tax subsidies actually move?” rather than “Romania DiD on micro-enterprise counts.” That would help.

### Is it a scope problem?

Yes. The paper needs either:
- more direct entry outcomes,
- more evidence on alternative margins of adjustment,
- or broader comparative evidence showing Romania is not a one-off.

### Is it a novelty problem?

Moderately. The general idea that size-dependent tax regimes distort behavior without obviously creating much real activity is not new. What is new here is the focus on the extensive margin in an extreme policy episode. That novelty is real, but probably not enough on its own for AER unless the paper can deepen it.

### Is it an ambition problem?

Yes. The paper feels competent but safe. It is asking a big question with a relatively modest empirical architecture. For AER, it needs either a bigger empirical canvas or a sharper reconceptualization of the policy debate.

### Single most impactful piece of advice

**Rebuild the paper around the broader claim that simplified small-firm tax regimes may move reporting and incumbents more than entrepreneurial entry, and bring in more direct evidence on those alternative margins so the paper becomes about the economics of entrepreneurship policy—not just one null DiD in Romania.**
