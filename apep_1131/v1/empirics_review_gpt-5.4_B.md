## 1. Idea Fidelity

The paper pursues the broad spirit of the manifest: it studies whether state administrative capacity moderated UI payment delays during the Great Recession, uses BTQ timeliness data, ASPEP staffing, and a Bartik-style instrument based on pre-recession industry composition, and frames the contribution around “hollow state” capacity constraints. So the central research question is preserved.

That said, several key elements of the original idea were either weakened or altered in ways that matter for identification and contribution. First, the manifest emphasized a **state-month (or quarter) panel** using BTQ timeliness at high frequency, but the paper collapses to **state-year**, giving only 343 observations and losing much of the within-recession variation that was supposed to identify the capacity crunch. Second, the manifest proposed an instrument built around **predicted claims shocks interacted with pre-recession UI administrative capacity**; in the paper, the claims effect is estimated by 2SLS, but the main capacity result is presented as a **reduced-form interaction of the Bartik shock with a generic state-government staffing measure**, not a fully articulated IV design for the interaction. Third, the administrative capacity measure in the manifest was closer to **UI-specific staffing per covered employees**, whereas the paper uses **total state government FTE per 1,000 private workers**, a much noisier proxy. Finally, the manifest highlighted downstream outcomes such as consumption, SNAP, and recipiency; these are absent, so the paper stops short of showing meaningful consequences of delays for households or the stabilizer function.

In short, the paper follows the idea at a high level, but it does not fully implement the original identification strategy or data design, and those deviations substantially weaken the causal claims.

## 2. Summary

This paper asks whether state UI agencies with thinner pre-recession administrative capacity were less able to maintain first-payment timeliness when recession-driven claims surged. Using a Bartik instrument for state claims pressure, the paper finds no statistically precise average causal effect of claims on timeliness, but reports that the reduced-form relationship between predicted claims shocks and delays is more negative in thinly staffed states.

The topic is important and potentially publishable in principle, but in its current form the paper does not yet deliver a convincing causal contribution. The central result is a heterogeneous reduced-form pattern built on a coarse proxy for UI capacity, with unresolved concerns about pre-trends, exclusion, and the mismatch between the stated mechanism and the estimating equation.

## 3. Essential Points

1. **The main result is not yet a coherent causal estimate of the effect of administrative capacity on delays.**  
   The paper’s headline claim is about capacity moderating the effect of claims surges, but the main interaction specification is reduced form:
   \[
   \text{Timeliness}_{st} = \gamma_1 B_{st} + \gamma_2(B_{st}\times \text{Thinness}_s)+...
   \]
   This does not cleanly identify the causal effect of claims under different capacity levels unless the interaction itself is embedded in a proper IV framework and interpreted accordingly. As written, the paper moves between (i) a null 2SLS effect of claims, (ii) a significant reduced-form Bartik-by-thinness interaction, and (iii) a substantive claim about “staffing-driven capacity constraints.” Those are not the same object. The authors need to specify and estimate a proper heterogeneous-IV design, clearly state the estimand, and reconcile it with the null average 2SLS effect.

2. **The administrative capacity measure is too weakly tied to the mechanism.**  
   The paper uses total state government FTE per 1,000 private workers as “administrative thinness.” That is a very broad measure and may proxy for many state characteristics—government size, fiscal preferences, public-sector unionization, rurality, baseline service quality, or political ideology—rather than UI processing capacity. Since the contribution hinges on the capacity mechanism, this is a major issue. The paper needs a more UI-relevant measure (UI staff, labor department staff, workforce agency staffing, staff per covered worker, or pre-recession BTQ workload/staff ratios), or at minimum much stronger validation that this broad measure predicts actual UI processing resources.

3. **The identification is not persuasive given the pre-trend concern and the coarse annual design.**  
   The paper acknowledges a borderline-significant pre-period event-study coefficient and then downplays it because other coefficients are noisy. But with only annual data from 2006–2012, the design is already underpowered and unable to show the timing one would want for a recession-capacity mechanism. Moreover, the Bartik instrument may shift states along dimensions other than claims pressure—especially if exposed industries differ systematically in claim complexity, separations quality, or contemporaneous state fiscal stress. Without higher-frequency data, richer pre-trend evidence, and controls or tests for confounding channels, the exclusion restriction is not compelling.

## 4. Suggestions

The paper has a promising question and some interesting descriptive patterns. I would encourage the authors to substantially rework it around a sharper design rather than trying to sell the current estimates too aggressively.

First, **move back to the higher-frequency BTQ panel** that motivated the project. The monthly BTQ data appear to exist and are likely essential here. A state-month panel would greatly increase power, permit more convincing event-study evidence, and let you exploit the timing of the claims surge rather than averaging over calendar years. It would also let you align claims, workload, and timeliness more plausibly: annual claims are a blunt treatment for a process that operates week-to-week. Even a quarterly panel would be a major improvement over seven annual observations per state.

Second, **tighten the treatment and capacity concepts so that the empirical object matches the theory.** Right now the paper alternates among “claims surges,” “capacity erosion,” and “claims per staff,” but these are distinct mechanisms. The natural treatment here is arguably not just claims, but **claims pressure relative to administrative capacity**. If possible, construct a direct workload measure such as initial claims or first-payment workload per UI-relevant employee, and instrument the claims component with the Bartik shock. If UI-specific staffing data are unavailable annually, even a pre-period UI office staffing proxy or labor department staffing measure would be preferable to total state government FTE. A compelling paper here likely needs either a more targeted capacity proxy or a better validation exercise showing that your proxy correlates strongly with actual UI processing inputs.

Third, **reformulate the heterogeneous-effect design more carefully.** If the argument is that thin states are less able to absorb exogenous claims pressure, the natural specification is a 2SLS with endogenous claims interacted with pre-determined thinness, instrumented by Bartik and Bartik × thinness. Then you can interpret the interaction as heterogeneity in the causal effect of claims by capacity. At present, the reduced-form interaction may be interesting, but it is not enough to support the stronger language in the abstract and conclusion. If the heterogeneous 2SLS is too noisy, that itself is informative and should lead to a more modest claim.

Fourth, **probe the exclusion restriction more directly.** The instrument is based on industry exposure, but industries likely differ not only in layoff incidence but also in claim complexity, temporary layoff patterns, union representation, and employer reporting quality. Construction and manufacturing, which receive large Rotemberg weights, may generate claims that are easier or harder to process independent of volume. You should therefore test whether Bartik exposure predicts outcomes that should not move through agency congestion—for example, pre-recession timeliness, states’ non-UI administrative performance, or claim denial/appeals patterns if available. If available, controlling for state-specific recession severity measures, balanced-budget stress, or contemporaneous public-employment cuts would help clarify whether the Bartik is really isolating claims pressure rather than broader fiscal distress.

Fifth, **take the pre-trend evidence more seriously.** A borderline pre-period coefficient is not fatal, but in a paper whose main contribution is causal identification, it cannot be brushed aside. With monthly data, show dynamic effects from well before 2008 through recovery, ideally with confidence intervals that make the timing transparent. Consider adding state-specific linear trends, region-by-year effects, or pre-period controls interacted with recession years. If the result is sensitive to those choices, that should be reported honestly.

Sixth, **improve the interpretation of magnitudes and signs.** The baseline 2SLS estimate is positive and imprecise; the thin-state split is near zero and the thick-state estimate is positive. Those results do not naturally support the paper’s substantive claims. At present the discussion leans heavily on a significant interaction while treating the rest as “informative nulls.” That reads as too selective. A more credible framing would be: average effects are hard to detect; there is suggestive evidence of heterogeneous reduced-form deterioration in thinner states; the paper documents this pattern but cannot yet pin down the full causal mechanism. That is a narrower but more defensible contribution.

Seventh, **validate the main outcome more thoroughly.** BTQ timeliness is an excellent source, but readers will want reassurance that changes in 14-day timeliness represent meaningful delay rather than reporting artifacts or shifts in case mix. Show results for the full delay distribution if possible (7/14/21/28+ day bins), and report whether the deterioration reflects a shift from very fast to moderately fast processing or a true rise in long delays. You might also scale by BTQ workload and examine whether delays are concentrated at the height of the claims surge.

Eighth, **bring in at least one downstream outcome if the paper wants to claim implications for the safety net’s effectiveness.** The manifest proposed SNAP enrollment, consumption, or recipiency. I agree these may be difficult with this sample, but without them the final paragraphs overreach. Even a reduced-form exercise showing that states with larger Bartik × thinness exposure had larger increases in SNAP take-up or lower UI recipiency would materially strengthen the policy significance. If those analyses are too noisy, the paper should trim the welfare claims.

Ninth, **be more careful with modern shift-share inference and exposition.** The paper cites the right literature, but the implementation is not transparent enough for readers to evaluate. You should report the exact industry list, the distribution of Rotemberg weights, whether inference follows BHJ/Adao et al. logic or only state clustering, and how sensitive results are to excluding influential sectors. Given the small number of states and concentration of weights, this is not a cosmetic issue. Also, the Bartik summary statistic in Table 1 is oddly reported as mean 0.0, sd 0.0, min -0.1, max 0.0; that suggests either excessive rounding or a construction/reporting problem that must be fixed.

Tenth, **clean up the internal consistency of the paper.** There are several points where the narrative and tables do not line up perfectly. For example, the paper says the interaction implies a one-SD demand shock reduces timeliness by 3.1 pp in below-median staffed states, but the specification underlying that calculation is not fully shown. The paper also mentions wild cluster bootstrap confidence intervals in notes, but none are reported. Since this is a short paper, precision of presentation matters a lot.

Overall, I think the topic is strong and worth pursuing. But for an AER: Insights-style contribution, the bar is that the paper deliver a clean causal design and a result whose interpretation is not ambiguous. Right now the evidence is suggestive rather than persuasive. A revised version built on monthly data, a UI-specific capacity proxy, and a properly specified heterogeneous IV design could become a meaningful paper.
