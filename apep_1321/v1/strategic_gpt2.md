## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper studies a striking policy shock: Ghana’s 2019 revocation of hundreds of microfinance licenses at once, arguably one of the largest episodes of financial intermediary destruction in Africa. The core question is whether wiping out a large share of the microfinance sector depressed local economic activity, or whether alternative digital finance—especially mobile money—made local economies more resilient than standard banking-fragility logic would suggest.

A busy economist should care because the paper sits at the intersection of development finance, financial regulation, and fintech substitution: when traditional intermediaries disappear, does the real economy contract, or can digital finance act as a backstop?

### Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Not quite. The current opening gives the event and then immediately leans on “conventional wisdom,” but the real hook is sharper than that: this is not just another paper about bank closures; it is a paper about whether digital finance changes the consequences of financial sector collapse. The introduction presently treats the null as the result and mobile money as a post hoc interpretation. For AER positioning, the opening should foreground the broader question: **what determines whether financial intermediation shocks transmit to the real economy in low-income settings?**

### The pitch the paper should have

Here is the version the first two paragraphs should say:

> Financial intermediary failures are usually presumed to damage local economies by cutting off credit, payments, and savings services. But in many developing countries, households now have access to an alternative financial infrastructure—mobile money—that may decouple local economic activity from the health of traditional intermediaries.
>
> This paper studies that possibility using Ghana’s 2019 mass revocation of hundreds of microfinance licenses, one of the largest financial sector clean-ups in Sub-Saharan Africa. I ask whether districts more exposed to the microfinance collapse experienced larger declines in economic activity, and I find little evidence that they did. The result suggests that the real effects of intermediary destruction depend on the availability of digital substitutes, with implications for both financial regulation and how economists think about financial resilience in developing economies.

That is a cleaner AER story: not “here is a null on microfinance closures,” but “here is evidence that digital finance may alter the macro-real consequences of financial intermediation shocks.”

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper’s contribution is to show that a very large destruction of microfinance institutions in Ghana did not produce detectable declines in local economic activity, suggesting that digital finance may buffer economies against shocks to traditional financial intermediation.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest papers?

Only partially. The paper distinguishes itself from classic bank-closure / credit-supply papers by setting the shock in a low-income, mobile-money-rich environment, but the differentiation is still too generic. Right now the reader gets: “prior papers find negative real effects; I find a null in Ghana.” That is not yet enough. The contribution needs to be differentiated along two margins:

1. **Type of intermediary**: this is about microfinance, not banks.
2. **State of technological substitution**: this is about an economy with widespread mobile money, not a conventional banking system.

Those distinctions are present, but they are not yet doing enough work in the framing.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a gap in a literature?

It is mixed, but too often framed as filling a gap in the literature (“first causal estimate in Sub-Saharan Africa,” “contributes to nascent literature”). That is weaker.

The stronger framing is about the world:

- When regulators shut down weak intermediaries, does the local economy suffer?
- Does digital finance reduce the real consequences of institutional failure?
- Has mobile money changed the transmission of financial shocks in developing countries?

Those are world questions. The paper should lean much harder into them.

### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what’s new?

At present, they might say: “It’s a DiD on Ghana’s microfinance cleanup with null effects on lights, maybe because of mobile money.”

That is not fatal, but it is not a top-journal reaction either. The “maybe because of mobile money” is doing too much work without enough evidence. So the paper risks reading as “another reduced-form shock paper with a null and a speculative mechanism.”

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Most importantly: **make the paper about financial resilience and substitution, not about a single cleanup episode.** Concretely:

- A different framing: emphasize that the paper tests whether digital financial infrastructure attenuates the real effects of intermediary destruction.
- A different mechanism: the paper needs stronger evidence on substitution. District-level heterogeneity by pre-existing mobile money penetration, telecom coverage, agent networks, or urban/rural digital readiness would transform this from a null result into a more affirmative statement.
- A different comparison: if possible, compare the Ghana episode to settings or sectors where no such substitute existed, or compare more versus less digitally prepared places within Ghana.
- A different outcome variable: nighttime lights is broad, but it is poorly matched to microfinance-intensive margins. Outcomes closer to household liquidity, enterprise turnover, transaction activity, or local market participation would make the story more persuasive and less vulnerable to “you are measuring the wrong thing.”

As written, the paper’s contribution is real but modest. To be bigger, it has to either nail the substitution mechanism or more convincingly show that this is a fundamental statement about how fintech changes shock transmission.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Before this paper, the natural prior is that destroying a large share of local financial intermediaries should damage local economies, especially where households and firms rely on them for savings and credit.

### Tension

But Ghana in 2019 is not a standard bank-dependent setting. At the same time the microfinance sector was collapsing, mobile money was expanding rapidly. So there is a real conceptual tension: does intermediary destruction still bite when another financial technology can partially replace it?

### Resolution

The paper finds little detectable impact on district-level nighttime lights in more exposed areas.

### Implications

The implications, if true, are important:
- the real costs of regulatory cleanups may be smaller in digitally connected financial systems,
- traditional intermediation may be less indispensable than we think for local economic resilience,
- and economists should rethink how financial shocks propagate in developing economies where mobile money is widespread.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the ingredients of one, but not the execution. At present it still feels somewhat like a collection of empirical facts:
- big cleanup,
- null effect,
- mobile money rose,
- maybe substitution.

The story is there, but it is underpowered narratively because the mechanism is not integrated tightly enough into the empirical design. As a result, the paper reads like:
> “Here is a surprising null, and here is my best explanation,”
rather than
> “Here is a test of a broader theory of resilience through digital substitution.”

### What story should it be telling?

It should tell this story:

1. **Setup**: Historically, intermediary failures transmit strongly to the real economy.
2. **New environment**: In parts of the developing world, households now operate in dual financial systems—traditional institutions plus mobile money.
3. **Test case**: Ghana’s mass MFI revocation provides a natural test of whether this new architecture changes the consequences of institutional collapse.
4. **Finding**: Aggregate local activity does not fall in more exposed places.
5. **Interpretation**: This is consistent with a world in which digital rails provide resilience, even when brick-and-mortar intermediaries disappear.
6. **Implication**: The welfare and regulatory calculus around financial cleanups may be changing.

That is the AER version of the paper.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?

“Ghana wiped out roughly half its microfinance sector in one stroke, and the places most exposed do not show detectable declines in local economic activity.”

That is a good opening fact. People will pay attention.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

Initially, they would lean in. The policy event is dramatic.

But the next step matters enormously. If the presenter quickly says, “using nighttime lights, I find a null, maybe because of mobile money,” some people will start to drift. The null is interesting only if the paper can persuade the audience that:
1. the outcome is meaningful enough,
2. the null is substantively informative,
3. and the mechanism is more than conjecture.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Almost everyone will ask some version of:
- “How do you know this isn’t just the wrong outcome?”
- “Do you actually show substitution to mobile money?”
- “Were these MFIs economically important, or were many already moribund?”

Those are strategic questions, not referee nitpicks. The current draft does not fully preempt them.

### Is the null result itself interesting?

Yes, potentially very much so. A null can be highly interesting when:
- the prior strongly pointed the other way,
- the shock is genuinely large,
- and the null revises a broader belief.

This paper has that potential. But at the moment, the case is not made as forcefully as it needs to be. The null sometimes feels like “we don’t see much in lights,” rather than “this event forces us to rethink the dependence of local economies on traditional microfinance intermediation.”

It needs to work harder to establish why this null is informative rather than merely inconclusive.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The gap is mostly a mix of **framing problem**, **scope problem**, and **ambition problem**.

### Framing problem
The paper’s best idea is not “mass MFI destruction had no effect in Ghana.” The best idea is:
**digital financial infrastructure may change the real consequences of financial intermediary failure.**
That idea is there, but not yet fully claimed.

### Scope problem
The evidence is thin relative to the breadth of the implied claim. One aggregate proxy outcome plus speculative mechanism is probably not enough for AER-level excitement. The paper needs either:
- outcomes closer to the economic margins affected by microfinance,
- or heterogeneity that more directly tests the substitute-infrastructure hypothesis.

### Novelty problem
The event is novel. The substantive finding is potentially novel. But the empirical architecture is familiar, and absent stronger mechanism, the paper risks seeming incremental: “shock + DiD + null + plausible story.”

### Ambition problem
The paper is careful and sensible, but somewhat safe. It under-claims conceptually while over-claiming descriptively (“largest in Sub-Saharan Africa”) relative to what the evidence can carry. AER papers usually do the reverse: they ask a large question and then discipline it with compelling evidence.

### Single most impactful piece of advice

**Rebuild the paper around testing whether digital financial infrastructure attenuates the real effects of intermediary destruction, and add evidence that speaks directly to that mechanism.**

If the author can only change one thing, it should be that. Without stronger mechanism, this is a well-executed null around a dramatic event. With stronger mechanism, it could become a paper about how fintech changes the transmission of financial shocks.
