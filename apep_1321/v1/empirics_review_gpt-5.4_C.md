1. **Idea Fidelity**

The paper pursues the broad idea in the manifest, but in a materially weakened form. The core question is preserved: did Ghana’s 2019 mass MFI license revocations reduce local economic activity, exploiting geographic variation in exposure? However, several central elements of the original design are dropped or diluted.

First, the manifest proposed a district-level, monthly VIIRS design with roughly 100+ time periods; the paper instead uses annual Black Marble data from 2014–2023. That is a major loss of identifying variation and substantially reduces the credibility of the event-study and power claims. Second, the manifest envisioned district-level treatment intensity based on revocations per district population; the paper assigns region-level treatment to districts, leaving only 16 effectively treated clusters. That is not a minor implementation detail—it changes the design from a district-level dose-response DiD to something much closer to region-level treatment with district-level outcomes. Third, the manifest proposed secondary welfare evidence from DHS/Findex and explicit tests of substitution to mobile money. The paper does not actually estimate those outcomes; instead it infers substitution from national changes in Findex aggregates. That is suggestive background, not evidence.

So: the paper follows the spirit of the original idea, but not the stronger empirical design that made the idea attractive. The current draft is best viewed as an exploratory null-result paper based on coarse treatment variation, not yet the compelling district-level causal study envisioned in the manifest.

2. **Summary**

This paper studies whether Ghana’s 2019 mass revocation of microfinance licenses reduced local economic activity. Using a dose-response DiD with district-year nighttime lights and region-level revocation intensity, the paper finds no statistically significant effect on night lights and interprets the null as evidence that mobile money provided a “digital safety net.”

The question is interesting and important. But the current empirical implementation does not yet support the paper’s strongest claims: identification is weak, inference is fragile because treatment varies at only 16 regional units, and the mobile-money mechanism is asserted rather than demonstrated.

3. **Essential Points**

**1. The identifying variation is much weaker than the paper acknowledges, and the pre-trend evidence is not reassuring.**  
Treatment is assigned at the regional level, not the district level, so the effective number of treated units is 16, not 260. Moreover, treatment intensity is plainly correlated with urbanization, baseline luminosity, and likely preexisting growth trends. The significant 2014 event-study coefficient and the significant placebo DiD with a 2017 cutoff are not small warning signs; they are first-order threats to the design. The paper currently says this “reinforces” the null. I do not agree. Differential trends can bias estimates in either direction once treatment is measured coarsely and correlated with latent growth. At a minimum, the paper needs a more credible trend-adjusted design—e.g., region-specific trends, matched comparisons among observationally similar districts, or a design restricted to more comparable southern districts.

**2. The inference is not yet appropriate for the effective level of treatment assignment.**  
With 16 regions, ordinary cluster-robust standard errors are unreliable. The randomization inference is a useful step, but the implementation needs to be much more transparent and better aligned with the design. What exactly is permuted: raw intensity ranks across 16 regions, or full treatment vectors? Are permutations restricted to preserve the south/north structure or urbanization balance? Why 1,000 draws only? Also, the appendix mentions wild cluster bootstrap, but no results are reported in the paper. Given the small number of treated clusters and the fact that outcomes are measured at district level while treatment is at region level, I would want RI and wild-cluster-bootstrap p-values presented in the main tables, not as an afterthought.

**3. The paper overinterprets a noisy null as evidence of mobile-money resilience.**  
The estimated effects are not especially precise relative to plausible economic magnitudes. A coefficient of -0.076 per unit of intensity is not obviously trivial when the treatment range is roughly 1.5 units from min to max; that implies a min-to-max effect around 11 percent in log lights. That is not negligible. The confidence interval allows economically meaningful negative effects. More importantly, the mechanism claim rests on national Findex trends from 2017 to 2021, which cannot identify substitution in more exposed districts. The result the paper currently delivers is narrower: no robust detectable effect on annual district-level nighttime lights. It does not yet show local economic resilience, and it certainly does not show that mobile money caused that resilience.

4. **Suggestions**

The paper can still become useful, but it needs to be reframed and strengthened empirically.

First, I would strongly encourage the authors to return to the original higher-frequency design. Monthly VIIRS would transform the paper. With annual data, you have only five pre-periods and four post-periods; that is simply thin for a stagger-free event around a single national policy. Monthly data would let you examine immediate dynamics, pre-trends more credibly, and treatment-year timing around May and August 2019. It would also help separate the revocations from the COVID period, which is otherwise entangled with your post-treatment window. Right now, with 2020–2023 as the main post period, the estimated “treatment effect” is a mixture of revocations, pandemic shocks, fee waivers for mobile money, and broader macroeconomic changes.

Second, the treatment needs much sharper measurement. The current region-level intensity is a serious limitation. If the Bank of Ghana or revocation notices can be linked to institution branch locations, even imperfectly, you could construct district exposure based on revoked branches, office addresses, or preexisting MFI presence. Even a count of licensed MFIs by district before the purge would be preferable. If district assignment is impossible, then I would suggest being fully honest that this is a region-level treatment design and aggregating the analysis to the region-year level as the primary specification. The current district-level regressions create an illusion of much more variation than actually exists.

Third, you need a better strategy for comparability. The stark baseline differences between high- and low-treatment places are not incidental; they are central. Greater Accra and Ashanti are different economies from the northern regions along almost every dimension that matters for trends in lights. One constructive way forward is to restrict the comparison set to districts in the southern and middle belts, or to estimate effects within a narrower support where baseline lights, population density, and urban status overlap. Another is to interact baseline characteristics with time or include region-specific linear trends. These are not perfect fixes, but they are better than relying on a simple parallel-trends claim that the placebo tests already undermine.

Fourth, the paper should present magnitudes in a more economically interpretable way. The current standardized effect size of -0.016 is not informative because it scales by the standard deviation of the outcome in a way that obscures the policy meaning. Instead, report implied effects for a move from the 25th to the 75th percentile of exposure, or from a low-exposure region to Greater Accra. Also report confidence intervals for those contrasts. As written, the paper oscillates between calling the estimate “small” and admitting a region-level estimate of about -0.166, which is not so small. The reader needs one coherent statement of what effects are ruled out.

Fifth, be much more careful with the outcome construction. Logging mean radiance with zeros handled by adding one or truncating at -4.605 deserves explicit discussion. In many district-level NTL applications, means are driven by a few bright pixels and are sensitive to top-coding and outliers. I would recommend showing robustness to alternative outcomes: log(1+mean radiance), median radiance, share of lit pixels, total radiance, and winsorized measures. If MFI effects are concentrated in peri-urban commercial corridors, extensive-margin light measures may be more informative than simple means.

Sixth, the interpretation of the positive level effect in Table 2 should be removed or tightened substantially. Saying it “reflects the continued growth of already-bright urban districts rather than any treatment effect” is speculative and undercuts the design. If levels and logs tell different stories, that is itself evidence that the results are fragile to functional form. Better to diagnose why: nonlinearity, heteroskedasticity, composition of bright districts, or mechanical trends.

Seventh, the mechanism section needs actual evidence or much weaker claims. The cleanest route would be to exploit district or regional variation in mobile-money agent density, transaction volumes, telecom coverage, or preexisting mobile-money adoption. If more exposed places with better mobile-money infrastructure were less affected, that would support the substitution hypothesis. In the absence of such evidence, the abstract and title should be toned down. “The Digital Safety Net” is too strong for a paper that does not directly test the mechanism. A more accurate title would emphasize the null result on nighttime lights after MFI closures.

Eighth, consider adding outcomes closer to the purported mechanism. The manifest mentioned DHS and Findex. Those are not ideal for district-level causal inference, but even region-level heterogeneity in borrowing, saving, account ownership, or source of credit would be more informative than national descriptive trends. If household survey power is limited, acknowledge that and position them as suggestive corroboration rather than causal evidence.

Ninth, the paper should discuss the composition of the revoked institutions much more carefully. The Bank of Ghana revoked both insolvent institutions and firms that had already ceased operations. If many revoked MFIs were already dormant, then “destruction of intermediation” is overstated. This matters enormously for the plausibility of the null. A purge of dead institutions should have little effect; a purge of active lenders should. The paper’s headline framing implicitly assumes the latter, but the institutional details suggest the former may be partly true. If possible, separate active from already non-operational institutions, and separate microfinance from savings-and-loans entities.

Finally, I would reframe the contribution more modestly. There is still a publishable paper here if the authors present it as evidence that a dramatic regulatory cleanup did not produce detectable aggregate effects in annual district lights, while emphasizing the substantial limits of the design and the outcome measure. What the current draft should not claim is a well-powered null proving local economic resilience due to digital substitution. The underlying question is excellent; the current evidence is simply not yet strong enough for that conclusion.
