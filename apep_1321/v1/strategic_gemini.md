### 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
This paper examines the 2019 "clean-up" of Ghana’s microfinance sector—the largest mass revocation of financial licenses in African history—to test whether the sudden destruction of credit intermediaries actually harms local economic activity. Using satellite nighttime lights and a dose-response difference-in-differences design, the author finds a precisely estimated null effect. The paper argues that in modern developing economies, digital financial infrastructure (mobile money) has become a sufficiently robust substitute to absorb the shock of traditional institutional collapse.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this pitch quite well in the first three paragraphs. However, it leads with the "institutional clean-up" (policy) rather than the "substitution" (theory).
*   **The pitch it should have:** "Does the destruction of financial intermediaries still matter in an era of ubiquitous fintech? Using the largest mass microfinance closure in African history as a natural experiment, I show that the disappearance of half the country's lenders had zero detectable impact on local economic activity. This suggests that mobile money has moved from a niche tool to a systemic 'digital safety net' capable of replacing traditional institutional credit during crises."

---

### 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**One-sentence contribution:** The paper identifies a systemic boundary for the "real effects of credit shocks" literature by showing that digital financial alternatives can neutralize the impact of mass intermediary destruction.

*   **Clarity:** It is clearly differentiated from the "MFI impact" literature (Banerjee et al.) by looking at the *destruction* of the industry rather than its introduction, and from the "bank shock" literature (Bernanke; Khwaja & Mian) by focusing on the fintech era.
*   **Framing:** It is currently framed as a "well-powered null." This is dangerous. It should be framed as a "Resilience Result."
*   **Growth:** To make the contribution bigger, the author needs to move beyond the national aggregate Global Findex data. Even one district-level proxy for mobile money (e.g., cell tower density or agent locations) would turn this from "something else probably happened" to "here is the digital mechanism at work."

---

### 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
*   **Neighbors:** Bernanke (1983) on credit shocks; Jack & Suri (2014) on mobile money; Banerjee et al. (2015) on MFI efficacy; Henderson et al. (2012) on NTL.
*   **Positioning:** It should more aggressively "attack" the Bernanke/Khwaja-Mian consensus by arguing those findings are contingent on a pre-digital technological regime.
*   **Narrow/Broad:** Currently a bit niche (African microfinance). It needs to speak to the **General Interest** question of "Fintech and Macroeconomic Stability."
*   **Missing:** It should connect to the "Leapfrogging" literature and the "Shadow Banking" literature. Are these MFIs just being replaced by an even less regulated digital shadow?

---

### 4. NARRATIVE ARC
*   **Setup:** Conventional wisdom says if you kill the lenders, the economy dies.
*   **Tension:** Ghana killed *half* its lenders in a day, yet the lights stayed on.
*   **Resolution:** Fintech infrastructure provides a "shadow" resilience that traditional metrics and theories haven't fully accounted for.
*   **Implications:** Financial regulation and "clean-ups" are less risky—and traditional MFIs perhaps less essential—than we previously thought.

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong but the "Implications" section is the weakest. It feels like the author is afraid of their own null result. They need to lean into the radical implication: if you can delete 347 MFIs and nothing happens, what does that say about the marginal value of MFIs?

---

### 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
*   **Lead Fact:** "Ghanaian regulators shut down 347 lenders in 24 hours, and you can't even see it in the satellite data."
*   **Reaction:** People will lean in. It's a "dog that didn't bark" story.
*   **Follow-up:** "Is it because MFIs didn't matter in the first place, or because Mobile Money is that good?" This is the billion-dollar question the paper must address more deeply.

---

### 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
*   **Front-load the mechanism:** The Global Findex data on mobile money growth (currently on page 10) needs to be in the Introduction and perhaps a stylized-fact Figure 1. 
*   **Robustness:** Move the "excluding Greater Accra/Ashanti" to a main results table to prove the null isn't just driven by rural noise.
*   **NTL Limitations:** The author is too apologetic about Nighttime Lights. NTL is a standard AER tool. State the limitations once and move on.

---

### 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The gap is **Ambition**. A null result in a single country is a tough sell for the AER unless it fundamentally changes how we think about a global phenomenon. 

**Single most impactful piece of advice:** 
Stop framing this as a "Policy Evaluation of Ghana’s Clean-up" and start framing it as "The Death of the Credit Shock: How Fintech Neutralizes Institutional Collapse." You must find a way to bring in *any* sub-national data on mobile money—even if it's just geocoded agent locations—to turn the suggested mechanism into a proven one.
