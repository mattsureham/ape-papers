**Idea Fidelity**

The manuscript tracks the original idea closely. It exploits the Bank of Ghana’s May–August 2019 mass revocation of microfinance (and savings-and-loans) licenses and uses district-level nighttime lights as the aggregate outcome. The proposed dose-response difference-in-differences design with treatment intensity defined by revoked MFIs per 100,000 regional population is implemented, and the theme of digital-financial substitution (via mobile money) as a mechanism is retained. The paper therefore covers the key elements set out in the manifest. A few areas—most notably the reliance on regional-level treatment intensity and the handling of parallel trends—could be fleshed out further to enhance the fidelity of the implementation.

**Summary**

The paper asks whether the sudden destruction of 347 microfinance institutions in Ghana in 2019 had measurable effects on local economic activity. Using a dose-response difference-in-differences design across 260 districts and satellite-based nighttime lights from 2014–2023, the author finds no statistically significant post-treatment decline in economic activity, even for the highest-intensity regions. The results are interpreted through the auxiliary observation that mobile-money account ownership rose sharply in the same period, suggesting that digital financial services may have substituted for the lost microfinance capacity.

**Essential Points**

1. **Credibility of parallel-trends with urban treatment variation.** Treatment intensity is strongly correlated with urbanization—Greater Accra and Ashanti account for most revoked licenses and had very different growth trajectories than the sparsely populated north even before 2019. The event study and placebo test flag a significant pre-trend at 2014 and over the 2014–17 window. Relying on a standard TWFE model without district-specific trends risks conflating the treatment effect with underlying urbanization patterns. The paper needs to demonstrate that the results survive accounting for differential trends (e.g., district-specific linear trends, pre-period adjustments, or a more flexible matching strategy) or provide stronger evidence that the 2014 divergence is a one-off that does not bias the estimate.

2. **Limited variation in treatment assignment and clustering.** Treatment intensity is assigned at the 16-region level, yet the regressions exploit 260 districts. This means the effective variation is only across 16 units, which raises both statistical power and inference concerns. Standard errors clustered at the regional level with just 16 clusters are fragile, and even the randomization inference presented (permuting treatment at the region level) does not fully address the issue because treatment assignment is not random conditional on observables. The paper should either obtain more granular treatment data (e.g., district-level counts of revoked MFIs, if available) or adopt an estimation approach that acknowledges the limited number of treated units—such as wild cluster bootstrap/t-test or a region-level analysis with fewer, but properly powered, observations.

3. **Interpretation of the null effect and alternative explanations.** The central claim is that mobile money substituted for the destroyed MFIs, ensuring no aggregate impact. However, the mechanism is only loosely supported: the paper cites national-level trends in Findex data without tying them to the timing or geography of the revocations. Without district-specific (or at least regional) mobile-money adoption trends, the substitution story is speculative. Moreover, a null result could also stem from measurement issues (e.g., nighttime lights may not capture informal-sector disruptions) or from the fact that many revoked MFIs were already insolvent and inactive. The authors need to strengthen the narrative by (i) documenting whether the revoked MFIs were operational up to 2019, (ii) showing that districts with more closures saw differential growth in mobile-money usage or financial inclusion (even at a proxy level, e.g., mobile-money agent density), and (iii) discussing and ideally ruling out the alternative explanation that the observable impact was too small to detect because the affected borrowers represented a tiny share of district-level economic activity.

**Suggestions**

1. **Addressing pre-trends more fully.** The 2014 pre-trend and 2017 placebo are worrying. One way to address this is to reestimate the main model with district-specific linear (or even quadratic) trends; this controls for differential urban growth trajectories. Another option is to implement a synthetic control or matching approach where high-intensity regions are matched to low-intensity ones based on pre-2019 nighttime light growth and other covariates, then compare subsequent trajectories. Showing robustness to these specifications would greatly increase confidence in the causal interpretation.

2. **Enhancing treatment measurement.** The treatment variable currently assigns the same intensity to all districts within a region because data appear to be region-level. If possible, construct a district-level measure—for example, by geocoding the locations of each revoked MFI branch or by allocating regional revocations proportional to the number of branches/social media activity known from pre-2019 sources. Even an approximate district-level intensity (e.g., using branch counts from regulatory filings or media reports) would provide more variation and reduce the effective number of clusters, improving statistical power and interpretability.

3. **Revisiting inference strategies.** Given that there are only 16 clusters and the treatment variable varies at that level, reliance on cluster-robust standard errors is problematic. Complement (or replace) them with a wild cluster bootstrap-t procedure and report those p-values. You might also consider aggregating the analysis to the regional level entirely (single observation per region-year) and using a permutation inference framework that tests the observed treatment assignment against all possible intensity permutations. While region-level aggregation reduces sample size, it aligns the unit of analysis with the unit of treatment (avoiding over-claimed precision) and makes inference more transparent.

4. **Expanding the mechanism analysis.** The mobile-money substitution hypothesis is attractive but underdeveloped. Consider collecting district- or region-level data on mobile-money agent density, transaction volume, or account ownership (even if from BoG reports or telecom company disclosures) to show heterogeneous adoption related to treatment. Alternatively, examine downstream outcomes linked to mobile money (e.g., utility bill payments, merchant receipts) if those data exist. If granular data are unavailable, consider additional indirect evidence: for instance, demonstrating that districts with higher treatment intensity experienced smaller declines in financial account ownership (from Findex, even if regional) would support the substitution channel.

5. **Broader outcomes and heterogeneity.** The paper rests on aggregate nighttime lights. Consider adding secondary outcomes (e.g., nighttime lights volatility, measures of night-time area lit, luminosity thresholds) or combining with household/survey data (DHS 2022, Findex) to test whether welfare, borrowing, or savings behavior changed in treated districts, even if only suggestive. Furthermore, exploring heterogeneity (e.g., separating urban vs. rural districts, or high vs. low initial light intensity) could reveal whether the null effect masks differential impacts: perhaps urban districts were buffered by other financial services while peri-urban/rural districts without mobile-money infrastructure saw adverse effects.

6. **Clarifying the timing and control of confounders.** The 2019 treatment coincides with other shocks (e.g., regulatory changes, Ghana’s macroeconomic adjustments, or even COVID-related disruptions starting in 2020). Could any of these be differentially correlated with treatment intensity? The paper should discuss and, if possible, control for time-varying confounders such as district-level infrastructure investment, exposure to industrial zones, or even the rollout of electricity improvements (which could directly affect nighttime lights). If such data are not available, make explicit why they are unlikely to bias the results.

7. **Strengthening the discussion of null results.** Null findings are harder to sell. Ensure the discussion clearly distinguishes (i) absence of measurable change in nighttime lights, (ii) possible unobserved effects in informal sectors, and (iii) what the confidence intervals allow us to rule out. Providing a power calculation (e.g., the minimum detectable effect in log nighttime lights) helps readers understand the sensitivity of the analysis. The Appendix’s standardized effect sizes are useful—consider bringing a version into the main text to emphasize that even moderate effects are statistically ruled out.

In sum, the manuscript addresses an important policy question, but its causal claims require a more nuanced treatment of pre-trends, inference, and mechanism evidence. Addressing the points above would materially strengthen the paper’s credibility and contribution.
