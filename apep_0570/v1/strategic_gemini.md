---

### 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper exploits a rare, politically-induced "tax holiday" in Malaysia—the sudden abolition of a 6% VAT (GST) followed three months later by a partial reimposition of a sales tax (SST)—to test for asymmetric price adjustment. It finds that prices fell significantly when taxes were removed (55% pass-through) but rose much less when taxes returned, suggesting a "reversed asymmetry" where prices fall like rockets but rise like feathers. This is a first-order question for public finance and macroeconomics: do the benefits of temporary tax cuts actually reach consumers, and do firms use the "fog" of tax changes to permanently increase markups?

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this well in the first paragraph of the introduction, grounding the study in the "dramatic electoral surprise" that provided the exogenous shock. However, the second paragraph is a bit of a "literature dump" on Peltzman (2000). 

**The pitch the paper should have (The "AER version"):**
"Consumption tax changes are often intended to provide immediate relief to households, yet their effectiveness depends on whether firms pass these changes through to consumer prices symmetrically. We exploit a unique natural experiment in Malaysia where a 6% Goods and Services Tax was zeroed and then partially replaced three months later, providing a rare within-study comparison of downward and upward price adjustments. Contrary to the 'rockets and feathers' literature, we find evidence of reversed asymmetry: high political salience and aggressive government monitoring forced rapid price declines that were not fully reversed when taxes returned, suggesting that political economy can overrule standard market-structure drivers of price stickiness."

---

### 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:** The paper provides empirical evidence that political salience and government enforcement can reverse the canonical "rockets and feathers" pattern of price asymmetry, leading to persistent consumer welfare gains from temporary tax holidays.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It differentiates itself from Benzarti et al. (2020) by looking at a middle-income context and finding the *opposite* direction of asymmetry. 
*   **Question vs. Literature:** It currently sits somewhat in the middle. To be AER-ready, it needs to lean harder into the "Question about the World": *Under what conditions do firms lose the power to keep markups high during tax transitions?*
*   **The "Smart Economist" Test:** A reader would explain this as: "It’s the Malaysia paper that shows if the government screams loud enough, prices actually fall faster than they rise."
*   **What would make it bigger?** The contribution would be magnified by more explicitly linking the "incomplete reimposition" to **inflation expectations** or **firm-level reputation**. If firms fear "profiteering" labels, this is a paper about the power of the "bully pulpit" in economics.

---

### 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

*   **Closest Neighbors:** Peltzman (2000) on asymmetry; Benzarti et al. (2020) on VAT pass-through; Chetty et al. (2009) on salience; Weyl & Fabinger (2013) on pass-through theory.
*   **Positioning Strategy:** It should **synthesize** the structural industrial organization view (market power) with the behavioral/political economy view (salience/enforcement). 
*   **Missing Conversations:** The paper is silent on the **macro-labor/search** literature regarding price stickiness. Why is there "hysteresis" in the price level? Connecting to the "Menu Cost" vs. "Rational Inattention" debate would broaden the audience.
*   **Right Conversation?** It is currently having a Public Finance conversation. It should also be having a **Macro-Development** conversation: how does the transition from VAT to Sales Tax (moving up/down the supply chain) affect price discovery in emerging markets?

---

### 4. NARRATIVE ARC

*   **Setup:** The world assumes prices are sticky downward (feathers) and flexible upward (rockets).
*   **Tension:** A surprise election leads to a "tax holiday" accompanied by intense government threats against "profiteering." This creates a conflict between standard firm incentives (keep the tax cut as markup) and political costs (fines/reputation).
*   **Resolution:** Prices fall 3.2% (rockets), but when the tax is reimposed, they only recover 1.9% (feathers). 
*   **Implications:** Temporary tax cuts can have permanent "ratchet effects" on the price level if implemented during periods of high salience.

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong. The "tension" is the highlight. The paper should emphasize that the "resolution" is a win for consumers but a permanent revenue loss for the state.

---

### 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

*   **The Lead Fact:** "In Malaysia, the government abolished a 6% tax and prices actually fell in 16 days—but when they brought the tax back, firms were too scared to raise prices again."
*   **Reaction:** Lean in. Every policymaker wants to know if they can "force" pass-through.
*   **Follow-up:** "Was it the competition or the threat of jail?" 

**Evaluation:** If the results are "suggestive" (p=0.075 for symmetry), the "so what" relies on the *sign* of the point estimate being the opposite of the literature's consensus. The author needs to lean into the "suggestive" nature as a "challenge to the universality of Peltzman."

---

### 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

*   **Front-loading:** The "Institutional Background" (Section 2) is excellent but a bit long. Move some of the tax history to an appendix to get to the "Rockets/Feathers" data faster.
*   **Robustness:** The "Sensitivity to Estimation Window" (Section 6.1) is crucial. It’s not just a check; it’s a finding. The fact that the 2015 GST introduction contaminates the long-run trend is a key lesson in DiD design for tax papers.
*   **Conclusion:** The conclusion is currently a summary. It should be an **extrapolation**. What does this mean for the "Green Tax" transitions or Carbon Taxes? If those are salient, will pass-through be different?

---

### 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The gap is **statistical precision** and **mechanism isolation**.

1.  **The "Suggestive" Problem:** The p-value for the symmetry test is 0.075. In the AER, "suggestive" is a hard sell for a lead result. The author needs to find a way to increase power—perhaps by using more granular (weekly) data if available, or by exploiting the 5% vs 10% SST rate variation more aggressively.
2.  **The Mechanism Problem:** Is it "Political Salience" or is it "Supply Chain Position" (VAT vs. Sales Tax)? The paper mentions this in 7.1 but needs to *test* it. If the author can show that pass-through was higher for products with more "Anti-Profiteering" enforcement actions, the paper moves from a "clean DiD" to a "major contribution."

**Single Most Impactful Advice:** Quantify the "Political Salience" or "Enforcement" channel—use news archives or government press releases to create a "salience index" by product and show that this index explains the "reversed asymmetry."
