## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper studies how consumer prices respond when a politically salient consumption tax is first removed and then partially reimposed. Using Malaysia’s 2018 switch from a 6 percent GST to a narrower SST, it asks whether prices fall as much when taxes go down as they rise when taxes come back, and finds incomplete pass-through on the way down and only suggestive evidence of asymmetry on the way back up.

A busy economist should care because this is, in principle, a rare policy episode that could speak to three big questions at once: tax incidence, asymmetric price adjustment, and whether political salience/enforcement shapes pass-through. That combination could be interesting. But the paper currently overclaims on asymmetry relative to what it actually shows, and buries the most credible contribution under too much econometric throat-clearing.

### Does the paper articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Not really. The first paragraph is vivid but too historical; the second starts broadly on pass-through, but the introduction then sprawls into a literature review before the reader gets a clean statement of the paper’s core claim and its limits. The current draft wants to be about “reversed rockets and feathers,” but the paper itself does not convincingly own that result. The stronger pitch is narrower: a politically salient tax cut in a middle-income country generated substantial but incomplete pass-through, and the same episode offers suggestive—though not definitive—evidence that upward and downward adjustment may differ.

### What the first two paragraphs should say instead

**Paragraph 1:**  
When governments cut consumption taxes, do consumers actually see lower prices—and do prices rise back just as quickly when taxes return? This paper studies Malaysia’s 2018 elimination of the 6 percent GST and its replacement three months later with a narrower SST, a rare episode in which many products experienced a sudden tax decrease followed by partial re-taxation. Because the reform was triggered by a surprise election and differed sharply across products, the episode offers an unusually clean setting to study pass-through and the symmetry of price adjustment.

**Paragraph 2:**  
Using monthly CPI data for 101 product classes, I show that the GST removal reduced consumer prices for previously taxed goods, but by much less than the full tax amount: in the preferred short window, pass-through is about 55 percent. Products later brought under the SST show only a partial price rebound, but that asymmetry is imprecisely estimated, so the strongest takeaway is not “rockets down, feathers up.” It is that tax pass-through in this salient political episode was substantial, incomplete, and potentially shaped by the public visibility of the reform.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

We know that consumption taxes are often partly passed through, but pass-through varies across settings and may be asymmetric. Most evidence comes from OECD contexts and often studies either a tax increase or a tax cut, not both in the same environment.

### Tension

Malaysia’s 2018 reform provides a rare test: a sudden, highly salient tax repeal followed quickly by partial reimposition. If prices respond differently in the two directions, that could reveal something about market power, frictions, or political salience. But the episode also involves a non-identical tax instrument on the way back up, which complicates the asymmetry claim.

### Resolution

Prices fell meaningfully after GST removal, but less than one-for-one. Prices later rose only partially for products re-covered by SST, but that upward response is noisy. The cleanest result is incomplete downward pass-through; the asymmetry result is intriguing but not conclusive.

### Implications

Tax cuts do not automatically deliver full consumer relief, even when politically salient. But salience and enforcement may matter for the direction and extent of pass-through, especially in middle-income settings.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

Serviceable, but not fully coherent. At present it reads like a collection of potentially interesting findings and caveats attached to three different papers:

- a pass-through paper,
- an asymmetry paper,
- a political enforcement paper.

The best story is:

> Malaysia’s tax reversal lets us observe price adjustment in both directions. The strongest fact is incomplete pass-through of a salient tax cut. The upward response is smaller, hinting that political salience may matter, but this is a suggestive extension rather than the main settled contribution.

That would give the paper discipline. Right now the title and abstract promise more asymmetry than the body can safely deliver.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party?

“Malaysia scrapped a very visible 6 percent VAT-type tax after a surprise election, and consumer prices fell by only about half the tax cut.”

That is the strongest, cleanest fact.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

They would lean in modestly—especially public finance and development economists. The setting is unusual and politically vivid. But if the lead is instead “we may have found reversed rockets and feathers, though the key test is not conventionally significant,” people will start checking their phones.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Probably one of these:

- “Why only half?”  
- “Is that because of market power, compliance, or measurement?”  
- “Does the same-setting up/down comparison really tell us about asymmetry given GST and SST aren’t the same tax?”  
- “Can you actually show salience/enforcement mattered?”

Those are good questions. The paper should be written to answer them in that order.

### If findings are null or modest, is that still interesting?

Yes, for the asymmetry result specifically. A null or ambiguous asymmetry finding can still be interesting if framed correctly: this setting provides a rare chance to test the claim, and the answer is “less dramatic and less universal than the rockets-and-feathers narrative suggests.” But the paper currently cannot decide whether asymmetry is a headline result or an exploratory one. It should choose the latter unless stronger evidence appears.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

At the moment, this looks more like a competent field-journal/public-finance-journal paper than an AER paper. The gap is mostly not technical; it is strategic.

### What is the main gap?

Primarily a **framing problem**, with some **ambition problem**.

- **Framing problem:** The paper has not decided what its main claim is. AER papers usually make one central conceptual move very clearly.
- **Ambition problem:** The paper settles for documenting pass-through in one event and then gestures at several broader ideas without fully cashing them out.
- **Novelty problem:** Tax pass-through from VAT/GST changes is a known literature. To rise above that, the paper needs a cleaner “why this episode changes what we know” argument.
- **Scope problem:** Less severe than the others. The paper may not need more outcomes if it can sharpen the central question. But to make the political-salience angle truly big, it would need stronger evidence on mechanism.

### What would excite the top 10 people in this field?

One of two versions:

1. **Version A: the clean public-finance paper**  
   “A rare down-then-up tax reform in a middle-income country shows that even highly salient tax cuts pass through only partially to consumers, with important implications for using tax relief as policy.”

2. **Version B: the bigger conceptual paper**  
   “Political salience fundamentally changes pass-through dynamics: tax cuts under intense public scrutiny are transmitted differently than tax increases.”  
   But this version requires much stronger mechanism evidence than the current draft has.

Right now the paper gestures at Version B while only securely delivering Version A.

### Single most impactful advice

**Stop selling the paper as a definitive reversed-asymmetry result and reframe it around the strongest contribution: a rare same-setting estimate of incomplete consumer-tax pass-through after a highly salient GST repeal, with asymmetry as suggestive secondary evidence.**
