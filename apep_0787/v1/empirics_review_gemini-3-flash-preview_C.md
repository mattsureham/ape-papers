This review evaluates the paper "Working Sick, Getting Hurt? Paid Sick Leave Mandates and Workplace Injury Rates" from the perspective of an applied econometrician.

### 1. Idea Fidelity
The paper maintains high fidelity to the original idea manifest. It successfully operationalizes the OSHA ITA data (the first study to do so for PSL), utilizes the recommended Callaway-Sant’Anna (CS) estimator, and performs the suggested high-physically-hazardous vs. office-industry triple-difference. One minor omission is the "Quasi-RDD at PSL coverage thresholds" (e.g., California’s 26+ employee threshold), though the paper notes these thresholds as a limitation in the discussion. The sample size and temporal window align with the feasibility check.

### 2. Summary
The paper provides a causal test of the hypothesis that mandatory paid sick leave (PSL) reduces workplace injuries by mitigating "presenteeism." Using a staggered difference-in-differences design on a large panel of OSHA establishment-level records, the author finds no statistically significant effect on injury rates, suggesting that prior cross-sectional findings (e.g., Asfaw et al., 2012) are likely driven by selection bias rather than a causal link.

### 3. Essential Points
*   **Treatment Timing and Window:** The author excludes "always-treated" states (CT, CA, MA, DC, OR, VT) to focus on the 2017–2023 window. However, several states in the "treated" group adopted laws *during* the transition (e.g., NY and MI in 2019/2020). With data ending in 2023, the post-treatment window for several large states is quite short (2-3 years). Given that injury rates are stock-and-flow processes influenced by safety cultures that take time to shift, the null result might reflect a lack of statistical power or insufficient duration for behavioral changes to manifest, rather than a lack of a theoretical effect.
*   **The "Large Firm" Selection Bias:** The OSHA ITA data is heavily skewed toward establishments with 250+ employees (universal mandate) or 20+ in high-hazard sectors. These large firms are exactly the units most likely to have offered PSL *voluntarily* prior to state mandates. If the treatment effect of a mandate is zero for firms already providing the benefit, and the data lacks the small firms (e.g., <20 employees) where the mandate actually bites, the "null" result is a mechanical artifact of the sample composition. The author mentions this in the discussion, but it is an existential threat to the paper's claim that the "causal claim" of Asfaw is debunked.
*   **Standard Errors and Magnitude:** The CS estimate for the Total Case Rate is -0.26 with an SE of 0.36. The pre-treatment mean is 3.03. This means the 95% confidence interval ranges from approx. -0.96 to +0.44. A -0.96 reduction would be a **31% decrease** in injuries—larger than the Asfaw (2012) estimate. Labeling this a "precise null" is misleading; the standard errors are actually quite large relative to the hypothesized effect. The paper should reframe the result: it cannot *rule out* the effects found in prior literature.

### 4. Suggestions

*   **Refining the Control Group:** Using "never-treated" states as a control group in a period of shifting federal OSHA enforcement priorities (the Trump-to-Biden transition) is risky. I recommend a "Not-Yet-Treated" vs "Already-Treated" sensitivity check within the CS framework to see if results are sensitive to the inclusion of states that eventually pass laws.
*   **Measurement of "Effective" PSL:** Not all PSL mandates are equal. Some allow 40 hours, others 72. Some have waiting periods of 90 days, others 0. I suggest a dose-response analysis or at least a table in the appendix characterizing the "generosity" of each state’s mandate to see if "stronger" mandates produce any movement in the point estimates.
*   **Zero-Inflated and Count Models:** Workplace injuries at the establishment level are often zero-inflated or follow a Poisson/Negative Binomial distribution. While the paper aggregates to the state-year level (where OLS is fine), a robustness check using the raw establishment-level data in a GLM framework (e.g., fixed-effects Poisson) would be more standard for this type of safety data.
*   **The DJTR Anomaly:** In Table 1, the DJTR (Job Transfer/Restriction) rate for treated states shows a significant CS estimate of -0.23 (p < 0.01), yet the author dismisses this as "small." This is a ~30% reduction from the pre-treatment mean (0.78). This is actually a *massive* and statistically significant effect. Why is it significant when the Total Case Rate is not? Perhaps PSL allows workers to stay home for the *entire* recovery period rather than coming back to "light duty" (Job Transfer). This could be the most interesting finding in the paper—that PSL shifts the *type* of injury recovery—and it is currently buried as a null.
*   **Triple-Difference Industry Grouping:** The "High-Hazard" vs. "Low-Hazard" split is a smart move. However, you should check if "Low-Hazard" (Office) workers actually had much higher baseline PSL coverage. If the "control" group in your triple-diff already had 90% coverage via private contracts, the triple-diff is essentially testing the effect on high-hazard firms against a static baseline. Providing BLS National Compensation Survey (NCS) data on pre-mandate PSL rates by industry would strengthen the "bite" argument.
*   **Wild Cluster Bootstrap Interpretation:** You report a bootstrap p-value of 0.89 for the TCR. With only 8 treated clusters, the wild bootstrap is the correct approach, but it reinforces that the state-level variation is likely too "noisy" to find this effect. I suggest looking for a more granular identification strategy (e.g., the city-level mandates in states like PA or NJ that preceded state laws) to increase the number of treated units.
*   **Visuals:** The paper lacks an Event Study plot. This is non-negotiable for a DiD paper in the AER: Insights format. Readers need to see the pre-trends to judge the validity of the parallel trends assumption, especially given the COVID-19 overlap.
*   **Typo in Table 1:** Total employees for never-treated states is listed as 15.5 million (Mean) with a Standard Deviation of 149 million. This suggests extreme outliers or a data entry error in the aggregation. Check if one or two states (like Texas or Florida) are skewing the distribution of the "Never-Treated" group.
