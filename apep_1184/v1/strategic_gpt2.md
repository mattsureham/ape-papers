## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether the EU’s COVID-era suspension of the “use-it-or-lose-it” airport slot rule protected incumbent airlines from competition and reduced activity at congested airports. Using the temporary relaxation and phased reinstatement of slot-usage requirements, it finds no detectable effect on airport-level passenger volumes: the feared competitive freeze does not show up in aggregate throughput.

Why should a busy economist care? Because this is a rare, plausibly exogenous shock to a major allocation rule in a high-value regulated market, and the paper speaks to a broad question: when regulators relax anti-hoarding rules, do incumbents actually use that discretion to entrench themselves in a way that matters for real outcomes?

### Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Reasonably well, but not optimally. The current introduction is competent and informed, but it is too institutional too early. It starts with slot values and the regulation itself, then gets to the economic question. A top-journal intro should open with the real-world fear and the broader economic stakes: do grandfathered rights plus crisis-era regulatory forbearance entrench incumbents, or are such concerns overstated when demand is depressed?

The first two paragraphs should do less scene-setting and more argument-setting. Right now the paper risks sounding like “a paper about a niche EU aviation rule.” It should instead sound like “a paper about whether temporary suspension of anti-hoarding rules actually shields incumbents in concentrated markets.”

### The pitch the paper should have

When COVID collapsed air travel, the EU suspended the rule requiring airlines to use airport slots or lose them. Critics warned that this would let incumbents hoard scarce capacity at congested airports, blocking entry and slowing recovery; supporters argued the waiver simply avoided wasteful “ghost flights” in a period of weak demand. This paper uses the unprecedented suspension and phased reinstatement of the rule to test whether relaxing anti-hoarding regulation actually protected incumbents in a way that reduced market activity. At the airport level, the answer is no: coordinated airports did not experience lower passenger throughput than comparable airports within the same country.

That framing is stronger because it is about the world, not just the regulation.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper provides the first causal evidence on whether suspending and then gradually restoring the EU’s airport slot use-it-or-lose-it rule reduced activity at slot-coordinated airports, and finds no detectable airport-level effect.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3-4 papers?

Partially. The paper says the existing slot literature is mostly theoretical and that no prior study estimates the causal effect of changing the threshold. That is useful, but the differentiation still feels mostly mechanical: “they are theory, I am empirics.” That is not enough for AER-level positioning.

What is missing is sharper differentiation along the substantive margin:
- Prior papers study **efficiency of slot allocation systems**.
- This paper studies **whether anti-hoarding rules matter for realized competitive outcomes during a major negative shock**.
- Prior work debates **ghost flights and allocation design**.
- This paper tests **whether temporary regulatory forbearance actually entrenched incumbents in a measurable way**.

That distinction needs to be made much more explicitly.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a gap in a literature?

It straddles both, but leans too much toward “there is no causal evidence in this literature.” The stronger version is world-facing: in a market with valuable grandfathered rights, does temporary suspension of use requirements entrench incumbents enough to matter?

That is the right question. The paper should make that the center of gravity.

### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what's new?

They could probably say: “It studies the EU slot waiver and finds no airport-level effect.” But they might also say: “It’s another regulatory DiD, with a null.”

That is the danger. The novelty is there, but the paper currently under-sells what kind of null this is. It is not just “no effect of policy X on outcome Y.” It is “a widely feared incumbency mechanism in a classic congested-capacity setting does not show up in aggregate throughput.” That is more interesting than the current intro lets on.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Most importantly: **move closer to the margin where the theory says effects should be strongest.** Airport-level throughput may simply be too aggregated. The paper itself basically admits this. The biggest way to enlarge the contribution would be:

1. **Route-level outcomes**  
   Entry, exit, frequency, route concentration, and carrier shares at coordinated airports would directly test the “incumbency shield” claim. Airport passengers are one step too far removed.

2. **New entrant outcomes**  
   Did the waiver reduce entry by non-incumbent carriers? Did it preserve incumbent shares on contested routes? That would make the paper much more about competition and much less about aggregate traffic accounting.

3. **Scarcity heterogeneity**  
   Effects should be larger at the most capacity-constrained airports or the most slot-scarce times of day. If there is no effect even there, the null is much more powerful.

4. **Link to ghost flights versus hoarding tradeoff**  
   The paper hints at this but does not fully exploit it. A more ambitious framing would ask: when anti-hoarding rules are relaxed, do we mainly avoid waste, mainly lose competition, or neither? That is a more general regulatory lesson.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Airport slots are scarce, valuable, and largely grandfathered to incumbents. The use-it-or-lose-it rule is supposed to prevent carriers from sitting on this scarce capacity and to create some opening for entry.

### Tension

COVID created a sharp collapse in demand, and the EU suspended the rule. That created a classic policy tradeoff: avoid wasteful ghost flights, but risk letting incumbents hoard capacity and freeze competition.

### Resolution

Using the suspension and phased return of the threshold, the paper finds no detectable effect on airport-level passenger throughput at coordinated airports relative to other airports within the same country.

### Implications

At least at the airport level, fears of a broad competitive freeze were overstated. In this setting, demand constraints or other frictions seem to have mattered more than formal slot-release requirements for aggregate throughput.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

Yes, more than many papers do. This is one of its strengths. The title is good, the underlying tension is real, and the null is interpretable.

But the arc weakens in the back half because the paper drifts into “here are the estimators and robustness checks” without continually reminding the reader what substantive hypothesis is being adjudicated. Also, the resolution is narrower than the setup. The setup is about competition and entry; the resolution is about airport-level throughput. That mismatch is the paper’s core storytelling problem.

### If it is a collection of results looking for a story, what story should it be telling?

It is not just a collection of results, but it does need a sharper story:

**The policy debate feared that temporary waiver of anti-hoarding rules would shield incumbents and meaningfully depress market activity. The paper shows that, despite the salience of slots and strong prior rhetoric, this mechanism did not matter for aggregate airport throughput during recovery.**

Then immediately add the discipline:
**That does not mean no competitive effects existed; it means they were too localized or too composition-based to move airport-level totals.**

That is the honest and strategically strongest version.

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

### What fact would you lead with?

“I exploit the only time in 30 years the EU suspended its use-it-or-lose-it airport slot rule, and I find that the feared incumbency shield had no detectable effect on airport-level passenger throughput.”

That is the line.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

Some would lean in initially because the institutional shock is unusual and the title is strong. But they may reach for their phones if the very next sentence is “at the airport level.” That qualifier immediately shrinks the contribution.

The paper’s biggest challenge is that the most natural audience response is: “Interesting, but what happened to route-level entry and concentration?” And the paper does not answer that.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Almost certainly:
- “Fine, but did incumbents keep rivals off specific routes?”
- “What happened to entry by low-cost carriers or new entrants?”
- “Are airport-level totals too coarse to detect the relevant distortion?”
- “Was demand simply too weak for slot access to matter?”

The paper anticipates this, but not forcefully enough. These are not side questions; they are the central questions the audience will care about.

### If the findings are null or modest: is the null itself interesting?

Yes, but only if framed correctly.

This null is potentially interesting because:
- The policy debate was vivid and high-stakes.
- The institutional setting is one where one might genuinely expect anti-hoarding rules to matter.
- A null in such a setting can be informative about the limits of regulatory bottlenecks when demand is the real constraint.

However, the paper must make clear why this null is informative rather than disappointing. The key is to argue that airport slots are a canonical example of an allocation rule believed to shape competition; if relaxing that rule did not move airport throughput even during a major shock, then economists should update toward a view in which slot access is not the first-order constraint for aggregate recovery.

Right now the paper does this somewhat, but not with enough confidence.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

Be honest: in current form, this is not yet an AER paper.

### What is the gap?

Primarily a **scope problem** and secondarily a **framing problem**.

- **Scope problem:** The outcome is too aggregated relative to the mechanism. The paper is about competition and incumbency shielding, but the evidence is on airport-level passengers. That is one level too coarse.
- **Framing problem:** The paper sometimes presents itself as resolving the competition debate, when it really resolves a narrower question: whether the waiver measurably reduced airport-level throughput.
- **Ambition problem:** The paper is competent and sensible, but safe. It stops where the interesting question begins.

### Is it a novelty problem?

Not exactly. The policy variation is genuinely novel. The issue is not that the shock is uninteresting; it is that the paper does not fully cash out the novelty because it studies a coarse margin.

### What is the single most impactful piece of advice?

**Move the paper to the competitive margin where the “incumbency shield” hypothesis lives—route-level entry, carrier shares, or concentration at coordinated airports—or else rewrite the paper much more narrowly as evidence that the waiver did not affect aggregate airport throughput during recovery.**

If they can only change one thing, that is it.

Because in its current form, the paper’s strongest internal critique is already written by the author: route-level effects may exist and airport-level effects may be too coarse. Once the paper admits that, the reader naturally wants the route-level paper instead.
