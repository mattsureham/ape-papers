## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple and important question: when governments digitize procurement, does it actually make public contracting more competitive, or does it just move paperwork online? Using Colombia’s shift from an information-only portal to a fully transactional e-procurement platform, the paper argues that digitization increases competition when it reduces firms’ costs of participating, not merely their costs of learning about opportunities.

A busy economist should care because this is not just a procurement paper. It speaks to a broader question in political economy, development, and public economics: when does state digitization change real market outcomes rather than just administrative form?

### Does the paper articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Reasonably well, but not as sharply as it should. The opening gets close, but the paper still sounds a bit like “another e-government reform paper with staggered adoption.” The real hook is stronger: **the paper claims to resolve a puzzle in the existing literature by distinguishing information frictions from participation frictions.** That distinction should be the headline from sentence one.

### The pitch the paper should have

“Governments around the world have digitized public procurement on the theory that transparency will increase competition. Yet prior evidence suggests that putting contract information online often improves selection without attracting more bidders. This paper shows why: the key friction is not learning about opportunities, but participating in them. Studying Colombia’s transition from an information-only procurement portal to a fully transactional platform, we show that digitizing the act of bidding—not just the posting of opportunities—substantially increases competition in public contracting.”

That is the story. Everything else should serve it.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### Contribution in one sentence

The paper’s core contribution is to show that **transactional e-procurement, unlike information-only digitization, can increase competition in public contracting by lowering bidder participation costs.**

### Is this clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?

Partially, but not yet cleanly enough.

The paper does identify Lewis-Faupel et al. as the key contrast, and that is smart. But beyond that, the differentiation is muddier. Right now the contribution is split across three frames:
1. a procurement competition paper,
2. a digital state capacity paper,
3. a “first causal evidence on Colombia” paper.

The first is the strongest; the third is the weakest. “First evidence from Colombia” is not an AER contribution in itself.

The paper should more explicitly distinguish itself from:
- papers showing digitization improves monitoring/transparency,
- papers showing e-procurement improves quality or prices,
- papers documenting corruption or favoritism in procurement,
- papers about entry costs in auctions more generally.

The differentiation should be: **most prior work studies disclosure and monitoring; this paper studies transaction costs of participation.**

### World question or literature gap?

Mostly framed as a world question, which is good. The strongest version is:
- Why do some digital government reforms change market structure while others do not?

The weaker framing is:
- “We fill a gap by studying a transactional platform in Latin America.”

The introduction oscillates between these. It should commit to the first.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the intro?

Yes, but only barely. A good reader would probably say:
> “It’s a paper on Colombian e-procurement arguing that digitizing bidding, not just posting, increases competition.”

That is decent. But a less charitable reader might still summarize it as:
> “It’s another staggered-adoption DiD on procurement digitization.”

That is a warning sign. The introduction needs to make the conceptual distinction more memorable and less design-centric.

### What would make the contribution bigger?

Most importantly: **tie the findings to economically meaningful outcomes beyond the procurement modality margin.** Right now the paper’s strongest empirical fact is about competition, but the broader claim is about market access and state effectiveness. To feel bigger, the paper would benefit from at least one of the following dimensions in the main framing:

- **New firm entry / supplier breadth:** Did transactional procurement bring in more unique suppliers, especially geographically distant or smaller firms?
- **Price or savings:** Not just award/reserve ratio in one table, but a front-and-center implication for procurement costs.
- **Market integration:** Did local procurement markets become less captive and more contestable across space?
- **Composition of winners:** Did the reform reduce concentration among incumbent or connected suppliers?

Conceptually, the paper wants to say the market changed. It would feel much bigger if the headline evidence reflected that directly.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

### Closest neighbors

The nearest neighbors seem to be:

1. **Lewis-Faupel, Neggers, Olken, and Pande (2016, AER)** on e-procurement in India and Indonesia.
2. **Krasnokutskaya and Seim (2011)** on bid preparation costs and participation in procurement auctions.
3. **Bandiera, Prat, and Valletti (2009)** on waste and inefficiency in public procurement.
4. Work on **digital state capacity / anti-corruption technology**, such as **Muralidharan, Niehaus, and Sukhtankar (2016)** and **Banerjee et al. (2020)**.
5. Likely also the growing procurement empirics literature summarized in **Bosio et al.** and related World Bank / procurement market design work.

### How should the paper position itself?

It should **build directly on Lewis-Faupel et al.** and use that paper as the foil. Not attack, but refine:
- “Their evidence suggests information-only e-procurement does not increase competition.”
- “Our evidence suggests that is not a failure of digitization per se; it is evidence that search frictions were not the binding constraint.”

That is a strong, clarifying contribution.

Relative to auction-entry theory, the paper should **use Krasnokutskaya-Seim as the microfoundation**, not as a coequal contribution. That literature helps explain the mechanism.

Relative to digital state capacity papers, it should **connect but not overclaim**. Procurement digitization is not the same as biometric welfare payments. The connection is interesting, but the current draft risks sounding like it wants to belong equally to too many literatures.

### Is it positioned too narrowly or too broadly?

At present, a little too broad in aspiration and too narrow in execution.

Too broad because it gestures at corruption, state capacity, platform markets, and digital government all at once.

Too narrow because most of the actual evidence is still framed as Colombian procurement modality shares and single-bidder rates.

The paper should narrow the conceptual claim to one big idea:
**Digitization changes outcomes when it lowers participation costs, not just information costs.**
That is broad enough to matter and narrow enough to defend.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?

The paper should probably speak more to:
- **market access / firm participation** literatures,
- **administrative burden / take-up** ideas from public economics and political science,
- **platform adoption / two-sided markets** if it really wants to make the maturity/network-effects point,
- the more recent **procurement competition and single-bidding** literature, including work in Europe and anti-corruption procurement design.

The “administrative burden” angle is potentially fruitful: firms may know opportunities exist but face costly compliance frictions. That reframes procurement digitization as reducing bureaucratic burden for private participants.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?

Mostly yes, but it is leaving some value on the table. The highest-impact conversation is not “Colombia adopted SECOP II.” It is:
**Why do some digital reforms actually expand market participation while others merely improve visibility?**

That conversation reaches development, public finance, IO, and political economy readers.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Public procurement is economically large, and low competition is a central problem. Governments have digitized procurement systems in hopes of improving outcomes, but prior evidence suggests digitization often improves transparency or contractor quality without increasing bidding competition.

### Tension

If putting procurement online does not attract more bidders, why do so many governments invest in e-procurement? Is the problem that digitization does not matter, or that prior reforms digitized the wrong margin?

### Resolution

Colombia’s move from an information-only system to a fully transactional system appears to increase competition: more procurement through competitive modalities and fewer single-bidder processes among earlier adopters. The paper interprets this as evidence that participation costs, not search costs, are the binding friction.

### Implications

Design matters. Governments should not expect “transparency portals” alone to make procurement markets competitive; reforms that digitize the actual act of bidding may have larger effects on market access and contestability.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

Yes, but it weakens in the middle because the results are not yet perfectly aligned with the story.

The story is about **competition through lower participation costs**. But the first main result is on the **share of contracts in competitive modalities**, which feels more administrative than economic. The later process-level result on single-bidder rates is closer to the paper’s real claim.

So the current draft has a story, but the ordering subtly undercuts it. It reads a bit like:
- main result: platform increases “competitive procurement share”
- supporting result: maybe fewer single bidders

For narrative purposes, I would reverse the psychological priority:
- lead with the most intuitive competition fact,
- then interpret aggregate shifts in procurement structure as consistent with that.

In other words, the paper should not feel like a classification paper with a bidder appendix. It should feel like a market participation paper.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?

I would say:
> “Moving procurement online only seems to increase competition when firms can actually submit bids online. In Colombia, shifting from a posting portal to a transactional platform reduced single-bidder procurement meaningfully.”

That is the dinner-party fact.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

Some would lean in—especially development, public, and political economy economists—because this resolves a known puzzle. But the reaction depends heavily on how it is presented.

If presented as:
- “We study staggered adoption of SECOP II in Colombia...”
they reach for phones.

If presented as:
- “Transparency didn’t fix procurement competition because the real friction was applying, not knowing”
they lean in.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Likely:
- “Did it reduce prices or bring in new firms?”
or
- “So is the effect really about bidder entry, not just agencies changing how they classify contracts?”

That follow-up question is revealing. The paper’s current framing invites it, and does not yet answer it as forcefully as an AER-caliber paper would want.

### If findings are modest or null

The binary department-level effect is modest; the process-level single-bidder result is more interesting. The paper should not oversell the modest result and should instead emphasize why even a moderate reduction in single-bidder contracting is economically meaningful. Nulls would be interesting if they sharpened the distinction between information and transaction costs, but the paper is not really built as a null paper.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

This is not mainly a technical-polish problem. It is a **framing-plus-scope** problem.

### What is the gap?

- **Framing problem:** The paper has a potentially strong idea but still presents itself too much like a standard policy evaluation of one platform rollout.
- **Scope problem:** The evidence does not yet fully match the ambition of the claim. The claim is about lowering participation costs and changing market competition; the current headline outcome is only an indirect proxy for that.
- **Ambition problem:** The paper is competent, but somewhat safe. It settles for showing that SECOP II affected competitive procurement measures rather than pushing hard on what this means for market access, supplier entry, prices, or state effectiveness.

### Is it a novelty problem?

Not entirely. The underlying question is not fully answered in the literature, and the information-vs-transactional distinction is genuinely interesting. But unless the paper leans hard into that conceptual distinction, readers may perceive it as incremental within the crowded “digitization and public procurement” space.

### Single most impactful advice

**Rebuild the paper around a single big claim: transactional digitization increases bidder entry because it lowers participation costs, and then organize the evidence so that the most direct competition outcomes—not procurement modality shares—carry the story.**

That is the one change that would most increase its odds.

If the authors can do only one thing, they should make the paper feel less like “a staggered rollout in Colombia” and more like “the economics of why some digital state reforms change markets and others don’t.”
