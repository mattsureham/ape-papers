## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
The paper asks whether the *type* of digital infrastructure matters for state capacity and market competition. Using the staggered rollout of a transactional (bidding) vs. informational (billboard) e-procurement system in Colombia, it tests if reducing participation costs, rather than just search costs, breaks the "single-bidder trap." This is vital for economists because it moves the "e-government" conversation from a generic "digitization is good" to a specific understanding of which frictions (information vs. transaction costs) actually bind in developing markets.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this very well. The distinction between SECOP I (informational) and SECOP II (transactional) is the "hook." It directly challenges the pessimistic competition findings of *Lewis-Faupel et al. (2016)* in the first two paragraphs. The current pitch is excellent.

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**One-sentence contribution:** The paper demonstrates that transactional e-procurement reduces single-bidder contracts and increases the share of competitive awards by lowering participation costs, whereas previous literature found that mere information transparency was insufficient to stimulate competition.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It is sharply differentiated from *Lewis-Faupel et al. (2016)* and *Bosio et al. (2022)* by isolating the "transactional" mechanism.
*   **Framing:** It is framed as a question about the **WORLD** (Why is there no competition in public markets?) rather than just a gap in literature.
*   **Clarity:** A smart economist would immediately grasp the "Participation Cost" vs. "Information Cost" distinction. It is not "just another DiD"; it is a mechanism test.
*   **Growth Potential:** To make this "bigger," the authors need to link the competition results more firmly to **Price** and **Efficiency**. Table 3 shows a 4.2pp drop in award-to-reserve ratios that isn't significant. If they can find a way to power this up (perhaps through the 21m contract records) to show actual fiscal savings, it becomes a slam-dunk AER paper.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
*   **Neighbors:** *Lewis-Faupel et al. (2016)* (AEJ: Policy), *Bandiera et al. (2009)* (AER), *Best et al. (2023)* (AER), and *Krasnokutskaya & Seim (2011)* (AER).

*   **Missing Conversations:** The paper could benefit from connecting to the **Industrial Organization (IO)** literature on entry in auctions. It speaks to "State Capacity," but it’s fundamentally an entry cost paper.
*   **Right Conversation?** Yes. It sits at the intersection of Development, IO, and Public Economics.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC
*   **Setup:** Developing countries spend trillions on procurement, but competition is dead (single bidders).
*   **Tension:** Information transparency was supposed to fix this (SECOP I), but it didn't. Why?
*   **Resolution:** Because the barrier isn't "knowing" about the contract; it's the "cost of bidding" (paperwork, travel). SECOP II solves this.
*   **Implications:** Governments should stop building "digital billboards" and start building "digital marketplaces."

**Evaluation:** The arc is exceptionally strong. It is not a collection of results; it is a hypothesis-driven inquiry.

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
At a dinner party, I’d lead with: *"We finally figured out why e-procurement fails to bring in new bidders—it’s not that they don't see the ads, it's that it's too expensive to even fill out the forms."*
*   **Reaction:** Lean in. The "Single-Bidder Trap" is a massive policy headache.
*   **Follow-up:** "Does it actually save the government money?" This is the weak point. If the price effect is insignificant, the cynic will say, "Who cares if there are more bidders if the price is the same?"

---

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
*   **Front-loading:** The distinction between SECOP I and II is the "gold" here. Ensure Section 2's description of the "physical presence" requirements of the old system is vivid to hammer home the transaction cost argument.
*   **Table 2:** Column 2 (continuous intensity) is arguably a "mechanical" result as the authors admit. They should lead with the binary DiD and perhaps an **Event Study plot** (mentioned in Appendix B) in the main text. AER readers live for the Event Study plot.
*   **Appendix to Main:** Move the Event Study plot from the Appendix to the main results. It’s the visual proof of parallel trends.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The gap is currently **Scope and Precision**. 
1.  **The Price Effect:** The AER is obsessed with welfare. An increase in competition is a "means," but the "end" is lower prices or better quality. The insignificance in Table 3 (Award/Reserve ratio) is a hurdle.
2.  **The "Who" is entering:** Are these "new" firms or just "politically connected" firms finding it easier to bid? If they can show *new* firms entering the market, the story about "breaking entry barriers" becomes much more powerful.

**Single piece of advice:** Focus relentlessly on the **firm-level dynamics**—prove that this platform allowed *young* or *distant* firms to compete for the first time, and push the price analysis harder to show a welfare gain.
