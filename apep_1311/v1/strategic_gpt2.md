## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether digitizing **the transaction itself**, rather than merely posting procurement information online, increases competition for public contracts. Using Colombia’s transition from SECOP I to the transactional SECOP II, the paper argues that reducing firms’ participation costs lowers single-bidder procurement and shifts procurement toward more competitive modalities.

A busy economist should care because public procurement is enormous, single-bidder contracting is a first-order state-capacity problem, and the paper’s core claim is conceptually sharp: **the binding friction in procurement may be participation costs, not information frictions**.

### Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Mostly yes, and the basic ingredients are there. But the introduction currently does two things that weaken the pitch:

1. It leads with a broad procurement problem, then quickly jumps to a narrow platform comparison.
2. It frames the result partly as “contrast with Lewis-Faupel et al.” rather than first establishing the bigger world question.

The first two paragraphs should do less cataloging and more sharpen the causal economic question:

> Governments increasingly digitize procurement, but digitization can mean two very different things: publishing opportunities online or allowing firms to complete the entire bidding process online. These two reforms target different frictions. The first reduces search costs; the second reduces the costs of participation itself.
>
> This paper asks which friction matters more for competition in public contracting. Studying Colombia’s shift from an informational portal (SECOP I) to a fully transactional platform (SECOP II), we show that when procurement is digitized in a way that lets firms actually bid online, competition rises: single-bidder procurement falls and competitive contracting expands. The broader lesson is that digital state capacity matters not only through transparency and monitoring, but through lowering entry costs for private firms interacting with the state.

That is the pitch the paper should have. It is cleaner, more world-oriented, and more portable beyond Colombia.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper’s contribution is to argue that **transactional e-procurement increases competition by lowering bidder participation costs, implying that the effectiveness of digitization depends on whether it changes firms’ ability to enter, not merely their access to information**.

### Is this clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?

Partly, but not yet sharply enough.

The paper most clearly differentiates itself from **Lewis-Faupel et al. (2016)**, which is the obvious anchor: informational e-procurement improved quality but not competition. That contrast is useful and likely the strongest comparative hook.

But relative to the broader procurement and state-capacity literatures, the contribution is still blurry. Right now, a smart economist might summarize it as: “another paper showing digitization helps procurement.” The paper wants the reader to instead say: “Ah — the distinction between *informational* and *transactional* digitization is economically central.”

That distinction is present, but it needs to dominate the introduction much more forcefully.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or filling a gap in a LITERATURE?

It starts with a world question, which is good, but it drifts into a literature-gap frame by the middle of the introduction: “first large-scale causal evidence on Latin America’s most ambitious procurement reform,” “staggered rollout,” “process-level data,” etc.

That is not the strongest version. AER wants the world question first:

- Why do many procurement reforms fail to increase competition?
- What kind of digitization actually changes market structure?
- What friction prevents firms from bidding for public contracts?

Those are stronger than “we provide causal evidence on SECOP II.”

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?

Not confidently enough. They would likely say:

> “It’s a DiD on e-procurement in Colombia showing more online procurement is associated with more competition.”

That is too generic.

The paper needs the reader to say:

> “It isolates a conceptual distinction in digital government: transparency reforms reduce search costs, but transactional reforms reduce participation costs — and that second margin is what increases competition.”

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Three possibilities:

1. **Make the object of interest actual bidder entry, not just procurement modality.**  
   The paper has some process-level bidder evidence, but the headline result is still the share of contracts using competitive modalities. That sounds bureaucratic rather than economic. For a bigger paper, the main outcome should be something like:
   - bidders per process,
   - single-bidder share,
   - entry of new firms,
   - geographic expansion of bidders,
   - participation by SMEs or remote firms.

2. **Make the mechanism more concrete.**  
   If the story is reduced participation costs, show which firms enter:
   - firms farther from the procuring entity,
   - firms with no prior procurement history,
   - smaller firms,
   - firms entering new departments or sectors.  
   That would transform the paper from “digitization matters” to “digitization integrated fragmented public markets.”

3. **Reframe from procurement administration to market design/state capacity.**  
   The big contribution is not Colombia and not SECOP per se. It is that the state changes competition by altering the costs private firms face when transacting with it.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

### Closest neighbors

The nearest papers/conversations seem to be:

1. **Lewis-Faupel, Neggers, Olken, and Pande (2016)** on e-procurement in India and Indonesia.
2. **Krasnokutskaya and Seim (2011)** or related auction-entry papers on participation costs and bidder entry.
3. **Bandiera, Prat, and Valletti (2009)** on waste in public procurement.
4. Work on **digital state capacity / e-government**, e.g. **Muralidharan, Niehaus, and Sukhtankar (2016)** and **Banerjee et al. (2020)**.
5. Potentially the growing empirical procurement literature synthesized in work like **Bosio et al.** on public procurement and institutional design.

Depending on where the author wants to land, there is also a neighboring literature on:
- procurement competition and corruption,
- transaction costs and market access,
- platform adoption / two-sided markets.

### How should the paper position itself relative to those neighbors?

- **Build on Lewis-Faupel et al., not merely “contrast” with it.**  
  The current framing is a little too eager to say “our findings contrast with Lewis-Faupel.” That is fine as a motivating contrast, but the stronger move is:
  > Lewis-Faupel showed that not all e-procurement reforms increase competition. We explain why: reforms that only publish information are different from reforms that lower the cost of participating.

That sounds cumulative rather than adversarial, and it makes the paper look like it is resolving a puzzle, not cherry-picking a contradiction.

- **Import more explicitly from auction-entry theory.**  
  The paper’s mechanism is an entry-cost story. That should not be a side citation; it should be a central intellectual backbone. The paper should sound more like it is testing a market-design prediction in a public-sector setting.

- **Connect to state capacity, but don’t get lost there.**  
  The paper currently gestures toward digital governance and anti-corruption. That is useful, but secondary. The core conversation should be procurement competition and entry frictions.

### Is the paper positioned too narrowly or too broadly?

A bit of both, oddly.

- **Too narrow** in the sense that it becomes about one platform migration in Colombia.
- **Too broad** in the sense that it claims to speak to corruption, e-government, and state capacity all at once.

The right position is narrower and stronger:
> This is a paper about how the design of digital procurement platforms affects bidder entry and competition.

That audience is substantial and coherent.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?

Not necessarily unaware, but under-engaged with:
- auction participation and endogenous entry,
- market access / fixed costs of participation,
- platform design / two-sided adoption,
- broader empirical procurement competition literature outside the corruption frame.

If the paper can show that SECOP II reduced effective geographic or administrative barriers, it could also speak to literatures on:
- firm participation in public markets,
- barriers to doing business with the state,
- digital market integration.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?

Almost. But the most impactful conversation is not “does e-government reduce corruption?” It is:

> **What frictions keep public procurement uncompetitive, and what kind of institutional design relaxes them?**

That is the conversation AER readers are more likely to find broadly interesting.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Governments spend huge sums through procurement, but many public contracts attract only one bidder. Standard digitization reforms have often been sold as competition-enhancing, yet prior evidence suggests putting procurement online does not necessarily increase competition.

### Tension

Why doesn’t digitization reliably increase competition? Is the bottleneck lack of information about opportunities, or the cost of actually participating in the bidding process? If the former, online disclosure should help. If the latter, only a fully transactional platform should matter.

### Resolution

The paper finds that moving from an informational platform to a transactional one is associated with more competitive procurement and fewer single-bidder processes, with no corresponding effect in a placebo setting where bidding infrastructure should not matter.

### Implications

The design of digital government matters. Digitization that merely increases transparency may not alter market structure; digitization that reduces participation costs can. That has implications for procurement reform, state capacity, and how economists think about digital public-sector platforms.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the ingredients of one, but the arc is not fully disciplined. Right now it is somewhere between a real story and a collection of related results.

The central problem is that the headline department-level outcome — “competitive procurement share” — does not feel like the natural resolution to the setup. If the setup is “why are there so many single-bidder contracts?”, the payoff should ideally be a clean competition outcome, not a modality-share outcome that sounds partly administrative.

So the current story feels slightly split:

- Story A: SECOP II changes the composition of contracts across procurement modalities.
- Story B: SECOP II reduces bidder participation costs and increases competition.

Story B is the AER story. Story A is the compliance/public administration story. The paper should commit much more clearly to Story B.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?

I would lead with:

> “Putting procurement notices online may not do much, but letting firms actually bid online appears to reduce single-bidder public contracts.”

That is a fact economists will immediately understand and care about.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

They would lean in for the conceptual distinction. Not for the current empirical headline as written.

“Competitive procurement share rises by 25.6 percentage points” is not a naturally gripping fact, partly because it sounds mechanical and the paper itself admits as much. “Single-bidder rates fall when bidding can be done online” is much more compelling.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Almost certainly:

> “Which firms enter — new firms, smaller firms, firms from farther away, or just the same incumbents using a different interface?”

That is the crucial follow-up. If the paper cannot answer it, that limits how far the contribution can go.

A second follow-up would be:

> “Does this lower prices or improve contract quality, or is it just changing formal procedure?”

Again, that is where the paper could become materially bigger.

### If findings are modest, is that okay?

Yes, but then the paper has to lean harder on the conceptual distinction. The binary adoption effect is modest; the intensity effect is larger but the paper concedes it is partly mechanical. So the paper cannot sell itself as a blockbuster reduced-form effect paper. It has to sell itself as a **conceptual paper about what kind of digitization matters**.

That is viable, but only if the framing gets much sharper.

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

### What would make it read better?

1. **Front-load the real insight.**  
   The first page should establish:
   - procurement digitization comes in two forms,
   - they target different frictions,

2. **Move the “first large-scale causal evidence on Colombia” language down or cut it.**  
   That is not the lead. It is background support.

3. **Reorder the results so the most intuitive competition evidence comes first.**  
   If there is credible single-bidder evidence, that should appear earlier and more prominently. Right now the paper leads with the less compelling modality-share outcome.

4. **Shorten the generic state-capacity discussion.**  
   The discussion section currently expands into broad claims about digital government. That is fine in moderation, but the paper should spend more time clarifying the mechanism and less time gesturing toward every adjacent literature.

5. **Trim self-conscious caveating around the headline coefficient.**  
   It is good that the paper acknowledges mechanical confounds, but strategically it is damaging that one of the headline results is introduced and then partially undercut immediately. If that coefficient is not the best representation of the contribution, do not center it.

6. **Conclusion should do more than summarize.**  
   The current conclusion is acceptable but thin. It should end with a broader economic claim:
   - states do not just allocate contracts; they design access to public markets,
   - digitization can widen that access when it lowers participation costs,
   - this changes who competes for state demand.

### Are there results buried that belong in the main text?

Potentially yes. The appendix mentions:
- event study,

- perhaps cohort restrictions.

If there is convincing event-time evidence or stronger heterogeneity consistent with the mechanism, some of that belongs in the main paper. The main text currently feels a little underpowered narratively.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

At the moment, the gap is mainly a mix of **framing problem** and **scope problem**, with a bit of **ambition problem**.

### Framing problem

The science, at least as presented, is attached to the wrong headline. The paper’s real idea is stronger than its current lead result. It should not be “SECOP II increased competitive procurement share.” It should be:

> “Digital procurement only increases competition when it lowers firms’ participation costs.”

That is the claim that belongs in a top general-interest journal conversation.

### Scope problem

The evidence package is still a little narrow for AER. To excite the top people in the field, the paper likely needs at least one of the following:

- direct evidence on bidder entry by new/small/remote firms,
- stronger price or allocation outcomes,
- clearer evidence that the market became geographically broader or less concentrated,
- a more persuasive mechanism tying platform design to entry costs.

### Novelty problem

The conceptual distinction is novel enough to be interesting. The empirical design alone is not. If the contribution is read as “staggered DiD on e-procurement,” this is not an AER paper. If it is read as “the form of digitization determines whether public markets become competitive,” it has a chance.

### Ambition problem

The paper is competent, but currently too safe. It settles for a plausible story when it should be trying to show a deeper transformation in how firms access the state.

### Single most impactful piece of advice

**Rebuild the paper around bidder entry and single-bidder contracting as the headline outcome, and treat the modality-share result as supporting context rather than the main event.**

That one change would improve:
- the pitch,
- the narrative arc,
- the “so what,”
- the literature positioning,
- and the paper’s general-interest appeal.
