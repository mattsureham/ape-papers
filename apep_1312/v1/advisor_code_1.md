**Idea Fidelity**

The paper largely follows the original idea manifest. It studies North Macedonia’s 2019 progressive income tax reform using the publicly available monthly sector-level wage data, exploits the 12-month “on-off” nature of the reform, and adopts a continuous-treatment difference-in-differences (DiD) leveraging cross-sector exposure intensities. The manifest’s emphasis on wage suppression in high-exposure sectors, the use of gross/net wage series, and the unique identification arising from the brief reform are all present. However, two elements are underemphasized: the manifest proposed an interrupted time series design and highlighted the visible January 2019 “dip and rebound” suggestive of a sharp treatment effect, whereas the paper relies entirely on a DiD-style specification without leveraging the precise timing structure or incorporating synthetic control ideas. The manifest’s suggested mechanism test—examining the gross/net gap to detect reporting versus real adjustments—is present but the paper treats the null results almost exclusively as a power issue rather than revisiting the original mechanism narrative.

**Summary**

This paper studies North Macedonia’s one-year foray into progressive income taxation by estimating wage responses across 19 NACE sectors. Using sector-level wages from 2012–2024, it exploits pre-reform wage dispersion relative to the MKD 90,000 threshold as continuous treatment intensity in a DiD with month and sector fixed effects. The main finding is a statistically insignificant wage suppression during 2019 and no clear rebound in 2020, a null result attributed primarily to limited statistical power at the sectoral aggregation level.

**Essential Points**

1. **Exposure Measurement and Treatment Variation:** The identifying variable—sector mean wage relative to the 90,000 MKD threshold—does not directly capture the share of workers exposed to the top bracket. With the highest sector-average barely reaching 72% of the threshold and only about 1% of taxpayers affected, this averaging conceals the actual treatment intensity and undermines the interpretation of β₁ as the response of high-exposure sectors. The authors should either construct a more accurate exposure variable (e.g., estimate the fraction of workers above the threshold via wage distribution assumptions or firm-level data) or explicitly acknowledge and quantify the resulting measurement error.

2. **Aggregation and Power Constraints:** While the paper transparently treats the null as a power issue, it reaches this conclusion without formally estimating the minimum detectable effect or engaging deeply with the consequence that the intervention affected almost no taxpayers. With only 19 sectors and limited cross-sectional variation, even large behavioral responses at the individual level could vanish in sectoral averages—raising concerns about the substantive contribution. The authors should provide a formal power analysis tied to plausible elasticities or illustrate that the design can detect economically meaningful effects, otherwise it is unclear whether the null is informative.

3. **Other Concurrent Shocks and the Boomerang Narrative:** The reform’s unique timing is central, yet the event-study evidence shows post-reform coefficients that remain negative and grow over time, suggesting differential trends beyond the policy window. The paper should better rule out other sector-specific shocks in 2019–2020 (e.g., macro shocks, sectoral policy changes, or the onset of the COVID-19 pandemic) that could contaminate the “boomerang” interpretation. Without addressing these, attributing the absence of rebound or the persistent post-2020 decline solely to lack of reporting responses is speculative.

**Suggestions**

- **Richer Exposure Construction:** Instead of relying on sector averages, consider reconstructing the wage distribution to approximate the share of employees that would have faced the 18% rate. One approach is to use published wage distribution percentiles (if available) or combine the average wage with plausible distributional assumptions (e.g., log-normal). Alternatively, leverage any auxiliary data (tax authority reports, labor force surveys) to estimate how many workers per sector earned above the threshold. This would sharpen the treatment definition and potentially provide a clearer link between the reform and expected behavioral responses.

- **Incorporate Synthetic Control or Interrupted Time Series Elements:** Given the reform’s abrupt onset and reversal, the data lend themselves to a synthetic control or an interrupted time series approach that compares the 2019 “treated” period to a counterfactual trajectory constructed from pre-2019 trends in low-exposure sectors. Even a simple difference between January 2018–December 2018 and 2019, adjusting for seasonal effects, could provide complementary evidence to the continuous DiD and exploit the manifest’s “visible January dip” narrative. This would make fuller use of the high-frequency structure and highlight whether the null is due to flatness relative to a clean counterfactual.

- **Mechanism Exploration:** The gross/net gap analysis is a promising mechanism check, but it needs more development. If tax reporting were adjusted, one would expect gross wages to fall while net wages remain steady (or even increase) once the employers compensate via non-taxable benefits. Presenting the gross minus net gap over time, by exposure group, would reinforce this argument. Moreover, consider exploring employment shares or hours worked per sector to rule out compositional shifts—though individual-level data are unavailable, any published sectoral employment counts could shed light on whether shifts in composition drive the observed patterns.

- **Power Analysis with Plausible Elasticities:** The paper rightly notes the design is underpowered, but the discussion would benefit from quantifying this. Compute the minimum detectable effect (MDE) given 19 clusters and the observed variance, and compare it to plausible wage responses implied by taxable income elasticities (Saez et al., Kleven-Schultz). If the MDE far exceeds the expected response, the null is less informative; if not, the null becomes evidence. Presenting confidence intervals in terms of earnings (e.g., MKD) rather than log points may also help policymakers assess the reform’s practical implications.

- **Address Potential Confounders:** The period 2019–2020 includes other economic events (e.g., election-related spending, fiscal changes, the global emergence of COVID-19 in early 2020). Even though the post-repeal period is only partly within the pandemic, it’s important to discuss and, if possible, control for aggregate shocks (e.g., GDP growth, inflation) that could differentially affect high-exposure sectors. One way is to include controls for sector-specific demand indicators (if available) or to demonstrate that the sector exposure interacted with macro trends is insignificant before 2019.

- **Clarify the Role of Compositional Change:** Sectoral averages can shift due to workforce composition changes rather than wage adjustments. While the paper mentions this limitation, it would improve the contribution to quantify it. For instance, if Statistics North Macedonia publishes average employment levels by sector, include them to check whether high-exposure sectors saw large employment shifts in 2019. If the data show stability, it bolsters the wage interpretation.

- **Refocus the Discussion on Policy Implications:** The conclusion currently emphasizes data limitations but could better articulate what the null means for policy debates. If the sector-level aggregates show no detectable wage suppression, does that imply high earners lacked incentives to adjust due to factors like weak enforcement or limited opportunities for tax avoidance? Expanding the discussion to contrast this transition economy context with settings where wage responses are documented would highlight the novelty claimed in the idea manifest.

- **Enhance Transparency of Inference Procedures:** The wild cluster bootstrap and permutation tests are valuable, but more detail on implementation (e.g., how the treatment year is randomized, exact weights) would improve replicability. Including a figure showing the distribution of placebo coefficients from the permutation exercise could also reinforce the point about limited statistical power.

- **Explore Alternative Outcomes or Data Transformations:** While gross and net wages are natural, consider also examining year-on-year growth rates or first differences to mitigate non-stationarity and make the parallel trends assumption more credible. If available, monthly wage growth rates would highlight whether 2019 deviates from typical fluctuations.

By addressing these points, the paper can better support its null finding and clarify the implications of North Macedonia’s one-year experiment for progressive taxation debates in transition economies.
