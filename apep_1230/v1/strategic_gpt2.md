## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether targeted federal oversight can screen low-quality or opportunistic entrants out of a Medicare-funded healthcare market. Studying CMS’s 2023 enhanced oversight regime for new hospice providers in four states, it finds a dramatic collapse in new entry driven entirely by for-profit firms, suggesting that tighter screening can sharply reshape market composition in end-of-life care.

Why should a busy economist care? Because the paper is really about a broad question: when public payment, severe information asymmetry, and weak consumer discipline make a market vulnerable to low-quality entry, can regulation improve selection at the gate rather than restrict supply indiscriminately?

**Does the paper itself articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Not quite. The current opening is vivid and data-rich, but it reads more like a policy brief on a hospice fraud problem than like an economics paper with a general question. It gets to the intervention quickly, but the bigger economic idea — screening versus blunt entry restriction in credence-good markets — is underdeveloped. The first paragraphs emphasize “for-profit problem” and fraud concentration, which risks sounding normative and sector-specific before the reader understands the more general contribution.

**What the first two paragraphs should say instead:**

> Many healthcare markets combine public reimbursement, severe information asymmetry, and weak consumer discipline, creating fertile ground for low-quality or fraudulent entry. A central policy question is whether regulators can screen out problematic entrants without broadly suppressing legitimate supply — a question that matters well beyond hospice, from home health to Medicaid contracting to other publicly financed credence goods.
>
> This paper studies that question in the U.S. hospice industry, where nearly all recent entry was for-profit and heavily concentrated in four states. When CMS imposed enhanced prepayment review and enrollment scrutiny on new hospices in those states in 2023, new entry fell sharply — almost entirely because for-profit entry collapsed while nonprofit entry did not. The results suggest that targeted oversight can operate less like a conventional barrier to entry and more like a screening device that changes who enters the market.

That is the AER pitch. Right now the paper has the ingredients, but not the framing discipline.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:**  
The paper shows that targeted enrollment oversight in hospice dramatically reduced new provider entry, with the effect borne almost entirely by for-profit entrants, implying that regulation can screen market composition rather than simply reduce supply.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?

The differentiation from adjacent literatures is still too literal and not yet conceptual. A top-journal contribution needs to be something like:

- existing work studies payment incentives, ownership, or blunt entry regulation;
- this paper studies **targeted screening at entry** in a setting with extreme asymmetric information;
- the novel finding is not merely “regulation reduces entry,” but “screening changes the composition of entrants in a way closely aligned with ex ante concern.”

That distinction is present, but not sharpened enough.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or filling a gap in a LITERATURE?
Too much the latter. The introduction contains “this paper contributes to three literatures,” which is standard but weak. The stronger framing is about the world:

- How do regulators police entry in credence-good markets?
- Can targeted scrutiny substitute for consumer discipline where patients cannot effectively choose?
- Does ownership form proxy for differential regulatory vulnerability in publicly funded care markets?

That is more powerful than “the hospice sector has received little attention from economists.”

### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what’s new?
They could get close, but many would still summarize it as: “a DiD on CMS hospice oversight that finds for-profit entry fell a lot.” That is not enough. The paper needs them to say:

> “It’s a paper about screening versus blanket regulation in healthcare entry: when CMS tightened scrutiny in the states where dubious hospice expansion was concentrated, almost only for-profit entry disappeared.”

That “screening versus blanket restriction” language needs to be made central.

### What would make this contribution bigger?
A few possibilities:

1. **Make the outcome more structural/compositional, not just counts of entrants.**  
   Show that the policy changed the composition of ownership, chain affiliation, PE-backed entry, related-party ownership, or other markers of suspect business models. Right now “for-profit vs nonprofit” is useful but a bit coarse.

2. **Link entry screening to market equilibrium or patient-facing implications.**  
   The paper gestures at quality, but only cross-sectionally and weakly integrated. A bigger paper would connect the entry response to downstream consequences: provider quality, coding, live discharge, geographic access, or spending. Without that, the story remains “regulation changed who entered,” not “regulation improved this market.”

3. **Widen the framing to a broader class of markets.**  
   If this is really about regulation in credence-good markets with public payment, the paper should say so explicitly and maybe briefly compare hospice to home health, DME, or Medicaid managed care contracting.

4. **Make the mechanism more than ownership form.**  
   The paper’s best angle is not “for-profits are bad”; that’s too blunt and invites skepticism. The stronger angle is “when scrutiny rises, entrants whose business models are least robust to verification disappear.” If the data permit, separating independent small for-profits, chain entrants, and other organizational forms would help.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Before this paper, we observe explosive for-profit hospice entry concentrated in a handful of states in a Medicare-funded sector with major information asymmetries and weak consumer discipline at the end of life.

### Tension
Economists are skeptical of entry regulation because it often protects incumbents and reduces supply. But in this setting, laissez-faire entry may admit low-quality or opportunistic firms. So the key tension is: **does stricter oversight merely suppress entry, or does it selectively screen out problematic entrants?**

### Resolution
After CMS imposed enhanced oversight, new hospice entry collapsed in treated states, and essentially all of the decline came from for-profit entrants; nonprofit entry was unchanged.

### Implications
Targeted oversight may operate differently from conventional entry restrictions: it can alter who enters rather than just how many enter. That has implications for healthcare regulation, fraud prevention, and market design in publicly financed credence goods.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
It has the outline of one, but it is not yet fully disciplined. At moments it feels like a collection of results:

- huge entry decline,
- ownership decomposition,
- cross-sectional quality differences,
- randomization inference,
- California carveout,
- claim denial rates.

These pieces are all potentially relevant, but the paper needs to make one story dominant. The story should be:

> In markets where consumers cannot effectively screen providers, the regulator may have to do the screening. PPEO appears to have changed the composition of who was willing or able to enter.

Everything else should serve that arc. Right now some material reads like it was added because it was available rather than because it is narratively indispensable.

In particular, the cross-sectional quality section is useful for motivation but too weakly tied to the main design to carry much argumentative weight. It should either be recast as descriptive background or substantially expanded into a more integrated implication.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?
“CMS introduced enhanced scrutiny for new hospice providers in four states, and new entry fell by about 80 percent — almost entirely because for-profit entry vanished while nonprofit entry didn’t budge.”

That is a good lead fact.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
Some would lean in. The ownership asymmetry is the hook. If you just said “oversight reduced entry,” they would tune out. If you say “it screened out one ownership form almost exactly,” that is more interesting.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Almost certainly:
- “Does that mean it improved quality or just reduced competition?”
- “Were those deterred firms actually fraudulent, or just more compliance-sensitive?”
- “Is this about hospice, or about public-service markets more generally?”

That tells you what the introduction and conclusion need to answer preemptively.

### If the findings are modest or null
Not applicable here; the findings are large. The issue is not small effects but interpretation. The paper must avoid overclaiming that it has shown fraud reduction or welfare improvement. The result is interesting enough without that stretch.
