## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether targeted federal screening of new hospice providers can clean up an end-of-life care market that had become dominated by rapid for-profit entry in a handful of states. It shows that when CMS imposed enhanced oversight on new hospice enrollments in four high-entry states, new entry collapsed almost entirely among for-profit firms, suggesting that regulatory scrutiny can sharply reshape market composition in sectors where public reimbursement and information asymmetries create room for low-quality or fraudulent entry.

A busy economist should care because this is not just a paper about hospice. It is about a broader question: when does regulation screen out bad actors rather than simply suppress supply? That question matters for health care, education, skilled nursing, and any publicly financed market where ownership form, quality, and fraud risk may be linked.

### Does the paper itself articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Not quite. The current opening is vivid and topical, but it is more prosecutorial than analytical. It leads with “the hospice industry has a for-profit problem,” which is rhetorically strong but risks making the paper sound like an exposé or policy brief rather than an economics paper centered on a general question about market design, screening, and organizational form.

The first two paragraphs should do three things more clearly:

1. Start with the broad economic question.
2. Explain why hospice is an especially revealing setting.
3. State the headline result in terms of market screening, not just enrollment decline.

### The pitch the paper should have

Publicly financed service markets often face a basic regulatory tradeoff: tighter entry screening may protect program integrity, but it may also deter socially valuable supply. This paper studies that tradeoff in the U.S. hospice industry, where nearly all recent entry had become for-profit and heavily concentrated in four states. When CMS imposed enhanced oversight of new hospice providers in those states, new entry fell dramatically—and almost entirely among for-profit firms—suggesting that targeted scrutiny can screen the margin of entry rather than simply shut down the market.

That framing is stronger because it turns the paper from “here is a policy change in hospice” into “here is evidence on when regulation screens rather than rations.”

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper provides evidence that targeted entry oversight in hospice sharply reduced new provider entry by for-profit firms while leaving nonprofit entry unchanged, implying that regulatory screening can selectively reshape market composition in publicly financed health care markets.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest papers?

Only partially. The paper says “no causal study evaluates the effect of entry screening on hospice market structure,” which may be true, but that is not enough for AER-level positioning. “First paper on hospice entry screening” is a field-gap claim, not a major intellectual contribution. The more important differentiation is not from other hospice papers per se, but from broader literatures on:

- entry regulation,
- for-profit provision under public payment,
- fraud control and program integrity,
- market screening versus supply restriction.

Right now the paper does not sharply distinguish whether it is:
- a hospice paper,
- a for-profit ownership paper,
- a regulation paper,
- or a fraud-screening paper.

It wants to be all four, but without a hierarchy.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or filling a gap in the LITERATURE?

It starts with the world, which is good, but then retreats quickly into literature-gap framing: hospice has received “remarkably little attention,” “no causal study evaluates...,” etc. That is weaker. AER wants the paper to answer a question about how markets work.

The stronger question is:
**When regulators increase scrutiny in markets with severe information asymmetry and public reimbursement, do they reduce supply indiscriminately, or do they selectively deter the low-value margin of entry?**

That is a world question.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the intro?

A smart economist could say: “It’s a DiD showing that enhanced oversight cut hospice entry in four states, mostly among for-profits.” That is intelligible, but still sounds like “another DiD paper about a policy shock.”

What is missing is the second clause that makes it memorable:
“...and that distinction matters because it suggests oversight can screen entrant type rather than simply depress overall supply.”

That needs to be the conceptual centerpiece, not a decomposition buried as a result.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Most importantly: **show consequences beyond entry counts.** Right now the paper is strongest on who does not enter. But for an AER audience, the natural next question is whether this changed the market in a substantively important way.

Specific ways to make it bigger:

1. **Market consequences**
   - Did incumbent behavior change?
   - Did concentration rise?
   - Did patient access change?
   - Did utilization, lengths of stay, site of death, or hospice penetration change?

2. **Program integrity outcomes**
   - Fraud investigations, claim denials, billing patterns, revocations, anomalous utilization?
   - Even descriptive evidence would help make “screening” more than an inference from ownership type.

3. **Quality consequences**
   - Not just cross-sectional differences in treated states, which are weakly connected to the intervention.
   - Better: post-policy changes in quality, visit intensity, live discharge, complaint rates, or spending among surviving/new cohorts.

4. **Displacement**
   - Did entry rise in nearby or similar untreated states?
   - If yes, this becomes a paper about spatial reallocation of suspect entry under targeted regulation—a bigger story.

5. **Mechanism more directly tied to entrant characteristics**
   - Chain-affiliated vs independent.
   - PE-backed vs not.
   - Prior ownership histories.
   - Re-enrollments/transfers vs genuinely new firms.
   This would make the “screening” claim feel more economic and less moralistic.

In short: the current paper shows a large compositional response. To be bigger, it should show what that response means for the functioning of the market.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

A publicly financed end-of-life care market has seen explosive entry by for-profit providers, heavily concentrated in a small set of states, amid concerns about low quality and fraud. In these markets, patient choice and competitive discipline are weak because consumers are vulnerable, quality is hard to observe, and Medicare pays.

### Tension

Regulators face a classic dilemma: stricter oversight may curb opportunistic entry, but it may also suppress legitimate supply. We do not know whether enhanced scrutiny in such markets acts like a blunt barrier to entry or a selective screen.

### Resolution

When CMS imposed enhanced oversight on new hospice providers in four states, new entry fell dramatically, and essentially all of the reduction came from for-profit entrants; nonprofit entry did not change.

### Implications

Targeted oversight may be capable of screening the entrant pool rather than indiscriminately reducing provider supply. More broadly, in publicly financed credence-good markets, ex ante scrutiny may improve market composition where downstream consumer discipline is weak.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the ingredients, but not fully the arc. Right now it reads somewhat like:

- Here is a bad industry pattern.
- Here is a policy intervention.
- Here are the estimates.
- Here are some quality differences supporting the story.

That is serviceable, but still feels a bit like a collection of related facts orbiting the claim that for-profit hospice entry is problematic.

### What story should it be telling?

The story should be:

**This is a paper about regulation as screening technology.** Hospice is the ideal setting because:
- quality is hard for consumers to observe,
- public reimbursement creates entry incentives,
- organizational form predicts behavior,
- and the policy was targeted rather than sector-wide.

That story is cleaner and more general than “for-profit hospices are bad and CMS cracked down.”

   The introductory details about exact review counts, revocations, and claim denial percentages are useful, but the intro is overloaded with them. Better to keep one or two memorable facts and leave the rest for background.

4. **Be careful with loaded language.**
   “The American hospice industry has a for-profit problem” is punchy but overshoots. It makes the paper sound polemical and invites readers to push back before hearing the evidence. Tone down the moral rhetoric; amplify the economic puzzle.

5. **The quality section is currently doing too much argumentative work for weak evidence.**
   Cross-sectional quality differences are not central evidence on the policy. They should be clearly labeled as background/descriptive context, not as quasi-confirmation of the main mechanism.

6. **Conclusion should do more than summarize.**
   The current conclusion is fairly strong, but it could do a better job clarifying what economists should update on:
   - not “for-profit hospice is bad,”
   - but “targeted oversight can act as screening rather than quantity restriction in credence-good markets.”

Mostly yes. The main result arrives early enough. But the best conceptual point—the selective deterrence of for-profit entry as evidence on screening—is not foregrounded enough.

### Are there results buried in robustness that should be in the main text?

Potentially the leave-one-out and California exclusion matter a lot narratively because readers will immediately worry the result is “just California.” The California issue should be integrated into the main empirical discussion more centrally.

Somewhat. But it still leans toward advocacy. It should end with the broader economics lesson, not the normative indictment of the pre-policy market.
