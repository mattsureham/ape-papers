## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether tobacco advertising bans generate measurable fiscal savings in healthcare spending, not just health benefits. Using staggered adoption of tobacco billboard bans across Swiss cantons, it argues that restricting advertising lowers per-capita health insurance costs, especially hospital spending, with effects that build over time.

Why should a busy economist care? Because the paper tries to connect a classic regulation question—do ad bans reduce harmful consumption?—to a broader public finance question: do behavioral regulations pay for themselves, at least partly, through lower medical spending?

Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs? **Mostly, but not as sharply as it should.** The current opening is competent and policy-relevant, but it is still a bit “Swiss healthcare costs are large; here is my natural experiment.” For AER, the opening should be less about Switzerland and more about the general economic question: **when does sin regulation create fiscal dividends, and can we measure them credibly?** The Swiss setting should enter as the vehicle, not the headline.

### The pitch the paper should have

> Governments regulate tobacco advertising to improve health, but the fiscal payoff of those regulations is largely unknown. This paper asks a simple question with broad relevance for public economics and health policy: when governments restrict exposure to tobacco advertising, do healthcare expenditures actually fall?
>
> I answer this question using staggered adoption of tobacco billboard bans across Swiss cantons and administrative data covering the universe of insured residents. The central result is that billboard bans reduce healthcare spending, especially hospital costs, and that these savings grow gradually over time—suggesting that behavioral regulation can generate meaningful long-run fiscal returns, not just health benefits.

That is the AER version of the paper. Right now the paper is close to that, but not fully there.

### One-sentence contribution

### Evaluation

**Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3-4 papers?**

That distinction is there, but it should be made cleaner and earlier.

It is **mixed**, but it should be much more firmly framed as a question about the world. The strong version is: *Do ad bans merely shift marketing margins, or do they generate real fiscal savings through better health?* The weaker version is: *No paper has yet estimated this specific effect in this specific setting.* The introduction currently contains too much of the latter.

**Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what’s new?**  
They could probably say: “It’s a staggered DiD on Swiss tobacco billboard bans and healthcare spending.” That is not good enough. The paper risks sounding like “another DiD paper about tobacco regulation” unless the author constantly emphasizes that the real novelty is **linking advertising regulation to realized public/private medical expenditure**.

**What would make this contribution bigger?**  
Several possibilities:

1. **Stronger fiscal framing.**  
   Translate the estimates into a clearer fiscal object: insurer spending, premium pressure, cantonal hospital finance, or public subsidy implications. “Healthcare costs” is broad; “fiscal dividend” promises public finance relevance. The paper needs to cash that out more concretely.

2. **Stronger bridge from regulation to economics, not just health policy.**

4. **A cleaner welfare/policy comparison.**

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Governments widely restrict tobacco advertising because smoking is harmful, but the downstream budgetary payoff of those restrictions is uncertain. We know regulation may affect smoking, yet we do not know whether it meaningfully reduces healthcare spending.

### Tension
Advertising bans are politically salient and widely used, but their economic payoff is hard to measure because health improvements are slow-moving and expenditure effects are noisy. So the puzzle is whether this form of regulation produces a real fiscal dividend or simply symbolic public health policy.

### Resolution
Using staggered canton-level billboard bans, the paper finds lower healthcare spending, concentrated in hospital categories and increasing over time.

### Implications
If true, advertising restrictions have not only health benefits but also fiscal benefits, which changes how economists and policymakers should evaluate this policy tool relative to taxes and cessation interventions.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

**It has the ingredients, but not a fully disciplined arc.** The paper does tell a story, but it also reads at times like a competent sequence of results: main estimate, event study, category decomposition, robustness, discussion. The narrative is there, but the author keeps interrupting it with methodological throat-clearing and literature accounting.

The story it should be telling is:

1. **Policy relevance:** ad bans are common but their fiscal payoff is unknown.  
2. **Conceptual contribution:** fiscal effects are the missing downstream object.  
3. **Empirical opportunity:** Swiss cantons let us observe this with administrative spending data.  
4. **Main result:** bans lower spending, especially hospital spending, and slowly.  
5. **Interpretation:** this is what one would expect if regulation reduces smoking-related disease.  
6. **Broader implication:** economists should evaluate sin regulation not only by behavior but by downstream budget consequences.

That story is stronger than the current “here is my natural experiment and set of estimates.”

I would lead with: **“Tobacco advertising bans appear to reduce healthcare spending, with the biggest savings showing up in hospital costs and building over a decade.”**

**Some would lean in, but not all.** Health economists and public economists would lean in. Others would ask: is this just a niche Swiss tobacco paper? The current framing does not fully prevent that reaction.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Probably one of these:

- “Is this about smoking behavior, or about some broader package of tobacco-control policies?”
- “How big are the savings relative to the policy’s broader welfare effects?”
- “Does this tell us anything general about ad regulation versus tobacco taxes?”
- “Why do the effects show up where they do?”

Strategically, the paper should be designed to answer those questions at the level of framing, even before the econometrics.

### If findings are modest or null-like

The paper is in an awkward middle ground. The point estimates are economically interesting, but the paper itself foregrounds enough caution that the reader may come away feeling the result is suggestive rather than definitive. That is not fatal, but it means the **story** has to do more work. The author needs to make the case that even suggestive evidence on realized spending effects is valuable because the policy debate has mostly operated on assumptions rather than expenditure evidence.

At present, the paper does this somewhat, but not forcefully enough. It sometimes reads like “we found something interesting, though please note the caveats,” rather than “this is the first credible evidence on an important downstream margin.”
