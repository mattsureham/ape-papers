### 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
This paper asks whether banning non-compete agreements (NCAs) can reduce racial inequality in the labor market. Using a quadruple-difference design, it finds that while bans do not close the racial gap in job-switching (mobility), they significantly increase earnings for Black workers in high-NCA industries. This suggests that NCAs suppress wages for Black workers not by preventing moves, but by undermining their bargaining power—a "bargaining dividend" that materializes once the threat of exit becomes credible.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this well in the second paragraph. However, the first paragraph is a bit "fact-heavy" on summary statistics. 
*Suggested revision for the first two paragraphs:* Lead with the theoretical tension. "Non-compete agreements are theorized to suppress wages by restricting mobility, but if different groups face different costs to challenging these contracts, then universal bans may function as a racial equity tool. This paper tests whether state-level NCA bans close racial gaps in earnings and turnover, using administrative data to isolate the 'bargaining' versus 'mobility' channels of inequality."

### 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**One-sentence contribution:** The paper identifies a racially asymmetric "bargaining dividend" from non-compete bans, proving that contract-based wage suppression contributes to the racial wage gap independently of actual job-to-job mobility.

*   **Differentiation:** It differentiates itself from **Starr (2019)** and **Johnson et al. (2023)** by shifting focus from the average worker to racial heterogeneity. It moves beyond **Bertrand & Mullainathan (2004)** by focusing on *contractual* rather than *preferential* barriers.
*   **Framing:** It is framed as a question about the **WORLD** (racial equity and labor power).
*   **Clarity:** A smart economist would immediately grasp the "threat of exit vs. actual exit" distinction. 
*   **Bigger Contribution:** To make this "AER-big," the author needs to explore *why* the mobility gap doesn't budge. Is it because of "employer-side discrimination in hiring" (as the paper hints)? Adding even a descriptive analysis of whether Black workers *receive* fewer outside offers in these sectors would turn this from a policy evaluation into a landmark study on the anatomy of discrimination.

### 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
The paper sits at the intersection of the **New Monopsony** literature (Manning, Naidu) and **Racial Inequality**.
*   **Neighbors:** Starr et al. (2021) on NCA prevalence; Kline et al. (2022) on systemic discrimination; Shi (2023) on bargaining models.
*   **Strategy:** The paper correctly builds on the NCA literature but should more aggressively "attack" or at least challenge the Silicon Valley "mobility-only" narrative (Gilson, 1999). It should position itself as a bridge between the Law & Economics of contracts and the Structural Labor literature on wage gaps.
*   **Missing Conversations:** It should speak more to the **Search and Matching** literature. The "bargaining dividend" is essentially an increase in the worker's share of the match surplus once the outside option is unblocked.

### 4. NARRATIVE ARC
*   **Setup:** The labor market is rife with NCAs and racial wage gaps.
*   **Tension:** If NCAs restrict mobility, and mobility is the way to escape low wages, do Black workers (who have fewer resources to fight NCAs) suffer a "double penalty"?
*   **Resolution:** Yes, but the penalty isn't what we thought. It's not that they *can't* move; it's that they can't *threaten* to move.
*   **Implications:** Closing the wage gap requires fixing the rules of the game (contracts), but closing the mobility gap requires fixing the players (hiring discrimination).

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong. The "bargaining dividend" vs. "mobility dividend" is a compelling narrative hook.

### 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
At an economist dinner party, I’d lead with: *"Banning non-competes gave Black workers in tech a 4% raise without them even having to change jobs."* 
*   **Reaction:** Lean in. It challenges the intuition that you have to actually leave a job to get a "non-compete raise."
*   **Follow-up:** "If they aren't switching jobs more, how do they know they have the power?" (This leads back to the credible threat/bargaining theory).

### 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
*   **Front-loading:** The DDDD results are the star. The simple DiD (Column 1, Table 2) is a distraction—move it to the appendix or a footnote. 
*   **Robustness:** Table 4 (Gap Evolution) is actually more interesting than the authors realize. It shows a persistent narrowing of the earnings gap post-2020. This should be a figure in the main text to visualize the "parallel trends" and the "break" at treatment.
*   **Section 6 (Discussion):** This needs to be beefed up. The "Limitations" section is too defensive. Instead, turn the "five-state" limitation into a call for broader federal policy, using the results as a proof-of-concept.

### 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The distance is **Medium**. The "Science" is competent (DDD/DDDD is standard), but the "Story" is the path to the AER.
*   **The Ambition Problem:** The paper currently feels like a very high-quality policy evaluation. To be a top-tier AER paper, it needs to be a **Theory-Testing** paper.
*   **Single Biggest Improvement:** Explicitly link the empirical "bargaining dividend" to a formal (even if simple) bargaining model in the spirit of Nash or Binmore. If the author can show that the 3.8% raise is mathematically consistent with the removal of a specific "litigation cost" or "search cost" for Black workers, the paper moves from "neat finding" to "essential theory."
