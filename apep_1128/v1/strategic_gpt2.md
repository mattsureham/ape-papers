## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether banning non-compete agreements reduces racial inequality in labor markets. Its core claim is striking: in knowledge-intensive industries, recent state non-compete bans appear to raise Black workers’ earnings relative to White workers, but do not narrow racial gaps in turnover—suggesting that the main effect is improved bargaining power inside jobs, not more mobility across jobs.

A busy economist should care because this connects two active conversations that usually sit apart: non-competes as a labor market power institution, and racial inequality as a product of differential constraints and discrimination. If true, the paper says labor market regulation can reduce racial wage gaps even when it does not change observed job switching.

### Does the paper articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Reasonably clearly, but not optimally. The current opening is competent and informative, but it still reads a bit like “here is a policy, here is a design, here is a result.” It undersells the paper’s most interesting idea: non-compete bans may change who has credible outside options, and that matters for racial inequality even without visible mobility responses.

The introduction should open less with prevalence of NCAs and more with the puzzle:

1. If non-competes work by suppressing mobility, then banning them should increase job switching.
2. But if the real action is bargaining power, wages may rise even when mobility does not.
3. For Black workers, that distinction is especially important because outside options may be less usable even before any actual move occurs.

### The pitch the paper should have

“Do non-compete bans reduce racial inequality by helping Black workers move to better jobs, or by strengthening their bargaining position in their current jobs? Using recent state bans and administrative worker data, this paper finds that bans raise Black earnings in high–non-compete industries relative to White workers, but do not reduce racial turnover gaps. The central implication is that non-competes widen racial inequality less by physically trapping workers than by weakening the credibility of their outside options.”

That is the paper’s AER-worthy version. The current intro is close, but not yet sharp enough.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper’s contribution is to show that non-compete bans may narrow racial earnings gaps in knowledge-intensive sectors without increasing Black worker mobility, implying a bargaining-power channel rather than a mobility channel.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3-4 papers?

Partly, but not enough. The paper says it is “the first evidence on racial heterogeneity in the effects of NCA bans,” which may be true, but “first evidence on heterogeneity” is not by itself a compelling top-journal contribution. The stronger differentiator is not heterogeneity per se; it is the paper’s conceptual claim that labor market regulation can affect racial inequality through bargaining without changing realized transitions.

Right now the paper is differentiated mostly by:
- a race interaction,
- a quadruple-difference design,
- and a new label (“bargaining dividend”).

That is not yet enough. The paper needs to differentiate itself on the substantive question about the world: **Do outside-option institutions matter for racial inequality mainly through movement or through bargaining?**

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or filling a gap in a LITERATURE?

Mixed, but tilted too much toward literature-gap framing. The strongest parts are world-facing: do bans change mobility or wages, and what does that reveal about racial inequality? But the intro drifts into “contributes to three literatures” mode too quickly, including an unnecessary “methodological contribution” claim. That weakens the pitch.

For AER, the contribution should be framed almost entirely as a fact about the world:
- Black workers’ outside options appear to matter for pay even when they do not translate into actual turnover.
- Therefore, some labor market inequalities are about the credibility of exit, not exit itself.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the intro?

Yes, but only if they are sympathetic. A smart economist could say: “It’s a paper on non-compete bans showing Black wages rise but Black turnover doesn’t, so maybe bans improve bargaining power more than mobility.” That is the good version.

The risk is that another smart economist says: “It’s another staggered DiD on state policy with subgroup heterogeneity.” The paper is not yet far enough from that second reaction.

### What would make the contribution bigger?

Three possibilities:

1. **Sharper mechanism framing.**  
   The paper should more directly claim that observed mobility is an incomplete measure of outside options. That is a broader point with implications beyond non-competes.

2. **Better outcome hierarchy.**  
   If the wage result is the headline, the paper should orient the entire narrative around wage-setting and only then use turnover/hiring as tests of competing channels. Right now outcomes are presented too symmetrically.

3. **Broader conceptual comparison.**  
   The paper would be bigger if it explicitly compared two views of racial inequality:
   - barriers to changing employers,
   - versus weak bargaining power within existing employment relationships.  
   That is a much larger framing than “effects of NCA bans by race.”

If the author could add only one substantive dimension, it would be **within-job wage growth or retention/tenure patterns** to better tie the story to bargaining within ongoing matches. Even absent new data, the framing should emphasize that this is about wage-setting within firms, not just mobility regulation.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

### Closest neighbors

The nearest neighbors appear to be:

- On the racial inequality side, likely:
  - **Bertrand and Mullainathan (2004)**
  - **Kline, Rose, and Walters (2022)** or adjacent work on systemic discrimination
  - perhaps **Lang and Lehmann (2012)** style review framing

Also, conceptually, the paper should be in conversation with labor market power and outside-option bargaining work, not just the non-compete literature.

### How should the paper position itself?

Mostly **build on**, not attack.

This is not a paper overturning prior non-compete results. If anything, it should say:
- the existing literature has emphasized wages and mobility for the average worker;
- this paper asks what those same institutions imply for racial inequality;
- the key new lesson is that wage effects need not be accompanied by realized mobility effects, especially for groups facing broader labor market frictions.

That is a constructive extension and reinterpretation of the literature, not a repudiation.

### Is it positioned too narrowly or too broadly?

Paradoxically, both.

- **Too narrowly** in empirical packaging: it can read like a paper about five recent state bans, two NAICS sectors, one placebo sector, and one subgroup interaction.
- **Too broadly** in contribution claims: the intro says it contributes to three literatures, including methodology, which diffuses focus.

The right audience is narrower but deeper: labor economists interested in labor market power, contract restrictions, wage setting, and racial inequality. The paper should own that conversation rather than trying to gesture at everything.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of, or underengaged with?

Two underexploited conversations:

1. **Monopsony / labor market power / outside-option literature.**  
   The paper should talk more explicitly to the idea that institutions altering exit options can change wages without actual separations. That is central to the interpretation.

2. **Within-firm inequality / rent-sharing / bargaining literature.**  
   If the finding is fundamentally about bargaining power at the current employer, the paper should connect to wage-setting and surplus division, not just discrimination in hiring.

Also, the “mobility versus bargaining” distinction has resonance with job ladder and search models. That conceptual literature could help make the story feel less like a policy note and more like a general labor-market insight.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?

Not quite yet. It is currently having a conversation with “the non-compete literature plus a race heterogeneity add-on.” The more impactful conversation is:

**What do labor market institutions reveal about the mechanism behind racial inequality—restricted mobility, or weakened bargaining power?**

That reframing would substantially raise the paper’s ceiling.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Non-competes are widespread in high-skill labor markets and are thought to suppress wages and mobility. Black workers may be especially constrained by them because the practical costs of using outside options are higher.

### Tension

If non-competes constrain Black workers more, then banning them should reduce racial inequality. But through what margin? We might expect more mobility—but if broader discrimination still limits job changes, perhaps the main response will instead be better pay in existing jobs.

### Resolution

After recent state bans, Black workers in knowledge-intensive sectors earn more relative to White workers, but do not separate at higher rates and do not show clear hiring gains.

### Implications

The paper implies that labor market institutions can widen racial wage gaps by weakening credible exit threats even when they do not visibly reduce job switching. Policy can therefore affect racial inequality through bargaining power, but such policy alone may not solve employer-side barriers to hiring and mobility.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has one, but it is not fully capitalized on. The ingredients are all there; the execution is still too results-list-like. The introduction proceeds from policy background to design to identification to coefficients to literature, rather than leaning into the puzzle.

The story should be:

1. Non-competes are supposed to matter by blocking movement.
2. For racial inequality, that prediction is especially testable.
3. But the surprising fact is that wages move and turnover does not.
4. Therefore, actual movement is the wrong margin for understanding some institutional effects on inequality.

That is a real narrative. The current draft too often sounds like a careful applied paper explaining its specification.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party?

“State non-compete bans seem to raise Black workers’ earnings in tech/professional sectors without increasing Black turnover.”

That is the memorable fact.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

Lean in—at least initially. The combination of racial inequality, labor market institutions, and the “without more mobility” twist is interesting. The finding is counterintuitive in a useful way.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Probably one of these:
- “So if they’re not switching jobs, where do the gains come from exactly?”
- “Does this mean non-competes mostly matter through bargaining rather than mobility?”
- “Why Black workers in particular—what makes outside options less usable for them?”
- “Is this a broad fact or a narrow feature of a few states and sectors?”

Those are good follow-up questions. They indicate the paper has a live core idea.

### If findings are modest or null, is the null interesting?

Yes, the null on turnover is absolutely interesting—but only if the paper really leans into it as the puzzle, not as a disappointing secondary result. Right now the paper does that somewhat, but it could do so more forcefully. The null is not “we failed to find mobility.” The null is: **the institutional effect appears in wages without appearing in realized exits.** That is the paper’s conceptual leverage.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

This is primarily a **framing and ambition problem**, with some scope concerns.

The paper’s best version is not “a quadruple-difference study of racial heterogeneity in non-compete bans.” That is field-journal language.

Its best version is: **a paper showing that outside-option institutions affect racial wage inequality through bargaining rather than realized mobility.** That is a much bigger statement and potentially a top-journal one.

### What is the gap to AER?

- **Framing problem:** substantial. The science may be competent, but the story is still packaged too much as a policy evaluation exercise.
- **Scope problem:** moderate. The outcomes are a bit narrow, and the mechanism remains somewhat inferred rather than richly developed.
- **Novelty problem:** moderate. Non-compete papers are now common; “heterogeneity by race” alone is not enough. The novelty has to come from the mobility-versus-bargaining insight.
- **Ambition problem:** yes. The paper is careful and plausible, but currently too safe in its self-presentation.

### Single most impactful advice

Reframe the paper around one big question: **Do institutions that expand outside options reduce racial inequality through actual worker mobility, or through stronger bargaining within existing jobs?** Everything else should serve that question.

If the author does only one thing, it should be to rewrite the introduction, results framing, and conclusion so that this is unmistakably the paper’s core contribution.
