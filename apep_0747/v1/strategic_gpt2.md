## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether weakening neighbors’ legal rights against pollution changes who lives near polluters. Using staggered expansions of state Right-to-Farm laws that insulated CAFOs from nuisance suits, it argues that reallocating property rights toward factory farms induces demographic sorting in exposed rural counties, increasing Hispanic population share. A busy economist should care because the paper’s core claim is larger than agriculture: environmental inequality may be shaped not just by where dirty facilities are sited, but by how law reshapes residential equilibrium afterward.

The paper does articulate much of this pitch in the first two paragraphs, but not in the sharpest possible way. Right now the introduction starts from CAFOs and environmental justice, then moves into policy variation. That is competent, but the paper’s highest-value framing is not “another paper on CAFOs”; it is “property-rights changes can cause environmental injustice through sorting.” The first two paragraphs should lead with that broader claim.

### The pitch the paper should have

“When legal rules make it harder for residents to challenge local pollution, do polluted places become systematically poorer and more disadvantaged? This paper studies seven recent expansions of state Right-to-Farm laws that shield factory farms from nuisance lawsuits and shows that, in counties with intensive hog production, these laws change demographic composition in ways consistent with environmental-quality sorting. The central message is that environmental injustice is not determined only by where polluters locate; it is also produced by how property-rights regimes reallocate bargaining power and thereby alter who remains, who leaves, and who moves in.”

That is the AER-facing pitch. CAFOs are the setting, not the headline.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:**  
The paper claims to provide causal evidence that weakening nuisance rights around industrial agriculture changes local demographic composition, supporting a Tiebout-style sorting mechanism as a driver of environmental injustice.

This is a real contribution, but it is only partially differentiated from the neighboring literatures as currently written.

### Is it clearly differentiated from the closest papers?
Not yet clearly enough. The paper cites the right broad literatures, but the differentiation is still somewhat generic:

- environmental justice papers document disproportionate exposure;
- Been/Banzhaf raise sorting versus siting;
- this paper uses a legal shock to isolate sorting.

That logic is good, but the paper needs to be much more explicit about exactly what prior papers *could not* do and what this one uniquely does. The key differentiator is not just “causal evidence,” which every paper claims. It is: **a policy shock that changes recourse rights without moving facilities, allowing the paper to study post-siting demographic adjustment.** That point should be repeated and sharpened.

### World question or literature-gap question?
It is mixed, but too often framed as filling a literature gap. The stronger framing is a world question:

- weaker: “I provide the first causal test of the ‘coming to the nuisance’ hypothesis identified by Banzhaf.”
- stronger: “Do legal protections for polluters themselves create environmental inequality by changing residential sorting?”

That second version is about the world and has more punch.

### Could a smart economist explain what is new?
At present, maybe, but not confidently. A smart reader could say: “It’s a DiD/DDD paper on Right-to-Farm laws and Hispanic population share in hog counties.” That is not enough. You want them to say: “It shows that property-rights changes can generate environmental injustice through endogenous sorting, even holding facility location fixed.”

That is the version that travels.

### What would make the contribution bigger?
Several possibilities:

1. **Make the object of interest broader than Hispanic share.**  
   Right now the flagship result is one compositional outcome, and specifically Hispanic share. That makes the contribution feel narrower and more contingent than it should. If the big idea is sorting, the paper would be larger if the main empirical object were a broader index of socioeconomic vulnerability or a clearer margin of in/out migration, housing turnover, rents, or occupancy.

2. **Directly foreground the property-rights channel rather than the demographic subgroup.**  
   If the best evidence remains demographic, then the paper should not oversell “Hispanic share” as the contribution. The contribution is about legal recourse and sorting; Hispanic share is one manifestation.

3. **Show the result matters beyond CAFOs.**  
   Even if no extra empirics are added, the paper should frame itself as evidence for a general mechanism relevant to pollution governance, nuisance law, and local public economics. Otherwise it will read as a niche agriculture paper.

4. **Clarify whether this is about environmental justice or labor-market sorting.**  
   The paper currently invites the concern that this is partly an agricultural labor-demand story. Even without resolving that econometrically here, the framing must acknowledge that the paper speaks to a broader question: when legal shocks lower local amenity value, who sorts in? That is bigger than any one ethnic group.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Polluting facilities often sit near disadvantaged communities, but it is hard to know whether that pattern is produced by discriminatory siting, subsequent sorting, or both. Meanwhile, nuisance law is one of the main ways neighbors can resist local pollution.

### Tension
If states weaken those legal protections, what happens? Facilities do not necessarily move, but the value of living nearby may change. That creates a conceptual possibility: environmental injustice can be produced after siting, through changes in property rights and residential sorting.

### Resolution
The paper finds that when states expand Right-to-Farm protections, high-CAFO counties see increases in Hispanic population share relative to less exposed places, consistent with sorting after a legal shock.

### Implications
Environmental justice is partly endogenous to legal institutions. Policies that shield polluters may not just reduce litigation; they may alter the social incidence of pollution by changing who lives nearby.

### Does the paper have a clear arc?
It has the ingredients of a clear narrative arc, but the paper still feels somewhat like a collection of empirical results in search of the biggest story. The current draft oscillates among several stories:

- CAFO environmental justice,
- Right-to-Farm laws as legal history,
- Tiebout sorting,
- Hispanic demographic change,
- agricultural labor market dynamics.

The author needs to choose one primary story and subordinate the rest.

### What story should it be telling?
The paper should tell this story:

1. **Nuisance rights are a key local environmental protection.**
2. **When those rights are weakened, local amenity value falls in exposed places.**
3. **That changes residential equilibrium, not necessarily facility location.**
4. **Therefore environmental inequality can be caused by law-induced sorting.**
5. **Right-to-Farm expansions provide a rare clean test of that mechanism.**

Everything else is supporting material.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?
“I’d lead with: when states immunize factory farms from nuisance suits, the demographic composition of high-exposure rural counties shifts in ways consistent with environmental sorting.”

That is the dinner-party fact. Not the exact 0.29 percentage points. The exact estimate comes later.

### Would people lean in?
Some would lean in—especially environmental, urban, public, and law-and-econ economists—because the *mechanism* is interesting. But many would reach for their phones if the paper is presented as “a county-level DDD on Hispanic share after Right-to-Farm reforms.” The fact pattern is not naturally universal; the framing has to do the work.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Immediately: **“How do you know this is sorting rather than labor demand or some other agriculture-related compositional change?”**

That is not a referee-style identification complaint; it is the obvious substantive question about interpretation. Even if the paper cannot fully settle it, the framing should anticipate it better. Right now the paper admits alternative mechanisms rather late. That weakens the story because the first thing an economist wants to know is whether this is really about environmental-quality capitalization versus labor markets.

### Are modest findings still interesting?
Yes, potentially. The result size is modest, but modest effects can still matter here because the contribution is conceptual: demonstrating that legal rights can shape environmental inequality through equilibrium sorting. However, the paper should not spend so much energy defending the magnitude in standardized terms. That reads slightly defensive. The way to make a modest effect interesting is not to say “small but meaningful”; it is to show that the effect reveals a mechanism we have struggled to identify.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

Honest answer: the main gap is **not primarily technical; it is conceptual ambition and framing.** There is a real paper here, but in current form it reads like a competent field paper with one suggestive result, not yet like a must-read general-interest contribution.

### What is the gap?

#### 1. Framing problem
This is the biggest one. The paper is underselling its strongest idea and overselling its narrowest result. It should be a paper about **how legal rights shape the social incidence of pollution through sorting**, not a paper mainly about **Hispanic share in hog counties**.

#### 2. Scope problem
Also real. For AER, the payoff from one main compositional outcome is thin. The paper would feel bigger with either:
- stronger evidence on the mechanism chain, or
- broader evidence on distributional consequences.

Without that, the paper risks feeling like a narrow application of a familiar empirical design.

#### 3. Novelty problem
Moderate, not fatal. The underlying conceptual ingredients—sorting, capitalization, environmental justice, legal shocks—are all familiar. The novelty is in their combination and in this institutional setting. That can work, but only if the paper insists clearly on what is new.

Yes. The draft feels careful but safe. It is written as if it is trying not to overclaim. That is admirable scientifically, but editorially it leaves value on the table. AER papers usually make readers update on a larger question. This one could, if it leaned harder into the general mechanism.

### Single most impactful advice
**Reframe the paper from a CAFO-demography study into a general paper on how reallocating nuisance rights changes the equilibrium distribution of environmental burdens.**

That is the one change that most increases the paper’s ceiling. If the author cannot make readers believe that this is a paper about legal incidence and environmental sorting in general, it will remain a narrower field contribution.
