The manifest promised a comprehensive analysis of “Deaths of Despair Through the Resource Cycle,” including cause-specific mortality (suicide, drug overdose, alcohol, traffic accidents) over the full boom-bust period (1999–2021) using CS-DiD with shale exposure, and explicitly included tests of asymmetry and causal decompositions. **Summary**

This paper asks whether counties with pre-boom oil-and-gas exposure experienced different drug-overdose mortality trajectories during the shale boom (2005–2014) and the early bust (2015). Using county and year fixed effects (and occasional state trends), the author finds no average effect of oil exposure on drug overdose rates but uncovers a “selective shield”: in counties with above-median pre-boom overdose rates, oil exposure significantly reduced mortality growth in both the boom and 2015 bust. The key estimates rely on a triple-difference interacting oil exposure with pre-boom vulnerability, suggesting that economic opportunity protected already-vulnerable communities.

**Essential Points**

1. **Identification relies on a potentially endogenous treatment indicator.** The binary oil exposure indicator (any NAICS 211 establishment in 2001–2004) may capture persistent county characteristics—such as rurality, baseline economic structure, or access to formal employment—that are themselves correlated with drug overdose trajectories over time. With only county and year fixed effects, the paper assumes that there are no diverging pre-trends conditional on this indicator beyond what the event study shows. Yet the event study coefficients are noisy and some early post-2004 coefficients are borderline significant; without richer controls for differential pre-boom trends or measurable characteristics, it is difficult to rule out that the interaction with high pre-boom overdose levels simply proxies for counties that were already on different trajectories. A more credible identification strategy would exploit geological variation directly (e.g., distance to identified shale plays) or include semi-parametric controls for pre-boom mortality trends and socio-demographic characteristics to ensure the high-pre-boom interaction isn’t confounded by omitted variables.

2. **The treatment is time-invariant despite dynamic exposure to the boom/bust cycle.** By fixing the treatment at the 2001–2004 establishment count, the paper implicitly treats all “oil counties” as equally exposed during 2005–2015, yet shale activity varied substantially across counties and over time (e.g., Bakken vs. Marcellus, or early vs. late entrants). If vulnerable counties also tend to be those with persistently high establishment growth or greater gig activity, the triple-difference may capture those dynamics rather than a pure differential response to pre-boom vulnerability. A credible test would interact an evolving measure of oil/gas activity (production, drilling permits, employment) with the boom/bust indicators and compare it to the pre-boom classification, ensuring that the heterogeneous effect reflects the actual intensity of the cycle rather than static geography.

3. **Mechanism and external validity are only loosely tied to the proposed “selective shield.”** The narrative rests on the idea that employment and economic opportunity shielded vulnerable populations, yet the paper does not present direct evidence about labor-market outcomes, treatment capacity, or migration patterns that would substantiate this channel. Without it, alternative interpretations (e.g., targeted public health efforts in resource-rich counties, better reporting differences, or differential fentanyl timing) remain plausible. Establishing the mechanism is crucial especially since the average effect is null and all the substance of the paper hinges on a heterogeneous finding; if the “shield” is misattributed, policy implications may be misleading.

If these issues are not addressed satisfactorily, the paper risks having an identification strategy that is too fragile and anecdotal to support its claims, and I would recommend rejection.

**Suggestions**

1. **Strengthen the treatment variation.** Consider exploiting continuous measures of shale potential or boom intensity, rather than a simple binary based on pre-boom establishments. For example, use county-level drilling rig counts, oil/gas production volumes, or HDD-level estimates of shale acreage to construct exposure. These measures can be used in a dose-response framework (continuous treatment) and allow the boom/bust interaction to reflect actual economic shocks instead of a pre-existing label. Doing so would also facilitate using the HonestDiD or CS-DiD methods mentioned in the manifest and would align more closely with the geological endowment argument.

2. **Control for differential pre-trends more explicitly.** While the event study provides a baseline check, the pre-period estimates are noisy, and the heterogeneity analysis depends on comparisons within quintiles. Include county-specific quadratic trends (or even more flexible splines) interacted with the vulnerability indicator to ensure that the selective effect is not driven by differing secular trends. Alternatively, implement an event-study that allows the high-vulnerability subgroup to have its own set of leads to verify that the pre-trends are flat within that group. These exercises would build confidence that the triple-difference is capturing a causal effect of the boom rather than pre-existing divergence.

3. **Directly test the proposed mechanism.** The “economic opportunity shielding vulnerable communities” story would be bolstered by showing that oil counties with high pre-boom overdose rates experienced larger labor-market improvements (e.g., employment growth, wage increases, insurance coverage, opioid prescription reductions) during the boom relative to comparable non-oil counties, and that these improvements correlate with the mortality effects. This could involve linking CBP employment data, ACS earnings, or BLS unemployment rates to the main regression. If such mediators are unavailable, consider falsification tests: does the selective shield appear for other health outcomes unrelated to employment (e.g., cancer deaths)? If not, that would support the story.

4. **Extend the analysis beyond the 2015 bust.** The manifest promised a longer boom-bust window through 2021, which also would allow testing whether the shield persisted (e.g., through the fentanyl wave) or reversed. If later mortality data are available (the NVSS microdata mentioned in the manifest), incorporate them even if only for a subset of cause-specific outcomes; this would make the policy claims about “deaths of despair” more compelling.

5. **Expand outcomes to other “despair” causes.** Even if the analysis focuses on drug overdoses, exploring whether similar heterogeneity appears for suicides or alcohol-related deaths would show whether the selective shield is broader than the opioid epidemic, aligning better with the original research question and with the resource-cause framing. If data limitations prevent that, acknowledge the limitation explicitly and discuss how the current result might or might not generalize to other causes.

6. **Reconcile measurement error concerns with the null results.** The mortality rates are derived from categorical bins with midpoint imputation, which introduces classical measurement error but also adds heteroskedasticity (since counties with fewer deaths may be more likely to be top-coded). Consider re-estimating using counties with sufficient counts, restricting to larger counties, or comparing with NVSS microdata to show that the null average effect is not an artifact of measurement error. Alternatively, use simulation or sensitivity analysis to quantify how much differential measurement error could influence the triple-difference.

7. **Clarify the scope of the protective effect.** Present the heterogeneous results in terms of county-years or cumulative deaths avoided to make the policy implications more concrete. For example, estimate how many fewer overdose deaths occurred in high-vulnerability oil counties due to the selective shield, and contrast that with the overall number of deaths. This would help readers assess whether the effect is economically significant given the null average.

8. **Address state-level policy confounders.** Some states expanded Medicaid or implemented harm-reduction programs during the sample period, and these policies might differentially affect oil vs. non-oil counties. Including time-varying state policies (or interacting state-year dummies with the vulnerability indicator) can rule out that the selective shield is partly driven by contemporaneous policy responses correlated with oil production.

9. **Revisit the bust period definition.** Using only 2015 as the bust year risks conflating short-run volatility with a sustained downturn. Consider defining the bust as 2015–2017 (or including later data) if oil prices and production reflect a persistent bust, and test whether the protective effect weakens or strengthens. This would also align better with the manifest’s aim to capture the full resource cycle.

10. **Improve presentation of heterogeneity.** Instead of estimating quintiles separately (which reduces power), consider interacting the continuous pre-boom drug rate with the treatment within a single regression, possibly using a polynomial or spline to capture nonlinearity. This allows for formal tests of monotonicity and facilitates plotting the marginal effect as a smooth function of pre-boom vulnerability.

In sum, the paper addresses an important question, but its current empirical strategy does not fully leverage the rich variation available, and the selective shield story needs more support from robustness checks, mechanism tests, and expanded outcomes. Addressing these suggestions would greatly strengthen the paper’s credibility and contribution.
