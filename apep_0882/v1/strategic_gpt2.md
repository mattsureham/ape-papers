## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether the shale boom changed the trajectory of the opioid epidemic in the places exposed to it. The headline result is not that oil counties did better on average, but that shale-driven economic opportunity appears to have reduced overdose mortality growth specifically in counties that were already vulnerable before the boom began.

A busy economist should care because this is potentially a paper about a first-order question in political economy and health economics: when local labor demand improves, does that actually blunt “deaths of despair,” and if so, for whom? That is a world question, not just a shale question.

### Does the paper articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Not quite. The current opening is competent, but it spends too much time setting up “two competing hypotheses” before telling the reader what the paper’s real contribution is. The paper’s actual selling point is **heterogeneous protection against overdose in already-vulnerable places**. Right now, the intro initially sounds like “another paper on whether shale booms help or hurt local outcomes,” and only later reveals the more interesting claim.

### What the first two paragraphs should say instead

The first two paragraphs should lead with the substantive question and the surprising answer:

> The opioid epidemic did not hit all places equally or at the same time. Some counties entered the 2000s already struggling with overdose mortality, while others were largely untouched. Over the same period, the shale revolution delivered large positive labor-demand shocks to selected rural counties. This paper asks whether economic opportunity changed the course of the opioid epidemic—and shows that the answer depends crucially on a county’s pre-existing vulnerability.
>
> Using a county-year panel covering all US counties from 1999 to 2015, I find no meaningful average effect of shale exposure on overdose mortality. But that average null masks a stark pattern: in counties with high pre-boom overdose rates, shale exposure significantly reduced subsequent overdose mortality growth. The paper’s core claim is therefore not that shale booms broadly improved public health, but that positive labor-demand shocks can act as a selective shield in places where addiction risk is already high.

That is the pitch the paper should have. The current title and abstract already point in this direction; the introduction should follow through immediately.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### Contribution in one sentence

The paper’s contribution is to show that a major positive labor-demand shock—the shale boom—did not reduce overdose mortality on average, but did reduce overdose mortality growth in counties that entered the boom already vulnerable to the opioid epidemic.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest papers?

Only partially. The paper distinguishes itself from “decline causes despair” papers by claiming to provide the positive-side complement, but that differentiation is still too generic. The reader needs a crisper statement of what is new relative to at least three nearby literatures:

1. papers linking adverse labor-market shocks to opioid mortality,
2. papers on the local effects of shale booms,
3. papers on heterogeneous treatment effects in place-based shocks.

Right now, the paper risks sounding like: “Take a familiar local shock, run DiD on overdose deaths, and then interact treatment with baseline risk.” That may be true descriptively, but it is not yet framed as a distinctive advance.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a gap in a literature?

Mostly the former, which is good. The best version of this paper is about the world: **whether economic opportunity mitigates addiction-related mortality in distressed places**. But the paper occasionally slips into “I contribute to three literatures” mode. That weakens it. AER readers want the paper to answer a big question first and then tell us which literatures need to update.

### Could a smart economist explain what is new after reading the introduction?

Not confidently enough. They might say: “It’s a county-level DiD on shale and overdose mortality, with heterogeneous effects by baseline overdose rates.” That is not yet memorable. You want them to say: “It shows that positive labor-demand shocks don’t lower overdose deaths everywhere, but they do in places where the epidemic had already taken hold. That’s the key result.”

### What would make the contribution bigger?

Several possibilities:

- **Broader mortality outcomes.** If the core idea is that labor-demand shocks buffer despair-related mortality, overdose alone may be too narrow. Adding suicides, alcohol-related mortality, or total external-cause mortality could make the claim much bigger.
- **Sharper mechanism outcomes.** If the story is employment as a protective factor, then showing stronger effects in prime-age men, non-college populations, disability-intensive counties, or high-opioid-prescribing places would greatly enlarge the paper’s significance.
- **Direct connection to labor-market margins.** The paper currently infers mechanism from the context. It would be stronger if framed around whether the same places that saw mortality protection also saw the relevant employment/income changes.
- **A more ambitious comparison.** Right now the paper is framed as “shale booms and overdoses.” Bigger framing would be: “When does local economic growth translate into health gains?” Shale is then the source of variation, not the subject.
- **Temporal scope.** Ending in 2015 makes the paper feel like an early-wave opioid paper. Extending through the fentanyl era could make the result much more consequential.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

The United States experienced two major changes in the 2000s: the opioid epidemic spread geographically, and the shale revolution created large local economic booms in resource-rich counties.

### Tension

Theory cuts both ways. Economic opportunity might reduce addiction and overdose by improving employment and structure, or it might worsen them via disruption, injuries, prescribing, and cash. Moreover, average effects may hide heterogeneity because some counties were already vulnerable while others were not.

### Resolution

On average, oil exposure had essentially no effect on overdose mortality. But in counties with high pre-boom overdose rates, shale exposure significantly reduced overdose mortality growth.

### Implications

Positive labor-demand shocks may protect already-distressed places from worsening mortality, implying that economic development has health value that standard local-growth analyses miss.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the ingredients, but the arc is not yet fully disciplined. The paper currently feels like:

1. question,
2. null average result,
3. then the “real” result appears as heterogeneity,
4. then a broad policy leap in the discussion.

That structure can work, but only if the paper openly embraces the idea that the average null is a misleading headline and the heterogeneity is the main event. Right now it still reads somewhat like a paper surprised by its own most interesting result.

### What story should it be telling?

The story should be:

- The opioid epidemic was path-dependent and uneven across place.
- Therefore, the effect of economic opportunity should depend on whether a place was already on the margin of crisis.
- Shale provides a large positive labor-demand shock to test that proposition.
- The data show exactly that pattern: no average effect, but meaningful protection in high-risk counties.

In other words: **not “shale and opioids,” but “economic opportunity is protective only where vulnerability is already high.”**

That is a coherent narrative. The current draft gets there, but too late.

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

### What would make it read better?

A lot of this is about sequencing.

#### 1. Front-load the main insight
The introduction should reveal immediately that the paper’s central contribution is the heterogeneous effect by pre-boom vulnerability. Right now, the reader meets the average null first and the important result second. That is backwards.

#### 2. Compress the generic institutional background
The institutional section is serviceable but conventional. It can be shorter. Most economists do not need a long recap of the shale revolution or the broad opioid timeline unless it directly advances the paper’s conceptual argument.

#### 3. Move some methodological throat-clearing out of the main text
Discussion of midpoint imputation, minimum detectable effects, and interval regression belongs later or in an appendix unless it directly changes the strategic pitch. For editorial positioning, those details crowd out the core message.

#### 4. Reorganize results around the puzzle
A better structure is:

- Main empirical fact: average effect is zero
- Key result: this masks strong heterogeneity by pre-boom vulnerability
- Interpretation: selective shield
- Then robustness / alternative treatment definitions

At present, the structure does this, but the prose still treats the average null as the headline and the heterogeneity as a secondary refinement. It should be the opposite.

#### 5. Put the most intuitive heterogeneity figure/table at center stage
The quintile pattern sounds like the most visually compelling result. That should be a main exhibit, perhaps even previewed in the introduction if there is a figure. The monotonic gradient is the paper’s best storytelling asset.

#### 6. Trim the “three literatures” paragraph
It is too standard and drains energy. Replace with one paragraph on what economists should learn about the world from this paper.

#### 7. Rethink the conclusion
The conclusion currently adds some policy stakes, but it reaches quickly toward decarbonization and “just transition” claims that feel larger than the evidence base in the paper. That may alienate readers by seeming opportunistic. The conclusion should be more restrained and tightly tied to the actual contribution: local economic opportunity may have health spillovers in already-distressed communities.
