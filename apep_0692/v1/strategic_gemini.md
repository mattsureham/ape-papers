## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
This paper asks whether restrictive state-level immigration policies (E-Verify) cause unauthorized workers to move to neighboring "friendlier" states or if they create a regional "chilling effect" that suppresses employment even across the border. Using a triple-difference design on administrative county data, the author finds that Hispanic employment in border counties of non-mandating states actually *drops* by 16%—overturning the common policy assumption that enforcement merely relocates the "problem" to neighbors.

**Evaluation:** The paper does a commendable job of articulating this in the first two paragraphs. It immediately identifies the "standard question" in the literature, the "prevailing assumption" in policy (displacement), and then offers a "central finding" that contradicts that assumption. It is a model of clarity for an intro.

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
The paper’s contribution is the documentation of negative geographic spillovers of immigration enforcement via an employer-side "expectations channel" (chilling effect) rather than a worker-side "relocation channel" (displacement).

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It is well-differentiated. While Watson (2014) and Alsan/Yang (2022) look at chilling effects on *public service* usage, this paper applies that logic to the *labor market* and cross-state spillovers, which is a significant leap.
*   **World vs. Literature:** It frames itself as answering a question about the world (the "where do they go?" policy debate).
*   **The "Smart Economist" Test:** They would say: "It's the paper that proves E-Verify scares employers in neighboring states into hiring fewer Hispanic workers, even when they aren't legally required to use the system."
*   **Bigger Contribution:** To move from a strong *Applied Economics* paper to a "Big AER" paper, the author needs to spend more time on the *why*. Is it statistical discrimination (fearing a future mandate) or animus? Using the industry heterogeneity is a start, but a more formal model of employer behavior under geographic policy uncertainty would elevate the contribution.

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
The paper sits at the intersection of Labor, Migration, and Political Economy.

*   **Neighbors:** Bohn et al. (2015) on Arizona; Watson (2014) on chilling effects; Agan and Starr (2018) on statistical discrimination.
*   **Positioning:** It builds on the "E-Verify works" literature but *attacks* the "displacement" assumption inherent in most policy models of federalism. 
*   **Unexpected Connection:** The paper could bridge to the **"Policy Uncertainty"** literature (e.g., Baker, Bloom, and Davis). The chilling effect is essentially a reaction to the increased *probability* of future enforcement. Framing it as a spatial study of how regulatory uncertainty propagates would broaden the audience significantly.

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC
*   **Setup:** States are passing E-Verify; everyone assumes workers just hop the border.
*   **Tension:** If displacement is true, border counties in neighboring states should see an employment *boom*.
*   **Resolution:** They see a *bust*. The "chill" is stronger than the "move."
*   **Implications:** Partial enforcement is more "effective" (at reducing aggregate employment) but more "costly" (to neighboring economies) than we thought.

**Evaluation:** The arc is exceptionally strong. It is a "surprise" paper—it tests a common-sense hypothesis and finds the opposite. This is the "Goldilocks" zone for top-tier journals.

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
At an AEA cocktail party, you lead with: *"Did you know that when Arizona passed E-Verify, Hispanic hiring didn't go up in New Mexico's border counties—it actually crashed by 15%?"*

Economists will lean in. The follow-up will be: *"Is that because the workers left the region entirely, or because the New Mexico employers got scared?"* The paper’s "New Hires" vs. "Incumbents" result (Table 2) provides a very satisfying answer to that follow-up.

---

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
*   **Front-loading:** The paper is already well front-loaded.
*   **The "Distance Decay" Result:** Table 4, Column 2 (Ring 2) is actually one of the most interesting results. The fact that the effect *doesn't* decay immediately suggests this isn't about local labor markets, but about a "regional vibes" or "political contagion" shift. This deserves more than a robustness check; it's a core part of the mechanism.
*   **Industry Heterogeneity:** Table 3 is vital. I would like to see a more "continuous" measure of industry Hispanic-intensity rather than just 4 buckets to really nail the statistical discrimination story.

---

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The gap is **Mechanism Rigor.** 

Referees will worry that the "Chilling Effect" is a black box. Does it happen because employers are confused about the law (thinking it applies to them)? Or because they expect their own state to pass a mandate next? Or because the supply of Hispanic labor in the *entire region* (including across the border) dropped?

**Single biggest advice:** Provide more evidence for the "Employer Expectations" channel. If the author could find data on state-level legislative mentions of E-Verify in the "untreated" states, they could show that the chill is strongest where a local mandate was being debated, which would turn this from a "spatial fact" into a "political economy masterpiece."
