## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple and policy-relevant question: when one state mandates E-Verify, do Hispanic workers displaced from that state move into neighboring non-mandating states, or does enforcement instead depress Hispanic employment there too? Using county-level administrative employment data, the paper argues for the latter: nearby enforcement appears to generate a cross-border “chilling effect” on Hispanic employment and hiring.

A busy economist should care because this is, in principle, a question about the geographic incidence of state immigration enforcement in a federal system. If true, the result says these policies do not just reallocate workers across borders; they may reshape labor demand and employer behavior across a wider region.

**Does the paper articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Mostly yes, and more clearly than many submissions. The paper opens with the right motivating question—“where do those workers go?”—and quickly contrasts a displacement hypothesis with its own contrary finding. That said, the introduction is still too much “within this literature the standard question is…” and not enough “here is the big fact about the world.” The first two paragraphs should make the main empirical surprise and its broader stakes more vivid.

**What the first two paragraphs should say instead:**

> State immigration enforcement is usually understood as a local policy with local effects: if one state makes formal employment harder for likely unauthorized workers, those workers should show up somewhere else. In the U.S. policy debate, the usual concern is cross-state displacement—strict states push Hispanic labor into neighboring lenient states. Yet whether that actually happens has remained largely untested.
>
> This paper shows that the opposite appears to happen. When a state adopts an E-Verify mandate, Hispanic employment and hiring fall not only inside the state but also in adjacent counties of neighboring non-mandating states. The key implication is that state immigration enforcement has cross-border labor-market effects that operate through employer behavior and perceived enforcement risk, not just worker relocation.

That is the pitch. It puts the world question first, the surprising answer second, and the broader implication third.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:**  
The paper’s contribution is to document that state E-Verify mandates generate negative cross-border spillovers on Hispanic employment in neighboring non-mandating counties, consistent with a regional “chilling effect” rather than geographic displacement.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest papers?
Only partly. The paper does a decent job distinguishing itself from papers on **within-state** effects of E-Verify, but the differentiation remains somewhat mechanical: “they study direct effects; I study spillovers.” That is a valid distinction, but at AER level it is not yet enough. The introduction needs to be sharper on why this is not just another margin of the same policy, but a conceptually different object: **the external incidence of immigration enforcement across jurisdictional boundaries**.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a literature gap?
It starts with a question about the world (“where do those workers go?”), which is good. But it drifts too quickly into “no study has tested this claim with administrative data at the county level.” That is literature-gap framing. The stronger framing is: **state policy may alter labor demand beyond the legal reach of the state itself**. That is a world claim, and stronger.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the intro?
Yes, but with some risk of simplification. They would probably say: “It’s a triple-difference paper showing that E-Verify in one state reduces Hispanic employment just across the border too.” That is understandable, but it still sounds like “another DiD paper about immigration enforcement” rather than a paper that changes how we think about policy spillovers.

The intro does not yet help the reader articulate the broader novelty in a more memorable way, e.g.:  
- **state enforcement has extraterritorial labor-market effects**,  
- **the effect works through employer expectations rather than worker movement**,  
- **jurisdictional boundaries are not the relevant boundaries of impact**.

### What would make this contribution bigger?
A few possibilities:

1. **Stronger comparison to the canonical displacement prediction.**  
   Right now the paper says “no displacement; instead chilling.” To make that bigger, it should more directly frame itself as adjudicating between two rival models of incidence: worker relocation vs employer deterrence/statistical discrimination.

2. **Sharper mechanism evidence on employer behavior.**  
   The hiring result helps. But the story would get much bigger if the paper could more persuasively show this is about **employer screening/perceived compliance risk**, not simply broader regional Hispanic labor-market decline. Even short of micro-level hiring data, stronger framing around employer expectations would elevate the paper.

3. **Broader welfare or incidence framing.**  
   The paper could matter more if it emphasized that partial enforcement creates **cross-jurisdiction externalities** and therefore distorts local policy choice. That links the result to federalism and public economics, not just immigration labor.

4. **Different or additional outcomes.**  
   The current outcomes are employment, hires, and earnings. The contribution would feel larger if it connected to business formation, vacancies, industry adjustment, informality proxies, or worker reallocation elsewhere. Right now the paper documents a negative margin, but doesn’t fully map where the missing employment goes.

In short: the contribution is real, but still reads as narrower than it should.

### How should it position itself relative to those neighbors?
**Build on**, not attack. The right line is not “the prior E-Verify literature missed the point,” but “the prior literature established direct effects; this paper studies where those effects propagate.” The policy-spillover literature should be brought in more centrally, because that is what makes the exercise travel beyond immigration economics.

Relative to discrimination papers, the author should be careful. The current draft leans a bit hard into “statistical discrimination” language given the evidence shown. Better to say the findings are **consistent with employer deterrence or screening based on perceived enforcement risk**, and that one possible interpretation is statistical discrimination. Don’t overclaim that literature tie unless there is sharper evidence.

### Is the paper positioned too narrowly or too broadly?
Currently it is **too narrowly positioned in immigration-enforcement labor economics**, with a somewhat opportunistic gesture toward discrimination and policy spillovers. The paper’s natural audience is broader: labor, public economics, political economy of federalism, and applied micro scholars interested in externalities of state policy.

### What literature does it seem unaware of?
Two literatures feel underdeveloped:

1. **Federalism / interjurisdictional externalities.**  
   This should be more central. The key question is when subnational policy creates spillovers beyond legal jurisdiction.

2. **Perceived enforcement / compliance overreach.**  
   There is likely a broader law-and-economics / regulation literature on firms overcomplying when enforcement risk rises nearby. This paper could speak to that. Right now “expectations channel” is asserted but not strongly embedded in a broader intellectual tradition.

Potentially also:
- labor demand under legal uncertainty,
- ethnicity-based screening under regulatory risk,
- internal migration/location choice under enforcement.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?
Not quite. It is having the obvious conversation—E-Verify affects Hispanic employment—but the more powerful conversation is: **how far do state policies travel, and through what behavioral channels?** That is the conversation that could interest a wider AER audience.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Before this paper, the accepted understanding is that E-Verify reduces formal employment of likely unauthorized workers in the adopting state. The open practical question is what happens spatially afterward.

### Tension
The policy debate assumes displacement into neighboring non-mandating states. But there is little direct evidence on whether this actually occurs at the border. A rival possibility is that nearby enforcement scares employers and workers, reducing formal employment even outside the treated state.

### Resolution
The paper finds negative, not positive, cross-border effects on Hispanic employment and especially hiring in neighboring counties. The pattern is interpreted as a chilling effect.

### Implications
State immigration enforcement may have regional externalities that cross legal boundaries. That matters for how economists think about the incidence of immigration policy, the behavior of employers under compliance risk, and the welfare accounting of state-level enforcement.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
Yes, more than many papers. This is not a random pile of tables. There is a real setup-tension-resolution structure, and the core surprise is intuitive.

But the arc is still **smaller than it should be**. The current story is:
- people expected displacement,
- I found no displacement,
- maybe chilling instead.

The stronger story is:
- economists and policymakers treat subnational enforcement as geographically bounded,
- but jurisdictional boundaries do not bound behavioral responses,
- the incidence of state enforcement is regional, not local.

That shift would turn a competent applied paper into one with broader conceptual stakes.

Also, the paper currently jumps rather quickly from reduced Hispanic hiring to “statistical discrimination.” That is a possible ending to the story, but not yet the cleanest one. The cleaner resolution is **cross-border employer deterrence under perceived enforcement risk**. Statistical discrimination can remain as one interpretation rather than the headline.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?
“If a state adopts E-Verify, Hispanic employment appears to fall not just there, but also across the border in neighboring states that did not adopt it.”

That is a good lead fact. It is surprising and legible.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
They would **lean in initially**. The punchline is counterintuitive enough to get attention. The immediate reason is that it overturns the folk model of displacement.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Almost certainly: **Why?**  
More specifically:
- Are workers not moving, or are employers in neighboring states screening them out?
- Is this really a border effect or a broader regional trend?
- How much of this is about unauthorized workers versus legal Hispanic workers?
- Where do the workers go instead—informal sector, farther-away states, nonemployment?

Those follow-up questions are good news: they mean the core fact is interesting. But they also reveal the current limitation of the paper. It has a striking reduced-form result and a plausible mechanism, but not yet a fully satisfying account of the underlying behavioral channel.

### If findings are modest or null, is that okay?
This is not a null paper. The main estimate is sizable. The issue is not lack of result; it is whether the paper can convert a strong reduced-form fact into a bigger conceptual contribution.
