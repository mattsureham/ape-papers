## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether making voting voluntary changes public safety, not by changing crime itself, but by changing how much crime the state bothers to detect. Using Chile’s 2012 shift from compulsory to voluntary voting, the paper argues that places with bigger turnout collapse saw fewer police-detected crimes but more homicide, suggesting a decline in democratic accountability to poorer communities rather than an improvement in safety.

A busy economist should care because this is potentially a striking bridge between political economy and crime: electoral participation may shape not just redistribution or spending, but the production and measurement of a core state capacity outcome.

**Does the paper articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Mostly yes. The opening paradox—crime falls while insecurity rises—is vivid and useful. But the introduction gets pulled too quickly into institutional detail, then into a literature tour, before fully crystallizing the core claim: this is a paper about **how franchise contraction affects state effort and the meaning of administrative crime data**.

### The pitch the paper should have

“Chile’s 2012 move from compulsory to voluntary voting caused turnout to collapse, especially in poorer municipalities. This paper asks whether that democratic contraction changed public safety provision. I show that municipalities with larger turnout losses recorded sharp declines in police-detected crimes such as drug offenses and burglary, but increases in homicide, a crime that is hard to hide through lower police effort. The central implication is that when poor voters exit the electorate, recorded crime can fall because accountability and detection fall—not because communities become safer.”

That is the version that belongs in the first two paragraphs. Then, only after that, bring in Fujiwara and the franchise literature.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution in one sentence:**  
The paper argues that a large turnout collapse induced by ending compulsory voting weakened local democratic accountability in Chile, reducing policing effort in poorer municipalities and creating a gap between recorded and actual crime.

### Is this clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?
Partially, but not sharply enough.

The paper has a real contribution, but it currently sounds like a combination of:
1. franchise/public goods papers showing who votes affects policy,
2. crime papers showing police presence affects measured crime,
3. a standard reform-by-exposure DiD.

What is new is **the synthesis**: turnout composition affects policing effort, and therefore the informational content of crime data. That is a stronger and more distinctive claim than “another paper on compulsory voting” or “another paper on crime and politics.” Right now the introduction does not foreground that synthesis hard enough.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or filling a gap in a LITERATURE?
It starts with a world question, which is good: why did recorded crime fall while insecurity rose? But it drifts into “reverse Fujiwara” and “contributes to three literatures,” which weakens the frame. The paper is strongest when framed as a world claim:

- When excluded citizens stop voting, states may not merely redistribute less—they may see and record less.
- Administrative crime data are politically endogenous to the franchise.

That is much bigger than “there is little evidence on the reverse direction of franchise expansion.”

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the intro?
Yes, but only barely. Right now the likely summary is:  
“It's a paper on Chile’s voting reform showing turnout declines are associated with lower recorded crime and higher homicide, interpreted as reduced policing effort.”

That’s decent, but still a little too close to “a DiD paper about turnout and crime.” The introduction needs to make the innovation legible in one line:

> The paper’s novelty is not the reform or the estimator; it is showing that democratic participation can affect **state detection capacity**, so political reforms can change the meaning of administrative outcomes themselves.

### What would make this contribution bigger?
Three concrete ways:

1. **Lean harder into state capacity / measurement, not just compulsory voting.**  
   The biggest version of the paper is not “compulsory voting matters for public safety.” It is “political inclusion changes what the state measures and enforces.” That has much broader reach.

2. **Bring in more direct evidence on the allocation margin if possible.**  
   Not as an identification fix, but as a scope expansion. Anything on police deployment, municipal public-safety budgets, station staffing, patrol intensity, arrest activity, or mayoral priorities would enlarge the paper by tying the turnout shock to state behavior more directly. Right now “detection gap” is a compelling interpretation, but the paper would become much more top-journal if it showed the reallocation of state effort itself.

3. **Generalize beyond crime categories.**  
   The most ambitious framing would suggest a broader class of outcomes where citizen exit changes measured performance because frontline bureaucrats respond to electoral incentives. Crime is one case; service inspections, code enforcement, school accountability, or health inspections are analogous. Even if not empirically added, this broader conceptual framing would make the contribution feel larger.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Before this paper, the natural view is: broader participation tends to increase redistribution/public goods, and crime statistics are often used as outcomes in evaluating public safety.

### Tension
Chile’s reform created a huge turnout collapse concentrated among poorer voters. Recorded crime falls, but perceptions of insecurity rise. So is safety improving, or is the state simply detecting less? More generally, if excluded voters lose voice, does public safety provision change in ways that distort the very outcomes we observe?

### Resolution
The paper finds that larger turnout losses are associated with fewer police-detected crimes but more homicide, while victim-reported domestic violence is flat. The paper interprets this divergence as evidence that reduced accountability lowered policing effort in places where politically marginal voters exited.

### Implications
The implications are potentially important:
- compulsory voting may sustain public goods through accountability,
- franchise contraction can reduce state presence in disadvantaged communities,
- administrative crime data can become misleading when enforcement itself is politically endogenous.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
Yes, more than many papers do. In fact, the paper’s strongest asset is the narrative logic of the “detection gap.” The problem is not absence of story; it is that the paper sometimes tells **three stories at once**:

1. compulsory voting and turnout composition,  
2. reverse franchise/public goods,  
3. crime measurement and detection.

The first is the institutional setting. The second and third are candidate main stories. For impact, it should choose one master story and subordinate the others.

### What story should it be telling?
The best story is:

> Democratic contraction weakens accountability in poor communities, and one consequence is that the state exerts less effort in detecting and recording crime there. As a result, political reforms can make official crime look better while actual safety worsens.

That’s cleaner, bigger, and more memorable than “reverse Fujiwara in Chile.”

- a “reverse Fujiwara” paper.

Those are all smaller than what it actually has. The strongest version is:

- with crime data as the application,
- showing that political reforms can change measured outcomes by changing detection effort.

That is the AER framing.

### Scope problem
Even with better framing, the paper may still feel a bit narrow unless it more directly demonstrates the accountability-to-policing channel. The findings are intriguing and memorable, but for AER the top people in the field will ask for one more layer:
- evidence on budgets,
- police deployment,
- municipal public safety investment,
- campaign platforms,
- mayoral responsiveness,
- neighborhood targeting,
- some direct indicator of state effort.

Without that, the paper risks reading as a very clever inference from outcome divergence rather than a full account of how democratic contraction translated into weaker policing.

### Novelty problem
Moderate. The ingredients are familiar. But the combination is novel enough if framed properly. The paper should stop acting like the novelty is “we study the reverse direction of franchise expansion” and instead claim the more original point: **the franchise shapes what the state detects.**

### Ambition problem
Yes, somewhat. The paper is competent and smart, but it still feels like it is trying to publish a careful field paper rather than trying to reset a conversation. The results are actually more ambitious than the manuscript’s self-presentation.

### Single most impactful advice
**Reframe the paper around the political endogeneity of state detection and, if at all possible, add one direct piece of evidence on policing/resource allocation to turn the “detection gap” from an interpretation into the paper’s demonstrated mechanism.**
