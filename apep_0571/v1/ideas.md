# Research Ideas

## Idea 1: When Voting Becomes Optional: Electoral Disengagement and the Erosion of Public Safety in Chile
**Policy:** Chile's Law 20.568 (January 31, 2012) simultaneously introduced automatic voter registration and voluntary voting, replacing compulsory voting. The first election under the new rules (October 2012 municipal elections) saw turnout collapse from ~87% to ~43% of the eligible population, with sharply differential declines by commune socioeconomic status.
**Outcome:** Commune-level crime statistics (Delitos de Mayor Connotación Social — DMCS) from CEAD: homicides, robberies (violent/non-violent), burglaries, vehicle theft, sexual offenses, domestic violence, drug offenses. Annual frequency by comuna, 2005-2023. Secondary: SINIM municipal budgets (public safety spending), SERVEL electoral data (turnout decomposition), ENUSC victimization survey.
**Identification:** Bartik-style continuous-treatment DiD. Pre-reform (2002 Census) commune characteristics (poverty rate, education, age structure, rurality) predict commune-specific turnout decline. Comunas with larger predicted turnout drops receive higher "doses" of democratic disengagement. Compare high-predicted-loss vs. low-predicted-loss comunas before (2005-2011) vs. after (2013-2023). Alternative: IV using actual turnout change instrumented by predicted change from demographics.
**Why it's novel:** All existing literature on Chile's 2012 reform studies WHO votes (Cox & González 2022; Contreras et al. 2016; Barnes & Rangel 2014). No paper studies what happens to PUBLIC POLICY OUTCOMES when the electorate changes. Tests the reverse direction of Fujiwara (2015, Econometrica): losing poor voters → less public safety.
**Feasibility check:** Confirmed: 346 comunas with differential turnout drops averaging 40pp. DMCS crime data downloaded (2010-2012 xlsx + 2018-2025 parquet). SERVEL replication data from Harvard Dataverse. ~6,200 commune-year observations. READY grade.
