## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether making voting voluntary weakened democratic accountability in a way that reduced public safety. Using Chile’s 2012 shift from compulsory to voluntary voting, it argues that places with larger turnout collapses subsequently showed fewer police-detected crimes but more homicide, implying not safer communities but a widening “detection gap” caused by weaker policing effort.

A busy economist should care because the paper is really about a broader question: does political participation affect not just what governments do, but what the state is able—or willing—to see? That is a potentially important political economy point with implications for public goods provision, crime measurement, and democratic design.

**Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Mostly, yes. The opening is much better than the typical submission: it starts with a puzzle, states the core claim quickly, and introduces a large institutional shock. But it could be sharper and more ambitious. Right now the intro opens as “crime in Chile after a reform.” The stronger version is “when poor voters exit the electorate, the state may withdraw from the neighborhoods they leave behind—and official statistics can improve even as reality worsens.”

**The pitch the paper should have in the first two paragraphs:**

> Democratic participation may shape public safety not only through policy choices, but through the everyday effort of the state. When politically marginal citizens stop voting, elected officials may face weaker incentives to direct police effort toward their communities, causing recorded crime to fall even if actual safety deteriorates.
>
> This paper studies that mechanism in Chile’s 2012 reform that replaced compulsory voting with voluntary voting and caused turnout to collapse unevenly across municipalities. I show that municipalities with larger turnout declines later experienced sharp drops in police-detected crimes such as drug offenses and burglary, but increases in homicide, a crime that does not depend on proactive police detection. The central implication is that reduced electoral participation can create a “detection gap”: better-looking crime statistics without safer communities.

That version makes the paper about the world first, Chile second.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:**  
The paper claims that when compulsory voting ends and turnout falls disproportionately among poorer citizens, the resulting loss of democratic accountability reduces policing effort in affected communities, lowering police-detected crime on paper while increasing serious violence.

**Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?**  
Somewhat, but not fully. The paper does differentiate itself from the franchise/public-goods literature by stressing the **reverse direction** of enfranchisement and from the crime literature by stressing **detection versus actual crime**. That is the right instinct. But the differentiation is still a bit mechanical: “Fujiwara but in reverse, with crime.” That is not yet memorable enough for AER-level positioning.

The key novelty is not merely reverse Fujiwara. It is the claim that **electoral participation affects state observability itself**. If that is the contribution, the paper needs to say it much more explicitly and repeatedly. Right now that idea is present, but it is not yet elevated into the main contribution.

**Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or as filling a literature gap?**  
It starts with the world, which is good, but then quickly drifts into literature bookkeeping. The strongest version is a world question: *When low-income citizens leave the electorate, does the state stop policing their communities, making official statistics less informative?* That is stronger than *there is little evidence on the reverse direction of franchise contraction*.

**Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what’s new?**  
Yes, but with some risk of dilution. The good version: “It shows that when turnout collapsed in Chile, crime statistics improved where homicide worsened, suggesting weaker democratic accountability reduced police effort.” The bad version: “It’s another DiD paper on turnout and crime.” The paper is currently hovering between those two. It has a clear idea, but it has not yet made that idea feel canonical.

**What would make this contribution bigger?**  
Most importantly: **broaden the object from crime to state capacity/measurement.** The paper should not present itself as mainly about crime. It is about how democratic participation shapes the state’s allocation of monitoring and enforcement effort, with crime as the cleanest application.

Specific ways to enlarge the contribution:
- **Different framing:** “Voting affects what the state sees” or “democratic accountability affects administrative visibility.”
- **Different outcome family:** add outcomes beyond crime where state effort affects measured incidence—inspections, code violations, labor enforcement, school attendance enforcement, tax enforcement, environmental citations. Even suggestively, this would make the paper feel much larger.
- **Different mechanism evidence:** anything directly tying turnout collapse to municipal safety spending, patrol allocation, precinct resources, or police presence would make the contribution feel less like an elegant interpretation and more like a paper about institutional response.
- **Different comparison:** connect the Chile case to other forms of effective disenfranchisement—voter ID, registration barriers, felon disenfranchisement, compulsory voting reforms—so the paper reads as a general proposition, not a one-off case study.

**Closest neighbors:**

5. On the crime side: **Levitt (1997)**, **Di Tella and Schargrodsky (2004)**, **Chalfin and McCrary / Chalfin (2018)** on policing and crime.

You could also add **Lott and Kenny (1999)** or **Husted and Kenny (1997)** as theory/background, but they are not the papers this manuscript must beat in the reader’s mind.

**How should the paper position itself relative to those neighbors?**  
Build on the franchise/public-goods papers, not attack them. The right move is: those papers show that bringing new voters into the electorate changes the allocation of public goods; this paper argues that removing voters can also change the **measurement environment** because some public goods are observed only when the state exerts effort. That is additive, not adversarial.

Relative to the crime literature, the paper should not pretend to outdo the policing papers on policing. It should instead say: this literature studies how police affect crime; I show one political mechanism that may determine where that police effort is directed.

**Is the paper currently positioned too narrowly or too broadly?**  
It is positioned a bit too narrowly in application and a bit too broadly in claimed implication. It says “three literatures” and touches all of them, but that can read like diffusion rather than reach. The paper should pick a primary conversation.

My advice:  
- **Primary conversation:** political economy of participation, representation, and public goods.  
- **Secondary conversation:** crime measurement and state capacity.  
- **Tertiary conversation:** compulsory voting.

Right now compulsory voting may be too prominent relative to its actual general-interest value. “Compulsory voting affects crime in Chile” is too niche. “Political participation affects the state’s willingness to detect and record social harm” is much more central.

**What literature does the paper seem unaware of, or under-engaged with?**  
It needs more connection to:
- **State capacity / bureaucratic responsiveness / administrative data quality.**
- **Political selection of enforcement effort**—not just policing, but regulation more generally.
- **Measurement/endogenous visibility** literatures, where observed administrative outcomes depend on monitoring effort.
- Possibly **political geography of service delivery** and **unequal government responsiveness**.

Even if the exact papers differ by field, the manuscript should signal awareness that its real contribution sits at the boundary of political economy and state capacity, not just voting rules and crime.

**Is the paper having the right conversation?**  
Not quite. The most impactful framing is not “another compulsory voting paper” and not even “another franchise-and-public-goods paper.” The right conversation is: **how democratic inclusion shapes the state’s allocation of attention, enforcement, and measurement.** That is the conversation AER readers will find more interesting.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

**Setup:**  
Chile moved from compulsory to voluntary voting, turnout collapsed, especially in poorer municipalities, and recorded crime later fell.

**Tension:**  
That decline in recorded crime may not mean safer communities. If the voters who exited were those most reliant on public protection, politicians may have reduced policing effort where turnout fell most. Then administrative crime data would understate actual deterioration.

**Resolution:**  
Municipalities with larger turnout declines saw fewer police-detected crimes but more homicide, consistent with reduced detection and weaker deterrence rather than improved safety.

**Implications:**  
Democratic participation affects public goods provision in a deeper way than standard spending stories suggest: it can influence whether harms are detected, recorded, and politically legible at all. That matters for franchise policy, crime evaluation, and the interpretation of administrative data.

**Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?**  
Yes, more than many submissions. The paper is not a random pile of regressions. It has a real story. The best part is the divergence between drug offenses/burglary and homicide—that is a narratively strong resolution.

That said, the arc is still slightly split between two stories:
1. reverse franchise/public goods; and
2. crime detection gap.

These are connected, but the manuscript treats them almost as co-equal. It needs one master story. The better one is the second, nested inside the first.

**What story should it be telling?**  
Not “compulsory voting reduced crime detection in Chile.”  
Rather: **When politically weak citizens exit the electorate, the state’s protective and monitoring effort can retreat from their communities, making official data less informative. Chile’s voting reform reveals that mechanism in crime.**

That story is cleaner, larger, and more memorable.

If there are municipal budget, police staffing, patrol, or station-level data, that belongs in the main results—not buried or omitted. If there is no such evidence, the discussion section should be tighter and less certain in its wording.

It currently summarizes effectively, but it could do more to elevate the general lesson about administrative data and democratic inclusion, and less to restate the Chilean irony.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The gap is mostly **framing plus scope**, with a secondary **novelty/ambition** issue.

This is not a bad paper with no story. On the contrary, it has a strong empirical pattern and an unusually crisp intuition. But to become an AER paper, it needs to stop behaving like a smart applied paper about Chilean crime and start behaving like a paper about **democracy and state capacity**.

### What is the main gap?

**1. Framing problem:**  
The science may be there, but the paper undersells the biggest idea. The title, introduction, and literature review tilt too much toward “crime and compulsory voting,” when the high-level contribution is about the endogeneity of state detection to electoral inclusion.

**2. Scope problem:**  
The paper currently relies on a single application and a single inferred mechanism. For AER, that can work only if the insight feels fundamental. To make it feel fundamental, it either needs broader evidence or much sharper conceptual positioning.

**3. Ambition problem:**  
The manuscript is competent and often elegant, but it is still a bit safe in how it thinks about its own contribution: reverse Fujiwara, one reform, one outcome family. The top people in the field will ask for a bolder statement of why this changes how we think about political participation and public data.

**4. Novelty problem, but not fatal:**  
“Turnout affects public goods” is known. “Police effort affects crime recording” is known. The new combination is interesting. The challenge is to convince readers that the combination creates a genuinely new object: **politically determined administrative visibility**.

### Single most impactful advice

**Reframe the paper around a broader claim: democratic participation affects not only public-goods provision but the state’s detection and measurement of social harm, with crime in Chile as the proving ground.**

If the author changes only one thing, it should be that.
