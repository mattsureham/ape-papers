## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper exploits Chile’s 2012 shift from compulsory to voluntary voting to show how democratic contraction can create a "detection gap" in public services. In districts where turnout collapsed (disproportionately poorer ones), recorded "discretionary" crimes like drug offenses plummeted while "always-reported" crimes like homicide rose, suggesting that politicians redirected policing effort away from newly silent voters. It warns that narrowing the franchise doesn’t just change policy; it can create a statistical illusion of improvement by degrading the very data used to measure government performance.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this well, but it leads with the "Chilean paradox" (safety surveys vs. stats). While evocative, for the AER, it should lead more aggressively with the theoretical stakes. 
*The pitch the paper should have:* "Standard models of the franchise predict that when groups stop voting, they lose public goods. This paper shows that this loss can manifest as a 'detection gap,' where administrative metrics (like crime rates) improve precisely because the state has stopped monitoring the communities in question, creating a dangerous 'statistical illusion' of policy success during democratic backsliding."

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution:** The paper identifies a "reverse Fujiwara" effect in which democratic contraction leads to a systematic decline in the monitoring/detection of social ills in marginalized areas.

*   **Differentiation:** It is well-differentiated. While Fujiwara (2015) and Cascio & Washington (2014) look at the *expansion* of voice, this looks at its *removal*. Crucially, it moves beyond "spending" to "detection," which is a novel angle in the political economy of the franchise.
*   **World vs. Literature:** It frames itself as answering a question about the world (why do crime stats fall while people feel less safe?), which is a strong starting point.
*   **Explainability:** A smart economist would say: "It’s the paper that shows compulsory voting prevents the police from ignoring the poor." 
*   **What would make it bigger?** Direct evidence of police *resource* reallocation (budgets, patrol hours, or station closures). The paper relies on the "homicide vs. drugs" wedge as a proxy for effort. While clever, seeing the "gears" of the budget moving would elevate this from a "crime paper" to a definitive "political economy of the state" paper.

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

The paper sits at the intersection of Political Economy (Franchise) and the Economics of Crime.

*   **Closest Neighbors:** Fujiwara (2015) on responsiveness; Levitt (1997) and Chalfin & McCrary (2018) on policing elasticities; and the recent literature on compulsory voting (e.g., Bechtel et al., 2016).
*   **Strategy:** It currently "builds on" the franchise literature. It should "attack" or at least "complicate" the crime literature. Most crime papers treat administrative data as truth; this paper should position itself as a methodological warning to the entire field: *Your dependent variable is an endogenous outcome of the political process.*
*   **Niche vs. Broad:** It is currently slightly too "Crime-heavy." To be AER-level, it needs to speak more to the "State Capacity" and "Bureaucratic Incentives" literatures. 
*   **Missing Conversations:** It should look at the literature on "Street-Level Bureaucracy" (Lipsky) or more recent work on how politicians use the police as a visible signal of effort vs. actual safety.

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

*   **Setup:** Compulsory voting ensures everyone counts; the state must monitor all neighborhoods.
*   **Tension:** Chile makes voting optional; the poor stop voting. If the "accountability" story is true, why do crime statistics—the most visible metric of success—improve in those poor areas?
*   **Resolution:** The "Detection Gap." The stats only look better because the police stopped looking. Homicides (which can't be ignored) prove things actually got worse.
*   **Implications:** Measures of government performance are only reliable when the measured population has a political voice.

**Evaluation:** The arc is exceptionally strong. It is not a "collection of results"; it is a single, cohesive story.

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

*   **The Lead Fact:** "When the poor stopped voting in Chile, drug arrests fell by 80% but murders went up—the government didn't fix the crime, they just stopped counting it."
*   **Reaction:** People will lean in. It’s a cynical and intuitive story about how "data-driven" governance can be gamed by political incentives.
*   **Follow-up:** "Did the police actually move their trucks to the rich neighborhoods, or did they just start taking longer lunch breaks?" (This highlights the need for the resource data mentioned in Section 2).

---

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

*   **Front-loading:** The distinction between "discretionary" and "always-reported" is the "money" in this paper. This needs to be the focal point of the conceptual framework.
*   **Appendix:** The 2012-2017 data gap is the biggest weakness. The author spends too much time defending it in the text. Move the "Honest DiD" and sensitivity analysis to a very prominent "Robustness of the Gap" section to kill this concern early.
*   **Heterogeneity:** The "Youth Exit" section (6.5) is interesting but feels like a "bonus." If it’s not central to the "Detection Gap" story, move it to the appendix to keep the main narrative lean.

---

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The gap between this and a "Yes" at the AER is **Mechanism Evidence**.

The "Detection Gap" is a brilliant inference, but it is currently just that—an inference based on the divergence of two crime types. To be a "Top 5" paper, you need to see the mechanism. If the author can find *any* data on municipal police spending, number of patrol cars, or even "calls for service" (311/911 data) that shows the police literally responding less frequently to those comunas, the paper becomes a "slam dunk."

**Single most impactful piece of advice:** Scour the SINIM (Sistema Nacional de Información Municipal) data or police administrative records for a "middle-step" variable—something that measures *effort* directly (patrol hours, fuel consumption, or personnel counts) rather than just the *outcome* of that effort (recorded crime).
