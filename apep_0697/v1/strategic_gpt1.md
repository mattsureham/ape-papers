## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple, policy-relevant question: when a country bans foreign homebuyers outright, does that actually cool the housing market, or do domestic buyers simply take their place? Using New Zealand’s 2018 foreign-buyer prohibition, the paper shows that the ban sharply reduced foreign purchases, but apparently did not reduce transactions in more exposed areas and provides little evidence of aggregate price effects.

A busy economist should care because foreign-buyer restrictions are politically salient across many countries, and the policy rhetoric usually assumes foreigners are an important marginal force in housing affordability. If an outright ban mostly changes who buys rather than how much housing costs, that is a useful and general lesson.

**Does the paper itself articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Mostly, but not sharply enough. The current introduction gets close, but it spends too much time on the tax-versus-ban distinction as a conceptual category and not enough on the core economic question: **are foreign buyers actually marginal enough to matter for prices and market tightness?** The paper’s strongest hook is not “this is the first ban rather than a tax.” It is: **even removing foreigners almost entirely may leave the housing market largely unchanged because domestic demand replaces them.**

### The pitch the paper should have

Governments around the world increasingly blame foreign buyers for housing unaffordability, and many have imposed special taxes or outright restrictions on their participation. But the key economic question is not whether such policies reduce foreign purchases; it is whether removing foreign demand actually relaxes housing markets, or whether domestic buyers simply absorb the released supply.

This paper studies New Zealand’s 2018 nationwide ban on foreign purchases of existing homes—the cleanest case to date of an outright prohibition rather than a tax. I show that the ban nearly eliminated foreign buyers, yet high-exposure areas saw no corresponding decline in transaction activity, and aggregate price data show no clear housing-market cooling. The central lesson is that in a supply-constrained market with deep domestic demand, banning foreign buyers may change the composition of purchasers more than the equilibrium itself.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:**  
The paper provides evidence from New Zealand’s 2018 foreign-buyer ban that eliminating foreign purchasers can drastically reduce foreign participation without meaningfully reducing overall housing-market activity, suggesting substantial domestic substitution.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3-4 papers?

Only partially. The paper differentiates itself mainly by saying: “existing papers study taxes; I study a ban.” That is a start, but it is not enough for AER-level positioning. “Ban instead of tax” sounds like an institutional variation unless the paper makes clear why the distinction changes what we learn about equilibrium incidence. The stronger distinction is:

- prior work asks whether foreign demand matters;
- this paper asks whether **removing foreign demand entirely** changes market outcomes;
- the answer appears to be: not much, because domestic demand fills the gap.

That is a genuine conceptual advance if framed well.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or filling a gap in the LITERATURE?

Right now it oscillates between both, and too often slips into “no study has examined a ban.” That is a literature gap, not a world question. The stronger framing is the world question:

- Are foreign buyers actually central to housing affordability?
- What happens in equilibrium when they are removed?

That is the version readers care about.

### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what’s new?

At present, they might say: “It’s a DiD on New Zealand’s foreign-buyer ban showing foreign share falls a lot, but prices maybe don’t.” That is not bad, but it still sounds like “another policy-evaluation paper.”

What you want them to say is:  
“Interesting paper: New Zealand nearly eliminated foreign homebuyers, and basically the market just re-sorted to domestic buyers. So the politics targets foreigners, but the economics says the real constraint is supply.”

That is much better.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Most importantly: **direct local price evidence.** The paper’s ambition exceeds what the data can presently sustain. If the central policy claim is about housing affordability, then transaction composition and aggregate synthetic-control prices are not enough. The paper needs one of the following to become materially bigger:

1. **Regional or property-level price outcomes** in exposed vs. unexposed areas.
2. **Measures of local market tightness** beyond raw transactions: days on market, sale-to-list ratios, inventory, rents, new listings, bid dispersion.
3. **Clear buyer substitution evidence**: did domestic investors, owner-occupiers, or returning NZ residents replace foreign buyers?
4. **Segment heterogeneity**: luxury markets, central-city apartments, resort areas, newly built vs. existing homes.

Without one of these, the paper remains a strong first stage plus suggestive interpretation.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Housing unaffordability is politically explosive, and foreign buyers are a popular scapegoat. Many governments tax foreign buyers; New Zealand went further and banned them from purchasing existing homes.

### Tension
The policy logic assumes foreign buyers are an important marginal source of demand. But that may be wrong: if domestic demand is deep, removing foreigners may simply reshuffle ownership without easing prices or market tightness.

### Resolution
The ban dramatically reduced foreign participation, but the paper finds no corresponding decline in transaction volumes in high-exposure areas and no obvious aggregate house-price decline.

### Implications
The identity of buyers may matter less than policymakers assume. In a constrained housing market, banning foreigners may be politically potent but economically weak as an affordability tool.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

Yes, **in outline**, but it is not yet fully disciplined. The current draft has the bones of a very good story, but also signs of being a collection of empirical outputs arranged around a plausible interpretation.

The main issue is that the paper wants to tell a story about **affordability and equilibrium**, while the core evidence is mainly about **buyer composition**. That mismatch creates narrative strain. It shows up repeatedly:

- strong result on foreign-share decline,
- weaker result on transaction counts,
- very weak evidence on prices,
- then strong policy conclusion about affordability.

The story should be tightened to this:

1. Foreign-buyer bans are sold as affordability policy.
2. New Zealand offers a rare test because it actually removed foreign buyers.
3. The ban succeeded mechanically.
4. But housing markets appear to have reallocated demand domestically rather than cooled.
5. Therefore, the policy’s direct effect is on composition; any affordability effect is limited or elusive.

That is cleaner and more honest.

- a test of a highly salient policy belief,

Right now it is close, but not forceful enough. It can’t read like “we couldn’t find a price effect because we lacked the right data.” It has to read like “the first-order empirical fact is compositional substitution, and that itself is deeply informative.”

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

This paper is not far away in topic; it is far away in **decisiveness**.

The main gap is a mix of **scope problem** and **framing problem**.

- **Framing problem:** the paper’s best insight is broader than its current framing. It should be about whether targeted demand restrictions change equilibrium outcomes or merely composition.
- **Scope problem:** the evidence does not yet fully match the policy claim, especially on prices and market tightness.
- **Novelty problem:** “first ban instead of tax” by itself is not enough.
- **Ambition problem:** the paper feels competent but still somewhat safe. It has one very clean institutional result and then backs away from making a bigger empirical case.

If I ask what would excite the top 10 people in this field, the answer is not “a neat DiD showing the foreign share fell.” They would want one of two things:

1. **A definitive equilibrium result**: prices, rents, or market tightness did not move despite a near-complete removal of foreign buyers.
2. **A deeper mechanism paper**: exactly which domestic buyers stepped in, in which segments, and why.

Right now the paper has only the first-stage and an inference.

### Single most impactful advice

**Get credible local price or market-tightness evidence, and reorganize the paper around the equilibrium question rather than the policy-instrument distinction.**

If the author can only change one thing, that is it.
