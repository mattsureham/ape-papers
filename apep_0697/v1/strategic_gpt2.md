## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper studies New Zealand’s 2018 ban on foreign purchases of existing homes, the first national policy to ban rather than tax foreign residential buyers. Using regional variation in pre-ban foreign buyer exposure, it shows that the ban sharply reduced foreign participation, but did not produce corresponding declines in transaction activity and offers only weak national-level evidence of no price effect, suggesting domestic buyers replaced foreign ones.

A busy economist should care because the policy debate is not really about foreign-buyer shares; it is about whether excluding foreign capital makes housing more affordable. New Zealand is an unusually clean setting because the policy was extreme, salient, and national.

**Does the paper articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Mostly, but not optimally. The opening is competent and the ban-vs-tax distinction is clear. The problem is that the introduction promises an answer to the economically important question — do bans lower prices / improve affordability? — but the paper can only answer a narrower one: do domestic buyers replace foreign buyers in transaction composition? That slippage happens immediately and weakens trust in the framing.

**What the first two paragraphs should say instead:**

> Governments around the world have restricted foreign homebuyers in the name of affordability, but we still know little about whether such restrictions change housing market outcomes or merely change who buys the same homes. Existing evidence is almost entirely about taxes on foreign buyers. New Zealand’s 2018 prohibition on most foreign purchases of existing homes — the first national outright ban — provides a rare opportunity to study what happens when foreign demand is removed almost entirely rather than merely made more expensive.
>
> The key economic question is whether foreign buyers are an important source of housing demand or simply one set of bidders among many. If they are pivotal, an outright ban should reduce transactions and, ultimately, prices in the places most exposed to foreign demand. If domestic demand is ready to step in, the policy may dramatically reduce foreign-buyer shares while leaving market activity and affordability essentially unchanged. I show strong evidence for the latter on transaction composition and turnover; evidence on prices is suggestive but limited by available data.

That version is more honest and more strategically effective: it leads with the world question, not the estimator.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution in one sentence:**  
The paper’s contribution is to show, in the first national setting with an outright foreign-buyer ban, that eliminating foreign buyers greatly changed who bought homes but may have done little to change how much housing was traded, consistent with substantial domestic demand substitution.

### Evaluation

**Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3-4 papers?**  
Partly. The paper does distinguish itself from work on foreign-buyer taxes by emphasizing that New Zealand used a ban, not a surcharge. That is real differentiation. But the paper currently overstates how much that distinction alone buys it. “First ban rather than tax” is a neat institutional wrinkle, not yet a major contribution unless it yields a stronger answer to the central economic question.

**Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or as filling a gap in a literature?**  
It starts with a world question, which is good, but repeatedly retreats into “no study has examined a ban.” That is a literature-gap frame. For AER, the stronger framing is: *When policymakers remove foreign demand from a housing market, what actually adjusts — prices, quantities, or buyer composition?*

**Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain what’s new?**  
They could say: “It’s the New Zealand foreign-buyer ban paper; bans eliminated foreign purchases, but locals stepped in.” That is better than “another DiD paper about X,” but only marginally. Right now the paper risks being remembered as: *a nice first-stage paper about a ban*. That is not enough for AER.

**What would make this contribution bigger? Be specific.**
1. **Direct price evidence at the regional level.** This is the obvious missing piece. The paper is about housing affordability policy but cannot speak credibly to affordability. Without regional price outcomes, the paper’s main contribution remains incomplete.
2. **Who substituted in?** Owner-occupiers? domestic investors? returning expatriates? corporate buyers? The political economy differs radically depending on the answer.
3. **Which homes were affected?** If foreign demand concentrated in specific segments — CBD apartments, luxury properties, resort markets — then aggregate transaction nulls may mask meaningful equilibrium adjustments within market segments.
4. **Welfare-relevant margins.** If the policy changed composition but not prices, did it alter vacancy, new construction, rental outcomes, or local sorting? Any one of these would make the paper feel less like a compliance study.
5. **A clearer conceptual frame.** The paper should present itself as an incidence/equilibrium paper: restrictions on one class of buyers may be offset by reallocation among remaining buyers.

The single biggest way to make the contribution larger is straightforward: **add credible local price evidence and make the paper about market incidence, not policy compliance.**

**Build on and reframe them.** Not attack them. The useful move is:

- Tax papers tell us what happens when foreign demand is made more expensive.
- This paper asks what happens when foreign demand is removed almost entirely.
- Therefore the comparison is informative about the elasticity of residual domestic demand and about whether foreign buyers are truly marginal price-setters.

That is a strong bridge. But the current draft does not fully exploit it. It says “this paper differs because it studies a ban.” Fine, but that is descriptive. The stronger claim is: *bans are a stress test of the foreign-demand hypothesis.*

Oddly, both.

- **Too narrowly** in the empirical core: it is basically a paper on New Zealand property-transfer composition.
- **Too broadly** in the rhetoric: it gestures toward capital controls, macroprudential policy, welfare, and housing affordability at large without enough evidence on those margins.

It needs a sharper audience. The right audience is probably **urban/public/housing economists interested in the equilibrium incidence of buyer-based restrictions**. “Capital controls” is too grand for what the evidence can support.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?
It should speak more directly to:

- the **housing affordability / supply elasticity** literature,
- the **incidence of taxes and quantity restrictions** literature,
- the **market design / rationing vs pricing** distinction,
- and possibly the **political economy of salient housing policies**.

There is also a conversation with **place-based demand shocks** and who is truly marginal in asset markets. Right now the paper cites some of this, but the integration is superficial.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?
Not quite. The highest-value conversation is not “here is one more study of foreign buyers.” It is:

> In thin, supply-constrained asset markets, does restricting one class of buyers change equilibrium outcomes, or only buyer identity?

That framing connects to broader economics themes: incidence, substitution, rationing, and the equilibrium effects of politically popular but poorly targeted restrictions.

That is the conversation AER readers might care about.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Governments increasingly blame foreign buyers for housing unaffordability and respond with taxes or restrictions. New Zealand went further and imposed an outright ban.

### Tension
If foreign buyers are driving prices, then a ban should improve affordability, especially where they were most active. But if they are only a small slice of aggregate demand, removing them may just let domestic buyers take their place.

### Resolution
The ban dramatically reduced foreign-buyer participation, but transaction activity did not fall in more exposed places; suggestive aggregate evidence does not show a price decline.

### Implications
Policies that target buyer identity may have large political appeal yet little market impact if residual domestic demand is elastic. The relevant problem may be housing supply or broader demand pressure, not foreign ownership per se.

### Evaluation
The paper **does have a plausible narrative arc**, but it is not fully disciplined. At present it reads like:

- strong institutional setup,
- strong first-stage result,
- weaker quantity result,
- much weaker price result,
- then broad policy implications.

So the narrative outruns the evidence. The paper wants the resolution to be “bans do not improve affordability because locals replace foreigners,” but the actual evidence is “bans clearly reduce foreign shares, and transaction volumes do not obviously fall in exposed areas.” That is a narrower and less satisfying resolution.

So yes: **it is somewhat a collection of results looking for a bigger story**.

**What story should it be telling?**  
Not “foreign buyer bans don’t affect prices” — the paper cannot carry that.  
Instead:

> “Foreign buyer bans can be highly effective at changing buyer composition while leaving the broader market largely intact. In New Zealand, the policy successfully removed foreigners but did not create a visible hole in transaction activity, implying substantial substitution by domestic buyers. The main lesson is about incidence and equilibrium adjustment, not just policy compliance.”

That story is true to the evidence and still interesting.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?
“New Zealand cut foreign buying from 2.4% of transactions to 0.5%, but the places most exposed to foreign buyers did not see corresponding declines in transaction activity.”

That is the right lead fact. It is concrete and mildly surprising.

### Would people lean in?
Some would. Not all.

Economists interested in housing, urban, public finance, or political economy would lean in because the policy is salient and the treatment is unusually stark. But many would immediately ask the obvious question:

### What follow-up question would they ask?
“Fine — but did prices fall?”

And that is where the current paper loses altitude. The answer is basically: “I cannot really tell, except from a weak aggregate synthetic-control exercise.” That is not fatal for a field journal; it is a major limitation for AER.

### If findings are null or modest, is the null itself interesting?
Potentially yes. A null on transactions after an outright ban is interesting because the policy was dramatic and politically salient. Learning that even a ban may mostly reshuffle buyers is useful. But the paper has to work harder to make the null feel informative rather than incomplete. Right now it still feels a bit like the paper stopped one dataset short of the central question.

The null becomes much more powerful if presented as:
- a rejection of the common political premise that foreign buyers are quantitatively central,
- evidence that buyer-targeted restrictions can have large compositional effects but limited equilibrium effects,
- and a challenge to models in which foreign buyers are the decisive marginal bidders.

Without that sharpening, readers may file it under “interesting setting, partial answer.”
