### 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper evaluates whether one of the most expensive regulatory regimes in the world—Anti-Money Laundering (AML) compliance—actually achieves its primary goal: catching financial criminals. Exploiting the staggered adoption of the EU’s 5th AML Directive, the author finds that despite massive compliance costs, there is a precisely estimated zero effect on the detection of money laundering offences.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this pitch exceptionally well. The first two paragraphs successfully establish the "striking paradox" of a $214 billion industry with a 1% success rate. It identifies a clear causal gap and provides the "so what" immediately. The current pitch is AER-ready.

### 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution:** The paper provides the first causal evidence that major expansions in AML information infrastructure fail to increase the detection of financial crime at the national level.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It is well-differentiated. Most AML literature is either descriptive (Pol, 2020) or uses non-causal cross-sectional data (FATF reports). By using the staggered transposition of EU directives, it moves the field from "opinion/anecdote" to "quasi-experimental."
*   **Question vs. Literature:** It answers a question about the WORLD (Does this $214B system work?). This is its strongest selling point.
*   **What would make it bigger?** To truly "pop" for the AER, the paper needs to move beyond the null on *detection* to explore *incidence* or *displacement*. If I don't see more arrests, is it because criminals are deterred (incidence down) or because they moved to the shadow economy (displacement)? The house price result (5.3) is a hint of this, but it’s currently a footnote. Expanding the "real effects" section would turn this from a "failed policy" paper into a "market for crime" paper.

### 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

*   **Closest Neighbors:** Findley et al. (2014) on shell companies; Kalemli-Özcan et al. (2024) on EU directives; and the broader "optimal enforcement" literature (Becker 1968, Stigler 1970).
*   **Positioning:** It currently sits as a "Policy Evaluation." It should instead position itself as a test of the **"Information vs. Capacity"** hypothesis in regulatory economics. The literature often assumes more data = better enforcement. This paper challenges that premise.
*   **Missing Conversations:** The paper should speak more to the **Political Economy of Bureaucracy**. Is the null result due to "Regulatory Capture" (banks complying in form but not substance) or "Bureaucratic Inertia" (police can't process the flood of SARs)? Connecting to the literature on "Street-Level Bureaucracy" or "Administrative Burden" would broaden the audience.

### 4. NARRATIVE ARC

*   **Setup:** Global AML is a massive, expensive, and growing regulatory beast.
*   **Tension:** We keep feeding the beast more data (5AMLD), assuming data is the bottleneck to catching "bad guys."
*   **Resolution:** It’s not. More data results in zero additional detection.
*   **Implications:** We are in a "Compliance Trap" where we prioritize the *supply* of information over the *capacity* to use it.

**Evaluation:** The narrative is strong, particularly the "Compliance Trap" branding. However, the "Resolution" section is a bit thin. The author needs to lean harder into *why* the null exists in the discussion section.

### 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

**The Dinner Party Fact:** "The EU forced every country to create public registers of company owners and regulate crypto to stop money laundering, and it resulted in exactly zero additional arrests."

**The Lean-In:** Economists will lean in because of the staggering cost-benefit mismatch ($214B vs. 0).

**The Follow-up:** "Wait, so did the criminals just get smarter, or are the police just ignored the new data?" The paper's current inability to fully answer this is the main hurdle to a "Revise & Resubmit."

### 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

*   **Front-loading:** The paper is well-structured. The institutional background on 5AMLD (Section 2) is necessary but could be more concise to get to the results faster.
*   **Appendices:** Move the "Leave-one-out" and "ML share of total crime" to a Technical Appendix.
*   **Expansion:** Section 5.3 (Secondary Outcomes) should be elevated. If the house price result can be sharpened, it provides the "flip side" of the null—showing that while detection didn't go up, the *market* for laundered money was actually disrupted.

### 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The science is clean, but the **scope** is currently "Economics Letter" or "Applied Economics" level. To make it AER, it needs to be an **Ambitious Theory-Testing** paper.

**The single most impactful piece of advice:**
Don't just report a null; explain the **leakage**. If the 5AMLD didn't increase detection, did it cause "spatial displacement" (money moving to the 4 non-transposed countries) or "asset displacement" (money moving out of real estate)? If the author can show *where the money went* when the lights turned on, this becomes a top-tier paper on the limits of multilateral regulation.
