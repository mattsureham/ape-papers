## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple, important question: when regulators crack down on one major opioid distributor, does opioid supply actually fall, or does it just reroute through rival firms? Using the full universe of DEA shipment records around the 2007 suspension of Cardinal Health facilities, the paper argues that enforcement sharply reduced Cardinal’s role but did little to reduce total county-level opioid supply because competitors stepped in.

A busy economist should care because this is not really just an opioid paper. It is a paper about the incidence of enforcement in a networked market: when policy targets one node in a concentrated supply chain, does the market contract or reorganize?

Does the paper itself articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs? Mostly yes, but not optimally. The current opening is reasonably good, but it still reads like a sector-specific policy paper before revealing the broader economic question. The first two paragraphs should lead with the general mechanism, not the institutional detail.

**What the first two paragraphs should say instead:**

> Regulators often target individual firms in the hope of shrinking socially harmful markets. But when supply chains are concentrated and substitutable, enforcement against one firm may simply reallocate business to its rivals rather than reduce aggregate activity. Whether such targeted enforcement changes total market supply or merely reshuffles market share is a first-order question for drug policy, antitrust, and regulation more broadly.
>
> This paper studies that question in the legal opioid distribution market. I examine the DEA’s 2007 suspension of Cardinal Health distribution centers using the universe of opioid shipment records in ARCOS. I find that the crackdown substantially reduced Cardinal’s shipments and market share in exposed counties, but had little effect on total opioid pills shipped because other distributors replaced the lost supply. The central message is that firm-level enforcement can fail to reduce aggregate supply when competing supply-chain nodes can absorb displaced volume.

That is the AER pitch. The current intro gets there, but too much of the framing is still “opioid policy episode” rather than “general economic lesson from a high-stakes setting.”

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:**  
The paper shows that enforcement against a major opioid distributor reduced that firm’s shipments but did not materially reduce overall local opioid supply, because rival distributors substituted in—evidence of a “waterbed effect” in a legal, regulated supply chain.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest papers?
Only partly. The paper distinguishes itself from demand-side opioid policy work and from broader supply geography papers, but the differentiation is still a bit mechanical: “nobody has done this exact quasi-experiment before.” That is not enough. A top-paper contribution needs to be differentiated conceptually, not just empirically.

The sharper distinction is:
- prior opioid papers mostly study prescribers, patients, or state policies;
- this paper studies **intermediaries**;
- prior enforcement displacement arguments are largely about **illicit** markets;
- this paper shows similar displacement in a **legal, highly concentrated, regulated** market.

That is the distinctive contribution. It should be stated repeatedly.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a gap in a literature?
It is mixed, but still too often framed as filling a literature gap. “No published paper has used DEA enforcement against a specific distributor…” is weak framing. AER wants the world question: **Can targeted enforcement reduce harmful supply in markets with close intermediated substitutes?**

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the intro?
Some could, but too many would summarize it as “a DiD paper on opioid distribution after a DEA action.” That is the danger. The paper needs to make the novelty impossible to miss:
- not just an enforcement paper;
- not just an opioid paper;
- a paper on **substitution across intermediaries and the limits of node-specific regulation**.

### What would make this contribution bigger?
Most importantly, make the contribution about **market organization and policy design**, not only opioid supply.

Specific ways to make it feel bigger:
1. **Reframe the outcome hierarchy.** Put total supply first, distributor reallocation second, and opioid context third. Right now the paper sometimes reads as if the main result is Cardinal lost share. That is not the result; that is the first stage of the story.
2. **Deepen the mechanism evidence on substitution.** Not more robustness—more economics. How concentrated were counties pre-shock? Were effects larger where pharmacies had more outside options? Did multi-distributor counties substitute faster than single-source counties? That would connect the result to theories of switching costs, capacity, and oligopoly.
3. **Tie to broader classes of interventions.** The discussion briefly mentions quotas. That could be elevated: the real comparison is targeted firm enforcement versus system-wide supply constraints.
4. **Consider downstream margin if available.** Not because every paper needs mortality, but because “stable pill supply despite targeted enforcement” is more powerful if linked to why this matters in welfare terms. Even a limited downstream descriptive bridge would enlarge the stakes.

The single best way to make the contribution bigger is to show that the strength of the waterbed effect varies predictably with **substitutability and market structure**. Then the paper becomes a general economics paper, not just an interesting institutional episode.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Before this paper, the natural presumption is that suspending a major distributor should reduce harmful opioid availability, especially in a legal and tightly regulated supply chain.

### Tension
But concentrated markets with close substitutes may be resilient: removing one distributor may just move volume to rivals. So the key tension is between **regulatory intent** and **market adaptation**.

### Resolution
The paper finds that Cardinal’s shipments collapse in exposed places, but total opioid supply barely changes because competitors absorb the displaced business.

### Implications
The implication is that targeted enforcement against a single intermediary may do little to reduce aggregate supply when substitute distributors exist. Effective policy may require interventions that bind the whole system rather than a single firm.

This is, in principle, a very good narrative arc. The problem is that the paper doesn’t fully trust it. It keeps slipping into a “here are the tables” mode and into literature-gap language. The narrative is there, but it needs stronger discipline.

At present, it is **not** a collection of unrelated results, but it is still somewhat overburdened by method exposition and underpowered on conceptual storytelling. The real story should be:

1. Regulators targeted one major node.
2. That node was important enough that a naive observer would expect total supply to fall.
3. It did not.
4. The reason is market substitution.
5. Therefore policy aimed at harmful supply must account for network structure, not just bad actors.

That is the story. Everything in the paper should serve it.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party?
“The DEA suspended major Cardinal Health distribution centers during the opioid crisis, Cardinal’s local market share dropped sharply—and total opioid pill shipments hardly changed because other distributors replaced it.”

That is a good fact. Economists will lean in.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
Lean in, initially. The premise is vivid, the setting matters, and the result is counterintuitive enough. But the follow-up determines whether they stay interested.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
They will ask: **why was substitution so easy?**  
And then: **when should we expect targeted enforcement to work versus fail?**

That is exactly where the paper needs to do more. Right now it answers “what happened” more clearly than “what determines whether this happens.” The latter is what moves it toward AER.

### If the findings are modest or null, is the null interesting?
Yes—this is one of the relatively rare cases where a null aggregate effect is genuinely interesting. The paper is not saying “we found nothing.” It is saying “the policy hit the targeted firm hard, yet the market outcome barely changed.” That is a meaningful null because it is paired with a strong compositional change.

But the paper must present the null as a **substantive equilibrium result**, not as absence of power or absence of effect. The compelling object is:
- large effect on the treated intermediary,
- no meaningful effect on the aggregate outcome,
- therefore substitution neutralized the intervention.

That logic needs to be cleaner and more forceful.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

This is primarily a **framing-plus-ambition problem**, with some scope issues.

- **Not mainly a framing problem only**: the framing can be improved substantially, but framing alone will not fully solve it.
- **Some scope problem**: one enforcement episode against one firm is a bit narrow for AER unless the paper extracts a broader mechanism from it.
- **Some novelty problem**: the intuition that supply-side enforcement displaces activity is not new. The novelty is its demonstration in a legal pharmaceutical distribution market. That is interesting, but the paper must sharpen why that extension changes our beliefs.
- **Most of all, an ambition problem**: the paper currently settles for “here is an interesting null with reallocation.” AER wants “here is a general lesson about when targeted regulation of intermediaries fails.”

### The gap between current form and an AER paper
To excite the top people in this field, the paper needs to do more than document one waterbed episode. It needs to tell us something **portable**:
- about intermediary regulation,
- about market substitution under enforcement,
- about when firm-specific sanctions can or cannot move aggregate outcomes.

If the paper can establish that the waterbed effect is strongest where substitutes are readily available and weakest where they are not, then it becomes a paper with a model in the background even if no formal theory is written. Without that, it risks being seen as a well-executed case study.

### Single most impactful advice
**Reframe the paper around a general economic question—when does targeted enforcement in concentrated supply chains reduce aggregate harmful supply versus merely reallocate it—and organize the evidence to explain why substitution was so effective here.**
