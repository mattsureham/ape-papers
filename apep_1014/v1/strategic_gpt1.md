## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether eliminating the tipped subminimum wage reduces racial earnings inequality in food services. That is a question economists should care about because the tipped wage is a major, long-standing institutional exception in the U.S. labor market, and if customer discrimination is partly transmitted through tipping, then wage-setting rules may shape racial inequality in a large low-wage sector.

The paper does articulate a pitch early, and the first paragraph is actually strong: it has a concrete institution, a mechanism, and a normative hook. But the pitch becomes muddled almost immediately because the paper starts as a paper about the *tipping penalty*—customer discrimination amplified by the tip credit—and later reports evidence that seems to say the effect is *not mainly about tipping*, but about the general minimum wage floor. That is not a small wrinkle; it changes what the paper is about.

### The pitch the paper should have

Here is the cleaner version of the first two paragraphs the paper should say:

> The tipped minimum wage is one of the most unusual labor-market institutions in the United States: it allows employers in a large service sector to substitute a low statutory base wage with customer-determined pay. If customer tipping behavior differs by worker race or ethnicity, then the tipped wage system may do more than lower wages—it may translate consumer discrimination into persistent earnings inequality.
>
> This paper studies whether raising or eliminating the tipped subminimum wage narrows racial earnings gaps in food services. Using recent state reforms and administrative earnings data by race and industry, I show that reforms increasing the wage floor are followed by meaningful racial earnings convergence in food services. I then ask whether this operates through the tipping channel specifically or through the broader wage-floor channel, and show that the broader floor appears to do most of the work.

That pitch is tighter because it preserves the motivating mechanism without overcommitting to a claim the paper later weakens.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

This paper provides evidence that reforms raising or eliminating the tipped subminimum wage are associated with narrower racial earnings gaps in food services, and suggests that the main channel is the higher wage floor rather than tipping-specific discrimination per se.

### Evaluation

**Is the contribution clearly differentiated from the closest papers?**  
Only partly. The paper tries to distinguish itself from three literatures—minimum wage and racial inequality, tipping discrimination, and tipped-wage employment effects—but the differentiation is not yet crisp enough. Right now the reader can tell it is “minimum wage meets tipping meets race,” but not exactly whether the novelty is:
1. a new outcome: racial earnings gaps,
2. a new policy: tipped minimum wage reforms,
3. a new mechanism: discriminatory tipping translated into pay inequality,
4. or a decomposition showing the tipping channel is weaker than the general wage-floor channel.

Those are not the same contribution. The paper needs to choose.

**Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a literature gap?**  
Mostly the former at the start, which is good: “does the tipped subminimum wage amplify racial inequality?” That is a world question. But the intro then drifts toward “first causal estimate” language. That is weaker and less memorable.

**Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?**  
Not confidently. A smart economist might say: “It’s a DiD on tipped minimum wage reforms and racial pay gaps in restaurants.” That is serviceable, but not enough for AER. The economist would be less likely to say the stronger thing: “It shows how a labor-market institution converts consumer discrimination into wage inequality.” And the reason they would not say that is that the paper itself backs away from it in the DDD section.

**What would make the contribution bigger?**  
Three possibilities:

1. **Make the object of interest more exact.** “Food services” is broad. If the paper could get closer to occupations that are actually tipped versus non-tipped within the sector, the contribution becomes sharper and more plausibly about the institution rather than the sector.

2. **Elevate the conceptual contribution.** The big idea is not “one more minimum wage paper.” It is that institutions can mediate how market discrimination translates into earnings. That framing is much bigger than the current one.

3. **Reframe the paper around a decomposition rather than a verdict.** The current paper oversells “tipping penalty” and then undercuts itself. A more ambitious contribution would be: reforms in tipped sectors reduce racial inequality, but mostly because they remove an industry-specific exemption from the wage floor rather than because they neutralize discretionary tipping disparities. That is more subtle, but also more intellectually interesting.

Right now it is oddly both:

The paper is adjacent to it, but not fully in it.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
The U.S. tipped minimum wage is an old, peculiar policy that allows a large sector to rely on customer tips to make up earnings. There is evidence that customers tip differently by race.

### Tension
If tips are racially biased, then the tipped subminimum wage may amplify racial earnings inequality. But it is not obvious whether changing the tipped wage floor actually changes earnings gaps in practice, or whether any effect just reflects general minimum wage increases.

### Resolution
Reforms increasing the tipped wage floor are associated with narrower racial earnings gaps in food services. But the paper’s own DDD exercise suggests the effect may run mainly through the broader minimum wage channel, not the tipping-specific channel.

### Implications
Raising wage floors in tipped sectors may reduce racial earnings inequality. More broadly, labor-market institutions can shape whether consumer discrimination becomes worker pay inequality.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the *ingredients* of one, but not the discipline. The paper currently feels like two different stories:

1. **Story A:** The tip credit amplifies racial bias in tipping into a “tipping penalty.”
2. **Story B:** The observed convergence is mostly just a general minimum wage effect.

These are not fully incompatible, but the paper presents them as though Story A is the headline and Story B is an interpretive footnote, when in fact Story B is potentially the more credible finding based on the paper’s own evidence.

So yes, at present this is closer to **a collection of results looking for a stable story**. The story it should tell is:

> Tipped-wage reforms reduce racial inequality in food-service earnings, but the evidence suggests this happens less because they neutralize discriminatory tipping directly and more because they remove a sector-specific exception to the wage floor. The broader lesson is that institutions governing pay composition matter for inequality.

That is a real narrative, and an interesting one.

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

### What fact would I lead with at dinner?

I would lead with this:

> “States that raise the tipped wage floor see racial earnings gaps shrink in restaurants—and the evidence suggests the main reason is that they stop letting such a large share of pay be determined by a very low base wage.”

That is a better dinner-party line than quoting the 1.5 percentage-point estimate.

### Would people lean in?

Yes, initially. The topic is concrete, salient, and tied to a famous U.S. institutional oddity. Economists will care because it connects labor-market institutions, race, and consumer behavior.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Immediately:  
**“Is this really about tipping discrimination, or is it just another minimum wage result?”**

That is the central strategic problem. The paper must anticipate and answer that question cleanly. Right now, the answer is muddled, and in places the paper practically answers: “mostly another minimum wage result.” If that is true, the paper should own it and explain why the tipped setting is still analytically important.

### If the findings are modest, is that okay?

Yes. A 1.5 percentage-point shift in a racial earnings ratio is not trivial. The issue is not that the effect is too small; it is that the paper has not yet convinced the reader what conceptual bucket the effect belongs in. The paper does not fail because the estimate is modest. It risks failing because the interpretation is unstable.
