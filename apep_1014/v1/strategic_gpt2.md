## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether eliminating the tipped subminimum wage reduces racial earnings inequality in the restaurant sector. Using recent state reforms and administrative earnings data, it argues that raising the wage floor for tipped workers narrows Black-white and Hispanic-non-Hispanic earnings gaps in food services, suggesting that tipped-wage policy is part of the machinery generating racial inequality in low-wage labor markets.

A busy economist should care because this is, in principle, a clean intersection of three big conversations: racial inequality, labor-market institutions, and the economics of tipped work. If true and well-framed, the paper says something broader than “another wage-policy paper”: it says the design of wage floors determines how consumer discrimination maps into labor-market inequality.

Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs? Not quite. The opening is strong rhetorically, but the paper overcommits immediately to a specific mechanism—customer discrimination in tipping—before the paper’s own later results partially undermine that mechanism. By the time one reaches the triple-difference, the story has shifted from “tips amplify discrimination” to “the general wage floor matters more than the tipping-specific channel.” That is a major repositioning, and the introduction should not promise a paper it does not ultimately deliver.

### The pitch the paper should have

Here is the version the first two paragraphs should say instead:

> Food-service labor markets are unusual because wage-setting is split between employers and customers. In much of the United States, employers of tipped workers are allowed to pay a subminimum cash wage, leaving a large share of compensation to tips and making the effective wage floor in restaurants lower than in other low-wage sectors. This paper asks whether that institutional design contributes to racial earnings inequality.
>
> I study state reforms that raised or eliminated the tipped subminimum wage and combine them with administrative earnings data by race and industry. The central finding is that higher tipped wage floors narrow racial earnings gaps in food services. More broadly, the paper shows that wage-floor institutions in tipped sectors shape racial inequality, though the evidence suggests the effect may operate at least as much through the general low-wage floor as through tipping-specific discrimination alone.

That framing is less flashy than “the tipping penalty,” but much more intellectually honest and much more durable.

It begins with a world question, which is good: do tipped wage institutions generate racial inequality? But it slips repeatedly into literature-gap framing: “first causal estimate,” “has not connected,” “literature has focused on.” For AER, the world question is much stronger.

The paper should center:

- whether tipped sectors are a special locus of racial inequality,
- whether eliminating the subminimum wage changes that equilibrium.

And after reading the full paper, they might be even more confused because the headline mechanism and the later decomposition do not align.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Several concrete possibilities:

1. **Reframe the core question away from tips per se and toward compensation architecture.**  
   The paper becomes bigger if it is about how customer-mediated pay and subminimum wage rules jointly shape inequality, not just about one policy change.

2. **Show whether the effect is truly sector-specific.**  
   Right now the DDD actually weakens the tipping-specific interpretation. If the big finding is that the general minimum wage channel dominates, then the paper needs to embrace that and ask a broader question: is food service simply where low wage floors bite hardest for minority workers?

3. **Use outcomes that better match the conceptual claim.**  
   If the claim is about worker welfare and racial inequality, earnings ratios alone feel thin. Bigger outcomes would include:
   - annual earnings rather than monthly averages,
   - worker retention / separations,

4. **Clarify the mechanism by comparing tipped-intensive and less tipped-intensive segments within food services.**

5. **Make the contribution comparative rather than local.**  
   The Arizona/DC/Michigan reforms are few. The paper could be bigger if it treats these as evidence on a more general question: do compensation systems that rely on customer discretion magnify inequality relative to employer-paid wage floors?

The single biggest substantive enlargement would be: **either prove the tipping-specific mechanism more directly, or stop selling the paper as tipping-specific and sell it as a paper on racial inequality and wage-floor design in customer-facing low-wage labor markets.**

   The paper should speak more to wage-setting institutions, not only minimum-wage policy.

Not quite yet. The most impactful conversation is not “another minimum wage paper” and not “another tipping paper.” It is:

**How do labor-market institutions determine whether discrimination by customers becomes inequality in wages?**

That is a much better conversation, and a more AER-worthy one.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Restaurants are a peculiar labor market where compensation depends partly on customer tips, and policy allows employers to pay a subminimum cash wage. Prior research suggests tipping is racially unequal, but we do not know whether tipped-wage policy translates that into earnings inequality at scale.

### Tension

If customer discrimination in tipping matters, then the tipped subminimum wage may amplify racial inequality. But there is also a competing possibility: any observed convergence after reform simply reflects a standard minimum-wage effect rather than anything distinctive about tipping.

### Resolution

The paper finds that reforms raising tipped wage floors are associated with narrower racial earnings gaps in food services. But the paper’s own DDD suggests the general minimum-wage channel may dominate the food-service-specific tipping channel.

### Implications

The broad implication is that wage-floor design affects racial inequality in low-wage sectors. The narrower implication—that tip-credit elimination specifically mutes the effect of customer discrimination—is less securely established than the paper claims.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the ingredients of one, but currently it is **two competing stories stitched together**:

- Story 1: “Tipping discrimination is amplified by subminimum wages.”
- Story 2: “Actually, the main effect seems to be a general minimum-wage channel.”

Those are not identical stories. The second one does not kill the paper, but it absolutely kills the current title and much of the introduction as written.

### What story should it be telling?

The paper should tell this story:

1. **Setup:** Food service is a customer-mediated pay sector with an unusually low legal wage floor.
2. **Question:** Does that compensation structure widen racial inequality?
3. **Evidence:** Reforms that raise tipped wage floors narrow racial earnings gaps.
4. **Interpretation:** The effect appears to run at least partly—and perhaps mainly—through restoring a stronger base wage floor, rather than solely through neutralizing tipping discrimination.
5. **Implication:** The design of compensation systems matters for racial inequality; customer-dependent pay and weak employer wage obligations are a problematic combination.

That is coherent. The current “tipping penalty” story is more dramatic but less supported.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?

I would lead with:

> “When states raise the tipped wage floor, racial earnings gaps in food services get smaller.”

That is the cleanest and most defensible headline.

I would not lead with:
> “Customer discrimination in tipping causes racial earnings inequality.”

That is more provocative, but the paper itself does not cleanly establish it.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

They would lean in at first, because tipped wages and racial inequality are both salient and the institutional setting is distinctive. But the lean-in depends on the paper delivering a sharp takeaway. Right now the listener’s interest would fade once they hear that the paper’s internal decomposition blurs the mechanism.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Immediately:

> “Is this really about tipped wages specifically, or just another minimum wage result in a low-wage sector?”

That is the killer follow-up, and the current draft does not have a fully satisfying answer.

A second follow-up:

> “Can you separate front-of-house tipped workers from back-of-house workers or otherwise show the tipping channel directly?”

Again, the current paper cannot really do that.

### If findings are modest, is that okay?

Yes. The Black-white effect is modest in magnitude, but potentially important. A modest effect can be AER-relevant if it changes how we think about the institutional transmission of inequality. The paper needs to make that conceptual case better.

The problem is not that the effect is too small. The problem is that the interpretation overreaches relative to what the estimates can support.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

Be honest: in current form, this is **not yet an AER story**. The issue is not competence. The issue is that the paper has not decided what its big claim is.

### What is the gap?

Primarily a **framing problem**, secondarily a **scope problem**, and to some extent an **ambition problem**.

#### Framing problem
The paper is titled and introduced as if it has identified a tipping-specific discrimination channel, but its own results point toward a broader low-wage-floor story. That mismatch is fatal at the editorial stage because it leaves the reader unsure what the paper actually contributes.

#### Scope problem
The evidence is fairly aggregate: state-quarter-industry-race ratios. That may be enough for a strong reduced-form contribution, but not enough to sustain the richer conceptual claims about tipping discrimination, sorting, and occupational channels.

#### Ambition problem
The paper is content to be “the first causal estimate of X.” That is not enough for AER. It needs to use this setting to say something larger about labor-market institutions and inequality.

### What would excite the top 10 people in this field?

A paper that uses tipped-wage reforms to answer a bigger question:

**When worker pay depends on customers rather than employers, does discrimination become more economically consequential—and can wage-floor policy shut that transmission channel down?**

To excite people, the paper would need either:
- cleaner evidence on the tipping-specific mechanism, or
- a more convincing broader claim about wage-floor design and racial inequality that does not hinge on the tipping-specific mechanism.

### Single most impactful advice

**Stop selling this as a paper proving a “tipping penalty” mechanism unless you can directly isolate that mechanism; instead, reframe it as a paper on how tipped-sector wage-floor design shapes racial inequality, with the tipping-specific channel as a motivating possibility rather than the headline conclusion.**

That one change would fix much of the paper’s strategic problem.
