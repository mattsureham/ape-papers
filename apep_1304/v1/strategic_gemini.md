## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
The paper asks a fundamental, cross-border question: Does granting legal status to asylum seekers in the U.S. significantly boost the economies of their home countries through increased remittances? Leveraging the quasi-random assignment of asylum cases to "lenient" vs. "harsh" judges, the authors estimate the causal impact of legal status on aggregate national remittance inflows.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this clearly. It successfully pivots from the "domestic-only" focus of current immigration literature (wages, crime) to a global macroeconomic perspective.
**Proposed Refined Pitch:** "While $650 billion in remittances dwarfs foreign aid, we lack causal evidence on how U.S. asylum adjudications—a primary gatekeeper of legal work status—shape these flows. We use a judge-leniency instrument to show that while individual asylum grants are life-changing, marginal shifts in court leniency fail to move the macroeconomic needle for origin countries."

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**Contribution:** The paper provides the first causal "bounded null" estimate of the effect of legal status on aggregate origin-country remittance flows using a judge-leniency IV.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It is well-differentiated. While the "judge-leniency" design is a staple of labor/public econ (Kling, Dobbie, etc.), applying it to a *cross-border macroeconomic outcome* is novel.
*   **World vs. Literature:** It answers a question about the **WORLD** (the "deportation tax"). 
*   **Explainability:** A smart economist would say: "It uses random judge assignment to see if more asylum grants mean more money for Haiti or Guatemala; surprisingly, it doesn't."
*   **How to make it bigger:** The paper is currently hampered by the "aggregate" nature of the data. The contribution would be massive if the authors could link **individual** court outcomes to **individual** remittance transfers (e.g., via FinTech or Western Union data). Since they can't, they need to lean harder into the "Why" of the null (e.g., family reunification vs. informal channels).

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
The paper sits at the intersection of **Public/Labor (IV methods)** and **Development/International Finance (Remittances)**.

*   **Closest Neighbors:** Kling (2006) / Dobbie et al. (2018) for the method; Yang (2008) and Clemens (2014) for the remittance content.
*   **Positioning:** It should **synthesize** these. It uses the "gold standard" micro-method (judge IV) to settle a "fuzzy" macro-debate (Remittances).
*   **Unexpected Connection:** The paper should speak more to the **International Finance** literature on "Dutch Disease" or "Externalities of Enforcement."
*   **Conversation:** It is having the right conversation, but it needs to be careful not to be dismissed as "just another judge IV paper." The "cross-border" element is its ticket to the AER.

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC
*   **Setup:** Remittances are a lifeline for the developing world.
*   **Tension:** U.S. courts decide who stays and works legally, but we don't know if these "shocks" to legal status actually translate into national-level wealth for the home country.
*   **Resolution:** A robust null. Marginal changes in grant rates (at the current scale) do not change aggregate remittance volumes.
*   **Implications:** Arguments for/against asylum policy should stay focused on humanitarian or domestic grounds, as the "development dividend" is non-existent at the margin.

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong. It sets up a "common sense" expectation (more grants = more money) and then systematically dismantles it.

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
*   **The Lead Fact:** "Giving 1,000 more people asylum doesn't detectably increase their home country's total bank inflows."
*   **Reaction:** Economists will **lean in** because the null is counter-intuitive.
*   **The Follow-up:** "Is it because they were already remitting illegally, or because they brought their families over once they got status?" 
*   **The Null:** This is a "valuable null." It suggests that the stock of migrants is so large that the flow of new asylees is a rounding error, or that informal channels are highly efficient.

---

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
*   **Front-loading:** The paper is well-structured.
*   **Appendix:** The 29 leave-one-country-out charts are great but belong in an online appendix to keep the main narrative lean.
*   **Deepen the Discussion:** Section 8 (Why is the dividend missing?) is the most important part of the paper for an AER audience. It needs to be expanded with more "back-of-the-envelope" calculations to prove the "composition" explanation.

---

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
**The Gap:** The current version feels a bit like a very high-quality technical exercise. To be a "Big A" AER paper, it needs to move from "We found a null" to "This null changes how we think about the macroeconomics of migration."

**Ambition:** The authors should attempt to quantify the "Family Reunification" channel. If they can show that asylum grants *increase* the probability of dependents moving to the U.S., it explains the null in a way that is theoretically rich.

**Single Biggest Advice:** Spend 5 more pages on the **mechanism of the null**. Prove, using other data (like visa petitions for relatives), that the "Legal Status -> Family Moves -> Remittances Drop" story is the reason the dividend is missing.
