This review evaluates the paper "Building an Industry from Ashes: The Grenfell Tower Fire and the Birth of the UK Fire Safety Sector" according to the standards of an *AER: Insights* submission.

### 1. Idea Fidelity
The paper follows the original idea manifest with high fidelity. It successfully implements the proposed continuous-treatment DiD using LA-level flat density and the tiered regulatory shocks. It utilizes the suggested Companies House SIC codes and correctly identifies the "regulatory cascade" as the primary driver of the five-fold increase in industry formation. The inclusion of the triple-difference (DDD) specification using non-fire construction SICs as a control group directly addresses the manifest’s identification strategy. One minor omission is the "Scotland as geographic placebo" mentioned in the manifest, though the paper compensates for this by focusing on English LAs and providing a robust DDD within England.

### 2. Summary
The paper investigates how a major disaster and its subsequent regulatory response can spontaneously generate a new compliance-based industry. Using a local authority-month panel of UK company registrations, it finds that areas with high high-rise density saw a significant and escalating surge in fire-safety firm incorporations following the 2017 Grenfell Tower fire. The results highlight "regulatory genesis" as a hidden economic consequence of safety legislation, where the speed of market formation becomes a bottleneck for policy implementation.

### 3. Essential Points
1.  **Selection and "Noise" in SIC Codes:** The paper relies on several broad SIC codes (e.g., 71200 - Technical testing; 43999 - Other specialised construction). While the DDD approach helps, these codes likely contain a high volume of firms entirely unrelated to fire safety. The author must provide a validation exercise—for example, keyword-searching the names of a random sample of 200 firms in the "Post" period within these SICs—to estimate the "signal-to-noise" ratio. If only 20% of new firms in SIC 71200 are actually fire-safety related, the coefficients require significant rescaling to be interpretable as "industry birth."
2.  **The "Shell Company" vs. "Real Firm" Problem:** In the UK, incorporating a company is trivial (~£12). A spike in incorporations may represent individuals setting up "one-man-band" consultancies to process EWS1 forms rather than the birth of a substantive "industry" with employees and capital. The paper should attempt to use Companies House data on "Total Assets" or "Number of Employees" (where available for small companies) to distinguish between substantive entry and mere administrative registration for tax/liability purposes.
3.  **Endogenous Siting of Registered Offices:** Companies House data reflects the *registered office* address, which is often an accountant’s office or a residential address, not the location of economic activity. If fire safety consultants disproportionately register their firms in urban centers (which have high flat shares) regardless of where their clients' buildings are located, the "Treatment Intensity × Flat Share" interaction might pick up a mechanical "urban service hub" effect rather than localized demand response. The author needs to demonstrate that the results hold when controlling for a "General Urbanization × Post" flexible trend.

### 4. Suggestions

*   **Refining the Triple-Difference (DDD):** The current DDD uses "control construction" (plumbing, electrical). However, these sectors might also have been affected by the Building Safety Act (e.g., new requirements for electrical safety in high-rises). A cleaner placebo might be an entirely unrelated service sector that also correlates with urban density, such as "Accounting/Legal services" or "Computer Programming." This would more effectively isolate the *specific* regulatory shock from general urban entrepreneurship trends.
*   **The EWS1 Bottleneck Narrative:** The discussion mentions that the "binding constraint... was the supply of qualified professionals." If the author can find a proxy for the *price* of these services (perhaps through news reports or government tenders for remediation), it would add a powerful "Price × Quantity" dimension to the market-creation story.
*   **Visualizing the Shocks:** Table 3 (Regulatory Phases) is excellent. A figure plotting the event-study coefficients alongside vertical lines for each of the five regulatory milestones (Hackitt, EWS1, etc.) would be the "money shot" of the paper. It would visually confirm whether the "jumps" in formation align precisely with the policy announcements.
*   **Survival Analysis:** Because the data goes up to 2024, the author can check how many of the "Phase 1" and "Phase 2" firms are still "Active." If the survival rate of these firms is low, it suggests a speculative bubble in compliance services rather than the birth of a sustainable industry. This would be a high-value addition to the "Discussion" section.
*   **Cladding Remediation Data:** The manifest mentions "Building Safety Remediation data (ODS from GOV.UK)." Integrating this as a second measure of treatment intensity (actual number of dangerous buildings per LA) would be more precise than the "Flat Share" proxy, as not all flats had combustible cladding. A horse-race between "Flat Share" and "Known Dangerous Buildings" would strengthen the demand-side argument.
*   **Small Sample Caveats:** The "Smoke Test" in the manifest notes that for some codes (84250), the baseline was only 6 firms per year. In a panel of 326 LAs, this means most cells are zero. The paper uses Poisson, which is good, but should also consider a Negative Binomial model if overdispersion is present, or a Zero-Inflated model.
*   **Terminology:** The term "Regulatory Genesis" is catchy and effectively summarizes the paper's contribution. I suggest leaning into this framing in the Introduction and Conclusion to enhance the paper’s "Bigger Picture" appeal.
