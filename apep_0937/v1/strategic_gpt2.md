This paper has an intuitively arresting premise and a title that helps it. A disaster triggers not just regulation, but the emergence of a new compliance industry. That is a potentially publishable idea in a top general-interest journal because it speaks to regulation, entrepreneurship, disasters, and the unintended economic consequences of policy. But in its current form, the paper overclaims novelty, leans too hard on the UK institutional narrative, and has not yet convincingly translated a vivid case study into a broad economics question of first-order interest.

## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether major disasters can create entirely new markets by triggering regulation that requires specialized compliance services. Using the Grenfell Tower fire and the ensuing UK building-safety regime, it argues that the post-disaster regulatory response generated a sharp increase in entry by fire-safety-related firms, especially in places with more apartment buildings, revealing a hidden economic consequence of regulation: compliance-market creation.

Why should a busy economist care? Because the paper is not really about one fire; it is about whether regulation reallocates entrepreneurial effort by creating demand for new intermediaries, specialists, and certifiers. If true, that broadens how economists should think about the incidence and welfare consequences of regulation.

### Does the paper itself articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Almost, but not quite. The opening is vivid and memorable, but it is still too tied to the Grenfell story before it states the general economic question. The current introduction says “this paper studies the least-attended economic consequence: the birth of an entirely new industry,” which is strong, but it gets to the general stakes too slowly and too locally. A busy AER reader needs the paper to announce, immediately, that the question is about **how regulation creates markets**, not just about what happened in one UK sector.

### The pitch the paper should have

A stronger first two paragraphs would say something like:

> Economists usually study regulation as a constraint on firms: it raises costs, changes prices, and distorts entry and investment. But many regulations also create demand for new specialist services—inspectors, certifiers, engineers, auditors, and compliance intermediaries. When such markets do not yet exist at scale, regulation may not just burden existing firms; it may create entirely new industries and redirect entrepreneurial activity toward compliance.
>
> This paper studies that process in the aftermath of the 2017 Grenfell Tower fire. I show that the UK’s post-Grenfell building-safety rules generated rapid entry by fire-safety-related firms, with the largest increases occurring in places with more apartment buildings and intensifying as successive regulatory requirements came online. The broader point is that disaster-induced regulation can produce “compliance-market creation,” an economically important but largely unmeasured margin in the effects of regulation.

That version starts with the world, not the case.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### Contribution in one sentence

The paper’s contribution is to argue that disaster-induced regulation can create new compliance-service industries, and to document such industry creation in the UK fire-safety sector after Grenfell.

### Is this clearly differentiated from the closest papers?

Not yet. The paper cites classic regulation papers and some firm-entry papers, but the differentiation is still too schematic: “they study regulation as restricting entry; I study regulation as generating entry.” That contrast is clean rhetorically, but it is not enough intellectually. A skeptical reader will think: many papers have shown that regulation creates demand for lawyers, consultants, auditors, or certifiers; why is this a new contribution rather than a vivid illustration?

The paper needs to be much clearer on what is actually new:

- Is the novelty that a **discrete disaster shock** generates a regulatory cascade that can be dated?
- Is it that the response takes the form of **new firm entry rather than incumbent rent extraction**?
- Is it that the effect unfolds through **supply bottlenecks in certification capacity**?
- Is it that this is a measurable, geographically patterned form of **market creation** rather than a general increase in professional services?

Right now it gestures at all four, which blurs the contribution.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a literature gap?

It is partly framed about the world, which is good, but it slips too often into literature-gap language. The strongest parts are the claims that regulation can create industries and that the speed of compliance-market formation affects how long disruption persists. The weakest parts are the paragraphs that say, in effect, “existing theory looks at rents/incumbents, I look at entrants.”

For AER, the paper should more consistently frame the contribution as:

- In the world, some regulations require specialized service capacity that does not yet exist.
- When that happens, regulation affects equilibrium through a missing-market margin.
- Grenfell offers a sharp case of this broader phenomenon.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the intro?

At the moment they might say: “It’s a DiD on firm formation after Grenfell using apartment share as exposure.” That is not enough. They should instead come away saying: “It shows that regulation can create new industries, and that the bottleneck is not just the regulated capital stock but the supply of certifiers.”

The introduction needs to make it much harder for the reader to reduce the paper to “another regional differential-exposure design.”

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Three specific ways:

1. **Shift the key outcome from firm counts to market capacity.**  
   The most interesting world question is not whether more firms register, but whether regulation creates the specialist capacity needed to implement itself. Employment, professional accreditation, revenues, mortgage-processing delays, certification backlogs, or remediation timelines would make the paper much larger. Right now “new companies were formed” is one step too far from the real economics.

2. **Make the bottleneck mechanism central.**  
   The most compelling line in the paper is that one million apartments became effectively unmortgageable until specialists certified them. If the paper can connect firm creation to the duration of the housing-market freeze, that becomes much more consequential. Without that, the reader may view firm formation as descriptive rather than important.

3. **Generalize beyond Grenfell in framing or evidence.**  
   Even a modest comparative angle—another UK regulatory certification market, another safety-driven market, or a broader conceptual taxonomy of “compliance-market creation”—would make the contribution feel less case-study-specific.

3. **Disaster economics and post-disaster adjustment**

4. **Intermediation / experts / certification markets**
   - This is the major missing conversation
   - Auditors, appraisers, inspectors, ratings, third-party certifiers, compliance consultants, environmental remediation consultants, healthcare coders, etc.

### How should the paper position itself relative to those neighbors?

Build and synthesize, not attack. The right claim is not “the prior literature missed this entirely.” That sounds naive and likely false. The better claim is:

- Existing work shows regulation changes firm behavior, entry conditions, and incumbents’ rents.
- Separate literatures study third-party certification and compliance intermediaries.
- This paper connects them by showing that regulation can create an industry where one did not previously exist at scale, especially after a sudden shock.

That is more modest and more credible.

### Is the paper currently positioned too narrowly or too broadly?

Paradoxically, both.

- **Too narrowly** because it spends many pages on UK institutional detail before clearly identifying the broad economics question.
- **Too broadly** because “regulations create markets” is stated at such a high level that it risks seeming obvious unless pinned to a more precise mechanism.

The sweet spot is narrower conceptually and broader substantively: “specialized compliance markets as an implementation margin of regulation.”

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?

The biggest omission is the literature on **regulatory intermediaries, certification markets, and expert service provision**. This paper should be in conversation with work on auditors, environmental consultants, healthcare billing/coding/compliance services, appraisers, testing/certification bodies, and the industrial organization of compliance sectors.

It also may be under-engaging with:
- urban/regional entrepreneurship under demand shocks,
- occupational licensing / professional capacity,
- policy implementation bottlenecks,
- organizational responses to new legal mandates.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?

Not fully. It currently frames itself as regulation + disaster + firm formation. The more impactful conversation is **regulation + market design/implementation + intermediation**. The surprising and useful point is not that businesses form when demand rises. It is that regulation often presumes specialist market capacity, and when that capacity is missing, implementation itself becomes an economic problem.

That is the unexpected literature connection that could raise the paper’s ceiling.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Before this paper, the world is one in which economists think of regulation mainly as imposing costs on existing actors or changing incumbent rents. In the Grenfell setting, there was a latent stock of risky buildings, but only a thin fire-safety services sector.

### Tension

The disaster triggered rules that made certification and assessment suddenly essential at scale. But if the necessary specialist service market barely exists, then regulation cannot bite cleanly; it first has to call into existence the very industry required for compliance. This creates a puzzle: can regulation itself generate a new market, and does the speed of that market’s emergence shape the real disruption that follows?

### Resolution

The paper finds that after Grenfell, fire-safety-related firm formation rose sharply, especially in places with more flats, and rose further with successive regulatory milestones. The author interprets this as evidence of demand-driven compliance-market creation.

### Implications

Regulatory impact should include not just direct compliance costs, but also the creation of specialist compliance sectors and the transition frictions that arise when those sectors are initially missing. In post-disaster settings, implementation capacity may be as important as the statutory rule itself.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

There is a real narrative arc here, which is a strength. But the current draft still reads somewhat like a collection of empirical sections attached to a clever phrase (“regulatory genesis”). The paper’s story should be less “here is a neat new term and a case” and more “here is a neglected economic mechanism with broad applicability, for which Grenfell is a sharp test case.”

The story it should be telling is:

1. Regulation often requires specialist intermediaries.
2. Those intermediaries may not exist at sufficient scale.
3. Therefore regulation can create markets, not just distort them.
4. This margin matters because it determines the speed and incidence of adjustment.

That is the real paper. Grenfell is the vehicle.

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

### What fact would I lead with?

I would lead with: “After Grenfell, a building-safety regulatory cascade helped create a fire-safety compliance sector at scale; firm entry in the relevant sector nearly tripled, and the increase was largest where the housing stock made certification demand greatest.”

That is dinner-party legible.

### Would people lean in?

Some would. The topic has real-world salience, and the idea that regulation can create industries is interesting. But many would quickly ask: “Is that surprising?” or “Is firm registration really the economically important margin?” That is the vulnerability.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Almost certainly: “Did this actually matter for the housing market, safety outcomes, or implementation delays?” Or: “How much of this is real industry creation versus relabeling, broad SIC coding, and urban business formation?”

Those are not just econometric questions; they are strategic ones. They reveal that the paper’s current endpoint—firm formation—is one step short of the payoff.

### If findings are modest, is the modesty itself interesting?

The findings are not null, but they are modest in conceptual depth relative to the broad framing. “There were more firms after regulation” can feel like a predictable equilibrium response unless the paper shows why this is economically consequential. The paper partly does that with the mortgage-market freeze example, but only rhetorically. To pass the “so what?” test at AER level, it needs to make the consequences of missing compliance capacity feel central rather than anecdotal.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The gap is mostly **ambition and framing**, with some **scope** issues.

- **Framing problem:** yes. The science may be fine, but the paper is still written as a clever case study rather than a broadly important economics paper.
- **Scope problem:** yes. Firm registrations alone are probably too narrow an outcome for AER unless the paper can tie them to market capacity, implementation frictions, or large downstream consequences.
- **Novelty problem:** somewhat. The exact case is novel, but the general idea that regulation creates demand for compliance services is not obviously new. The paper has to define a sharper and more surprising version of that claim.
- **Ambition problem:** definitely. The current version is competent but safe. It documents a pattern. AER wants a paper that changes how economists think about regulation.

### What is the single most impactful piece of advice?

**Reframe the paper around implementation capacity: show that the economically important effect of post-disaster regulation is not merely more firm formation, but the creation of a missing compliance market that governs how fast regulation can actually be implemented.**

That one change would force better framing, stronger stakes, and likely better outcome choices.
