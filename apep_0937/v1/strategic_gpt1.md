## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether a major disaster can do more than destroy economic activity—whether it can also create an entirely new compliance industry through the regulatory response it triggers. Using the Grenfell Tower fire and subsequent UK building-safety regulation, the paper argues that places with more apartment buildings saw disproportionately more entry of fire-safety-related firms, suggesting that regulation created a new market for certification, assessment, and compliance services.

Why should a busy economist care? Because the paper is trying to identify a general mechanism: regulation does not just impose costs on existing firms; it can induce entrepreneurial entry and reallocate talent into compliance production. That is potentially interesting well beyond housing or the UK.

### Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?
Mostly, but not quite. The current opening is vivid and specific, which is good, but it gets trapped in the Grenfell institutional details before crystallizing the broader economic question. The introduction currently reads like “a paper about Grenfell and fire-safety firms,” when it wants to be “a paper about how disaster-driven regulation creates markets.”

The first two paragraphs should pivot faster from event to question to generality. Right now the paper’s best idea—“regulatory genesis”—arrives too late and feels coined after the fact rather than posed as the organizing question from the outset.

### The pitch the paper should have
A major disaster often triggers regulation, but we know surprisingly little about whether such regulation merely burdens existing firms or instead creates entirely new markets for compliance. This paper studies the Grenfell Tower fire and the UK building-safety rules that followed, showing that the regulatory response generated a sharp, geographically concentrated wave of entry by fire-safety-related firms in places with greater exposure to affected buildings. The broader implication is that compliance markets are a first-order but overlooked margin of regulatory incidence: post-disaster regulation can redirect entrepreneurship toward certification and monitoring, and the speed of that market creation shapes how long disruption persists.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### Contribution in one sentence
The paper’s contribution is to argue that disaster-induced regulation can create a new compliance-services market, and to document such market creation through increased firm entry after Grenfell in areas more exposed to the affected building stock.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?
Not yet clearly enough. The paper cites broad literatures—Stigler/Peltzman on regulation, disaster economics, and firm entry—but the actual contribution sits between them rather than squarely advancing any one of them. That can be good, but only if the authors sharply explain what prior work does **not** do.

Right now the differentiation is too schematic:
- “prior regulation papers study rents/incumbents; I study entry”
- “prior disaster papers study prices/fiscal effects; I study compliance markets”
- “prior entry papers study rules that deter entry; I study rules that generate entry”

That is directionally right, but still vulnerable to the reaction: “OK, this is an event-study/DiD showing sectoral entry after a policy shock.” The paper needs to do more to distinguish itself from the large universe of papers showing that regulation shifts economic activity across sectors.

### World question or literature-gap question?
It is closer to a **world** question than a literature-gap question, which is good: what kinds of industries does regulation create after disasters? But the paper keeps slipping back into literature bookkeeping. Its strongest framing is about the world: when governments respond to crises by requiring inspections, certifications, and assessments, the missing market for compliance becomes part of the real economic incidence of regulation.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new?
At present, a smart economist could say: “It’s a DiD paper showing that Grenfell-related regulation increased entry of fire-safety-related firms.” That is competent, but not yet memorable.

The introduction needs to arm the reader with a stronger one-liner, something like:
> “The novelty is not that regulation matters for firms; it is that post-disaster regulation can create a bottleneck industry from scratch, and that bottleneck itself becomes part of the disaster’s economic cost.”

That is what would stick.

### What would make this contribution bigger?
Specific ways to enlarge the contribution:

1. **Show the market-creation margin more directly.**  
   The title and framing claim the “birth” of an industry. But the outcome is incorporations in broad SIC codes, some of which clearly predate Grenfell and are not pure fire-safety firms. The paper would feel bigger and less fragile if it could better isolate genuinely new fire-safety specialists—e.g., firm names, text descriptions, certifications, professional registries, job postings, or websites.

2. **Connect entry to real bottlenecks.**  
   The most important implication in the paper is not just “more firms formed,” but “market creation determined how long the housing market freeze lasted.” That is potentially AER-level. To get there, the paper would benefit from even one outcome linking supply creation to actual frictions: delays in EWS1 assessments, mortgage processing, apartment transactions, remediation timing, or professional wages.

3. **Clarify whether this is about regulation broadly or post-disaster regulation specifically.**  
   If the paper is about post-disaster regulation, emphasize the sequencing: disaster → regulation → certification bottleneck → entrepreneurial entry → persistence of disruption. If it is about regulation more broadly, one case study is probably too narrow.

4. **Make the mechanism less generic.**  
   “Demand-driven industry creation” is true but thin. The more interesting mechanism is that regulation requiring scarce expertise creates *credentialed intermediation markets*. That is richer and more distinctive than simply saying local demand went up.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

### Closest neighbors
The closest neighbors are not the canonical Stigler/Peltzman papers. Those are too distant. The paper’s real neighbors are in at least four conversations:

1. **Regulation and economic incidence / compliance costs**
   - Greenstone (2002)
   - Greenstone, List, and Syverson (2012)
   - Djankov et al. (2002)

2. **Disasters and economic adjustment**
   - Deryugina (2017)
   - Koster and van Ommeren (2015)
   - More broadly, disaster recovery / spatial equilibrium papers

3. **Entrepreneurship and firm entry under policy shocks**
   - Aghion et al. (2021) if innovation/regulation is the angle
   - Garicano et al. (2016) on thresholds and firm distortions
   - Also papers on policy-induced entry in healthcare, environmental services, audit/compliance, etc.

4. **Housing-finance / building safety / transaction frictions**
   - Work on mortgage market freezes, certification constraints, housing illiquidity, and regulatory bottlenecks
   - This may actually be the most underexploited adjacent literature

### How should the paper position itself relative to those neighbors?
Mostly **build on and bridge**, not attack.

The paper should say:
- relative to regulation papers: we extend incidence beyond costs on regulated entities to include creation of compliance suppliers;
- relative to disaster papers: we show a supply-side adjustment margin triggered by regulatory response rather than physical damage alone;
- relative to entry papers: we identify a setting where regulation stimulates, rather than deters, entry—but into compliance production.

The current introduction tries to do this, but with too much emphasis on classic citations and not enough emphasis on the actual modern adjacent literatures that readers will use to place the paper.

### Too narrow or too broad?
Paradoxically, both.

- **Too narrow** in institutional detail: it spends a lot of space on UK fire-safety milestones.
- **Too broad** in theoretical ambition: “regulatory genesis” is presented almost as a general law from one case.

The paper should be narrower in claim and broader in conversation. It should say: this is a sharp case study that reveals a general but underappreciated margin. That is stronger than claiming to have established a sweeping new category of regulation economics.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?
It seems especially under-engaged with:
- compliance/intermediation markets in economics and adjacent fields;
- professional services as bottlenecks;
- housing market transaction frictions and certification constraints;
- public administration / regulatory implementation literatures;
- perhaps occupational licensing / credentialing, if the mechanism is scarcity of qualified assessors.

That last point matters: one reason the story is interesting is not simply that regulation created demand, but that the service required trained, quasi-licensed specialists. That places the paper near labor-market and occupation-supply questions too.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?
Not fully. The most impactful framing may come not from “economics of regulation” in the abstract but from **implementation capacity** and **compliance bottlenecks**. The interesting question is not merely whether regulation creates costs; it is whether regulation creates shortages of the very intermediaries needed to comply, thereby amplifying disruption. That is a sharper and more modern conversation.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
A disaster exposes a latent safety problem in the housing stock. The government responds with cascading regulation requiring inspections, certifications, and ongoing safety case management.

### Tension
Those rules cannot function without specialized providers, but such providers may not exist at scale ex ante. So regulation may create a market bottleneck: the policy can only bite to the extent a compliance sector emerges.

### Resolution
After Grenfell, fire-safety-related firm formation rises disproportionately in high-flat-share areas, and this differential increases as the regulatory cascade deepens.

### Implications
Compliance markets are part of the incidence of regulation. Policymakers should account for the entrepreneurial and professional capacity needed to implement new rules, especially after disasters, because shortages in compliance supply can prolong market freezes and adjustment costs.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
It has the bones of one, but not full discipline. At times it feels like:
- a Grenfell case study,
- a firm-entry paper,
- a regulation-cost paper,
- and a disaster-recovery paper,

all at once. The results are coherent, but the story is not yet fully hierarchized.

The best story is:
> Disaster regulation created a missing market; entry into that market was rapid but incomplete; that market-formation process is an overlooked component of regulatory incidence.

Everything should serve that story. Some current material pulls away from it—for example the broad claims about “industry birth” and the long institutional chronology. The paper needs more tension around the bottleneck: why should economists care whether these firms formed? Because without them, the housing market and remediation process remained stuck.

So yes, there is a story here—but the paper should tell one story, not four related ones.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?
I would lead with:
> “After Grenfell, the UK didn’t just tighten building-safety rules—it effectively had to create a new compliance sector, and firm entry in fire-safety services surged in the places with the most affected buildings.”

That is the memorable fact.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
Some would lean in, but many would only do so if the second sentence arrives quickly:
> “This matters because the bottleneck wasn’t just dangerous buildings; it was the shortage of assessors needed to certify them, which helped freeze parts of the housing market.”

Without that follow-through, the audience reaction is: “Interesting niche UK institutional story.” With it, the reaction is: “Ah—this is about implementation capacity and hidden regulatory incidence.”

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Almost certainly:
1. “Did the new firms actually relieve the bottleneck?”
2. “Were these real firms or just relabeled consultancies?”
3. “How much of the post-Grenfell housing disruption can be traced to missing compliance capacity rather than the safety problem itself?”

Those are exactly the questions the paper needs to anticipate strategically, even before referees get into design.

### If findings are modest/null?
The findings are not null, but they are still vulnerable to feeling modest because the core outcome is firm registration counts. The paper therefore must make the case that this is not just another sectoral-entry response. The value is in showing a hidden margin of regulatory incidence. If that argument is not sharpened, the evidence risks reading like a stylized fact rather than a major economic insight.

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

### What would make it read better?

1. **Shorten the identification discussion in the introduction.**  
   The intro is too eager to reassure. This is not where the paper wins readers. AER readers need the question, the mechanism, the punchline, and the contribution. The econometric details can come later.

2. **Move some institutional detail out of the main text or tighten it heavily.**  
   Four regulatory phases are useful, but the current background section is long relative to the scale of the paper. Keep only the minimum needed to understand why compliance demand ramped up over time.

3. **Front-load the key conceptual claim.**  
   “Compliance markets as hidden regulatory incidence” should appear in paragraph 1 or 2, not as a discussion-section takeaway.

4. **Bring the best result earlier and more crisply.**  
   The escalating phase pattern is actually one of the most interesting pieces because it supports the regulatory-cascade interpretation. It should be highlighted sooner, perhaps even previewed in the introduction as the main substantive result rather than a secondary table.

5. **Do not bury the biggest caveat—industry measurement.**  
   Broad SICs are not just a technical limitation; they are central to whether the “birth of an industry” claim is believable. The paper should either soften the claim throughout or bring stronger descriptive evidence into the main text.

6. **The discussion is more interesting than parts of the intro.**  
   The paragraph on compliance markets as a hidden cost is the conceptual heart of the paper. It should migrate upward.

7. **Conclusion currently mostly summarizes.**  
   It should do one more thing: tell the reader what belief to update. Namely, regulation should be analyzed not just through compliance burdens on regulated parties, but through the creation, scarcity, and location of compliance suppliers.

### Are good results buried?
Yes. The phase-escalation result is valuable and should probably have higher billing. Also, any evidence that the post-Grenfell surge is unusually concentrated in the most plausibly relevant subcategories should be in the main text, not just gestured at.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The gap is mostly **ambition plus framing**, with some **scope** concerns.

### Is it a framing problem?
Yes, substantially. The paper has a potentially publishable idea, but it has not yet found the highest-value way to state it. “Birth of the UK Fire Safety Sector” is vivid, but also sounds narrower and more journalistic than the underlying economics question.

### Is it a scope problem?
Yes. For AER, one outcome—firm registrations in broad SICs—is likely too thin unless the conceptual claim is extremely sharp or the supporting evidence becomes richer. To excite top people in the field, the paper needs to say more about the consequences of this market creation or bottleneck, not just its existence.

### Is it a novelty problem?
Somewhat. The reader may wonder whether this is just a particular instance of policy-induced entry. The paper needs to establish why compliance-market creation is economically distinct from ordinary demand shocks or regulation-induced reallocation.

### Is it an ambition problem?
Yes. The paper is competent but currently safe. It documents a neat fact. AER papers usually either change how we think about a broad question or bring unusually persuasive evidence to a major one. This paper aspires to the former but currently lands closer to the latter.

### Single most impactful advice
If the author can change only one thing: **reframe the paper around compliance bottlenecks as a missing margin of regulatory incidence, and support that framing with at least one direct piece of evidence that the new firms mattered for real economic frictions, not just incorporation counts.**

That is the difference between “interesting case study” and “paper economists remember.”
