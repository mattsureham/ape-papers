 **Referee Report: "The Floor Lifts All Boats? Minimum Wages and the Racial Labor Income Gap in Low-Wage Services"**

1. **Idea Fidelity**

The submitted manuscript deviates substantively from the original research design outlined in the manifest. Most critically, the paper abandons the county-level geographic variation that was central to the proposed identification strategy. The manifest specified analysis at the **county×quarter×industry×race** level using QWI rh/n3 files, enabling (1) Callaway-Sant'Anna (2021) staggered difference-in-differences with cohort-specific treatment effects, and (2) Dube-Lester-Reich (2010) contiguous border-county designs. Instead, the paper aggregates to **state-quarter-industry** cells (7,326 observations), eliminating the within-state cross-sectional variation necessary for the proposed robust estimators. This aggregation is not justified and fundamentally alters the estimand from local labor market effects to state-level aggregate impacts. Additionally, the promised continuous treatment (dose-response) analysis using the real MW level relative to the federal floor is relegated to a minor robustness check (binned categories), while the main specification uses a triple-difference design not described in the original proposal. The paper retains the core research question and data source but sacrifices the granular variation that provided the credible identification strategy.

2. **Summary**

This paper examines whether state minimum wage increases narrow the Black-White labor income gap in low-wage service industries using Quarterly Workforce Indicators (2005–2023). Using a triple-difference design comparing low-wage (retail, accommodation/food service) and high-wage (finance, professional services) industries across states with varying minimum wage levels, the author finds that minimum wage increases raise the Black-to-White wage bill ratio by approximately 17 log points, driven by both employment and earnings compression channels.

3. **Essential Points**

**i. Aggregation to state level undermines identification.** The paper aggregates county-level QWI data to state-quarter-industry cells, eliminating the within-state variation that was intended to identify treatment effects. The manifest explicitly proposed using "county×quarter×industry×race cells" to leverage staggered adoption across local labor markets and implement border-county designs. By aggregating to 49 states, the paper relies on cross-state variation confounded by dozens of simultaneous policy changes and economic shocks, rendering the "staggered DiD" label in the abstract misleading (no cohort-specific estimators are implemented). The triple-difference design does not resolve this: comparing high-wage and low-wage industries within states absorbs some state-level shocks but cannot address industry-specific trends correlated with MW adoption (e.g., states raising MW may simultaneously expand public sector employment that competes for Black workers in low-wage sectors). **Required:** Re-estimate using county-level data as specified in the original design, or provide rigorous justification for aggregation (e.g., binding constraints at the state level) and implement robust staggered DiD estimators (Callaway-Sant'Anna or Sun-Abraham) to handle heterogeneous treatment effects.

**ii. Inconsistent results across specifications require resolution.** The TWFE results (Table 1) show null or negative point estimates for the wage bill and employment gaps, while the triple-difference specification (Table 2) flips these to large, significant positive effects. The standardized effect sizes in the Appendix (Table A1) report "moderate negative" effects for the pooled sample, contradicting the abstract's emphasis on large positive impacts. This inconsistency suggests the results are sensitive to the identifying assumptions imposed. The triple-difference relies on the assumption that, absent MW changes, the racial earnings gap would have evolved similarly across high-wage and high-wage industries within states—an assumption that is neither visually validated (no pre-trend figures) nor theoretically compelling given deep occupational segregation patterns. **Required:** Provide event-study plots showing the evolution of the racial gap in high-wage vs. low-wage industries in treated vs. control states pre- and post-treatment. Reconcile the sign reversal between TWFE and triple-difference specifications, potentially through a decomposition of the bias terms.

**iii. Mechanism interpretation is unsupported.** The paper attributes positive employment effects to monopsony power reducing employer discrimination, but the QWI employment variable measures employment stocks (*Emp*), not flows or hours. A rising Black employment share in low-wage industries could reflect (a) reduced separations, (b) increased hiring, (c) compositional shifts within the industry, or (d) general equilibrium effects drawing workers from other sectors. Without analyzing hires/separations (*HirN*, *SepS*) or hours worked—which are available in the QWI—the monopsony interpretation is speculative. Moreover, the "decomposition" assumes additive separability of the coefficients (earnings + employment = wage bill), which holds mechanically for the outcomes but not necessarily for the regression coefficients given covariance between errors. **Required:** Validate the mechanism using QWI flow data (hires/separations) to distinguish between retention and hiring margins, and formally test the additivity constraint in the decomposition.

4. **Suggestions**

**Empirical Strategy and Identification.** If the authors retain the state-level aggregation (which I strongly discourage), they should implement robust estimators for staggered adoption. The current TWFE specification is biased under heterogeneous treatment effects (Goodman-Bacon 2021). The Callaway-Sant'Anna estimator was promised in the manifest but not delivered; it should be the primary specification, using the 115% of federal floor threshold mentioned in the manifest to define treatment timing, rather than the continuous log MW interaction which conflates timing and intensity.

The triple-difference design requires stronger justification for the "parallel trends in gaps" assumption. High-wage and low-wage industries likely differ in their exposure to automation, offshoring, and skill-biased technical change that differentially affect Black workers. Consider augmenting the design with a matched sample of counties across state borders (Dube-Lester-Reich) where MW changes are sharp and local labor market conditions are more comparable, or implement a synthetic control approach at the state-industry level.

**Measurement and Data.** The treatment variable is measured as log real MW, but the manifest proposed a binary treatment (exceeding 115% of federal floor) with continuous dose as a robustness check. The continuous measure conflates states that raised MW early (e.g., Washington) with those that raised it late but sharply (e.g., New York). Consider estimating separate effects by timing (early vs. late adopters) and magnitude (dose).

The paper uses *EarnS* (average earnings) as the wage measure, but this is top-coded and may capture intensive margin (hours) rather than wage rates. Consider using *Payroll* where available (the manifest notes suppression issues) or examining *HirA* (average earnings of new hires) to isolate the wage-offer distribution.

**Interpretation and Robustness.** The placebo test in high-wage industries (Table 3) is underpowered and conceptually distinct from a true placebo. A better test would examine outcomes for workers *above* the 75th percentile of the wage distribution within low-wage industries, who should not be affected by MW floors. Additionally, test for spillovers: MW increases in retail may raise wages in adjacent sectors (e.g., warehousing), violating the SUTVA assumption implicit in the triple-difference.

The monopsony interpretation would be strengthened by showing that Black workers are concentrated in firms with higher wage markdowns pre-reform. The QWI does not contain firm identifiers, but the *Job-to-Job Flows* (J2J) data could link workers to origin/destination firms to test whether MW reduces differential turnover for Black workers.

**Presentation and Transparency.** The Appendix reports standardized effect sizes that classify the TWFE results as "moderate negative"—directly contradicting the main text's emphasis on "striking" positive effects. The abstract should clarify that results are driven by the triple-difference specification, not the baseline TWFE. Report pre-trend coefficients for the 20+ quarters of pre-period data available; a single sentence stating "no detectable pre-trend" is insufficient given the sensitivity of difference-in-differences to trend assumptions.

Finally, clarify the sample construction: the manifest specified 17 never-treated states at $7.25, but the paper reports 32 never-treated states. Which states are included? Excluding states with indexed MWs (e.g., Florida, Ohio) or local variations (e.g., Illinois, where Chicago differs from downstate) is crucial for a clean treatment definition.

**Conclusion.** This paper addresses an important and timely question with a novel administrative data source. However, the aggregation to state level fundamentally alters the research design, the identification strategy lacks the robust staggered DiD methods promised, and the mechanism interpretation is premature. I recommend **revise and resubmit** contingent on the authors either (a) reverting to county-level analysis with border-county or robust staggered estimators, or (b) providing rigorous justification for state-level aggregation coupled with detailed pre-trend validation and mechanism tests using QWI flow data.
