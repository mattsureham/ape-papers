## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

Using administrative data by race, industry, state, and quarter, it argues that higher minimum wages reduce the racial gap in total labor income, with gains coming through both higher relative earnings and improved relative employment for Black workers.

A busy economist should care because this is a potentially important reframing of the minimum wage debate: not just “how many jobs,” but “which workers gain.” If persuasive, the paper would speak to one of the central distributional questions in labor economics—whether wage floors compress racial inequality in the contemporary U.S. labor market.

**Does the paper articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Partly, but not well enough. The current opening is competent and topical, but it still reads too much like “there is a gap in the literature, and I fill it with a triple-difference design.” The first two paragraphs should more sharply foreground the world question and the surprising substantive claim: minimum wages may be an instrument of racial income convergence, and the key uncertainty is whether employment losses undo the gains.

**What the first two paragraphs should say instead:**

> Black workers remain overrepresented in low-wage service jobs, so minimum wage policy is also racial inequality policy. But economists still do not know whether higher wage floors narrow Black–White labor income gaps in today’s labor market, because the answer depends on two opposing margins: higher wages for workers near the floor, versus possible reductions in jobs or hours in the same sectors.
>
> This paper asks which force dominates. Using administrative data that track Black and White employment and earnings by state, quarter, and industry, I show that recent state minimum wage increases narrowed the Black–White labor income gap in low-wage services, and did so through both earnings compression and relative employment gains for Black workers. The result recasts minimum wages as a distributional policy with racial incidence, not just an employment policy.

That is the pitch. It is simpler, more world-facing, and immediately tells the reader what belief might change.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Black workers are disproportionately employed in low-wage service sectors where minimum wages bind, and those sectors remain central to racial earnings inequality.

### Tension
Minimum wages mechanically compress low-end wages, but they may also reduce employment or hours for exactly the workers they are meant to help. So it is unclear whether minimum wage hikes actually narrow racial labor income gaps once both margins are counted.

### Resolution
The paper finds that higher minimum wages are associated with a narrowing of the Black–White labor income gap in low-wage services, with contributions from both relative earnings gains and relative employment gains for Black workers.

### Implications
If true, minimum wage policy is not just a labor standard with ambiguous aggregate employment effects; it is a policy that can reduce racial inequality in labor income. That would shift how economists think about the welfare and incidence of wage floors.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
It has the skeleton of one, but it is not fully disciplined. At times it reads like a sequence of empirical exercises organized around a design rather than a story organized around a question.

The biggest narrative problem: the paper has **two different stories** competing for dominance.

1. **Story A:** Minimum wages affect racial inequality through wage compression and employment.
2. **Story B:** QWI data enable a new decomposition and triple-difference design.

Story A is the real paper. Story B is support material. The draft too often lets Story B take over.

### What story should it be telling?
The clean story is:

- Black workers are more exposed to wage floors because of where they work.
- That makes minimum wages a racial inequality policy.
- The unresolved question is whether employment offsets erase those gains.
- In contemporary low-wage services, they do not; if anything, the employment margin reinforces convergence.
- Therefore, the incidence of minimum wages is more equalizing across racial groups than the standard aggregate debate suggests.

That is a strong narrative. The current draft is close, but it needs more discipline.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Almost certainly:  
**“Why is the employment effect going in that direction?”**

That is the paper’s pivotal question. If the author cannot give a compelling economic interpretation, the result will feel like an artifact rather than a contribution. Right now, the draft’s monopsony explanation is possible but too generic. The paper needs a more convincing account of why relative employment for Black workers improves—e.g., lower discrimination rents, stronger retention, reduced turnover, tighter labor markets interacting with employer power, compositional shifts, etc. Even absent full causal proof, the paper must organize the reader’s thinking more clearly.

This is not a null paper, so the issue is not whether the null is interesting. The issue is whether the headline result feels like a genuine empirical discovery or a fragile pattern in a narrow setup. The framing should make it feel like the former.
