## 1. Idea Fidelity

The paper broadly follows the manifest’s core idea: it studies 2019–2021 ULR adoption in 11 states, uses QWI race-disaggregated data, focuses on healthcare, and estimates a Black-versus-White, healthcare-versus-placebo, treated-versus-control triple difference with a staggered-adoption supplement. In that sense, the paper is faithful to the intended research question.

That said, several important elements of the original design were altered or not fully implemented. First, the manifest emphasized the Azure QWI race-Hispanic panel and suggested weekly earnings; the paper instead uses state-level QWI monthly earnings of “stable workers” and does not clearly document the exact series or extraction choices. Second, the manifest proposed state-level controls (healthcare demand, beds, union density), but these are dropped. Third, the motivating mechanism is specifically about licensed healthcare occupations, yet the implemented outcome is for the entire NAICS 62 sector, which includes many workers not directly affected by licensing portability. This is the biggest departure from the original idea and has first-order implications for interpretation.

## 2. Summary

This paper asks whether Universal Licensing Recognition laws reduced the Black-White earnings gap in healthcare. Using state-by-quarter QWI data and a triple-difference design comparing Black versus White workers, healthcare versus manufacturing, and ULR versus non-ULR states, the paper finds no statistically or economically meaningful average effect.

The topic is important and potentially publishable in principle: a race-disaggregated analysis of licensing portability is novel and policy-relevant. However, in its current form, the paper does not yet convincingly identify the causal effect of ULR on the earnings gap among the workers plausibly exposed to the policy, so the main conclusion feels stronger than the evidence supports.

## 3. Essential Points

1. **Treatment-outcome mismatch is currently too severe for the paper’s interpretation.**  
   ULR applies to licensed occupations, but the paper estimates effects on all workers in NAICS 62. That sector includes many unlicensed or weakly affected jobs (aides, support staff, administrative workers, social assistance). A null effect for the sector is not strong evidence that “licensing portability does not appear to be a binding constraint on the Black-White healthcare wage gap.” At most, it is evidence of no detectable reduced-form effect on the sector-wide racial earnings gap. The paper must either narrow the sample to plausibly licensed healthcare segments or rewrite the claims much more modestly.

2. **The identification argument for manufacturing as the comparison industry is underdeveloped and not yet persuasive.**  
   The DDD assumption is not just “parallel trends”; it requires that, absent ULR, the Black-White earnings gap in healthcare would have evolved similarly to the Black-White gap in manufacturing across treated and untreated states. That is a strong cross-sector assumption, especially through COVID, when healthcare and manufacturing experienced very different shocks, labor demand changes, hazard premia, and racial compositional changes. The event-study pre-trends help, but they do not by themselves validate manufacturing as the right counterfactual during 2020–2022. The paper needs a more convincing justification and stronger evidence that results are not driven by sector-specific shocks.

3. **Data construction and measurement need much greater clarity and validation.**  
   There are internal inconsistencies: the text describes “average monthly earnings of stable workers,” while notes elsewhere refer to total quarterly earnings divided by beginning-of-quarter employment; this is not the same construct. The QWI variable definition, weighting, suppression/missingness rules, and treatment of small Black cells are not adequately documented. Given the small number of state-race-industry-quarter cells and the use of a highly aggregated panel, measurement error and compositional change are central concerns. The paper needs a transparent data appendix and more direct validation that the observed racial gap series are reliable enough for a causal design.

## 4. Suggestions

The paper has a good question and a clean writing style, but it needs sharper alignment between policy, sample, and estimand. My main recommendation is to reconceive the contribution as a **reduced-form sector-level analysis** unless the authors can get closer to licensed occupations. Right now the paper’s strongest claim is too broad relative to what is actually estimated. If the data permit, the ideal fix is to move from NAICS 62 as a whole to subindustries or worker groups where licensing exposure is much more concentrated. Even imperfectly, separating hospitals, ambulatory care, nursing/residential care, and social assistance would already help. A result concentrated in healthcare settings with a higher share of licensed professionals would be much more informative than an average over the whole sector. If such disaggregation is infeasible, the paper should repeatedly acknowledge that its null is diluted by many untreated workers.

Relatedly, I would encourage the authors to rethink the mechanism section. The introduction tells a compelling story about interstate mobility of licensed Black healthcare workers, but the empirical design never observes mobility, licensing status, occupation, or destination sorting. That is acceptable for a short paper, but then the interpretation needs to remain reduced-form. I would suggest reframing the conclusion from “licensing portability is not a binding constraint” to something like: “ULR did not generate detectable short-run changes in the sector-level Black-White earnings gap in healthcare.” That may sound narrower, but it is more credible and still useful.

On identification, the manufacturing comparison needs more work. The paper should show the underlying DDD components explicitly: trends in the Black-white gap within healthcare and manufacturing, separately for treated and untreated states. A figure with the four gap series would greatly improve transparency. I would also like to see alternative comparison groups that are closer to healthcare in workforce composition and pandemic exposure but not directly affected by ULR—for example retail, accommodation/food, education services where available, or even a stacked placebo set rather than a single comparator. The retail placebo in the robustness table is a start, but it is not enough because it is presented only as one coefficient, with no conceptual discussion. If the sign and magnitude move materially across placebo sectors, that would indicate the DDD is sensitive to the comparison industry and should temper causal claims.

COVID remains a major challenge. The paper is right that Arizona provides some pre-COVID variation, but the Arizona-only estimate is itself not especially reassuring: it is small but statistically significant and opposite-signed. More importantly, one state over one year is a thin basis for inference. I would suggest a fuller pre-COVID analysis using only Arizona and Pennsylvania through 2019Q4 if timing permits, or at least a specification that drops all post-2019 quarters and asks whether the early adopters show any anticipatory or immediate effects before the pandemic begins. Another helpful exercise would be interacting post-2020 indicators with the healthcare/race cells to test whether treated states experienced differential pandemic shocks not captured by the fixed effects.

The paper would also benefit from a more careful discussion of composition. QWI earnings for race-by-industry cells may move because of changes in who is employed, not because the same workers earn more. This is especially salient in 2020–2022. For example, if low-wage Black healthcare workers disproportionately exit or enter employment, average earnings can change mechanically. Because the outcome is based on stable workers, compositional dynamics may still be important. I strongly recommend adding employment as a companion outcome, separately by race and industry. If ULR affects extensive-margin employment or alters the composition of stable workers, the earnings result should be interpreted differently. A null on earnings alongside no employment response would support the paper’s story more than earnings alone.

The data section should be expanded substantially. The authors should name the exact QWI variables used, explain whether they are using seasonally adjusted or not, document how monthly earnings are constructed from quarterly values, and clarify why 128 cells are missing or zero. Are missing cells concentrated among Black workers in small states such as Idaho, Montana, and Utah? If so, that matters for both weighting and external validity. A simple appendix table showing missingness by race, industry, and treatment status would help. So would reporting the employment-weight distribution, since the weighted estimates may be driven by a few large states.

I also think inference should be handled a bit more cautiously. State-clustered standard errors with 51 clusters are usually acceptable, and the wild-cluster bootstrap is reassuring. But because treatment is staggered across only 11 treated states, it would be useful to report randomization-inference or permutation-style p-values based on placebo adoption assignments, especially for the event-study patterns. That would fit the short-paper format well and address concerns that nominal standard errors understate uncertainty in this policy setting.

On the Callaway-Sant’Anna implementation, more detail is needed. The paper says it first constructs the DDD gap and then applies a staggered DiD estimator. That is a reasonable approach, but the resulting ATT is not directly comparable to the regression coefficient unless the aggregation and weighting are spelled out. Please clarify what the observation is in that stage, whether the gap is weighted by underlying employment, and how missing race-industry cells are handled when forming the differenced outcome. I would also report the event-study graph, not just a sentence about the dynamic ATT. Right now the paper has two dynamic stories that point in somewhat different directions: the regression event study drifts negative, while the Callaway-Sant’Anna dynamics are described as small positive effects. That discrepancy needs explanation.

Finally, the paper should moderate some rhetorical overreach. Statements like “the binding constraint channel does not hold empirically” and “the null is definitive” are stronger than warranted. With short post-treatment windows, sector-level rather than occupation-level outcomes, and a comparator sector facing different shocks, the evidence is suggestive of no short-run average effect, not dispositive against the mechanism. Tightening the claims would improve credibility.

In short, I think this is a promising question with an appealing design skeleton, but the current draft is not yet a convincing causal paper for AER: Insights format. The path forward is clear: better align the sample with licensed workers, strengthen the cross-sector identification argument, and document the QWI outcome construction much more carefully. If those issues are addressed, the paper could become a useful contribution—even if the main result remains null.
