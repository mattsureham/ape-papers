**SUBJECT:** Strategic Positioning – "License to Earn? Universal Licensing Recognition and the Black-White Healthcare Wage Gap"

---

### 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
This paper asks whether removing interstate "red tape" for professional licenses can reduce racial inequality. Specifically, it tests if Universal Licensing Recognition (ULR) helps close the 33% Black-White wage gap in healthcare by facilitating mobility toward higher-wage states. It finds a "precise null": making licenses portable does absolutely nothing for the racial earnings gap in the short run.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this well in the first two paragraphs, moving from a specific motivating example (a nurse in Alabama) to the policy lever and the finding.
*   **The Pitch it Needs:** It should emphasize the "Supply-Side" vs. "Demand-Side" tension more aggressively. The pitch should be: "Economists often argue that supply-side barriers like licensing disproportionately harm minorities. By evaluating the largest liberalization of licensing in recent history, I show that these barriers are not the binding constraint on racial wage gaps, suggesting that policy focus should return to within-market discrimination and occupational sorting."

### 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**Contribution:** This paper provides the first causal evidence that universal licensing portability fails to narrow racial earnings disparities in the most heavily licensed sector of the U.S. economy.

*   **Differentiation:** It moves beyond the "average effects" of Johnson and Kleiner (2020) and Thornton and Timmons (2023) to look at distribution. It differs from Blair and Chung (2020) by looking at the *removal* of barriers rather than the *existence* of them.
*   **Question vs. Literature:** It is framed as answering a question about the world (how to fix the wage gap), but it leans heavily on "filling a gap" in the ULR literature.
*   **"Another DiD paper?":** To a cynic, it looks like "another DiD/DDD paper." To a supporter, it is a "critically informative null."
*   **Making it bigger:** To be a "big" AER paper, it needs to prove *why* it's a null. Is it because Black workers don't move (mobility data)? Or because when they move, the gap follows them (within-firm discrimination)? Adding a second outcome variable—**interstate migration flows by race**—would transform this from a wage paper into a definitive paper on labor market integration.

### 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
*   **Neighbors:** Johnson & Kleiner (2020) on ULR; Blair & Chung (2020) on licensing/race; Chetty et al. on geographic mobility and race.
*   **Strategy:** It currently "adds to the body of work." It should instead **challenge the optimism** of the supply-side school. It should position itself as a reality check on the idea that "deregulation is a tool for equity."
*   **Missing Conversations:** It needs to speak to the **Internal Migration** literature (e.g., Ganong and Shoag). If ULR doesn't close gaps, is it because housing prices or social capital matter more than licenses?

### 4. NARRATIVE ARC
*   **Setup:** Licensing is a massive, race-blind barrier that should theoretically hurt minorities most.
*   **Tension:** 11 states just removed this barrier. Did a "rising tide lift Black boats" more than White ones?
*   **Resolution:** No. The gap is stubborn.
*   **Implications:** Licensing is a red herring for racial equity.
*   **Evaluation:** The arc is clean but lacks "teeth." The paper feels a bit too "I ran the regression and here is the zero." It needs to build the "tension" by citing the high hopes policymakers had for these laws.

### 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
*   **Dinner Party Fact:** "We spent years lobbying for license portability to help minority workers move to better jobs, but when states finally did it, the racial wage gap didn't budge by even half a percent."
*   **Reaction:** Lean in.
*   **Follow-up:** "Did they actually move, or did they just stay in Alabama anyway?" (This is why the paper needs migration data).

### 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
*   **Front-loading:** The "Arizona-only" pre-COVID result is actually your most interesting "stress test." Move it closer to the main results to preempt the "it's just COVID noise" objection.
*   **Appendix:** The "Standardized Effect Sizes" (Table 5) is great for *AER: Insights* style clarity—keep it prominent.
*   **Conclusion:** It is currently a summary. It should be a "Policy Path Forward." If not this, then what?

### 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The distance is **Medium**. To move from "solid labor paper" to "AER lead article," the author must solve the **Mechanism Mystery**.

**The single most impactful piece of advice:**
Supplement the earnings data with **migration data** (e.g., from the ACS or CPS) to show whether the null is due to a "mobility failure" (Black workers didn't move) or a "matching failure" (they moved but didn't get higher pay). An AER reader wants to know *how* the market failed to equilibrate.
