**SUBJECT:** Strategic Positioning: "Last Orders for Crime? Cumulative Impact Assessments and Alcohol-Related Violence in England"

---

### 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
The paper examines whether giving "legal teeth" to alcohol licensing restrictions reduces crime by exploiting a 2018 UK reform that turned advisory "Cumulative Impact Assessments" into a statutory rebuttable presumption against new licenses. It asks if shifting the burden of proof from regulators to applicants in high-saturation zones effectively curtails the negative externalities of the nighttime economy. This matters because alcohol-related violence accounts for nearly half of violent crime in England, yet we know little about the efficacy of supply-side "surgical" zoning vs. blunt population-wide taxes or bans.

**Evaluation:** The paper does *not* articulate this pitch clearly. The current abstract and introduction immediately dive into the institutional weeds (CIAs, statutory strengthening) without first establishing the broader economic tension: the trade-off between the "right to trade" and localized social costs. 

**The pitch the paper should have:**
"Does the legal burden of proof in licensing decisions determine the level of street violence in urban centers? While economists have long studied alcohol taxes and age limits, we lack evidence on whether 'surgical' zoning—which restricts entry only in saturated zones—effectively reduces crime. This paper exploits a 2018 UK reform that elevated localized licensing restrictions from advisory guidance to a statutory presumption of refusal, finding that while the legal architecture shifted, the impact on violent crime was surprisingly modest, suggesting that supply-side constraints may be inframarginal in high-density environments."

---

### 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**Contribution:** The paper provides the first causal evaluation of localized "rebuttable presumption" licensing regimes on crime, demonstrating that strengthening the legal standing of zoning restrictions has a negligible-to-modest impact on realized violence.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It is well-differentiated from the "blunt instrument" literature (Carpenter & Dobkin) and the "dry law" literature (Biderman et al.) by focusing on the *administrative burden* of entry rather than a total ban or price change.
*   **Framing:** Currently, it is framed too much as "filling a gap in the UK licensing literature." It needs to be framed as an inquiry into the **micro-foundations of place-based regulation**: why does changing the legal default of an amenity restriction fail to move the needle on externalities?
*   **Clarity:** A smart economist would likely say, "It’s a DiD on a UK licensing tweak." To make it an AER paper, they should be able to say, "It’s a study on why the 'legal architecture' of regulation matters less than the physical stock of existing externalities."
*   **Making it bigger:** The paper needs to move from "Did crime go down?" to "Why didn't it go down more?" It needs data on **actual license applications and refusals** to show the mechanism (or lack thereof). If applications didn't drop, the "rebuttable presumption" was a paper tiger.

---

### 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
*   **Closest Neighbors:** *Biderman et al. (2010)* on Brazilian dry laws; *Chaifin & McCrary (2018)* on policing and crime; *Lovenheim & Slemrod (2010)* on liquor privatization.
*   **Positioning:** It should stop "contributing to three literatures" (the standard junior-paper trope) and instead **synthesize** them. It should position itself as the bridge between "Urban/Place-based Policy" and "Regulatory Economics."
*   **Missing Conversations:** The paper is silent on the **Industrial Organization of the Nighttime Economy**. If I restrict new pubs, do the existing ones just get more crowded and violent? It needs to speak to the "Internalization of Externalities" literature (Coasean bargaining in licensing hearings).

---

### 4. NARRATIVE ARC
*   **Setup:** Cities are saturated with booze; violence is high. 
*   **Tension:** Regulators try to stop new entries, but they have no legal "teeth" to win appeals against big corporate pub chains.
*   **Resolution:** A 2018 law gives them the teeth (the rebuttable presumption).
*   **Implications:** Surprisingly little changes. 

**Evaluation:** The narrative arc is "serviceable" but lacks the "Aha!" moment. The resolution (modest/null effects) feels like a "failed policy" story. To make it a "great paper" story, the author needs to explain *why*—is it because of geographic displacement to the next street over? Or because the "stock" of existing pubs is so high that the "flow" of new entrants is irrelevant?

---

### 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
*   **The Lead Fact:** "When the UK made it legally 'guaranteed' that new pubs would be blocked in high-crime zones, crime didn't actually budge."
*   **The Reaction:** People might reach for their phones unless the author can explain the **political economy angle**: Why did the government pass a law that did nothing? Was it "regulatory theater"?
*   **Follow-up Question:** "Did the pub owners just move one block outside the CIA zone?" (The paper currently can't answer this—this is a major weakness for AER).

---

### 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
*   **Front-load the Mechanism:** We need to see if the law actually stopped pubs from opening. If the N of new licenses didn't change, the crime result is trivial.
*   **The "June Snapshot" Problem:** The author uses only June data to avoid "reporting anomalies." For the AER, we need the full 12-month panel. Using 1/12th of the data looks like "p-hacking" or laziness, even if justified by "summer peaks."
*   **Appendix:** Move the "Two-way fixed effects" methodology discussion to the appendix. In 2024, we assume you know how to do a robust DiD; don't spend three paragraphs on it in the Intro.

---

### 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The gap is **Ambition and Scope**. Currently, this is a very competent "Applied Micro" paper that belongs in *Journal of Policy Analysis and Management* or *Journal of Quantitative Criminology*. 

**To make it AER:** The author must move beyond the "Reduced Form" crime count. They need to show the **General Equilibrium** of the street. If I block a new bar on Street A, does Street B get worse? Does the price of a pint on Street A go up (Market Power)? 
**The single most impactful advice:** Acquire the "Local Authority" level data on **license applications and appeals** to prove that the "statutory strengthening" actually changed the behavior of the courts and developers. Without the "supply" side of the supply-side regulation, you only have half a paper.
