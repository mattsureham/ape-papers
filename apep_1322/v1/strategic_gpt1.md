## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a timely and important question: when states abolish single-family-only zoning, does “legalizing” duplexes through fourplexes actually change what gets built? Using four recent state reforms, the paper’s headline claim is that most such preemption laws have had little immediate effect on missing-middle construction, except in Oregon, where stronger state enforcement appears to matter.

A busy economist should care because the housing debate has moved from “is zoning restrictive?” to “which reforms actually bite?” If the answer is that legal reform without implementation does little, that is potentially a useful lesson for housing policy, political economy, and state-local governance.

### Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Not quite. The introduction is competent and readable, but it takes too long to get to the actual intellectual hook. The first two paragraphs emphasize that this is “the first multi-state quasi-experimental evaluation,” which is a literature claim, not the real reason to care. The actual hook is stronger: the paper speaks to whether the current flagship housing reform strategy—state preemption of single-family zoning—changes supply in practice, and whether implementation design determines whether legal change turns into construction.

### What the first two paragraphs should say instead

Something like:

> Across the United States, states are increasingly overriding local single-family zoning in the hope that legalizing duplexes, triplexes, and fourplexes will expand housing supply. But the central policy question is no longer whether single-family zoning is restrictive in theory; it is whether state preemption laws actually translate into new housing on the ground.
>
> This paper studies four of the first major state reforms—Oregon, California, Maine, and Montana—and finds that legalization alone usually does not produce a construction response. The exception is Oregon, where the state paired legalization with deadlines, model codes, and enforcement. The broader lesson is that in housing, implementation design may matter as much as formal deregulation.

That is the pitch. It leads with the world question, the finding, and the reason it matters.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper argues that state preemption of single-family zoning generally does not increase missing-middle construction unless the state also compels and enforces local implementation, as Oregon did.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest papers?

Only partially.

The paper does distinguish itself from local upzoning papers and from broad work on housing regulation, but the differentiation is still a bit mechanical: “first multi-state quasi-experimental evaluation,” “state-level preemption rather than local reform,” etc. That is useful, but not enough. What needs to be clearer is the conceptual distinction:

- prior work asks whether zoning constraints matter;
- some prior work asks whether local upzoning changes construction;
- this paper asks whether **state override of local zoning** works, and whether **implementation architecture** determines whether legal reform is real or symbolic.

That is a stronger, more memorable distinction.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a gap in a literature?

Right now it is mixed, with too much weight on filling a literature gap. The stronger version is unquestionably a world question:

- When higher levels of government try to force housing liberalization, does anything happen?
- Is “legalization” enough, or do local governments neutralize reform unless the state also controls implementation?

That is an important world question. The paper should lean harder into it.

### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what’s new?

At present, maybe, but not confidently. They might say: “It’s a DiD on state zoning preemption with mostly null effects and one positive case in Oregon.” That is accurate but not yet distinctive.

The goal should be for them to say: “It shows that state zoning reform is often symbolic unless the state also forces local code changes and enforces them—Oregon worked, the others didn’t.” That is a much sharper contribution.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Most importantly, the paper needs to make the outcome more consequential. Right now the main outcome is the share of permits in 2–4 unit structures. That is natural for this question, but it risks sounding niche: a composition shift in a narrow building category over a short horizon.

Ways to make it bigger:

1. **Connect missing-middle permits to total housing supply more directly.**  
   Even if the main outcome remains missing-middle share, the paper should frame clearly whether this is about meaningful net supply or merely compositional reshuffling.

2. **Push harder on policy design as the object of study.**  
   The paper is potentially about the economics of implementation, not just zoning. If it can more systematically classify reforms by whether they mandate code rewriting, constrain local discretion, provide defaults, and include enforcement, the contribution becomes broader than “one state worked.”

3. **Tie to state capacity / local circumvention / regulatory substitution.**  
   The most general lesson is not just about housing. It is about whether higher-level deregulation survives lower-level administrative resistance. That could be interesting far beyond urban economics.

4. **Show why Oregon is not just a one-off anecdote.**  
   Right now the paper risks reading as pooled null + Oregon exception. To make the contribution bigger, the Oregon result has to stand for a general proposition about implementation, not just a state case study.

The more impactful conversation may be:

- **Why do nominal deregulations often fail to produce real responses?**

That is a bigger and more interesting conversation. Housing is the application; implementation and regulatory substitution are the themes.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

States are increasingly preempting local single-family zoning because local politics block reform and economists widely view zoning as a housing-supply constraint.

### Tension

The puzzle is that changing the law may not change what gets built. States can legalize new housing types on paper, but local governments may still choke off projects through process, standards, or cost. So does preemption actually work?

### Resolution

In the short run, most of these state reforms did not measurably increase missing-middle construction; Oregon did, and appears to have done so because it combined legalization with mandated code changes and enforcement.

### Implications

The policy lesson is that legalization is not enough. The design of implementation—deadlines, defaults, templates, and enforcement—may determine whether preemption changes supply. More broadly, regulatory reform can fail if governments leave room for regulated actors or subordinate governments to substitute into other constraints.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the ingredients, but the arc is not fully disciplined. At times it reads like:

- pooled null result,
- then heterogeneity,
- then institutional discussion,
- then a broader “implementation gap” claim.

The actual story should be cleaner:

1. There is a major national policy movement around state zoning preemption.  
2. The key question is whether these laws alter construction, not just legality.  
3. Across four early reforms, the average answer is no.  
4. But Oregon shows that reform can matter when the state controls implementation.  
5. Therefore, the right object of analysis is not just deregulation, but the administrative design of deregulation.

That is the paper’s story. It should tell that story relentlessly.

At present, the paper sometimes feels like a collection of sensible results looking for a bigger frame. The frame is there, but it needs to dominate every section.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

This is not primarily an econometrics problem. It is a **framing and ambition** problem, with some scope issues.

### What is the gap between current form and an AER paper?

#### 1. Framing problem
The paper is better than its current framing. Its real idea is not “first multi-state DiD of zoning preemption.” Its real idea is “state deregulation often fails because local implementation margins undo it.” That is much more interesting.

#### 2. Scope problem
The empirical object is somewhat narrow: missing-middle permit shares over a very short post period. For AER, the paper needs to convince the reader that this narrow window reveals a broader mechanism of first-order importance. Right now it hints at that, but does not fully earn it.

#### 3. Novelty problem
The general intuition that deregulation may not bite because of other constraints is not new. What is potentially new is showing it in this very current and policy-relevant setting, with cross-state variation in implementation design. The paper needs to lean into that specific novelty.

#### 4. Ambition problem
The paper is competent but safe. It presents a sensible policy evaluation. AER needs something more: either a broader conceptual contribution, or evidence that reshapes how the field thinks about state-local housing reform.

### What is the single most impactful piece of advice?

**Reframe the paper around implementation design as the object of study, not zoning preemption per se.**  

In other words: do not sell this as “we estimate the average effect of four state reforms.” Sell it as “the first generation of state housing preemption shows that legal deregulation without administrative control is mostly symbolic; only prescriptive, enforceable reform changes outcomes.” If the authors can make that proposition feel general rather than Oregon-specific, the paper gets much closer to belonging in AER.
