**RE:** Strategic Positioning of "Upzoned but Unbuilt? State Single-Family Zoning Preemption and the Missing Middle Gap"

---

### 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
The paper examines whether state-level laws that override local single-family zoning actually lead to the construction of "missing middle" housing (duplexes/fourplexes). Using a multi-state difference-in-differences approach, it finds that "legalization" is generally a failure—except in Oregon, where the state didn't just give permission but mandated local code rewrites and provided enforcement. 

**Evaluation:** The paper does a decent job in the first two paragraphs, but it leans too heavily on the "first multi-state evaluation" trope. It should pivot from "testing a law" to "testing the efficacy of regulatory federalism." 

**The Pitch the Paper Should Have:** 
"Can state intervention solve the local political economy trap of restrictive zoning? We evaluate the first wave of state-level 'missing middle' preemptions and find a stark 'implementation gap': simply legalizing density results in zero new housing unless the state also strips local governments of the administrative tools used to obstruct development. Our findings suggest that in the face of local resistance, 'permissive' deregulation is a dead letter, and only 'prescriptive' state-mandated implementation generates a supply response."

---

### 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**One-sentence contribution:** The paper identifies that the success of housing deregulation depends less on the removal of a specific zoning prohibition and more on the state’s ability to prevent local governments from substituting new administrative barriers (the "implementation gap").

*   **Differentiation:** It moves past the "does zoning matter" literature (Glaeser/Gyourko) and the "local upzoning" literature (Manville/Monkkonen) by looking at the *inter-governmental* friction of state preemption.
*   **Question vs. Literature:** The current draft is 50/50. It needs to be 100% about the WORLD: Why doesn't housing get built even after it's legal?
*   **What would make it bigger?** The "So What" would be much bigger if the author could quantify the *cost* of the administrative substitutes. If Oregon worked, was it because of the "model code" (lower transaction costs for developers) or the "enforcement" (higher cost for obstructionist cities)? Disentangling these mechanisms would turn this from an evaluation into a major theory of regulatory design.

---

### 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
The paper currently sits in Urban Economics (R31). It should be fighting for space in **Political Economy** and **Public Economics**.

*   **Neighbors:** Hsieh and Moretti (2019) on the macro effects of housing supply; Magliocca (2021) or similar on local zoning; and the burgeoning literature on "State vs. Local" power.
*   **Strategy:** The paper should **synthesize** the housing supply literature with the literature on **regulatory capture**. It needs to speak to the "federalism" crowd—showing that preemption is not a magic wand. 
*   **The Right Conversation:** The paper is currently a "housing paper." It should be a "State Capacity" paper. Why do some states have the capacity to force local change while others (like California, arguably a much larger economy) fail?

---

### 4. NARRATIVE ARC
*   **Setup:** Everyone knows single-family zoning is the "final boss" of housing affordability. States have finally started fighting it.
*   **Tension:** In three out of four states, the "victory" resulted in exactly zero houses being built.
*   **Resolution:** Oregon is the outlier because it closed the "implementation gap" via prescriptive mandates and state-level enforcement.
*   **Implications:** Deregulation is an administrative task, not just a legislative one.

**Evaluation:** The narrative arc is strong but the "Resolution" is currently too descriptive. It reads like a list of Oregon's law features. It needs to be framed as a generalizable model of "Contracting under local resistance."

---

### 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
*   **The Dinner Party Fact:** "California legalized fourplexes statewide and the number of new permits barely budged." 
*   **The Reaction:** People lean in—especially in the current political climate—but the follow-up is: "Was it just because interest rates spiked?" The paper addresses this, but it needs to be the centerpiece of the robustness section.
*   **The Null:** The null is the star of the show here. A "success" in Oregon is interesting, but a "failure" in California is a headline.

---

### 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
*   **Front-load the Oregon vs. California comparison.** Don't wait for Section 6. The paper is currently structured as "Pooled Result (Null) -> Heterogeneity (Oregon)." It should be structured as "The Oregon/California Natural Experiment."
*   **Move Montana to the Appendix.** The author admits Montana has pre-trend issues and mean reversion. It muddies the water. Focus on the high-quality contrast between OR and CA.
*   **Conclusion:** The conclusion is currently too much summary. It should spend more time on the "unprofitable vs. illegal" point raised at the very end. That is a profound AER-level question.

---

### 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The distance is **Medium**. To close the gap, the paper needs to move from "Policy Evaluation" to "Mechanism Identification."

**The Single Most Impactful Advice:** 
Shift the focus from "state-level results" to a "theory of administrative substitution." Use the variation in local "objective design standards" (mentioned on p. 10) to show *how* cities in California killed the law that Oregon's state capacity prevented them from killing. If you can show that cities with the most restrictive "setback" requirements post-SB9 saw the lowest permits, you've proven the mechanism of the "implementation gap."
