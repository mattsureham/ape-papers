### 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
This paper asks a fundamental distributional question: when the minimum wage rises, does the resulting "hiring squeeze" disproportionately exclude Black workers, or does wage compression actually "level the playing field" by making discrimination more expensive? Using a high-powered administrative panel (QWI), the author finds that while total hiring falls, the Black-White hiring gap narrows significantly in high-impact counties. This suggests that minimum wages may unintentionally serve as a tool for reducing taste-based discrimination.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this remarkably well. The opening line—"The minimum wage debate has produced a remarkably precise answer to the wrong question"—is classic AER-style framing. It immediately pivots from the tired "employment effect" debate to a more nuanced "compositional" debate.

---

### 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**Contribution:** The paper identifies a "compositional hiring squeeze" where minimum wages narrow racial hiring gaps by reducing the margin for taste-based discrimination.

*   **Differentiation:** It moves beyond the imprecise CPS-based estimates of Cengiz et al. (2019) and Neumark et al. (2014) by using administrative QWI data, and it provides a theoretical bridge between the minimum wage literature and the Becker (1957) discrimination model.
*   **Framing:** It is framed as a question about the **world** (Who gets hired when the price of labor rises?), which is high-impact.
*   **Specific Improvement:** To make the contribution "bigger," the author should look at **retention**. If the "hiring squeeze" brings in more minorities, but they are fired faster (or "last hired, first fired" in subsequent downturns), the equity case is weakened. A section on the durability of these hires would be a knockout blow.

---

### 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
The paper sits at the intersection of **Labor (Minimum Wage)** and **Public/Labor (Discrimination)**.

*   **Closest Neighbors:** Cengiz et al. (2019) on low-wage effects; Bertrand & Mullainathan (2004) on discrimination; and Cullen & Pakzad-Hurson (2023) on pay transparency.
*   **Positioning:** It builds on the "near-zero average effect" consensus to justify looking at second-order distributional effects. It synthesizes the Becker (1957) taste-based model with modern DiD/DDD methods.
*   **Missing Conversations:** The paper could better engage with the **Search and Matching** literature. Is the narrowing gap due to employer behavior (taste) or worker search behavior (reservation wages)?

---

### 4. NARRATIVE ARC
*   **Setup:** Decades of research show small average employment effects of the minimum wage.
*   **Tension:** If average effects are small, we might assume nothing is happening, but price floors should logically change *who* gets hired if discrimination is present.
*   **Resolution:** Administrative data shows a narrowing racial gap precisely where the wage floor "bites" hardest.
*   **Implications:** The "equity case" for the minimum wage doesn't depend on job creation; it can be justified as an anti-discrimination mechanism.

**Evaluation:** The narrative arc is very strong. It’s not just "I have a new dataset"; it’s "I have a puzzle and a mechanism."

---

### 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
*   **The Lead Fact:** "Raising the minimum wage closes 20% of the racial hiring gap in high-impact counties."
*   **Response:** This is a "lean-in" fact. It challenges the common conservative critique that minimum wages hurt minorities (the "disemployment of the least skilled" argument).
*   **Follow-up:** "Does this happen because Black workers are more productive than we thought, or because White workers stop applying for these jobs?" (The supply-side question).

---

### 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
*   **Front-loading:** The paper is well front-loaded. The "Compositional Hiring Squeeze" label is a sticky, effective term.
*   **Appendix:** The Callaway-Sant’Anna event studies (Table 2) are necessary for the "science," but the DDD (Table 3) is the "story." I would suggest moving more of the robustness checks to an appendix to keep the focus on the mechanism.
*   **Conclusion:** The conclusion is strong but could be bolder about the trade-off. If hiring *overall* falls (Column 1), but the gap narrows, we are effectively trading total volume for racial equity. That is a policy-relevant trade-off that deserves more "meat."

---

### 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The gap between this and a "Safe Reject" is **Mechanism**. Currently, it's a very high-quality reduced-form result. To be an AER paper, the author needs to work harder to rule out the **Statistical Discrimination** story (Arrow/Phelps). If the minimum wage makes hiring "higher stakes," does it lead to *more* screening? The paper touches on this but doesn't prove it's "Taste-Based" vs. "Statistical."

**Single Most Impactful Advice:** Add an analysis of **job requirements** (using Job Openings data or similar) to see if employers increase credential requirements (e.g., "high school diploma required") when the minimum wage rises. If they *don't* increase requirements but still hire more Black workers, the Becker "taste-based" story becomes undeniable.
