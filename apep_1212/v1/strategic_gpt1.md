## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether the COVID-era anti-Asian backlash changed where Asian Americans worked, pushing them out of customer-facing sectors like retail and hospitality and into less public-facing sectors like information and professional services. A busy economist should care because the paper is trying to make a broader point: discrimination may not just lower wages or employment; it may re-sort workers across sectors in persistent ways.

Does the paper articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs? Not quite. The opening is vivid, but the paper overcommits too quickly to a discrimination story that the design, as described, does not cleanly isolate. The paper’s actual contribution is more defensible and more interesting if framed as documenting a distinctive pandemic-era sectoral reallocation of Asian American workers consistent with discrimination/customer hostility, rather than as having already proven that anti-Asian hate caused it.

### The pitch the paper should have

“In the early months of COVID, Asian Americans faced both a public-health shock and a sharp rise in anti-Asian hostility, especially in public-facing settings. This paper asks whether those forces changed not just Asian Americans’ employment levels, but the sectors in which they worked. Using administrative employment data by race, sector, state, and quarter, I show that Asian workers moved disproportionately out of customer-facing industries and into knowledge-sector jobs after 2020, with effects that persisted for years. The broader implication is that social hostility can reshape labor-market sorting, not just wages or unemployment.”

That is the paper’s best version. It is accurate, broad enough to matter, and does not promise more than the evidence can deliver.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper documents that, after the onset of COVID, Asian American workers shifted disproportionately out of customer-facing sectors and into knowledge-economy sectors, suggesting that discrimination and related pandemic-era forces can operate through persistent sectoral sorting.

### Evaluation

**Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest papers?**  
Only partially. Right now the paper claims several things at once: first causal evidence on labor-market consequences of anti-Asian discrimination; evidence on customer discrimination; evidence on labor-market adjustment; a novel DDD application. That is too many contribution claims, and several are weaker than advertised. The contribution that feels most real is not “novel DDD” and not “causal evidence of anti-Asian discrimination” per se. It is: **persistent sectoral reallocation of a racial group during a salient episode of social hostility**. That needs sharper differentiation from adjacent work on hate incidents, COVID labor shocks, and occupational sorting.

**Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a literature gap?**  
It starts with a world question, which is good. But by the end of the introduction it slides into literature bookkeeping. The stronger framing is clearly the world question: *What happens to minority workers’ sectoral allocation when a large, visible discrimination shock hits public-facing interactions?* That is much better than “there is no paper using DDD on QWI race-by-sector data.”

**Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?**  
Not cleanly. They might say, “It’s a triple-difference paper on Asian employment during COVID.” That is not enough. The paper needs the novelty to be memorable: not employment losses, but **re-sorting across sectors**; not transitory disruption, but **persistent occupational redistribution**; not just “anti-Asian hate matters,” but “social hostility changes labor-market allocation across sectors.”

**What would make this contribution bigger?**  
Three possibilities:

1. **Move from a two-bin sector comparison to a richer reallocation map.**  
   Right now “customer-facing” vs. “knowledge” is a bit schematic, and the exact mirror-image result is partly mechanical in a two-sector setup. A more compelling paper would show where workers actually went across a wider set of sectors, ideally ordered by customer contact, remote feasibility, or task content. That would make “sorting” feel real rather than imposed by design.

2. **Connect to a broader economic margin than employment counts.**  
   The headline would be bigger if the paper could show consequences for occupation quality, career ladders, firm types, entrepreneurship, or geographic mobility. “They moved sectors” is interesting; “they were pushed into better/worse long-run trajectories” is much bigger.

3. **Lean into the general mechanism.**  
   The paper should be about how prejudice in public-facing interactions reshapes labor allocation. If the authors could tie sector shifts to customer exposure, remote work feasibility, or local co-ethnic business networks more systematically, the paper would feel less like one episode and more like a general economic mechanism.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

### Closest neighbors

The paper seems to sit at the intersection of several literatures:

1. **Customer/employer discrimination**
   - Becker (1957)
   - Holzer and Ihlanfeldt (1998) on customer discrimination / minority employment in customer-contact jobs
   - Charles and Guryan (2008) on prejudice and labor market outcomes

2. **COVID labor-market disruption and heterogeneous impacts**
   - Mongey, Pilossoph, and Weinberg (2021) on social distancing and labor demand/task exposure
   - Cortes and Forsythe (2020) on heterogeneous labor-market impacts during COVID
   - Related work on remote work and sectoral reshuffling during the pandemic

3. **Anti-Asian backlash / hate incidents during COVID**
   - Gover, Harper, and Langton (2020)
   - Stop AAPI Hate reports / Jeung et al.
   - Han et al. and adjacent sociology/public health papers on anti-Asian hostility

4. **Networks and ethnic enclaves**
   - Munshi (2003)
   - Beaman (2012)
   - Broader immigrant-network and co-ethnic business literature

### How should it position itself?

Primarily **build on** the discrimination and COVID reallocation literatures, while using the anti-Asian backlash as the motivating shock. It should not “attack” the hate-incidents literature; that literature is measuring different outcomes. It should say: those papers documented hostility; this paper asks whether labor markets were re-sorted in response.

The paper should also be more modest relative to the discrimination literature. It does not really identify pure discrimination in the Becker/Charles-Guryan sense. It identifies a differential pandemic-era shock for Asian workers in public-facing sectors, plausibly including discrimination. That distinction matters for credibility and positioning.

### Is it positioned too narrowly or too broadly?

Oddly, both.

- **Too narrowly** in the sense that it uses a specific episode and a specific group with a somewhat bespoke sector split.
- **Too broadly** in the claims: “first causal evidence,” “reveals discrimination as sorting mechanism,” “labor market phenomenon obscured by public health framing.”

The right level is: a focused empirical episode with broader implications for discrimination and labor reallocation.

### What literature does it seem unaware of?

It needs deeper engagement with:
- the **COVID sectoral/remote-work/task-content** literature,
- the literature on **occupational segregation and sorting**,
- possibly work on **ethnic enclaves and minority-owned firms**,
- and perhaps **racialized consumer demand / customer prejudice** outside standard labor economics.

Right now it cites some canonical discrimination papers, but the conversation it should be having is really about **how social shocks interact with job task structure and customer exposure**.

### Is it having the right conversation?

Not yet. The current conversation is “anti-Asian hate had labor market effects.” That is a decent start, but still a bit niche and socially obvious. The more impactful conversation is: **when discrimination rises in public interactions, labor markets reallocate minority workers across sectors; this is an underappreciated margin of adjustment.** That is a stronger economics conversation.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Before this paper, we knew COVID was associated with a surge in anti-Asian hostility, and we knew the pandemic disrupted labor demand very differently across sectors. We knew far less about whether these forces changed the sectoral allocation of Asian American workers.

### Tension
If Asian workers disproportionately left customer-facing sectors after 2020, was that just generic pandemic sector collapse, or did the combination of social hostility and public exposure differentially push them into other parts of the labor market? More broadly, does discrimination alter sorting, not just pay or employment levels?

### Resolution
The paper finds that Asian employment fell relatively more in customer-facing sectors and rose relatively more in knowledge sectors, with persistence through the sample period and attenuation in states with larger Asian populations.

### Implications
The intended implication is that discrimination can operate through sectoral sorting and have lasting effects on occupational distributions.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the ingredients of one, but the current draft is not fully in control of its own story. The main problem is that the paper’s **most persuasive story** and its **strongest claims** are misaligned.

- The persuasive story: there was a pandemic-era shock that differentially affected Asian workers in public-facing jobs, leading to persistent reallocation.
- The strongest claims: this is causal evidence of anti-Asian discrimination and sectoral sorting caused by hate.

Because the paper itself admits it captures a bundle of forces, the discrimination story feels asserted more than demonstrated. That creates narrative slippage.

### What story should it be telling?

It should tell a disciplined story about **public-facing exposure under social hostility**.

A cleaner narrative arc would be:

1. COVID created unequal sectoral disruption.
2. For Asian Americans, the shock was compounded by a sharp rise in public hostility.
3. Therefore the key margin to study is not only whether they lost jobs, but whether they moved away from high-contact sectors.
4. Administrative data show they did, and the shift persisted.
5. This suggests a broader mechanism: labor markets respond to prejudice via re-sorting.

That is much stronger than trying to prove more channel-specific discrimination than the design can bear.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?

“After 2020, Asian American employment shifted sharply out of customer-facing sectors and into less public-facing sectors, and that pattern persisted for years.”

That is the dinner-party line.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

They would lean in initially, because the episode is salient and the result is intuitive but not trivial. But the next question would come quickly.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

“Is this really discrimination, or just differential COVID exposure, remote-work opportunities, immigration/composition effects, or pre-existing occupational trends?”

That is the central issue for the paper’s positioning. Again, not a referee-style identification objection, but a strategic one: if the first question every reader asks is “does this isolate anti-Asian hostility at all?”, then the framing must preempt that by being more precise.

### If findings are modest or null

Not applicable here; the results are not null. But note that the most eye-catching “symmetric reallocation” result is undercut by the paper’s own admission that the symmetry is partly mechanical in a two-sector design. So the paper should not oversell that as if it were a surprising empirical discovery.

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

### What would make it read better?

1. **Shorten the institutional background substantially.**  
   It currently reads like scaffolding for a paper that wants to sound more grounded than it is. Most of this can be compressed into a page. The reader does not need three mini-essays before getting to the core empirical idea.

2. **Front-load the conceptual contribution, not the method.**  
   The paper should tell me on page 1 that the object of interest is sectoral sorting under social hostility. The DDD design should appear after that, as a tool, not as part of the headline.

3. **Move “novel DDD application” language out of the introduction or kill it.**  
   That is not a top-journal contribution in itself and cheapens the framing.

4. **Bring the limitation much earlier.**  
   The paper should acknowledge in the introduction—not buried in empirical strategy/discussion—that it identifies a composite pandemic-era differential consistent with discrimination. Readers will trust the paper more if it is candid upfront.

5. **Reorganize the results around the economic question.**  
   Suggested flow:
   - Main fact: Asian workers left customer-facing sectors relative to comparison groups.
   - Dynamics: break at COVID onset; persistence.
   - Reallocation: where did they go?
   - Heterogeneity: states with larger Asian populations.
   - Then supporting outcomes like hires/separations/earnings.

6. **Do not bury the fact that the placebo is significant.**  
   That is strategically damaging if left in robustness with a dismissive gloss. It belongs in the main discussion of what the paper can and cannot claim. Not because of technical concern per se, but because it affects the story’s credibility.

7. **The conclusion should do more than summarize.**  
   Right now it lands on a slogan—“it resorted them”—which is catchy but slightly overdramatic relative to the evidence. The conclusion should instead sharpen the general lesson: discriminatory public environments can reshape labor allocation.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

Honest answer: in its current form, this feels **far from AER**, mainly because of a mismatch between ambition and evidence. The paper has a plausible, timely fact, but the current package is not yet a top-field-defining contribution.

### What is the gap?

Mostly:

- **A framing problem:** the paper overstates what it identifies.
- **A scope problem:** the reallocation result is too constrained by a two-sector comparison.

Less a novelty problem than it first appears. The idea is not dead-on-arrival; it is just not yet elevated enough.

### What would excite the top 10 people in this field?

A paper that showed, convincingly and generally, that racialized social hostility changes labor-market sorting along dimensions of customer contact, remote feasibility, and community protection—and that this matters for medium-run career trajectories, not just sector shares in a four-sector panel.

Right now, the paper is one interesting reduced-form fact wrapped in very strong rhetoric.

### Single most impactful piece of advice

**Reframe the paper around persistent sectoral reallocation under public hostility, and stop claiming to have identified anti-Asian discrimination itself.**

That one change would improve credibility, sharpen the economic contribution, and give the paper a real shot at being taken seriously as more than “another COVID DiD.”
