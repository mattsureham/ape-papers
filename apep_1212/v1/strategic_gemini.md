**SUBJECT:** Strategic Positioning of "Pushed Out: COVID-Era Discrimination and the Sectoral Reallocation of Asian American Workers"

---

## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether the surge in anti-Asian hostility during COVID-19 acted as a persistent labor market shock, forcing Asian Americans out of customer-facing roles and into "knowledge economy" sectors. Using a triple-difference design on administrative data, it finds an 11% relative decline in Asian employment in hospitality/retail that persisted through 2024, suggesting that interpersonal discrimination can trigger large-scale, permanent sectoral reallocation.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this well, but the second paragraph currently focuses heavily on the mechanics of the "three reasons why it matters." It is a bit too clinical. To truly capture an AER audience, the pitch should emphasize the *novelty of the margin*: we usually think of discrimination as a wage tax; this paper argues it is a "sorting tax" that changes the shape of the economy.

**The revised pitch:** 
"While economic models traditionally view discrimination as a wage penalty, the 2020 surge in anti-Asian hostility provides a unique natural experiment to study discrimination as a sectoral sorting mechanism. I find that the increased 'contact tax' in customer-facing industries drove a massive, 11-percent permanent reallocation of Asian American workers into the knowledge economy. These results demonstrate that interpersonal shocks do not just harm individuals—they structurally reshape the occupational distribution of entire demographic groups for years after the initial surge."

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution:** The paper provides the first quasi-experimental evidence that interpersonal discrimination drives persistent sectoral reallocation (sorting) rather than just temporary employment losses or wage gaps.

**Evaluation:**
- **Differentiation:** It differentiates itself from the "hate crime documentation" literature (which is descriptive) and the classical Becker "employer prejudice" literature (by focusing on *customer* and *environment* hostility). 
- **World vs. Literature:** It frames itself as answering a question about the world (COVID-era shocks), which is strong.
- **The "Smart Economist" Test:** A reader would likely say: "It’s a COVID-shocks paper, but specifically about the persistence of anti-Asian sentiment on job sorting."
- **How to make it bigger:** The paper needs more on the *productivity* or *welfare* implications. If workers are "pushed" into the knowledge economy, are they now misallocated (earning less than their potential) or were they "upgraded"? The symmetric reallocation result is provocative but needs more "So What?" on the aggregate economy.

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

- **Closest Neighbors:** Becker (1957) on taste-based discrimination; Holzer and Ihlanfeldt (1998) on customer discrimination; Autor (2014) on labor market polarization; and the emerging COVID-19 labor impacts literature (e.g., Chetty et al.).
- **Strategy:** It should **synthesize** the discrimination literature with the labor reallocation literature. Currently, these feel like two separate citations. It should argue that "shocks to social preferences" belong in the same conversation as "shocks to technology" (Autor) as drivers of structural change.
- **Niche vs. Broad:** It is currently a bit narrow (Asian-specific). It should speak to the broader literature on *environmental hostility* (e.g., how harassment affects women in STEM or minorities in high-status professions).

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

- **Setup:** Asian Americans were integrated into customer-facing sectors (hospitality/retail).
- **Tension:** COVID-19 introduced a sudden, localized "tax" on being Asian in public-facing roles.
- **Resolution:** A sharp, 11% drop in employment that didn't just disappear when the lockdowns ended, but shifted into professional services.
- **Implications:** Discrimination is a structural force that alters the long-run trajectory of labor supply.

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong. However, the "Resolution" is currently a bit "black box." The paper identifies the *what* but only hints at the *how* (using the Asian population share as a buffer). The story would be more compelling if it explored whether this was a "voluntary" exit for safety or a "forced" exit due to employer/customer behavior.

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

**The Dinner Party Fact:** "The COVID-era surge in hate incidents actually permanently changed the career paths of hundreds of thousands of Asian Americans, effectively 'cleansing' them out of the service sector and into office jobs."

**Response:** People will lean in. The follow-up question will be: "Wait, isn't moving from a restaurant to a tech firm an *improvement*? Did this accidentally help their careers in the long run?" The paper needs to be ready to answer that (currently Table 2 Column 4 hints at it with earnings, but doesn't go deep enough).

---

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

- **Front-load the Event Study:** The event study (Table 3) is the most powerful evidence of the "sharp break." It should be a Figure (Figure 1), not a table.
- **Mechanisms:** Section 5.3 (Reallocation) is too brief. This is the most "AER-y" part of the paper. It should be expanded with more granular industry data if possible.
- **Persistence:** The persistence through 2024 is the "hook." The conclusion should do more than summarize; it should speculate on whether this "new normal" is permanent or a slow-moving lag.

---

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

**The Gap:** The paper is currently a very high-quality *Applied Micro* paper. To be an *AER* paper, it needs to move beyond "this happened" to "this is a generalizable lesson about how social shocks shape economies."

**The Single Biggest Improvement:** Expand the welfare analysis. If 220,000 workers moved sectors, was there a "matching" cost? Did they take a pay cut compared to the *growth* they would have seen in their original sectors? If you can show that this reallocation led to a persistent "misallocation" of talent, the paper becomes a major statement on the hidden costs of social animosity.
