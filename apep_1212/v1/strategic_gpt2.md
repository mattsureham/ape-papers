## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether the COVID period changed where Asian Americans worked: did Asian workers move out of customer-facing sectors like retail and hospitality and into less public-facing sectors after 2020? Using state-by-race-by-sector quarterly administrative employment data, the paper documents a persistent relative decline of Asian employment in customer-facing sectors and a corresponding relative increase in professional/information sectors.

Why should a busy economist care? Because if true, this is not just a story about hate incidents or temporary job loss; it is a story about discrimination or perceived hostility changing the sectoral allocation of labor for an entire demographic group.

### Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Only partly. The introduction is vivid and readable, but it overshoots. It currently sells the paper as causal evidence on “COVID-era anti-Asian hostility” and “discrimination-induced sectoral sorting,” while the paper later concedes that the design identifies a broad pandemic-era differential, not anti-Asian discrimination specifically. That mismatch is the central strategic problem.

### What the first two paragraphs should say instead

The paper should open with a world question, not a literature gap, and it should state the object of inference honestly:

> The COVID shock did not affect all workers equally. For Asian Americans, the pandemic coincided with a sharp rise in anti-Asian hostility, heightened public health stigma, and major changes in where work could be performed. This raises a first-order labor-market question: did the pandemic period push Asian workers out of customer-facing sectors and into other parts of the economy?
>
> Using Quarterly Workforce Indicators by race, sector, state, and quarter from 2016–2024, this paper shows that Asian employment fell differentially in retail and hospitality relative to White workers and relative to Asian workers in professional/information sectors, with a persistent offsetting increase in the latter. The paper’s contribution is to document a durable pandemic-era sectoral reallocation of Asian American employment; anti-Asian hostility is a plausible mechanism, but the evidence is best interpreted as capturing the composite effect of pandemic-era forces that especially affected Asian workers in public-facing jobs.

That is the pitch the paper can credibly own.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper documents that, during and after 2020, Asian American employment shifted persistently away from customer-facing sectors and toward knowledge-economy sectors relative to plausible within-data comparison groups.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?

Not yet. The paper lists several literatures, but it does not sharply distinguish itself from:
1. COVID labor market reallocation papers,
2. classic customer-discrimination papers,
3. work on anti-Asian hate during the pandemic,
4. broader papers on discrimination and occupational sorting.

Right now the contribution risks sounding like: “a DDD paper using QWI to show Asian workers did worse in customer-facing sectors after COVID.” That is not enough for AER unless the author is very clear about what is new relative to the existing conversation.

The novelty is not “using DDD,” and it is not “first causal evidence” on anti-Asian discrimination—that claim is too strong given the design. The more defensible novelty is: **the paper brings administrative labor-market data to bear on a demographic reallocation margin that the hate-crime/public-health literature cannot observe.**

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world or filling a gap in a literature?

It starts as a world question, which is good, but then slides into literature-gap mode and method-selling. The stronger framing is the world question:

- Did the pandemic era durably alter the sectoral allocation of Asian American workers?
- Were customer-facing jobs relatively shed?
- Was this a temporary collapse or a persistent resorting?

That is much stronger than “the literature has not used QWI with a DDD.”

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the intro?

At present, they would probably say: “It’s another difference-in-differences paper on COVID and Asian workers, with a discrimination interpretation.” That is not what the author wants.

The intro needs to make it easy for a reader to say instead:  
**“This paper shows that the pandemic era didn’t just reduce outcomes for Asian Americans; it changed where they worked, with persistent movement out of public-facing sectors.”**

### What would make the contribution bigger?

A few concrete ways:

1. **Make total reallocation more granular.**  
   Right now “customer-facing” versus “knowledge” is coarse and somewhat stylized. The contribution would feel bigger if the paper could show the broader occupational/industrial redistribution rather than a two-bin contrast.

2. **Strengthen the “sorting” claim with richer margins.**  
   If the paper can show whether the shift was driven by new hires, quits, recalls, entrant cohorts, or establishment transitions, then the sorting story becomes much more substantive.

3. **Use outcomes that speak directly to economic significance.**  
   Not just employment shares, but career progression, earnings trajectories, job stability, or movement into remote-work-compatible sectors. Right now the conclusion gestures at occupational restructuring, but the evidence is still mostly on sectoral counts.

4. **Reframe from “anti-Asian discrimination” to “hostility/public-facing exposure interacted with pandemic labor-market restructuring.”**  
   Paradoxically, this would make the contribution bigger because it would become more believable and more general.

5. **Bring in comparison groups beyond White workers.**  
   If the paper is really about a distinct Asian-specific shock, then comparison to other minority groups or immigrant-heavy groups could make the contribution sharper and more interesting.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

### Closest neighbors

The paper seems to sit at the intersection of four literatures:

1. **Customer/taste-based discrimination**
   - Becker (1957)
   - Holzer and Ihlanfeldt / Holzer et al. on customer discrimination and minority employment
   - Charles and Guryan (2008)

2. **Labor-market adjustment/reallocation after shocks**
   - Autor, Dorn, Hanson-style sectoral reallocation logic
   - Cortes and Forsythe (2020) on heterogeneous labor-market effects of COVID
   - Related COVID reallocation papers on remote work and sectoral sorting

3. **Pandemic-era anti-Asian hostility**
   - Most of this is outside economics: Stop AAPI Hate, public health, sociology, criminology
   - The paper relies heavily on this literature substantively, but it is not yet converting it into an economics conversation

4. **Race, ethnicity, and post-shock labor-market discrimination**
   - The paper should probably engage more with studies of labor-market effects of backlash after 9/11, immigration shocks, or identity-targeted harassment, even if not Asian-specific

### How should the paper position itself relative to those neighbors?

Mostly **build on and connect**, not attack.

- Build on the anti-Asian hate literature by saying: that literature documents incidents and harms, but not labor-market reallocation.
- Build on the discrimination literature by saying: classic models emphasize wages and hiring; this paper points to sectoral allocation.
- Build on COVID labor papers by saying: they document broad labor-market reshuffling; this paper shows that reshuffling was demographically uneven.

The paper should not attack prior work for failing to do “causal” identification on anti-Asian discrimination, because its own design does not isolate that channel cleanly enough to sustain that rhetoric.

### Is the paper currently positioned too narrowly or too broadly?

Oddly, both.

- **Too broadly** in claiming to identify anti-Asian discrimination and permanent sorting from a broad post-2020 indicator.
- **Too narrowly** in its empirical implementation, which collapses the world into two sector categories and one demographic comparison.

The strategic sweet spot is: a paper about **pandemic-era demographic reallocation under public-facing exposure**, with Asian Americans as the most salient case.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?

It seems under-engaged with:

- Economics papers on **COVID, remote work, and sectoral composition**
- Economics papers on **backlash/discrimination after salient shocks** toward ethnically marked groups
- Literature on **occupational segregation and ethnic sorting**
- Literature on **urban/regional ethnic networks** beyond the two citations it gives

It also needs to be more careful with the non-econ literature it uses. Stop AAPI Hate is a useful descriptive source, but it cannot do all the conceptual heavy lifting.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?

Not quite. It is trying to have the “anti-Asian hate causes labor-market discrimination” conversation, but the evidence is better suited to the “major social shocks can reallocate minority workers across sectors” conversation.

That second conversation is more credible and, in some ways, more interesting to economists.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Before this paper, we know there was a surge in anti-Asian hostility during COVID, and we know the pandemic radically altered the labor market, especially in customer-facing sectors.

### Tension

What we do not know is whether Asian workers were differentially pushed out of public-facing sectors—and whether that was just a temporary collapse or a durable re-sorting of employment.

### Resolution

The paper finds a relative decline in Asian employment in customer-facing sectors after 2020 and an offsetting relative increase in knowledge sectors, with persistence well beyond the initial shock.

### Implications

The implication is that large social shocks may alter the allocation of labor across sectors for targeted groups, potentially changing occupational distributions even when aggregate employment recovers.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the bones of one, but it is not fully disciplined. Right now it is torn between two stories:

1. **Discrimination story:** anti-Asian hostility pushed Asian workers out.
2. **Reallocation story:** Asian workers moved out of customer-facing sectors during a pandemic-era restructuring.

The problem is that the evidence is cleaner for Story 2 than Story 1, but the writing insists on Story 1. That creates credibility slippage throughout the paper.

### What story should it be telling?

The paper should tell this story:

> COVID created a large shock to public-facing work. For Asian Americans, that shock interacted with stigma, hostility, and exposure in ways that made customer-facing sectors relatively less viable. The result was not merely temporary displacement but a lasting reallocation across sectors.

That narrative preserves the anti-Asian hostility motivation without overclaiming that the paper isolates it.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?

“After 2020, Asian American employment shifted persistently out of retail and hospitality and toward professional/information sectors relative to comparison groups.”

That is the strongest clean fact.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

Economists would lean in initially, because the topic is timely and the pattern is interesting. But the very next question would be:

**“How much of this is anti-Asian discrimination versus general COVID sectoral reshuffling and remote-work compositional change?”**

And if the presenter cannot answer that cleanly, the room will cool quickly.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Likely one of these:
- Why those control sectors?
- Is this just remote-work sorting by education?
- Is this about Asians specifically, or about workers differentially exposed to public interaction?
- Do we see similar patterns for other groups?
- Is the effect concentrated in places with more measured anti-Asian incidents?

That tells you the strategic issue: the first-order audience question is about interpretation, not estimation.

### If findings are modest or null

The findings are not null, but they are less spectacular than the prose implies. The paper does make a decent case that persistence matters. Still, the “symmetric reallocation” finding is currently oversold, especially since the paper itself notes it is partly mechanical in a two-sector design. That is exactly the kind of fact that should not be made to carry too much narrative weight.

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

### What would make it read better?

1. **Shorten the institutional background substantially.**  
   It currently repeats intro material and pads a relatively simple empirical setup. A top journal intro should do most of this work.

2. **Move method throat-clearing later and tighten it.**  
   The paper spends too much time narrating the DDD design and not enough time clarifying the economic object of interest.

3. **Front-load the interpretive caveat.**  
   The admission that the paper identifies a composite pandemic-era differential rather than anti-Asian discrimination should not appear buried in the empirical strategy. It belongs in the introduction.

4. **Bring the strongest descriptive figure/table earlier.**  
   If there is a simple plot showing Asian versus White employment by sector over time, that belongs very early. The reader should see the pattern before reading fixed-effect algebra.

5. **Demote weak robustness and avoid self-inflicted wounds.**  
   The placebo result being significant is not something to tuck into a standard “everything is robust” section. It should be discussed candidly as a limitation, because strategically it undercuts the strongest causal rhetoric.

6. **Cut the “novel application of DDD” claim.**  
   No AER editor cares that the application of DDD is novel per se. That is not a contribution claim; it is a technique note.

7. **Rewrite the conclusion.**  
   “It did not merely harm Asian Americans—it resorted them” is punchy, but too certain relative to the evidence. The conclusion should be more precise and more general: the paper documents persistent sectoral reallocation during a period of heightened hostility and labor-market disruption.

### Is the paper front-loaded with the good stuff?

Moderately, but not enough. The main result appears reasonably early, but the reader still has to parse a lot of framing that is more confident than the evidence allows. The key interpretive honesty should come before, not after, the splashy claims.

### Are there results buried in robustness that should be in the main text?

The placebo significance is strategically important; it should be integrated into the main discussion of what the paper can and cannot claim.

Also, the hires and separations results are potentially more useful for the “sorting” story than the paper currently makes of them. If one wants to tell a reallocation story, flows matter more than another significance table.

### Is the conclusion adding value?

Some, but mostly it is summarizing and amplifying. It should instead do more conceptual work:
- what economists should update about discrimination,
- what policymakers should update about labor-market consequences of social hostility,
- and what remains unresolved.

Primarily a **framing problem**, secondarily a **scope problem**.

- **Scope problem:** The empirical contrast is too coarse to fully support the stronger “sectoral sorting” and “permanent reshaping” claims.
- **Novelty problem:** In current form, the paper risks feeling like a competent application of standard reduced-form tools to a timely topic, rather than a paper that changes how the field thinks.
- **Ambition problem:** The paper chooses a safe design and then writes as if it has answered a much larger question than it actually has.

### What would excite the top 10 people in this field?

One of two things:

1. **Much tighter and more honest framing** around what the data actually show: pandemic-era reallocation of Asian workers away from public-facing sectors.
2. **Or materially broader evidence** that nails the mechanism or the general equilibrium significance: richer sectoral detail, direct link to hostility exposure, comparison across other groups, or stronger evidence on worker transitions and long-run career consequences.

**Stop selling this as causal evidence on anti-Asian discrimination and instead own the more credible, more original contribution: documenting a persistent pandemic-era sectoral reallocation of Asian American workers away from public-facing employment.**

If the author makes that one change, the paper immediately becomes more believable, more coherent, and easier to place in a serious economics conversation.
