### 1. Idea Fidelity
The paper partially pursues the original idea manifest but deviates substantially from the core identification strategy. It faithfully uses QWI data (2016–2024, state × race × sector × quarter), the exact sectors (customer-facing: NAICS 72, 44–45; knowledge: 54, 51), outcomes (employment, hires, separations, earnings), and triple-difference structure (Asian vs. White; customer-facing vs. knowledge). It also documents persistent effects through 2024 (beyond the manifest's 2022 horizon) and references the Atlanta shootings for timing. However, it misses the key continuous treatment from GDELT GKG anti-Asian media coverage (state × quarter intensity), instead using a binary national "Post" (2020Q1+). This drops the planned D3 variation (high- vs. low-intensity states) and DDD with continuous treatment, weakening causal claims about anti-Asian sentiment specifically. The research question shifts from sentiment-driven reallocation to a broader "pandemic-era discrimination tax," diluting novelty tied to GDELT × QWI combination.

### 2. Summary
This paper uses a triple-difference (DDD) design on Census QWI data to estimate that Asian American employment in customer-facing sectors (hospitality, retail) fell 11.3% (SE=0.020) relative to White workers and knowledge-economy sectors (professional services, information) after 2020Q1, with symmetric gains in knowledge sectors indicating reallocation. Event studies show flat immediate pre-trends but a sharp post-onset drop that persists through 2024, attenuated in high-Asian-share states. The results frame discrimination as a sorting mechanism reshaping occupational distributions, contributing to literatures on taste-based discrimination and labor reallocation.

### 3. Essential Points
1. **Missing state-varying treatment (GDELT integration):** The binary "Post" (national COVID onset) confounds anti-Asian sentiment with all pandemic shocks (lockdowns, demand collapse in hospitality/retail), undermining claims of identification via discrimination. The manifest's continuous GDELT measure (state × quarter media intensity, varying 20× across states) was planned for D3 but is absent; without it, state × quarter FEs absorb all state-time variation, leaving parallel trends as the sole assumption. Authors must incorporate GDELT as a continuous interactor (e.g., Asian × CF × GDELT_{s,t}) or explicitly justify dropping it, with falsification tests (e.g., orthogonality to non-Asian outcomes).

2. **Pre-trends violation in event study:** Immediate pre-trends (t=-4 to -1) are plausibly flat but longer-horizon coefficients (e.g., t=-12: +0.030**, t=-2: +0.011***) are significant and positive, reflecting pre-COVID Asian integration into customer-facing sectors (as noted). This violates parallel trends at extended lags, biasing the persistent post-effect downward. Authors must extend pre-period tests (e.g., synthetic controls or trend extrapolation), report full event-study coefficients (not selective), and interact trends with state covariates.

3. **Reallocation claim unsubstantiated:** The "symmetric" +11.3% knowledge-sector DDD is stated in text but not tabled/estimated; the main β identifies only the *relative* CF-knowledge gap for Asians vs. Whites, not absolute knowledge gains. If estimated separately (flipping sector coding), it risks double-dipping FEs and mechanical offsets. Authors must table the knowledge-only DDD specification, decompose net employment effects (CF drop minus knowledge gain), and test if total Asian employment is unaffected (e.g., aggregate across sectors).
