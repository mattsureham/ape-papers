## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
The paper examines the impact of the 2016 Panama Canal expansion—a $5.25 billion project designed to shift trade and jobs to the US East and Gulf Coasts—on regional labor markets. Using a difference-in-differences design, it finds the opposite of the "infrastructure-as-jobs" narrative: East and Gulf Coast port employment actually fell relative to the West Coast, suggesting that established logistics hubs enjoy a "gravity" or lock-in that mega-projects cannot easily overcome.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this well, but it frames itself slightly too much as a "policy evaluation." To be an AER paper, it needs to lead with the *economic puzzle*.

**The Pitch the Paper Should Have:**
"Why do dominant economic hubs persist despite massive infrastructure shocks designed to bypass them? I exploit the 2016 Panama Canal expansion as a natural experiment to test whether removing a physical constraint on trade flows can redistribute regional employment. Despite a $5 billion investment, I find a counter-intuitive relative decline in treated port employment, suggesting that incumbent advantages in automation and network density create an 'employment gravity' that physical infrastructure alone cannot break."

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**Contribution:** The paper documents that massive capacity-expanding infrastructure can paradoxically strengthen the relative position of incumbent hubs through endogenous responses (like automation) and network lock-in.

- **Differentiation:** Most literature (Moretti 2010, Kline & Moretti 2014) focuses on the *positive* multipliers of infrastructure. This paper is a "failure to launch" story, which is rarer and more interesting.
- **Framing:** It is currently 60% "filling a gap in the canal literature" and 40% "answering a question about the world." It needs to be 90% the latter—focusing on the **fragility of place-based policies.**
- **Bigger Contribution:** To hit the AER bar, the author needs more than just employment counts. They need to prove the *mechanism*. Is it truly automation at West Coast ports? If the author could bring in port-level capital expenditure or patent data, this becomes a first-order paper on "Defensive Innovation."

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
- **Closest Neighbors:** Feyrer (2019) on trade routes; Autor et al. (2013) on trade shocks/labor; and the New Economic Geography (NEG) classics (Krugman, Fujita).
- **Positioning:** It should position itself as a **modern empirical test of NEG.** The paper is currently too narrow (speaking to "port economists"). It needs to speak to **Urban/Regional** and **Trade** economists broadly.
- **Unaware of:** The paper should connect to the "Sunk Costs and Hysteresis" literature (e.g., Baldwin and Krugman). Why does trade stay in Los Angeles? Because the sunk costs of the supply chain are higher than the savings of the wider canal.

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC
- **Setup:** A $5 billion "game-changer" project.
- **Tension:** Billions invested in the East, yet the West remains king. Why?
- **Resolution:** The data shows not just a "null" result, but a relative decline in treated areas.
- **Implications:** Infrastructure is not a "Field of Dreams" (build it and they will come). Agglomeration is stickier than geography.

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong but the "Resolution" is currently a bit thin. The "Automation Response" (Section 5) is the most exciting part of the paper, but it's currently relegated to a discussion. It should be the climax of the empirical section.

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
- **The Fact:** "The biggest shipping upgrade of the century actually made the 'losing' ports relatively stronger."
- **Reaction:** People lean in. It’s counter-intuitive. 
- **Follow-up:** "Wait, did the East Coast ports just get more efficient and hire fewer people, or did the ships actually stay in LA?"
- **The "So What":** If the findings are robust, it suggests that current US industrial policy (Place-Based Transitions) might be fighting a losing battle against established agglomeration.

---

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
- **Move to Appendix:** The leave-one-out and placebo tests are standard; they don't need to take up a full page in the main text.
- **Front-load:** The "Automation Response" and "Logistics Lock-in" need to be tested, not just discussed. If the author can't find data on automation, they should at least show vessel-call data (AIS) to see if the *trade* moved even if the *jobs* didn't.
- **Conclusion:** It's too "policy-heavy." It needs to return to the theory of the firm and the geography of trade.

---

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The gap is **ambition and mechanism.** Currently, it's a very clean, very competent "Note" on the Panama Canal. To be a "Paper" in the AER, it needs to solve the mystery it uncovers.

**The single most impactful piece of advice:**
Stop treating the "Automation Response" as a discussion point and start treating it as your second main result; find data (even proxy data like equipment imports or port-level productivity) to prove that West Coast incumbents innovated in response to the canal's threat.
