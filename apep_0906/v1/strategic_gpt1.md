## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple, policy-relevant question: when one of the world’s most consequential shipping bottlenecks was dramatically expanded, did U.S. economic activity reallocate in the way everyone expected? Using employment outcomes in major port counties before and after the 2016 Panama Canal expansion, the paper argues that the answer is no: East and Gulf Coast ports did not gain port-related jobs relative to the West Coast, suggesting that logistics networks are much stickier than infrastructure boosters typically assume.

Why should a busy economist care? Because this is not really a paper about one canal; it is a paper about whether large transportation infrastructure shocks actually move economic geography, or whether agglomeration, network complementarities, and incumbent advantage swamp the mechanical effect of lower transport costs.

### Does the paper itself articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Not quite. The current opening is readable and vivid, but it is still too close to a “here is a famous event” setup. It spends more energy on the institutional story than on the big economic question. The first two paragraphs should more explicitly say: economists think trade costs shape location; the Panama Canal expansion was a rare, globally salient shock to trade costs; and the surprising result is that employment geography barely moved in the expected direction.

### The pitch the paper should have

A stronger opening would say something like:

> How much can a major transportation infrastructure shock reshape economic geography? The 2016 Panama Canal expansion sharply increased the feasibility of all-water shipping from Asia to East and Gulf Coast ports, and was widely expected to shift U.S. trade activity away from the West Coast. This paper shows that, despite the scale and salience of the shock, the expected labor-market reallocation did not materialize: port-related employment growth was weaker, not stronger, on the East and Gulf Coasts relative to the West Coast.
>
> The broader lesson is that lowering a transport bottleneck is not enough to reallocate economic activity when incumbent logistics hubs benefit from dense complementary networks, scale economies, and organizational lock-in. The paper uses this episode to speak to a larger question in trade and urban economics: when do infrastructure improvements change location decisions, and when do they leave existing economic geography largely intact?

That is the AER version of the story. Right now the paper is closer to “did this specific project create jobs where expected?”

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### Contribution in one sentence

The paper claims that a massive, highly anticipated reduction in maritime routing constraints did not shift U.S. port employment toward newly advantaged East and Gulf Coast ports, implying substantial persistence in trade geography.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?

Only partially. The paper gestures at three literatures—trade infrastructure, local labor markets, and agglomeration/path dependence—but it does not sharply distinguish what is new relative to each.

As written, the contribution risks sounding like:
- another reduced-form infrastructure paper,
- another local labor market DiD,
- another “agglomeration matters” paper.

To stand out, it needs a cleaner contrast:
1. **Relative to trade-cost and routing papers:** most show that access matters; this paper shows that even a very large access shock may not meaningfully reallocate local activity when downstream logistics networks are sticky.
2. **Relative to place-based infrastructure papers:** most study direct local spending or construction effects; this paper studies a global infrastructure shock that changes relative market access, which is conceptually different.
3. **Relative to agglomeration papers:** this paper provides an externally induced test of persistence in hub location, rather than inferring agglomeration from cross-sectional patterns.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a gap in a literature?

It is mixed, but still too literature-gap coded. The strongest version is a world question:

**When do major declines in transport frictions actually move economic activity across places?**

That is much stronger than:
**There is not enough evidence on port employment effects of the Panama Canal expansion.**

The current paper oscillates between these. It should commit to the former.

### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what’s new?

At the moment, maybe, but not confidently. They would probably say: “It’s a DiD on the Panama Canal expansion showing no employment gain on the East/Gulf Coast.” That is understandable, but not yet memorable.

What you want them to say is:
“Interesting paper—uses the Panama Canal expansion as a test of whether a huge trade-route shock can alter U.S. economic geography, and finds surprisingly little reallocation, suggesting logistics agglomeration is extremely persistent.”

That version has a real intellectual hook.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Several possibilities:

1. **Move closer to actual trade reallocation, not just employment.**  
   Employment is one margin, but not the cleanest one for the economic question. If the paper could show the same non-effect in container throughput, vessel calls, import flows, or product composition, the contribution becomes much bigger. Right now the risk is that the finding is interpreted as “jobs didn’t move,” not “trade geography didn’t move.”

2. **Show where the anticipated gains went.**  
   Did traffic stay on the West Coast? Did firms switch to other margins—larger ships, different inland routing, more automation, different commodities? A stronger outcome set would make the paper about equilibrium adjustment rather than about one labor-market symptom.

3. **Sharpen the mechanism.**  
   “Logistics lock-in” is plausible but currently speculative. A more compelling version would show that persistence is strongest where rail intermodal links, warehousing density, or importer distribution networks were most entrenched.

4. **Reframe away from job creation rhetoric and toward persistence of economic geography.**  
   “Infrastructure-as-job-creation narrative” is a smaller claim than “large transport-cost shocks may not reconfigure economic geography in mature logistics systems.”

The biggest gain would come from shifting the paper’s center of gravity from employment effects of a canal to the broader economics of spatial persistence under major infrastructure shocks.

### How should the paper position itself?

It should **build on** transport/geography papers and **speak back to** agglomeration/path dependence papers.

It should **not** spend too much time trying to join the generic “infrastructure spending creates jobs” literature. That is too broad and, frankly, not where this paper is strongest. This is not really about local multipliers from public works. The project itself is not U.S. place-based spending; it is a change in relative shipping technology and access. That distinction matters.

A better positioning would be:
- **Build on:** transport-cost and market-access literatures that predict spatial reallocation when connectivity improves.
- **Add to:** path dependence and agglomeration literatures by showing that even a major exogenous improvement in access need not dislodge incumbent hubs.
- **Connect to:** supply-chain and logistics literatures emphasizing organizational complementarities beyond pure transport costs.

### Is the paper currently positioned too narrowly or too broadly?

Oddly, both.

- **Too narrowly** in the empirical framing: “Did East/Gulf ports gain employment after the canal expansion?”
- **Too broadly** in the literature claims: it says it contributes to three big literatures, but without enough precision to persuade any of them.

It needs a narrower intellectual claim and a broader economic framing:
- Narrower claim: this is about persistence in logistics geography after a large market-access shock.
- Broader implication: infrastructure alone may not move activity when complementary networks are entrenched.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?

It seems under-engaged with:
- **Market access / transport infrastructure causal literature** beyond the brief citations
- **Path dependence in economic geography**
- **Supply chain organization / logistics network literature**
- **Time costs in trade** as distinct from distance or vessel size
- **Port and maritime economics** more specifically

The current references are somewhat generic. A top journal paper in this space would need to show deeper command of the conversation.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?

Not yet. The best conversation is not “did a famous infrastructure project create jobs?” The right conversation is “why are some major reductions in transport frictions surprisingly weak in shifting economic geography?” That is a much more interesting and durable question.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Before the paper, the standard expectation was that the Panama Canal expansion would materially shift trade flows and local port activity toward East and Gulf Coast ports by allowing larger vessels to bypass the West Coast.

### Tension

A large, salient, widely anticipated decline in routing constraints should have created measurable reallocation if transport access is a first-order determinant of port location. But mature logistics systems may be sticky because of rail links, warehousing, contracts, customs routines, labor pools, and scale economies.

### Resolution

The paper finds no positive employment reallocation toward East/Gulf ports; if anything, relative employment, hiring, and earnings outcomes are weaker there after the expansion.

### Implications

The paper implies that reducing a physical bottleneck is not enough to relocate activity in the presence of strong agglomeration and network complementarities. This matters for how economists think about transport infrastructure, market access, and the persistence of regional advantage.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the ingredients, but the arc is not fully disciplined. Right now it reads somewhat like:
1. famous event,
2. empirical design,
3. unexpected coefficients,
4. speculative mechanisms.

So it is not a random collection of results, but it is still more result-led than idea-led.

### What story should it be telling?

The paper should tell one clean story:

> The canal expansion is a rare test of a core prediction in trade and spatial economics: if market access changes sharply, activity should relocate. It didn’t, at least not in local labor demand at newly advantaged U.S. ports. That non-reallocation is itself the result, and it points to a world in which logistics agglomeration and organizational complementarities make economic geography much more inertial than simple transport-cost logic suggests.

That is the story. Everything else should support it.

The title, interestingly, is already closer to this deeper story than the introduction is. “Persistence of Port Employment Gravity” is a better conceptual frame than much of the actual intro text.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?

I would lead with:

> The 2016 Panama Canal expansion was supposed to shift U.S. port activity from the West Coast to the East and Gulf Coasts, but in the labor market data the expected reallocation does not show up—if anything, East and Gulf Coast port employment outcomes deteriorate relative to the West Coast.

That is the dinner-party fact.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

Some would lean in, but only if presented as a test of economic geography rather than as a narrow port-employment study. The surprise is real. The issue is not the empirical punchline; it is whether the paper convinces readers that this punchline changes how they think about a broader economic question.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Immediately:

**“Okay, but did trade flows actually move and just not show up in employment—or did nothing move at all?”**

That is the central vulnerability in strategic positioning. The paper currently uses employment to stand in for reallocation of port activity. Reasonable, but not enough for a top-field audience unless handled carefully. Readers will want to know whether the null/negative employment finding reflects:
- no shift in shipping activity,
- shift in traffic but not jobs because of automation/productivity,
- different commodity mix,
- delayed adjustment,
- or broader macro shocks.

Again, referees can sort identification and robustness. But strategically, this follow-up question tells you the paper’s current evidentiary center is not the strongest match for its broadest claim.

### If the findings are null or modest, is the null interesting?

Yes, potentially very interesting. This is exactly the kind of null that can matter: a large, globally salient, high-stakes infrastructure shock failing to produce the expected spatial reallocation.

But the paper needs to lean harder into **why this null matters**:
- it disciplines overconfident claims about infrastructure-induced reallocation,
- it reveals limits of partial-equilibrium transport logic,
- it highlights the role of complementary logistics systems,
- it cautions against naive “if you build capacity, they will come” reasoning.

Right now the paper does some of this, but not enough. It still occasionally reads like “we expected positive effects and didn’t find them,” which can sound like a failed treatment rather than a substantive lesson about the world.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

Be honest: in current form, this is not yet an AER paper. The gap is not primarily polish. It is strategic.

### What is the main gap?

Mostly a **framing problem**, secondarily a **scope problem**, and somewhat a **novelty/ambition problem**.

#### Framing problem
The paper’s most interesting idea is bigger than its current framing. It should be about the persistence of economic geography after a major market-access shock, not just about whether the Panama Canal expansion created jobs in treated counties.

#### Scope problem
For the broader claim, the evidence base feels a bit narrow. Employment, hires, and earnings are useful, but they are one layer removed from the central object of interest: trade and logistics reallocation. To excite top people in trade/urban/public, the paper likely needs either:
- richer outcomes closer to flows and port activity, or
- a much tighter conceptual argument for why labor-market outcomes are the right sufficient statistic.

#### Novelty problem
The episode is novel and high-profile, but the design and outcome choice make it feel smaller than it should. A smart reader may say: “Interesting result, but this is still a county-level DiD around a known event.” To clear the AER bar, the paper needs a more unmistakable claim on a first-order question.

#### Ambition problem
The paper is competent and clean, but somewhat safe. It stops at documenting the surprising sign and offering plausible mechanisms. The more ambitious version would use the event to say something broader and more definitive about when infrastructure shifts equilibria.

### Single most impactful piece of advice

**Reframe the paper around a bigger question—whether large transport-cost shocks can meaningfully reshape economic geography in mature logistics systems—and support that claim with outcomes closer to actual trade reallocation, not just employment.**

If the author can only change one thing, that is it.
