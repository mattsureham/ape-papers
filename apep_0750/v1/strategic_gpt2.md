## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple, policy-relevant question: when the EU required member states to adopt preventive restructuring procedures inspired by Chapter 11, did that actually reduce business failures? Using staggered implementation across European countries, the paper’s headline finding is no: the reform appears to have had no detectable effect on aggregate bankruptcy declarations.

A busy economist should care because this is not just about insolvency law. It speaks to a broader question: when governments harmonize legal institutions at scale, do formal legal changes meaningfully alter real economic outcomes, or do they mostly relabel existing processes?

### Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Not quite. The current introduction is energetic, but it leans too quickly into grandeur (“the most ambitious insolvency reform in history”) and then into method. The first paragraph gestures at stakes; the second paragraph becomes an estimator paragraph. What is missing is a clean statement of the substantive world question: can legal harmonization of bankruptcy procedures save firms, or is legal form largely inert absent deeper institutional change?

### The pitch the paper should have

The first two paragraphs should say something like:

> European policymakers increasingly rely on legal harmonization to improve economic performance. One of the clearest recent tests is the EU Preventive Restructuring Directive, which required member states to create pre-insolvency restructuring procedures intended to help viable firms avoid liquidation. The central economic question is whether importing Chapter 11-style tools into diverse national systems actually reduces business failure.
>
> This paper studies that question using the staggered implementation of the directive across 26 EU countries. I find that adoption of the directive had no detectable effect on aggregate bankruptcy declarations. The result suggests that large legal reforms may change the formal menu of procedures without materially changing firm outcomes—either because cases are reclassified across legal categories or because institutional practice is slow to respond to statutory change.

That is the AER version of the pitch: a big question about law versus practice, with the directive as a sharp empirical setting.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper provides quasi-experimental evidence that a major, EU-wide harmonization of preventive restructuring law did not measurably reduce aggregate business bankruptcies.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?

Only partially. The paper distinguishes itself from broad cross-country legal origins work and from single-country insolvency studies, but the differentiation is still somewhat mechanical: “they are descriptive / single-country; I am quasi-experimental / multi-country.” That is true, but not yet memorable.

What is new here is not just design. It is the object of study: a coordinated supranational legal transplant implemented across heterogeneous institutional environments. The paper should lean harder on that. Right now the contribution risks sounding like “another DiD on a policy rollout,” when the stronger claim is: this is a rare test of whether harmonized formal law changes economic outcomes across states with different legal infrastructures.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or as filling a gap in a literature?

It starts as a world question, which is good, but then slips into literature-gap language (“first causal evaluation,” “extends the literature beyond single-country studies”). The world question is much stronger:

- Do Chapter 11-style reforms reduce failure outside the institutional ecosystem that made Chapter 11 meaningful?
- Does legal harmonization produce real convergence, or only statutory convergence?

That is the frame to keep.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?

Not confidently. They would probably say: “It’s a cross-country staggered DiD on the EU restructuring directive, and the effect on bankruptcy filings is null.” That is accurate but not yet distinctive enough.

The introduction needs to tell them what belief should change. For example: “Economists and policymakers often assume that making restructuring more available should mechanically reduce liquidation; this paper shows that legal availability alone may not be enough.” That gives the null result intellectual content.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Several possibilities:

1. **Better outcome framing**
   The current outcome—aggregate bankruptcy declarations—is policy-relevant but blunt. The paper itself admits that the directive may have shifted composition rather than totals. If the authors had proceeding-level evidence on restructurings versus liquidations, or even a country-level series on insolvency composition, the contribution becomes much larger: not “no effect” but “reclassification rather than rescue.”

2. **Sharper heterogeneity tied to treatment intensity**
   The current “countries with and without pre-existing frameworks” split is directionally right but underdeveloped. If the paper could classify reforms by how transformative they were, it could speak more directly to whether legal transplants work only when they actually change institutions.

3. **Stronger mechanism or external implication**
   Right now the mechanism discussion is speculative. A bigger paper would connect the null to court capacity, legal profession adaptation, creditor coordination, or firm awareness. Even descriptive evidence here would help.

4. **A broader framing**
   The paper becomes more important if presented as evidence on the limits of legal harmonization and institutional transplantation—not just insolvency reform per se.

My sense is that the single biggest “make it bigger” move is to pivot from “did bankruptcies fall?” to “what do we learn about when formal legal change translates into real economic change?” The current paper has the setting for that, but not yet the full narrative.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Before this paper, policymakers and many economists would expect that making restructuring easier should help viable firms avoid liquidation. The EU directive is a major natural test of that logic because it mandated preventive restructuring across many countries.

### Tension

There is a gap between formal legal reform and real economic change. Some countries already had similar procedures; others did not. Courts, professionals, and firms may not use new legal tools even when statutes change. So the puzzle is whether a sweeping harmonization reform actually changes firm failure outcomes.

### Resolution

The paper finds no detectable effect on aggregate bankruptcy declarations after transposition.

### Implications

The implication is not merely that “this directive didn’t work.” It is that formal harmonization may be insufficient without complementary institutional capacity, take-up, or deeper behavioral change. More broadly, legal form may travel more easily than legal function.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the pieces, but they are not yet assembled into a crisp story. At present it reads somewhat like:

1. Big reform
2. Staggered rollout
3. Null results
4. Several possible mechanisms
5. Policy implication

That is workable, but still closer to a competent evaluation than a fully satisfying AER narrative.

### What story should it be telling?

The story should be:

- **Setup:** Europe tried to transplant a rescue-oriented insolvency model.
- **Tension:** Economists often infer from theory and cross-country comparisons that such reforms should preserve firms—but it is unclear whether changing statutes changes actual restructuring behavior.
- **Resolution:** Across Europe, the reform did not lower aggregate bankruptcy declarations.
- **Implication:** The payoff to legal harmonization depends on institutional implementation, not merely legal adoption.

That is a real story. The current draft hints at it, but keeps retreating into estimator talk and robustness cataloguing.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?

“I would lead with: Europe imported Chapter 11-style preventive restructuring across almost all member states—and aggregate bankruptcy rates didn’t budge.”

That is a reasonably good dinner-party fact.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

Some would lean in, especially those in law and finance, institutions, or European policy. But many mainstream economists would ask almost immediately: “Is bankruptcy declarations the right outcome?” If the answer remains uncertain, attention drops.

So the paper clears the first hurdle, but not decisively.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Almost certainly:

- “Did it change the composition of proceedings even if not the total?”
- “Was this a real reform in countries that already had similar systems?”
- “Does this show legal harmonization is toothless, or just that your outcome misses the relevant margin?”

That tells you where the paper is vulnerable strategically. The natural next question is exactly where the paper currently has only speculation.

### Is the null result itself interesting?

Yes—conditionally. A null result can be very interesting when it overturns a strong prior and involves a major policy intervention. This paper has that potential. But the burden is to show that the null is informative, not just inconclusive.

The paper partly does this by emphasizing that the reform was consequential and that large negative effects are ruled out. That helps. But it still needs to work harder to explain why a null on this particular outcome teaches us something fundamental about institutional reform rather than merely reflecting outcome mismeasurement.

Right now it is an interesting null, but not yet an unavoidable one.
