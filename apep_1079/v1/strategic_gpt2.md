## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether the 2018–2019 U.S.-China trade war imposed a disproportionate labor-market cost on Asian-American workers because they were concentrated in the manufacturing industries most exposed to Section 301 tariffs and because anti-China rhetoric might have spilled over into discrimination against workers perceived as Chinese. Using county-industry-race employment data, the paper finds no detectable differential employment loss for Asian workers: the trade war may have moved manufacturing employment, but it did not produce an “identity tax” in these data.

A busy economist should care because this is, in principle, a sharp intersection of two major conversations: the distributional effects of trade shocks and the labor-market consequences of racialized political rhetoric. If the paper could show that trade policy aimed at a foreign country spills over onto co-ethnic domestic workers, that would be an important result. If it can convincingly show that such spillovers did *not* occur, that too could matter—but only if the paper makes the ex ante stakes feel real and the null genuinely informative.

### Does the paper articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Not quite. The current opening is reasonably clear, but it oversells the “racial shadow” angle before establishing why this is a first-order economic question rather than an interesting social concern attached to a standard trade design. The introduction also rushes into design details in paragraph two before the reader is fully persuaded that the question is economically central.

The first two paragraphs should do three things more crisply:

1. Establish the broad world question: when trade policy targets a geopolitical rival, do domestic workers linked to that rival by identity bear additional labor-market costs?
2. Explain why the Section 301 episode is the right setting: concentrated exposure of Asian workers in tariffed sectors plus unusually salient anti-China rhetoric.
3. State the headline finding plainly: despite these conditions, the paper finds no differential employment penalty for Asian-American workers.

### The pitch the paper should have

> Trade policy may redistribute jobs across places and industries, but it can also reshape who bears those costs if exposed workers are disproportionately drawn from particular demographic groups—or if geopolitical rhetoric spills over into discrimination against workers associated with the targeted country. The 2018–2019 U.S.-China trade war is a natural case: Asian-American workers were heavily concentrated in some of the most tariff-exposed manufacturing industries, and public debate raised concern that anti-China policy might create a domestic “identity tax.”
>
> This paper asks whether that happened. Using county-industry-race-quarter data on U.S. manufacturing employment, I find that although tariff exposure affected manufacturing outcomes, it did not differentially reduce employment for Asian-American workers relative to otherwise similar workers in the same local labor markets. The result suggests that the distributional incidence of this trade shock operated through industry and geography, not through an additional race-linked employment penalty.

That is the AER-facing pitch. Less slogan, more world question.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper’s contribution is to test whether the U.S.-China trade war generated race-specific employment losses for Asian-American manufacturing workers and to report that it did not.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest papers?

Only partially. The paper does identify a niche not directly occupied by the cited trade-war papers or by the racial China-shock paper, but the differentiation is still too mechanical: “they study aggregate/firm effects; I study racial heterogeneity.” That is a literature gap, not yet a major economic contribution.

The closest contrast is clearly with work on racial heterogeneity in the original China Shock, especially Autor et al. on racial effects through industrial composition. The paper needs to say more sharply: why should Section 301 be expected to differ from WTO-era import competition? Right now the distinction—retaliatory, salient, explicitly anti-China—is stated, but not developed into a bigger claim. Without that, the contribution reads as “same idea, later episode, different subgroup focus.”

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a literature gap?

It oscillates, but mostly it still feels gap-driven. The stronger frame is the world question:

- When policy targets a foreign adversary, do domestic workers associated with that adversary suffer collateral labor-market damage?
- More broadly: when do trade shocks map into racial incidence, and when do they not?

That is stronger than: “no one has studied racial heterogeneity in Section 301 tariffs.”

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?

A smart economist could probably say: “It’s a paper on whether the China trade war hurt Asian-American workers more, and the answer is no.” That is better than “another DiD paper about X,” but only marginally. Right now they would not say: “This changes how I think about the incidence of trade shocks.” They would say: “Interesting extension.”

The problem is not total lack of novelty; it is insufficient elevation of the novelty.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Most importantly: make the paper about **when trade shocks translate into racialized labor-market incidence**, not just whether one episode had a non-result. Concretely, the contribution would get bigger if the paper did one of the following:

- **Compare Section 301 directly to the original China Shock** in a common framework and show why racial incidence differed across episodes.
- **Move beyond employment levels to job composition**: occupations, earnings, hiring, separations, managerial vs production roles. The discussion gestures at “skill insulation,” but the paper does not turn that into the contribution.
- **Directly test identity salience** with local anti-Asian sentiment, media tone, or hate incidents. The paper admits it wanted to do this and could not. Unfortunately that admission reveals the current paper is a weaker version of its own most interesting idea.
- **Reframe around incidence decomposition**: how much of demographic exposure to trade shocks is mechanical sorting versus behavioral discrimination? That is a broader and more portable question than this one episode.

If the paper could credibly deliver one of those, it would feel much larger.

On the race/discrimination side, the cited anti-Asian discrimination papers are relevant but feel secondary unless the paper can actually identify the discrimination channel more directly.

### How should the paper position itself relative to those neighbors?

Build on, not attack.

- Relative to the China Shock literature: “That literature shows trade shocks can have unequal racial incidence through industrial composition. I ask whether the same is true in a politically salient tariff episode explicitly aimed at China.”
- Relative to the Section 301 literature: “That literature quantifies trade and firm effects. I study demographic incidence.”
- Relative to discrimination papers: “Those papers document rising anti-Asian hostility; I ask whether such hostility translated into measurable employment penalties in exposed sectors.”

The paper should not overclaim that it overturns or “rejects” an identity-salience channel in any broad sense. It has employment data, not direct discrimination measures. The current language sometimes gets too close to that.

### Is the paper positioned too narrowly or too broadly?

At present, oddly, both.

- **Too narrow** in its empirical framing: county-industry-race-quarter employment effects of Section 301 tariffs.
- **Too broad** in its rhetorical framing: “identity tax,” “racial shadow,” “rejecting identity-based channels.”

The paper needs a middle ground: a disciplined, economically meaningful question about the demographic incidence of trade shocks under geopolitical salience.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?

Even without naming specific additional papers, the paper should speak more to:

- **Incidence and distributional consequences of trade policy** beyond aggregate manufacturing.
- **Political economy of identity and economic shocks**—how rhetoric and policy interact.
- **Occupational/task composition within industries** if it wants to discuss skill insulation.
- Possibly **ethnic networks and immigrant labor-market sorting** more seriously, since the whole premise depends on nonrandom sorting of Asian workers into exposed industries.

Right now the literature review is competent but a bit checklist-like.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?

Not fully. The most impactful conversation is not “race and labor markets” in general; it is:

**When a macro policy shock maps onto demographic categories, is the incidence driven by sorting, discrimination, or within-firm/task insulation?**

That conversation links trade, labor, identity, and incidence. It is more interesting than either a standard trade paper with subgroup interactions or a standard discrimination paper without direct discrimination measurement.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Before this paper, we know trade shocks have uneven local and industrial effects, and we know Asian-American workers were overrepresented in some of the sectors most exposed to Section 301 tariffs. We also know anti-China rhetoric intensified during the trade war period.

### Tension

This creates two plausible reasons Asian-American workers might be hit harder:

1. **Mechanical composition**: they are more concentrated in exposed sectors.
2. **Identity salience/discrimination**: employers or coworkers may treat workers associated with China differently.

The puzzle is whether a trade war against China translated into a racialized domestic labor-market shock.

### Resolution

The paper finds no differential employment loss for Asian workers in exposed places/sectors. Employment may move overall, but not in a way that imposes an extra penalty on Asian workers.

### Implications

The trade war’s incidence, at least in these data, does not appear to have included an additional labor-market penalty for Asian-American manufacturing workers. More broadly, not every geopolitically charged trade shock generates racialized labor-market outcomes, even when exposed industries are demographically skewed.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

Serviceable, but not strong. The paper has the ingredients of a narrative arc, but the middle and ending are unstable.

Why unstable?

- The paper wants to tell an “identity tax” story.
- But the actual evidence is mostly a **null on differential employment** plus some mixed base effects across specifications.
- The discussion then speculates about mechanisms—skill insulation, import substitution—without really owning the fact that the paper’s strongest result is simply the absence of a differential employment effect.

So the paper is not exactly a collection of results looking for a story, but it is a paper with **one clean story and several half-developed backup stories**.

### What story should it be telling?

It should tell this story:

> Trade shocks can become demographically unequal either because workers are sorted into exposed sectors or because the shock activates identity-linked treatment. Section 301 created conditions under which both channels seemed plausible. Yet in employment data, the shock does not translate into an additional penalty for Asian-American workers. That negative result is informative because it bounds the extent to which geopolitical targeting spilled over into labor-market incidence.

That is cleaner than alternating between “no identity tax,” “import substitution gains,” “manufacturing declines relative to services,” and “maybe skill insulation.”

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?

I would lead with:

> “Asian-American workers were heavily concentrated in the manufacturing industries most exposed to the China tariffs, but I don’t find that the trade war cost them jobs relative to White workers.”

That is the fact.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

Some would lean in—especially trade economists, labor economists, and people interested in race and political economy. But many would ask, within 20 seconds: “Interesting, but is that because exposure differences were actually small, or because the outcome is too coarse, or because employment is the wrong margin?”

That is the challenge. The paper’s headline is interesting enough for attention, but not enough for sustained excitement unless it answers those follow-up questions well.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Most likely one of these:

- “Why didn’t the concentration translate into bigger losses?”
- “Is employment too aggregated to detect discrimination?”
- “Does the result differ within electronics, or by occupation, skill, or local anti-Asian sentiment?”
- “How does this compare to racial incidence in the original China Shock?”

Those are exactly the questions the paper should be organized to answer, at least partially.

### If the findings are null or modest, is the null itself interesting?

Potentially yes, but the paper has not fully made the case. A null result is valuable here only if the paper establishes:

1. Why a differential effect was genuinely plausible ex ante.
2. Why the absence of one updates our beliefs.
3. What magnitudes are ruled out.
4. Why employment is a meaningful margin on which to test the claim.

The paper does some of this—especially the “well-powered null” rhetoric—but it overstates the precision of what is learned. “No identity tax” is too sweeping. “No detectable differential employment penalty” is stronger and more defensible.

Right now the null reads as interesting but not yet definitive enough to feel like a field-shifting null.
