## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether the 2018–2019 trade war with China—which was explicitly targeted at an Asian adversary—disproportionately harmed Asian-American workers through either their concentration in targeted industries (e.g., electronics) or through increased labor market discrimination. Leveraging the high concentration of Asian workers in "crosshair" industries, the author finds a "well-powered null": while the trade war caused sectoral shifts, it did not create a racialized "identity tax" in employment.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this reasonably well, but the first two paragraphs are a bit "heavy" on mechanics. 

**The pitch the paper should have:**
"Do trade wars with foreign adversaries create a 'racial shadow' for domestic workers who share the adversary's identity? During the 2018–2019 U.S.-China trade war, Asian-American workers were four times more likely to work in the electronics industries most heavily targeted by tariffs. This paper tests whether this exposure translated into disproportionate job losses or discriminatory 'identity taxes,' ultimately finding that while the trade war reshaped manufacturing, the feared racialized labor market crisis failed to materialize."

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution:** The paper provides the first empirical test of racialized labor market heterogeneity resulting from the Section 301 "Trade War" tariffs, specifically rejecting the "identity-salience" discrimination hypothesis in aggregate employment data.

- **Differentiation:** It is well-differentiated. While Fajgelbaum et al. (2020) and Amiti et al. (2019) cover aggregate effects, and Autor et al. (2022) look at the original "China Shock," no one has yet looked at the racial distribution of the *retaliatory* trade war.
- **World vs. Literature:** It frames itself as answering a question about the WORLD (specifically the intersection of trade policy and racial equity), which is a major strength for the AER.
- **"Another DiD?":** A smart economist would say: "It’s a clever look at the distributional consequences of the trade war using race as the margin of interest." It feels fresh.
- **Bigger Contribution:** To make it "AER-big," the author needs to bridge the gap between their null result and the documented rise in anti-Asian sentiment/hate crimes (Gee et al., 2021). If hate crimes went up but employment didn't budge, *why*? Is it because manufacturing is more insulated from consumer-facing prejudice than service sectors?

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

- **Closest Neighbors:** Autor, Dorn, and Hanson (2013/2022) on trade shocks; Fajgelbaum et al. (2020) on the trade war; Bertrand and Mullainathan (2004) on racial frictions.
- **Positioning:** It builds on the "China Shock" literature but acts as a necessary "correction" or "update" for the trade war era.
- **Missing Conversations:** The paper needs to speak more to the **Personnel Economics** and **Sociology of Labor** literatures. If discrimination didn't happen in the factory, why? Is it because of "Skill Insulation" (as the author suggests) or because firm-level HR practices or unionization buffers workers from political sentiment? 

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

- **Setup:** Asian-American workers are highly concentrated in the very industries the U.S. government "attacked" via tariffs.
- **Tension:** Anti-China rhetoric was at an all-time high. Economic theory and public discourse suggest these workers should have been hit twice: once by industry sorting and once by prejudice.
- **Resolution:** A robust null. No "identity tax" is found.
- **Implications:** Trade policy, even when nationalist and adversarial, may be too "blunt" or the labor market too "rigid" for political sentiment to translate into immediate discriminatory hiring/firing.

**Evaluation:** The narrative is strong. It’s a "dog that didn't bark" story, which is often more interesting for the AER than another "X caused Y" paper.

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

- **The Lead Fact:** "Asian workers were 4x more exposed to the trade war, but didn't lose their jobs any faster than White workers."
- **Reaction:** People will lean in. It's counter-intuitive given the high-profile rise in anti-Asian sentiment during this period.
- **Follow-up:** "Does this mean employers are less prejudiced than the general public, or just that they couldn't find anyone to replace the engineers in NAICS 334?"

---

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

- **Front-loading:** The "Skill Insulation" hypothesis in Section 6 is actually the most interesting part of the paper. It should be brought forward or tested more rigorously (e.g., by splitting the sample by education/skill level within the QWI if possible).
- **Earnings vs. Employment:** The earnings result in Table 3 (Column 4) is a significant decline for *everyone* but no racial gap. This is a huge finding. It suggests the "cost" of the trade war was felt in the wallet, not the pink slip, for all races equally.

---

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The gap is **mechanisms**. A null result in the AER needs to be "anatomically" complete. 

**Single Most Impactful Advice:** The author needs to prove *why* the null exists. Is it because Asian-American workers in these industries are "too skilled to fire" (Skill Insulation)? If the author can use the QWI to show that the null holds for high-earners but *maybe* there was a tax for low-earners, the paper becomes a high-level study of how human capital protects against discrimination.
