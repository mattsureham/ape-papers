# Research Ideas

## Idea 1: The Economic Integration Lottery: How Immigration Judge Leniency Shapes Local Labor Markets
**Policy:** The US asylum system adjudicates ~270,000 cases/year across 68+ immigration courts. Cases are randomly assigned to judges within each court (GAO 2008, 2017), and judges exhibit dramatic leniency variation — the median within-court disparity is 56 percentage points (TRAC 2024). When asylum is granted, applicants receive immediate work authorization, access to federal benefits, and a path to permanent residence. When denied, they face deportation or join the unauthorized population.
**Outcome:** County-level total employment, average weekly wages, employment in low-wage service sectors (NAICS 72, 56), business establishment counts (BLS QCEW); noncitizen population share, poverty rates for foreign-born (Census ACS).
**Identification:** Immigration judge leniency IV (UJIVE estimator). Within each EOIR court, cases are randomly assigned to judges. We construct a court-by-year leave-one-out average leniency instrument for the court-level asylum grant rate, linked to county-level economic outcomes. First stage: 56-pp within-court variation implies extraordinarily strong instrument. Exclusion: Judge identity affects outcomes only through the grant decision, conditional on court × year FE. Built-in placebo: high-wage sectors (NAICS 52 Finance, 54 Professional Services) should not respond to low-skill asylum labor supply.
**Why it's novel:** No existing paper exploits immigration judge leniency for economic outcomes. Separates legal status from immigration — holds immigrant population roughly fixed, asks whether legal status per se affects local labor markets. Different from the Borjas/Card debate about immigration levels.
**Feasibility check:** Confirmed — EOIR case data 4.24 GB downloadable (HTTP 200), BLS QCEW API working, Census ACS API working, TRAC judge reports accessible. ~2.7M asylum cases, ~735 judges, ~68 courts, ~500 counties. 56-pp within-court judge disparity confirmed by TRAC 2024.
