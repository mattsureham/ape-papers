**Idea Fidelity**

The paper faithfully implements the manifest idea. It exploits the 301-employee threshold in Japan’s Women’s Active Engagement Act, draws on the MHLW enterprise database, and focuses on the contrast between female management representation and the gender wage gap. The promised RDD identification via disclosure compliance at the threshold is executed (albeit through a parametric strategy necessitated by the binned running variable), and the paper draws the specified policy implication that transparency may be actionable for promotions but not for structural pay gaps. **Summary**

This paper studies the causal impact of Japan’s 2016 mandatory gender-disclosure regime on firm-level outcomes by exploiting the 301-employee disclosure threshold. Using the full MHLW Women’s Active Engagement Enterprise Database and a parametric regression discontinuity framework that imposes separate linear size trends on each side of the cutoff, it finds that the mandate raised female management share by roughly 3.8 percentage points while leaving the gender wage gap unaffected. These results are interpreted as evidence that transparency moves outcomes amenable to managerial action but not structural pay disparities.

**Essential Points**

1. **Credibility of the Parametric RDD with a Binned Running Variable.** The identification hinges on the parametric separation of the firm-size gradient from the discontinuity at 301 employees, yet the running variable is only observed in seven discrete bins (101–300 vs. 301–500 is a huge jump). This means the “local” exchangeability assumption cannot be tested with usual RDD diagnostics, and the treatment estimate is very sensitive to the assumed functional form. The paper should bolster credibility by (a) providing evidence that linear trends adequately capture the size–outcome relationship (e.g., via specification tests or higher-order polynomials), (b) demonstrating that excluding different subsets of bins or using alternative functional forms yields consistent results, and (c) justifying why the size gradient is smooth enough for the discontinuity to isolate a policy effect. Without these diagnostics, the results risk conflating policy effects with residual size differences, especially given the stark opposite signs in the raw adjacent-bin comparison.

2. **Interpretation of the Threshold as a Sharp Treatment.** Compliance jumps from 14% to 89%, making the threshold better viewed as a fuzzy RDD where actual disclosure is the treatment. The current specification treats the 301+ indicator as the “treatment” while simultaneously including steep size trends; this conflates the policy-induced change in disclosure with systematic differences between large and mid-sized firms. The paper should clarify the estimand: is it the intent-to-treat effect of being above the threshold, or the local average treatment effect via the first stage? If the latter, the paper should present IV estimates (or at least reduced-form/first-stage ratios) so that the reader can disentangle compliance from size. Moreover, it should address whether the 11% of larger firms that do not disclose differ systematically, and whether they drive the management result.

3. **Temporal Dimension and Placebo Thresholds.** The paper is cross-sectional yet credits the 2016 mandate for current outcomes without showing any before-after change. Without pre-intervention data, it is hard to rule out that firms above 301 employees have always had more women in management conditional on size, or that the relationship changed for reasons unrelated to disclosure (e.g., differential industry trajectories). Similarly, while the paper reports placebo tests at other cutoffs, these are still cross-sectional and may capture different selection dynamics. To fortify the claim that the mandate caused the change, the authors should seek pre-mandate benchmarks (even if imperfect) or exploit the 2022 extension to replicate the design at 301+ disclosure of the wage gap, thus providing a quasi-difference-in-discontinuities approach.

**Suggestions**

1. **Expand the Parametric Flexibility.** Because the running variable is binned, it would be helpful to demonstrate robustness to richer functional forms. For example, instead of linear trends, include quadratic terms (or interact bin dummies with a continuous “bin index”) and show that the discontinuity estimate for female management share remains stable. Alternatively, estimate the model only using firms in narrower ranges of bins (e.g., 100–400 employees on each side) and compare results. If the discontinuity vanishes when trimming to a subset, that would raise flags about identification; if it persists, it strengthens the case. In addition, consider fitting a spline on the bin midpoints and reporting the goodness-of-fit; if the linear trend poorly fits, the residual differences could be confounding the effect.

2. **Instrumental Variable Interpretation.** The paper already documents the massive first-stage jump in disclosure. It would therefore be straightforward to present a Wald-IV estimate where disclosed status (or disclosure intensity) is instrumented by the 301+ indicator, implicitly capturing the causal effect of disclosure instead of merely the discontinuity. This would address concerns about the 11% of large firms that may not comply, and clarify whether the management result reflects the subset of firms that actually disclose. Relatedly, report balances (or placebo outcomes) for pre-determined firm characteristics (e.g., industry, age, prior female representation) to assess smoothness. If large firms differ on observables, the IV interpretation can be defended only by showing no discontinuity in those variables.

3. **Leverage the 2022 Extension or Additional Outcomes.** The manifest alluded to stacked treatments (2016, 2022, 2026), but the paper stops at the 2016 threshold. The 2022 requirement that 301+ firms disclose the gender wage gap provides another discontinuity, this time for a specific outcome. Using the same database but tracking the change in wage-gap reporting and outcomes across the 2022 rollout could function as a replicated experiment: did wage-gap disclosure (previously voluntary even for 301+ firms) lead to detectable changes in management or pay? Even if data are not yet available for 2022 outcomes, the paper could explore whether, among the firms that newly disclosed the wage gap, there are systematic differences in other outcomes relative to earlier disclosers. If feasible, the 2026 extension to 101+ firms offers a natural future research direction.

4. **Clarify Mechanisms and Heterogeneity.** The paper’s theoretical discussion posits that managers can adjust promotions but not structural pay. To substantiate this, consider exploring whether the management effect is concentrated in firms with more transparent promotion pipelines or in industries where internal promotion decisions are more visible. Conversely, analyze whether the mandate affected proxies for structural pay (e.g., tenure distributions, regular vs. non-regular workforce shares) to show that those margins were untouched. Additionally, the large positive effect on female section chief or board shares reported in the appendix deserves integration into the main narrative: do those patterns reinforce the promotion-focused interpretation? Finally, addressing whether the wage-gap null hides heterogeneous effects (e.g., among firms with higher female board shares) would enrich the story.

5. **Address Selection into Disclosure More Fully.** Table 5 highlights that firms voluntarily disclosing below the threshold are systematically different. This selection could imply that even above-threshold firms with poor gender outcomes delay or avoid disclosure, meaning the observed effect is driven by a selected subset. Consider estimating the model on a sample of firms that disclose voluntarily (if enough exist) to trace the counterfactual trajectory. Alternatively, include interactions between the threshold and observable proxies for compliance propensity (e.g., listing status, Tokyo location) to see whether the management effect varies. If the treatment effect is concentrated among firms that would otherwise be non-disclosers, that supports the mandate’s policy relevance.

6. **Improve Presentation of Placebos and Density Tests.** The text mentions McCrary-style density evidence but only qualitatively. Given the identification challenges with binned data, the authors could construct a pseudo-density plot at the threshold using the reported bin counts and compare them to the expected Pareto shape, perhaps via a formal test of over-fitting. For the placebo thresholds (e.g., 100 and 500 employees), explicitly report the estimated size trends and discuss why those cutoffs should not produce policy effects. Doing so would reassure readers that the discontinuity is not an artifact of nonlinearity in size effects.

In sum, this paper tackles an important question with a unique dataset, but its identification strategy hinges on strong functional-form assumptions. Strengthening the parametric evidence, clarifying the treatment definition, and exploring additional sources of variation would greatly improve the credibility and policy relevance of the findings.
