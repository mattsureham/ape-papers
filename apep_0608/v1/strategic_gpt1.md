## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple, policy-relevant question: when governments force firms to disclose gender-equality statistics, do firms actually change workplace outcomes, or do they just comply on paper? Using Japan’s 301-employee cutoff for mandatory disclosure, the paper argues that transparency changed female representation in management but did not change the gender wage gap, suggesting that disclosure works on margins managers can directly move and fails on margins rooted in deeper organizational structures.

A busy economist should care because disclosure mandates are now everywhere, and the broader question is not “does transparency matter?” but “what kinds of outcomes can transparency realistically change?” That is a first-order policy design question, not just a Japan question.

### Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Mostly, but not sharply enough. The current opening gets to the topic quickly, but it still reads like “here is a policy and here is a threshold.” The strongest version of the paper is not about the existence of a threshold; it is about the limits of transparency as a governance tool. The introduction should start with the substantive puzzle, not the institutional machinery.

It also needs to avoid overcommitting to “disclosure” as the sole treatment when the policy also involved action plans and targets. Even if the paper ultimately interprets the regime as primarily a transparency mandate, the intro should acknowledge that it is studying a disclosure-centered accountability package.

### The pitch the paper should have

Governments increasingly rely on transparency mandates to reduce workplace inequality, but we know little about whether making disparities public changes firms’ behavior or merely changes what they report. This paper studies Japan’s gender-equality reporting mandate at the 301-employee threshold and shows a sharp asymmetry: the policy increased women’s representation in management, but did not move the gender wage gap. The broader lesson is that transparency is most effective for outcomes that are visible, attributable, and directly under managerial control, and much less effective for outcomes generated by deeper features of the employment relationship.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### Contribution in one sentence

The paper’s core contribution is to show that a firm-level gender-transparency mandate appears to change promotion-related outcomes but not pay inequality, implying that disclosure is more effective on actionable organizational margins than on structurally determined ones.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3-4 papers?

Only partially. The paper does a decent job distinguishing itself from Denmark/Canada-style pay transparency results by arguing that Japan’s regime is weaker and therefore reveals limits of transparency. But the differentiation is still too institutional and not conceptual enough.

Right now the paper’s differentiation is:

- Denmark found wage-gap effects.
- Canada found wage compression.
- Japan finds no wage-gap effect but a management effect.

That is a start, but it is not yet a big contribution unless the paper more forcefully says: **the treatment margin matters**. Mandatory transparency is not one thing. Disclosure-only regimes, disclosure-plus-remediation regimes, and hard quotas are distinct policy technologies. This paper is most valuable if it helps build that taxonomy.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or filling a gap in a LITERATURE?

It is mixed, but too often sounds like the latter. “First RD evidence on Japan’s Act” is not an AER contribution. “Transparency changes some dimensions of inequality but not others, depending on managerial actionability” is a contribution about the world. The introduction should lean much harder into the latter and demote the “cleaner source of variation” framing.

### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what’s new?

They could, but only if they are charitable. In its current form, many would summarize it as “another quasi-experimental paper on disclosure and gender outcomes.” The paper does have a more interesting idea inside it — the asymmetry across outcomes — but it is not yet elevated enough above the empirical design.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

The biggest way to make it bigger is to show a **systematic gradient of actionability across outcomes**, not just one positive and one null result.

Concretely:

- Put more weight on a hierarchy of outcomes:
  - female management share
  - section-chief share
  - board share
  - wage-gap measures for all/regular/non-regular workers
- Then frame the contribution as: disclosure moves outcomes with direct managerial levers and clear accountability, but not outcomes that depend on job structure, hours, tenure, and contract type.

At present, the paper claims an actionability mechanism, but the broader set of outcomes seems awkwardly handled rather than integrated. In particular, the appendix appears to contain results on section-chief and board shares that do not obviously fit the clean “promotion yes, pay no” story. Those results are not side details; they are central to whether the proposed mechanism is persuasive. If some promotion-related outcomes move and others do not, that is not a nuisance — it is the real story.

A second way to make the contribution bigger would be to compare Japan explicitly to other disclosure regimes along institutional dimensions:

- disclosure only
- disclosure + action plans
- disclosure + sanctions
- quotas / hard targets

That comparison would move the paper from “Japan case study” to “what disclosure policy design actually does.”

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Governments increasingly require firms to disclose gender-related statistics because transparency is supposed to create reputational and internal pressure for change.

### Tension

We do not know whether disclosure changes actual workplace outcomes, and if it does, whether it works equally across different dimensions of inequality. A second tension is that prior evidence often focuses on pay outcomes or stronger regulatory packages, leaving unclear whether disclosure alone can move anything meaningful.

### Resolution

The paper finds a strong disclosure response and then an asymmetry in outcomes: female management representation rises, while the gender wage gap does not.

### Implications

Transparency is not a general cure. It may work when managers can take discrete, visible actions that outside stakeholders can monitor, but it may not work for outcomes produced by broader employment structures.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the beginnings of one, but it is not fully controlled. Right now the paper is oscillating between two stories:

1. a policy-evaluation story about Japan’s law, and
2. a conceptual story about what transparency can and cannot change.

The second is much stronger, but the paper keeps slipping back into the first.

More importantly, the paper risks looking like a collection of selected outcomes arranged to support a clean asymmetry. The appendix makes this problem more acute. If board share and section-chief outcomes do not line up neatly with the “managerial discretion” story, then the narrative arc needs to absorb that complexity rather than hide it.

### What story should it be telling?

The paper should tell this story:

- Transparency creates pressure to improve metrics.
- But not all metrics are equally manipulable or governable.
- Japan’s regime reveals which dimensions of gender inequality are responsive to soft accountability and which require harder institutional change.
- The important lesson is not just that one variable moves and one does not, but that the effectiveness of disclosure depends on the organizational production function of the outcome being disclosed.

That is the story an AER reader can use.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?

“I’d lead with: Japan forced firms above 300 employees to disclose gender-equality metrics, and this appears to have increased women in management by nearly 4 percentage points while leaving the gender wage gap unchanged.”

That is the cleanest and most discussable fact.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

They would lean in — briefly. The topic is timely, and the asymmetry is intuitive enough to spark interest. But the next question would come immediately, and this is where the paper currently feels vulnerable.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

They would ask: **Why management but not pay?** And then: **Do other career outcomes move too, or is this just one favorable measure?**

That second question is the crucial one. If the answer is “some related outcomes don’t move, or move the other way,” the paper has to confront that head-on. Otherwise, economists will infer that the story is overfitted to the headline result.

### If the findings are null or modest, is the null itself interesting?

Yes, the wage-gap null is interesting — but only if framed correctly. A null on pay is valuable if the paper convincingly argues that disclosure alone should not be expected to unwind gaps generated by hours, tenure, occupational sorting, and contract status. Then the null is not a failed experiment; it is informative evidence about the limits of light-touch regulation.

The paper is close to making that case, but it needs to do so more forcefully and with a cleaner comparative-policy framing.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

This is not primarily a “clean identification” editorial question. Strategically, the gap is threefold:

### 1. Framing problem
The science may be competent, but the story is not yet pitched at the right altitude. The paper is more interesting than “Japanese disclosure threshold paper,” but less coherent than “general theory of what transparency can change.” It needs to fully become the latter.

### 2. Scope problem
The paper is trying to support a broad claim — disclosure works on actionable outcomes — with a relatively thin set of headline outcomes. To belong in AER, it needs a more systematic map of outcomes or a much sharper institutional comparison showing why this regime differs from Denmark/Canada/UK.

### 3. Ambition problem
The paper is careful and sensible, but somewhat safe. The ambitious version would treat Japan as one observation in a broader policy-design argument about transparency versus remediation mandates. Right now it stops one step short.

### Is it a novelty problem?

Somewhat. Pay transparency and gender disclosure are already active literatures. So the novelty cannot just be “another mandate, another design.” The novelty has to be the **selective effectiveness** result and the policy-design taxonomy that follows from it.

### Single most impactful advice

If the author changes only one thing, it should be this:

**Reframe the paper around a general “actionability gradient” of disclosure effects across outcomes, and restructure the evidence so that all major outcomes either support, refine, or challenge that gradient in the main text.**

That would simultaneously fix the framing, clarify the contribution, and raise the paper’s ambition.
