## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple, policy-relevant question: when governments protect the visual environment around historic monuments, do nearby homeowners gain from nicer preserved surroundings or lose from tighter development constraints? Using France’s nationwide 500-meter monument buffer, the paper argues that heritage regulation has both effects: light-touch protection raises nearby property values, while stricter protection lowers them.

A busy economist should care because this is not really a paper about “beauty” per se; it is a paper about how a ubiquitous but understudied form of land-use regulation capitalizes into housing markets. The potentially interesting idea is that preservation policy is not one thing: its economic incidence depends on regulatory intensity.

### Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Not quite. The current opening is vivid and competent, but it takes too long to get to the genuinely interesting part. The paper initially sounds like a standard capitalization paper—does a boundary raise prices?—when the real hook is the sign reversal across protection intensity. That heterogeneity is the story. The pooled 2.6% effect is not.

The first two paragraphs should make the main economic question much sharper: **when does preservation function as an amenity, and when does it function as a regulatory tax?** Right now the introduction buries that under institutional detail and a generic ambiguity setup.

### The pitch the paper should have

“Historic preservation is usually treated as a bundled policy whose net effect on nearby housing prices is either positive or negative. This paper shows that the bundle matters: the same preservation regime can create an amenity premium under light oversight and a restriction penalty under strict oversight. Using France’s uniform 500-meter monument boundary and its two-tier system of protection, I show that property values rise near lightly protected monuments but fall near heavily protected ones, suggesting that preservation policy trades off neighborhood quality against development constraints.”

That is the pitch. It says what the paper is about, what is new, and why the result matters beyond France.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper’s contribution is to argue that historic preservation has opposite capitalization effects depending on regulatory intensity, with lighter monument protection generating a positive local amenity premium and stricter protection generating a negative restriction penalty.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?

Only partially. The introduction cites the standard hedonic/preservation literature, but the differentiation is still a bit generic: prior papers estimate “the net effect of designation,” while this paper “separates amenity from restriction channels.” That is a plausible distinction, but it needs to be made much more concretely.

The author should identify exactly what prior papers did and why the French setting changes the question. For example:

- U.S. historic district papers often estimate the capitalization effect of designation or district status.
- Those papers tend to conflate preservation amenity, neighborhood selection, and regulatory burden.
- This paper leverages within-country variation in **protection intensity under a common legal perimeter**, which is closer to a comparative statics exercise on stringency than a simple treated/untreated comparison.

That would be a stronger differentiation.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a gap in a literature?

It is currently framed as both, but too much of the prose still sounds like “the literature has not separately estimated X.” That is weaker than a world-facing framing.

The stronger framing is:

- Cities increasingly use preservation to shape the built environment.
- Economists do not know whether such rules increase neighborhood value or merely raise the cost of adaptation and redevelopment.
- The answer depends on the intensity of regulation.

That is a question about the world. The paper should lean harder into that.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?

At present, maybe, but not confidently. A smart economist might say: “It’s a spatial RD on monument boundaries in France and they find some positive capitalization, with heterogeneity by monument type.” That is not strong enough. They should instead say: “It shows preservation is not uniformly value-enhancing; stringency flips the sign.”

If the introduction leaves the reader with “another RD paper about local amenities,” the paper has undersold itself.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

A few ways, strategically:

1. **Make the object of interest regulatory stringency, not the boundary.**  
   The boundary is the empirical lever, but the paper’s claim to importance is about how the intensity of preservation regulation changes welfare incidence in housing markets.

2. **Tie the result more directly to housing supply and urban adaptation.**  
   Right now the paper gestures at land-use regulation, but somewhat timidly. If strict preservation lowers prices, is that because it impairs renovation value, redevelopment option value, or household composition? The paper need not fully answer that empirically to frame the stakes more ambitiously.

3. **Push beyond average sale price into margin-specific outcomes.**  
   Strategically, the contribution would feel larger if it connected to:
   - renovation-intensive properties,
   - redevelopment potential,
   - transaction volume,
   - composition of buyers/sellers,
   - new construction permits or alterations.
   
   Even one auxiliary outcome tied to mechanism would enlarge the paper considerably.

4. **Reframe monument type as a proxy for regulatory burden, not cultural prestige.**  
   Otherwise the reader may think the heterogeneity is just about nicer versus less nice monuments. The paper knows this and tries to address it conceptually, but it needs to be the central framing challenge.

Mostly **build on**, not attack.

It should say:

- The preservation literature is right that protected historic environments can command a premium.
- But those estimates are reduced-form bundles that mix amenity benefits and regulatory costs.

Relative to the land-use regulation literature, the paper should say:

- Most regulation papers focus on use, density, or quantity constraints.
- Preservation is a qualitatively different regulatory technology: it governs design, delay, and compatibility with the existing built environment.
- Yet it can still affect property values and possibly housing adaptation in ways economists care about.

That is a good bridge.

### Is the paper currently positioned too narrowly or too broadly?

A bit of both, oddly.

- It is **too narrow** in the sense that it reads like a specialized French institutional paper about ABF perimeters.
- It is **too broad** in the sense that it gestures toward “the broader literature on land-use regulation” without fully making that connection persuasive.

The right audience is not just preservation scholars and not all of urban economics. It is economists interested in how regulations that protect local public goods alter private asset values and urban adjustment margins.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?

Not unaware, exactly, but under-engaged with:

1. **Urban amenity and spatial equilibrium literature**  
   The paper is fundamentally about capitalization of local quality and local constraints.

2. **Design review / procedural regulation / discretionary permitting**  
   The ABF mechanism seems closer to discretionary review than to classic zoning. That is a useful conceptual literature to invoke.

3. **Option value of redevelopment / irreversible investment under regulation**  
   If strict oversight reduces value, one natural interpretation is that it taxes flexibility. That literature could sharpen the economic mechanism.

4. **Cultural economics / heritage economics**  
   If the author wants a broader readership, this should be present but not dominant.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?

Not fully. Right now it is having the conversation “what is the effect of historic preservation on nearby prices?” That is a legitimate but somewhat crowded and second-tier conversation.

The more impactful conversation is: **what kinds of land-use regulation create valuable neighborhood amenities, and when do they instead destroy option value and adaptation capacity?** Historic preservation is then the setting, not the whole point.

That reframing would materially improve its ceiling.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Governments protect historic environments to preserve beauty, identity, and local character. Such preservation may make neighborhoods more desirable, and housing prices often capitalize those benefits.

### Tension

But preservation also constrains what owners can do with their property. The key unresolved question is whether preservation primarily creates neighborhood amenity or imposes a regulatory burden—and whether that balance depends on how strict the protection is.

### Resolution

Using France’s 500-meter monument boundary and two-tier monument system, the paper finds a positive average capitalization effect overall, but more importantly a reversal across protection intensity: prices rise around lighter-touch monuments and fall around stricter ones.

### Implications

Preservation policy should not be evaluated as a yes/no intervention. The economic effects depend on stringency. For policymakers, the relevant margin is not simply whether to protect heritage, but how much discretion and constraint to impose on surrounding property owners.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the ingredients, but the arc is currently only **serviceable**, not sharp. The paper too often reads like a collection of RD results, validity tests, and heterogeneity tables rather than a paper marching toward one core idea.

The biggest narrative problem is that the paper’s most interesting result—the sign reversal—is introduced as “heterogeneity” after the main effect, rather than as the resolution to the paper’s motivating question. That is backwards. The sign reversal is the paper. Everything else is setup.

A better story would be:

1. Preservation is a bundle of amenity and restriction.
2. Existing work estimates the bundle’s net value.
3. France’s two-tier regime lets us observe how that net value changes with stringency.
4. It flips sign.
5. Therefore preservation policy sits on a knife-edge between public-good provision and regulatory burden.

That is a much cleaner narrative.

There is also a quieter but important issue: the paper itself includes several later caveats that muddy the clean early story. Even without adjudicating those empirically, the editorial point is that the introduction promises a cleaner answer than the paper later seems comfortable defending. The narrative should be rewritten to be a bit less triumphalist and a bit more disciplined: **the core evidence is suggestive about the margin of regulatory intensity**, not a sweeping estimate of the average value of monument-zone protection.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?

“Historic preservation can either raise or lower nearby housing values, and in France the sign flips with regulatory stringency: light protection raises prices, strict protection lowers them.”

That is the fact worth leading with.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

They would lean in—at least initially—because the sign reversal is intuitive, surprising, and economically meaningful. It connects two broad economist instincts that often compete: amenities matter, and regulation has costs.

The average 2.6% effect, by contrast, is not a dinner-party fact. Lots of local amenities and local regulations move prices by a few percentage points. The sign reversal is the memorable object.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Probably one of these:

- “How do you know stricter monuments mean stricter regulation rather than better amenities?”
- “Does this show up in renovation, redevelopment, or transaction volume?”
- “Is the negative effect really about reduced option value for owners?”
- “Is France special, or is this a general lesson about discretionary design regulation?”

Those are exactly the questions the paper should be written to anticipate.

### If findings are modest: is the modesty itself interesting?

The pooled estimate is modest and not especially exciting by itself. The paper is not a “small average treatment effect” paper; it is a “masking of opposite-signed effects” paper. That is where the value lies.

So the author should not overinvest in selling the average premium. The main effect is best treated as the pooled average of two opposing forces, not the headline result.

Not enough. A reader has to wade through too much before realizing what is genuinely new.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

Bluntly: in current form, this is **not yet an AER story**. The issue is not polish. It is that the paper does not yet know whether it is:
- a France-specific policy evaluation,
- a property-values capitalization paper,
- or a broader contribution on how discretionary preservation regulation trades off amenity against flexibility.

For AER, it needs to be the third.

### What is the main gap?

Mostly a **framing plus ambition problem**, with some novelty risk.

- **Framing problem:** The paper undersells the central idea and oversells a standard local-price effect.
- **Ambition problem:** The paper stops at property-value capitalization when the broader question is about the economic incidence of preservation regulation.
- **Novelty problem:** Without the intensity-based sign reversal framing, it becomes “another spatial boundary paper on local housing prices.”

### What would excite the top 10 people in this field?

A version of this paper that makes a cleaner, bigger claim:

> Preservation regulation creates value when it coordinates neighborhood aesthetics at low compliance cost, but destroys value when it meaningfully constrains adaptation and redevelopment; France’s two-tier system reveals where that threshold lies.

To really get there, the paper likely needs either:
1. stronger mechanism evidence tied to regulatory burden, or
2. a more ambitious conceptual framing that uses the housing-price evidence to answer a first-order question about urban regulation.

Even absent new data, the paper can improve a lot by centering the contribution on **the non-monotone effect of preservation stringency**.

### The single most impactful piece of advice

**Rebuild the paper around the idea that preservation is a non-monotone form of land-use regulation whose effect flips with stringency; make the classé/inscrit reversal the headline, and treat the pooled boundary estimate as supporting context rather than the main result.**
