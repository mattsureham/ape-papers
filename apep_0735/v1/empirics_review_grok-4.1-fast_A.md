### 1. Idea Fidelity
The paper closely pursues the original idea manifest, exploiting the sharp 500-meter ABF regulatory boundary around historic monuments in a spatial RDD to estimate effects on property values from DVF transactions, with appropriate data sources (DVF and Monuments Historiques database) and a sample size exceeding 2.7 million transactions near 44,240 monuments. Key elements like the administrative arbitrariness of the boundary, donut-hole tests, placebo tests (at 200-800m), and heterogeneity by monument type (classé vs. inscrit) are faithfully implemented. However, it misses two promised components: (i) Sitadel construction permit data, which could have directly tested supply-side channels (e.g., permit delays/denials), and (ii) a DiD complement using the 2016 LCAP reform's adapted perimeters for quasi-experimental variation on regulatory relaxation. The research question on "aesthetic gatekeeping" and property values is well-matched, though reframed slightly toward amenity vs. restriction penalties.

### 2. Summary
This paper uses a spatial regression discontinuity design (RDD) at France's uniform 500-meter historic monument boundaries to estimate the effect of binding ABF aesthetic regulation on residential property prices, finding a pooled 2.6% premium inside the zones that reverses to a 2.5% discount near strictly protected *classé* monuments and a 5.0% premium near lighter *inscrit* ones. The heterogeneity highlights competing amenity and restriction effects, contributing novel evidence on how regulatory intensity shapes capitalization in housing markets. It advances beyond U.S. historic district studies by leveraging a national, fixed-radius regime.

### 3. Essential Points
**(1) Validity threats undermine identification credibility.** The McCrary density test rejects continuity ($p=0.007$), covariate balance fails for floor area ($p=0.008$), and multiple placebo cutoffs show significant jumps (e.g., 300m, 600-800m, all $p<0.01$), suggesting unmodeled spatial gradients or sorting rather than a clean discontinuity. Donut-hole tests are inconsistent (e.g., ±50m yields -7.7%, $p<0.001$), and the parametric commune-FE specification flips to a null/negative (-2.1%). Authors must provide convincing diagnostics (e.g., higher-order polynomials, spatial fixed effects, or matching on observables) or bound the bias to credibly claim causality; without this, the RDD does not isolate the regulatory effect from monument proximity amenities.

**(2) Sorts by nearest monument conflate multiple treatments.** Assigning treatment based on distance to the *nearest* monument ignores overlap (properties often near multiple monuments, with 46,700+ sites covering dense urban areas). This induces mechanical sorting bias, as denser areas (with more monuments) have higher baseline prices. Authors must test/robustify using multi-monument exposure (e.g., sum of distances or count within radii) or restrict to isolated monuments (e.g., >1km from others), matching the manifest's intent for "discontinuities."

**(3) Empirical approach mismatches supply-side research question.** The DVF price outcome captures demand capitalization but cannot distinguish amenity from restriction channels without supply data. The manifest promised Sitadel permits to quantify construction effects (delays/denials), yet this is absent. Heterogeneity by *classé*/*inscrit* is suggestive but correlational (stringency unmeasured). Implement a supply outcome (e.g., permits/starts per area) or mechanism tests (e.g., interact with renovation flags) to link prices to "aesthetic gatekeeping"; otherwise, reject as the question exceeds the evidence.

### 4. Suggestions
The paper is well-written, concise, and AER:Insights-appropriate in style, with clear institutional detail, replication-friendly code hints (e.g., rdrobust), and engaging framing (e.g., Versailles anecdote). The heterogeneity finding is novel and policy-relevant, decomposing net effects in a way U.S. literature overlooks—expanding this could elevate it. Below are targeted improvements, prioritized by impact.

**Strengthen RDD implementation and diagnostics (primary focus for revision).** Report density plots (bin-scatter for distance histogram) and covariate RDD graphs alongside outcome jumps to visualize issues; the text summary alone is insufficient. Adopt the multi-scale bandwidth selector from Calonico et al. (2020) *rdrobust* updates and test IK/IK2 optimal bandwidths alongside CCT. For placebos, fit a fully-flexible global polynomial (e.g., 5th-order spline) across 0-1000m to residualize the gradient before local estimation—this absorbs smooth proximity effects better than separate placebos. Simulate bias under plausible sorting (e.g., via Imbens-Kalyanaraman placebo inversion) to quantify worst-case contamination. Include maps of discontinuities by commune or region (e.g., Paris vs. province) using 100m bins to show spatial heterogeneity.

**Incorporate promised variation for sharper ID.** Revive the 2016 LCAP reform: identify municipalities with adapted perimeters (public data likely via Sitadel or ABF reports) and run a triple-difference (post-2016 × adapted × distance<500m) or boundary-specific RDDs comparing default vs. adapted cuts. This directly tests "relaxing aesthetic gatekeeping unlocks supply," per manifest. Match Sitadel permits (~400k records) to transactions by parcel/year: estimate RDD on permit density/probability or days-to-approval near 500m, then correlate with price effects. This grounds the price reversal in mechanisms (e.g., *classé* sites show 20%+ permit denial jumps?).

**Refine sample and specifications.** Trim to transactions 2015-2024 for pre-reform stability, excluding 2023-2024 volatility (noted in robustness). Control for time-varying confounders: add year×commune FE or interact distance with year trends. For overlap, compute a "minimum distance" exposure (to nearest *classé* or *inscrit*) and re-run subsamples excluding multi-monument parcels (threshold: >2 within 1km). Heterogeneity table is strong—extend to urban/rural (INSEE grid cells), high/low density (pop/km²), or buyer types (repeat vs. first-time, if inferable from DVF). Test interactions: τ × MonumentHeight (proxy visual impact) or τ × Topography (elevation variance for visibility).

**Enhance interpretation and external validity.** Quantify economic magnitudes: the 2.6% pooled premium implies €100-200/m² at medians; decompose aggregate as weighted average of *classé*/*inscrit* (e.g., 14.8k vs. 31.9k monuments). Discuss spillovers: do outer-zone prices rise from restricted inner supply? Link to EU debates (e.g., compare to UK's Article 4 directions). Appendix SDE table is innovative—standardize heterogeneity too (e.g., *classé* SDE = -0.031). Robustness: cluster SE at monument or 1km grid; winsorize prices at 1%/99%; restrict to houses/apartments separately throughout.

**Minor polish.** Fix typos (e.g., "CourdeCassation2020" → "Cour de Cassation"; abstract N=2.7M vs. text 5.2M raw). Add outcome graphs (rdplot outputs). Bibliography: cite French sources (e.g., LCAP text) and recent spatial RDDs (e.g., Basse et al. 2023 on European zoning). Simulate power for small effects in narrow bandwidths. With these, it could be a strong accept—promising work on a timely policy friction.
