## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple but potentially important question: when a place loses a large, earmarked stream of resource revenue, does violence rise because public capacity falls, or fall because the rent itself was attracting predation? Using the abrupt 2020 elimination of Mexico’s Fondo Minero, the paper argues for the latter margin: overall crime does not move much, but homicides decline in mining municipalities, consistent with the idea that visible local fiscal rents can become targets for organized violence.

A busy economist should care because this is a sharp inversion of the usual “more public money, less crime” intuition. If true, it speaks to a broad question about state capacity, natural-resource governance, and criminal contestation: under weak institutions, transfers may not just fail to buy safety—they may create something worth fighting over.

### Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Reasonably well, but not optimally. The opening is better than average: it has a question, a reversal, and a policy setting. But it still reads a bit like a standard reduced-form public finance paper with a crime outcome attached, and only gradually reveals the bigger point. The first two paragraphs should do less institutional throat-clearing and more conceptual work. The paper’s real hook is not “we study a fund in Mexico”; it is “public money can become loot.”

### The pitch the paper should have

Here is the version the paper should lead with:

> Governments routinely transfer money to poor or resource-affected communities on the assumption that more local fiscal capacity reduces crime. But in places where organized groups can capture procurement, contracts, or local office, those transfers may instead create a valuable prize worth fighting over. This paper studies that tradeoff using the sudden abolition of Mexico’s Fondo Minero, a large earmarked transfer to mining municipalities.
>
> We show that eliminating this transfer did not increase overall crime and was followed by a decline in homicides in mining communities. The central implication is not just about one Mexican fund: it is that in weak institutional environments, the design and visibility of local public revenue can shape violence by creating—or removing—contestable rents.

That is the AER-facing pitch. It elevates the paper from “DiD on a Mexican program” to “new lens on why public money can generate violence.”

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper’s contribution is to argue that removing an earmarked local resource transfer can reduce lethal violence by eliminating a contestable fiscal rent, rather than increasing crime through lower public spending.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?

Not yet sharply enough. The paper cites several broad literatures, but the differentiation is still fuzzy because the contribution sits at the intersection of three existing ideas:

1. resource rents can fuel violence;
2. intergovernmental transfers can worsen corruption or political selection;
3. organized crime in Mexico contests local governments and local rents.

The paper needs to be much crisper about what exactly is new relative to each:

- Relative to **resource curse/conflict** papers: the novelty is not simply “resource rents matter.” It is that **subnational public transfers derived from resource revenues**, and specifically **earmarked procurement flows through municipal governments**, may create violence through a different institutional channel than mine-site appropriation or commodity-price shocks.
- Relative to **fiscal transfer/corruption** papers: the novelty is not “transfers distort politics.” It is that the downstream consequence may be **violent contestation**, not merely corruption or bad politicians.
- Relative to **Mexico violence** papers: the novelty is not “organized crime affects municipalities.” It is that **fiscal architecture itself** may alter the local prize structure.

At present, a smart economist could probably say: “It’s a DiD paper on a Mexican mining fund, with a homicide decline they interpret as rent-target removal.” That is not bad, but it is not yet “obviously new” in top-journal terms.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or filling a gap in the LITERATURE?

Mostly about the world, which is good. The introduction opens with an empirical-world claim—more money should reduce crime—and then asks when that logic fails. That is the right instinct.

But the framing still occasionally slips into literature bookkeeping (“contributes to three literatures”). For AER purposes, the paper should more forcefully plant itself around a world question:

**When do public transfers stabilize communities, and when do they create valuable rents that generate violence?**

That question is stronger than “we add to resource curse / fiscal transfers / Mexico crime literatures.”

### Could a smart economist explain what is new after reading the introduction?

Not quite cleanly. They would probably understand the main result, but the novelty would still sound somewhat narrow: “another quasi-experimental paper showing an unusual crime effect in a developing-country setting.” The mechanism term “violence dividend” is memorable, but it risks sounding like branding unless tied to a larger conceptual distinction.

The introduction should make explicit that the paper is about **the political economy of visible, place-based fiscal rents**. That is the idea someone can repeat to a colleague.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Specific ways to enlarge the contribution:

1. **Make the object of study broader than one fund.**  
   Right now the paper is very tied to Fondo Minero. The larger object is “earmarked, geographically concentrated, procurement-based transfers.” The paper should say explicitly that Fondo Minero is a test case of a more general class of transfers.

2. **Show the violence is political/fiscal, not merely criminal.**  
   The biggest way to make the paper bigger is to connect homicide declines to outcomes more tightly linked to municipal capture:
   - mayoral candidate killings,
   - attacks on local officials,
   - procurement irregularities,
   - contractor concentration,
   - public works spending,
   - municipal election violence,
   - cartel presence or fragmentation.
   
   Even one of these would greatly elevate the mechanism from suggestive to field-shaping.

3. **Lean into heterogeneity that speaks to theory.**  
   The paper would become more important if the homicide decline were strongest where the rent-seeking mechanism should be most intense:
   - weak local state capacity,
   - higher preexisting cartel presence,
   - more procurement-intensive municipalities,
   - politically competitive municipalities,
   - municipalities with more mining intensity or larger public works exposure.
   
   That would make it a paper about institutional conditions, not just one average treatment effect.

4. **Tie to transfer design, not transfer levels.**  
   The strongest version of the paper is not “less money reduced homicide.” It is “the *design* of public transfers determines whether they become contestable rents.” To support that, the paper needs more emphasis on earmarking/procurement/visibility, ideally contrasted with other transfer types.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Governments often use transfers to compensate communities, build local public goods, and reduce deprivation. In standard thinking, taking that money away should worsen local conditions and increase crime.

### Tension

But in weak institutional settings, earmarked, visible transfers may themselves become targets of organized crime through procurement capture, extortion, and violent competition for municipal control. So the same fiscal flow can either buy order or create a prize.

### Resolution

The abolition of Mexico’s mining fund did not raise total crime and appears to reduce homicides in recipient municipalities. The pattern across crime types suggests the disappearance of a violence-generating rent, rather than a broad deterioration in local public safety.

### Implications

The security effects of public transfers depend not only on how much money is sent, but how it is structured, how visible it is, and whether local institutions can protect it from predation. Transfer design may therefore be a violence-prevention question, not just a public finance question.

### Does this paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the bones of one, yes. This is not a random collection of tables. There is a real story here.

But the arc weakens in two ways:

1. The paper tells the story too confidently relative to the evidence presented.
2. The narrative pivots between “null total crime,” “homicide decline,” and “violence dividend” in a way that can feel unstable.

The paper should stop trying to make total crime the headline. It is plainly not the headline. The headline is the homicide result and what it says about contestable local rents. Total crime is supporting context, not the marquee finding.

### What story should it be telling?

The story should be:

> Public transfers have two opposing effects in weakly governed places: they can improve public goods, but they can also create appropriable rents. Fondo Minero is a clean case where the second force may dominate for lethal violence. When the rent disappeared, homicides fell.

That is a coherent story. It is stronger than “we found a null on total crime plus a homicide decline.”

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?

“I’d lead with this: when Mexico abolished a large earmarked mining transfer to local governments, homicide fell in the affected municipalities rather than rising.”

That is a good dinner-party fact.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

Initially, they would lean in. It is a counterintuitive result, and economists like results that reverse the sign of the baseline intuition.

But the next 30 seconds matter. If the explanation becomes “because maybe the fund was a rent-seeking target,” they will ask whether this is really about mining rents, municipal procurement, cartels, or just one-off Mexican noise. So the paper gets attention, but it does not yet fully hold it.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Likely one of these:

- “Why homicide specifically?”
- “Do you see effects where cartel presence is stronger?”
- “Can you show it’s really about municipal capture/procurement?”
- “Is this about transfer design or just about mining municipalities being weird?”

That is revealing. The paper’s success depends on answering the second-layer question, not just the first-layer surprise.

### If the findings are modest or null, is the null interesting?

The null on total crime is mildly interesting, but not enough by itself for AER. “A big fiscal withdrawal did not change total crime” is not a headline result unless used to discipline theory. The homicide decline is what makes the paper matter.

So the paper should not pretend the null is independently major. The null is useful because it sharpens the compositional point: the effect is not a broad crime response but a selective violence response.
