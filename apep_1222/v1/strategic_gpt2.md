## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a sharp and interesting question: when a government abruptly stops sending earmarked resource revenues to local communities, does violence rise because public capacity falls, or fall because the rents themselves stop attracting organized predation? Using the elimination of Mexico’s Fondo Minero, the paper argues for the latter in one important margin: total crime does not rise, but homicides fall, consistent with the idea that visible fiscal rents can become objects of violent contestation.

A busy economist should care because this is not just “crime in Mexico” or “a transfer shock.” It potentially reframes how we think about decentralized public money in weak-governance environments: transfers may provide public goods, but they may also create appropriable rents that attract violence.

**Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Reasonably well, but not optimally. The opening is stronger than most papers, but it still leads with a broad “more money should mean less crime” frame and then quickly dives into institutional detail. The real hook is more provocative and more general than the current version lets on: **can public money itself become a target of organized violence?** That should be front and center immediately.

### The pitch the paper should have

> Governments send targeted revenues to resource-producing communities to compensate them for the costs of extraction and to reduce local deprivation. The standard view is that removing such transfers should worsen public safety by reducing local public goods and economic opportunities. But in weak institutional environments, visible and earmarked fiscal flows may themselves attract violent competition by organized groups seeking control over contracts, procurement, and local government.
>
> This paper studies that possibility using the abrupt abolition of Mexico’s Fondo Minero, which cut off earmarked mining revenues to municipalities that hosted extractive activity. We show that the withdrawal of these funds did not increase overall crime and was followed by a decline in homicides, suggesting that some fiscal transfers generate a “violence dividend” when removed because they eliminate a concentrated rent-seeking target. The broader implication is that the security effects of public transfers depend not just on how much money governments send, but on how contestable the money becomes once it arrives.

That is the AER-facing version: world question first, then setting, then result, then implication.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution in one sentence:**  
The paper’s contribution is to argue that earmarked local resource transfers can increase organized violence by creating contestable rents, so that eliminating such transfers may reduce homicides even when standard fiscal-crime models predict the opposite.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?
Only partly. The paper names several adjacent literatures, but the differentiation is still a bit mushy. Right now the reader could summarize it as: “a DiD paper on a fiscal shock in Mexican mining municipalities with a homicide result.” That is not enough. The paper needs to more sharply distinguish itself from:

1. **Resource curse / commodity shocks and conflict** papers: these study prices, extraction, or windfalls, often through armed-group incentives.
2. **Intergovernmental transfers and corruption/governance** papers: these study political selection, corruption, and fiscal institutions.
3. **Crime and state presence in Mexico** papers: these study trafficking routes, political competition, and organized violence.
4. **Police/public spending and crime** papers: these emphasize deterrence and local state capacity.

The novel intersection is not simply “resource rents matter for violence.” It is: **publicly administered, earmarked, subnational fiscal flows can themselves become violently contested objects.** That is the real contribution, and the current draft does not yet isolate it crisply enough.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or filling a gap in a LITERATURE?
Mostly framed as a world question, which is good. The best parts say: standard models predict one thing; the world may work differently when public money is lootable. That is much stronger than “this contributes to three literatures.” The introduction should lean harder into the world question and use the literature only to clarify the contrast.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the intro?
Not quite reliably. A smart economist would likely say: “It’s a paper showing that ending a mining transfer in Mexico didn’t raise crime and may have reduced homicide.” That is accurate but not yet memorable. The introduction needs to get them to say instead: **“It shows that targeted public transfers can attract violent rent-seeking, so removing them can reduce homicides.”**

### What would make this contribution bigger?
A few specific ways:

- **Better mechanism-facing outcomes.** The current crime decomposition helps, but it still stops short of directly tying the story to municipal procurement, political violence, contractor extortion, or attacks on local officials. If the paper could show anything about mayoral killings, attacks on politicians, procurement irregularities, public works contracts, or violence around election/control of municipal governments, the contribution would become much larger.
- **Stronger conceptual framing around state-administered rents.** Right now it sounds like an oddity of one Mexican fund. It needs to become a paper about when public transfers pacify versus destabilize.
- **A more comparative framing.** Why should this travel beyond mining? The authors should say explicitly that the same logic may apply to disaster funds, oil revenue sharing, infrastructure block grants, or anti-poverty funds in weak states.
- **A clearer contrast with direct transfers to households versus project-based transfers to local governments.** That comparison would make the paper much more policy-relevant and conceptually important.

The biggest upside is not more outcomes per se; it is outcomes that specifically map to the “contestable local rents” mechanism.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Governments often send resource revenues or compensatory transfers to local communities. The conventional expectation is that these funds should improve welfare and public safety by financing public goods and reducing deprivation.

### Tension
But those same funds may be visible, predictable, and concentrated enough to become prizes for organized criminal groups, especially when they flow through local governments via procurement-heavy channels. So the core tension is: **are transfers instruments of development, or objects of predation?**

### Resolution
In the Fondo Minero case, eliminating the transfer did not raise total crime and appears to have reduced homicides. The paper interprets this as evidence that the fund created violent rent-seeking opportunities that disappeared when the fund was abolished.

### Implications
The design of transfers matters. In weak-governance settings, channeling money through earmarked local projects may unintentionally create violent rents. Public finance and security are therefore linked through institutional design, not just budget size.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
Yes, but it is weakened by two things:

1. The paper sometimes lapses into being a **result bundle**: total crime null, homicide negative, a few robustnesses, then a discussion.  
2. It spends a lot of time narrating caveats in a way that dilutes the central story before the reader has fully absorbed it.

The story should be simpler and stronger:

- **Question:** Can targeted public money attract violence?
- **Case:** Mexico’s abrupt abolition of Fondo Minero.
- **Fact:** Homicides fall, while broad crime does not rise.
- **Interpretation:** Organized violence was contesting the rents.
- **Implication:** Transfer design affects security.

The paper should tell that story relentlessly. Right now, it occasionally sounds like it half-disbelieves its own main contribution.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party?
“When Mexico abruptly stopped sending earmarked mining revenues to local governments, homicides fell rather than rose.”

That is a good lead. It has surprise, policy relevance, and a clean conceptual tension.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
They would lean in—at least initially—because the sign is surprising. The immediate reaction is: “Wait, how could cutting local funds reduce violence?” That is exactly the kind of reaction a strong paper wants.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Almost certainly: **“Why?”**  
Then: **“Was it because the money was being captured locally?”**  
And after that: **“Does this generalize beyond this one fund in Mexico?”**

That tells you what the paper needs to do. The key vulnerability is not that the result is boring; it is that the mechanism and generalizability are not yet persuasive enough.

### If findings are modest or partly null, is the null itself interesting?
The total-crime null is mildly interesting, but not enough to carry the paper. The paper is right to center the homicide result. The null becomes valuable only as part of the composition story: **crime does not broadly rise despite a negative fiscal shock, because the main movement is a decline in organized violence rather than a general change in economic crime.**

So the paper should not sell itself as “a precise null on total crime.” That is a side fact. The actual product is the homicide result plus the rent-contestation interpretation.
