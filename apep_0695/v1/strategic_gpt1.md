## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper studies whether the Dominican Republic’s 2013 mass denationalization of roughly 210,000 Haitian-descent Dominicans left a detectable mark on the country’s aggregate labor market. The headline finding is no: national measures of informality, unemployment, labor force participation, and school enrollment do not worsen, which the paper interprets as evidence that a severe citizenship shock can devastate a small minority while remaining invisible in macro statistics.

A busy economist should care because the paper is trying to make a broader point about what aggregate data can and cannot see when the policy shock is legally dramatic but demographically concentrated. That is potentially interesting. But in its current form, the paper’s actual contribution is narrower than its moral urgency suggests: it is an aggregate null about one country episode, and the paper itself repeatedly explains why the null was almost mechanically expected.

### Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Partly. The opening is vivid and memorable. The question is stated clearly. But the introduction initially sounds like a paper about the labor-market consequences of denationalization; only later does it become clear that the real contribution is an aggregate null plus an argument about “statistical invisibility.” That shift matters. Right now the paper leads with a dramatic event and then delivers a result that the authors themselves say national data were never well-suited to detect.

The first two paragraphs should be much more explicit that this is **not** primarily a paper about estimating the full economic cost of denationalization. It is a paper about whether one of the most extreme legal exclusions a state can impose shows up in the national statistics economists often use to judge labor-market harm.

### The pitch the paper should have

“Can a state erase the citizenship of 210,000 people without leaving a trace in aggregate economic data? Studying the Dominican Republic’s 2013 denationalization ruling, I show that national labor-market indicators exhibit no detectable deterioration, not because the policy was benign, but because even severe harm concentrated in a small marginalized group is too small to move macro aggregates. The paper’s contribution is to show the limits of aggregate economic indicators as measures of exclusion: policies can be economically devastating for targeted populations while remaining invisible in the national data.”

That is the strongest available pitch from the existing paper. It is still not fully an AER pitch, but it is cleaner and more honest than the current one.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper argues that the Dominican Republic’s mass denationalization shock produced no detectable break in national labor-market outcomes, illustrating how aggregate data can fail to register severe harms borne by a small targeted minority.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3-4 papers?

Not convincingly enough. The paper differentiates itself mainly on method—trend-adjusted interrupted time series, placebo tests, power calculation—relative to one close predecessor. That is not a strong top-journal differentiator. “First time-series econometric analysis” is a weak contribution claim. AER papers do not get in because they apply a slightly cleaner design to an already intuitive aggregate question.

The closest neighbors appear to be:
- **Amuedo-Dorantes and Antman (2017?)** or related work on Dominican citizenship policy and schooling/employment
- Immigration enforcement papers on legal status and labor-market outcomes, e.g. **Kossoudji and Cobb-Clark (2002)**, **Orrenius and Zavodny / Orrenius (2015)**, **Dustmann et al.**
- Recent papers on enforcement spillovers and fear, e.g. **East et al.**, **Alsan et al.**
- Political science / statelessness work such as **Belton (2020)** and **Chen (2023)**

Relative to those, this paper is not yet saying, “they thought X, but the world actually works like Y.” It is saying, “here is an aggregate test of a dramatic episode, and the aggregate series do not move.”

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or as filling a gap in a LITERATURE?

Too much as a literature/method gap. The introduction literally says no economics paper has brought formal time-series methods to bear. That is not a world question. The world question is stronger: **Can citizenship stripping matter enormously for lives while not mattering at all for aggregate labor-market statistics?** That framing is better. It turns the paper from “no one has run this design” into “aggregate indicators are poor detectors of exclusionary policy when harm is concentrated.”

### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what’s new?

Right now they would probably say: “It’s an ITS paper on the Dominican denationalization ruling. At the aggregate level they find basically nothing, which they interpret as the affected group being too small to move national labor outcomes.”

That is intelligible, but it is still perilously close to “another reduced-form paper on a policy shock with null aggregate effects.” The introduction does not yet produce a crisp, memorable “new fact” that would survive retelling.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Most importantly: **show the divergence between aggregate invisibility and concentrated harm, rather than just asserting it.** Specific ways:
1. **Micro or subnational evidence**: province-level labor-market outcomes, ENI microdata, or repeated cross-sections that allow exposure-based comparisons.
2. **A direct mechanism/outcome that better matches denationalization**: formal employment, possession of ID documents, school progression, access to health services, civil registration, mobility, wages, or sectoral downgrading among Haitian-descent populations.
3. **A stronger comparison**: compare this denationalization episode to other immigration enforcement shocks where targeted populations were larger or more diffuse, to show when aggregate data do and do not pick up exclusion.
4. **A reframing around measurement**: instead of selling a policy-effect estimate, sell a paper about the failure of standard macro indicators to capture rights-based exclusion. But that framing requires more than a back-of-the-envelope; it needs evidence that the missing harms exist below the aggregate level.

As written, the paper’s ambition is limited by the fact that it proves national aggregates do not move while repeatedly noting they were not expected to move.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

A state stripped citizenship from a large number of native-born residents of Haitian descent in 2013. The economics literature says legal status matters for labor markets; losing it should plausibly worsen employment opportunities and push people toward informality.

### Tension

The affected population is large in humanitarian terms but small in national demographic terms. So there is a real puzzle: can an enormous rights shock be economically meaningful for those affected yet undetectable in aggregate data?

### Resolution

The aggregate series show no negative break. Informality measures even continue trending down. The paper resolves this by arguing that the harmed population is too small to move national indicators.

### Implications

Economists should not infer harmlessness from aggregate nulls when harm is concentrated in a marginalized minority; national statistics may systematically miss exclusionary policy damage.

### Does this paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the bones of one, but not yet a fully convincing one. The setup is strong, the tension is promising, and the implications are potentially interesting. The problem is the resolution is underpowered narratively, not just statistically: “we find no aggregate effect, and here is why aggregate data were unlikely to show one” risks feeling like a story that collapses into its own caveat.

At moments the paper reads like a collection of:
- a dramatic institutional background,
- aggregate ITS estimates,
- a power argument,
- suggestive province facts,
- and a discussion of what micro data could do.

That is not yet a satisfying top-journal story. It is a paper pointing toward a stronger paper.

### What story should it be telling?

The clean story is:

1. **Citizenship is labor-market infrastructure.**
2. **But aggregate indicators are a poor lens for concentrated exclusion.**
3. **The Dominican episode is a stark demonstration of this measurement failure.**

To tell that story credibly, the paper needs evidence at two levels:
- aggregate invisibility,
- plus some direct evidence of localized or group-specific disruption.

Without the second, the paper is mostly a demonstration that national aggregates are coarse.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?

“I can show you a case where a government stripped citizenship from 210,000 people and the national labor-market statistics barely budged.”

That is a good dinner-party opening line. People will initially lean in.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

They would lean in for the event and the paradox. But the next question comes fast: **“Okay, so what happened to the affected people?”** If the answer is mostly “we can’t observe them directly here, but aggregate arithmetic suggests they were invisible,” interest will fade.

The current paper has a compelling first sentence and a weaker second sentence. That is a problem for AER.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Almost certainly:
- “Do you actually show the affected people were harmed, or just that the aggregate didn’t move?”
- “Can you isolate more-exposed provinces or Haitian-descendant workers?”
- “Is the contribution that denationalization has no macro consequences, or that macro data are the wrong tool?”

Those questions expose the paper’s current strategic vulnerability.

### If findings are null or modest, is the null itself interesting?

Potentially yes, but only if the paper makes the null conceptually sharp. Nulls are interesting when they overturn a strong prior or reveal a broader principle. Here the null is less interesting because the paper itself tells us it was expected ex ante due to arithmetic and low power. That undercuts the suspense.

The null could be interesting if framed as:
- not “we looked and found nothing,” but
- “this episode reveals a systematic failure of aggregate indicators to register concentrated exclusion.”

But that claim requires stronger support than a power calculation and moral intuition.
