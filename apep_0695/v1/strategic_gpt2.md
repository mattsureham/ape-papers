## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper studies a dramatic policy shock: in 2013, the Dominican Republic retroactively stripped citizenship from roughly 210,000 people of Haitian descent, many of them Dominican-born. The paper asks whether an event of that scale left any detectable imprint on national labor market outcomes, and finds essentially no aggregate labor-market deterioration—arguing that this is because the harmed population was too small and marginalized to move national statistics.

Why should a busy economist care? In principle, because it speaks to a bigger question than the Dominican Republic: when states remove legal status from a minority population, do the consequences show up in the aggregate economy, or are severe harms fully hidden by macro averages?

**Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?** Not quite. The opening is vivid, but the paper does not get quickly enough to the genuinely interesting claim. Right now the introduction reads like a conventional policy-evaluation paper about a specific episode. The stronger pitch is not “I estimate aggregate labor market effects of a shocking event.” It is: **some of the largest rights shocks in modern economies can be economically devastating and yet leave no trace in aggregate data—and that fact is itself substantively important.**

### The pitch the paper should have

A stronger first two paragraphs would say something like:

> In 2013, the Dominican Republic retroactively revoked citizenship from roughly 210,000 people of Haitian descent—one of the largest episodes of mass denationalization in the modern Western Hemisphere. For affected individuals, losing citizenship meant losing access to formal work, schooling, identification documents, and political rights.  
> 
> This paper asks a broader economic question: when the state strips legal status from a marginalized minority, do the consequences register in aggregate labor market statistics? Using national time-series data from 2005–2023, I show that they do not. The core finding is not that denationalization was harmless, but that aggregate labor-market data can be structurally incapable of detecting severe harms concentrated in a small population. The paper’s contribution is to turn that invisibility—from a limitation into the object of study.

That is the version that belongs in a top-journal conversation. The current intro gets there eventually, but too late and too defensively.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:**  
The paper argues that a massive citizenship shock in the Dominican Republic had no detectable effect on aggregate labor-market indicators because harms were concentrated in a small marginalized group that national statistics are not built to see.

That is a real contribution in principle. But the paper does not yet differentiate it cleanly from adjacent work.

### Is the contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?

Not really. The paper currently defines novelty as:
- first time-series econometric analysis of mass denationalization,
- better trend adjustment than a prior paper,
- a two-shock design,
- explicit power arithmetic.

That is **methodological differentiation**, not conceptual differentiation. “We do a cleaner aggregate pre/post than Amuedo-Dorantes et al.” is not an AER contribution. “We show that aggregate labor-market statistics can systematically miss severe rights shocks when they hit small marginalized populations” is much stronger.

The paper needs to differentiate itself from at least four neighboring conversations:

1. **Immigration enforcement / legal status papers**  
   Those papers typically study deportation risk, DACA/TPS/legalization, E-Verify, raids, or undocumented status. This paper’s distinctiveness is that the targeted population is **native-born and denationalized**, not cross-border migrants.

2. **Papers on citizenship/legal status as economic infrastructure**  
   The contribution should be that citizenship is not just a legal category; its removal can sharply alter labor-market access while remaining invisible in aggregate indicators.

3. **Papers using aggregate data to study immigration or enforcement shocks**  
   The paper’s distinction should be that the absence of an aggregate effect is not evidence of no effect, but evidence of compositional invisibility.

4. **The Dominican Republic / Haitian-descent literature**  
   Here the paper should not oversell “first econometric study.” It should say: existing work documents severe micro harms and political/legal consequences; this paper shows why aggregate economic statistics are a poor instrument for observing those harms.

### World question or literature-gap question?

At present, too much of the contribution is framed as filling a literature gap: “no paper in economics has brought formal time series methods…” That is weak.

The paper should be framed as answering a **world question**:

> When governments remove citizenship or legal status from a minority population, should economists expect to see macro labor-market consequences? Or can large rights shocks be economically consequential yet aggregate-invisible?

That is a stronger and more general question.

### Could a smart economist explain what is new after reading the intro?

At the moment, probably not cleanly. They might say:  
“It's a short ITS paper on the Dominican denationalization case, using aggregate outcomes, finding nulls because the affected group was only 2%.”

That is not enough. You want them to say:  
“It’s a paper about how aggregate statistics miss concentrated legal-status shocks. The Dominican case is a sharp illustration.”

Right now it still risks sounding like “another reduced-form paper on a policy shock with null aggregate results.”

### What would make the contribution bigger?

Most concretely:

- **Reframe the contribution around invisibility rather than null effects.**
- **Bring in outcomes that more directly index exclusion from citizenship**, not just broad labor-market aggregates: formal-sector registration, ID possession, school continuation, access to public services, benefit take-up, documentation status, wage formality. Even if not available here, the paper must organize itself around these margins.
- **Use subnational variation more ambitiously** if possible. The paper gestures to provincial exposure but cannot exploit it. That is a major missed opportunity in terms of narrative scale.
- **Connect to a broader class of shocks**: citizenship revocation, legal-status loss, statelessness, registry exclusion, deportability. If the paper cannot add data, it should at least elevate the general lesson.

The best version of this paper is not “denationalization had no aggregate effect.” It is “aggregate economics can fail to register severe institutional violence.”

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

### Closest neighbors

The closest neighbors appear to be:

1. **Amuedo-Dorantes and coauthors on Haitian-descended populations in the Dominican Republic**  
   This is the direct country-specific neighbor and should be treated seriously, not just as a weaker pre/post benchmark.

2. **Kossoudji and Cobb-Clark (2002)** on IRCA/legal status and wages  
   A classic legal-status paper.

3. **Orrenius and Zavodny / related immigration enforcement work**  
   For how enforcement affects labor markets, informality, and native/immigrant outcomes.

4. **Dustmann, Schönberg, Stuhler and related work** on labor-market effects of migration/legal status  
   As a benchmark for labor supply, informal-sector pressure, and incidence.

5. **Recent papers on fear, deportation risk, and spillovers**, e.g. **Alsan et al.**, **East et al.**  
   These are closer in spirit because they show that legal vulnerability changes behavior even without actual removal.

Also relevant, though outside mainstream labor:
- **Political science / law / human rights work on statelessness and denationalization** such as Belton, Chen, and related scholarship.
- Potentially literature on **state capacity, legibility, and the economics of identity documentation**.

### How should the paper position itself relative to those neighbors?

Mostly **build on and redirect**, not attack.

- Build on the immigration enforcement literature by saying: that literature studies shocks to migrants’ legal vulnerability; this paper studies an even more extreme shock—revocation of citizenship from the native-born.
- Build on the statelessness literature by saying: legal scholars have documented rights harms; economists should notice that those harms may not appear in the macro series they usually rely on.
- Build on the labor/informality literature by saying: the relevant margin may be hidden in population composition and measurement.

It should **not** define itself as “we improve on Amuedo-Dorantes by adding trend breaks.” That is too small and too combative in the wrong way.

### Is the paper positioned too narrowly or too broadly?

Paradoxically, both.

- **Too narrowly** in method: it reads like a national ITS application.
- **Too broadly** in aspiration: it occasionally suggests it answers big questions about citizenship and labor markets, but the evidence base is only a handful of national annual series.

It needs a tighter conceptual scope and a more honest empirical scope.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?

The paper should speak more explicitly to:

- **Legal status / documentation / identity economics**
- **State capacity and administrative exclusion**
- **Distributional invisibility in aggregate statistics**
- **Measurement and welfare of marginalized groups**
- Possibly **race/ethnicity and labor-market exclusion in Latin America**
- **Development literature on informality and legal identity**

At present, the “aggregate invisibility” idea feels under-theorized. There is likely useful work in development, public economics, and political economy on populations omitted or blurred by administrative and survey systems.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?

Not yet. It is currently having the conversation:
> “Can interrupted time series detect an aggregate labor-market break after TC/0168?”

That is too small.

The more interesting conversation is:
> “What kinds of institutional harms are systematically invisible in macroeconomic data, and what does that imply for how economists study exclusion?”

That broader conversation could attract labor, development, political economy, and public economists. Right now the paper is not fully reaching for it.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
A state revokes citizenship from a large number of people. Standard economics would predict reduced access to formal employment, shifts into informality, weaker schooling incentives, and labor-market dislocation.

### Tension
Yet the affected group is a small share of the population. So there is a tension between **severe individual consequences** and **possibly undetectable aggregate consequences**. That tension is the paper’s real narrative engine.

### Resolution
The paper finds no detectable deterioration in national labor-market aggregates after the ruling. It interprets this as evidence that the shock was macro-invisible, not harmless.

### Implications
Economists should be cautious about inferring no harm from null aggregate estimates when shocks are concentrated in small, marginalized populations. Citizenship/legal status may be essential economic infrastructure for those affected while still leaving no macro trace.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the bones of one, but right now it is **partly a collection of results looking for a story**.

The story should be:

1. This is an extreme, morally and institutionally consequential policy shock.
2. Standard labor economics predicts large effects for affected individuals.
3. But aggregate data may be incapable of seeing those effects.
4. We test the aggregate margin and find no break.
5. The absence of aggregate movement is substantively revealing: it exposes how macro data erase concentrated harm.
6. This changes how we should interpret nulls in policy settings involving marginalized minorities.

That is a clean arc. The current draft gets distracted by:
- “first ITS in economics,”
- long institutional detail,
- table-by-table narration,
- and some overclaiming around “the null is the finding” without fully elevating the conceptual stakes.

Also, the paper occasionally sounds as though it has discovered a surprise, when in fact it repeatedly says the null was expected ex ante from arithmetic. That is fine, but then the paper should embrace that structure:
- not “surprising null,”
- but “revealing null.”

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?

I would say:

> “The Dominican Republic stripped citizenship from 210,000 people, and if you looked only at national labor-market statistics, you would conclude basically nothing happened.”

That is a good opening fact. People will lean in.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

They would **lean in initially**, because the institutional episode is striking. But the next question is decisive.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

They would ask:
> “Interesting—but what do we learn beyond the obvious fact that 2% of the population won’t move national aggregates?”

And that is exactly the problem. Unless the paper answers that question convincingly, interest will dissipate.

The answer needs to be:
- that this is not trivial arithmetic,
- because economists routinely use aggregate outcomes to evaluate policy salience,
- and the paper shows a substantive mismatch between rights harms and macro observability.

### If the findings are null or modest: is the null itself interesting?

Potentially yes. But only if the paper makes a compelling case that the null is informative about **measurement, welfare, and visibility**, not just about this specific policy event.

At present, it is halfway there. Sometimes it does a good job saying “the null is itself the finding.” Sometimes it sounds like a failed search for an effect. The repeated emphasis on power calculations helps, but top-journal null-result papers need a stronger positive intellectual payoff.

Right now the payoff is:
- “the group was too small to detect.”

That is true, but not enough.

The payoff needs to be:
- “aggregate data can systematically fail as welfare metrics when exclusion is concentrated in low-visibility populations.”

That is a broader and sharper lesson.

   Right now it reiterates the null. It should instead widen the lens: what does this imply for economists’ reliance on aggregate indicators in settings of exclusion, statelessness, or legal-status precarity?

Not enough. The reader learns the substantive punchline reasonably early, but the truly interesting idea—that aggregate measures erase targeted harm—is not presented as the organizing principle early and forcefully enough.

Honestly, not many. The two-shock design is only worth foregrounding if it matters conceptually. It currently doesn’t deepen the story much. Some, but not enough. It is memorable in tone, but it mostly restates the central result. It should end by telling the reader what this implies for empirical economics more generally.

One more blunt point: the **acknowledgment that the paper was autonomously generated** is fatal in its current form for editorial positioning. Whatever one thinks substantively, that is not helping the paper clear the first screen.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

At the moment, this is **far from AER**.

Why?

### The main gap is a combination of framing, scope, and ambition.

- **Framing problem:** The best idea in the paper is “aggregate invisibility of concentrated legal-status harm.” The paper only partially commits to that idea.
- **Scope problem:** The evidence is too thin for the size of the claim. A handful of national annual aggregates is not enough to sustain an AER-level statement about citizenship and labor markets.
- **Novelty problem:** “First ITS on this episode” is not top-journal novelty.
- **Ambition problem:** The paper is content with showing no aggregate break and explaining why. That is sensible, but top-field readers will want either richer data, a more general framework, or a sharper conceptual payoff.

### What is the gap between current form and something that would excite the top 10 people in this field?

An AER-version of this paper would likely need one of two things:

1. **Much stronger data and design**  
   Microdata or subnational outcome data that actually trace the affected population or high-exposure areas, allowing the paper to show both:
   - no aggregate effect,
   - but large concentrated effects.
   
   That would be powerful: “macro null, micro devastation.”

2. **A much stronger conceptual contribution**  
   If the data cannot improve, the paper must become a broader conceptual paper about when aggregate statistics fail as welfare measures under targeted exclusion. But that is hard to do persuasively with one narrow case and thin evidence.

Right now, the paper sits awkwardly between those two versions.

### Single most impactful advice

**Rebuild the paper around the idea of “aggregate invisibility of concentrated institutional harm,” and either bring in data that show the hidden micro effects or scale back the claim.**

If the author can only change one thing, it should be this:
> **Stop selling the paper as an econometric estimate of a policy shock and start selling it as evidence about the limits of aggregate data in detecting concentrated legal-status harms.**

That will not by itself make it AER, but without that shift it definitely is not.
