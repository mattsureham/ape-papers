## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper studies a central policy question in labor economics: when governments expand “formal” employment by allowing much more flexible contracts, do workers actually become better protected, or does formality itself get watered down? Using Brazil’s 2017 labor reform and municipality-level exposure to sectors that adopted intermittent contracts, the paper argues that places more exposed to the reform saw lower average wages in the formal sector, with little clear evidence of employment gains.

A busy economist should care because the paper is trying to speak to something bigger than Brazil: whether labor-market formalization and labor-market protection can come apart. That is a potentially important idea. If true, it matters for how we interpret formality, labor regulation, and the welfare implications of “flexibilization” reforms in many countries.

### Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Not quite. The opening is reasonably strong, but the paper starts by describing an institutional novelty and then immediately overreaches into “economic welfare of workers,” which the actual results do not establish cleanly. The paper’s real core is narrower and stronger: the reform appears to increase formal-sector coverage through a contract type that lowers average formal-sector pay, suggesting that formalization may not mean what economists often assume it means.

The first two paragraphs should say that much more plainly. They should not lead with the reform as a curiosity; they should lead with the conceptual puzzle.

### The pitch the paper should have

“Economists and policymakers often treat formalization as synonymous with better jobs. But reforms that expand formal status through highly flexible contracts may increase the number of formal workers while weakening the content of formality itself. This paper studies Brazil’s 2017 introduction of intermittent contracts—formal jobs with no guaranteed hours—and shows that municipalities more exposed to sectors that adopted these contracts experienced declines in average formal-sector wages, with no comparably clear employment gains. The broader implication is that formalization can expand on paper while labor protection erodes in practice.”

That is the paper’s best version. It is sharper, more general-interest, and closer to what the evidence can actually support.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper’s contribution is to show that a major labor reform can expand a new form of formal employment while lowering average wages in the formal sector, highlighting that “formalization” and worker protection need not move together.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?

Only partially. The paper gestures at three literatures—labor flexibility, Bartik designs, and formalization—but does not clearly tell the reader which conversation it wants to enter first. Right now the contribution risks sounding like: “another policy-evaluation paper using cross-area exposure to a reform.” The phrase “formalization paradox” helps, but the paper has not yet earned that phrase by clearly distinguishing itself from adjacent work.

The differentiation problem is especially acute because the paper is not really about:
- the overall Brazil labor reform,
- employment effects of deregulation broadly,
- or Bartik econometrics.

It is specifically about **the meaning of formal status when the legal bundle attached to formal work changes**. That is a strong contribution, but it needs to be isolated and defended much more explicitly.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or as filling a gap in a LITERATURE?

It is mixed, and that hurts it.

The stronger framing is about the world:
- Does formalization improve jobs if the new formal contract strips away core protections?
- What happens when policy expands formal employment through zero-hour-style arrangements?

The weaker framing is literature-gap language:
- “no prior work has used RAIS universe data…”
- “contributes to the Bartik literature by documenting pre-trend violations…”

That latter material is not what gets this into AER. The world question is.

### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what’s new?

At present, maybe not cleanly enough. They might say: “It’s a shift-share DiD on Brazil’s labor reform and finds wages fell where intermittent contracts were more common.” That is competent, but it does not yet sound like a distinctive general-interest paper.

What you want them to say is: “It shows formalization can be misleading—Brazil expanded formal jobs via contracts with zero guaranteed hours, and the result was lower pay inside the formal sector rather than clear employment gains.”

That is a much better sentence. The introduction should be written to produce that sentence.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Be specific:

1. **Move from average formal wages to the structure of formal jobs.**  
   Right now the headline result is average wages in the formal sector. That invites an immediate “composition” response, which the paper itself acknowledges. The contribution becomes much bigger if it can show whether the reform changed:
   - wage distribution within formal jobs,
   - hours volatility,
   - entry into formality from informality/nonemployment,
   - incumbent workers’ wages,
   - separation/re-hiring patterns,
   - worker-level earnings instability.

2. **Separate relabeling from genuine job creation.**  
   The paper is currently strongest as a reinterpretation of formalization statistics. That gets much sharper if it can say whether intermittent contracts created new opportunities or mainly substituted for standard formal jobs.

3. **Connect to a broader class of labor-market reforms.**  
   The paper repeatedly mentions zero-hours contracts and platform work, but the evidence remains country-specific and somewhat narrow. The contribution would feel larger if the framing were: Brazil is a clean case of a broader phenomenon—formal labor-market expansion via reduced guarantees.

4. **Lean into the concept, not the method.**  
   The concept is bigger than the design. The more the paper sounds like “a Bartik application,” the smaller it feels.

- dual labor markets,
- the economics of hours volatility and schedule uncertainty,

If the authors want this to matter beyond Brazil and development, they need to speak to that literature much more clearly.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?

Not yet. The current draft is trying to have three conversations at once:

1. Brazil labor reform,
2. Bartik identification,
3. formalization and worker protection.

The third is the right one. The first is the setting; the second is background noise.

The most impactful framing here is not “another labor reform paper” but “a paper about how legal formalization can cease to mean what economists think it means.”

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Before this paper, the dominant instinct in economics is to view movement into formal employment as progress: more legal coverage, more protections, better jobs, better productivity, better measurement. Even when labor reforms increase flexibility, there is often an implicit hope that formality expands and welfare improves.

### Tension

Brazil’s reform creates a category that scrambles that intuition: a worker can be formally employed and still have zero guaranteed hours. That creates a conceptual tension. If the reform expands formal employment through contracts that weaken guarantees, should we interpret that as labor-market improvement or as dilution of protections? The empirical tension in the paper is that the formal sector’s average wage falls, while employment effects are much less clear.

### Resolution

The paper’s resolution is: exposure to intermittent contracts lowers average wages in the formal sector, especially during the pandemic, and there is no comparably decisive evidence of formal-employment expansion. Therefore, formalization through highly flexible contracts may compress wages rather than clearly improve job quality.

### Implications

The implications are potentially important:
- measured formalization can overstate improvements in worker welfare,
- labor reforms can change the *content* of formality, not just its incidence,
- policymakers should not equate more formal contracts with more protection.

### Does this paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the raw ingredients, but the execution is uneven. Right now it reads like:
- institutional setup,
- design explanation,
- results table,
- a discussion that retrofits a larger story.

So: **more a collection of results looking for a story than a fully disciplined narrative**.

### What story should it be telling?

It should tell one story, relentlessly:

**The legal category of formal employment changed. Once that happened, growth in formal employment no longer necessarily signaled better jobs. Brazil’s intermittent contracts show how formalization can rise while the quality of formal work deteriorates.**

Everything in the paper should serve that story:
- institution: why this reform changed the meaning of formality,
- data: why RAIS is useful for tracking that change,
- results: average wage decline, weak employment gains, stronger effects in downturns,
- implications: why economists and policymakers should rethink formalization metrics.

Anything that does not advance that arc should be compressed.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?

“Brazil introduced formal labor contracts with zero guaranteed hours, and the places most exposed to them ended up with lower average wages in the formal sector, not clear employment gains.”

That is the lead.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

They would lean in initially, because the institutional fact is genuinely interesting and the broader conceptual hook is strong. “Formal jobs with zero guaranteed hours” is memorable.

But they will lean back quickly if the paper cannot answer the obvious second-order question.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Immediately: **“Is this just composition, or did incumbent workers actually get worse jobs?”**

That is the paper’s central vulnerability strategically—not as an identification issue, but as a significance issue. If the answer is just “formal-sector averages fell because lower-paid workers entered formality,” then the result is still interesting, but much less dramatic than the current rhetoric suggests. If instead the reform degraded the quality of formal employment more broadly, then the paper gets much bigger.

### If the findings are modest: is the modest result itself interesting?

Yes, but only if framed correctly.

The employment effects are modest/unclear. That does not kill the paper. In fact, a strong version of the paper is: **the reform clearly changed the composition and meaning of formal work, even if it did not obviously expand employment.**

That is interesting because it changes how we read official labor-market progress. But the paper has to lean into that interpretive contribution rather than sounding disappointed that the employment result is not stronger.

The paper plays it a bit safe empirically relative to the ambition of the claim. If you are going to argue that formalization itself is a misleading metric, you need more than an average-wage result and a suggestive employment null.

If they can only change one thing, that is it.

If they do that, the paper becomes much more than “a DiD on Brazil.” It becomes a paper about how legal categories in labor markets can become detached from the protections economists assume they encode.
