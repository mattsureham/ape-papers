**1. Idea Fidelity**

The paper stays largely faithful to the original manifest. It correctly treats the January 1 2014 lifting of transitional work‑permit restrictions as the shock, uses a continuous‑treatment DiD exploiting pre‑2014 county‑level emigration intensity, and draws on the same data sources (Eurostat regional employment, population, and GDP series). The exposure variable, however, diverges from the manifest’s description. The manifest prescribed a treatment based on *pre‑2014 emigration to Germany* (derived from the POP309A × Germany‑share), whereas the paper uses *overall population decline between 2002‑2013* as the intensity measure. Both variables are correlated, but the latter is a noisier proxy for the relevant migration network and conflates other demographic trends (e.g., natural decline, internal migration). This shift weakens the identification strategy because the “treatment” is no longer a direct measure of the shock’s size. Apart from that, the paper follows the planned design (continuous‑DiD, event study, construction‑sector triple‑difference) and the intended outcomes (employment, population, employment‑rate, GDP per‑capita).

**2. Summary**

The paper documents a “composition paradox” in Romania’s 42 counties after the 2014 removal of EU‑15 work‑permit restrictions: counties with higher pre‑2014 population loss experience larger absolute drops in employment and population, yet per‑capita outcomes (employment‑to‑population ratio, GDP per‑capita) improve because the population declines faster than employment. A sector‑specific triple‑difference shows a positive effect for construction employment—the sector most linked to Romanian migration to Germany—providing a more credible channel‑specific estimate. The authors acknowledge pre‑trend violations for aggregate employment and frame the composition findings as the main contribution.

**3. Essential Points**

1. **Treatment Definition Mis‑alignment** – Using overall population decline as the exposure variable instead of the pre‑2014 Romanian‑to‑Germany emigration share departs from the manifest and raises concerns about omitted‑variable bias. Counties that lost population for reasons unrelated to German migration (e.g., declining birth rates, internal out‑migration) will be mis‑classified as “high‑exposure,” contaminating the estimated β.

2. **Parallel‑Trends Violation Not Fully Resolved** – The event‑study shows significant pre‑trend differences for employment (2008 coefficient ≈ 0.15, p ≈ 0.06) and placebo break tests (2011, 2012) reject the parallel‑trends assumption for the main outcome. While the authors are transparent about this, they nonetheless present the employment‑level coefficient as a “causal” effect without a robust alternative specification that restores identification.

3. **Sector‑Level Triple‑Difference Still Relies on the Same Exposure Proxy** – The DDD isolates construction but still interacts the same θ (population decline) with post‑treatment. If θ is a poor measure of the German‑linked shock, the DDD may simply be picking up differential trends in construction for counties that were already diverging, rather than a clean supply‑shock channel.

Given the seriousness of (1) and (2), the paper cannot be accepted in its current form. It should be **rejected** pending major revisions that (i) replace the exposure measure with the intended pre‑2014 Romanian‑to‑Germany emigration rate, (ii) provide a credible identification strategy that passes parallel‑trend checks (e.g., synthetic‑control, propensity‑score weighting, or a staggered‑treatment design using actual year‑by‑year inflows), and (iii) re‑estimate the sectoral DDD with the corrected treatment.
