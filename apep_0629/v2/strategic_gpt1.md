## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper uses a custom language model trained on Congressional floor speech to ask a simple but interesting question: when legislators speak, are they responding to the debate unfolding around them, or just delivering pre-scripted remarks? It proposes a new measure—the “Deliberation Index,” based on the gap between speech predictability from speaker identity alone and predictability from prior debate context—and uses it to compare the House and Senate.

A busy economist should care because the paper is really about whether institutional rules shape the structure of political communication: do tighter procedures produce more scripted speech, less deliberation, or paradoxically more direct responsiveness? That is a live political-economy question, and the paper has a potentially scalable measurement tool.

**Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Not quite. The opening is intellectually serious and reasonably clear, but it leans too quickly into Habermas, “democratic theory,” and perplexity mechanics. That framing risks making the paper sound like computational political theory rather than positive political economy. For AER, the first two paragraphs should lead with the world question: how legislative institutions shape the nature of speech and responsiveness, and why existing text methods cannot see that.

**What the first two paragraphs should say instead:**

> Legislatures differ not just in what politicians say, but in whether floor speech is actually responsive to the ongoing debate. In some institutions, speeches are largely pre-packaged performances; in others, what one speaker says constrains what the next speaker says. That distinction matters for how we understand legislative institutions, agenda control, and the functioning of representative democracy—but existing measures of political speech mostly capture content, ideology, or polarization, not conversational responsiveness.
>
> This paper introduces a scalable way to measure that responsiveness using the predictability of speech in sequence. Training a language model on three decades of U.S. Congressional debate, we compare how predictable a speech is from the speaker alone versus from the prior debate context. The difference—the Deliberation Index—captures how much the conversation itself shapes what comes next. We show that House speech is more predictable than Senate speech overall, but also more tightly linked to preceding turns, suggesting that strong procedural control can produce speech that is simultaneously more formulaic and more conversationally coupled.

That is the paper’s real pitch. It is stronger than the current introduction because it foregrounds institutions, not the tool.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution in one sentence:**  
The paper introduces a new, scalable measure of conversational responsiveness in legislatures and shows that the U.S. House is more predictable but also more context-dependent than the Senate.

Only partially. The introduction does distinguish itself from work on polarization, readability, and rhetorical style, but the differentiation is still mostly methodological: “they score texts independently; we model sequence.” That is true, but not yet enough. A top-journal contribution needs to sound less like “a new NLP metric” and more like “a new empirical object with substantive implications for institutional political economy.”

The closest neighbors appear to be:
- **Gentzkow, Shapiro, and Taddy (2019)** on measuring polarization/divergence in congressional speech.
- **Spirling (2016)** on language complexity in Parliament.
- **DQI / Steiner et al.** on deliberation quality.
- Possibly work on domain-specific political language models or rhetorical uniqueness (the cited **Zhou 2024**, though this is less canonical economically).

The paper does not yet crisply say: *those papers measure ideology/style/content; we measure sequential dependence generated by institutions*.

### Is the contribution framed as a question about the world or about filling a literature gap?
Right now, it straddles both, but too often tilts toward the literature and the tool. The stronger version is clearly a world question:

- **Strong framing:** Do legislative rules change whether floor speech is performance or interaction?
- **Weak framing:** No one has yet used autoregressive perplexity to measure deliberation.

At present, the paper is too close to the second.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?
They could probably say: “It uses language-model perplexity to measure whether congressional speeches respond to previous speeches.” That is decent. But many would still reduce it to: “another text-as-data paper on Congress, with a new metric.” The introduction is not yet forceful enough about why the metric changes the substantive question.

### What would make the contribution bigger?
Several possibilities:

1. **Frame it around institutions, not deliberation theory.**  
   “Habermas at scale” is niche. “How procedural control changes the structure of legislative speech” is bigger and more AER.

2. **Exploit within-Congress institutional contrasts more aggressively.**  
   The House–Senate comparison is interesting but broad and somewhat familiar. Bigger would be:
   - high-stakes vs low-stakes debates,
   - closed-rule vs open-rule debates,
   - majority-controlled agenda periods,
   - crisis periods,
   - committee vs floor speech,
   - lame-duck sessions or leadership-controlled episodes.
   
   Even conceptually, the paper should *advertise* these margins as where the measure matters.

3. **Make the object of interest more obviously economic/political-economic.**  
   Right now the outcome is “predictability.” Bigger would be connecting predictability/responsiveness to:
   - agenda control,
   - legislative bargaining,
   - polarization,
   - representation,
   - information aggregation.
   
4. **Clarify whether the surprising result is substantive.**  
   The biggest substantive hook is not that the House is more predictable; that is unsurprising. The hook is: **the House may be more formulaic overall yet more responsive turn-by-turn.** That is the counterintuitive result worth centering.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Legislative speech is often treated as content—ideology, polarization, readability, rhetoric—but less is known about whether speeches are actually responsive to each other in real time. House and Senate institutions differ sharply in procedural control, and those institutional differences may shape not just *what* is said but *how speech unfolds sequentially*.

### Tension
There are two competing intuitions:
- Strong procedural control should make speech more scripted and less deliberative.
- But procedural control may also force tighter engagement with the immediately preceding turn.

Existing measures cannot adjudicate this because they do not capture turn-by-turn dependence.

### Resolution
The paper builds a sequential predictability measure and finds:
- House speech is more predictable overall than Senate speech.
- Prior debate context materially improves prediction of most speeches.
- The House appears more context-responsive than the Senate despite being more formulaic overall.

### Implications
Institutional design shapes conversational structure in non-obvious ways. More tightly controlled legislatures may not simply suppress interaction; they may transform it into a more constrained but more locally responsive form. More generally, text-as-data methods can recover new empirical objects about institutions, not just ideology or style.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
It has the ingredients, but the arc is underdeveloped. Right now it reads somewhat like:
1. Here is perplexity.
2. Here is a new index.
3. Here are two descriptive facts.
4. Here is a discussion of what the facts might mean.

That is serviceable but not yet elegant. The paper is still too much a collection of measurement ideas and descriptive outputs looking for the strongest story.

### What story should it be telling?
This one:

> Legislatures differ in whether speech is performance or interaction. We introduce a way to measure that distinction. Applying it to Congress, we find that the more tightly controlled chamber is more scripted overall but more tightly coupled from turn to turn—revealing that procedural control can compress and structure responsiveness rather than eliminate it.

That is a genuine narrative. It has setup, puzzle, finding, and implication.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?
“Using a language model trained on Congressional speech, we find that House speeches are more predictable than Senate speeches—but also more shaped by what the previous speaker just said.”

That is the right lead because it contains both the intuitive finding and the surprising reversal.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
A decent share would lean in—**if** you say it that way. If you instead lead with “we introduce a Deliberation Index based on marginal and conditional perplexity,” phones come out immediately.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Probably one of these:
- “Is this really measuring deliberation, or just topic continuity and procedural formula?”
- “What exactly about House procedure generates higher responsiveness?”
- “Can you tie this to specific institutional changes or rule differences?”
- “Does this move with polarization, divided government, or crises?”

Those are good follow-up questions because they point to the paper’s opportunity. The result is interesting enough to invite curiosity.

### If findings are modest: is the modesty itself interesting?
The findings are not null, but they are still somewhat modest descriptively. The paper does make a decent case that a positive Deliberation Index is informative. But “85% of turns have DI > 0” is not, by itself, dinner-party material. It sounds a bit mechanical—of course context helps predict speech. The stronger hook is the **comparative pattern** across institutions and time, not the mere positivity of the index.

So the paper should de-emphasize the “85% positive” fact as a headline and emphasize the **House vs Senate decomposition**.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

At present, the gap is mostly **framing plus ambition**, with some **scope** concerns.

### Is it a framing problem?
Yes, primarily. The paper has an interesting empirical object and a potentially surprising comparative result, but it is framed like a measurement paper for computational politics. AER would need it to feel like a paper about political economy and institutional design that happens to use a new measurement tool.

### Is it a scope problem?
Also yes. The evidence currently supports a descriptive claim, but the paper’s substantive ambition remains limited. It needs either:
- richer institutional variation,
- sharper comparative tests,
- or a more developed set of implications about legislative procedure.

As written, it is a clever application to one setting. For AER, it likely needs to become a broader statement about institutions shaping communication.

### Is it a novelty problem?
Somewhat. “Use language models on political speech” is not novel enough. The novelty must be the **sequential conversational object** and what it reveals substantively. If similar descriptive patterns could have been guessed ex ante, then the paper needs the surprising part—the House being more responsive—to carry more weight.

### Is it an ambition problem?
Yes. The paper is competent but safe. It introduces a measure, applies it cleanly, and reports sensible facts. But it does not yet push hard enough on the deeper question its own findings raise.

### Single most impactful advice
**Reframe the paper around the paradoxical institutional finding—tight procedural control produces speech that is more scripted overall but more tightly responsive turn-by-turn—and make the Deliberation Index a tool for answering that political-economy question, not the question itself.**

That is the one change that would most improve its odds.
