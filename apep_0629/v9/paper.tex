\begin{abstract}
\noindent
We train a language model from scratch on U.S. Congressional floor debate (1994--2014) and use its perplexity---the effective number of plausible next words---to measure how legislative speech unfolds in conversational context. We decompose perplexity into a speaker-identity component and a debate-context component; their gap, the Deliberation Index, captures how much the preceding conversation helps predict the next speech. In 2015--2024 data excluded from model training, House speech is 3--8 points more predictable than Senate speech, yet the House has a \textit{higher} Deliberation Index ($+2.76$ vs.\ $+2.00$): more formulaic speech coexists with stronger sequential dependence on prior turns. An event study of 635 FEMA disaster declarations provides suggestive evidence that the measure tracks salient public events---perplexity spikes by 3.9 points ($t = 4.2$) in the week following a declaration, then overshoots below baseline.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

The U.S. House and Senate govern the same country under starkly different rules. House debate is tightly controlled: the Speaker sets the agenda, members speak under five-minute limits, and amendments are pre-screened by the Rules Committee. Senate debate is open: senators hold the floor at will, filibusters are possible, and amendments are unrestricted \citep{perssonTabellini2003, lee2009}. These differences are well documented for their effects on legislation, polarization, and bargaining \citep{mccarty2006, jenkins2006}. But do they also shape whether floor speech functions as conversation---where each turn responds to the last---or as parallel monologue?

We answer this by measuring the \textit{predictability} of legislative speech. We train a language model from scratch on Congressional floor debate (1994--2014; 386 million tokens, 1,081 identified speakers) and analyze its predictions on 2015--2024 data excluded from gradient updates but used for checkpoint selection (Section~\ref{sec:model}). Because the model has seen only Congressional text, its perplexity---the effective number of plausible next words \citep{shannon1948}---measures properties of Congress, not of the internet. We decompose perplexity into three levels: how predictable a speech is given the full preceding debate (\textit{conditional}), how predictable it is from speaker identity alone (\textit{marginal}), and the gap between the two---the \textit{Deliberation Index}, which isolates the contribution of debate context.

The central finding is a paradox. House speech is more predictable than Senate speech in every year from 2015 to 2024---a persistent 3--8 point gap consistent with tighter procedural control. Yet the House has a \textit{higher} Deliberation Index ($+2.76$ vs.\ $+2.00$): debate context helps predict the next turn \textit{more} in the House than in the Senate. More formulaic does not mean less sequentially dependent. One interpretation is that House rules compress speaking time and produce tight sequential coupling within a narrow register, while the Senate's looser structure allows longer, self-contained speeches that are individually less predictable but less tethered to what came before. This chamber comparison is descriptive, not causally identified: differences in topic composition, speech length, or member characteristics could contribute.

Three additional findings support the measure. The Deliberation Index is positive in 85\% of turns ($\DI = +2.52$ overall), consistent with floor debate functioning as conversation rather than parallel monologue. An event study of 635 FEMA disaster declarations provides suggestive evidence that salient events disrupt speech predictability at daily frequency: perplexity spikes by 3.9 points in the first week ($t = 4.2$), then overshoots below baseline. And the model reveals genuine speaker fingerprints: individual identification accuracy is 80 times the random baseline, while a TF-IDF classifier shows a structural break at 2011 that the neural model does not, illustrating that perplexity captures sequential dynamics beyond vocabulary.

Existing computational approaches measure what legislators \textit{say}---vocabulary divergence \citep{gentzkow2019}, linguistic complexity \citep{spirling2016}, rhetorical uniqueness \citep{zhou2024}---but score each text independently, ignoring the sequential structure of debate. Hand-coded deliberation measures \citep{steiner2004, bachtiger2019} capture conversational engagement but require thousands of coding hours on small samples. Our approach asks a different question: does the preceding conversation help predict the next turn? This bridges scalable text analysis and deliberation measurement, providing a new behavioral margin on which institutions differ.

\section{Related Literature}

\textit{Institutions and legislative behavior.} A large literature studies how legislative rules shape outcomes: agenda-setting power in the House versus the Senate \citep{perssonTabellini2000, perssonTabellini2003}, partisan conflict beyond ideology \citep{lee2009}, and negative agenda control via procedural gatekeeping \citep{jenkins2006}. These studies focus on votes, bills, and amendments. Our contribution is a new behavioral margin: we measure the \textit{informational structure of speech itself}---whether each turn depends on the prior debate or could have been delivered regardless---and document how this structure differs across institutional environments.

\textit{Computational political text analysis} has produced powerful tools for measuring what legislators say. \citet{gentzkow2019} measure vocabulary divergence between parties, with classifier accuracy rising from 55\% to 83\% over a century. \citet{spirling2016} tracks readability across two centuries of UK Parliament. \citet{zhou2024} use perplexity from fine-tuned GPT-2 to measure presidential rhetorical uniqueness. But these approaches score each text independently; the sequential structure of debate plays no role, and fine-tuning carries contamination from pre-training data. \citet{aroyehun2025} document a shift from evidence-based to intuition-based language in congressional speech, using text-level classifiers rather than sequential models.

\textit{Deliberation measurement} following \citet{steiner2004} and the Discourse Quality Index captures exactly what democratic theory cares about---whether legislators justify claims and acknowledge counter-arguments---but requires thousands of coding hours on small samples. \citet{bachtiger2019} call for scalable alternatives. Automated extensions \citep{fournierTombs2021, flores2024} inherit the DQI's strengths and its sample-size constraints.

\textit{Domain-specific political language models}---RooseBERT \citep{evrard2025}, ParlBERT \citep{klamm2022}, PoliBERTweet \citep{kawintiranon2022}---are powerful classification tools but use masked architectures incapable of computing perplexity, the left-to-right predictability that captures how debate \textit{unfolds over time}.

What is missing is the combination: an autoregressive model that reads debate sequentially, computes the predictability of each turn in its conversational context, and is free of contamination from pre-training on external text.

\section{Data}

We construct a corpus of U.S. Congressional floor debate spanning thirty years (1994--2024): 473 million tokens across 38,006 conversations involving 1,701 identified speakers. We draw on two public sources: the Congressional Record from GovInfo \citep{govinfo} (2011--2024), which we parse via the \texttt{unitedstates/\allowbreak{}congressional-record} parser \citep{unitedstates_parser} into topic-level conversations; and the Eugleo/\allowbreak{}us-congressional-speeches dataset \citep{eugleo2023} (1994--2010), which provides earlier speeches grouped into day-level conversations. We link each speaker to party, state, and chamber via the public \texttt{congress-legislators} dataset.

We split temporally: training on 1994--2014 (386M tokens) and analyzing 2015--2024 (87M tokens, exclusively GovInfo). The 2015--2024 period serves dual roles: it provides the validation loss for early stopping during training (Section~\ref{sec:model}) and all reported empirical analyses. This overlap means the overall perplexity level is optimistically tuned to this period, but comparative findings---House vs.\ Senate, high vs.\ low Deliberation Index, pre- vs.\ post-disaster---are within-period contrasts unaffected by the choice of checkpoint (Section~\ref{sec:model}). Appendix~\ref{app:results} additionally reports speaker identification diagnostics on the full 1994--2024 span, including in-sample years, for model validation purposes. The structural difference between data sources---day-level versus topic-level conversations---motivates restricting the analysis period to GovInfo data only.

\section{Measurement Framework}

\subsubsection*{From deliberation to prediction}
Imagine you are sitting in the gallery of the U.S. Senate. A debate on immigration is underway. Senator A has just made an argument about border enforcement costs. Senator B rises. If you can predict what Senator B will say---the gist, the framing, the rhetorical moves---then Senator B is not really responding to Senator A. They are delivering a speech they would have delivered regardless. If Senator B \textit{surprises} you---takes the cost argument seriously, offers a counter-estimate, pivots unexpectedly---then the conversation is producing information. That is deliberation.

\subsubsection*{Perplexity as a measure of surprise}
This intuition has a precise mathematical formulation. Shannon's \citeyearpar{shannon1948} core insight was that \textit{information is surprise}: a message that tells you something you already knew carries no information. A language model $p$ reads text left to right, assigning a probability to every possible next token at each position---``The senator from'' $\rightarrow$ \textit{Texas} (8\%), \textit{New York} (5\%), \textit{the} (3\%), \ldots. Given a sequence $x_1, \ldots, x_T$, the \textit{perplexity} of the model on that sequence is
\begin{equation}
\PPL(x_1, \ldots, x_T) = \exp\!\left(-\frac{1}{T}\sum_{t=1}^{T} \log p(x_t \mid x_{<t})\right),
\label{eq:ppl}
\end{equation}
the exponential of the average negative log-likelihood per token. Perplexity has an intuitive interpretation: the effective number of equally likely next words. A perplexity of 10 means 10 plausible continuations; a perplexity of 100 means 100. Lower perplexity means more predictable text. What makes modern transformers \citep{vaswani2017} different from earlier approaches is their capacity to learn long-range dependencies: not just that ``I yield back'' follows ``the balance of my time,'' but that a senator's response to a budget amendment is shaped by an argument made four speakers ago.

\subsubsection*{Three levels}
We compute perplexity at three levels, each answering a different question. \textit{Conditional perplexity} evaluates (\ref{eq:ppl}) with $x_{<t}$ set to the full preceding debate (up to 2,048 tokens):
\begin{equation}
\Hc = \PPL(x_1, \ldots, x_T \mid \text{debate context}).
\end{equation}
This measures how surprising a speech is given everything said so far. \textit{Marginal perplexity} strips the context, conditioning only on speaker identity and chamber:
\begin{equation}
\Hm = \PPL(x_1, \ldots, x_T \mid \text{speaker, chamber}).
\end{equation}
This measures how predictable a speaker is regardless of what preceded them. The \textit{Deliberation Index} is their difference:
\begin{equation}
\DI = \Hm - \Hc.
\label{eq:di}
\end{equation}
When $\DI > 0$, the preceding conversation helps predict the next turn---the speaker is responding to what was said. When $\DI \approx 0$, context is irrelevant. When $\DI < 0$, context makes the speaker \textit{less} predictable.\footnote{The Deliberation Index operates on the perplexity scale (effective number of next words). On the cross-entropy scale, the corresponding quantity is $\log_2(\Hm) - \log_2(\Hc)$, which has a cleaner information-theoretic interpretation as mutual information. We use the perplexity scale because it is more intuitive for social scientists. Our qualitative findings are invariant to this choice.}

\subsubsection*{What perplexity captures---and what it doesn't}
Low conditional perplexity blends several phenomena: procedural formulae, topical continuity, partisan scripting, and genuine deliberative responsiveness. The Deliberation Index partially separates these by subtracting the speaker's baseline ($\Hm$), but topical continuity remains entangled with responsiveness. Our topic-level conversation structure (from GovInfo) provides a partial control: within a topic-level conversation, all speakers discuss the same subject, so differences across turns reflect conversational dynamics rather than topic selection. The honest summary: perplexity measures \textit{predictability}; the Deliberation Index measures how much \textit{debate context} contributes to predictability. These are necessary conditions for deliberation, not sufficient ones. But they are measurable at scale and reveal institutional structure that existing measures cannot see.

\subsubsection*{Testable predictions}
The framework yields four predictions, each testable in evaluation data (2015--2024) without further model training. \textit{H1 (Institutional design):} House speech should be more predictable than Senate speech, reflecting tighter procedural control over debate \citep{perssonTabellini2003}. \textit{H2 (Context-responsiveness):} The Deliberation Index should be positive on average---debate context should help predict the next turn if floor debate functions as conversation rather than monologue. \textit{H3 (Crisis disruption):} Perplexity should spike following salient public events (e.g., FEMA disaster declarations) that inject novel content with no procedural template. \textit{H4 (Institutional recovery):} Post-event perplexity should return to baseline as procedural routines reassert themselves.

\section{Model}
\label{sec:model}

The model is a 40.6-million-parameter decoder-only GPT transformer (depth 6, embedding dimension 384, 6 attention heads with grouped-query attention, 2,048-token context window), built on an open-source training pipeline \citep{karpathy2025}. We train a BPE tokenizer with 32,768 tokens including 1,701 speaker-specific tokens (one per legislator) and chamber markers. Training runs for 12,000 steps on 98.3 million of 386 million available tokens, reaching minimum validation loss at step 11,000, in approximately 2.5 hours on consumer hardware (Apple M2 Max, throughput $\sim$11,500 tokens/second). Full architecture details appear in \Cref{app:training}.

\subsubsection*{Evaluation protocol} We use a temporal split: training on 1994--2014, with all downstream analyses on 2015--2024. The training checkpoint is selected via early stopping on 2015--2024 loss (minimum at step 11,000 of 12,000), so the evaluation period also serves as the validation set for model selection. This is standard in language modeling but warrants transparency: early stopping optimizes the \textit{level} of perplexity (the overall fit of the model) but does not bias \textit{within-period comparisons}. All of our primary findings---the House--Senate gap, the Deliberation Index decomposition, and the FEMA event study---are relative contrasts within the evaluation period, conditioned on the same model checkpoint. A three-way split (e.g., train on 1994--2012, validate on 2013--2014, evaluate on 2015--2024) would provide a cleaner separation at the cost of a shorter evaluation window; we report results on the full 2015--2024 period for statistical power across five congressional terms.

The House has a \textit{higher} Deliberation Index ($+2.76$) than the Senate ($+2.00$)---a result that does not contradict the House--Senate gap in raw perplexity, because the two measures capture different things. The House is more \textit{formulaic} (lower $\Hc$) but exhibits stronger \textit{sequential dependence} (higher $\DI$). This is consistent with procedural constraints producing tight conversational coupling, but it could also reflect narrower topic agendas or shorter speech lengths in the House. Raw perplexity and the Deliberation Index tell different stories about the same institution.

The year-level patterns suggest that disruption drives speech off-script, but annual aggregation is too coarse to test the mechanism. We sharpen this with an event study exploiting the precise timing of FEMA major disaster declarations.

We match 635 major disaster declarations to daily conversation-level perplexity computed exhaustively across all 23,859 evaluation conversations (87 million tokens scored; \Cref{fig:fema}). Each disaster contributes a $[-30, +30]$-day window; perplexity on each day is expressed as a deviation from the disaster-specific pre-period mean (days $-30$ to $-1$). Days outside the speech data range contribute no observation for that disaster, so declarations near the boundaries of the evaluation period have slightly shorter windows.\footnote{The Congressional Record data end in December 2024. Disasters declared after November 30, 2024 have truncated post-periods. The number of disasters contributing observations declines slightly at the window extremes.} The pre-period shows a mild upward drift beginning around day $-10$, consistent with floor discussion of approaching disasters before formal FEMA declarations. In the first week after a declaration (days 0 to $+7$), mean perplexity across all disaster windows rises by 3.9 points (SE = 0.93, $t = 4.2$, $p < 0.001$).\footnote{This is the cross-disaster average for the full event week (days 0--7). The 7-day moving average in \Cref{fig:fema} shows the daily trajectory, which peaks near day $+3$ and declines thereafter; the moving average peak is lower than the window mean because it incorporates the onset and decline phases.} The post-period (days $+8$ to $+30$) shows a significant \textit{overshoot below baseline} ($-1.1$ points, SE = 0.28, $t = 4.0$)---speech becomes temporarily \textit{more} predictable than the pre-disaster norm before recovering.

The magnitude provides a useful benchmark: the +3.9 point event-week spike equals roughly two-thirds of the permanent House--Senate gap (6.0 points), suggesting that a single week of disaster response shifts floor speech predictability by a substantial fraction of what distinguishes the two chambers. Disasters inject novel content---emergency appropriations, oversight hearings, constituent appeals---that has no procedural template. The subsequent below-baseline period is consistent with procedural templates reasserting themselves, though it could also reflect speech composition changes or calendar effects. This event-level pattern complements the year-level finding: the 2020 pandemic spike visible in \Cref{fig:perplexity} is consistent with many such acute disruptions compressed into a single year.

\section{Discussion}

These patterns are descriptive; we now clarify what institutional interpretation they support and where they remain ambiguous.

\subsubsection*{The formulaic-but-responsive paradox} The most striking result is that lower raw unpredictability does not imply weaker sequential dependence on prior debate. The House is more \textit{formulaic} (lower $\Hc$) but exhibits stronger \textit{context dependence} (higher $\DI$). One interpretation is that the House's procedural constraints---five-minute time limits, controlled recognition, pre-screened amendments---compress each member's speaking time and produce tight sequential coupling even within a narrow register \citep{perssonTabellini2003, jenkins2006}. The Senate's looser rules allow longer, self-contained speeches that are individually less predictable but less tethered to prior turns \citep{lee2009}. However, this institutional interpretation is not causally identified: the House and Senate differ on many dimensions beyond procedural rules---chamber size, term length, constituency breadth, topic mix, and speech length---and the cross-chamber comparison cannot isolate the contribution of any single factor. Credibly identifying the causal effect of procedural rules would require within-chamber variation, such as changes in rule regimes or comparisons across open versus closed amendment procedures. Our evidence is descriptive: these two institutions differ systematically in how their speech relates to prior debate, in a direction consistent with the procedural hypothesis.

\subsubsection*{FEMA event study} The disruption-and-overshoot pattern---a 3.9-point spike followed by a dip below baseline---is suggestive of institutional absorption of novel content, though the design has important limitations. The mild pre-declaration drift (beginning around day $-10$) indicates that the administrative declaration date does not mark the clean onset of the shock to congressional discourse, as floor discussion of approaching disasters likely begins earlier. The 635 declarations produce substantially overlapping $\pm30$-day windows, inducing dependence across observations that our standard errors do not fully account for. The design lacks an explicit counterfactual time series; congressional speech has strong seasonality and calendar effects that could confound the post-declaration spike. We view this exercise as descriptive validation---evidence that the measure tracks salient public events---rather than causal identification of the effect of exogenous shocks on deliberation.

\subsubsection*{Limitations} Several limitations constrain interpretation. The Deliberation Index is based on 832 sampled turns from five odd-numbered years; the overall result is robust ($t \approx 9.5$) but the House--Senate difference is imprecisely estimated given standard deviations of 7--8. Exhaustive scoring or a formal stratified sampling design would strengthen inference. The DI measures statistical sequential dependence, not deliberative quality \textit{per se}: scripted exchanges, topical continuity, and genuine engagement may all raise it. Falsification exercises---permuting turn order (DI should collapse), replacing context with same-topic text from other debates, validating against hand-coded deliberation ratings---would sharpen the measurement claim but have not yet been performed.

The House--Senate comparison is descriptive. Beyond procedural rules, the chambers differ in speech length, topic mix, member characteristics, and electoral incentives; controlling for these would help isolate the DI gap's sources. Our model is a single configuration and training run; robustness across seeds and model sizes is untested. The evaluation period also served as the validation set for early stopping, and includes speakers who entered Congress after training ended. A three-way split and separate analysis for incumbents versus new entrants would address these concerns. Finally, the DI operates on the perplexity scale, where differences are nonlinear; log-perplexity or cross-entropy differences would provide better-behaved estimands.

\subsubsection*{Extensions} The most pressing extension is causal identification. Within-chamber rule changes---open versus closed amendment rules in the House, filibuster reforms in the Senate, or comparisons of the same legislator speaking under different procedural environments---could isolate the contribution of institutional rules to the patterns we document. Methodologically, permuted-turn and wrong-context placebo tests would sharpen the construct validity of DI. Other natural extensions include decomposing $\DI$ into within-party and cross-party components to trace the polarization trajectory, comparing floor debate to committee hearings within the same legislator, and linking the Deliberation Index to legislative outcomes---bill passage rates, bipartisan amendment activity, or coalition formation. If debates with high sequential dependence produce more durable legislation, the measure would move from descriptive to policy-relevant. The method requires only transcribed debates with identified speakers and generalizes to any legislature.

\section{Conclusion}

We introduce a framework for measuring the information content of legislative debate using the perplexity of a purpose-trained language model. Applied to thirty years of U.S. Congressional floor speech, it reveals that the House is simultaneously more formulaic and more sequentially dependent on prior debate than the Senate---a pattern consistent with procedural rules shaping conversational structure, though not causally identified here. The Deliberation Index is positive in 85\% of turns and responds to salient public events at daily frequency. The method requires no proprietary data and generalizes to any legislature with transcribed debate, opening a new margin on which to compare legislative institutions.

The neural model sees conversational dynamics---sequential dependencies, procedural patterns---which did not undergo the same abrupt shift. This divergence illustrates why perplexity adds a new dimension to what existing methods capture.

\section{Data Pipeline Details}
\label{app:data}

The Congressional Record is downloaded from GovInfo's bulk data API as ZIP archives by date. Each archive contains HTML files for individual debate segments. We parse using the \texttt{unitedstates/congressional-record} parser (v2.0.8), extracting speaker BioGuide ID, chamber, text, and turn number. GovInfo rate-limits requests; our script implements exponential backoff.

For HuggingFace data (1994--2010), speaker matching links informal identifiers to BioGuide IDs using the \texttt{congress-legislators} dataset (67.7\% match rate by speech count, 90.2\% by word count). Unmatched speeches are predominantly presiding officer announcements. For GovInfo data, BioGuide IDs are embedded directly; we enriched 618 of 620 unknown speakers from the legislator database.

HuggingFace-era conversations are grouped by (date, chamber); GovInfo-era by document/topic. This difference motivates restricting the Deliberation Index to GovInfo evaluation data (2015--2024).
