**Idea Fidelity**

The paper mostly pursues the original manifest idea, but it diverges in a couple of important respects. The manifest promised a school-level staggered DiD exploiting lottery-induced enrollment shocks using IDEB data merged with project delivery dates, with a focus on the local ‘receiving neighborhood’ effects of MCMV housing. The submitted manuscript instead uses municipality-level IDEB averages (rather than school-level outcomes) and treats the date of the first FAR contract as the treatment. The original idea emphasized the lottery as a source of exogenous enrollment shocks; the paper relies on municipal treatment timing driven by federal contracting decisions, which may not be arguably random. The identification strategy also deviates from the manifest’s school-level staggered DiD in favor of a municipality-level Callaway–Sant’Anna estimator, which is reasonable, but the paper never establishes how the definition of “municipalities near large MCMV deliveries” maps to the actual local effects promised in the idea. These shifts should be acknowledged and, if possible, reconciled with the original research question.

**Summary**

The paper studies whether Brazil’s Minha Casa Minha Vida (FAR modality) housing program affected IDEB school quality in municipalities that received the first FAR contracts between 2009 and 2023. Using a Callaway–Sant’Anna staggered DiD with never-treated controls, the author finds no statistically significant effect on overall primary or lower-secondary IDEB scores, though municipal schools exhibit a mild negative point estimate while state schools show a positive one. The paper also contrasts these results with biased TWFE estimates and argues that “absorption” patterns across governance types explain the aggregate null.

**Essential Points**

1. **Credibility of treatment timing variation.** The identifying assumption is that the timing of FAR contracts within eligible municipalities is exogenous to local IDEB trends. Yet the paper provides no direct evidence supporting this: municipalities with earlier contracts are systematically larger and better-performing (see Table 1), and FAR expansion was part of a discretionary federal program that likely responded to urban planning, political representation, or fiscal capacity. Without controlling for observable determinants of FAR timing or showing that placement across otherwise similar municipalities is as good as random (e.g., via balance tests on pre-trends or covariates), the parallel-trends assumption is fragile. The pre-period consists only of two waves, so the notional “flat” pre-trend may simply lack power. The author should either provide stronger evidence (e.g., matching on observables, placebo tests, or direct discussion of federal program rules that fixed timing) or relax the claim to describe the estimates as descriptive correlations.

2. **Aggregation masks geographic heterogeneity.** The manifest promised “schools near housing projects,” but the empirical work aggregates to municipalities that receive any FAR project. Municipalities can be large and internally heterogeneous; many schools may be far from the project and unaffected. This aggregation may dilute effects and make negative results unsurprising. The author should either (a) show that results are robust when focusing on municipalities where housing projects comprise a large share of population or when constructing weighted averages giving more weight to neighborhoods closer to projects, or (b) explicitly acknowledge that the analysis captures only city-wide average effects—i.e., the net of many unaffected schools—which limits the policy interpretation in terms of the “receiving neighborhood.”

3. **Mechanism interpretation is speculative.** The divergence between municipal and state schools is interesting, but the paper infers compositional resorting without directly measuring enrollment changes or student composition. Given that IDEB aggregates over schools and that municipal/state schools serve different populations, positive effects in the state network could arise from unrelated state-level reforms or selective completion. The author needs to bolster this interpretation—e.g., by linking to enrollment data from the Censo Escolar, showing increases in municipal enrollment post-treatment, or demonstrating that municipal schools experienced higher student-teacher ratios. At minimum, the paper should tone down causal language around the mechanism and note the possibility of confounding state-level reforms.

If more than these three issues are judged critical, the paper may not be suitable for publication in its current form.

**Suggestions**

1. **Strengthen the identification narrative.** To make the DiD design more convincing, provide richer background on how FAR contracts were allocated over time. Were selection rules deterministic (e.g., priority for metropolitan regions) or discretionary? Can the author show that the timing is orthogonal to observable determinants of IDEB, beyond levels? For example, estimate regressions of cohort assignment on pre-treatment IDEB trends, municipal characteristics, political variables, or proximity to federal infrastructure programs. Placebo tests using “fake” treatment dates before 2009 could help demonstrate that municipalities selected for early FAR contracts did not already exhibit differential trends. If such analyses fail, consider alternative identification strategies such as synthetic controls or instrumental variables that exploit sudden federal budget infusions or developer-specific timing.

2. **Leverage additional data for finer geospatial precision.** The manifest suggested school-level outcomes; some of that granularity may still be available. Can the author exploit school geolocation or distance to FAR projects from project data, even if imperfect? If so, construct a treatment dummy for schools within X kilometers of a project. Even within municipalities, you could assign treatment intensity based on the number of units per capita or per school. Complementing the municipality-level analysis with sub-city variation would better capture the sharp local shocks the paper claims to evaluate and help assess whether the null is due to aggregation.

3. **Use enrollment and infrastructure data to unpack mechanisms.** As suggested, incorporating Censo Escolar data on enrollment, student-teacher ratios, and infrastructure (classrooms, facilities) would help trace the channels. For example, show that municipal schools in treated cities experienced sharper enrollment growth or worsened pupil-teacher ratios than state schools. Alternatively, if such data are not available for all years, cite descriptive statistics that are consistent with the proposed mechanism. This would make the “absorption illusion” narrative more compelling.

4. **Clarify the control group and relax treatment definition tests.** The robustness checks show that expanding treatment to all modalities flips the sign and significance, which suggests treatment heterogeneity. The paper should elaborate on why FAR-only treatment is the policy-relevant object and whether results hinge on the eligibility rules or beneficiary type. Additionally, the never-treated municipalities may not be comparable to treated ones; consider using not-yet-treated municipalities as controls (beyond what’s already reported) or constructing a matched sample. The “stacked DiD” approach is a good idea—consider presenting cohort-specific effects graphically to show whether later cohorts drive the null.

5. **Report effect sizes in context.** The paper already translates the ATT into standard deviation units (Appendix A), but it would be helpful to relate these to policy benchmarks (e.g., typical annual gains in IDEB, or the magnitude of reforms like the Fundeb). This would aid interpretation of the “small” effects and help readers assess whether the null is substantively meaningful.

6. **Explore heterogeneity beyond governance type.** The absorption illusion may vary by municipal capacity, size, or pre-existing IDEB. Are effects different in large metropolitan centers versus smaller towns? Do municipalities with higher initial IDEB see different responses? Presenting such heterogeneity would deepen the policy relevance and help explain why the state network improved while municipal schools declined.

7. **Discuss potential measurement bias due to biennial IDEB.** The biennial frequency can obscure short-run effects. The author notes this limitation but could go further by presenting sensitivity analyses using annual Censo Escolar indicators that are available (e.g., pass rates or scores). Even if these are coarse, they would help cross-validate the main results or reveal dynamics between IDEB waves.

By addressing these suggestions, the paper would better align with the ambitious policy question it sets out to answer and offer more credible insight into how mass housing programs interact with local education systems.
