### 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
This paper tests whether targeted safety regulations cause "risk substitution"—where firms, constrained by finite budgets, reduce one hazard (silica exposure) only to let safety slide in other areas (injuries, hearing loss). Using a triple-difference design on establishment-level OSHA data, the author finds no evidence of this "safety balloon" effect; in fact, overall safety improved, suggesting that engineering controls for specific toxins create positive spillovers for general workplace safety.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this pitch excellently. The "safety balloon" metaphor is intuitive and the first two paragraphs clearly frame the paper as a test of a foundational regulatory concern (the Peltzman effect). It identifies the specific policy (Silica Standard 2018) and the data (ITA) immediately.

### 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
The paper provides the first establishment-level test of cross-hazard substitution in workplace safety, finding that targeted regulation generates complementarities rather than trade-offs.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It is well-differentiated from the classic OSHA literature (Viscusi, etc.), which mostly looks at aggregate injury rates. By disaggregating by hazard type *within* the firm, it identifies a mechanism (substitution) that prior work could only guess at.
*   **Framing:** It is framed as a question about the WORLD (Do firms trade off risks?), which is the AER’s bread and butter.
*   **Clarity:** A smart economist would immediately understand this as a test of the "fixed-pie" versus "rising-tide" view of safety investments.
*   **Bigness:** To make this bigger, the paper needs to more aggressively bridge the gap between "Occupational Health" and "Firm Behavior." Specifically, it needs more evidence on *why* small firms show stronger complementarities. Is it because of a single manager's attention, or the physical nature of small-shop engineering?

### 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
The paper sits at the intersection of Regulatory Economics and Labor Economics.
*   **Neighbors:** Peltzman (1975) on offsetting behavior; Viscusi (1979/1984) on OSHA's efficacy; and recent work by Johnson (2020) on "shaming" as a regulatory tool.
*   **Positioning:** It builds on the Peltzman/Viscusi tradition but challenges the "offsetting" narrative in the context of modern industrial engineering.
*   **Missing Conversations:** The paper should speak more to the **Industrial Organization** of the firm. If safety is a "joint product" of capital investment (ventilation), the paper is actually about the production function of safety. Connecting to the literature on "Management Practices and Productivity" (e.g., Bloom & Van Reenen) would elevate it from a "policy evaluation" to a "firm behavior" paper.

### 4. NARRATIVE ARC
*   **Setup:** Regulators assume targeted rules work; skeptics argue firms just move the danger around.
*   **Tension:** OSHA's massive silica mandate (high compliance cost) provides the perfect "squeeze" to see if other safety margins pop out.
*   **Resolution:** The balloon doesn't inflate. Instead, the whole balloon gets stronger.
*   **Implications:** Concerns about "regulatory substitution" may be overstated for hazards requiring capital-intensive engineering solutions.

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong. However, the "COVID-era" sensitivity (Section 5.3) is a major plot hole. If the result disappears when excluding 2020-2021, the story changes from "Engineering spillovers" to "COVID-era anomalies." The author needs to resolve this tension more convincingly.

### 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
At a dinner party, I would lead with: *"When OSHA forced factories to install high-tech vacuums for dust, workers stopped breaking their legs, too."*
People would lean in because it suggests that "red tape" can have accidental benefits. The follow-up would be: *"Is that just because the factory became more organized overall, or because the vacuums literally sucked up the debris that people trip on?"* This is the "Attention vs. Infrastructure" question the paper starts to answer.

### 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
*   **Front-loading:** The paper is well-structured.
*   **Appendix:** The construction-sector placebo mentioned in Section B of the appendix should be moved into the main text. If it works, it’s a powerful corroboration.
*   **The COVID problem:** The attenuation in Section 5.3 is the "smoking gun" for a skeptic. This shouldn't be a footnote. The author needs a version of the main table that interacts the effect with "High-COVID" vs "Low-COVID" states or industries to prove it's not a fluke.

### 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The gap is currently **Mechanisms.** As it stands, it's a very high-quality "No Result" (we don't see substitution). To be AER-caliber, it needs to prove *why* we see complementarity.
*   **Ambition:** The heterogeneity by firm size (Section 5.4) is the most interesting part of the paper but is currently the shortest. Small firms showing stronger spillovers is counter-intuitive if you believe in budget constraints.
*   **Single Biggest Improvement:** Deconstruct the "complementarity" finding. Use the data to distinguish between the **Physical spillover** (the ventilation system) and the **Managerial spillover** (the safety training). If the author can show that "Total Injuries" fell even in areas of the plant *not* affected by the new ventilation, the "Management Attention" story wins.
