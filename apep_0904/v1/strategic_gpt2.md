## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether federal procurement officers distort contract values to remain below the Simplified Acquisition Threshold, where buying is much less procedurally burdensome. Using the full distribution of U.S. federal contracts and exploiting the 2020 increase in the threshold from \$150,000 to \$250,000, the paper shows that excess mass in contract values moves with the threshold, suggesting that procurement rules induce real behavioral distortion and revealing the burden of red tape.

Why should a busy economist care? Because this is not really a procurement-paper-only question; it is about how organizations respond to administrative thresholds, and whether procedural regulation changes real economic decisions rather than just paperwork.

### Does the paper articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Mostly yes, but not optimally. The introduction is readable and gets to the question quickly. Still, the current framing is too inward-looking and “method-forward”: it says “how much do officers distort contract values?” and “I use bunching.” That is fine, but the stronger AER pitch is about **what administrative burden does to public-sector behavior and resource allocation**. The current first paragraphs understate the broader stakes.

### What the first two paragraphs should say instead

A stronger opening would be something like:

> Governments rely on procedural rules to safeguard public spending, but those same rules may distort what the government buys. In U.S. federal procurement, a sharp threshold determines whether a contract can be purchased with streamlined procedures or must go through full competition and documentation requirements. If those requirements are costly, contracting officers should reshape purchases to stay below the line.
>
> This paper measures that distortion using the universe of federal contracts and a threshold change that raised the Simplified Acquisition Threshold from \$150,000 to \$250,000. I show that contract values bunch sharply below the threshold and that this bunching migrates when the threshold moves, implying that procurement red tape changes real purchasing behavior, not just administrative process.

That version puts the world question first: **Do procedural rules distort public purchasing?** The bunching design then appears as the tool, not the headline.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### Contribution in one sentence

The paper shows that federal procurement officers systematically compress contract values to remain below the Simplified Acquisition Threshold, and that this distortion moves when the threshold changes, implying sizable behavioral costs of procurement red tape.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?

Not yet clearly enough. The paper cites Carril and says “first multi-threshold bunching analysis” plus “migration test,” but that is a fairly thin differentiation if the reader already believes thresholds generate bunching. As written, the contribution risks sounding like: “another threshold-manipulation paper, now in procurement, with a policy change that provides a neat validation exercise.”

The closest intellectual neighbors seem to be:

- **Carril (2022)** on bunching/manipulation around an earlier procurement threshold.
- **Coviello, Marini, and Spagnolo (2018)** and related work on manipulation around procurement thresholds in Italy.
- **Bandiera, Prat, and Valletti (2009)** on passive waste/compliance costs in procurement.
- **Kleven (and bunching literature generally)** for method, though this is not the audience-defining literature.
- Possibly **Bosio et al. (2022)** and public procurement design papers on regulation and efficiency.

Relative to those papers, “we have a later threshold, a new setting, and a migration test” is credible but not yet big.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a gap in a literature?

It starts with the world, which is good, but then the contribution paragraph drifts into literature-gap framing: “first multi-threshold bunching analysis,” “internal replication device,” etc. That is not where the value is. The strongest framing is:

- Governments use administrative thresholds everywhere.
- We know less about whether compliance rules distort the real object being procured.
- This paper shows that they do, in a massive public market, and that the distortion tracks the policy threshold.

That is a world question. The literature-gap framing should be secondary.

### Could a smart economist explain to a colleague what’s new here?

Right now, many would say: “It’s a bunching paper on federal procurement thresholds.”  
A more favorable reader would say: “It shows procurement red tape causes real distortions, and the threshold change lets them show the distortion migrates with the rule.”

That second sentence is what the introduction needs to force.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Specific ways to make it bigger:

1. **Translate bunching into a more economically legible object.**  
   Right now the main object is excess mass. That is publishable, but not field-shaping. The paper needs a stronger bridge from bunching to the economics of public purchasing: scope reduction, purchase splitting, timing distortion, vendor fragmentation, or downstream procurement quality.

2. **Separate the margins of response.**  
   The paper itself admits it cannot distinguish undersizing from splitting. That is precisely the margin that would elevate the paper. If it can show whether agencies buy less, buy in pieces, delay purchases, or alter vendor choice, the contribution becomes much more substantive.

3. **Show consequences, not just distortion.**  
   For AER, “red tape causes bunching” is interesting. “Red tape causes bunching and lowers competition/raises prices/reduces fulfillment quality/changes small-business participation/induces contract splitting” is much stronger. Even one credible downstream consequence would matter.

4. **Generalize the object beyond this one threshold.**  
   The paper could frame SAT as a clean case of a broader phenomenon: administrative thresholds in the state. Without that, it risks feeling like a very competent institutional note.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

The government uses bright-line procurement thresholds to trade off safeguards against administrative simplicity. Below the threshold, procurement is easy; above it, it becomes much more cumbersome.

### Tension

If those procedures are genuinely costly, officers may respond not by complying neutrally, but by reshaping the contract itself. That means procurement regulation may distort what government buys, not just how it buys it.

### Resolution

The distribution of contract values shows sharp bunching just below the threshold, and when Congress moves the threshold, the bunching moves too. That is strong evidence that the rule is shaping behavior.

### Implications

Administrative burdens in procurement are large enough to induce real distortion. Procedural safeguards may therefore generate deadweight costs and potentially misallocate public purchases.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the ingredients, but the narrative is still somewhat “results-forward” rather than fully story-driven. The paper is not a pile of random regressions; there is a real story here. But it currently reads as:

1. There is a threshold.
2. Here is bunching.
3. Here is migration.
4. Here are placebos and robustness.
5. Therefore red tape matters.

What is missing is a stronger sustained emphasis on the central tension: **rules designed to improve procurement may instead change the object being procured**. That is the story.

The paper should be telling this story more explicitly:

- Governments use rules to protect integrity.
- Rules create fixed compliance costs.
- Fixed compliance costs create incentives to distort economically meaningful decisions.
- We can see that distortion directly in the distribution of contracts.
- The threshold change lets us distinguish regulatory distortion from generic heaping.
- Therefore, procurement policy needs to weigh integrity benefits against real allocation costs.

That is a clean setup-tension-resolution-implications arc. The paper is close, but not quite there.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?

“I can see thousands of federal contracts per year getting packed just below the procurement threshold, and when Congress raised the threshold from \$150K to \$250K, the bunching moved with it.”

That is the right lead fact.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

Many would lean in initially. The migration fact is intuitive and visually compelling. Economists like thresholds, and they like seeing institutional rules show up cleanly in distributions.

But the second question they will ask is decisive. If the answer is weak, attention will fade.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Immediately:  
**“Okay, but what is the real consequence? Are they splitting contracts, buying less, reducing quality, changing vendors, or just using accounting tricks?”**

That is the paper’s biggest vulnerability from a strategic-positioning standpoint. The paper has a neat fact, but the next question is both natural and currently underanswered.

### If findings are modest or null, is that okay?

This is not a null paper; the main finding is quite stark. But the underlying welfare interpretation remains somewhat modest because the paper cannot say exactly what distortion means in practice. The paper tries to infer lower-bound compliance costs, but those calculations still feel back-of-the-envelope. So the risk is not that the finding is null; it is that the finding is **conceptually incomplete**.

Some, but mostly it summarizes. The conclusion should end on the broader claim: procedural safeguards can produce real quantity distortions in public procurement, and threshold design is therefore a first-order economic margin, not an administrative footnote.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

Be honest: in its current form, this feels more like a strong field journal or good general-interest second-tier paper than an obvious AER paper.

### What is the gap?

Primarily:

- **A scope problem**
- **An ambition problem**
- Secondarily, **a framing problem**

The framing is decent and fixable. The larger issue is that the paper currently establishes an elegant distortion fact, but not enough of the broader economic consequences. For AER, the paper needs to do more than show there is bunching around a procurement threshold, even with a nice migration test. ### Is this a novelty problem?

Partly, yes. Threshold bunching is a mature empirical genre. Procurement threshold manipulation is also not wholly new. The migration test is a nice twist, but by itself it is probably not sufficient novelty for AER.

### Is this an ambition problem?

Yes. The paper is careful and competent, but safe. It stops at the first-order fact instead of pushing into the higher-stakes implications.

### Single most impactful advice

**If the author can do only one thing, they should show what officers do to get below the threshold—split contracts, reduce quantities, change timing, or alter vendors—and quantify at least one economically meaningful consequence of that behavior.**

That is the difference between:
- “there is bunching around a rule,” and
- “red tape changes real public purchasing outcomes.”

The latter is an AER conversation.
