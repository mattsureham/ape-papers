## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether federal procurement rules distort what the government buys. Using the distribution of millions of U.S. federal contract values, it shows that contracts bunch just below the Simplified Acquisition Threshold, and that this bunching moves when Congress raises the threshold—suggesting contracting officers actively reshape purchases to avoid more burdensome procedures above the cutoff. A busy economist should care because this is a clean revealed-preference window into the real cost of administrative procedure inside a huge market: public procurement.

The paper mostly does articulate this pitch, but not as sharply as it could. The current opening is competent and concrete, yet it still reads a bit like “here is a threshold and here is a bunching design” rather than “here is a major economic margin through which administrative burden distorts state capacity.” The first two paragraphs should foreground the world question much more aggressively: not “is there bunching at the SAT?” but “do procurement rules cause the state to buy differently than it otherwise would?”

### The pitch the paper should have

“The federal government buys over $700 billion annually, but the rules governing how it buys change discontinuously at a single dollar threshold. If those procedural requirements are costly, contracting officers will not passively comply—they will reshape contracts to stay below the line. This paper shows that they do: contract values pile up just below the Simplified Acquisition Threshold, and when the threshold rises, the pile-up moves with it. That migration reveals something economically important: administrative burden in procurement is large enough to distort the government’s own purchasing decisions.”

That is the AER version of the pitch. It is about state capacity, administrative burden, and distorted economic choices—not mainly about bunching mechanics.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper provides revealed-preference evidence that federal procurement rules impose large enough compliance costs to induce systematic distortion of contract values, and it strengthens that claim by showing the distortion migrates when the regulatory threshold changes.

### Is this clearly differentiated from the closest papers?

Partially, but not enough. The paper does name Carril and says “first multi-threshold bunching analysis” with a migration test, but that still sounds incremental. “Same phenomenon, more years, more thresholds” is not yet a top-journal contribution unless it is tied to a larger question.

The closest differentiation currently is:
1. universe data,
2. multiple SAT regimes,
3. threshold-change migration test.

Those are real advantages, but the paper needs to explain why they add up to a qualitatively different inference. The strongest angle is not “I estimate bunching at two thresholds instead of one”; it is “threshold changes let me separate true regulatory manipulation from generic round-number heaping and treat the move as an internal replication of revealed compliance costs.” That is much stronger.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a gap in a literature?

It gestures toward the world question, but too often falls back into literature-gap framing. The stronger framing is: **How much does administrative procedure distort public-sector decision-making?** That is a question about the world. The weaker framing is: **There is not yet a multi-threshold bunching paper on the SAT.** That is a literature gap.

Right now the intro contains both, but the literature-gap version is too visible.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?

A smart economist could probably say: “It’s a bunching paper showing federal contracts cluster below procurement thresholds, and when the threshold moved the bunching moved too.” That is decent, but still close to “another DiD paper about X” in spirit—i.e., “another threshold-manipulation paper.” They would not yet naturally say: “This changes how I think about the cost of administrative procedures in the state.”

That is the problem. The empirical fact is interesting; the paper’s current introduction does not fully elevate it into a broader conceptual contribution.

### What would make the contribution bigger?

Most importantly, make the object of interest bigger than bunching itself.

Specific ways:
- **Different framing:** Position this as evidence on administrative burden and state capacity, not just procurement manipulation.
- **Different outcome concept:** Show more clearly whether the distortion implies real procurement fragmentation, reduced contract scope, or timing distortions. Right now the paper says “undersizing or splitting” but cannot distinguish them. That is the biggest reason the contribution remains somewhat abstract.
- **Different comparison:** Compare procurement to other administrative thresholds in government, or compare agencies with different exposure to compliance burden. Even descriptive heterogeneity across agency type, contract type, urgency, or product category could make the story much richer.
- **Different mechanism emphasis:** The migration test is the headline mechanism. It should be elevated from an “identification innovation” to the core substantive result: agents respond to anticipated procedural regimes, not just current rules.
- **Broader welfare framing:** If the paper could better connect bunching to the cost of public purchasing inefficiency—fragmentation, reduced competition where it matters, or reduced quality—it would feel larger.

The paper is one conceptual step away from being about **administrative burden as a first-order economic distortion**. That step needs to be taken explicitly.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

The government buys a lot, and procurement rules become much more onerous above a bright-line threshold.

### Tension

If those procedural requirements are costly, bureaucrats should alter economically relevant choices to avoid them—but it is hard to measure those compliance costs directly, and hard to distinguish true threshold avoidance from generic round-number clustering.

### Resolution

Contract values bunch below the threshold, and when the threshold rises from $150K to $250K, the bunching migrates with it. That strongly suggests the mass is generated by regulation rather than numerology.

### Implications

Administrative burden is not merely annoying; it changes the allocation of public purchases. Procurement rules may therefore create deadweight loss inside the state itself, inducing undersizing or fragmentation of contracts.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the ingredients, but the arc is only partially realized. Right now the paper is closer to “a sequence of sensible empirical findings” than to a fully satisfying story. The results are aligned, but the narrative energy dissipates because the paper does not quite decide what the main story is.

There are at least three possible stories in the draft:

1. A procurement paper about SAT manipulation.
2. A bunching paper with a migration design.
3. A paper about administrative burden and state capacity.

The strongest is #3, supported by #1 and #2. But the draft often reads like #1 with bits of #2.

### What story should it be telling?

It should tell this story:

“Governments often try to ensure accountability by layering procedure onto procurement. But when procedure changes discontinuously at arbitrary thresholds, bureaucrats optimize around the rules. Using the movement of a major federal procurement threshold, this paper shows that compliance burden has a revealed price: officers distort contract size to avoid it. The result is a measurable form of passive waste generated not by corruption or incompetence, but by the design of the rules themselves.”

That is a coherent setup-tension-resolution-implications arc. It is bigger, cleaner, and more memorable.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?

“When the federal procurement threshold moved from $150,000 to $250,000, the pile-up of contracts moved with it.”

That is the sticky fact. It is intuitive, vivid, and speaks directly to causality without requiring a methods lecture.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

Economists would lean in—at least initially. Public economists, IO/public procurement people, and political economy people especially. But they would lean in because of the migration fact, not because of the exact bunching estimate.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Almost certainly: **“Does this actually change what the government buys, or is it mostly cosmetic reclassification/splitting?”**

That is the key substantive vulnerability in the paper’s strategic positioning. Not an identification vulnerability—a substantive one. The paper wants the reader to infer real distortion in procurement scope, but it cannot yet show whether contracts are truly undersized, split, delayed, or otherwise reorganized. The current text acknowledges this honestly, but that follow-up question is exactly where the paper’s ambition currently hits a ceiling.

If the author had a convincing answer to that one question, the paper would become much more exciting.

### Are the findings modest or null?

They are not null; the effect is visually and conceptually salient. The issue is not whether the result exists. The issue is whether the implied welfare and policy stakes are large and concrete enough for AER-level interest.

The migration result is genuinely interesting. The remaining challenge is translating “there is bunching” into “this materially changes how we think about procurement design and administrative burden.”

This is not primarily a “science is wrong” problem. It is mostly a **framing plus scope** problem.

### What is the gap?

- **Framing problem:** Yes. The paper undersells itself as a procurement bunching paper when it could be a paper on administrative burden and state capacity.
- **Scope problem:** Also yes. The paper shows distortion but does not yet nail down its economic form or consequences. That leaves the stakes somewhat inferred rather than demonstrated.
- **Novelty problem:** Somewhat. Threshold bunching in regulated settings is a known genre. The migration test helps, but on its own may not be enough for AER unless the paper makes a bigger substantive point.
- **Ambition problem:** Yes. The paper is competent, clean, and careful, but still a little safe. It stops at “there is bunching and therefore compliance costs are nontrivial.” A top-field audience will ask for more: what kind of distortion, where, and why it changes our understanding of procurement design.

### Be honest: how far is it from AER?

Medium-to-far in current form. The core empirical fact is strong enough to interest good journals. But for AER, the paper needs either:
1. a much bigger framing that lands in the administrative-burden/state-capacity debate, or
2. richer evidence on what the bunching means economically—splitting, scope reduction, procurement quality, competition, timing, or agency performance.

Right now it is a very solid result in search of a larger economic claim.

### Single most impactful piece of advice

If the author changes only one thing, it should be this:

**Reframe the paper around administrative burden and show, as directly as possible, what real procurement distortion the bunching represents—splitting, undersizing, or fragmentation—so the result is about state capacity rather than merely contract heaping.**
