## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
The paper examines whether banning employer credit checks reduces racial inequality in the labor market. Using a triple-difference design across US states, it finds that these bans significantly increased Black hiring in the finance sector—an industry where credit screening is prevalent—without a corresponding decline in new-hire earnings. It addresses whether removing "noisy" and racially biased screening tools can improve equity without sacrificing match quality.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this well, but it leads with a narrative anecdote. While the anecdote is effective for a general audience, for the AER, the first two paragraphs should pivot more aggressively toward the *economic trade-off*. 

**The pitch the paper should have:**
"Employer credit checks represent a significant barrier to entry for Black job seekers, who are disproportionately likely to have subprime credit due to historical wealth disparities. This paper provides the first causal empirical test of whether banning this screening tool improves racial equity or, as theory suggests, reduces matching efficiency. Exploiting staggered state-level bans and using administrative data, I find that Black hiring in finance increases by 19% of a standard deviation with no detectable impact on wages, suggesting that credit history acts as an inefficient barrier rather than a necessary signal of productivity."

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**Contribution:** This is the first paper to provide causal empirical evidence that restricting employer access to credit reports increases the representation of Black workers in high-screening industries.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It is well-differentiated from the "Ban the Box" literature (Doleac & Hansen) because credit history lacks the direct (if controversial) productivity link that a criminal record might imply. It moves beyond the theory-only NBER paper by Cortés et al. (2021).
*   **Framing:** It is currently framed as filling an empirical gap in a literature. It should be framed as a question about **Information and Discrimination**: Do noisy signals of "character" perpetuate historical inequality at no benefit to the firm?
*   **Clarity:** A smart economist would get it: "It’s the Ban-the-Box logic applied to credit scores in the finance sector."
*   **Bigger Contribution:** To make this "AER-sized," the author needs to dig deeper into the **firm’s response**. If they can't use credit checks, do they move to another biased signal (referrals, degrees)? The current paper is a bit of a "black box" regarding firm behavior.

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
The paper sits at the intersection of Labor (discrimination) and Public Economics (regulation of information).

*   **Closest Neighbors:** Cortés, Glover, & Tasci (2021) [Theory]; Doleac & Hansen (2020) [Ban the Box]; Clifford & Shoag (2017) [Credit checks/Labor].
*   **Strategy:** It should **build on** the theory of Cortés et al. by treating their model as the null hypothesis. It should **contrast** with the Ban-the-Box literature, where "statistical discrimination" often leads to *worse* outcomes for Black men. The fact that this ban *works* (where BTB failed) is a major story that isn't highlighted enough.
*   **Niche vs. Broad:** Currently a bit niche (Finance sector focus). It needs to speak to the broader "Economics of Privacy and Information" to hit the AER's general interest requirement.

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC
*   **Setup:** Employers use credit scores to proxy for "responsibility."
*   **Tension:** Credit scores are racially biased and may not actually predict job performance. Theory is split: bans could help Black workers or cause firms to discriminate more broadly.
*   **Resolution:** Bans work. Black hiring goes up in finance.
*   **Implications:** Information is not always "good." Restricting certain types of noisy, biased information can be Pareto-improving for equity and efficiency.

**Evaluation:** The arc is clear but the "Resolution" is a bit thin. The divergence between the TWFE results and the Callaway-Sant'Anna results (Table 3, Panel B) creates a "narrative sag" where the author has to defend the result rather than celebrate the insight.

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
**The Lead Fact:** "Banning credit checks increased Black hiring in banks by nearly 20% without lowering their starting salaries."
**Reaction:** Economists will lean in because it contradicts the common finding in "Ban the Box" papers that removing information *hurts* Black applicants.
**The Follow-up:** "Wait, if it didn't hurt the firms, why were they using credit checks in the first place?" (The paper needs a better answer for this).

---

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
*   **Front-loading:** The discussion of the Callaway-Sant’Anna (CS) divergence in the middle of the results section (page 8-9) is a momentum killer. Move the technical defense of TWFE vs. CS to an appendix or a dedicated "Identification Robustness" section. 
*   **The "Finance" Focus:** Explain earlier and more forcefully why Finance is the laboratory. If it works in Finance (the "high-stakes" environment), it likely works everywhere.
*   **Placebos:** The Agriculture placebo is excellent. Keep it prominent.

---

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The paper is currently a very high-quality *Applied Economics* or *Journal of Labor Economics* paper. To make it an **AER** paper, it needs to address the **"Why?"** of the firm. 

If credit checks are useless and discriminatory, why did 47% of firms use them? Is it agency problems in HR? Is it a legacy "rule of thumb"? Is it because the cost of the check is low and firms are risk-avoids?

**The Single Most Impactful Advice:** Shift the framing from "a policy evaluation of credit bans" to "an investigation into why firms use non-productive, racially biased signals." If the author can show that these bans *corrected a firm-level mistake* rather than just imposing an equity-efficiency trade-off, this is an AER lead article.
