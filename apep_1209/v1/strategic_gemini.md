## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
This paper identifies the causal impact of cannabis dispensaries on local property values by exploiting a unique natural experiment: Illinois’s randomized lottery for retail licenses. Using this quasi-random entry to overcome the endogenous site-selection bias that plagues previous studies, the paper finds that dispensaries cause a modest (6%) decline in nearby home prices, primarily driven by "stigma" in affluent neighborhoods rather than increased crime.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this clearly in the second paragraph, but the first paragraph is a bit "textbook." It starts with general market stats ($4 billion in sales) that most AER readers already roughly know. 

**The pitch the paper should have:**
"Whether the entry of 'sin' industries harms local communities is a central question in urban economics, yet identifying causal effects is nearly impossible because retailers choose locations based on the very neighborhood characteristics that drive property values. This paper provides the first causally identified estimate of cannabis retail externalities by exploiting Illinois’s 2021–2023 license lotteries, which decoupled the *right to enter* from strategic market timing. We find that even with a 'clean' entry, dispensaries impose a 6% price penalty that is concentrated in high-income areas—suggesting that cultural stigma, not crime, is the primary economic friction of legalization."

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**Contribution:** The paper provides the first quasi-experimental estimate of cannabis dispensary externalities on property values using a randomized license lottery.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It differentiates itself well from Conklin et al. (2020) and others by highlighting the "site-selection problem." However, it needs to be more aggressive about why "lottery randomization of *who*" helps "randomization of *where*."
*   **Framing:** It is framed as answering a question about the WORLD (how legalization affects wealth), which is strong.
*   **Explanation:** A smart economist would say: "It’s a lottery-based DiD that finds dispensaries are a mild disamenity."
*   **What would make it bigger?** The "stigma" vs "crime" mechanism needs more "meat." If the author could link the "stigma" finding to political economy—do neighborhoods that voted *against* legalization see larger price drops?—the contribution moves from "urban externality" to "political economy of social norms."

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
*   **Neighbors:** Linden & Rockoff (2008) on sex offenders; Pope (2008) on fear of crime; Anderson & Rees (2023) on cannabis economics.
*   **Positioning:** It should **synthesize** Linden & Rockoff and Pope. The paper currently sits in the "cannabis literature," but it should position itself as a "Hedonic/Externalities" paper that happens to use cannabis.
*   **Missing Conversations:** The paper misses the "NIMBY" and "Land Use" literature. Why are these effects concentrated in high-income areas? Is it because they have more "voice" or because their housing supply is more inelastic?
*   **Right Conversation?** It’s having the "Property Value" conversation. It should try to have the "Social Normalization" conversation. Does the effect fade over time as dispensaries become "normal" like liquor stores?

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC
*   **Setup:** Cannabis is legalizing everywhere; we don't know the local costs.
*   **Tension:** Prior studies are biased because dispensaries pick "bad" or "up-and-coming" neighborhoods on purpose. We don't know if the price drop is the shop or the neighborhood.
*   **Resolution:** The lottery breaks this link. Even with "clean" entry, prices fall 6%.
*   **Implications:** The cost of legalization is a wealth transfer away from homeowners near retail, particularly in wealthy areas.

**Evaluation:** The arc is clear. However, the "Resolution" is slightly weakened by the author's own admission of "zip-code centroid" geocoding. This creates a "tension" in the data quality that distracts from the narrative.

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
*   **Lead Fact:** "Opening a pot shop is like a 6% tax on your neighbor's house, but only if your neighbors are rich."
*   **Reaction:** People will lean in. The "affluent neighborhood" heterogeneity is the most interesting and counter-intuitive result.
*   **Follow-up:** "Is it because of the smell, the people loitering, or just the 'type' of person who shops there?"
*   **Null Results:** The null effect on violent crime is a crucial "So What?" because it kills the most common political argument against dispensaries.

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
*   **The Geocoding Issue:** Moving the "zip-code centroid" discussion to the appendix or framing it as a "lower bound" is essential. In the main text, it reads like a "failed data" confession.
*   **The Lottery Detail:** Section 2 is a bit dense. Move the specific names of the four lotteries to a table; focus the prose on the *randomness* and the *constraints* on winners.
*   **Front-loading:** The crime results (Table 4) are just as important as the price results for the "narrative." They should be integrated earlier to build the "stigma" argument.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The gap is **Ambition**. Currently, it feels like a very high-quality *Applied Economics Letters* or *Journal of Urban Economics* paper. To be **AER**, it needs to move beyond "estimating a number" to "explaining a phenomenon."

**Single Most Impactful Advice:** 
Exploit the "Social Equity" aspect of the lottery. Illinois specifically gave licenses to "Justice Involved" individuals. Is the 6% price drop higher for social-equity licensees than for "corporate" licensees? If you can show that the "stigma" is actually a "race/class bias" against the owners, you have a Top-5 paper on the economics of discrimination and land use.
