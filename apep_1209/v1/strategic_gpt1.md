## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple, policy-relevant question: when legal cannabis dispensaries open, what happens to nearby housing values? Using Illinois’s dispensary-license lotteries as a plausibly cleaner source of variation than typical dispensary siting decisions, the paper argues that nearby home prices fall modestly, with larger declines in higher-income neighborhoods, while reported drug crime rises but other crime does not.

Why should a busy economist care? Because legalization is no longer a niche policy question; it is a major reorganization of local retail space, neighborhood amenities, and regulated vice markets. If dispensaries are capitalized into housing prices, that is a first-order welfare and political-economy margin through which legalization affects households.

### Does the paper articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Not quite. The current opening gets to the topic quickly, but it leads with Illinois facts, then a broad claim that this is “the single largest financial externality” most families will ever experience, which feels inflated and invites skepticism. Then it pivots immediately to a literature gap framed as “none of them are causal,” which is more seminar-room than world-question.

The first two paragraphs should do less throat-clearing about prior estimates and more to foreground the economic question in the world: legalization changes neighborhoods, and economists do not know whether markets treat dispensaries as amenities, nuisances, or mostly irrelevant. Then the paper should state, very cleanly, that Illinois’s lottery provides unusually credible variation to study that question.

### The pitch the paper should have

Legal cannabis is now a durable part of the urban retail landscape, but we still do not know how households value living near a dispensary. Do dispensaries look to the housing market like a coffee shop, a liquor store, or a local disamenity? Using Illinois’s randomized dispensary-license lotteries and property transactions in Cook County, this paper shows that dispensary openings modestly reduce nearby housing prices, with effects concentrated in higher-income neighborhoods, while increasing reported drug crime but not violent or property crime. The broader message is that cannabis retail creates small but real neighborhood externalities, driven more by perception and stigma than by serious public-safety deterioration.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### Contribution in one sentence

The paper’s contribution is to provide unusually credible evidence on how legal cannabis dispensary entry affects nearby housing values, using Illinois’s lottery-based licensing regime to study neighborhood capitalization effects.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?

Only partly. The paper does say prior dispensary/property-value work suffers from endogenous siting, and it emphasizes the Illinois lottery. That is the right instinct. But the differentiation is still too generic: “first causal estimate” / “first with random variation” is not enough on its own, especially because the design does not literally randomize storefront locations. Strategically, the paper should claim a more defensible distinction: not “fully causal because lottery,” but “the cleanest evidence to date because entry is less selected than in prior settings.”

It also needs to be clearer about which papers it is improving on and how:
- papers on marijuana dispensaries and housing values in Denver/California/Washington that rely on observational spatial comparisons,
- broader hedonic papers on localized disamenities,
- cannabis papers focused on crime, health, or labor market effects rather than neighborhood capitalization.

Right now, a reader could still summarize this as “another reduced-form paper on dispensaries and house prices, but in Illinois.”

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a gap in a literature?

Too much as a literature gap. The strongest version is a world question: **How does the arrival of legal cannabis retail change neighborhood desirability, and through what channel?** The literature gap should support that, not lead it.

### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain what's new?

At present, they could probably say: “It’s a DiD paper on dispensaries and property values using Illinois lottery licenses, which maybe makes the siting cleaner.” That is decent but not memorable. They are less likely to say: “It shows housing markets treat dispensaries as small neighborhood disamenities and that this seems to operate through stigma rather than conventional crime.”

That second version is what the paper should aim for.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

A few concrete possibilities:

1. **Shift the core framing from ‘effect on prices’ to ‘what kind of neighborhood externality is cannabis retail?’**  
   This elevates the paper from a local hedonic estimate to a broader statement about legalized vice markets.

2. **Tighten the mechanism evidence around stigma versus nuisance versus crime.**  
   Right now the mechanism claims are more interpretive than central. If the paper can more persuasively distinguish visible disorder, regulatory scrutiny, parking/traffic, and stigma/perception, the contribution becomes conceptually richer.

3. **Make the comparison more explicit.**  
   The most interesting comparison is not just before/after near/far; it is whether dispensaries resemble liquor stores, pharmacies, or other regulated retail entrants in the housing market. Even a framing comparison, if not an empirical one, would help.

4. **Expand beyond one outcome if possible.**  
   Housing prices are important, but neighborhood retail externalities are often multi-dimensional. If there were a strong complementary outcome on sales volume, time on market, buyer composition, or neighborhood turnover, the paper would feel more ambitious.

5. **Lean harder into political economy / incidence.**  
   “Who dislikes dispensaries?” is potentially as important as the average effect. The high-income heterogeneity hints at that. If developed, this could become a paper about distributive conflict over legalized cannabis, not just a property-value paper.

At present, the paper gestures toward some of these, but it does not really inhabit those conversations.

The more interesting conversation is: **What happens when a formerly illicit market becomes a legal neighborhood retail presence?** That connects urban economics, public economics, and political economy. It broadens the audience substantially.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Cannabis legalization is spreading, and retail dispensaries are becoming common neighborhood institutions. Yet economists do not know whether households view them as amenities, nuisances, or largely neutral retail entrants.

### Tension

The natural way to study this—compare places near dispensaries to other places—is badly compromised by endogenous siting. Operators choose where to locate, and those neighborhood traits also affect property values. So we lack credible evidence on whether dispensaries themselves change neighborhood desirability.

### Resolution

Using Illinois’s lottery-based license allocation as a cleaner institutional setting, the paper finds modest declines in nearby housing prices, concentrated in higher-income areas, along with increases in reported drug crime but not in property or violent crime.

### Implications

Legal cannabis retail appears to impose small but real local externalities, and those externalities may reflect perception and stigma more than serious criminal spillovers. That matters for welfare, local regulation, zoning battles, and the political sustainability of legalization.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the ingredients, but the arc is not fully disciplined. At times it reads like:
- a property-values paper,
- a quasi-natural-experiment paper about lottery licensing,
- a crime paper,
- and a mechanism paper about stigma.

Those pieces are related, but the manuscript has not quite decided what the main story is.

### What story should it be telling?

The paper should tell one central story:

**Legalization does not just affect consumers and tax revenues; it changes the neighborhood geography of a formerly illicit market. Illinois’s lottery lets us see how households value that change. Housing markets register dispensaries as modest local disamenities, especially in affluent neighborhoods, and the pattern is more consistent with stigma/visible-drug-market perceptions than with broader crime increases.**

That is a coherent AER-style narrative. The current paper is close, but not yet as tightly organized around that spine as it should be.

That is the most dinner-party-relevant line because it juxtaposes a real market effect with a non-result on the more sensational crime margin.

They would **lean in initially**, because legalization plus home prices is a live, intuitive topic. But the interest will quickly depend on whether the presenter can answer: “Why should I believe this is telling me something general, rather than one noisy local estimate?” That is where the current paper is not yet fully persuasive in framing terms.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Most likely:
- “So is this really about crime, or just stigma?”
- “Why are effects bigger in high-income areas?”
- “Are dispensaries more like liquor stores or more like nuisance land uses?”
- “Is this specific to Chicago/Cook County?”

Those are exactly the questions the paper should organize itself around.

### If the findings are modest: is the modest result interesting?

Yes, potentially. In fact, **the modesty is part of the story**. If legalization opponents fear major neighborhood deterioration and supporters claim no local costs, then a modest capitalization effect with limited crime spillovers is informative. But the paper needs to say that more forcefully. Right now, the modesty sometimes reads as statistical thinness rather than substantive calibration.

The value of the result is: **dispensaries matter, but less than many people think, and in a way more tied to perception than to severe public-safety harms.**

That is interesting. The paper should own that.
