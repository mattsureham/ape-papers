## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple, policy-relevant question: when legal cannabis retail arrives in a neighborhood, what happens to nearby housing values? Using Illinois’s dispensary license lotteries as a plausibly cleaner source of variation than standard market entry, the paper argues that dispensary openings modestly reduce nearby property prices and raise reported drug crime, with effects concentrated in higher-income neighborhoods.

A busy economist should care because cannabis legalization is one of the largest ongoing policy changes in local retail regulation, and housing values are where diffuse neighborhood externalities get capitalized. If the paper really could credibly measure how legal cannabis retail changes neighborhood welfare, that is an important question.

### Does the paper articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Not quite. The current introduction gets to the question quickly, but the pitch is muddled by two choices:

1. It overstates the importance in a slightly breathless way (“the single largest financial externality…”), which sounds promotional rather than authoritative.
2. It frames the novelty too quickly as “lottery-assigned” / “genuinely random variation in location,” when the paper itself later concedes that location is not randomized. That weakens the strategic positioning right at the top.

The first two paragraphs should do three things more cleanly:
- start with the world question,
- explain why the answer is hard to know,
- preview the Illinois institutional feature as an unusually useful but incomplete solution.

### The pitch the paper should have

> As cannabis legalization expands, cities and homeowners want to know whether dispensaries are neighborhood amenities, nuisances, or mostly irrelevant. Housing prices are the natural place to look because they summarize how local residents value all of the traffic, stigma, safety, and convenience effects of retail cannabis.
>
> But this question is hard because dispensaries do not locate randomly: they choose neighborhoods with particular demand, rents, and zoning environments. This paper uses Illinois’s dispensary licensing lotteries—an unusually policy-driven expansion of cannabis retail—to study how nearby property values and crime change when dispensaries open, and finds modest negative capitalization in housing markets, especially in higher-income neighborhoods.

That is a cleaner AER-style opening: world question first, institutional opportunity second, moderation in the claim of exogeneity.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper’s contribution is to provide evidence, using Illinois’s lottery-based dispensary expansion, that legal cannabis retail modestly lowers nearby housing values and changes the composition of reported crime, with effects appearing to reflect neighborhood stigma more than serious crime spillovers.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest papers?

Only partially. The paper tries to differentiate itself on design—“lottery-assigned” dispensaries—but that differentiation is strategically shaky because the lottery assigns licenses, not sites. So the paper’s current “what’s new” is more fragile than the authors seem to realize.

Right now, the paper risks sounding like:
- another hedonic paper on local disamenities,
- another DiD paper around retail openings,
- another cannabis paper with a cleaner-than-usual institutional setting.

That is not yet enough for AER unless the introduction very clearly explains what we learn about the world that prior papers could not tell us.

### World question or literature gap?

It is trying to be about the world, which is good: what are the neighborhood externalities of legal cannabis retail? But the paper repeatedly slips into “first causal estimate,” “stronger exogeneity,” and “fills a gap” language. That makes it sound like a methods-adjacent contribution rather than a substantive one.

For AER, the framing needs to be much more decisively:

- not “there is no causal paper on dispensary property values,”
- but “we do not know whether legal vice retail harms neighborhoods, and that matters for zoning, local opposition, and welfare analysis.”

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?

At present, maybe—but not crisply. They would probably say: “It’s a DiD paper on cannabis dispensary openings in Illinois, using the license lottery as suggestive exogeneity.” That is not a strong enough takeaway.

You want them instead to say:

> “It’s one of the first papers to study the local equilibrium effects of legal cannabis retail in a setting where expansion was driven by a state lottery rather than standard endogenous market entry, and it finds small but real housing-market disamenities.”

That is better, but the paper needs to earn that framing more carefully.

### What would make the contribution bigger?

Most importantly: **make the paper about the neighborhood incidence of cannabis legalization, not just about one hedonic estimate.**

Specific ways to make it bigger:

- **A different framing:** Recast from “dispensary effect on property values” to “how does legal cannabis retail get capitalized into neighborhood welfare?” That makes housing values the summary object, and crime/mechanisms supporting evidence.
- **A stronger comparison:** Compare dispensaries explicitly to other controversial but legal retail entrants—liquor stores, payday lenders, smoke shops, pharmacies, methadone clinics, or adult-use retail more broadly. Without this, the result is hard to interpret.
- **A better mechanism set:** Right now “stigma” feels asserted, not central. If stigma is the mechanism, the paper should lean into variation where stigma should matter: political preferences, prior cannabis attitudes, school proximity, homeowner share, racial composition, prior criminalization intensity, etc.
- **A broader implication:** Explain whether the result implies meaningful local costs of legalization or just transitional NIMBY concerns. That is the real policy question.

The biggest missed opportunity is that the paper almost has a political economy story—local opposition to cannabis retail may be driven by perceived neighborhood decline rather than actual violent/property crime. That is more interesting than a standalone 6 percent hedonic estimate.

That connects cannabis to a broader economic question that many more readers care about.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Cannabis legalization has expanded rapidly, and dispensaries are now routine local institutions. Yet communities still fight over them, often because of fears about neighborhood quality and property values.

### Tension

We do not know whether those fears are real, because dispensaries choose locations endogenously and previous evidence is mixed. The key puzzle is whether legal cannabis retail imposes meaningful local costs, and if so whether those costs come from actual crime/disorder or from social perceptions.

### Resolution

Using Illinois’s lottery-era expansion, the paper finds modest declines in nearby housing prices and some increase in reported drug crime, but not in property or violent crime. The paper interprets this pattern as evidence that the main capitalization channel is stigma or neighborhood perception rather than severe public-safety deterioration.

### Implications

If true, the findings imply that local opposition to cannabis retail may reflect real but limited capitalization costs, concentrated where stigma is higher. This matters for zoning, compensation, and how we think about the incidence of legalization across neighborhoods.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has a **serviceable but underdeveloped** one.

The results are not random; there is a real story here. But the paper currently reads too much like:
- institutional setup,
- estimation,
- main table,
- heterogeneity,
- crime table,
- conclusion.

The “story” emerges only after the reader does the interpretive work. For AER, the paper should tell the reader much more clearly:

1. People fear dispensaries for two reasons: tangible disorder and intangible stigma.
2. Housing markets tell us whether neighborhoods perceive dispensaries as harmful overall.
3. Illinois gives a particularly informative expansion setting.
4. The answer is: small negative capitalization, concentrated where stigma should bite, with little evidence of serious crime spillovers.

That is a coherent arc. The current draft only intermittently delivers it.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?

“Lottery-era cannabis dispensary openings in Illinois appear to lower nearby home prices by about 6 percent, but they don’t increase violent or property crime.”

That is the hook.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

They would **initially lean in**, because cannabis plus housing values is intuitive and policy-relevant. But the next question would come very fast:

> “Wait—does the lottery really randomize locations, or just license awards?”

And that is exactly the strategic vulnerability of the paper. The first interesting fact prompts a follow-up that targets the paper’s framing, not its substance.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Likely one of these:
- “Why should I believe this is about dispensary externalities rather than where they chose to open?”
- “Is 6 percent big or small relative to other neighborhood disamenities?”
- “If crime doesn’t go up, what exactly is being capitalized?”
- “Why higher-income neighborhoods?”

Those are actually good questions. The paper should be structured to answer them explicitly and early.

### If the findings are modest: is that interesting?

Yes, **modest effects can be interesting here**, because the policy debate is highly charged. A finding that dispensaries are neither harmless amenities nor neighborhood-destroying crime magnets is informative.

But the paper does not yet fully capitalize on that. It should make the stronger case that learning “the local costs are real but limited, and not driven by serious crime” is exactly the kind of policy calibration economists can provide when public rhetoric is polarized.

Right now, the modesty of the result makes the paper feel a little smaller than it should.

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

### What would make it read better?

A few straightforward changes.

#### 1. Shorten the institutional detail in the introduction
The exact names of the four lotteries, applicant counts, and point thresholds are too much for the first pages. That material belongs in the institutional background, not in the core pitch.

#### 2. Move limitations later and compress them
The introduction spends too much high-value real estate telling the reader why the design is imperfect. Some caution is good; a full limitations paragraph before the reader is invested is not.

In a top-journal paper, the introduction should mostly answer:
- what is the question,
- why does it matter,
- what do you find,
- why should I believe it is informative.

Not every caveat needs to appear before page 4.

#### 3. Front-load the interpretive comparison
The comparison to liquor stores vs sex offenders is actually memorable. Bring that logic earlier. It helps non-specialists understand the economic magnitude.

#### 4. Tighten the contribution paragraph
The current “three literatures” paragraph is generic. Replace with one or two sharper paragraphs that define the primary conversation and secondary conversations.

#### 5. Promote mechanism framing earlier
The paper’s interesting interpretive angle is not just “negative effect.” It is “negative effect without serious-crime effects, suggesting perception/stigma matters.” That should appear in the introduction’s preview of findings.

#### 6. De-emphasize “methodological contribution”
Saying “the most important contribution is methodological” shrinks the paper. For AER, the contribution should be substantive. The design is a means, not the product.

#### 7. The conclusion should do more than summarize
The conclusion is competent but mostly recap. It should end by sharpening the larger implication:
- how legalization’s benefits and costs are locally distributed,
- whether opposition is economically rational or exaggerated,
- and what this implies for municipal siting policy.

### Are there buried results that should be in the main text?

The robustness appendix note that very close effects may be different and the effect zone seems broader than hyper-local is potentially useful for the narrative. If the argument is about stigma/perception rather than odor/loitering, the spatial shape of the effect is central, not auxiliary.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

This paper is not primarily suffering from econometrics in the editorial sense; it is suffering from **positioning and ambition**.

### What is the gap?

Mostly:

- **Framing problem:** The paper sells itself as cleaner causal identification, but the really interesting contribution is a broader one about the local incidence of legal cannabis retail.
- **Ambition problem:** The current manuscript is a competent application with one main reduced-form result and a crime table. That is more field-journal scale than AER scale unless the story becomes more conceptually important.
- **Novelty problem, partially:** “DiD around controversial retail openings with housing prices” is a familiar template. The paper needs a more distinct conceptual payoff.

### What would excite the top 10 people in this field?

A paper that answered:

> When a previously illicit market becomes legal and spatially visible, how much of the local backlash reflects real neighborhood harms versus stigma, and how does that get priced into housing markets?

That is bigger than “dispensaries lower house prices by 6 percent in Cook County.”

To get there, the authors need to:
- stop overclaiming random location assignment,
- sharpen the substantive question,
- build the mechanism and welfare interpretation into the core narrative,
- and position the paper in the larger literature on controversial retail and neighborhood change.

### Single most impactful piece of advice

**Reframe the paper away from “lottery-assigned causal estimate of dispensary effects” and toward “what housing markets reveal about the neighborhood incidence of legal cannabis retail,” using Illinois’s lottery expansion as an unusually informative setting rather than the entire claim to novelty.**

That one shift would improve the introduction, contribution statement, literature positioning, and overall ambition all at once.
