**Summary**

This paper uses the Swiss Second Home Initiative’s sharp 20% threshold to study whether banning new second-home construction reallocated housing toward permanent residents. A regression discontinuity design on the federal housing inventory finds no statistically significant jump in primary-home share at the cutoff, while municipalities above the threshold experienced a statistically significant increase in total dwelling stock. These results suggest that the ban redirected construction toward permitted (primary) housing without converting existing vacation stock.

**Essential Points**

1. **Power and interpretation of the null on composition.** The main outcome — change in the primary-home share — yields a point estimate of +1.58 percentage points with a p-value of 0.21. Given the clear policy goal, the paper should discuss statistical power more explicitly. Is the null due to a small effect size or insufficient precision? Reporting minimally detectable effects within the chosen bandwidth or formally conducting a power analysis (e.g., the smallest effect that would be significant at conventional levels) would help readers assess whether the absence of significance is substantively meaningful. At present, the paper leans toward interpreting the result as “the ban failed to convert” when the confidence interval still includes economically non-negligible values.

2. **Mechanism behind the positive dwelling-stock effect.** The interpretation that the ban redirected construction toward primary homes hinges on the assumption that the (+3.6%) difference in dwelling-stock growth reflects new primary residences rather than continued vacation development under different classifications or measurement artefacts. The paper should provide more direct evidence for this mechanism. For instance, can the author show that the additional dwellings are registered as primary residences in the inventory (rather than, say, mixed-use or short-term rentals)? Alternatively, can building permit data, developer activity, or municipal reports be used to trace the nature of the new construction? Without such evidence, the causal chain from “ban on secondary homes” to “more primary-home construction” rests on a plausibility argument alone.

3. **Dynamic results and potential confounders.** The dynamic panel RDD shows statistically significant effects up through 2024 that dissipate in 2025, and the discussion attributes this to the 2024 partial relaxation and potential catch-up by controls. However, there is no formal test or discussion of other concurrent shocks (e.g., the COVID-period tourism slump, regional economic trends, or housing market cycles) that might differentially affect municipalities near the cutoff over time. The author should clarify whether such shocks coincide with the observed pattern and, if possible, control for or rule out alternative explanations. For example, showing that pre-trend tests are flat before 2017 or incorporating time-varying regional controls could strengthen the claim that the attenuation in 2025 stems from policy relaxation rather than other confounders.

**Suggestions**

1. **Clarify running-variable measurement and timing.** The paper states that the running variable is the “baseline second-home share,” but it would help to be explicit about when the baseline measurement was taken relative to the 2012 vote and the 2016 law implementation. If the baseline corresponds to the earliest available inventory (2017/18), then municipalities may already be subject to the ban, which could bias the estimated discontinuity if the policy had already influenced second-home shares by then. Explicitly clarifying that the running variable is pre-treatment (or, if not, presenting evidence that the policy did not already affect the running variable) will reassure readers about the validity of the RDD.

2. **Expand placebo and falsification checks.** The paper uses placebo cutoffs at 15%, 25%, and 30%, which is commendable. To further bolster credibility, consider additional falsification exercises, such as applying the same RDD to outcomes that the policy should not plausibly affect (e.g., municipality-level employment in non-tourism sectors) or demonstrating that the effect on primary-share change is absent before the policy took effect (if pre-2017 data is available). Another idea is to check whether the results hold when restricting to municipalities without alpine geography to ensure that geography-driven trends are not driving the discontinuity.

3. **Investigate heterogeneous effects.** The interpretation that the ban induces substitution into primary homes could be sharpened by exploring heterogeneity. Do the dwelling-stock growth effects concentrate in municipalities with higher demand pressure for primary housing (e.g., close to regional centers)? Are the effects stronger in cantons that actively enforce or monitor the ban? Presenting heterogeneity by canton, pre-existing vacancy rates, or demographic variables would also help assess the generalizability of the findings.

4. **Link to vacancy or population outcomes.** The core policy motivation is converting “cold beds” into permanent residences. If possible, augmenting the analysis with outcomes like vacancy rates, year-round occupancy, or permanent population growth would directly speak to that goal. The paper mentions empty dwelling rates in the manifest but does not report them. Even if these data are available only for a subset of municipalities, showing that vacancy rates do not decline (or even increase) above the cutoff would strengthen the argument that composition did not shift.

5. **Document the 2024 relaxation more rigorously.** The attenuation in 2025 is attributed to the October 2024 relaxations, but the paper could describe the extent and timing of those relaxations in more detail (e.g., which types of second-home conversions became allowed, whether municipalities had to opt in). If data permit, the author could test whether municipalities that took advantage of the relaxation differ from those that did not, or whether the relaxation had heterogeneous effects depending on the type of exemption.

6. **Discuss external validity and policy implications carefully.** The concluding paragraphs generalize to short-term rental regulations around the world. Given that the Swiss policy is a hard construction ban rather than a restriction on short-term rentals per se, it would be helpful to explicitly delineate similarities and differences to other contexts. Also, discussing under what institutional conditions the substitution pattern might not arise (e.g., if all primary housing were already fully built-out) would provide nuance for policymakers drawing lessons.

7. **Provide code and replication materials.** The paper mentions a repository, which is great. Ensure that the replication package includes the code to (a) reconstruct the running variable and outcomes, (b) estimate the dynamic RDDs, and (c) produce the robustness tables. Transparency about data cleaning decisions (e.g., dealing with municipalities that merge or split) would further enhance credibility.

8. **Address standard errors and clustered inference.** RDD estimates are reported with bias-corrected SEs, but the paper could clarify whether inference accounts for clustering at the canton level or other spatial dependence, especially since municipalities within the same canton may share unobserved shocks. If clustering is infeasible due to the small number of treated municipalities near the cutoff, acknowledging the limitation would be helpful.

9. **Interpret standardized effect sizes with caution.** The appendix classifies the null result on primary share as a “Large positive” SDE, which may confuse readers given the statistical insignificance. It would be clearer to distinguish between effect magnitude and statistical significance—perhaps by presenting standardized effects alongside confidence intervals or explicitly noting that the “Large positive” label refers to magnitude even though the estimate is not distinguishable from zero.

Implementing these suggestions would deepen the empirical credibility and policy relevance of the paper without necessarily altering its main contribution.
