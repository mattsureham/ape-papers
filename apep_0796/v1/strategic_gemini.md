## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
The paper examines the Swiss "Second Home Initiative," a 20% cap on vacation homes, to see if banning luxury/secondary construction actually converts a town’s housing stock into primary residences for locals. Using a sharp RDD, it finds that while the ban successfully stops new vacation units, it triggers a "substitution response" where developers simply build more units labeled as "primary," resulting in higher total housing growth but zero net change in the town's social composition. Busy economists should care because this suggests that category-specific supply restrictions are "leaky"—they change the labels on new buildings rather than the character of existing communities.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates the *mechanism* well in the abstract, but the first two paragraphs of the introduction are slightly too focused on the Swiss institutional context ("did it work?") rather than the broader economic phenomenon of regulatory substitution.

**The Pitch the Paper Should Have:**
"Can quantity restrictions on one segment of the housing market reallocate the existing stock to preferred users? We exploit a sharp 20% regulatory threshold in Switzerland that bans new vacation home construction to test whether such 'supply-side' interventions solve the problem of hollowed-out resort communities. We find that while the ban is binding, it fails to shift housing composition because it triggers a 'hydraulic' response: developers substitute toward permitted housing categories, increasing total supply without changing the relative share of permanent residents."

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**Contribution:** The paper identifies a "substitution channel" in housing supply, where a ban on one dwelling type stimulates growth in an adjacent category, leaving the aggregate composition of the stock unchanged.

- **Differentiated?** Yes. It moves beyond price effects (Hilber & Schöni, 2020) to focus on the *compositional* and *substitution* effects, which is a significant step forward in the land-use literature.
- **Framing:** It is currently framed as "Did the Swiss ban work?" (Literature/Policy gap). To be AER-level, it must be framed as "How do developers respond to category-specific quantity constraints?" (World/Theory question).
- **Explanation:** A smart economist would say: "It's an RDD showing that when you ban second homes, developers just build 'primary' homes instead, so the town stays just as empty/full as before."
- **Bigger Contribution:** To make this bigger, the author needs to explore the *nature* of this new 'primary' construction. Are these "primary" homes actually being lived in, or are they "primary" only on paper (regulatory arbitrage)? Data on resident registration or electricity usage would turn this from a "labeling" result into a "welfare" result.

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
- **Closest Neighbors:** Hilber & Schöni (2020) on Swiss prices; Glaeser/Gyourko on supply constraints; Buchak et al. (2018) on regulatory arbitrage (shadow banking).
- **Positioning:** It should build on the "Housing Supply Elasticity" literature (Saiz, 2010). Instead of treating supply as a single aggregate, it should argue that elasticity is *category-specific*.
- **Unexpected Conversation:** The connection to Buchak et al. (2018) is the strongest strategic move. Framing this as **"Regulatory Arbitrage in Real Estate"** elevates it from a local Swiss study to a general principle of regulatory economics.

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC
- **Setup:** Policymakers use bans to reallocate scarce resources (housing) from "socially unproductive" (vacationers) to "productive" (residents) uses.
- **Tension:** If supply is fungible, a ban on one category might just push investment into the next best thing, leaving the social objective unmet.
- **Resolution:** The Swiss ban increased total construction (the substitution) but failed to move the needle on the percentage of primary residents.
- **Implications:** Supply bans are insufficient without demand-side teeth (taxes, occupancy requirements).

**Evaluation:** The arc is currently "Serviceable." It feels a bit like a "well-identified null" until the growth result appears. The paper needs to lead with the "Substitution Growth" as a surprising feature, not a secondary finding.

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
- **Lead Fact:** "When Switzerland banned vacation homes, towns actually started building *faster*, but it didn't bring in a single extra permanent resident."
- **Reaction:** People will lean in—it's counter-intuitive.
- **Follow-up:** "Wait, so who is buying the 'primary' homes?" This is the paper's current weak point. If the author can't answer who is in those new houses, the "So What?" loses its punch.

---

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
- **Front-loading:** Move the "Substitution Mechanism" discussion (currently in Section 6) much earlier. The fact that total stock growth *increases* is the most AER-worthy finding.
- **Appendix:** The "Dynamic RDD" (Section 5.2) shows the effect "faded" by 2025. This is actually quite damaging to the long-run story. If the results are transitory or driven by a 2024 law change, that needs to be a core part of the narrative or moved to a "Persistence" section.
- **Eliminate:** The standardized effect size table (Table 6) feels like "filler" for a top-tier journal; the raw coefficients and their economic interpretation are what matter.

---

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The gap is **Ambition.** Right now, it’s a very clean, very competent evaluation of a Swiss policy. To be an AER paper, it needs to be a paper about the **Lability of Housing Labels.** 

**The Single Most Impactful Advice:** Prove that the "primary" homes built after the ban are actually being used as "shadow" second homes. If you can show that electricity consumption didn't rise alongside the "primary home" construction, you have a blockbuster paper on the futility of category-based zoning.
