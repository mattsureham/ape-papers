### 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper investigates whether the length of public comment periods actually influences the substance of federal regulations. By matching 3,703 proposed rules to their final versions, it discovers a striking "implementation gap": while Executive Order 12866 ostensibly mandates a 60-day floor for significant rules, agencies in practice only extend windows by a negligible 3.4 days, and these longer windows are not associated with increased rule revision.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this pitch with unusual clarity in the second and third paragraphs. It avoids the common trap of hiding the "punchline." The current opening is strong, but to be an AER-level pitch, it should slightly elevate the stakes: it’s not just about a "descriptive fact," but about the **efficacy of democratic administrative constraints.**

---

### 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**The Contribution:** The paper documents a massive decoupling between doctrinal administrative law and agency practice, showing that "significant" rule designations fail to generate a meaningful "treatment" of extra deliberation time, and that variation in time has no observational link to rule change.

*   **Differentiation:** It moves beyond the "input" literature (Yackee, Balla) which looks at *who* comments, and focuses on the "output" (how the rule changes).
*   **Framing:** It is currently framed as "answering a question about the world" (Do agencies listen?), which is strong.
*   **Clarity:** A smart economist would say: "It’s a paper showing that a major regulatory 'requirement' is actually a myth and that more time doesn't lead to more revision."
*   **Making it bigger:** The contribution would be bigger if it could distinguish *why* there is no correlation. Is it "Capture" (the rule is pre-determined) or "Capacity" (agencies are too backlogged to process more info)? Even a suggestive look at agency-level characteristics (beyond volume) would help.

---

### 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

*   **Neighbors:** McCubbins, Noll, & Weingast (1987) on administrative procedures as political control; Yackee (2006) on interest group influence; and the legal literature on EO 12866 (Coglianese).
*   **Strategy:** The paper "synthesizes" legal doctrine with empirical reality. It should "attack" the legal scholarship more aggressively for its naive assumption that EO 12866 is a binding constraint.
*   **Niche vs. Broad:** It risks being seen as "Public Administration." To get into the AER, it needs to speak to the **Political Economy of Bureaucracy.** 
*   **Missing Conversation:** It should connect to the literature on "Street-Level Bureaucracy" or "Soft Law"—situations where high-level mandates are ignored by the agents responsible for implementation.

---

### 4. NARRATIVE ARC

*   **Setup:** We have laws (APA, EO 12866) designed to ensure the public can change the government's mind.
*   **Tension:** We assume these laws work. Specifically, "Significance" should be a major shock to the deliberative process.
*   **Resolution:** It’s a dud. The 60-day floor doesn't exist in reality, and the "time" lever is broken.
*   **Implications:** Calls for "better" or "longer" comment periods as a way to improve rule quality are likely misplaced because agencies have already "checked out" by the time the window opens.

**Evaluation:** The arc is exceptionally clear. The "failure" of the IV is used as a narrative feature, not a bug, which is a sophisticated way to tell a story.

---

### 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

*   **The Fact:** "The government is required to give 60 days for major rules, but in reality, they give basically the same amount of time as they do for minor rules—and even when they give more time, they don't change the rules anyway."
*   **Reaction:** People will lean in. It confirms a cynical (and thus interesting) view of the "Notice and Comment" process as theater.
*   **Follow-up:** "Does this mean the agencies have already decided the final rule before they even ask for comments?" (This is the "So What?" the paper needs to address more deeply).

---

### 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

*   **Front-loading:** The paper is already well-front-loaded.
*   **Appendix:** Move the "TF-IDF" results for the 128 rules into the main body. Even if the N is small, text-based distance is a much "sexier" and more modern metric than page counts for an AER audience.
*   **Eliminate:** The discussion of "rate limits" on the API (p. 10) belongs in a footnote or appendix; it clutters the narrative.

---

### 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The gap is **Ambition.** Right now, it’s a very high-quality "Note" or a short paper. To make it a full AER article, the author needs to dig into the *mechanism* of the implementation gap. 

**The Single Most Impactful Advice:** Shift the focus from "The IV failed" to "Why does the EO fail?" If the author can show *which* types of agencies or *which* political environments lead to more or less compliance with the 60-day floor (e.g., does compliance drop during "midnight rulemaking" at the end of an administration?), it becomes a major contribution to political economy.
