## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple, policy-relevant question: did France’s pioneering 2017 “right-to-disconnect” law actually reduce overwork among the workers most exposed to after-hours digital communication? Using cross-country and cross-occupation comparisons, the paper’s answer is no: France’s negotiation-based mandate appears not to have reduced long hours for knowledge workers, despite becoming the template for similar laws elsewhere.

A busy economist should care because the question is larger than France. Governments around the world are trying to regulate “always-on” work; this paper speaks to whether soft labor regulation can change work behavior in modern digital labor markets.

### Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Not quite. The ingredients are there, but the current opening is too journalistic and too “gap in the literature” oriented. It starts with a Slack-message vignette, then says “no economist has estimated whether the law works. This paper fills that gap.” That is serviceable, but not AER-level framing. The paper should open with the world question, not the literature hole.

### What the first two paragraphs should say instead

Something like:

> Digital communication has made high-skill work increasingly boundaryless: many employees are reachable everywhere and at all times. In response, a growing number of governments have introduced “right-to-disconnect” laws intended to curb after-hours work contact and protect workers’ nonwork time. But it remains unclear whether these laws actually change work behavior or merely formalize rights that firms can ignore.
>
> This paper studies the world’s first such policy: France’s 2017 right-to-disconnect law. I ask whether the law reduced overwork among the workers most exposed to after-hours digital demands. Using variation across countries, occupations, and time, I find no detectable decline in long working hours or usual weekly hours among high-connectivity workers in France relative to comparable workers elsewhere. The broader lesson is that negotiation-based disconnect mandates may be too weak to alter actual work patterns.

That is the pitch. It frames the paper as about the effectiveness of soft regulation in digital labor markets, not merely as “the first evaluation.”

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### Contribution in one sentence

The paper’s contribution is to show that France’s landmark right-to-disconnect law did not measurably reduce overwork among the digitally connected workers it was designed to protect, suggesting that negotiation-based labor mandates may be ineffective in changing actual working-time behavior.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?

Only partially. The paper does differentiate itself from broad working-time and work-culture literatures by saying “this is the first causal evaluation of any right-to-disconnect law.” That is clear enough at a superficial level. But it is not yet differentiated in a way that signals a major economic contribution.

Right now the novelty sounds like: “no one has run this design on this policy yet.” That is not the same as: “this changes how we think about labor regulation.” The paper needs to say more explicitly how it differs from:
- studies of working-time regulation that impose hard limits,
- organizational-behavior or psychology evidence on after-hours email,
- descriptive policy reports on right-to-disconnect laws.

The current intro names literatures, but it does not crisply position the paper as a test of **soft vs. hard enforcement in labor regulation**. That is the intellectually bigger contribution.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or filling a gap in a LITERATURE?

Too much the latter. “No economist has estimated whether the law works. This paper fills that gap” is the wrong instinct for a top-five introduction. The stronger frame is: governments are copying France; should we expect these laws to change labor-market outcomes? That is a world question.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?

At present, they would probably say: “It’s a DDD paper showing France’s right-to-disconnect law didn’t reduce long hours.” That is understandable, but still sounds like “another policy evaluation paper.” They would not yet say: “This paper shows soft bargaining mandates are ineffective tools for governing digital overwork.” The latter is the ambition level the paper should aim for.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Several possibilities:

1. **Better outcome framing.**  
   The current primary outcome is “share working >48 hours.” That is a blunt measure for a law about after-hours digital communication. Even if valid as one margin, it undersells the conceptual target. The paper needs to confront this more directly and perhaps reframe the contribution as about **measured overwork**, not digital intrusion per se. If there are any outcomes closer to timing/flexibility/unpaid after-hours work, those would make the contribution much bigger.

2. **Sharper mechanism framing.**  
   The paper’s most interesting idea is not France, but that **mandated negotiation without enforcement does little**. That mechanism should be elevated from a discussion point to the paper’s core contribution.

3. **Comparative institutional framing.**  
   The paper briefly mentions Portugal’s stricter law. That could be central: France is not just one country; it is the archetype of a soft-law model. The paper should explicitly frame itself as evaluating one regulatory model that has been exported internationally.

4. **Bigger comparison class.**  
   The paper is currently about one law in one country. It becomes bigger if framed as evidence on the limits of workplace rights that require process but not compliance.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

### Closest neighbors

The nearest neighbors are not actually all economics papers, which is part of the positioning problem. The likely neighbors are:

1. **Bell and Hart / Bell (2003)-type work on working-time regulation and opt-outs**  
   Important because it speaks to labor regulation that exists on paper but may not bind.

2. **Hamermesh (working time, timing, coordination)**  
   More adjacent than central, but useful for locating this in the economics of time allocation.

3. **Eurofound / ILO policy and institutional reports on right to disconnect**  
   Not glamorous citations, but probably the true closest precursor conversation.

4. **Occupational-health / management papers on after-hours email, smartphone use, burnout** such as Barber and Santuzzi; Derks et al.  
   These are important because the policy is motivated by those harms.

5. **Broader labor-regulation and enforcement literature**  
   This is where the paper should go more forcefully: laws that mandate procedures versus laws that create enforceable rights.

### How should the paper position itself relative to those neighbors?

Mostly **build on and discipline** them, not attack them.

- Relative to the psychology/management literature: “Those papers establish that after-hours connectivity is harmful; this paper asks whether a prominent legal remedy changes behavior at scale.”
- Relative to working-time regulation papers: “Those papers study hard caps or formal working-time rules; this paper studies a softer, negotiation-based intervention tailored to digital work.”
- Relative to policy reports: “Those reports describe the spread of R2D laws; this paper provides evidence on whether the foundational case achieved its stated aim.”

The paper should not overclaim that it overturns a literature. There is no big economics consensus here to overturn. It should instead present itself as bringing economic discipline to a policy debate that has run ahead of evidence.

### Is the paper currently positioned too narrowly or too broadly?

Oddly, both.

- **Too narrowly** because it is organized as “France’s law, first evaluation, null result.”
- **Too broadly** because it gestures at multiple literatures—working time, burnout, digital culture, labor law—without making clear which conversation it most wants to move.

The right audience is economists interested in labor regulation, labor-market institutions, and the economics of workplace boundaries in the digital era. The paper should target that audience explicitly.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?

The big omission is the **economics of enforcement and compliance**. The most natural economic conversation is not just “hours regulation,” but whether laws that require consultation/negotiation without sanctions or private rights actually bite. There is a large conceptual literature around the gap between de jure rights and de facto enforcement that the paper should engage more directly.

It may also be missing a conversation with:
- labor law and industrial relations work on mandatory bargaining versus substantive mandates,
- economics of workplace amenities and job design,
- perhaps the literature on remote work and flexibility if it wants to make claims about secular changes in digital work norms.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?

Not yet. The current conversation is “has anyone evaluated this specific law?” That is too small. The more impactful conversation is:

**Can soft labor regulation meaningfully constrain boundaryless work in digital labor markets?**

That is a much better AER conversation.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Digital technology has made work more continuous and less bounded by the worksite or workday. Policymakers have responded with “right-to-disconnect” laws meant to restore boundaries.

### Tension

France pioneered this approach, and other countries have copied it, but it is unclear whether such laws actually change behavior—especially when they rely on negotiation rather than enforceable prohibitions. There is a real conceptual tension between a salient modern labor-market problem and a possibly symbolic legal solution.

### Resolution

The paper finds no detectable reduction in long hours or usual hours among the workers most exposed to digital connectivity in France after the law.

### Implications

The French model may be largely symbolic. If policymakers want to reduce overwork, they may need enforceable limits rather than process mandates. More broadly, not all labor rights that sound responsive to technological change have real behavioral bite.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the skeleton of one, but not a fully successful arc. Right now it feels somewhat like a competent collection of results around a null finding rather than a tightly told story. The story is present, but the paper does not trust it enough.

The strongest story is:

1. Digital work created a new labor-market problem.
2. France created the flagship legal remedy.
3. The remedy spread globally before anyone knew whether it worked.
4. On the most visible measurable margin—overwork—it did not.
5. The reason is likely institutional design: negotiation without enforceable obligations.

That is a good story. The paper should tell it more directly and with less table-by-table procedural narration.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?

“France invented the right to disconnect, and eight years later there is no detectable evidence that it reduced long hours among the workers it was supposed to help.”

That is the fact.

A second, even better dinner-party line is:

“Germany’s managers cut long hours more than France’s after France passed the flagship disconnect law—even though Germany had no such law.”

That is vivid and memorable, though it should be handled carefully in the paper because it is illustrative, not the whole identification argument.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

They would lean in—at least initially. The topic is timely, legible, and broader than one country. It connects labor, technology, and regulation. The problem is not lack of intrinsic interest; it is that the paper currently presents the finding in a way that makes it feel smaller than it is.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Probably one of these:
- “Is the law just too soft to matter?”
- “Maybe hours are the wrong outcome—did it affect after-hours contact rather than total hours?”
- “Is France special, or is this telling us something general about symbolic labor rights?”

Those are good follow-up questions, and the paper should preemptively organize itself around them.

### Is the null result itself interesting?

Yes—but only if the paper leans into why the null is informative. Nulls become interesting when:
1. the policy is prominent,
2. expectations were high,
3. the result is substantively interpretable,
4. the paper explains what the null teaches us.

This paper has 1 and 2. It partly has 4. It needs to strengthen 3 by being clearer that the paper is about **aggregate measured overwork**, not the full universe of after-hours digital intrusion. Otherwise readers may wonder if it is simply testing the wrong outcome and finding nothing.

This does not feel like a failed experiment. It feels like a potentially important “policy diffusion outran evidence” paper. But it needs to say that more confidently.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

At present, the gap is mostly one of **framing and ambition**, with some **scope** concerns.

### Is it a framing problem?

Yes, primarily. The science may or may not survive refereeing, but as a story, the paper is undersold and somewhat misframed. It is currently “the first causal evaluation of France’s R2D law.” That is a field-journal framing. The AER framing is “what kinds of labor regulations can discipline digitally enabled overwork?”

### Is it a scope problem?

Also yes. The paper is vulnerable because the core outcome—weekly hours >48—is an imperfect proxy for the policy target. To become an AER paper, it likely needs either:
- outcomes closer to after-hours digital work or work-time boundaries, or
- a more explicit claim that the contribution is about **aggregate hours effects** of a widely cited policy, not the entire concept of digital disconnection.

Without that, readers may think the paper’s null is mechanically unsurprising.

### Is it a novelty problem?

Somewhat. “First evaluation” is novelty, but not enough. The paper needs novelty in interpretation: it should contribute to a broader understanding of soft law, workplace bargaining mandates, and digital labor-market regulation.

### Is it an ambition problem?

Yes. The paper is competent but safe. It has the raw material for a more ambitious argument than it currently makes. It should stop acting like a narrow policy evaluation and start acting like a paper about the limits of nonbinding labor-market protections in the digital era.

### Single most impactful piece of advice

If the author can change only one thing:

**Reframe the paper from a first evaluation of France’s law into a broader paper about why soft, negotiation-based labor rights fail to change behavior in digitally connected work.**

That one move would improve the introduction, literature positioning, narrative arc, and “so what?” all at once.
