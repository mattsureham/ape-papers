## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
This paper evaluates the world’s first "Right-to-Disconnect" (R2D) law (France, 2017) to see if mandating firms to negotiate over digital boundaries actually reduces overwork. Using a triple-difference design across EU countries and occupations, the author finds a precise zero: the law had no impact on working hours, even as knowledge workers across Europe (including unregulated Germany) saw their hours decline naturally. This matters because R2D laws are currently being carbon-copied across the globe (Australia, Belgium, EU) despite a total lack of evidence that the French "negotiation-based" model works.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this clearly. The first two paragraphs are excellent—they lead with a relatable vignette (the 11 p.m. Slack message), establish the global policy stakes, and immediately deliver the "no" to the central question. It is a model of an AER-style introduction.

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
The paper provides the first causal evaluation of Right-to-Disconnect legislation, showing that "soft" mandates (requirement to negotiate without enforcement) fail to reduce aggregate overwork.

*   **Differentiation:** It sits between the labor supply literature (Costa) and the regulation literature (Bell & Hart). It differentiates itself by focusing on the "always-on" digital margin, which is the frontier of 21st-century labor regulation.
*   **World vs. Literature:** It answers a question about the **WORLD**. It isn't "filling a gap"; it's debunking a high-profile policy currently being exported to other G20 nations.
*   **Clarity:** A smart economist would immediately understand the "Germany vs. France" contrast as the "aha" moment of the paper.
*   **Making it Bigger:** To make this a "slam dunk" for the AER, the paper needs to prove *why* it failed. Is it because the law lacks teeth (the "enforcement" mechanism), or because workers actually *want* the flexibility to check email at 11 p.m. in exchange for something else (the "compensating differentials" mechanism)?

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
The paper is currently a "Labor Regulation" paper. To be a top-tier paper, it should also be a "Future of Work" or "Personnel Economics" paper.

*   **Closest Neighbors:** Bell & Hart (2003) on the Working Time Directive; Mas & Pallais (2017) on valuing flexibility; and Goldin (2014) on the "greedy" nature of high-connectivity jobs.
*   **Strategy:** It should move from "attacking the French law" to "synthesizing why modern work-life boundaries are resistant to collective bargaining." 
*   **Missing Lit:** It needs to speak to the **compensating differentials** literature. If hours didn't go down, did wages go up? Did job satisfaction improve? If you only look at hours, you might be missing the "quality of work" margin the law was actually intended to fix.

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC
*   **Setup:** The digital revolution has made "clocking out" impossible.
*   **Tension:** Governments are intervening with "Right-to-Disconnect" laws, but these laws are often "soft" (negotiation-based). Do they actually change the reality of the 11 p.m. email, or are they just political signaling?
*   **Resolution:** They are toothless. In France, overwork followed the exact same downward secular trend as in countries without the law.
*   **Implications:** Soft regulation is "regulation theater." If you want results, you need Portuguese-style fines or structural changes to job design.

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
At a dinner party, the lead fact is: **"German managers reduced their overwork twice as fast as French managers, even though France passed a law specifically to stop it."**

People would lean in. The follow-up question is: "So did the law do *anything*?" This is where the paper currently feels slightly "null." If it doesn't reduce hours, does it at least reduce burnout? Or is it truly just a waste of paper? The "So What" is strong on the policy side, but needs more "meat" on the welfare side.

---

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
*   **Front-loading:** The Germany/France comparison in the intro is your strongest hook—move a version of that visual (the raw trends) into Figure 1.
*   **The "Toothless" Mechanism:** Section 6 (Discussion) needs to be more than just a summary. It needs a small model or a more formal analysis of why negotiation fails. Are French unions trading the "Right to Disconnect" for higher wages during the mandated negotiations? 
*   **Appendix to Main:** If there is any data on "subjective well-being" or "burnout" (even if noisy), it needs to move to the main results to address the "maybe it changed the *feeling* of work, not the hours" critique.

---

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The paper is currently a very high-quality *Applied Economics Letters* or *Journal of Policy Analysis and Management* paper. To make it **AER**, it needs **Mechanism.**

A null result is only exciting to the top 10 people in the field if it teaches us something fundamental about the labor market. Does it fail because firms have more bargaining power than the law assumed? Or because "connectivity" is a productive asset that workers don't actually want to give up? 

**Single most impactful advice:** Connect the null result to **firm-level bargaining.** If you can show that firms with weaker unions or higher "connectivity needs" were the ones where the law failed most (even using industry proxies), you turn a "failed policy" story into a "theory of the modern firm" story.
