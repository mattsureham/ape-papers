**Summary**

This short paper provides the first causal evaluation of France’s 2017 right-to-disconnect (R2D) law using Eurostat Labour Force Survey aggregates. Employing a triple-difference estimator that compares France to eight EU controls, high- to low-connectivity occupations, and pre- to post-2017 periods, the author finds no statistically or economically significant reduction in overwork (measured as >48 hours per week or usual weekly hours). Placebo tests, permutation inference, and robustness checks reinforce that the law, as implemented, appears toothless relative to broader European trends.

**Essential Points**

1. **Questionable Parallel-Gaps Assumption:** The event study (\Cref{tab:event}) shows sizable, statistically significant deviations from zero in the 2010–2015 period (e.g., −1.76pp in 2014), and the pre-treatment coefficients are not jointly indistinguishable from zero at standard levels once proper power considerations are taken into account. Since the identification hinges on the occupational gap (high vs. low connectivity) evolving similarly in France and controls absent the law, these sizeable pre-2017 fluctuations undermine the credibility of the estimator. The paper needs to demonstrate that the parallel-gaps assumption holds—for example, by (i) showing that the pre-2017 trend for the high-minus-low occupational gap is statistically indistinguishable across France and controls once the 2010–2015 fluctuations are addressed, (ii) restricting the pre-period to years that exhibit stable gaps, or (iii) using matching/weighting to align pre-period dynamics. At minimum, the event-study analysis must be reconciled with the identifying assumption rather than presented as benign noise.

2. **Occupational Comparability and Potential Confounders:** The treatment is defined at the intersection of France and “high-connectivity” ISCO 1–3 occupations, but the paper does not convincingly rule out that differential secular trends across occupations are driven by country-specific shifts unrelated to the R2D law—for instance, Germany’s managers (the placebo “treated” group) experienced pronounced declines, possibly due to other labour-market reforms or cultural shifts. The assumption that the high/low connectivity occupational gap is comparable across France and the control countries requires stronger support: are ISCO-coded occupations homogeneous in their connectivity exposure across countries and over time? The paper should explore whether France-specific shocks (Macron reforms, sectoral demand) differentially affected ISCO 1–3 relative to ISCO 7–9 and whether these shocks are idiosyncratic to France rather than the law. Without addressing this, one cannot interpret the triple interaction as identifying the causal effect of R2D.

3. **Measurement of the Targeted Margin:** The R2D law explicitly targets after-hours digital communication, not total weekly hours per se. The chosen outcomes—share working >48 hours and usual weekly hours—may not be sufficiently sensitive to capture the intended behavior change (e.g., the law might reduce after-hours email without affecting reported hours). Although this limitation is noted in the discussion, it needs more than a caveat: the paper’s conclusions about policy failure are overstated if the outcome cannot plausibly move even when the law works. The authors should either (i) provide evidence that the Eurostat measures are correlated with after-hours digital contact (e.g., via surveys or prior literature), (ii) supplement the analysis with more directly relevant measures (e.g., telework frequency, responses from CSO surveys on email habits), or (iii) temper the policy conclusions to reflect that the result may only pertain to aggregated hours rather than the nuanced behavior the law aimed to regulate.

**Suggestions**

1. **Strengthen the Pre-Trend Diagnostics.** The event study could be augmented by reporting joint pre-treatment tests (for example, a wild-cluster bootstrap \(F\)-test or permutation-based tests) to quantify how far the pre-trends deviate from zero. If certain years (2010–2015) drive the deviations, consider re-estimating the triple difference with a narrower pre-period (e.g., 2013–2016) or adding linear time trends interacted with high-connectivity and country indicators to absorb residual drift. Alternatively, allow for country-specific trends in the high-minus-low gap by estimating specification \[Y_{cot} = \cdots + \phi_c \cdot HighConn_o \cdot t\]. Even if the triple interaction remains small, demonstrating that the identification assumption holds after these adjustments would bolster credibility.

2. **Probe Heterogeneity in the High-Connectivity Group.** The high-connectivity category (ISCO 1–3) bundles managers, professionals, and technicians. Their exposure to digital communications—and their bargaining power—varies substantially. Consider splitting this group to see whether the null holds for managers separately versus technicians or associate professionals. Doing so can reveal whether any heterogeneity exists that might mask a localized effect. Moreover, explore whether certain sectors (e.g., finance, IT) drive the trends: if sectoral composition changed differentially in France versus controls, that could confound the triple diff.

3. **Use Additional Outcomes or Data to Pinpoint the Mechanism.** The Eurostat data alone may not capture the specific margin (after-hours digital norms) that the law targets. If possible, integrate alternative datasets—such as Eurofound’s European Working Conditions Survey (EWCS) which includes questions on “being contacted outside working hours” or the OECD’s Better Life Index items on work-life balance—to see if the law affected those reported conditions. Even if the EWCS is lower frequency, it could serve as a complementary check for a subset of years. Alternatively, survey evidence or firm-level reports from France’s labour inspectorate on negotiated “modalities” could contextualize whether the law led to substantive changes at the employer level.

4. **Clarify the Role of Country-Year Fixed Effects in the Placebo Tests.** The placebo results (Germany fake treatment, medium-connectivity placebo) are interpreted as informative about secular trends, but they also show significant, non-zero effects. It would help to explain why these are not contrary to the identifying assumption—especially since the placebo on ISCO 5 yields a significant negative coefficient, suggesting that broader occupational shifts drive the results. Elaborating on what mechanisms would induce such placebo results without implicating the law would alleviate concerns about omitted trends. For instance, if services/sales occupations contracted more in controls than in France for reasons unrelated to R2D, that could explain the negative coefficient without invalidating the main design. Documenting whether similar triple-difference estimates emerge when the treated occupation is replaced with other groups (e.g., ISCO 4 vs. ISCO 7–9) could further demonstrate robustness.

5. **Address Potential Spillovers or Anticipation Effects.** The triple difference assumes that the law’s effect is confined to high-connectivity occupations and begins in 2017. Yet negotiations could have started in 2016, and firms might have complied earlier or with spillovers to low-connectivity groups (through firm-wide culture changes). Consider a sensitivity check where Post is coded from 2016 or 2018, or where the “difference” is defined between e.g. ISCO 1–3 and ISCO 4–6 to see if the effect shifts. Additionally, the inclusion of firm-size threshold (50+ employees) implies that the policy might have differential effects depending on the share of large firms across occupations and countries. While firm-level data are unavailable in Eurostat, you could proxy for this by interacting with occupation-level employment shares (if available) or referencing external sources to argue that the high-connectivity occupations are indeed concentrated in larger firms.

6. **Discuss Power and Minimal Detectable Effects More Thoroughly.** The discussion claims the confidence interval rules out effects larger than 2.3 percentage points, but it would be helpful to translate this into policy-relevant terms: what would be a meaningful reduction in overwork (e.g., relative to the standard deviation of long-hours rates or the average change in controls)? Presenting power calculations (even post-hoc) would help the reader assess whether the null is due to imprecision or truly small effects. Moreover, the standardized effect table in the appendix should connect more tightly with the policy narrative rather than label the estimate “Moderate positive” when it is statistically indistinguishable from zero.

7. **Clarify the Interpretation of the Triple Interaction.** Because country-year and occupation-year fixed effects absorb much of the variation, the triple interaction is identified off relatively little residual variation. Spell out intuitively what variation remains: essentially, it is whether the difference between high- and low-connectivity occupations in France deviates from the same difference in the control countries, after adjusting for country- and occupation-specific trends. Emphasizing this will make clear why the null result matters (or does not) and help readers understand the estimand in policy terms.

---

By addressing these points—particularly tightening the identification through pre-trend diagnostics and clarifying the link between the law and the measured outcomes—the paper will provide a much stronger, more persuasive empirical evaluation of the right-to-disconnect.
