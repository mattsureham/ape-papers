## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple, policy-relevant question: did France’s pioneering 2017 “right to disconnect” law actually reduce overwork among the workers most exposed to after-hours digital communication? Using cross-country, cross-occupation variation in Europe, the paper’s answer is no: France’s soft, negotiation-based mandate did not measurably reduce long hours for digitally connected workers, despite becoming the template for similar laws elsewhere.

A busy economist should care because “always-on” work has become a central labor-market and welfare issue, and policymakers around the world are legislating in this space with almost no causal evidence. If the flagship policy in the area appears toothless, that matters for labor regulation, workplace organization, and the design of employee protections in the digital economy.

### Does the paper articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Not quite. The current opening is vivid, but it overplays anecdote and then immediately pivots to “this paper fills a gap.” That is a weaker opening than the paper deserves. The real hook is not “no one has estimated this yet”; it is “a globally influential labor policy may not have changed worker behavior at all.”

### What the first two paragraphs should say instead

Something like:

> After-hours digital work has become one of the defining labor-market concerns of the smartphone era. In response, France enacted the world’s first “right to disconnect” law in 2017, requiring medium and large firms to negotiate rules limiting out-of-hours digital contact; that model has since spread to other countries and is now part of the European policy conversation. The core economic question is whether such laws actually reduce overwork among the workers most exposed to always-on communication.

> This paper shows that France’s law did not. Comparing highly connected occupations to less connected occupations in France and other European countries before and after 2017, I find no detectable reduction in long hours or usual hours for the workers the law was meant to protect. The broader implication is that soft, negotiation-based regulation may have little bite in changing work behavior in the digital economy.

That is the pitch. The current introduction contains the ingredients, but the framing is too “first evaluation of X” and not enough “this tells us something important about whether soft labor regulation works.”

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### Contribution in one sentence

The paper’s contribution is to provide what it presents as the first causal evidence that France’s prototype right-to-disconnect law did not reduce measured overwork among the digitally connected workers it targeted.

### Is this clearly differentiated from the closest papers?

Only partially. The paper is reasonably clear that there is no prior causal evaluation of right-to-disconnect laws. But the contribution is still not sharply distinguished from adjacent literatures on hours regulation, labor standards, and workplace flexibility. Right now, the reader gets “first paper on R2D” plus “null effect,” but not a crisp sense of what general lesson extends beyond this one policy.

The closest conceptual distinction should be:

- This is not just another labor-regulation evaluation.
- It is an evaluation of a **new class of regulation**: soft governance of digital work boundaries.
- The central contribution is not merely “France had no effect,” but “negotiation mandates without enforceable rights may not alter equilibrium work norms.”

That is bigger, and more legible.

### Is the contribution framed as a question about the world, or filling a literature gap?

Too much as a literature gap. “No economist has estimated whether the law works. This paper fills that gap” is serviceable, but second-tier framing. The stronger frame is about the world:

- Can governments regulate the boundaries of digital work?
- Do soft mandates change always-on work norms?
- Is overwork in the knowledge economy responsive to procedural labor law?

Those are world questions. The paper should lead with them.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?

At present, maybe, but not confidently. They could probably say: “It’s a triple-difference paper on France’s right-to-disconnect law, and it finds no effect on long hours.” That is decent, but still perilously close to “another DiD paper about a European labor regulation.”

What’s missing is the sentence that makes the result travel: this is evidence on whether soft labor regulation can discipline digitally mediated work. Without that, the paper sounds narrower than it is.

### What would make the contribution bigger?

Most importantly: **move from overwork narrowly defined to the economics of digital labor boundaries more broadly**.

Specific ways to enlarge the contribution:

1. **Different outcome variable**
   - Right now the main outcome is >48 weekly hours. That is defensible, but blunt.
   - A stronger paper would ideally also speak to margins more tightly linked to the policy’s stated target: timing of work, evening/weekend contact, fragmented work, unpaid overtime, work-from-home spillovers, mental strain, or work-life conflict.
   - As written, the paper itself admits the law may affect after-hours email behavior without moving total reported hours. That admission shrinks the contribution unless the authors can better justify why hours is the relevant policy target.

2. **Different framing**
   - Reframe from “did France’s R2D law work?” to “what kinds of labor regulation can shape work in the digital economy?”
   - Then France becomes the flagship case, not the entire paper.

3. **Different comparison**
   - The paper has an underused comparative angle: France’s soft negotiation model versus harder-contact restrictions like Portugal’s.
   - Even if not fully estimable here, the introduction and discussion should foreground this contrast as the substantive takeaway: procedural rights may fail where enforceable prohibitions might succeed.

4. **Mechanism**
   - The mechanism is implicit: no enforcement, no individual rights, no penalties. That should be the centerpiece, not a discussion footnote.
   - The paper needs to own the argument that the law failed because it governs bargaining process rather than employer conduct.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

### Closest neighbors

The paper’s nearest neighbors are probably not the exact citations it currently emphasizes. The most relevant clusters are:

1. **Working-time regulation / labor standards**
   - Bell and Hart / Bell on long working hours and working time rules
   - Research around the EU Working Time Directive and opt-outs
   - Possibly Hamermesh-related work on time regulation, though that is more tangential

2. **Technology, work boundaries, and labor supply**
   - Golden on flexible work / working-time mismatch
   - Studies on telework, smartphone use, employer monitoring, and boundary control
   - Organizational economics / personnel economics work on availability expectations

3. **Law and economics of labor regulation**
   - Papers on whether labor standards with weak enforcement actually alter behavior
   - Broader literature on mandates versus rights-with-remedies

4. **Mental health / worker well-being / burnout**
   - The current intro cites psychology-type evidence on smartphones and sleep, but this is not yet integrated into an economics conversation.

### How should the paper position itself relative to those neighbors?

Primarily **build on and synthesize**, not attack.

The paper does not need to pick fights with prior working-time literature. It should say:

- Existing work shows that labor regulation sometimes changes hours, but effects depend on enforceability and margins of adjustment.
- Existing work on always-on work documents harms, but not whether regulation can fix them.
- This paper connects those literatures by testing whether a prominent soft regulation of digital work boundaries changes observed work behavior.

That is the right conversation.

### Is it positioned too narrowly or too broadly?

Currently, oddly, both.

- **Too narrowly** in its empirical self-description: France, one law, one outcome, one design.
- **Too broadly** in some of the rhetoric: “The evidence is in: the law did not work” and “a right that exists only on paper is not a right at all.” That sounds sweeping relative to the measured outcome.

The paper needs a middle path: broader conceptual positioning, more disciplined claims.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?

It seems under-engaged with:

- Personnel economics / organizational economics on work norms, incentives, and availability expectations
- Labor law / enforcement economics on why some workplace mandates bite and others do not
- Economics of telework and the reorganization of work time post-smartphone/post-pandemic
- Potentially gender and household labor supply literatures, since disconnect rights may matter through family-work boundaries even if total hours do not move

Right now the literature review leans too much on a few generic working-hours citations and a few psychology citations. That is not enough to signal “AER conversation.”

### Is the paper having the right conversation?

Not yet fully. The highest-value conversation is not “there was no evaluation of this law.” It is:

**Can governments regulate the temporal boundaries of work in the digital economy, and what makes such regulation effective?**

That could connect labor economics, organizational economics, law and economics, and public policy. That is a better conversation than the current niche framing around a single French statute.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Digital communication technologies have blurred the boundary between work and nonwork, especially for white-collar employees. Policymakers have responded with “right to disconnect” laws, and France pioneered the approach that others have emulated.

### Tension

The policy was highly visible and normatively attractive, but its actual effectiveness is unknown. More fundamentally, there is a real question whether a soft, negotiation-based mandate can change work behavior in environments shaped by organizational culture, global coordination, and implicit performance incentives.

### Resolution

The paper finds no detectable reduction in measured overwork among the occupations most exposed to after-hours digital communication. The flagship policy appears not to have shifted long hours.

### Implications

The implication is not just that one French law failed. It is that symbolic or procedural protections may be insufficient to alter work norms in the digital economy; policymakers may need enforceable restrictions, remedies, or different instruments entirely.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the bones of one, but currently it reads more like a competent set of results than a fully realized story. The ingredients are there, but the narrative is undermined by two issues:

1. **The paper alternates between narrow and sweeping claims.**
   - Narrow empirical object: long-hours measures in aggregate occupation-country cells.
   - Sweeping rhetorical conclusion: the law “did not work.”
   - That mismatch weakens the story.

2. **The core tension is underdeveloped.**
   - The interesting puzzle is not merely “does the law work?”
   - It is “can a soft labor regulation change behavior when the underlying source of overwork is organizational norms and incentives?”
   - That tension should structure the paper.

### What story should it be telling?

This story:

> The digital economy created a new labor-market problem: work can now intrude anywhere, anytime. France responded first, but with a weak instrument—mandatory negotiation rather than enforceable constraints. This paper shows that such soft regulation did not reduce overwork, suggesting a broader lesson: when work norms are sustained by firm incentives and worker career concerns, symbolic rights may not change equilibrium behavior.

That is an AER-style narrative. The present draft is not far off, but it needs to commit to that bigger story.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?

“France invented the right to disconnect, other countries copied it, and eight years later there’s no sign it reduced long hours among the workers most exposed to after-hours digital work.”

That is a good opening fact.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

They would lean in initially. The topic is contemporary, salient, and widely legible. But the second question will come immediately: “No effect on what exactly?” And then: “Is long weekly hours the right margin?”

That follow-up is unavoidable. If the paper cannot answer it convincingly, interest will fade.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Most likely:

- “Maybe it changed after-hours contact or stress, not total hours?”
- “Was the law too soft to matter?”
- “Is this really about disconnect laws, or just about weak enforcement in labor regulation?”

Those are actually good questions for the paper—they point toward the bigger contribution. The authors should anticipate them in the introduction, not only in the discussion.

### Is the null result itself interesting?

Yes, potentially very much so. But only if the paper makes the case that:

1. France’s law was globally influential;
2. the policy margin is substantively important;
3. the estimated outcome is a meaningful object for policymakers; and
4. the null teaches something general about policy design.

At present, the null is interesting, but the case is not made as powerfully as it could be. The result sometimes reads like “we looked and found nothing” rather than “this influential regulatory model appears ineffective on its most visible labor-market margin.”

The null needs to be sold as informative because the policy was consequential, not because the design is clever.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

This is not primarily a “bad paper”; it is a **positioning problem with some scope limitations**.

### What is the gap between current form and an AER paper?

#### 1. Framing problem
Yes, significantly. The paper is too often framed as:
- first evaluation of a law,
- null result,
- France case study.

That is not enough for AER. It needs to be framed as:
- evidence on whether soft labor regulation can govern digital work boundaries.

#### 2. Scope problem
Also yes. The current outcome—weekly hours / long hours—is probably too narrow to carry the entire weight of the “right to disconnect” concept. If this remains the only meaningful outcome, the claims have to be narrower. To make the paper feel bigger, it would help materially to speak to outcomes more tightly connected to after-hours digital intrusion or worker well-being.

#### 3. Novelty problem
Moderate, not fatal. The exact policy is novel enough, but the empirical template is familiar. So the novelty has to come from the question and the general lesson, not the design.

#### 4. Ambition problem
Yes. The paper is competent but safe. It does the obvious evaluation of the obvious outcome and then stops. A top-field paper would either:
- broaden the set of margins on which “disconnect” might matter, or
- use this case to make a sharper argument about policy design and enforceability in labor markets.

### Single most impactful piece of advice

**Reframe the paper around the broader question of whether soft labor regulation can meaningfully constrain digital work, and make the France result the leading case of that larger claim rather than a narrow evaluation of one law.**

If they can only change one thing, that is it. Even without new data, a serious reframing around policy design, enforceability, and the economics of digital work would make the paper much more legible to a general-interest audience.
