## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper uses the random assignment of drug trafficking cases to courtrooms in São Paulo to show that conviction outcomes vary dramatically depending on which courtroom hears the case. The big-picture claim is that when criminal law leaves the line between “user” and “trafficker” highly discretionary, the resulting incarceration system becomes not just harsh but arbitrary.

That is a question busy economists could care about: how much of punishment is driven by law and facts, versus institutional assignment and discretion? It connects judicial discretion, state capacity, and mass incarceration in a major middle-income democracy.

### Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Mostly yes, but it does so in a way that is more prosecutorial than analytical. The opening is vivid and memorable, but it reads like an op-ed or legal brief more than an AER introduction. It gets to the fact pattern quickly, which is good, but it does not quite frame the broader economic question cleanly enough: under what legal environments does random assignment translate into substantively different punishment?

The current introduction is also too eager to conclude “the difference is not case quality” before it has earned that rhetorically. Even if true, the first two paragraphs should set up the world-level question rather than announce the verdict.

### The pitch the paper should have

“Criminal justice systems often use random case assignment to prevent judge shopping, but when legal standards are vague, random assignment may itself generate large disparities in punishment. We study São Paulo’s electronic lottery that assigns drug trafficking prosecutions to courtrooms and show that conviction risk varies sharply across otherwise comparable courtrooms within the same assignment pool. The paper’s central message is that procedural randomness can coexist with substantive arbitrariness, especially when the law gives judges broad discretion over a high-stakes classification.”

That is the AER version of the pitch: less “look how outrageous this is,” more “here is a general institutional insight with Brazil as a powerful setting.”

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper shows that in São Paulo’s drug courts, random assignment to courtrooms generates very large differences in conviction risk, suggesting that vague legal standards can transform procedural randomization into substantively arbitrary punishment.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?

Not yet clearly enough. Right now the paper says, in effect, “this is a judge-leniency/random-assignment paper in Brazil, and the effects are large.” That is not enough. The contribution needs sharper differentiation along one of these margins:

1. **Setting**: a major developing-country judicial system with very high incarceration stakes.
2. **Institution**: not merely judge harshness, but a legal regime with no objective threshold separating criminal categories.
3. **Conceptual claim**: random assignment can be normatively problematic when legal standards are indeterminate.
4. **Outcome**: not sentencing conditional on conviction, but conviction itself in a discretionary classification environment.

At present, the paper gestures at all four, but does not commit to one as the central novelty. That makes the contribution feel diffuse.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or filling a gap in a LITERATURE?

It is partly framed as a world question, which is good, but it slides too often into literature-gap language and institutional advocacy. The strongest framing is the world question:

- When legal rules are vague, how much punishment is effectively assigned by lottery?
- Can random assignment systems produce fairness in procedure while producing unfairness in outcomes?

That is much stronger than “there is little evidence from Brazil” or “we extend judge-randomization designs to the developing world.”

### Could a smart economist explain what is new after reading the intro?

Not confidently. They would probably say: “It’s a judge-leniency paper on Brazilian drug cases showing big variation across courtrooms.” That is not enough. They should instead say:

> “It shows that in a vague legal regime, random assignment itself creates an economically meaningful conviction lottery. The point is not just heterogeneity across judges; it’s that legal indeterminacy turns anti-corruption assignment rules into a mechanism generating arbitrary incarceration.”

That sentence is not yet what the introduction delivers.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Most importantly: **connect the courtroom variation to the legal classification problem more directly.** Right now the paper studies trafficking prosecutions already filed as trafficking cases, but its rhetoric often suggests it is identifying the use-vs.-trafficking classification problem itself. That mismatch shrinks credibility and strategic force.

To make the contribution bigger, the paper would need one of the following:

- **A tighter outcome**: show heterogeneity specifically in conviction on trafficking versus downgrade/alternative dispositions, not just generic conviction.
- **A stronger mechanism**: demonstrate that dispersion is largest in borderline cases, or where factual ambiguity should matter most.
- **A more disciplined comparison**: compare drug trafficking cases to another criminal category with clearer legal thresholds. If the “lottery” is much larger where the law is vague, that becomes a much bigger and more general paper.
- **A broader framing**: recast the paper as about when discretion plus random assignment generate arbitrary state coercion, with applications beyond Brazil/drugs.

The single biggest way to make the contribution larger is probably the comparison-to-clearer-offenses design. That would transform the paper from “there is dispersion in Brazilian drug courts” to “legal indeterminacy amplifies assignment-based arbitrariness.”

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Courts often use random assignment to ensure impartiality. Brazil’s drug law imposes severe penalties but leaves broad discretion over high-stakes decisions. São Paulo is a huge, consequential setting where these institutional features matter at scale.

### Tension

If cases are randomly assigned, one might expect fairness in who gets what judge. But if legal standards are highly indeterminate, random assignment may just randomize exposure to heterogeneous courtroom behavior. Then procedural fairness and substantive fairness come apart.

### Resolution

The paper finds large differences in conviction rates across courtrooms receiving cases from the same assignment pool. Courtroom identity strongly predicts conviction outcomes, suggesting that who hears the case matters enormously.

### Implications

This should change how we think about fairness in judicial systems: preventing judge shopping is not enough. When law is vague, the assignment mechanism can become a punishment lottery. That has implications for legal design, drug policy, and the economics of discretion.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the ingredients, but the execution is uneven. Right now it feels more like:

1. startling fact,
2. lots of dispersion tables,
3. some balance and leniency machinery,
4. policy discussion about thresholds.

That is a sequence of results plus advocacy, not yet a fully disciplined narrative.

### What story should it be telling?

The story should be:

1. **Modern courts use random assignment to achieve procedural neutrality.**
2. **That safeguard fails to ensure substantive fairness when legal standards are open-ended.**
3. **Brazil’s drug law is an unusually stark setting to observe this failure.**
4. **The data show that random assignment translates into large differences in conviction risk.**
5. **Therefore, the design of legal standards matters as much as the design of assignment procedures.**

That is the paper’s real story. It is much stronger than “look at this shocking variation.”

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

This is not primarily a framing problem, though framing needs work. It is not mainly a methods problem either.

It is mostly a **scope/ambition problem** with some **novelty-positioning problem**.

As written, the paper is a strong and interesting fact paper: large courtroom-level heterogeneity in Brazilian drug convictions under random assignment. That is good. But for AER, the top people in the field will ask: what does this teach us beyond the now-familiar fact that judges differ?

### The gap to an AER paper

To become an AER paper, it needs to show one of the following much more forcefully:

1. **A general mechanism**: vague legal standards amplify assignment-based arbitrariness.
2. **A sharp comparative statics**: dispersion is especially large where factual/legal ambiguity is high.
3. **A broader benchmark**: the lottery is much larger in drug cases than in offenses with clearer statutory standards.
4. **A more consequential margin**: the assignment lottery materially changes incarceration exposure, not merely recorded conviction events.

Without one of those, the paper risks being seen as a competent, striking, but bounded judicial-disparity paper.

### Is it framing, scope, novelty, or ambition?

- **Framing problem:** yes, somewhat.
- **Scope problem:** yes, importantly.
- **Novelty problem:** partially, because judge heterogeneity is known.
- **Ambition problem:** yes. The paper currently settles for documenting a shocking spread when it should be trying to explain what institutional feature produces that spread and why the lesson generalizes.

### Single most impactful advice

**Reframe and extend the paper around the claim that vague legal standards amplify the consequences of random judicial assignment, and show that claim empirically with a comparison to a clearer offense category or to plausibly more/less ambiguous drug cases.**

If they can only change one thing, that is it. Right now the paper documents arbitrariness. It needs to explain why this arbitrariness is especially informative and generalizable.
