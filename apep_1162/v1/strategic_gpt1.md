## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper studies one of the largest recent employer payroll tax cuts in the OECD: Belgium’s 2016–2018 reduction in employer social security contributions. Because Belgian wages are unusually rigid due to automatic indexation and sectoral bargaining, the reform is framed as a clean test of a first-order economic question: if firms face meaningfully lower labor costs but wages do not adjust, do they actually hire more workers?

That is a question busy economists should care about. It goes to the heart of whether payroll tax cuts are genuinely employment policy or mostly transfers to firms, and it matters well beyond Belgium because many governments justify large tax expenditures on job-creation grounds.

**Does the paper itself articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Mostly yes, but not optimally. The ingredients are there, but the opening still reads a bit like a labor-public-finance paper introducing a policy evaluation, rather than a broader economics paper asking a sharp question about the world. The paper should get to the striking fact faster: **a massive labor-cost cut, in a wage-rigid economy, produced no jobs**. That is the hook.

**What the first two paragraphs should say instead:**

> Governments routinely cut employer payroll taxes to create jobs, but it is surprisingly hard to know whether cheaper labor actually increases employment. In most settings, wages adjust, incidence is shared, and any employment effect is hard to interpret. The key economic question is simpler and more fundamental: when labor truly becomes cheaper to firms, do firms hire more?
>
> Belgium’s 2016–2018 payroll tax reform offers a rare test. Employer social security contributions fell dramatically, while automatic wage indexation and sectoral bargaining sharply limited wage adjustment. Using cross-country sectoral comparisons, this paper shows that even this unusually large reduction in non-wage labor costs did not raise employment. The result suggests that, in rigid-wage environments, payroll tax cuts can operate more as transfers to firms than as job-creation policy.

That is the pitch. It is cleaner, broader, and more memorable than the current version.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution in one sentence:**  
The paper argues that a very large employer payroll tax cut in Belgium substantially reduced firms’ labor costs but did not increase employment, implying that in a rigid-wage setting payroll tax cuts may function mainly as profit transfers rather than employment subsidies.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest papers?
Only partially. The paper cites the standard payroll-tax incidence/employment papers, but the differentiation is still too mechanical: “they study X country / youth workers / pass-through; I study Belgium / rigid wages / null effects.” That is not enough for AER positioning. The contribution needs to be differentiated at the level of the economic question:

- Prior work often studies reforms where wage pass-through blurs interpretation.
- This paper claims a setting where wages are institutionally rigid, so the reform isolates the firm-side labor-demand margin.
- The surprising result is that even then, employment does not respond.

That is the real differentiator. The paper knows this, but it does not press the point hard enough.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or filling a gap in a LITERATURE?
It is mostly framed as a question about the world, which is good. The strongest version is:

- **World question:** Do lower employer labor costs create jobs when wages cannot adjust?
- **Weaker literature-gap framing:** There is little evidence on payroll tax cuts in rigid-wage economies.

The paper should lean even more aggressively into the former. “Belgium is a test of when cheaper labor does and does not create employment” is much stronger than “Belgium fills a gap in the payroll-tax literature.”

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?
They could, but they might still summarize it as “a DiD paper on a Belgian payroll tax cut with a null result.” That is the danger. The paper is vulnerable to being seen as a competent natural experiment rather than a belief-changing paper.

To avoid that, the introduction needs to hammer three things:
1. **The reform is unusually large.**
2. **Wages are unusually constrained.**
3. **The null therefore speaks to a central economic mechanism, not just one policy episode.**

### What would make this contribution bigger?
Most importantly: **make the object of interest larger than employment in Belgium.** The paper should more explicitly position itself as testing a widely used policy logic.

Specific ways to make it bigger:
- **Stronger mechanism framing:** not just “wages are rigid,” but “this setting isolates the canonical labor-demand channel.”  
- **Broader implications for tax incidence and policy design:** when do payroll tax cuts work through wages, through employment, or through rents/profits?
- **Direct evidence on where the money went** would make the contribution much bigger. Right now “profit windfall” is asserted more than demonstrated. If the paper could show margins like profits, markups, value added per worker, firm entry, vacancies, hours, or investment, the story would become far more consequential.
- **A clearer comparison to low-wage targeted subsidies** would help. The paper currently studies a broad-based employer tax cut. Many influential payroll-tax papers are about targeted groups. The paper should say more clearly why broad-based cuts may be especially weak employment policy.

As it stands, the contribution is real, but still a bit too easy to paraphrase as “null DiD in one country.”

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

### Closest neighbors
The obvious neighbors are:

- **Kugler and Kugler (2009)** on payroll taxes and employment in Colombia  
- **Saez, Schoefer, and Seim (2019)** on payroll tax cuts for youth in Sweden

- perhaps also **Saez, Matsaganis, and Tsakloglou (2012)** on Greek payroll tax changes

Those are the direct labor/public-finance neighbors.

### How should the paper position itself relative to them?
**Build on them, then sharpen the contrast.** Not “attack,” exactly. More like:

- Those papers show that payroll tax reforms can affect wages and employment, but incidence and effects depend heavily on institutions and targeting.
- This paper studies a case that comes close to isolating the pure firm-cost channel because wages are constrained.
- In that setting, even a large broad-based cost reduction does not generate jobs.

That is a useful synthesis with a sharp edge.

### Is the paper positioned too narrowly or too broadly?
Currently, oddly, both.

- **Too narrowly:** it spends too much time as “Belgium + payroll taxes + sector-level DiD.”
- **Too broadly in a vague way:** it gestures at broad claims about labor demand, product demand, and European unemployment institutions without fully integrating those literatures.

The best audience is not just “people who study payroll taxes.” It is the intersection of:
- labor demand,
- tax incidence,
- wage-setting institutions,
- and macro/labor interactions in job creation.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of or under-engaged with?
A few conversations are underdeveloped:

1. **Labor demand under product-market constraints / demand-constrained hiring**  
   The paper’s strongest interpretive claim is that product demand, not labor cost, is binding. That is a big claim and wants anchoring in labor-demand/macroeconomic literatures, not just payroll-tax papers.

2. **Search and matching / hiring frictions / vacancy creation**  
   If cheaper labor does not translate into employment, one natural question is whether hiring is constrained by frictions or weak demand. The paper need not estimate a model, but it should acknowledge this broader theory space.

3. **Institutional wage rigidity in Europe**  
   The Belgian institutions are central, but the paper currently uses them mainly as identification flavor. They should be part of the economic contribution, linked to bargaining and incidence.

4. **Tax expenditures and business subsidies**  
   There is a policy-design conversation here: when do broad employer subsidies become inframarginal transfers? The paper should speak to that more directly.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?
Not quite. The paper thinks it is in the payroll-tax reform conversation. That is true, but the more important conversation is:

> **When does lowering firms’ marginal labor cost actually create jobs, and when is it merely an inframarginal transfer?**

That is the conversation AER readers are more likely to care about.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Governments widely use employer payroll tax cuts to stimulate employment. Standard theory says cheaper labor should raise labor demand, but in many real-world settings the incidence of such cuts is muddied by wage adjustment.

### Tension
Belgium offers a rare case where wages are unusually rigid, so the reform should reveal whether firms hire more when labor actually becomes cheaper. The tension is that standard labor-demand logic would predict some employment response, especially given the size of the reform.

### Resolution
The paper finds no employment response. Employment in Belgian sectors evolves similarly to comparison countries despite a sizable fall in non-wage labor costs.

### Implications
The paper suggests that broad-based payroll tax cuts may fail as job-creation tools in rigid-wage economies and may instead operate largely as transfers to firms.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
Yes, but it is not yet fully disciplined. The core story is there. However, the paper sometimes drifts into being a collection of empirical checks around a null estimate: phase-in, expanded controls, leave-one-sector-out, placebo outcomes, event-study details, etc. Those are fine, but they are not the story.

The story should be:

1. Governments cut payroll taxes to create jobs.  
2. Usually we cannot cleanly observe the employment channel because wages move.  
3. Belgium gives a rare opportunity to isolate that channel.  
4. Even here, jobs do not rise.  
5. Therefore, cheaper labor alone is not enough; broad payroll tax cuts may be poor job policy in these institutional environments.

Everything in the paper should serve that arc. Right now, the paper occasionally sounds like it is trying to prove it has done enough diagnostics rather than persuading the reader of a large economic point.

A related issue: “profit windfall” is a strong narrative ending, but the paper does not really resolve the story with direct evidence on profits or firm rents. As written, the resolution is really “no employment effect,” and the implication is “consistent with transfer to firms.” That distinction matters. The paper should be more careful or provide evidence that lets it own the stronger ending.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?
“Belgium cut employer payroll taxes by one of the largest amounts in the OECD, in a setting where wages were basically locked in place—and employment didn’t budge.”

That is a good opening line. People would lean in.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
They would lean in at first, because the combination of a huge reform, rigid wages, and a null effect is genuinely interesting. The likely reaction is not boredom; it is skepticism followed by curiosity. The paper has a naturally strong seminar fact.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Probably one of these:
- “If not jobs, where did the money go?”
- “Is Belgium special, or does this generalize to rigid-wage economies?”
- “Was the reform too broad-based and therefore mostly inframarginal?”
- “Are you sure the relevant margin wasn’t hours, vacancies, or composition rather than headcount?”

That indicates the paper has a live “so what,” but also reveals its current limitation: the most natural follow-up question is exactly the one the paper cannot fully answer.

### If findings are null or modest: is the null itself interesting?
Yes. This is one of those cases where the null can be very interesting because:
- the treatment is large,
- the mechanism is economically central,
- and the policy stakes are high.

So the null does **not** feel like a failed experiment. It feels potentially important. But to make that case at the AER level, the authors need to lean hard into why this null is informative:
- the reform is large enough that zero is surprising,
- the institutional setting makes interpretation unusually sharp,
- and the upper bound on job creation is policy-relevant.

The paper does some of this, but it can do it much more forcefully and earlier.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

This is **not** primarily an identification problem for editorial purposes. It is a **framing and ambition problem**, with some scope concerns.

### What is the gap?
The paper has a publishable empirical result and a genuinely interesting null. But in current form, it still feels like a well-executed policy evaluation from a small literature rather than a paper that changes how economists think about labor-cost subsidies.

The main weaknesses are:

- **Framing problem:** The paper understates the general question and overstates the policy episode.
- **Scope problem:** It wants to conclude “profit windfall,” but does not yet show where the gains went.
- **Ambition problem:** It stops at “no employment effect” rather than fully exploiting the setting to learn about the margins of adjustment.

### Is there also a novelty problem?
A bit. Payroll tax incidence and employment effects are not new. So to matter at AER level, the paper must persuade the reader that this setting identifies a particularly important and previously unresolved margin. The novelty is not “Belgium” or “another payroll tax cut.” The novelty is the unusually clean test of the labor-demand channel in a rigid-wage environment.

### What is the single most impactful advice?
**Reframe the paper around a broad economic question—whether lowering firms’ marginal labor costs creates jobs when wages cannot adjust—and either provide direct evidence on where the surplus went or be much more disciplined in limiting the claim to “no employment effect.”**

If they can only change one thing, that is it.

Because right now the paper’s best asset is its clean and surprising core fact, but its weakest feature is that it has not fully built the intellectual architecture around that fact. If it did, this could become much more than a country case study.
