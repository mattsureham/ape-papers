## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper examines one of the largest employer payroll tax cuts in recent OECD history—Belgium’s 7.4 percentage point reduction—to test the elasticity of labor demand. By exploiting Belgium’s unique "rigid-wage" institutions (automatic indexation), the author isolates a pure cost reduction for firms and finds that despite a massive drop in non-wage costs, there was zero detectable employment response. For a busy economist, this is a "dogs that didn't bark" story: it suggests that in the presence of wage rigidities, payroll tax cuts function as profit windfalls rather than job creators.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this clearly. The first two paragraphs effectively set the stakes (billions spent on these reforms) and the specific "clean test" provided by the Belgian context. No rewrite of the pitch is necessary; it is punchy and direct.

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution:** The paper provides a "polar case" empirical bound on the employment effects of payroll taxes by studying a massive reform in an environment where wage pass-through is institutionally blocked.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It is well-differentiated from **Gruber (1997)** and **Saez et al. (2019)** by focusing on the *absence* of wage adjustment. While Saez et al. find rent-sharing and employment gains, this paper shows what happens when you turn the rent-sharing dial to zero.
*   **World vs. Literature:** It frames the contribution as a question about the world (Do these billion-euro tax shifts actually work?), which is a strength.
*   **Clarity:** A smart economist would say: "It’s the Belgian version of the Swedish tax cut study, but with rigid wages and a null result."
*   **What would make it bigger?** The contribution feels a bit "macro-lite" because it relies on sector-level Eurostat data. To make this an AER "must-read," the author needs to dig into **firm-level mechanisms**. Where did the money go? If it was a "profit windfall," can we see it in firm balance sheets, dividends, or investment?

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

*   **Closest Neighbors:** Saez, Schoefer, & Seim (2019) on Sweden; Gruber (1997) on Chile; Kramarz & Philippon (2001) on France; and the theoretical framework of Summers (1989).
*   **Positioning:** It builds on the "institutional context matters" literature but acts as a cautionary counter-point to the recent optimism around payroll tax cuts (like the Swedish case). It essentially says: "Context doesn't just shape the result; it can nullify it."
*   **Missing Conversations:** The paper would benefit from connecting to the **"Investment and Liquidity"** literature. If firms got a windfall and didn't hire, did they invest in R&D or pay down debt?
*   **Right Conversation?** Yes. It speaks to the core of labor and public economics.

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

*   **Setup:** Governments use payroll tax cuts as a standard lever for job creation.
*   **Tension:** Theory says this should work if it lowers the cost of labor, but Belgium’s rigid wages provide a rare "pure" test where the tax cut can't be "eaten" by wage increases.
*   **Resolution:** Even with a massive cost drop, firms didn't hire.
*   **Implications:** Tax cuts are inefficient job-creation tools in rigid economies; demand, not cost, is the binding constraint.

**Evaluation:** The narrative is exceptionally strong. It is not just a collection of regressions; it is an experiment designed to break a common policy assumption.

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

*   **The Fact:** "Belgium cut employer taxes by 7%—a €3 billion gift to firms—and the employment line didn't budge relative to Germany or the Netherlands."
*   **Reaction:** People will lean in. The "cost per job" calculation (>$120,000/job) is a devastating policy takeaway.
*   **Follow-up:** "So, did the firms just buy back shares or buy new machines?" This is the question the paper *must* eventually answer.

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

*   **Front-loading:** The paper is well-structured. The "First Stage" (Table 2) is crucial and rightly placed to prove the reform actually lowered costs.
*   **Appendices:** The leave-one-sector-out (Table 4) is good for the appendix, but the **Triple-Diff by Labor Intensity** (Table 3, Col 4) is a "main character" result and should be highlighted more. It’s the strongest evidence that the "cost of labor" channel isn't working.
*   **Length:** The discussion of the Brussels attacks is necessary but could be even more concise.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

**The Gap:** The current version is a very high-quality *Applied Economics Letters* or *Journal of Public Economics* paper. To reach the AER, it needs more **ambition regarding the "Why?"**

The null result on employment is "Step 1." The AER reader wants "Step 2": **The Anatomy of a Windfall.** Using sector-level data is the paper's biggest limitation. If the author cannot get firm-level data, they need to at least use sector-level investment, profit, or bankruptcy data to show the "resolution" of the windfall.

**Single most impactful advice:** Find a way to track the €3 billion. If it didn't go to wages (blocked) and didn't go to new hires (null result), show us—even at a coarse level—that it went to firm profits or capital investment.
