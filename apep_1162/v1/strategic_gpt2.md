## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper studies one of the largest recent employer payroll tax cuts in the OECD: Belgium’s 2016–2018 reduction in employer social security contributions. Because Belgian wage-setting institutions are unusually rigid, the reform is framed as a clean test of whether lowering firms’ labor costs, holding wages fixed, actually creates jobs; the headline finding is that it did not.

Why should a busy economist care? Because payroll tax cuts are routinely sold as job-creation policy, and this paper claims that even a very large cut may do little when labor demand is not the binding margin. If true, that is a policy-relevant result at the intersection of labor, public finance, and macro.

**Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Mostly yes, but not optimally. The current introduction is lucid and professional, but it spends too much of its initial energy on tax incidence theory and not enough on the striking empirical fact. The hook should be the scale of the reform and the surprising null in a setting where standard intuition would predict the best chance of finding an effect.

**What the first two paragraphs should say instead:**

> Governments often cut employer payroll taxes to create jobs, yet we still know surprisingly little about whether these policies work when wages cannot adjust. Belgium offers an unusually revealing case: between 2016 and 2018 it reduced the standard employer social security contribution rate from 32.4 to 25 percent—one of the largest broad-based payroll tax cuts in recent OECD history—while automatic wage indexation and sectoral bargaining sharply limited wage pass-through.  
>   
> This paper asks a simple question: when labor becomes much cheaper for firms but workers’ wages are effectively fixed, do firms hire more? Using sector-level comparisons between Belgium and neighboring European economies, I find no employment response despite a clear reduction in non-wage labor costs. The result suggests that in a rigid-wage setting, broad payroll tax cuts may mainly raise firm surplus rather than employment.

That version leads with the fact pattern, the puzzle, and the payoff.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution in one sentence:**  
The paper argues that Belgium’s large employer payroll tax cut provides a rare test of labor-demand responses under wage rigidity, and finds no detectable employment effect despite a clear fall in employer non-wage labor costs.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?
Partially, but not sharply enough. The paper correctly distinguishes itself from studies where payroll tax changes pass through to wages or target specific groups, but the distinction is still mostly “this is another payroll tax paper, except in Belgium and with rigid wages.” That is not yet a top-journal contribution by itself.

The closest comparators are:
- **Gruber (1997)** on Chile
- **Kugler and Kugler (2009)** on Colombia
- **Saez, Schoefer, and Seim (2019)** on Sweden
- **Kramarz and Philippon (2001)** on France  
and secondarily
- **Saez et al. (2012)** on Greece
- **Bennmarker et al. (2009)** on Sweden
- **Huttunen, Pirttilä, and Uusitalo (2013)** on Finland

The current draft says, in effect, “those papers study settings with pass-through, targeting, or different institutions; Belgium is the polar case.” That is directionally right. But to stand out, the paper has to say more explicitly what belief about the world changes because of this case. Right now the novelty is more **contextual** than **conceptual**.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or filling a gap in a LITERATURE?
Mostly the world, which is good. The central question—do lower employer labor costs create jobs when wages are rigid?—is a real-world question. The paper should lean even harder into that and less into “this contributes to the debate about payroll tax incidence.”

### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what’s new?
Yes, but with a caveat. A good economist could say:
> “It’s a paper on Belgium’s huge employer payroll tax cut; because wages were rigid, it’s supposed to isolate the employment margin, and they find no jobs effect.”

That is understandable. But another economist might still summarize it as:
> “Another DiD paper on a payroll tax reform with a null.”

That is the danger. The paper’s current framing does not fully elevate the result above the large pile of quasi-experimental policy papers.

### What would make the contribution bigger?
Most importantly, the paper needs to move from **“null in Belgium”** to **“what broad class of models or policy arguments does this discipline?”** Concrete ways to do that:

1. **Make the object of interest bigger than employment levels.**  
   The current story jumps from “no employment effect” to “profit windfall.” But profits are not measured. A bigger paper would trace where the surplus went: margins, prices, investment, firm survival, vacancies, hours, or productivity. Without that, the “windfall” language reads as inference rather than contribution.

2. **Strengthen the mechanism framing around the binding margin.**  
   If the story is really “product demand, not labor costs, constrained hiring,” the paper should connect to literatures on slack, markups, monopsony, or adjustment frictions—not just payroll tax incidence. That would make the result intellectually larger.

3. **Clarify whether the lesson is about rigid wages, broad-based cuts, mature economies, or post-crisis demand conditions.**  
   Right now all four are mixed together. A bigger contribution isolates which feature matters.

4. **If possible, show heterogeneity where standard theory most strongly predicts action.**  
   Not as a robustness exercise, but as part of the main story: tradable sectors, low-margin sectors, labor-intensive sectors, sectors facing stronger foreign competition, or sectors with high vacancy rates. If nowhere responds, the null becomes more powerful.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

### Closest neighbors
The paper’s immediate neighbors are:

- **Gruber (1997)**, payroll tax incidence in Chile  
- **Kugler and Kugler (2009)**, labor market effects of payroll taxes in Colombia  
- **Kramarz and Philippon (2001)**, payroll-tax subsidies and low-wage employment in France  
- **Saez, Schoefer, and Seim (2019)**, payroll tax cuts in Sweden  
- **Bennmarker, Mellander, and Öckert (2009)**, regional payroll tax cuts in Sweden  
- **Huttunen, Pirttilä, and Uusitalo (2013)**, low-wage labor cost subsidies in Finland

There is also a broader neighboring literature the paper only partially engages:
- incidence of labor taxation in systems with collective bargaining,
- employment effects of labor cost shocks under nominal/real wage rigidity,
- macro-labor work on whether labor demand or product demand binds employment.

### How should the paper position itself relative to those neighbors?
**Build on and qualify**, not attack. The paper should say:
- Earlier work shows payroll tax cuts can affect wages, employment, or both depending on institutional setting and targeting.
- Belgium lets us examine the case where pass-through is constrained and the reform is broad-based rather than narrowly targeted.
- The paper therefore tests a limiting case that is conceptually important for policy design.

That is stronger than implying prior work was somehow missing the “true” mechanism.

### Is the paper currently positioned too narrowly or too broadly?
A bit of both, oddly.

- **Too narrowly** in the sense that the empirical design and data are very sector-Eurostat-specific; the paper risks being read as “Belgium plus neighboring countries, 2013–2019.”
- **Too broadly** in the sense that the introduction occasionally claims to settle whether payroll tax cuts create jobs in rigid-wage economies generally.

The right scope is: **an important, unusually revealing case with broader implications**, not a universal verdict.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?
Not unaware, exactly, but under-engaged with:
1. **Macro/labor demand literature** on why cost reductions may not translate into hiring when output demand is limited.
2. **Search/matching and vacancy posting** work—if firms did not hire more, did they post more vacancies, substitute margins, or simply absorb the subsidy?
3. **Collective bargaining / wage-setting institutions** literature beyond a few citations. The institutional claim is central, so that literature should be more fully integrated.
4. Possibly **firm-side incidence / rent-sharing / markups** work, since “profit windfall” is doing a lot of rhetorical work.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?
Not quite. It is currently having the conversation:
> “What is another data point in the payroll-tax-effects literature?”

The more interesting conversation is:
> “When does lowering labor costs fail to increase labor demand, even when wages are rigid and the first stage is large?”

That latter framing connects labor, public finance, and macro in a more AER-like way.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Governments cut employer payroll taxes to stimulate employment. Standard theory says lower labor costs should raise labor demand, but empirical incidence and employment effects depend on institutions.

### Tension
Most existing evidence is muddied by wage pass-through, targeted reforms, or special populations. Belgium seems like the ideal test case because wages are unusually rigid, so one would expect the effect on employment—if labor demand is elastic—to show up clearly.

### Resolution
A very large payroll tax cut sharply reduced employer non-wage labor costs, yet employment in Belgium did not rise relative to comparable countries.

### Implications
Broad payroll tax cuts may be weak job-creation tools in rigid-wage settings; lowering labor costs is not enough if firms are constrained by output demand or other margins.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
Yes, more than many papers do. This is one of its strengths. But the resolution and implication are a bit overextended relative to what is shown.

The paper wants the story to be:
1. large reform,
2. rigid wages,
3. clean first stage,
4. no jobs,
5. therefore tax cut became profits because demand binds.

That is a good story skeleton. The problem is that step 5 outruns the evidence. The paper has a **story**, but the final act is too assertive given the outcomes observed.

### If it is a collection of results looking for a story, what story should it tell?
It is not just a collection of results. But the better story is:

> Belgium provides a tough test for the standard policy claim that cheaper labor creates jobs. In a setting where wage pass-through is limited and the cost shock is large, there is still no employment response. This does not mean payroll tax cuts never work; it means their effectiveness depends critically on which margin is binding.

That is more disciplined and more persuasive.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?
“Belgium cut employer payroll taxes from 32.4 to 25 percent—huge by OECD standards—and employment still didn’t move.”

That is a good line. People will listen.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
They would lean in initially. The setting is strong enough to attract attention. But the next question comes fast.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Almost certainly:
> “So where did the money go?”

And right now the paper cannot answer that convincingly. That is the strategic weakness.

A second question would be:
> “Why should I think Belgium tells me something general rather than just something about Belgium?”

That also needs a cleaner answer.

### If the findings are null or modest: is the null itself interesting?
Yes, potentially very interesting. This is not a boring null if the treatment is truly large and the setting is a strong test of a common policy argument. The paper is right to treat the null as informative rather than apologetic.

But the paper needs to make the case more carefully. Right now it sometimes sounds like:
> “We found no effect, therefore the policy failed.”

The stronger version is:
> “This was a high-powered test in a setting where standard logic most strongly predicts a response; the absence of one is informative about when payroll tax cuts should and should not be expected to work.”

That turns the null into a result, not a failed experiment.

- **Mostly an ambition problem:** The paper is competent and tidy, but currently a bit too safe and self-contained for AER.

The top people in this field would likely say:

**If the author can change only one thing, it should be this:**  
Reframe the paper around the broader economic question—*when do labor-cost cuts fail to increase employment because labor demand is not the binding margin?*—and support that framing with evidence on where the surplus went or, failing that, materially soften the “profit windfall” claim.

That is the difference between a careful Belgium reform paper and a paper with AER aspirations.
