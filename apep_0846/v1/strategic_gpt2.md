## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether reforming partition law—specifically the Uniform Partition of Heirs Property Act (UPHPA)—helps preserve Black homeownership by reducing forced sales of jointly inherited property. A busy economist should care because this is a potentially important and under-measured institutional channel linking property law to racial wealth inequality: the paper’s core claim is that seemingly technical legal rules governing inheritance disputes can shape long-run Black asset ownership.

The paper does **not yet articulate this pitch as clearly as it should in the first two paragraphs**. The opening is vivid and morally compelling, but it starts with Black farmland loss rather than the broader economic question the paper can answer. The reader gets legal background before getting the punchline: why this is an economics paper and what general lesson it offers.

### The pitch the first two paragraphs should have

Here is the version the paper should lead with:

> Black wealth is heavily tied to owner-occupied housing and inherited real estate, yet one feature of American property law has long made inherited Black property unusually vulnerable to forced sale. Under traditional partition rules, any co-owner of an inherited property can force liquidation of the entire parcel, often at a discount, creating a mechanism through which fragmented inheritance translates into involuntary asset loss.
>
> This paper studies whether reforming that rule changes ownership outcomes. I examine staggered state adoption of the Uniform Partition of Heirs Property Act, a law that makes forced partition sales harder and less extractive, and ask whether it increases Black homeownership. The broader question is whether legal protections around inherited property can slow racial wealth erosion; the answer matters for how economists think about property institutions, racial inequality, and the persistence of intergenerational wealth gaps.

That version gives the world question immediately: **Do inheritance and partition rules affect racial wealth accumulation?** Then the paper can go into the historical facts.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper’s contribution is to provide what it presents as the first causal evidence on whether heirs’ property reform affects Black homeownership, showing little immediate average effect but suggestive gains that grow over time.

### Evaluation

#### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?
Only partially. The paper clearly differentiates itself from **legal/descriptive heirs’ property scholarship** by saying “no published study has estimated the causal effect of UPHPA on any outcome.” That is a useful novelty claim. But relative to economics papers on racial wealth, homeownership, and property institutions, the differentiation is weaker. Right now the contribution reads a bit like: “Here is a new policy in a known area, and I estimate its effect with modern DiD.”

The issue is not whether the topic is new—it is—but whether the introduction makes clear **what economists learn here that they could not have learned from adjacent work on property rights, housing, wealth transmission, or legal institutions.** That is still fuzzy.

#### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or filling a gap in a LITERATURE?
Mostly as filling a literature gap: “no published study has estimated the causal effect.” That is weaker. The stronger frame is about the world:

- Why has Black property ownership been so fragile over generations?
- Do procedural rules in property law materially affect racial wealth preservation?
- Can legal protection of inherited property move measurable ownership outcomes?

Those are AER-style questions. “First causal evaluation of UPHPA” is not enough by itself.

#### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?
Not cleanly enough. Right now they might say:

> “It’s a staggered DiD on a property-law reform and Black homeownership.”

That is not fatal, but it is not memorable. The paper needs the colleague-summary to be:

> “It shows that a technical inheritance/partition rule is a real mechanism behind racial asset loss, and that changing the rule can slowly preserve Black ownership.”

That is bigger and more world-facing.

#### What would make this contribution bigger?
Very specifically:

1. **Use outcomes closer to the mechanism.**  
   Homeownership is understandable, but it is one step removed and somewhat blunt. The contribution would be much bigger with direct outcomes such as:
   - partition filings,
   - forced sale incidence,
   - foreclosure/tax sale interactions,
   - property retention following inheritance,
   - title clearing / succession formalization,
   - probate or estate-planning responses,
   - property value realization conditional on sale.

2. **Show this is about inherited-asset preservation, not just housing tenure.**  
   If the paper could connect to parcel-level or court-level outcomes, the story becomes: law changes wealth extraction. Right now it is law changes a county-level rate.

3. **Exploit heterogeneity tied to heirs’ property prevalence.**  
   The current splits are generic. A much bigger contribution would come from showing larger effects in places with ex ante high heirs’ property exposure, high intestacy risk, or high Black land fragmentation. That would make the paper feel less like “DiD on all counties” and more like a test of a sharp mechanism.

4. **Frame the paper around intergenerational wealth transmission, not only homeownership.**  
   Homeownership is a proxy. Wealth preservation is the real question.

- this is not classic titling/formalization,

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Black families have historically lost land and property at disproportionate rates, and one underappreciated reason may be the vulnerability of heirs’ property under partition law.

### Tension
A legal mechanism seems important in theory and in historical accounts, but we do not know whether changing the legal rule measurably changes real-world ownership outcomes.

### Resolution
The paper finds little immediate average effect of UPHPA on Black homeownership, but dynamic estimates suggest effects that emerge slowly and grow over time, with no comparable effect on white homeownership.

### Implications
Procedural reforms in property law may matter for preserving minority asset ownership, but their effects are gradual and likely accumulate through the slow resolution of disputes rather than immediate shifts in behavior.

### Evaluation
There **is** a real narrative arc here, but the paper does not fully trust it. It keeps oscillating between three stories:

1. a racial wealth-gap story,
2. a property-law mechanism story,
3. a staggered-DiD dynamics story.

The strongest story is clearly **#2 as a route into #1**:  
technical property-law reform can alter the long-run preservation of Black-owned assets.

Right now the paper sometimes feels like a collection of sensible results in search of a hierarchy. The hierarchy should be:

- Main story: partition law is an economically meaningful mechanism of racial asset loss.
- Evidence: UPHPA appears to increase Black homeownership only gradually.
- Broader implication: legal design of co-ownership and inheritance rules affects intergenerational wealth preservation.

The event-study dynamics are useful, but they should support the story rather than become the story.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?
I would lead with:

> “A technical change in partition law appears to raise Black homeownership over time by protecting inherited property from forced sale.”

That is the dinner-party version.

### Would people lean in or reach for phones?
Some would lean in—the topic is interesting, historically important, and underexplored. But many would reach for their phones if the next sentence is:

> “The overall ATT is small and insignificant, but…”

That is the paper’s current vulnerability. The headline finding is modest, and the stronger finding is dynamic and somewhat slower-burning. So the pitch needs to make clear why the gradual effect is exactly what one should expect from this kind of institutional reform.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Almost immediately:

> “Do you see the mechanism directly—fewer partition sales or more retention of inherited property?”

That is the central strategic weakness. The paper’s current main outcome is too distal from the mechanism, so this follow-up is unavoidable.

### If findings are null or modest, is the null interesting?
Only partly as currently framed. The paper does a decent job saying this is a slow-moving reform, so a small short-run average effect is not a failed experiment. But it needs to go further and say:

- why a near-zero pooled ATT is exactly what one expects when late adopters dominate exposure,
- why the relevant object is cumulative long-run preservation, not immediate jumps,
- and why “small in annual flow, meaningful in stock” is the right economics.

At present, the paper comes close, but the reader still feels a bit like they are being asked to forgive a weak main estimate because the dynamics are nicer. The paper needs to invert that: **the dynamics are the substantive result**.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

Bluntly: in current form, this feels more like a **good field-journal or strong specialty-journal paper with an important topic** than an AER paper. The topic is promising; the current execution is strategically not yet at AER level.

### What is the gap?

#### Mostly a framing problem
The science may be competent, but the paper is underselling and mislocating its strongest claim. It is too attached to “first causal study of UPHPA” and too willing to present a modest county-level homeownership estimate as the payoff.

#### Also a scope problem
For AER, the outcome is too narrow and too indirect relative to the mechanism. If the paper wants to make economists sit up, it needs either:
- more direct mechanism evidence, or
- a much sharper design showing where the reform should matter most.

#### Some novelty risk
The policy is new, but the empirical genre is familiar. Without direct mechanism evidence or a bigger conceptual frame, some readers will indeed say: “another DiD paper about a state law.”

#### Some ambition problem
The paper is careful but safe. It takes the available county-level data and runs a reasonable design. AER papers usually ask more of the setting: either deeper mechanism, broader conceptual payoff, or a more surprising implication.

### The single most impactful piece of advice
**Reframe the paper around inherited-property vulnerability as a mechanism of racial wealth loss, and then bring in at least one outcome or heterogeneity test that is much closer to that mechanism than county-level homeownership.**

If they can only change one thing, that is it. Not more robustness. Not more DiD discussion. They need to make the paper about **how property law shapes intergenerational wealth preservation**, not merely about whether a statute nudges a county-level homeownership rate.
