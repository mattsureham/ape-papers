**1. Idea Fidelity**

The paper largely pursues the original manifest idea, exploiting the phased Landing Obligation implementation across pelagic, demersal, and other species as a staggered natural experiment to study catch composition and fleet behavior. The main difference is that the paper condenses the originally more ambitious agenda (e.g., explicit use of STECF discard estimates, fleet capacity, and dosage via exemption rates) into a core analysis focused on Eurostat catches and a TWFE/DDD strategy. Key elements—staggered treatment, country × species-group units, Norway/Iceland as controls, and the choke-species mechanism—remain intact, but the manifest’s emphasis on extended robustness (HonestDiD, TAC controls, discard dosage) is not fully reflected. The empirical design relies on the same species-group DiD, but it would benefit from integrating additional manifest elements (e.g., quota controls, STECF discard data) to bolster identification.

**2. Summary**

The paper provides a first causal evaluation of the EU Landing Obligation by exploiting its staggered roll-out across species groups. Using Eurostat country × species-group catch data (2000–2024), the authors find that demersal catches in EU waters plummeted (≈71 percent) after the 2016 choke-species cohort went live, while pelagic catches remained unchanged. Placebo tests, a triple-difference with non-EU comparators, and placebo timing support the claim that the regulation reduced fishing effort—particularly in mixed demersal fisheries—rather than simply converting discards into landings.

**3. Essential Points**

1. **Credibility of Parallel Trends / Species-Group Comparators.** The identification rests on comparing pelagic versus demersal (“treated” in 2016) species within countries, yet these groups may differ in long-run trends due to biology, stock management, and TAC cycles. Table 2’s placebo indicates a modest pre-trend in demersal catches, and the large post-treatment estimate could be conflated with stock-driven declines that coincided with the Landing Obligation. The paper needs to demonstrate that the pre-treatment evolution of demersal and pelagic catches is not systematically different—ideally through richer event-study evidence, differential trends tests controlling for TAC trajectories, or by conditioning on pre-trends to ensure that the “within-country, across-species-group” comparisons are credible.

2. **Inference and Statistical Power.** The demersal coefficient is large but the wild cluster bootstrap p-value is 0.204, meaning conventional inference may be overstating significance due to the small number of clusters (17 countries). The paper highlights the bootstrap conservatism, but the current treatment of uncertainty is insufficient. Without robust evidence on significance, the striking qualitative conclusion (“demersal fishing collapsed”) is on shaky ground. The authors must either (a) present additional inference strategies that yield tighter bounds (e.g., bias-reduced linearization, randomization inference leveraging the small number of treatment cohorts), or (b) soften claims to reflect uncertainty. As it stands, the main result is only suggestive, yet the narrative reads as definitive.

3. **Mechanism Evidence for Choke Species.** While the qualitative story is plausible, the paper does not provide direct evidence that choke species—i.e., low-quota bycatch—caused the decline rather than alternative channel(s) such as quota cuts, market shocks, or fleet exits. The landing-to-catch ratio remaining constant is consistent with both reduced fishing and improved selectivity, so it is not decisive. The paper should either incorporate quota/effort data (e.g., TACs, STECF effort series) to show that the decline occurred even controlling for quotas, or provide micro-level checks (e.g., species-level quota exhaustion, trip lengths, or bycatch composition) to substantiate the choke-species mechanism. Without such evidence, the causal pathway remains speculative.

**4. Suggestions**

- **Strengthen Event-Study and Pre-Treatment Balance.** Include a fully fleshed event-study plot for each species group showing year-by-year estimates around treatment. This will allow readers to see whether demersal catches were on a parallel trajectory with pelagic before 2016. Use pre-treatment coefficients to test for differential trends formally and, if necessary, include pre-treatment trend interactions (e.g., country × species-group linear trends) so that identification relies on deviations from those trends.

- **Control for TACs, Stock Status, and Other Confounders.** Since TACs and stock rebuilding plans often differ by species group and change over time, the observed catch decline could partly reflect quota reductions unrelated to the Landing Obligation. Incorporating species-group–specific TACs (from Eurostat or ICES) and stock status indicators into the regression would help isolate the effect of the discard ban. Alternatively, weighting outcomes by TAC or running the analysis on residual catches after regressing out TAC changes would clarify how much of the decline is truly policy-driven.

- **Leverage STECF / Discard Data for Mechanism.** The manifest mentioned STECF discard estimates; integrating them could provide direct evidence that discards did not fall or that the landing-to-catch ratio stayed constant despite the reform. Similarly, include effort or capacity data (also available in STECF FDI) to check whether vessels indeed reduced fishing days or trips, consistent with the choke mechanism. A direct link between quota exhaustion on bycatch species and trip termination (e.g., showing that low-quota species hit limits more often post-LO) would substantially bolster the choke-species narrative.

- **Address Inference and Sample Composition.** Given the sensitivity of the p-value to clustering, consider alternative inference strategies tailored to a small number of clusters or to the cross-sectional nature of the data (e.g., wild cluster bootstrap with the Euclidean algorithm for small samples, or considering a permutation test over treatment cohorts). Also, clarify the role of the UK post-Brexit: is it treated as EU throughout 2015–2019? If so, explain why the change in membership does not affect the treatment indicator. If not, consider whether excluding the UK alters results.

- **Clarify Species-Group Classification and Treatment Dates.** The treatment grouping lumps broad categories (pelagic: 11 species, demersal: 19 species, “other”: >1,500 species) which could mask heterogeneity. Provide an appendix list of species codes per group and explain how species transitions (e.g., species moved between cohorts or reclassified) are handled. For the “other” group treated in 2019, the post-period is short; the lack of significance there might simply reflect insufficient time. Consider analyzing the 2017 cohort separately if data allow.

- **Quantify Economic Importance and Robustness of Cumulative Estimates.** The extrapolation to €28–€41 billion in foregone revenue is headline-grabbing but rests on multiple assumptions (uniform price, persistent decline, no substitution). Provide sensitivity analysis around these assumptions, or focus on reporting the more conservative per-country-year tonne reduction. This will make the economic narrative more credible.

- **Enhance Discussion of Controls and Fixed Effects.** Equation (1) includes country × species-group FE and year FE. Clarify whether standard errors cluster at the country level or country × species-group, and justify the choice given the small number of clusters. Additionally, explain how the “country × year” saturated FE specification deals with collinearity when combined with the treatment indicator—why is identification possible when treatment varies only by species group and year?

- **Consider Alternative Outcomes.** The landing-to-catch ratio is one outcome, but you could also analyze landings alone or discard estimates (if available). If STECF provides discard estimates by species, check whether discards declined but were swallowed by increased mortality. Introducing effort (days at sea) or capacity utilization metrics would further test whether the regulation reduced fishing rather than redirecting it.

- **Clarify the role of Norway and Iceland controls.** The triple-difference specification is valuable, but the paper should explain how the comparator sample is constructed—are Norway and Iceland the only non-EU countries, and are their species-group treatment indicators artificially set to “never treated”? Make explicit whether the same species-group classification applies, how missing data are handled, and whether non-EU countries share the same species mix.

Overall, the paper addresses an important policy question and introduces a promising identification strategy, but it needs to shore up the parallel-trends assumption, provide stronger inference, and offer more direct evidence on the choke-species channel to fully convince readers that the Landing Obligation reduced fishing rather than just discards.
