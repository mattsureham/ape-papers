## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper studies a major EU fisheries reform that banned discarding regulated fish at sea and asked vessels to land all catch against quota. The central claim is that, in mixed demersal fisheries, the policy created “choke species” constraints that reduced total fishing activity rather than simply converting discards into landings, while pelagic fisheries were largely unaffected. A busy economist should care because this is a potentially important example of how rigid environmental regulation in multi-product production can suppress output without solving the underlying inefficiency.

The paper comes reasonably close to articulating this pitch in the first two paragraphs, but it does not do so as cleanly or as credibly as it should. The current opening is vivid and policy-relevant, but the paper overstates what it can show: it can show a sharp differential decline in catches, and it can argue that this pattern is consistent with choke-species constraints; it does not really establish “reduced fishing instead of waste” with the confidence the title and opening imply. The introduction should dial back the prosecutorial tone and sharpen the economic question.

### The pitch the paper should have

“Why do some environmental regulations reduce the targeted externality while others mainly reduce production? This paper studies the EU’s Landing Obligation, which banned discarding at sea and required all regulated catch to count against quota. I show that after the policy took effect, catches fell sharply in mixed demersal fisheries but not in largely single-species pelagic fisheries, consistent with the idea that rigid quota accounting created ‘choke species’ constraints. The broader lesson is that in multi-product regulated industries, banning an undesirable practice without adding flexibility can shift behavior through reduced activity rather than cleaner production.”

That is the AER version of the story: not “I evaluate one fisheries rule,” but “here is a general economic mechanism, shown in a consequential setting.”

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:**  
The paper argues that the EU discard ban reduced catches in mixed demersal fisheries, consistent with choke-species constraints, and uses this episode to illustrate how inflexible command-and-control regulation can reduce output rather than improve selectivity.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?
Only partially. The paper is differentiated from the fisheries-science discussion of choke species because it tries to bring a causal reduced-form design to the question. But it is less clearly differentiated from the broader economics literature because the paper gestures at command-and-control versus flexible regulation without really showing that it adds something conceptually new beyond a familiar lesson: rigid rules can have unintended production effects.

Right now, the contribution is too often framed as:
- “first causal evaluation of the Landing Obligation,” and
- “there is no causal evidence in this literature.”

That is not enough for AER. “First causal evaluation” is a useful selling point, but it is not a durable contribution unless the underlying question is important and the answer changes how economists think.

### World question or literature gap?
The paper mostly starts with a **world question**, which is good: what happens when you ban discarding in a mixed-fishery quota system? But it repeatedly falls back to a weaker **literature-gap** formulation: no one has causally evaluated this policy. The stronger frame is the world one.

### Could a smart economist explain what is new?
At the moment, maybe, but not confidently. They would probably say:  
“It's a diff-in-diff paper on the EU discard ban showing demersal catches fell more than pelagic catches, maybe because of choke species.”

That is not yet crisp enough. The risk is exactly that the paper reads as “another DiD paper about regulation X” rather than “a paper about a general mechanism in production under regulation.”

### What would make the contribution bigger?
Very specifically, one of the following would enlarge the paper substantially:

1. **Directly observe the margin the paper wants to speak about.**  
   The title and framing are about waste/discards and fishing effort, but the core outcome is catch. If the paper brought in discard data, trip-level effort, days at sea, quota exhaustion, or vessel stoppages, the story would become much more convincing and much bigger.

2. **Show reallocation across species within mixed fisheries.**  
   If the mechanism is choke species, one wants to see not just a fall in aggregate demersal catch, but the specific pattern of target species left uncaught because low-quota bycatch binds.

3. **Connect the result to welfare or market consequences.**  
   Prices, profits, quota lease prices, fleet exit, substitution toward other fisheries, or consumer supply effects would make this a paper about economic incidence, not merely physical catches.

4. **Frame it as a general multi-product regulation problem.**  
   The strongest version is not “the EU fisheries rule backfired,” but “when compliance is imposed at the most restrictive product margin, multi-product firms may contract output rather than adjust composition.”

If the author could do only one substantive thing, it would be to bring in **direct evidence on effort/discards/choke constraints**. Without that, the paper’s headline outruns the evidence.

It should **build on** the fisheries-science literature and **translate** that literature into economics. It should not attack those papers; they are setting up the mechanism. The paper’s role is: “biologists and fisheries scientists predicted choke-species problems; economists should care because this is a clean example of inefficient rigidity in a quota-regulated common-pool resource.”

Relative to the economics literature, the paper should **not pretend** it is overturning the general insight that command-and-control can be inefficient. That claim is too broad and too familiar. Instead, it should say this setting reveals a specific version of that problem: **a non-tradable, species-specific compliance constraint in a joint-production technology.**

That is much more precise and much more interesting.

### Is the current positioning too narrow or too broad?
Oddly, both.

- **Too narrow** because much of the paper reads like a fisheries-policy note.
- **Too broad** because the command-and-control rhetoric reaches for sweeping implications the paper cannot quite support.

The right audience is not only fisheries economists. It is also economists interested in:
- environmental instrument design,
- multi-product firms under regulation,
- common-pool resource management,
- incomplete markets in regulatory compliance,
- and production distortions from input/output-specific constraints.

### What literature does it seem unaware of?
The paper should be speaking more explicitly to:
1. **Multi-product firm regulation / production distortion** literature
2. **Misallocation / non-tradability of rights** literature
3. **Compliance flexibility** literature in environmental economics
4. Possibly **agricultural and bycatch regulation** analogues, where production of one output jointly generates regulated incidental outputs

There may also be a useful connection to the literature on **complementarities and bottlenecks**: one scarce compliance margin shuts down an otherwise profitable activity.

### Is it having the right conversation?
Not quite. The current conversation is:
- “Here is a policy in fisheries; it may not have worked.”

The better conversation is:
- “Here is what happens when regulators impose inflexible accounting in joint production environments: the bottleneck margin governs the whole production set.”

That is the conversation more likely to travel.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Before this paper, the world looks like this: discarding is widely seen as wasteful; the EU bans it; policymakers and scientists debate whether the reform will improve selectivity or create choke-species problems in mixed fisheries.

### Tension
The tension is strong and real: a policy designed to reduce waste may, because of joint production and species-specific quotas, instead constrain fishing activity. The critical unresolved question is whether the Landing Obligation changed what vessels landed versus what they caught, or whether it just made them stop fishing earlier.

### Resolution
The paper’s resolution is: demersal catches fall a lot after the reform, pelagic catches do not, and the pattern lines up with the choke-species mechanism.

### Implications
The implication is that policy design matters: rigid bans without flexibility can shift behavior through output reduction rather than cleaner production.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
Yes, more than many papers do. This is not a random bag of results. There is a recognizable story.

But it is **an overclaimed story**. The narrative is stronger than the evidence currently on the page. The paper wants the resolution to be:
- “the discard ban reduced fishing instead of waste.”

But what it really shows is:
- “the discard ban coincides with a large relative decline in demersal catches, consistent with choke-species constraints.”

That may sound like a small distinction, but editorially it is a big one. The paper needs to align the narrative with what it can actually establish.

### What story should it be telling?
It should tell a story about **regulatory bottlenecks in joint production**. Fisheries are the setting, not the only point. The policy relevance of the discard ban is real, but the reason this could be an AER paper is the broader mechanism.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?
I would say:

“When the EU banned discarding, catches collapsed in mixed demersal fisheries but not in pelagic fisheries, suggesting the rule turned low-quota bycatch species into bottlenecks that shut down otherwise viable fishing.”

That is the dinner-party line.

### Would people lean in?
Some would. Environmental economists, IO-adjacent applied micro people, and resource economists would lean in. Many others would need the next sentence to understand why this is more than a fisheries anecdote.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Immediately:
- “Did discards actually fall?”
Then:
- “How do you know this isn’t just quota tightening or stock changes?”
And then:
- “What margin adjusted—effort, composition, exit, or quota trading?”

Those are exactly the questions the paper is currently vulnerable on. Again, not asking me to referee identification, but strategically: if the obvious follow-up question is the paper’s weakest point, the framing needs adjustment or the evidence base needs expansion.

### If the findings are modest or partly null
The pelagic null is useful because it supports the mechanism. The stable landing-to-catch ratio is potentially very interesting, but only if the audience trusts the measure and sees why it is informative about discarding. At present, the paper wants that null to carry too much weight. It is suggestive, not dispositive.

This does not feel like a failed experiment. It does feel like a paper with one very interesting fact and a somewhat inflated interpretation of that fact.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The gap is mostly a mix of **framing problem**, **scope problem**, and **ambition problem**.

### Framing problem
The science is more interesting than the current fisheries-policy packaging suggests, but the paper also overstates what it knows. It needs a cleaner conceptual frame: this is about regulation under joint production and bottleneck compliance constraints.

### Scope problem
The outcome set is too narrow for the claims. Catch alone is not enough to support a headline about waste, selectivity, or fishing effort. AER papers in this space usually do more to trace the mechanism and the economic margins of adjustment.

### Novelty problem
The specific policy is novel enough, but the broader lesson is not novel unless the paper sharpens what is special here: species-specific quota accounting in mixed fisheries is a particular form of non-tradable compliance right in a multi-output production technology.

### Ambition problem
The paper is competent but currently a bit safe in the wrong way. It is safe empirically but rhetorically ambitious. For AER, I would prefer the opposite: more ambitious in mechanism and scope, more disciplined in claims.

### Single most impactful advice
**Either bring in direct evidence on effort/discards/choke-species binding, or rewrite the paper so the claim is strictly about differential catch declines consistent with regulatory bottlenecks in mixed fisheries.**

That is the fork. Right now the paper straddles both and does neither fully enough.
