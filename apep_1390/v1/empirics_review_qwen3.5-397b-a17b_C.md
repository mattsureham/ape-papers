# Review: The Visiting Nurse Dividend

## 1. Idea Fidelity
The paper adheres closely to the Original Idea Manifest. It utilizes the specified data source (IPUMS Multigenerational Longitudinal Panel, 1930–1950), the exact policy variation (Sheppard-Towner Act, 1921–1929), and the identified control group (Massachusetts, Connecticut, Illinois). The identification strategy (Triple-Differences) matches the manifest's design parameters. However, there is a notable discrepancy in sample size regarding wage outcomes: the manifest estimated ~500,000–575,000 individuals with valid wages, whereas the paper reports 1.8 million. This suggests either a relaxation of restrictions not detailed in the manifest or a difference in how "valid wage income" is defined (e.g., including non-zero values that the manifest excluded). Additionally, the estimated magnitude shifted from the smoke test ($46–58) to the final estimate ($39), which is an acceptable refinement given the inclusion of controls and fixed effects. Overall, the paper executes the core empirical plan with high fidelity.

## 2. Summary
This paper provides compelling individual-level evidence that America's first federal public health program, the Sheppard-Towner Act, generated modest but persistent earnings gains for exposed cohorts. By exploiting the non-participation of three states, the author isolates a "health productivity channel," demonstrating that wage increases occurred without corresponding gains in educational attainment. The results suggest that prenatal health investments can yield long-run economic returns through biological capacity rather than human capital accumulation, particularly for rural and Black populations who lacked baseline medical access.

## 3. Essential Points
The following three issues must be addressed to ensure the validity of the causal claims:

1.  **Validity of the Three-State Control Group:** The identification relies entirely on Massachusetts, Connecticut, and Illinois as counterfactuals for the rest of the United States. These states were uniquely industrialized, urbanized, and wealthier than the national average during this period. While state fixed effects absorb level differences, the *trends* in industrialization and urbanization between these three states and the rest of the country (particularly the South and West) may differ systematically. If the structural economic transition away from agriculture differed between the control states and the treatment states during the 1920s, the DDD coefficient will capture these differential trends rather than the policy effect. The paper acknowledges that state-specific trends cause collinearity, but this essentially confirms that the design cannot rule out differential state trends. You must demonstrate that pre-treatment cohort trends in *wages* were parallel between these three states and the rest of the US, not just that the coefficients are zero.
2.  **Selection Bias in Linked Wage Data:** The 1950 Census income data was collected on a sample-line basis (not full count), and linking rates across censuses are not random. The sample size for wage outcomes (1.8M) is significantly larger than the manifest's feasibility check (~500K), suggesting a broader inclusion criteria that may introduce noise or selection bias. If individuals in MA, CT, and IL are systematically harder or easier to link across decades than those in participating states (due to migration patterns or name commonality), the "unexposed" group is selected differently than the "exposed" group. You need to report linking rates by state and cohort to ensure the control group is not artificially constructed by differential linking success.
3.  **Mechanism Identification via Null Results:** The claim of a "health productivity channel" rests heavily on the null effect on education. While elegant, this is an indirect identification of health. A null education result could also stem from labor market constraints (e.g., exposed cohorts needed to work earlier due to family economics despite being healthier). To bolster the health mechanism claim, you should utilize available health proxies in the 1950 Census, such as disability status or weeks worked due to illness, or link to mortality records if possible. Relying solely on the wage/education dichotomy leaves the mechanism vulnerable to alternative labor supply interpretations.

## 4. Suggestions
The following recommendations are intended to strengthen the empirical robustness and narrative clarity of the paper. These adjustments are not strictly required for publication but would significantly enhance the paper's credibility and impact.

**Strengthening the Control Group Comparison**
Given the reliance on only three control states, a standard DDD may be too coarse. I recommend constructing a **synthetic control group** for MA, CT, and IL. Instead comparing them to the national average, weight the participating states to match the pre-treatment wage trends and industrial composition of the three refusers. If a synthetic control approach yields similar results, it mitigates the concern that the three states are simply too unique to serve as controls. Alternatively, consider a **border discontinuity design** more aggressively. The paper mentions a border-state restriction but dismisses it due to noise. However, focusing on counties bordering MA, CT, and IL (e.g., upstate NY, PA, OH) might provide a more credible local counterfactual than the national average, even if power is reduced. You could combine this with a Bayesian shrinkage approach to stabilize the estimates.

**Addressing Linking and Sample-Line Selection**
The discrepancy between the manifest's expected wage sample (~500K) and the paper's actual sample (1.8M) requires clarification. The 1950 Census income question was not asked of every household. You should explicitly state the sample line probability and whether it varies by state. Furthermore, conduct a **linking balance test**. Regress the probability of being linked (1930→1950) on the treatment indicator. If exposure to Sheppard-Towner predicts linking success, your sample is selected on unobservables correlated with the treatment. If linking rates differ, apply **inverse probability weighting** to reweight the sample to the 1930 population characteristics. This ensures that the 1950 wage earners represent the original birth cohorts rather than a selected subset of survivors and migrants.

**Deepening the Mechanism Evidence**
To move beyond the "null education" proxy for health, exploit other census variables. The 1950 Census includes questions on **weeks worked due to illness** and **non-employment due to disability**. If the health productivity channel is real, exposed cohorts should report fewer weeks lost to illness. Additionally, consider **occupational hazard sorting**. If health improved, workers might shift away from physically demanding occupations toward less strenuous ones, even without higher education. Checking occupational injury risk scores (e.g., from later BLS data matched to 1950 codes) could provide direct evidence of physical capacity changes. Finally, if feasible, link to **Social Security Administration mortality data**. Even a subset match showing reduced mid-life mortality for exposed cohorts would provide direct biological evidence supporting the wage gains.

**Refining Standard Error Interpretation**
With only three control states, the effective degrees of freedom are low. While you cluster by birth state (50+ clusters), the variation driving the DDD is essentially the difference between the 3 control states and the 45 treatment states. Consider reporting ** Romano-Wolf adjusted p-values** for the heterogeneity splits (Race, Rural) to account for multiple testing. Additionally, perform a **wild cluster bootstrap** specifically for the DDD coefficient. Standard asymptotic SEs may be misleading when the treatment variation is concentrated in so few units. Showing that the result holds under bootstrap inference would alleviate concerns about false positives driven by the small control group.

**Contextualizing the Magnitude**
The $39 effect is statistically significant but economically small (1.5%). In the cost-benefit section, you calculate a high return based on aggregate wage gains. However, consider the **distributional impact**. A $39 average gain might imply a $0 gain for the median worker and a $200 gain for the top quartile, or vice versa. Use the distributional figures (Figure 5) to discuss whether the program reduced inequality or simply shifted the mean. Given the heterogeneity results (Black +$92, Rural +$70), emphasize that the *average* effect masks substantial gains for marginalized groups.
