## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper studies whether exposure in utero/infancy to the Sheppard-Towner Act—the first federal maternal and infant health program in the United States—improved adult economic outcomes. Using the fact that three states refused the program, the paper argues that exposed cohorts earned more as adults, with little change in schooling, suggesting that prenatal health investments can raise later-life productivity even without increasing formal human capital.

A busy economist should care because the paper sits at the intersection of fetal origins, the long-run returns to early-life public spending, and the design of maternal health policy: it asks whether prenatal care changes adult earnings, and through what channel.

### Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?
Not quite. The opening is vivid, but too literary and too historical before it gets to the economic question. The first two paragraphs currently emphasize “America’s first federal social program” and the historical color of visiting nurses. That is good texture, but the AER pitch should get to the big economic question faster: **Do prenatal health investments raise adult earnings, and do they do so through schooling or through direct health/productivity?**

### The pitch the paper should have
Here is what the first two paragraphs should basically say:

> Economists increasingly agree that early-life conditions shape adult outcomes, but we still know surprisingly little about whether prenatal health investments raise adult earnings directly through health or indirectly through schooling. Most evidence on early-life interventions bundles these channels together, because the same programs often affect both health and education.
>
> This paper studies the long-run effects of the Sheppard-Towner Act of 1921, the first large-scale U.S. maternal and infant health program. Exploiting the fact that three states refused to participate, and linking individuals across census decades, I find that cohorts exposed before birth/infancy earned more as adults but did not complete more schooling. The results suggest that prenatal health investments can generate lasting labor-market returns through improved adult productivity even when they do not increase formal educational attainment.

That is a much more AER-style front door.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution
The paper’s core contribution is to provide individual-level historical evidence that prenatal exposure to a large maternal-health program increased adult earnings without increasing schooling, implying that early-life health investments can affect long-run productivity through a direct health channel.

### Is this clearly differentiated from the closest papers?
Only partially.

The paper does differentiate itself from:
- **Moehling and Thomasson** on Sheppard-Towner’s short-run mortality effects,
- broader fetal-origins work showing long-run effects of early-life health,
- and interventions like **Head Start** or **mothers’ pensions** that affect both schooling and earnings.

But the differentiation is still not sharp enough. Right now the paper reads as:
1. “Here is the first individual-level long-run paper on Sheppard-Towner,” plus
2. “I get wages but not education.”

That is a real contribution, but the introduction overstates how distinct the “health productivity channel” claim is. A null effect on schooling and a positive wage effect is suggestive, but as a matter of positioning, that is not yet a full new paradigm. It is better pitched as **evidence consistent with direct health-to-productivity effects** rather than as cleanly “identifying” a new channel.

### Is the contribution framed as a question about the world or a gap in the literature?
It is mixed, but too often framed as filling a literature gap:
- “first individual-level evidence”
- “existing literature has not examined”
- “aggregate state-level data cannot follow individuals”

That is useful, but the stronger framing is the world question:
**When prenatal medical access expands, do children become more productive adults even if they do not get more education?**

That is the question top journals want.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?
They could, but not crisply enough. Right now they might say:
> “It’s a historical DiD/DDD paper on Sheppard-Towner that finds small adult wage gains and no education effect.”

That is not enough. You want them to say:
> “It shows that a prenatal health program raised adult earnings without raising schooling, so the long-run return to early-life policy may operate through health, not just education.”

That is a much better takeaway, and the introduction should force that interpretation.

### What would make the contribution bigger?
Several possibilities:

1. **Stronger mechanism outcomes.**  
   The paper’s biggest ambition is the “health productivity channel,” but it measures that channel indirectly. The contribution would feel much bigger if it could show outcomes closer to physical capacity or health-dependent productivity:
   - disability or work limitation,
   - occupation physical intensity,
   - labor supply margins,
   - longevity/survival,
   - hospitalization or institutionalization if linkable,
   - military rejection/fitness if historical linkages exist.

2. **Treatment intensity rather than binary exposure.**  
   If the paper could use variation in program rollout intensity—clinics, nurse visits, county-level spending—it would become a richer paper about where prenatal health investments matter most, not just whether a state participated.

3. **Sharper connection to modern prenatal care.**  
   The paper currently gestures at Medicaid obstetric policy and Nurse-Family Partnership, but this feels bolted on. A more systematic comparison to modern maternal-health interventions would raise the paper from a nice economic history contribution to a broader public economics/health economics paper.

4. **A cleaner “who benefits and why” design.**  
   The rural and Black heterogeneity is promising and likely the most interesting part of the paper. If that became the organizing result—prenatal care matters most where baseline access is weakest—the paper’s contribution would feel larger and more policy-relevant.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
We know early-life conditions matter, and there is evidence that some early-life interventions improve later outcomes. But for many programs, it is unclear whether earnings gains come from more schooling, better health, or both.

### Tension
The Sheppard-Towner Act is historically important and has been shown to affect infant mortality, but we do not know whether its prenatal/infant health investments produced economically meaningful adult returns. Moreover, if they did, through what channel did those returns operate?

### Resolution
The paper finds positive adult wage effects for exposed cohorts, especially in rural and Black populations, but little evidence of increased schooling or occupational upgrading.

### Implications
Prenatal health investment may generate long-run economic returns via direct productivity effects, especially where baseline medical access is weak. That matters for both the economics of early-life investment and the targeting of maternal health policy.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
It has the ingredients, but not a clean arc. At present it feels like:
- a strong abstract,
- a somewhat overwritten introduction,
- a large historical setup,
- then results,
- then an ambitious mechanism claim.

The narrative is there, but it is not disciplined enough. The story should not be:
> “Here is a fascinating forgotten program, here are lots of results, and by the way this changes how we understand the safety net.”

It should be:
> “Prenatal health may matter for adult productivity even without increasing schooling. Sheppard-Towner lets us test that proposition in a historically important setting.”

That is a tighter story.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?
“America’s first maternal-health program appears to have raised adult earnings, but not education.”

That is the hook.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
They would lean in briefly. This is not an automatic blockbuster, but it is definitely a conversation starter because it goes to a big question in early-life economics: **what exactly is the mechanism behind long-run returns?**

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Probably one of these:
- “So is this really evidence on prenatal health rather than some other cohort-state difference?”
- “How do you know the wage effect is not selection/composition rather than productivity?”
- “What makes this different from the rest of the fetal-origins literature?”

As an editor, that tells you something important: the paper’s **most interesting margin is mechanism**, and the paper should organize around that, not around the historical romance of visiting nurses.

### If the findings are modest, is that okay?
Yes, but only if the paper makes the case that the modest average effect is conceptually important. The paper partially does this by emphasizing low cost and concentration among underserved groups. That is smart.

Still, the current manuscript sometimes oversells a small average effect as if it were a major reinterpretation of the American safety net. A better move is:
- modest pooled average effect,
- much larger effects where care was genuinely novel,
- implication: returns are highly context-dependent and largest where baseline access is low.

That makes the modest overall magnitude intellectually interesting rather than disappointing.

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

### What would make it read better?

   The prose is energetic, but too often sounds like a magazine essay. Phrases like “farmhouse door,” “Tennessee hollow,” “Mississippi delta farm,” “forgotten first safety net,” and “reshaping how we understand” create heat without always adding analytical clarity. AER papers can be readable, but they need discipline.

2. **Get to the main result faster.**  
   The reader should know by page 2:
   - what the question is,
   - why it matters,
   - what the key result is,
   - and why it changes what we know.

3. **Compress the institutional background.**  
   The historical background is useful, but it is too long relative to the paper’s central economic contribution. The politics of repeal and Progressive Era activism can be cut substantially or moved to an appendix/background subsection.

4. **Elevate the heterogeneity.**  
   The rural and Black heterogeneity is arguably more interesting than some of the pooled estimates and robustness material. If the paper’s policy punchline is that returns are largest where access is weakest, then that heterogeneity belongs nearer the center of the paper’s framing.

5. **Scale back the formal “conceptual framework.”**  
   The framework as written is very light relative to the space it takes. “Wages depend on human capital and health capital” is true but not a real model. Either make it genuinely useful or condense it heavily. In current form, it reads like scaffolding for a claim the data only partially support.

6. **Trim robustness discussion from the introduction.**  
   The introduction spends too much time previewing placebo tests, border-state restrictions, and collinearity. That belongs later. The intro should sell the question and the answer.

7. **Rewrite the conclusion to add insight, not just rhetoric.**  
   The conclusion currently repeats the paper’s claims in elevated language. It would be more effective if it ended with two or three crisp implications:
   - prenatal care can have labor-market returns,
   - those returns need not run through schooling,
   - targeting matters because effects are largest where access is scarce.

### Are there buried results that should be in the main text?
Yes: the heterogeneity is the most strategically useful part of the paper and should be more central to the narrative, not treated as a later supporting mechanism section.

### Is the conclusion adding value?
Some, but too much of it is summary plus flourish. The last two pages could be cut by a third with no loss.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

### What is the gap?
Mainly **framing and ambition**, with a secondary **scope** issue.

- **Framing problem:** The paper is closer to an AER conversation than it realizes, but it keeps framing itself as a historical rescue mission for Sheppard-Towner.
- **Ambition problem:** It wants to claim a big mechanism result, but the evidence for mechanism is indirect.
- **Scope problem:** To make the claim feel field-defining rather than niche, the paper needs either richer mechanism evidence or a sharper general lesson about when prenatal care matters most.

### Is it a novelty problem?
Not exactly. The setting is novel enough, and the linked-census evidence is meaningful. The novelty problem is more about **incrementalism of insight**: many papers already show early-life health matters. So the burden is to show why this setting teaches us something genuinely new. Right now the answer is “because wages move and schooling doesn’t,” which is interesting but not yet enough by itself for AER-level excitement.

### Is it an ambition problem?
Yes. The paper is competent, and sometimes quite smart, but the intellectual ambition is split awkwardly between:
- a narrow historical contribution, and
- a broad mechanism claim.

It needs to choose and sharpen:
**This is a paper about the direct productivity returns to prenatal health investment.**

Then everything else should serve that.

### Single most impactful advice
**Reframe the paper around the big question—whether prenatal health investments raise adult earnings through direct productivity rather than schooling—and make the historical setting a vehicle for that question, not the headline contribution itself.**

If the author changes only one thing, it should be that.
