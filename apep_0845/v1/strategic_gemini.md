## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper evaluates the 2013 EU Professional Qualifications Directive, a major reform designed to reduce labor market barriers by streamlining the recognition of foreign credentials. Using a difference-in-differences design across 24 countries, the authors find that the reform failed to reduce the "overqualification gap" for mobile professionals. This suggests that administrative "red tape" is not the primary friction in international labor markets, pointing instead to deeper cultural and linguistic barriers.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this well in the abstract, but the first two paragraphs of the introduction focus heavily on the institutional "red tape" narrative. While the "German nurse" anecdote is effective, the intro should more aggressively signal that this is a paper about the **limits of administrative policy** in the face of **informal market frictions**.

**The pitch the paper should have:** "When does cutting red tape actually integrate a market? We study the world's most ambitious attempt to harmonize professional credentials—the 2013 EU Directive—and find it had zero impact on the tendency of foreign professionals to work in low-skilled jobs. Our results suggest that for the top end of the labor market, 'paperwork' is a secondary friction compared to the unaddressed barriers of language and tacit cultural knowledge."

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution:** The paper provides the first causal evaluation of a flagship EU labor reform and demonstrates that administrative harmonization is insufficient to overcome the "thicker" frictions of international labor mobility.

- **Differentiation:** It moves beyond the standard "occupational licensing reduces supply" literature (e.g., Kleiner) by looking at *cross-border* recognition rather than *within-country* entry. It differentiates from the migration literature by focusing on the *quality* of the match (overqualification) rather than just the *volume* of flows.
- **Framing:** It is currently framed as answering a question about the **WORLD** (does this specific reform work?), but it gains AER-level strength when framed as a broader question about the **HIERARCHY OF FRICTIONS** in labor markets.
- **Clarity:** A smart economist would call this "The EU recognition null result paper." To avoid being seen as "just another DiD," the authors must lean into the "Illusion" part of their title—the idea that policymakers are pulling the wrong levers.
- **What would make it bigger?** Evidence on *why* it failed. Is it just that firms don't trust foreign degrees regardless of the "European Professional Card"? Data on employer-side sentiment or job-posting requirements would move this from a "policy evaluation" to a "mechanism paper."

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

- **Neighbors:** Kleiner & Krueger (2013) on licensing; Dustmann et al. (2012) on immigrant downgrading; Parey & Waldinger (2011) on information and mobility.
- **Positioning:** The paper should **attack** the "red tape" consensus. It should frame itself as a cautionary tale: the literature has spent so much time proving that licensing is a barrier that it has ignored the possibility that, in international contexts, removing the license barrier doesn't solve the matching problem.
- **Unaware of:** The paper could benefit from connecting to the **Search and Matching** literature. Overqualification is a matching failure. If the reform reduced search costs (administrative hurdles) but didn't change the matching technology (language/culture), the result is theoretically consistent.

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

- **Setup:** The EU is a single market, yet 1 in 3 foreign professionals are "under-employed" relative to their degrees.
- **Tension:** Policymakers blame "red tape" and pass a massive reform. If they are right, high-regulation countries should see the biggest improvements.
- **Resolution:** They don't. The gap persists, and foreign workers in regulated sectors trend exactly like those in unregulated sectors.
- **Implications:** Administrative reform is a "paper tiger." Market integration requires "thicker" interventions (language/networks).

**Evaluation:** The narrative arc is very strong. It is a "clean" story. The risk is that it feels like a "failed experiment" (the reform failed) rather than a "successful discovery" (we discovered why the reform failed).

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

**The Dinner Party Fact:** "The EU spent years creating a 'Professional Card' to help doctors and nurses move countries, and it had exactly zero effect on whether they actually got jobs at their skill level."

- **Response:** People would lean in because it confirms a cynical view of EU bureaucracy, but the "So what?" follow-up will be: "Was the reform actually implemented, or did countries just ignore it?"
- **The "Null" Problem:** The paper does a good job of showing the null is "precisely estimated" (ruling out effects of 2-4 pp), but it needs to fight harder to prove the null isn't just due to "bad implementation" (which is a boring institutional story) but due to "fundamental frictions" (which is an interesting economic story).

---

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

- **Front-loading:** Move the "Placebo test" (non-EU workers) earlier. It is the most convincing piece of evidence that the reform didn't work.
- **Appendix:** The "Standardized Effect Sizes" (Table 5) are useful for the "So What?" test and should be integrated into the main results to show the power of the study.
- **Conclusion:** It currently summarizes. It needs to expand on the *political economy*—why do we keep passing administrative reforms if they don't work? Is it "the recognition illusion" among voters or politicians?

---

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The gap is **Ambition**. Currently, it is a very high-quality policy evaluation. To be an AER paper, it needs to be a paper about the **Theory of Frictions**.

**The single most impactful change:** The authors need to provide a more rigorous exploration of the *alternative* frictions. If they can use LFS data to show that the null is even more pronounced for workers from different "language families" (e.g., a Romance speaker moving to a Germanic country), they can prove that the "thicker" frictions they hypothesize are actually the ones doing the work.

### Strategic Assessment

- **Current framing quality:** Adequate (Leans too much on policy, not enough on theory)
- **Contribution clarity:** Crystal clear
- **Literature positioning:** Well-positioned
- **Narrative arc:** Strong
- **AER distance:** Medium (Needs more on "Why" to move past the "Null result" stigma)
