## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple and important question: when the EU tried to make it easier for professionals to work across borders by streamlining recognition of foreign credentials, did highly educated migrants become less likely to work in jobs below their skill level? The paper’s headline answer is no: despite a major reform of professional qualification recognition, overqualification among foreign professionals did not fall more in the countries where the reform should have mattered most, suggesting that paperwork is not the main barrier to effective labor-market integration.

That is a question busy economists should care about because it sits at the intersection of migration, occupational licensing, and market integration. The EU is the closest thing the world has to a large-scale natural experiment in dismantling formal mobility barriers; if even there recognition reform does not move outcomes, that is informative about what frictions really matter.

### Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Reasonably, but not sharply enough. The current opening is vivid and competent, but it starts too quickly at “overqualification among mobile professionals” and then moves into policy detail before making the broader stakes unmistakable. It still reads somewhat like a well-executed policy evaluation rather than a paper about a deeper question: **how much do formal credential-recognition barriers actually impede skilled labor mobility?**

The first two paragraphs should do less institutional listing and more conceptual positioning.

### The pitch the paper should have

“Economists often argue that skilled migrants fail to match efficiently across borders because governments make foreign credentials hard to recognize. The European Union’s 2013 reform of professional qualifications created a rare test of that view: if administrative recognition barriers are central, then reducing them should improve occupational matching for mobile professionals, especially in countries with more heavily regulated professions.

We study whether that happened. Using cross-country variation in the scope of regulation across EU member states, we show that the reform did not reduce overqualification among foreign professionals. The result suggests that even ambitious administrative harmonization may do little when deeper frictions—language, employer beliefs, professional networks, and imperfect implementation—remain binding.”

That is the AER-facing version. It starts with a world question, then uses the EU reform as a test.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### Contribution in one sentence

The paper’s contribution is to show that a major EU reform designed to ease cross-border recognition of professional qualifications did not measurably reduce the overqualification of foreign professionals, implying that formal recognition barriers alone are not the main constraint on occupational matching across borders.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?

Not yet clearly enough. The paper says it is the first causal evidence on the 2013 Directive, which may be true, but “first paper on this directive” is not by itself an AER contribution. The reader needs to understand how this differs from:

1. occupational licensing papers showing barriers matter within countries,
2. migration papers showing information/networks/language matter,
3. EU integration papers on labor mobility more broadly,
4. descriptive work on migrant overqualification.

Right now, the paper differentiates itself mainly by policy object rather than by intellectual question. That is too thin. The differentiation should be: **existing work shows formal barriers matter within countries and that migrants are mismatched across countries; this paper tests whether removing one prominent formal barrier actually improves matching in an integrated labor market.**

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a gap in a literature?

It is mixed, but still leans too much toward “there is little causal evidence on this reform.” That is a literature-gap frame. The stronger world question is: **what prevents skilled workers from matching efficiently across borders—formal recognition barriers or thicker frictions?**

That world question is much stronger and more generalizable.

### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what’s new?

At the moment they could probably say: “It’s a DiD on an EU credential-recognition reform and finds no effect on overqualification.” That is clear enough as a design summary, but not yet as a memorable intellectual contribution. The danger is exactly that it sounds like “another DiD paper about policy X.”

The introduction needs to make the novelty sound like this instead: “This paper tests a core premise behind professional mobility policy—that making credential recognition easier improves skill matching—and finds that it does not.”

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Several possibilities:

- **A sharper outcome variable:** Overqualification is reasonable, but it is one step removed from actual cross-border mobility and professional practice. A bigger paper would ideally connect recognition reform to actual migration flows, profession-specific entry, licensing applications, occupational upgrading, or earnings.
- **A more direct mechanism distinction:** The paper currently jumps from null effect to “cultural and linguistic frictions dominate.” That is one plausible interpretation, but not the only one; poor implementation is another. A bigger contribution would cleanly distinguish “barriers don’t matter” from “barriers were not truly removed.”
- **A profession-level analysis:** Since the reform targeted some professions more concretely than others, a more granular comparison could create a much more compelling story. The current country-level treatment-intensity approach feels broad and somewhat indirect for the underlying mechanism.
- **A stronger framing around external validity:** The paper could become bigger if it more explicitly treated the EU as a test case for the broader economics of credential portability in an age of skilled migration.

If the authors could expand only conceptually, the biggest payoff would come from reframing the paper around **the limits of administrative harmonization in labor-market integration**, not around one directive.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

There is a persistent and economically significant fact: foreign-trained or foreign-born professionals are disproportionately overqualified for the jobs they hold. A common explanation is that administrative obstacles to recognizing foreign credentials block efficient matching.

### Tension

The EU attempted one of the world’s most ambitious reforms to exactly this problem. If administrative recognition barriers are central, this reform should have improved matching, especially where regulated professions are more prevalent. But it is unclear whether formal harmonization can overcome deeper cross-border frictions.

### Resolution

The paper finds no differential reduction in overqualification where the reform should have had the greatest bite. Outcomes for EU foreigners and non-EU foreigners move similarly, pointing away from a directive-specific effect.

### Implications

Administrative simplification alone may be insufficient to integrate skilled labor markets across borders. Policymakers may need to focus less on legal recognition per se and more on language, employer beliefs, networks, and actual implementation.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

Yes, but only in skeletal form. The ingredients are there; the paper is not a random collection of results. Still, the arc weakens in two ways:

1. The paper overstates the resolution. It wants to conclude that “cultural and linguistic frictions dominate,” but the paper itself also gives a second interpretation: poor implementation. Those are materially different stories. The narrative currently slides too quickly from “no reduced-form effect” to “therefore deep frictions dominate.”
2. The treatment of the null result is a little unstable. At moments it is presented as economically profound; at others the paper concedes the reform may not have been meaningfully implemented. That ambiguity needs to be made central rather than tucked into the limitations/discussion.

### If it is a collection of results looking for a story, what story should it be telling?

The best story is:

**The EU tried to solve skilled migrant mismatch by harmonizing credential recognition. It mostly failed to change labor-market outcomes. That failure is informative, but the central lesson is not necessarily that credentials do not matter—it is that formal harmonization does not automatically become functional integration.**

That is a stronger and more intellectually honest story than the current “recognition illusion” framing, which is punchy but a bit too eager to claim that bureaucracy is irrelevant.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?

“I’d lead with: the EU undertook a major reform to make professional credentials portable across member states, and it did not reduce overqualification among foreign professionals.”

That is a good opening fact.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

They would lean in—briefly. It is a genuinely interesting policy fact. But the second sentence matters enormously. If the presenter says, “we use a continuous-treatment DiD with number of regulated professions,” attention may fade. If instead the presenter says, “this is a clean test of whether formal credential-recognition barriers are actually what keep skilled migrants from matching well,” attention stays.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Almost certainly: **“Does this mean recognition barriers don’t matter, or that the reform never really changed recognition in practice?”**

That is the key follow-up question, and the paper in its current form does not fully own that this is the natural central challenge. It acknowledges it later, but this distinction should be elevated much earlier because it determines what the result means.

### If the findings are null or modest: is the null itself interesting?

Yes, potentially very much so. But only if the paper makes a disciplined case for why this null is informative.

Right now the null is interesting because:
- the policy was salient and ambitious,
- the underlying economic stakes are large,
- the reform targets a widely cited friction,
- and the setting has broader relevance.

However, the paper risks feeling like a failed experiment if the reader comes away thinking: “Maybe the treatment wasn’t intense where it mattered, maybe take-up was low, maybe implementation was partial, and overqualification is too indirect.” To avoid that, the paper must frame the null as evidence on the **limits of headline legal reform as implemented in practice**. That is still valuable and credible.

In other words, the null can carry an AER-level story only if the paper stops overselling what exact mechanism the null proves.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The main gap is **not primarily technical** from an editorial-positioning perspective. It is a combination of **framing, scope, and ambition**.

### Is it a framing problem?

Yes, strongly. The paper has a potentially publishable idea but frames it too much as “first causal evidence on an EU directive” and too little as “a test of whether formal credential recognition barriers are the binding friction in skilled migration.”

### Is it a scope problem?

Yes. Country-level overqualification is a useful first pass, but by itself it may be too indirect and too aggregated for a top general-interest journal. The question is big; the evidence package is relatively narrow. To feel AER-sized, the paper likely needs either:
- more direct outcomes,
- more granular treatment variation,
- or more convincing mechanism separation.

### Is it a novelty problem?

Partly. The specific policy is novel enough. But the broader answer—“administrative reform did not solve migrant mismatch”—is not yet sufficiently differentiated from the large literature emphasizing language, networks, and other thick frictions. The novelty has to come from the **test**, not just the **conclusion**.

### Is it an ambition problem?

Yes. The paper is competent but safe. It evaluates one reform with one main outcome and then makes fairly broad claims. A stronger version would either:
- narrow the claims and become a sharp, credible policy-evaluation paper, or
- broaden the empirical scope to truly answer the larger question it wants to ask.

For AER, it probably needs the latter.

### The single most impactful piece of advice

**Reframe the paper around the broader question—whether formal credential-recognition reform translates into real labor-market integration—and make the core contribution the limits of legal harmonization as implemented, not just the null effect of one EU directive on overqualification.**

That one change would improve the introduction, literature positioning, interpretation, and perceived ambition all at once.
