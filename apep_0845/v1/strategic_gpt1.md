## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether the EU’s flagship 2013 reform of professional qualification recognition actually improved labor market matching for mobile professionals. Using cross-country variation in how heavily professions were regulated before the reform, it argues that simplifying recognition procedures did not reduce the persistent overqualification gap between foreign and native workers—suggesting that paperwork is not the main thing preventing skilled workers from using their skills across borders.

Why should a busy economist care? Because this is, in principle, a sharp test of a central premise in both labor and integration policy: if you remove formal barriers to mobility, do workers actually reallocate efficiently? The answer matters for how we think about licensing, migration frictions, and the real limits of market integration.

### Does the paper itself articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Not quite. The current opening is competent and readable, but it undersells the big question. It starts with anecdotes and descriptive motivation, then pivots quickly into directive details. The reader gets “there is a reform and we study it,” but not immediately enough “this is a test of a broader economic idea with implications beyond the EU.”

The first two paragraphs should more explicitly say:

1. There is a major economic puzzle: even in a supposedly integrated labor market with free movement, skilled migrants often work below their qualification level.  
2. A leading policy view says this happens because occupational recognition rules are too burdensome.  
3. The 2013 EU reform is an unusually clean, high-stakes test of that view.  
4. The answer is no: reducing administrative barriers alone did not meaningfully improve matching.

### The pitch the paper should have

Even inside the EU’s single market, highly educated mobile workers are often stuck in jobs below their skill level. A leading explanation is that cumbersome credential-recognition rules prevent skilled migrants from accessing the occupations they are trained for; the EU’s 2013 Professional Qualifications Directive was the bloc’s flagship attempt to fix exactly that problem.

This paper studies whether that reform actually improved occupational matching. Exploiting cross-country variation in how exposed countries were to the reform, we find that countries with more regulated professions did not experience larger declines in the overqualification gap for mobile professionals. The broader implication is that formal recognition barriers are not the whole story: deeper frictions—language, employer information, and professional specificity—appear to limit mobility even when administrative rules are streamlined.

That is the AER version of the paper. The current intro is close, but too institutional too early and too modest about the broader stakes.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper’s contribution is to show that the EU’s major 2013 credential-recognition reform did not materially reduce the overqualification of mobile professionals, challenging the view that administrative recognition barriers are a first-order determinant of cross-border skill mismatch.

### Is this clearly differentiated from the closest papers?

Only partially. The paper says it is the “first causal evidence” on the directive, which may be true, but that is not enough on its own. “First paper on policy X” is rarely a strong top-journal contribution unless policy X is itself central to a broad question economists care about. Right now the paper is differentiated institutionally, not conceptually.

The author needs to distinguish the contribution relative to at least three adjacent conversations:

- occupational licensing and labor supply within countries;
- migration frictions and immigrant downgrading/skill mismatch;
- EU integration and free movement.

At present, the paper gestures at all three, but it does not clearly tell the reader which one it is mainly advancing. That leaves the contribution feeling like “a DiD paper on an EU directive” rather than “a paper that changes how we think about what blocks labor mobility.”

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a literature gap?

It is mixed, but too often it drifts into literature-gap framing. “First causal evidence on the directive” is a literature-gap claim. The stronger world-question framing is:

- What actually prevents skilled workers from moving into jobs that match their qualifications across borders?
- Do formal recognition barriers meaningfully constrain labor market integration?
- How much can administrative harmonization accomplish without deeper social and informational integration?

That framing is much stronger and should dominate.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?

A smart economist could probably say: “It studies an EU reform to credential recognition and finds no effect on overqualification.” That is something. But they could also easily summarize it as “another policy DiD with a null result.” That is the risk. The introduction does not yet make the reader feel that the null is a substantive positive result about the world.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Several possibilities:

1. **Sharper outcome framing.** Overqualification is meaningful, but somewhat indirect. The paper would feel bigger if it could connect more explicitly to occupational access, profession-specific employment, wages, or cross-border mobility flows. Even descriptively, showing whether the reform failed to change migration, profession entry, or occupational upgrading would make the contribution feel more consequential.

2. **Mechanism through heterogeneity.** If the null is driven by language/cultural frictions, show that the null is weaker in places where those frictions should be lower: linguistically proximate countries, border regions, professions with standardized tasks, or occupations included in the European Professional Card. That would transform a null into a more persuasive substantive claim.

3. **A more ambitious comparative framing.** The paper could frame the EU as a “most likely case.” If even the world’s most developed supranational labor market cannot make skilled mobility work through administrative harmonization alone, that is a general lesson. Right now that point is implied but not forcefully made.

4. **Tie to broader economics of state capacity versus thick frictions.** The deeper claim here is not just about the EU. It is about when reducing formal barriers fails because informal barriers remain binding. That is a bigger idea.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

The world before this paper: the EU has free movement, but highly educated foreign professionals remain heavily overqualified. Policymakers believe this mismatch reflects administrative barriers in credential recognition.

### Tension

The tension is strong and potentially excellent: if paperwork and regulatory fragmentation are the main barriers, then a major harmonization reform in the world’s deepest integrated labor market should improve matching. If it does not, then either the reform did not really bite or economists/policymakers have been overestimating the importance of formal barriers relative to deeper frictions.

### Resolution

The paper finds no meaningful differential reduction in overqualification in more exposed countries, and similar patterns for EU and non-EU foreigners.

### Implications

Administrative harmonization alone does not meaningfully unlock skilled labor mobility. Barriers to integration appear to be thicker—language, tacit knowledge, employer beliefs, networks, and implementation failures.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the raw ingredients, but the arc is still weaker than it should be. Right now the paper sometimes reads like:

- here is the policy,
- here is the design,
- here is the null,
- here are robustness checks,
- here is an interpretation.

That is a results package, not a fully persuasive story.

### What story should it be telling?

The story should be:

1. **Europe created the closest thing we have to an integrated market for skilled labor.**
2. **Yet skilled migrants remain mismatched.**
3. **The canonical bureaucratic explanation received a major policy test.**
4. **That test fails.**
5. **Therefore, the main barriers to efficient matching are not just formal recognition rules.**
6. **This changes how we should think about mobility policy: paperwork reform is too thin a tool for thick borders.**

That is the narrative. The paper is near it, but not disciplined enough around it.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?

I would lead with:

**The EU spent a decade reforming professional credential recognition, and even in the countries where the reform should have mattered most, skilled foreign workers did not become less overqualified.**

That is the memorable fact.

### Would people lean in?

Moderately yes—if presented that way. Economists care about migration, licensing, and whether institutions actually move behavior. But they will only lean in if the paper emphasizes the broader lesson, not the directive minutiae.

If the pitch is “we study Directive 2013/55/EU using continuous treatment intensity,” they will reach for their phones.

If the pitch is “the flagship attempt to remove credential barriers in the EU failed to improve skill matching,” they will lean in.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Almost certainly:

**“So does this mean recognition barriers don’t matter, or that the reform wasn’t actually implemented?”**

That is the paper’s central vulnerability and also its central opportunity. The current draft acknowledges both interpretations, but too late. It should surface this tension much earlier and frame the contribution as narrowing the set of plausible stories rather than pretending to fully resolve them.

### Is the null result itself interesting?

Yes, potentially very interesting. But only if the paper insists that this was a consequential, high-stakes, “most likely case” policy test. A null in a small, obscure setting is forgettable; a null in the EU single market’s flagship mobility reform is informative.

Right now the paper does make some case for that, but not forcefully enough. It needs to say more clearly:

- this was the marquee institutional fix for a widely diagnosed problem;
- if administrative barriers were first-order, this is where we should have seen movement;
- failure here meaningfully updates beliefs.

Without that, the null can feel like a failed experiment. With that, it is a substantive result.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The gap is mostly a **framing and ambition problem**, with some **scope** concerns.

This is not obviously a bad paper. It is a respectable, coherent paper on an important policy. But AER papers usually do one of three things:

- answer a first-order question with unusual clarity,
- shift how a field thinks about a mechanism,
- or open a broad new empirical domain.

This paper is not there yet because it still feels like a careful policy evaluation rather than a paper that changes a larger conversation.

### What is the main gap?

**Framing problem first, ambition problem second.**

The paper has a potentially broad result but packages it as a narrow directive study. It should instead be a paper about the limits of formal market integration and the distinction between legal access and effective labor market access.

### Is there also a novelty problem?

Somewhat. Null results on administrative reforms are not inherently novel. The novelty has to come from the setting being a particularly important test case and from the conceptual claim being broader than the setting. The draft has not yet fully earned that.

### Is there a scope problem?

Yes. The paper asks a broad question—what moves workers?—but has a relatively narrow outcome: country-level overqualification gaps. That outcome is sensible, but on its own it may not be enough to support the paper’s broadest claims. The paper would be more AER-ready if it either:
- added more direct outcomes tied to mobility and occupational access, or
- narrowed the rhetoric and became a cleaner, sharper paper about one important margin.

Right now it wants broad implications on somewhat limited evidence.

### Single most impactful advice

**Reframe the paper as a test of whether removing formal recognition barriers is sufficient for skilled labor market integration, and organize the entire introduction and discussion around that big question rather than around the directive itself.**

That one change would do the most to elevate the paper.
