## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
This paper asks whether safety is a form of site-specific human capital. Using a unique dataset of over 200,000 mining injuries that separately tracks a worker’s total industry experience and their site-specific tenure, it finds that while site-specific knowledge doesn't make accidents less severe, it is a primary driver of preventing them altogether. This suggests that policies or market forces that increase worker turnover carry a "hidden" safety cost by resetting the site-specific tenure clock.

**Evaluation:** The paper’s current pitch is buried in descriptive data details. The first two paragraphs focus heavily on the MSHA Form 7000-1 and the "triple decomposition." While the data is a great asset, the *economic* pitch should lead with the "Tenure Shield" concept.

**The Pitch the paper should have:**
"Does safety knowledge travel with workers, or is it embedded in the local environment? We decompose the returns to experience into general industry knowledge and site-specific human capital to show that a veteran miner at a new site is as injury-prone as a novice. Our findings suggest that site-specific 'tenure shields' are a first-order determinant of workplace safety, implying that the costs of worker turnover include significant unpriced externalities in the form of increased accident risk."

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**Contribution:** The paper identifies that the returns to experience in workplace safety are predominantly site-specific rather than general, operating primarily on the extensive margin (prevention) rather than the intensive margin (mitigation).

- **Differentiation:** It moves beyond Viscusi (1993) or Lavetti (2023) by exploiting a rare data feature (three types of tenure) to solve a classic identification problem in the human capital literature: distinguishing between "good workers" and "workers who know the terrain."
- **World vs. Literature:** Currently, it leans toward a "filling a gap" framing (the "first decomposition..."). It should frame itself as answering: "Why do some mines kill people while others don't?"
- **"So What?":** A smart economist would say: "It’s the paper that proves why you shouldn't just hire experience; you have to keep it."
- **Bigger Contribution:** To make this an AER "must-read," the author needs to lean harder into the **policy externality**. If a safety inspection causes a mine to close and workers to move, does the regulator unintentionally increase the total number of deaths in the industry because those workers lose their "tenure shield"?

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
The paper sits at the intersection of **Labor Economics (Human Capital)** and **Industrial Organization (Safety/Regulation).**

- **Neighbors:** Becker (1962) on firm-specific capital; Kambourov & Manovskii (2009) on occupational specificity; and Garin et al. (2023) on mining safety.
- **Positioning:** It should **synthesize** the human capital literature with the safety literature. It currently treats them as separate "contributions." It should argue that safety is a *primary* return to firm-specific capital that has been overlooked because productivity is easier to measure than the absence of an accident.
- **Unexpected Connection:** The paper could speak to the **Management Economics** literature (Bloom/Van Reenen). Is site-specific safety knowledge a form of "managerial capital" or "organizational memory"?

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC
- **Setup:** Mining is dangerous; we assume experienced workers are safer.
- **Tension:** But experience is often just a proxy for tenure. If you move a 20-year veteran to a new mine, does that safety vanish?
- **Resolution:** Yes. The "shield" is site-specific. Interestingly, it doesn't make the accidents less painful (physics wins), it just stops them from happening (knowledge wins).
- **Implications:** Turnover is a public health hazard.

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong but the "Experience Paradox" (experienced workers having more severe accidents) feels like a distracting side-quest. The author should frame this more clearly as "Selection into Danger": experts are given the hardest jobs, which is why their accidents look worse.

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
At a dinner party, the lead fact is: **"A miner with 15 years of experience is as likely to get injured on day one at a new site as a teenager who has never seen a mine."**

That is a "lean-in" fact. The follow-up is: "Does this mean companies should pay 'tenure bonuses' purely for safety?" This leads to a rich discussion on the Social Cost of Carbon/Mining and labor market frictions.

---

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
- **Move to Appendix:** The "Standardized Effect Sizes" (Table 6) is a bit pedantic for the main text.
- **Front-load:** The "Asymmetry" results (extensive vs. intensive margin) are the most interesting part. They should be emphasized earlier in the results section.
- **Discussion:** Section 6 needs to be more ambitious. It currently sounds like a "limitations" section. It should be a "Policy Implications" section that calculates the dollar cost of a worker transfer in terms of expected injury risk.

---

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The gap is **ambition in the counterfactual.**

The science is solid, but it feels a bit like a "very good labor paper" rather than an "AER paper." To bridge the gap, the author should use their estimates to perform a **back-of-the-envelope welfare calculation.** 

**Single most impactful advice:** Quantify the "Safety Externality" of turnover. If the industry reduced turnover by 10%, how many lives would be saved, and what is the dollar value of that "Tenure Shield" in terms of VSL (Value of a Statistical Life)? This moves the paper from a descriptive decomposition to a fundamental statement on the costs of labor mobility.
