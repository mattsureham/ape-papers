## 1. Idea Fidelity

The paper captures part of the original idea, but only part. It uses the distinctive MSHA accident data with the three experience measures and does attempt the proposed decomposition of total, mine-specific, and job-specific experience. That is faithful to the manifest.

However, two important elements of the original design are either dropped or substantially altered. First, the mine-level “arrival cohort” design is not implemented correctly: the paper measures the “new-arrival share” using the share of **injured workers** with low tenure, not the share of **all workers/employees** with low tenure. That is not a workforce-composition measure; it is an accident-composition measure, and it is mechanically related to the outcome. Second, the inspection-induced turnover IV discussed in the manifest is not implemented at all. Since the paper’s policy discussion leans heavily on turnover costs induced by enforcement, the absence of the IV matters.

So the paper pursues the broad research question, but it misses a key identification component and, in the mine-level analysis, departs from the intended object in a way that undermines the central result.

## 2. Summary

This paper asks whether mine-specific tenure provides safety benefits beyond general mining experience, using MSHA injury records that report total mining experience, mine tenure, and job tenure for injured workers. The most credible finding is actually a negative one: conditional on an injury occurring, mine-specific tenure has at most small effects on injury severity. The headline mine-level result—that a higher share of new arrivals sharply raises injury rates—is currently not persuasive because the key regressor is constructed from injured workers rather than the workforce at risk.

## 3. Essential Points

1. **The mine-level specification is not identified as written.**  
   The “new arrival fraction” is defined as the share of **injured workers** in a mine-year with tenure below one year, and then used to explain the mine-year injury rate. This is fundamentally problematic. It conditions the regressor on realized injuries, so the regressor is partly an outcome of the accident process itself. If new workers are more injury-prone, their share among the injured will mechanically rise when the injury rate rises. This is not a valid measure of turnover or workforce composition. The central extensive-margin result therefore cannot support the paper’s policy claims in its current form.

2. **Several magnitudes are misstated or implausibly interpreted.**  
   The paper alternates between a coefficient of roughly 88–105 for a **one-unit** increase in the new-arrival share and a claim that a **one-percentage-point** increase raises injuries by 88–105 per 200,000 hours. Those are off by a factor of 100. The later interpretation (a 10 percentage point increase implies about 8.8–10.5 more injuries per 200,000 hours) is the coherent one. This is not a minor wording issue; it affects the reader’s assessment of economic significance. More broadly, some interpretations overreach relative to the estimates: the individual-level results mostly say mine tenure does little for severity conditional on injury, not that “the tenure shield” is established.

3. **The paper’s main conclusion outruns what the data can identify.**  
   All individual-level regressions are conditional on an injury having occurred, so they speak to severity selection, not injury risk. The paper repeatedly infers accident prevention from the combination of null severity effects and the mine-level composition regression, but that second piece is not credible in its current form. Without a valid risk design, the paper does not yet show that mine-specific tenure “prevents accidents.” At present, the strongest defensible conclusion is narrower: among reported injuries, mine-specific tenure has limited association with severity after conditioning on mine, occupation, and time.

## 4. Suggestions

The paper has a genuinely interesting data asset and could become a useful short paper, but it needs a sharper design and a more disciplined interpretation.

First, I would strongly recommend reframing the paper around the **individual-level severity decomposition**, which is the part most directly supported by the observed data. The triple-tenure information is indeed unusual and worth exploiting. But the paper should be candid that severity conditional on injury is a selected sample. That does not make the exercise uninformative; it just means the estimand is narrower. A cleaner title and abstract would help: right now the abstract leads with a strong risk claim that the evidence does not support.

Second, if the authors want to retain a mine-level injury-risk analysis, they need a true denominator. The relevant object is the share of **all workers or hours** at the mine accounted for by low-tenure workers. As written, MSHA production data appear to provide only total employment and hours, not tenure composition. If workforce composition cannot be observed directly, then the paper should not present the current accident-composition measure as if it were turnover. One possible salvage strategy is to use lagged accident reports to proxy recent inflows, but that would still need to be sold carefully as a noisy proxy, not “the share of new arrivals.” Another option is to narrow the claim to “injuries are disproportionately borne by recent arrivals,” which the accident data can actually show more directly.

Third, the paper should revisit the **economics of the magnitudes** throughout. The mine-level coefficient of around 100 per 200,000 hours for a full-unit shift in the arrival share is large but not absurd if baseline injury rates are on the order of tens per 200,000 hours; however, the paper needs to report baseline means for the mine-year injury rate so the reader can judge this. Is a 10 percentage point increase in new arrivals associated with a 20 percent rise in injuries? a doubling? Without the baseline, the magnitude is hard to interpret. Likewise, for the individual results, effects like -0.0007 on log(days away + 1) per year of mine tenure are tiny. The paper should embrace that fact rather than trying to rhetorically elevate it. A concise statement such as “conditional on injury, an additional year of mine tenure changes days-away severity by less than one-tenth of one percent” would be clearer and more credible.

Fourth, I would be more careful with the **standard errors and dependence structure**. Clustering at the mine level is the minimum reasonable choice, but with long mine panels and aggregate mine-year outcomes, serial correlation is likely important. Mine clustering handles that in principle, but the paper should tell the reader the number of clusters in each specification and whether any results are sensitive to wild-cluster bootstrap inference or to collapsing time more coarsely. For individual-level regressions with year fixed effects and many observations per mine-year, one might also consider clustering at the mine level as acceptable, but I would want reassurance that significance is not being driven by a few large mines. Showing weighted and unweighted versions, or influence diagnostics by mine, would help.

Fifth, the **positive coefficient on total mining experience** is interesting but needs a more sober treatment. The current “experience paradox” discussion is plausible, but entirely speculative. There are several alternative mechanisms: older workers may be assigned more dangerous tasks; experienced workers may report serious injuries more reliably; severity may vary by occupation rank in ways not absorbed by coarse occupation controls; or there may be nonlinear age/experience effects. Since age is likely unavailable, I would suggest at least richer controls: interact occupation with mine type or commodity, allow flexible polynomials in all three tenure variables, and report binscatter-style plots of residualized severity against each experience measure. If the positive total-experience slope survives, present it as a descriptive selected-sample fact, not as evidence for a specific behavioral model.

Sixth, the fixed-effects structure deserves clarification. The text says the design compares workers “at the same mine, in the same year, with the same occupation,” but the regression includes separate mine FE, year FE, occupation FE, and subunit FE—not mine-by-year or mine-by-occupation effects. That is a much weaker comparison than the prose suggests. If computationally feasible, I would prefer mine-by-year fixed effects in the individual severity regressions. That would absorb time-varying mine conditions, safety investments, and local shocks. Even better would be mine-by-year-by-subunit or mine-by-year plus occupation FE, depending on support. If the coefficient on mine-specific tenure remains small under more saturated FE, that would strengthen the paper considerably.

Seventh, the nonlinear tenure result should be treated cautiously. An inverted-U with severity peaking at 12.5 years of mine tenure is not an intuitive benchmark, and the proposed explanation—that workers with moderate tenure take on more complex tasks—sounds post hoc. Before emphasizing this pattern, I would want to see whether it is robust to trimming extreme tenure values, replacing a quadratic with tenure bins, and controlling more flexibly for total experience and job tenure. Given the collinearity among the three experience measures, the quadratic pattern could be fragile.

Eighth, the heterogeneity section currently does not add much because the key coefficients are mostly imprecise. Rather than reporting several subgroup tables with weak signal, I would focus heterogeneity on one or two ex ante predictions where the data are informative. For example: underground vs. surface, or coal vs. metal/nonmetal, but with interaction terms in the pooled sample and reported p-values for differences. The current text tends to infer too much from sign differences without formal tests.

Ninth, the sample selection issue from missing tenure fields deserves more attention. Eighteen percent of accidents are dropped due to missing experience information, “primarily in early years.” That could matter if reporting quality is correlated with mine safety or injury severity. A simple table comparing included and excluded records on observable outcomes, mine type, year, and severity would be useful. At a minimum, reweighting or a specification on a balanced later-period sample could reassure readers that missingness is not driving results.

Finally, I would align the contribution and conclusion with what the paper can truly deliver in AER: Insights format. The strongest concise paper here may be: **MSHA’s unique tenure measures reveal that mine-specific tenure explains little of injury severity conditional on accident once general experience, job tenure, mine, and occupation are controlled for; therefore the safety return to specific human capital, if it exists, is more likely to operate through accident incidence than severity.** That is a modest but clear result. To claim more, the authors need a valid extensive-margin design—ideally the workforce-based arrival-cohort measure from the manifest, and ideally the inspection-based IV as well. Without that, the current headline result is not ready.
