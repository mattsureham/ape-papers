**Idea Fidelity**

The submitted paper largely adheres to the original idea outlined in the manifest. It focuses on the effect of EU enlargement–driven posted worker inflows on far‑right voting in French départements, exploits the sectoral concentration of posted workers in construction and agriculture, relies on Bartik-style exposure, and uses department-level presidential election data. The paper also draws on the same data sources (DARES posted worker declarations, Eurostat employment shares, data.gouv.fr election results) and emphasizes the non‑tradable sector channel. However, two aspects in the manifest are underdeveloped. First, the manifesto emphasizes the quasi‑exogenous shock from the 2004/2007 enlargement dates, while the paper mainly operationalizes the shock through a binary post‑2007 indicator interacted with pre‑enlargement exposure; the variation from the 2004 shock is not explicitly separated, nor is the 2007 timing exploited beyond the “Post” dummy. Second, the manifest mentions a Bartik design interacting pre‑enlargement shares with national posted worker inflows; the paper does present such an instrument, but the main reported coefficient comes from the simpler interaction model rather than an IV or dynamic version of the Bartik. These departures do not undermine the core research question, but the manuscript could do more to foreground the original design.

**Summary**

The paper examines whether the surge in EU posted workers following the 2004 and 2007 enlargements increased support for the French far right. Using département-level differences in pre-enlargement employment in posted-worker-heavy sectors (construction and agriculture), the author interacts that exposure with a post-enlargement indicator in a diff‑in‑diff framework and reports that higher exposure predicts substantively large increases in Front National/Rassemblement National vote share. Robustness exercises with sectoral placebos, China import controls, and event studies are reported to bolster the causal claim.

**Essential Points**

1. **Pre-existing Trend Threat:** The event-study results reveal a strong, significant differential trend prior to enlargement, with high-exposure départements already evolving toward higher FN support before 2004. This undermines the parallel trends assumption underlying the diff-in-diff. The paper acknowledges the trend but does not convincingly show that the post-2004 shift reflects a structural break attributable to posted workers rather than the continuation of the prior convergence. The authors need to (a) report more flexible pre-trend tests (e.g., allowing for linear trends interacted with exposure), (b) explore whether any other shocks coincide with the “Post” period, and (c) ideally demonstrate that adjusting for pre-trends (e.g., including exposure-specific trends) leaves the main effect intact.

2. **Interpretation of the Bartik and Timing:** The Bartik shift-share instrument is introduced but not fully leveraged. Column (2) suggests the Bartik instrument loses power once fixed effects are included, raising doubts about using it to identify the causal effect. Meanwhile, the main specification relies on an interaction with a post-2007 indicator but does not exploit the actual timing of the A8 (2004) versus A2 (2007) shocks. This leaves the identification relying on a single post-treatment period and makes it difficult to separate enlargement effects from other secular changes. The authors should clarify whether the shock is genuinely exogenous and time-specific, and consider using the actual dynamics of posted worker inflows (e.g., a continuous Bartik term varying with year-by-year national shocks) rather than a coarse post dummy.

3. **Mechanism and Alternative Channels:** While the paper argues that non-tradable sector competition explains the results and provides a manufacturing placebo, it does not fully rule out alternative mechanisms associated with construction/agriculture regions—such as demographic change, local industrial decline, or concurrent policy shifts—that could drive both exposure and voting. The null for manufacturing is reassuring but does not guarantee exclusion. The authors should provide additional evidence (e.g., controlling for other local trends like urbanization, education, or housing demand; or exploiting heterogeneity in the timing of enlargement exposure) to strengthen the claim that posted workers, rather than correlated factors, drive the result.

If the authors do not address these issues convincingly, the paper risks overstating causal inference and should not be accepted in its current form.

**Suggestions**

1. **Strengthen Parallel Trends Strategy:** Expand the pre-trend analysis beyond the 1995 coefficient. For example, include interacted linear or quadratic trends in exposure, or run a leads-and-lags event study with more granular year indicators (e.g., using municipal election years or other institutional breakpoints) to assess whether the divergence around 2004 is abrupt. Including exposure-specific year slopes could reveal whether the post-2004 jump is statistically distinguishable from the pre-existing slope. If necessary, re-center the baseline period (e.g., 1995–2002) and test alternative estimators (e.g., Callaway & Sant’Anna, 2021-style DID) that are robust to differential pre-trends.

2. **Use Actual Posted Worker Inflows as a Continuous Shock:** Instead of relying primarily on a binary post indicator, incorporate the annual national posted worker inflow series into the interaction (Exposure × Annual ΔPosted Workers). This would allow the treatment to vary with the intensity of the supply shock over time, better reflecting the manifest’s Bartik design and teasing out whether FN vote share responds contemporaneously to posted worker inflows. It would also create variation between 2004 and 2007: if the shock is smaller in 2004 but larger in 2007, the continuous version should capture that and potentially improve identification.

3. **Clarify the Bartik Specification and Feasibility:** The paper should detail the construction of the Bartik instrument and explain why it becomes uninformative once fixed effects are included. Consider reporting the first-stage relationship (if an IV approach is intended), or explicitly stating that the Bartik is used descriptively rather than as an instrument. If feasible, present a version of the main regression using the Bartik term as the regressor (rather than the simple interaction) with appropriate standard errors and discuss the assumptions under which it identifies a causal effect.

4. **Control for Additional Confounders:** Add observable département-level covariates that could confound the relationship, such as education levels, share of small towns, median age, or housing market indicators. These controls could be included as interactions with time to capture diverging trends. Providing a table showing how main estimates change with richer controls would reassure readers that the posted worker channel is not proxying for persistent structural differences. Including time-varying economic controls (wage growth, public investment) would also help separate the posted worker effect from other macroeconomic developments.

5. **Disentangle Sectoral Pathways:** The sector-specific results (construction vs. agriculture) are a useful start, but the paper could go further by examining whether posted worker exposure in one sector matters more when that sector experienced larger absolute inflows. For instance, weight the sectoral employment shares by the national posted worker concentration per sector to reflect actual exposure intensity. Alternatively, exploit data on posted workers’ geographical distribution (if available) or firm-level postings to validate that the construction/agriculture shares do indeed capture exposure. If sector-specific data on posted worker occupations exists, consider interacting those instead of broad NACE categories.

6. **Assess Vote Share Dynamics Beyond Presidential Elections:** The current outcome is limited to presidential first-round vote share. If data permits, examine other political outcomes (e.g., European elections, legislative votes, or abstention rates) to see if the effect generalizes. That would also help alleviate concerns that the observed effect is specific to the FN’s presidential campaigns. Additionally, checking whether the posted worker exposure predicts RN performance in other contexts (e.g., municipal elections) would strengthen the narrative about a sustained political backlash.

7. **Contextualize Magnitudes and Mechanism:** The discussion section hints at mechanisms like visibility and exit barriers. To substantiate these, the authors could look for correlates in the data—e.g., whether the posted worker effect is stronger in départements with lower labor mobility, higher share of blue-collar employment, or more visible posting activities (from DARES occupational data). Simple descriptive statistics (maps showing changes over time) could illustrate how posted worker concentrations map onto regions with rising far-right support.

8. **Address the Role of EU Policy Debate:** Since the paper touches on the Posted Workers Directive reforms, consider contextualizing the findings within the broader EU policy discourse. For example, discuss whether regions with higher exposure were also more active in the domestic debate on posted workers or whether French policy (e.g., enforcement or wage harmonization efforts) evolved differently across départements. Linking the empirical results to the policy relevance could make the contribution clearer for an AER: Insights audience.

Implementing these suggestions would clarify the identification strategy, enhance the credibility of the causal claims, and deepen the reader’s understanding of the services-trade channel through which posted workers might influence far-right support.
