## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether a specific form of European labor-market integration — the rise of EU “posted workers” in sectors like construction and agriculture — helped fuel far-right voting in France. The core idea is interesting because it points to a channel distinct from the now-standard “China shock” story: not import competition in tradables, but local labor competition in place-bound, non-tradable work.

Does the paper itself articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs? Mostly, but not sharply enough. The current opening is competent, but it reads like a setup for an identification exercise rather than a big economic question about how globalization enters politics. The paper’s real hook is not “another labor market shock and populism paper”; it is “what happens politically when cross-border competition arrives in jobs that cannot move?”

### The pitch the paper should have

A stronger first two paragraphs would say something like:

> Much of the political-economy literature on globalization focuses on import competition in tradable goods. But many workers experience integration through a different margin: foreign labor entering local, non-tradable sectors such as construction and agriculture. These workers cannot be “outsourced abroad”; they appear on local job sites, compete face-to-face with native workers, and may generate a distinct form of political backlash.
>
> This paper studies that margin using the sharp increase in posted workers after EU enlargement and asks whether French areas more exposed to posted-worker-intensive sectors saw larger increases in far-right support. The central contribution is to bring the politics of services integration into a literature dominated by goods trade and permanent migration.

That is the AER-relevant story. Right now the paper gets there, but too slowly and too defensively.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper argues that exposure to non-tradable-sector labor competition from EU posted workers is associated with greater far-right support in France, suggesting a political-economy channel of services integration distinct from goods-trade shocks.

### Evaluation

#### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3-4 papers?
Partially, but not yet convincingly. The paper says “this is different from China shock” and “this is different from immigration,” but the differentiation is still too coarse. A reader can easily come away with: “OK, this is another exposure-based paper linking economic shocks to right-wing voting.” To matter at AER level, the paper needs to draw a sharper line between:
1. import competition in tradables,
2. permanent immigration,
3. local labor competition from temporary cross-border service provision.

That tripartite distinction is the paper’s strategic asset. It should be made explicit and repeated.

#### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or filling a gap in a LITERATURE?
It is mixed, but too often framed as a literature gap. The stronger framing is about the world: **How does globalization reshape politics when competition arrives in locally anchored jobs rather than traded goods?** That is a first-order question. “The literature has not studied posted workers” is not.

#### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the intro?
Not cleanly enough. Right now they might say: “It’s a DiD/Bartik paper on posted workers and FN voting in France, with suggestive evidence but pre-trends.” That is not a memorable contribution statement. The introduction needs to make the novelty less design-centric and more conceptual.

#### What would make the contribution bigger?
Most importantly: **move from France/FN/posting as a case study to a broader claim about the political consequences of non-tradable labor-market integration.** Concretely:
- A stronger comparison to **tradable-sector shocks** within the same setting.
- More direct evidence on **why** non-tradable competition is politically distinctive: visibility, local salience, inability to relocate, EU-rule resentment.
- Outcomes beyond vote share, if available: turnout, abstention, anti-EU voting, issue salience, occupational patterns, survey attitudes.
- A tighter test of whether this is about **EU-mediated labor arbitrage** rather than generic rural/construction-area conservatism.

The biggest missed opportunity is that the paper has an idea bigger than its current empirics. It should be selling the idea harder.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

### Closest neighbors
The nearest conversations seem to be:
- **Autor, Dorn, and Hanson (2013)** on import competition and labor markets.
- **Colantone and Stanig (2018)** on trade shocks and support for the far right.
- **Dippel, Gold, Heblich, and Pinto (2022)** / related China-shock-and-politics work.
- **Dustmann, Vasiljeva, and Piil Damm (2019)** and related immigration/political-backlash papers.
- **Muñoz (2024)** on the labor-market effects of posted workers.

Also relevant, even if not cited centrally enough:
- The broader literature on **temporary migration / firm-mediated labor mobility / cross-border service provision**.
- EU political economy papers on **integration, social dumping, and Euroscepticism**.
- Literature on **salience and visibility of economic shocks**, which may be more central than the author realizes.

### How should the paper position itself?
It should mostly **build on and re-route**, not attack. The right stance is:

- Build on trade-and-politics papers by saying: they show one way globalization affects politics; this paper studies another.
- Build on immigration-and-politics papers by saying: posted workers resemble immigration in local labor-market presence, but differ because they are temporary, firm-mediated, and legally tied to cross-border service provision.
- Build on Muñoz by extending labor-market effects into political consequences.

The paper should not posture as if it overturns trade-shock explanations. It does not. Its strongest claim is that the existing conversation is incomplete without this margin.

### Too narrow or too broad?
Currently a bit **too narrow empirically and too broad rhetorically**. It is empirically one-country, one-political-family, one institutional episode; rhetorically it sometimes sounds like it has identified a general new law of globalization politics. Better to say: France is a useful laboratory for studying a general mechanism.

### What literature does it seem unaware of?
The paper seems under-engaged with:
- **Comparative political economy of European integration**, especially social dumping / posted workers / anti-EU backlash.
- **Services trade / labor mobility** economics, not just trade and immigration.
- **Place-based political behavior** and the persistence of far-right geography.
- Potentially **regional development / territorial cleavage** literatures in France.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?
Almost, but not quite. It is currently having the “economic shocks and populism” conversation in a generic way. The more distinctive and potentially impactful conversation is:

> globalization through services, temporary labor mobility, and EU institutional design.

That is more original and more likely to get top-journal attention than a standard “shock X predicts populism Y” framing.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
The world before this paper: we know trade shocks and immigration can shape political behavior, especially support for nationalist or far-right parties.

### Tension
But globalization does not only arrive through imports or permanent migrants. The EU created a channel through which firms can move workers temporarily into local service jobs. Those jobs are spatially fixed and highly visible. Does that create a distinct political backlash?

### Resolution
French areas more exposed to construction and agriculture — sectors that absorb posted workers — saw larger subsequent gains in FN/RN support, with a null for manufacturing exposure. But the design cannot cleanly separate this from pre-existing trends, so the paper ends up documenting a strong patterned association rather than nailing causality.

### Implications
The politics of globalization may depend not only on exposure to trade or immigration, but on whether labor competition is local, visible, temporary, and institutionally linked to supranational rules. That matters for how economists think about European integration and the political consequences of service-market liberalization.

### Evaluation
There is a plausible narrative arc here, but the paper does not fully own it. Right now it reads somewhat like:
1. Here is a shock.
2. Here is a design.
3. Here is a result.
4. Here is a caveat that weakens the result.

That is not a satisfying top-journal narrative. The paper needs to be reorganized around a stronger story:

- **Globalization has a neglected local-labor channel.**
- **Posted workers are the cleanest empirical case of that channel.**
- **France shows that this channel maps onto far-right growth in exactly the sectors where it should.**
- **The evidence is suggestive rather than definitive, but the mechanism is too important to ignore.**

In other words, the story should be about discovering a politically salient margin of integration, not about squeezing a causal estimate from a strained design.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?
I would lead with:

> “Areas specialized in construction and agriculture — the sectors most exposed to EU posted workers — saw much larger rises in Front National support than other French areas.”

Even better, if the paper can responsibly say it:

> “The political backlash to globalization may be strongest not where goods are imported, but where foreign labor shows up on local job sites.”

That is the line that gets attention.

### Would people lean in?
Some would lean in — especially political economists, labor economists, and European economists — because posted workers are a concrete, institutionally distinctive phenomenon. But many will immediately ask whether this is just another way of picking up longstanding rural/peripheral/far-right geography. The paper as written does not fully disarm that reaction.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Almost everyone will ask:

> “Why is this not just construction-heavy places trending toward Le Pen for unrelated reasons?”

That is the key strategic problem. Since you asked me not to referee the identification, I won’t turn this into a methods critique — but editorially, the author must understand that the paper’s positioning should anticipate this question, not bury it.

### Are the modest / qualified findings still interesting?
Yes, potentially. But only if the paper explicitly presents itself as documenting a **new politically relevant margin of exposure** rather than claiming clean causal proof. Null-or-qualified findings can still matter at AER if they change the conceptual map. The current draft is halfway between “causal estimate” and “descriptive pattern,” which weakens both. It needs to choose.

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

### What would make it read better?

#### 1. Front-load the concept, not the method
The first 3-4 pages are too method-forward. The reader should learn before anything else:
- why posted workers are conceptually different,
- why non-tradable labor competition might matter politically,
- why France/EU enlargement is the right setting.

Right now the paper gets there, but it sounds like an econometrics paper looking for a contribution.

#### 2. Shorten the institutional background
The background section is serviceable but too long relative to the paper’s actual leverage. Compress the directive details and use that space to sharpen the conceptual distinction between:
- imports,
- immigrants,
- posted workers.

That triangle is the paper.

#### 3. Move some caveat language later, but not too late
The paper is admirably honest — more honest than many submissions — but it over-corrects by deflating itself too early. The introduction should not hide the pre-trend issue, but it should first establish why the question matters. Lead with the big idea; then clarify the evidentiary status.

#### 4. Bring the sector-specific pattern to center stage
The construction/agriculture vs manufacturing contrast is the most memorable result in the paper. It currently sits in robustness. Editorially, that is a mistake. It belongs in the main result sequence because it is central to the story.

#### 5. Demote the Bartik discussion
The Bartik material feels vestigial. By the paper’s own telling, the action is in the exposure × post design and the sectoral comparison. The Bartik specification does not seem to carry the paper and may distract from the cleaner conceptual point. If kept, it should be shorter and clearly secondary.

#### 6. Rewrite the conclusion
The conclusion mostly summarizes. It should instead do one of two things:
- either make the broader claim about globalization through non-tradable labor competition,
- or explain why economists should care about posted workers as a neglected margin of integration.

Right now it is too apologetic and too incremental.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

Bluntly: in current form, this is **not yet an AER paper**, mainly because the paper has a better idea than its present execution and framing.

### What is the gap?

#### Mostly a framing problem
The science may or may not ultimately support a top-journal claim, but the current manuscript undersells the central conceptual contribution. It reads like a careful graduate-field-paper version of a more ambitious idea.

#### Also a scope problem
The paper is too narrow for the ambition of the claims. One country, one partisan outcome, one institutional setting, and a result the paper itself heavily qualifies. For AER, either the evidence must be much tighter, or the conceptual contribution must be broadened with richer implications and mechanism.

#### Some novelty risk
“Economic exposure predicts far-right voting” is now crowded terrain. The novelty here is specifically **posted workers / service-market integration / non-tradable labor competition**. If that distinction is not made vivid, the paper will look derivative.

#### Some ambition problem
The paper is competent but safe. It settles too quickly for “this is a distinct channel” without really showing why that channel should alter how economists think about globalization and politics.

### Single most impactful advice
**Reframe the paper around the political economy of non-tradable labor-market integration — with posted workers as the flagship case — rather than around a single qualified DiD estimate in France.**

That is the one change that could most improve its odds. If the paper becomes “a new margin of globalization with distinct political consequences,” it has a shot at relevance. If it remains “a suggestive France DiD on posted workers and FN voting,” it will feel second-tier.
