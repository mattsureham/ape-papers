**SUBJECT:** Strategic Positioning – "Trading Non-Tradable Votes: EU Posted Workers and Far-Right Support in France"

---

## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether the "Posted Workers Directive"—a unique EU policy allowing firms to send their own employees to work abroad under home-country social security rules—explains the rise of far-right voting in France. It argues that while manufacturing workers can "retrain" or rely on trade protections, workers in non-tradable sectors like construction and agriculture are uniquely vulnerable to this "in-person" service competition, leading to a localized and potent political backlash.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this well in the abstract and the first paragraph of the introduction. However, it leads with the "what" (posted workers) rather than the "why it matters for economics." 

**The pitch the paper *should* have:**
"Economists have long studied how goods-trade shocks fuel populism, but we know little about the political consequences of 'trading non-tradables.' By exploiting the unique institutional framework of the EU Posted Workers Directive, this paper shows that when labor competition enters the local construction site or farm—places where native workers cannot escape through relocation or offshoring—the resulting political backlash is significantly more concentrated and durable than that of traditional trade shocks."

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**State of Contribution:** The paper identifies a "services-trade" channel for political realignment that is distinct from the manufacturing-based "China Shock" and traditional permanent migration.

*   **Differentiation:** It differentiates itself from **Muñoz (2024)** by moving from labor market outcomes to political outcomes, and from **Autor et al. (2013)** and **Colantone & Stanig (2018)** by focusing on non-tradable sectors rather than manufacturing.
*   **World vs. Literature:** It is currently framed as a "gap-filler" (Muñoz did labor, I do politics). It needs to be framed as an inquiry into the **visibility and "trapped-ness" of labor competition.**
*   **Clarity:** A smart economist would say: "It’s a Bartik-style paper showing that the sectors we thought were 'safe' from globalization (construction/ag) are actually the ones driving far-right growth because of specific EU rules."
*   **Making it bigger:** To make this an AER paper, the author needs to move beyond the "French case study." The contribution would be bigger if it theorized (and tested) *why* the backlash in non-tradables is different. Is it because it's more visible? Or because the workers have lower elasticity of labor supply?

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

*   **Closest Neighbors:** Muñoz (2024) [Labor effects of posting]; Colantone & Stanig (2018) [Trade and far-right]; Autor, Dorn, & Hanson (2013) [Local labor markets]; Dustmann et al. (2019) [Immigration and voting].
*   **Strategy:** The paper should **synthesize** these. It should position itself as the "missing link" between the immigration literature (people moving) and the trade literature (goods moving). Posted workers are *services* moving via *temporary* people.
*   **Niche vs. Broad:** Currently a bit niche (French political history). It needs to speak to the broader "Political Economy of Globalization."
*   **Missing Conversations:** The paper should connect to the **Geography of Discontent** literature (e.g., Rodriguez-Pose). It also misses the "Salience" literature—why does seeing a Polish worker on a local site matter more than seeing a "Made in China" sticker?

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

*   **Setup:** France has seen a 10-fold increase in posted workers and a doubling of far-right support.
*   **Tension:** We usually think construction/agriculture are insulated from trade shocks. Yet, these are exactly where the FN/RN grew fastest.
*   **Resolution:** Exposure to "posted" labor in these non-tradable sectors explains a significant chunk of far-right gains, even when controlling for the "China Shock."
*   **Implications:** Policy focus on "reskilling" manufacturing workers may be missing the real source of political instability, which lies in the "un-tradable" service sector.

**Evaluation:** The arc is currently weakened by the "Honest Limitation" (the pre-trend). The narrative shifts from "I found a cause" to "I found a correlation that might be a trend." To be AER, the narrative must be: "Even if this is a trend, the enlargement *accelerated* it in a way that proves the mechanism."

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

*   **The Lead Fact:** "A one-standard-deviation increase in construction exposure predicts a 5.5 percentage point jump in the Le Pen vote—that's a quarter of her total growth."
*   **Reaction:** People will lean in. The idea that "non-tradable" sectors are the new front line of the globalization backlash is counter-intuitive and "dinner party" ready.
*   **Follow-up:** "But isn't this just about rural resentment?" The author’s manufacturing placebo (the null result) is the crucial shield against this question.

---

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

*   **Front-load the Placebo:** The manufacturing "null" is actually one of the most interesting parts of the paper. It should be elevated to the main results section immediately following the baseline to prove the "non-tradable" story.
*   **Shorten:** The "Data" section on NUTS2 mappings is a bit dry; move the technical mapping details to an appendix. 
*   **The Trend Issue:** Don't bury the sensitivity to linear trends on page 8. Address it head-on in the Intro as a "feature, not a bug"—the paper documents a long-run structural realignment of the French working class that the 2004/2007 shocks "supercharged."

---

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

**The Gap:** Currently, the paper is an "identification-challenged" case study. The pre-trend kills the "clean DiD" story that AER referees often demand for "standard" topics. 

**Single Biggest Improvement:** Shift the ambition from a *policy evaluation* (Did EU enlargement do this?) to a *structural exploration* (Why is the political economy of service-trade different from goods-trade?). 

If the author can use the 2018 Directive revision (which changed the rules) or individual-level panel data to show that it is the *same* construction workers switching votes, the paper moves from "suggestive correlation" to "top-tier evidence."
