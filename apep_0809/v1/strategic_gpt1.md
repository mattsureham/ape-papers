## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether a specific form of European labor-market integration — the rise of EU “posted workers” after enlargement — helped fuel far-right voting in France. The core idea is that unlike import competition, posted workers enter place-bound, non-tradable sectors such as construction and agriculture, so any political backlash should be especially sharp in places where natives cannot easily avoid direct labor-market competition.

Why should a busy economist care? Because if true, this would broaden the canonical story linking globalization to populism: not just goods trade and permanent immigration, but temporary cross-border service provision in non-tradable sectors can reshape politics.

Does the paper itself articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs? **Reasonably, but not sharply enough.** The introduction gets to the right topic quickly, but it still reads a bit like “another exposure-based design around a globalization shock.” The truly distinctive angle is not France, or even posted workers per se; it is the claim that **the politics of globalization differ when competition arrives in non-tradable local labor markets rather than through imports or settlement migration**. That should be the paper’s opening frame.

### The pitch the paper should have

> Globalization does not only arrive through imported goods or permanent migrants. In Europe, it also arrives through posted workers: employees sent temporarily across borders to perform local services in sectors like construction and agriculture. Because these sectors are non-tradable and place-bound, native workers cannot easily relocate or switch away from the competition, making posted-worker inflows a potentially potent source of political backlash.  
>  
> This paper studies whether that channel mattered for the rise of the French far right after EU enlargement. Using pre-enlargement local exposure to posted-worker-intensive sectors, I show that far-right vote growth was concentrated in areas specialized in construction and agriculture rather than manufacturing, suggesting that the political consequences of European integration ran not only through trade and immigration, but through competition in local non-tradable labor markets.

That is the story. The current introduction eventually says something like this, but it should say it faster and with more force.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper’s contribution is to argue that the rise of posted workers after EU enlargement created a **non-tradable-sector labor competition channel** linking European integration to far-right support in France, distinct from the standard goods-trade and immigration channels.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest papers?

**Partially, but not enough.** The author names the right broad literatures, but the differentiation is still underdeveloped. Right now, the paper can be summarized too easily as: “a regional exposure paper showing that another globalization shock correlates with far-right voting.” That is not enough for AER positioning.

It needs to differentiate itself much more explicitly from at least four neighboring conversations:

1. **Trade shocks and populism**  
   - Colantone and Stanig (2018, AER or AER-style canon in this conversation)  
   - Autor, Dorn, Hanson-type China shock political economy papers  
   - Dippel, Gold, Heblich, Pinto-type trade/politics work  
   Relative to these, the paper’s novelty is **not** another import-competition story, but a local-services competition story.

2. **Immigration and far-right voting**  
   - Dustmann, Vasiljeva, Piil Damm  
   - Halla, Wagner, Zweimüller  
   - Edo, Giesing, Öztunc, Poutvaara  
   - Steinmayr  
   Relative to these, the novelty is that posted workers are **workers without local political incorporation** and often without residential settlement, so the mechanism is labor-market competition without the standard neighborhood/contact/fiscal channels of immigration.

3. **EU integration / free movement / posted workers**  
   - Muñoz (the paper cites this and should lean much harder on it)  
   - There is also a broader legal/political economy literature on the Posted Workers Directive, social dumping, and East-West integration. The paper should signal awareness of this conversation even if economics has relatively fewer flagship papers here.

4. **Tradability / local labor markets**  
   - Burstein et al. on tradability  
   - Mian and Sufi-style local demand/non-tradable exposure ideas  
   The paper’s conceptual edge is that **non-tradability changes the incidence and salience of competition**.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a gap in a literature?

**Mostly framed as a world question, which is good.** The paper asks whether posted workers contributed to far-right support. That is better than saying “the literature has not studied posted workers.” But it still drifts into “this extends literature X” language too quickly. The stronger framing is:

- World question: *How does integration in local service labor markets affect politics?*
- Not literature-gap framing: *Nobody has studied posted workers yet.*

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?

At present, they could say: **“It’s a paper on whether EU posted-worker exposure predicts FN support in France.”**  
That is accurate but not yet memorable. Worse, they might say: **“It’s another shift-share / DiD paper connecting globalization to populism.”**

The introduction should make them say:  
**“Interesting — they argue that the politically potent form of globalization is direct labor competition in non-tradable local sectors, not just imports or immigration.”**

### What would make the contribution bigger?

Several possibilities:

1. **A sharper outcome framing**
   - Not just FN vote share, but whether posted-worker exposure predicts specifically **Eurosceptic** voting, anti-EU attitudes, or support for anti-integration platforms.
   - This would connect the mechanism directly to the institutional source of the shock.

2. **A more distinctive comparison**
   - The current manufacturing placebo is helpful, but the bigger comparison is:  
     **posted-worker exposure vs import exposure vs immigrant settlement exposure** in the same framework.
   - If the paper could show these channels have different geographic and political signatures, the contribution gets much larger.

3. **A mechanism tied to visibility and labor-market incidence**
   - The paper hints at salience and place-boundedness. If it could more directly show that the effect is stronger where local workers are less mobile, less educated, or more concentrated in manual occupations, the story becomes much more important.

4. **A broader frame than France alone**
   - France is a natural case, but the strategic contribution would be bigger if framed as a general European political economy mechanism illustrated in France, rather than just a France case study.

The closest neighbors appear to be:

One might also place it near:
- **Malgouyres** on trade shocks in France.
- Work on **European integration and political backlash**, including free movement and Euroscepticism.

### How should the paper position itself relative to those neighbors?

**Build on and reframe them; do not attack them.**

The right stance is:

- Trade papers taught us that globalization can generate populist backlash.
- Immigration papers taught us that labor-market and cultural exposure can alter voting.
- This paper shows there is a third, underappreciated margin: **cross-border service provision in non-tradable sectors**.
- Muñoz showed posted workers matter for labor markets; this paper asks whether they matter politically.

That is a coherent bridge paper. The author should not oversell this as overturning existing accounts. The smart move is to present it as adding a missing but conceptually important globalization channel.

### Is the paper currently positioned too narrowly or too broadly?

**A bit too narrowly in data, but too broadly in claim.**

- Too narrowly because much of the exposition is very France-specific and institutional.
- Too broadly because it sometimes implies it has identified a major new causal political economy channel, then later concedes that the central estimate is not causally clean.

This creates a mismatch. If the causal leverage is limited, then the strategic pitch should emphasize:
- conceptual importance of the channel,
- suggestive geographic evidence,
- distinction from trade/manufacturing,
- agenda-setting value.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?

It could speak more directly to:

1. **European integration and political backlash**
   - Not just trade and immigration, but EU legal integration, social dumping, and free provision of services.

2. **Political economy of place-based labor-market exposure**
   - The fact that non-tradability creates locally concentrated incidence is central and should be anchored more clearly.

3. **Salience/contact/visibility literatures**
   - Posted workers are unusual because they may be visible as workers but less embedded as neighbors. That’s potentially a very interesting twist relative to immigration papers.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?

**Almost, but not quite.** Right now it is mostly having the “globalization and populism” conversation. That is the obvious one. The more interesting conversation is:

> Which forms of cross-border integration are most politically explosive, and why?

That is a more ambitious and more AER-worthy conversation than “do posted workers matter in France?”

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Before this paper, the standard political-economy account emphasizes goods-trade shocks and immigration as drivers of anti-globalization politics. EU posted workers represent a large but understudied form of integration that channels labor competition into local service sectors.

### Tension

If posted workers matter, they should be politically consequential precisely because they enter **non-tradable, place-bound** sectors where exposure is hard to escape and highly visible. Yet we do not know whether this form of integration actually maps onto far-right political gains.

### Resolution

Areas of France more exposed to construction and agriculture saw larger far-right gains after EU enlargement, and manufacturing exposure does not show the same pattern. But the paper’s own event study shows these areas were already trending toward the far right before enlargement, so the paper ends up with a suggestive channel rather than a clean causal break.

### Implications

The political consequences of globalization may depend not just on factor-price effects or import penetration, but on whether competition arrives in **local, non-tradable labor markets** through institutions like the Posted Workers Directive. That matters for how economists think about EU integration, labor-market competition, and populist backlash.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

**Serviceable, but compromised by internal conflict.**

The problem is that the paper has a potentially strong narrative, but it undercuts itself midway:

- “This is a quasi-exogenous shock”
- then “this is the preferred specification”
- then “actually the central estimate appears to be driven substantially by pre-trends”

Intellectually, the honesty is good. Strategically, the paper has not decided whether it is:
1. a causal paper on EU enlargement and far-right voting, or
2. a conceptual/descriptive paper documenting a politically meaningful geography.

Right now it tries to be both. That weakens the story.

### If it is a collection of results looking for a story, what story should it be telling?

The right story is:

> Far-right growth in France was disproportionately concentrated in places exposed to a distinctive form of European labor-market integration: temporary cross-border labor in non-tradable sectors. This does not yet isolate a clean causal treatment effect of enlargement, but it reveals a politically important geography that existing trade-centered accounts miss.

That story is more modest but also more coherent. It does not oversell what the design cannot carry.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?

I would say:

**“The rise of the French far right is more strongly associated with exposure to construction and agriculture — where EU posted workers compete directly with local labor — than with manufacturing, which is the sector political-economy papers usually focus on.”**

That is the intriguing fact.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

**They would lean in initially.** The posted-worker angle is fresh, and the non-tradable-sector framing is genuinely interesting.

But then they would ask the obvious question:

### What follow-up question would they ask?

**“Is this actually causal, or are these just the kinds of places that were already becoming FN strongholds?”**

And unfortunately, the paper’s answer is currently: “mostly the latter, or at least we can’t cleanly separate the two.” That is a serious strategic issue.

### If the findings are null or modest, is the null itself interesting?

The paper’s most important “negative” result is not that posted workers do not matter; it is that **the apparent effect is not a clean post-enlargement break once differential trends are allowed.** That can be interesting, but only if framed well.

Right now, it reads a bit like a partially failed causal design that the author admirably reports honestly. For AER purposes, that is not enough. To make the non-result valuable, the paper would have to make a stronger conceptual argument that:

- existing frameworks miss the non-tradable geography of populism,
- posted-worker exposure tracks that geography in a way manufacturing does not,
- and this pattern is itself a substantive new fact even absent a definitive quasi-experiment.

At the moment, the paper has not fully made that case.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

Bluntly: in its current form, **this is not close to AER**.

The issue is not mainly polish. It is that the paper currently combines:
- a nice idea,
- a topical setting,
- a plausible empirical design,
- but a central result that the paper itself says does not survive the most important trend adjustment.

That pushes it away from “AER paper” and toward “interesting field-journal paper if reframed carefully.”

### What is the main gap?

Mostly:
- **a framing problem**, and
- **an ambition/novelty problem**.

Less so a pure scope problem.

#### Framing problem
The paper is still presenting itself as if it has identified a causal effect of posted workers on far-right voting, while simultaneously conceding that it really has not. That is an unstable frame.

#### Novelty problem
The core empirical claim — exposure to a globalization shock predicts populist voting — is now familiar. The novelty has to come from the **type of globalization** and the **non-tradable mechanism**. That part is not yet pushed hard enough.

#### Ambition problem
The paper is competent and sensible, but safe. It does not yet force the reader to rethink how globalization enters politics.

### What would excite the top 10 people in this field?

A version of this project that could say one of the following:

1. **“Among the different forms of globalization, competition in non-tradable local labor markets is especially politically destabilizing.”**
2. **“Posted workers generate political backlash through a channel distinct from both imports and immigrant settlement, and here is evidence separating those channels.”**
3. **“European integration affected politics not only through markets for goods and permanent migration, but through the legally mediated cross-border provision of local services.”**

The current paper gestures at all three, but nails none.

### Single most impactful piece of advice

**Reframe the paper away from a shaky causal claim about EU enlargement and toward a broader, more original claim about how non-tradable-sector labor-market integration shapes populist politics — and make the sectoral comparison to trade/manufacturing the centerpiece rather than a side result.**

That is the one change that would most improve its strategic positioning.
