---

### 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether the distortionary effects of tax notches are universal or dependent on the price level at which they occur. Exploiting a 2023 Australian reform that moved a stamp duty exemption threshold from A$650k to A$800k, the author finds that while the lower threshold caused massive price bunching, the higher threshold—despite offering a similar proportional tax saving—caused zero price distortion. 

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this clearly. The first two paragraphs effectively set up the "theory vs. reality" tension. It successfully avoids being "just another bunching paper" by leading with the striking asymmetry of the result.

---

### 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution:** The paper documents a "boundary condition" for tax notches, showing that identical tax incentives fail to distort prices when they sit in "thinner" or more rigid segments of the price distribution.

*   **Differentiation:** It moves beyond Best & Kleven (2018) and Kopczuk & Munroe (2015) by providing a direct "within-market" comparison of two different thresholds using a difference-in-bunching design.
*   **Framing:** It is framed as a question about the world (Do notches always distort?) rather than a gap in literature.
*   **Clarity:** A smart economist would say: "It shows that moving a tax cliff to a higher price point killed the bunching, even though the tax break stayed the same."
*   **Expansion:** To make the contribution bigger, the author needs to formalize the *why*. Is it search frictions, agent heterogeneity, or credit constraints that vary across the price distribution? Moving from "it happened" to "here is the structural reason it must happen" is the AER-level leap.

---

### 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

*   **Closest Neighbors:** Best & Kleven (2018) on UK stamp duty; Kopczuk & Munroe (2015) on NYC mansion tax; Kleven (2016) on bunching theory.
*   **Positioning:** The paper "limits the scope" of the existing literature. It doesn't attack them; it identifies the "operating range" of their findings.
*   **Missing Conversations:** The paper should speak more to the **Search and Matching** literature in housing. The "rigidity" the author mentions is essentially a search friction or a bargaining story. Connecting the null result to the density of buyers/sellers in specific price tiers would bridge Public Finance and Urban/Macro.

---

### 4. NARRATIVE ARC

*   **Setup:** Tax notches distort behavior; we see this everywhere from income to housing.
*   **Tension:** NSW moved their notch up. We should see bunching follow the move.
*   **Resolution:** Bunching vanished at the old spot but *never appeared* at the new one.
*   **Implications:** Policy can be "Pareto-improving" by placing notches in these "dead zones" of the distribution where they provide transfers without distortions.

**Evaluation:** The narrative arc is very strong. It is a "Goldilocks" story—the notch was "just right" to distort at $650k, but the market was "too cold" at $800k.

---

### 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

*   **The Lead Fact:** "Australia moved a $30,000 tax cliff to a higher price bracket and the market distortion completely disappeared."
*   **Reaction:** Lean in. Economists love a precise null result that contradicts standard "Econ 101" intuition. 
*   **Follow-up:** "Is it because $800k buyers are richer and care less, or because there are just fewer houses at that exact price point?"

---

### 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

*   **Front-loading:** The paper is well-structured. The asymmetry is the star; Table 2 is the "money shot" and it appears early enough.
*   **Lot Area:** The lot area result (Section 5.3) is actually more important than the author treats it. It proves the notch was *seen* and *acted upon*, but the "price" margin was too sticky. This section should be expanded to discuss why quality adjusted while price didn't.
*   **Appendix:** The robustness checks in Table 3 are standard; they belong in the main text for a null result paper to prove the null isn't a fluke.

---

### 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The paper is currently a very high-quality "Applied Micro" piece. To make it a clear AER winner, it needs **Ambitious Mechanism Testing.** 

The "sparsity" explanation is currently hand-wavy. The author needs to prove *why* the $800k market is different. Is the variance of valuations higher? Are there fewer transactions? Are these properties more heterogeneous? If the author can show that "Bunching = f(Notch Size, Local Density of Substitutes)," they have a general theory of market friction, not just an interesting Australian fact.

**Single biggest piece of advice:** Use the "Lot Area" finding as a bridge to a formal model of why quality-adjustment happens when price-adjustment fails.
