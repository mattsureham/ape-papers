## 1. **Idea Fidelity**

The paper broadly pursues the original idea: it uses FRA quiet zone designations as staggered treatment, Zillow home values as the main outcome, and a modern staggered DiD framework centered on Callaway-Sant’Anna. It also follows the intended comparison between treated cities and cities with railroad crossings that never adopt, and it includes some of the proposed robustness checks.

That said, it misses several key elements of the original design in ways that matter for identification and interpretability. First, the manifest proposed **monthly** Zillow data with exact effective dates, but the paper collapses to **annual city-level averages** and codes treatment at the year of first quiet-zone designation. That is a substantial loss of identifying variation and likely introduces nonclassical timing error. Second, the manifest highlighted a **dose-response by crossings silenced** and secondary outcomes near crossings; the paper only offers coarse city-level heterogeneity and does not exploit the crossing-level intensity in a disciplined way. Third, the original idea was about a quasi-experiment in **noise elimination near crossings**, but the final implementation is at the **city level**, where attenuation is almost guaranteed. So the paper is faithful to the broad question, but it does not deliver the sharpest version of the proposed design.

## 2. **Summary**

This paper studies whether FRA quiet zone designations increase residential property values by reducing train-horn noise. Using staggered DiD methods on a panel of cities with railroad crossings, the paper reports a near-zero average effect on city-level Zillow home values and interprets this as evidence that horn noise does not materially capitalize into housing prices.

The topic is interesting, the policy variation is real, and a well-executed paper here could make a useful contribution. In the current draft, however, the design and exposition do not yet support the paper’s strong conclusion of a “precisely estimated null.”

## 3. **Essential Points**

1. **The paper does not establish credible parallel trends, and the presentation of the event-study evidence is internally inconsistent.**  
   This is the biggest problem. Table 3 shows negative and apparently statistically significant pre-treatment coefficients, and the table reports a pre-trend test \(p=0.002\). The text then says none are individually significant and later says the joint test yields \(p=0.056\). These cannot all be true. If pre-trends are present, the causal interpretation is weakened substantially, especially given that adoption is plausibly related to municipal capacity and neighborhood demand. You need to reconcile the numbers, report the exact estimator and aggregation used for the event study, and take the pre-trend evidence seriously rather than brushing it aside.

2. **The outcome/treatment aggregation is so coarse that the null is hard to interpret economically.**  
   Quiet zones affect homes near crossings, but the outcome is city-level annual ZHVI. This is almost the textbook setting for attenuation from spatial aggregation. Your own discussion effectively concedes this. As written, the result is not “quiet zones do not affect property values”; it is “quiet zones do not move average city-level Zillow values enough to detect with this design.” That is a much narrower and less surprising result. To make an economically meaningful claim, you need either finer spatial outcomes or a tightly argued exposure design showing that city-level treatment intensity is large enough to generate detectable effects if capitalization were meaningful.

3. **The identifying assumptions around treatment timing are too casually asserted, and selection into adoption is likely first-order.**  
   Municipalities adopt quiet zones after financing and installing safety upgrades, often in larger, richer, higher-capacity places. The paper repeatedly states that timing depends on budgets, engineering, and bureaucracy “rather than housing market dynamics,” but those are not exogenous forces; they are often tightly linked to local fiscal health, growth, and redevelopment. City fixed effects do not solve differential trends. You need a more convincing design-based argument for timing, stronger controls for evolving local conditions, and more informative comparisons among eventual adopters.

## 4. **Suggestions**

The paper is promising, but it needs a more disciplined empirical design and much cleaner exposition before it is publishable.

First, I would strongly encourage you to return to the **monthly panel**, which was the original strength of the research design. The FRA data contain exact effective dates, and Zillow is monthly. Aggregating to annual data throws away timing variation and creates treatment misclassification: a city designated in December is coded the same as a city designated in January. Monthly event time would also let you test whether any capitalization is immediate, gradual, or absent. If the concern is serial correlation, deal with that directly rather than coarsening the data. With monthly data, clustering at the city level may still be acceptable, but I would also report inference that is robust to a limited number of treated clusters across adoption cohorts, or at least show that your standard errors are not being understated by the long panel structure.

Second, the paper should better align treatment with **exposure intensity**. A city is treated when its first quiet zone begins, but many cities have multiple crossings and possibly multiple zones over time. A binary “PostQZ” indicator is too blunt. More informative specifications would include: fraction of public crossings in quiet zones, number of quiet-zone crossings, train frequency weighted by treated crossings, or an exposure measure scaled by likely affected housing stock. At minimum, the main specification should distinguish between a city that silences one crossing and a city that silences twenty. Right now, the estimated 1.2 percent ATT is difficult to interpret because the treatment dose varies enormously across cities.

Relatedly, you should think much harder about the **economically plausible magnitude**. A city-level null is entirely plausible even if local effects near crossings are meaningful. Suppose only a small share of homes lie within the relevant noise radius. Then even a 3–5 percent local effect would mechanically shrink to well under 1 percent in city averages. That means your confidence interval may not actually be very informative about the substantive question. I would recommend a back-of-the-envelope calculation in the main text: under reasonable assumptions about the share of housing exposed to train horns and the number of crossings silenced, what city-level effect should one expect if neighborhood-level capitalization were, say, 3 percent? This would let readers judge whether your estimates are genuinely “precise” or merely precise around an attenuated estimand.

Third, the comparison group deserves more scrutiny. “Cities with railroad crossings that never receive designation” is natural, but likely not ideal if never-adopters are systematically smaller, poorer, or administratively different. Your summary statistics already show large level differences, and those likely come with different trends as well. The eventual-adopter design is therefore important, but in the current draft it is underdeveloped. I would elevate that design: compare earlier adopters to later adopters, show balance and pre-trends within the adopter sample, and report the dynamic effects there. That would be more persuasive than leaning on never-treated controls.

Fourth, the paper needs a more careful treatment of **pre-trends and dynamic effects**. As written, the event-study section is not reliable. Clean this up completely. Report one event-study figure—not only a table—with confidence intervals, cohort composition by event time, and a clearly defined reference period. If pre-trends remain negative, then do not present the result as a clean null. Instead, be candid: “The design does not fully satisfy parallel trends, and the estimates should be interpreted cautiously.” A paper can survive imperfect pre-trends if it is transparent and reframes the contribution appropriately; it cannot survive contradictory reporting.

Fifth, I would reconsider the role of the **state-by-year fixed effects** specification. The paper treats the sign flip there as informative, but the interpretation is murky. State-by-year fixed effects absorb broad state housing cycles, which is sensible, but they do not solve differential local trends within states. Moreover, if treatment is concentrated in a few states and years, these controls can substantially change identifying variation. You should explain exactly what residual variation identifies the estimate in that specification and whether treatment timing is still sufficiently rich. A table showing the distribution of adoption by state and year would help.

Sixth, the paper would benefit from stronger **descriptive institutional evidence**. The adoption process matters a lot for identification. Can you show whether quiet zone implementation correlates with observable city growth, pre-treatment price appreciation, population, rail intensity, or state funding programs? Even a simple hazard-style descriptive model of adoption timing would be useful. If adoption is strongly predicted by booming housing markets or urban growth, that weakens the design; if it is mainly predicted by crossing characteristics, state programs, and rail operations, that helps.

Seventh, the draft has several **internal inconsistencies** that need to be fixed before readers can trust the results. The summary statistics table and the text report different average home values. The event-study table, significance markers, and discussion contradict each other. The abstract says “precisely estimated null,” but a 95 percent interval of roughly \([-2\%, 4.4\%]\) is not especially precise for many housing questions. The sample period is sometimes 2000–2024 and sometimes through February 2026. These may sound editorial, but they matter because they undermine confidence that the estimates are being handled carefully.

Eighth, I would scale back the paper’s strongest substantive claims. The statement that the findings “challenge” the hedonic noise literature is too strong for this design. You are not estimating the price effect of exposure to rail noise at the parcel or neighborhood margin; you are estimating the effect of city-level quiet-zone adoption on city-level ZHVI. Those are different objects. The more defensible contribution is narrower: after substantial city-level aggregation, and despite a large policy intervention, you do not find detectable effects in average city-level home values. That is still interesting, but it does not by itself imply that “noise does not capitalize” or that prior hedonic discounts mostly reflect non-noise factors.

Finally, if finer spatial housing data are at all feasible, that is the path most likely to turn this into a publishable paper. Even zip-code or tract-level outcomes, matched to crossing locations and treatment dates, would be a major improvement. A difference-in-differences design comparing areas close to treated crossings to areas farther away within the same city would address many of the current concerns: it would sharpen exposure, reduce omitted trends, and make the magnitude economically interpretable. If such data are not feasible, then the paper should be repositioned more modestly as evidence on the absence of **aggregate city-level capitalization**, not on the absence of housing-market effects per se.

In short: the idea is good, the policy setting is attractive, and the null result could be meaningful. But the current version overstates what the evidence can support, underplays pre-trend problems, and uses a level of aggregation that likely obscures the relevant economics.
