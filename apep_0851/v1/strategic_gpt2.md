## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether ending a high-profile tax break for foreign residents in Portugal reduced housing prices. That question matters because many governments claim that wealthy, tax-favored migrants are a major driver of local housing unaffordability; if removing the tax subsidy does not move prices, that would suggest policymakers are targeting a politically salient but quantitatively minor source of demand.

The paper does **not** articulate this pitch as clearly and sharply as it should in the first two paragraphs. It opens with a policy anecdote and then immediately reports estimates, but it does not sufficiently elevate the broader question: **Are expat tax incentives an important housing-market policy lever, or mostly political symbolism?** That is the version busy economists would care about.

### The pitch the paper should have

“Across Europe, governments increasingly blame tax-favored foreign residents for pushing up housing costs. But do preferential tax regimes for mobile high-income households meaningfully affect aggregate housing prices, or are they too small relative to supply constraints, tourism, and domestic demand to matter? This paper studies Portugal’s 2023 termination of the Non-Habitual Resident regime—one of Europe’s most prominent expat tax subsidies—and finds no detectable slowdown in national house prices, suggesting that removing tax privileges for wealthy migrants may have much smaller housing effects than public debate implies.”

Then second paragraph:

“Portugal is a useful case because the NHR was large, salient, and explicitly abolished as a housing-affordability measure. Using quarterly Eurostat house-price data in a cross-country comparison, I show that Portuguese prices did not decelerate after the policy change; instead, the data mostly reflect Portugal’s preexisting upward divergence from peers. Taken together, the evidence points to a broader lesson: even politically controversial migration-tax regimes may be too small, too localized, or too gradual in their housing-demand effects to move aggregate national price indices.”

That is a better AER-style setup: question first, world first, implications first.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper’s contribution is to show that abolishing Portugal’s preferential tax regime for foreign residents did not produce an observable decline in aggregate national house prices, suggesting that the housing effects of tax-induced high-income migration may be smaller than commonly claimed.

### Evaluation

#### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?
Only partly. The paper distinguishes itself from the migration-tax literature by focusing on downstream housing outcomes rather than migration flows, which is useful. But the differentiation is still thinner than it needs to be, because the paper risks sounding like: “here is a DiD on one policy change with a null result in aggregate prices.” That is not enough on its own.

The closest distinctiveness claim is:
1. major tax-migration regime,
2. policy reversal rather than introduction,
3. housing-market consequences rather than migration effects,
4. politically salient affordability motivation.

That is the right direction, but the introduction should make these dimensions more explicit and less defensive.

#### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or filling a gap in a LITERATURE?
At present it is too often framed as a literature gap: “downstream effects remain untested.” The stronger framing is world-facing: **governments are using or dismantling expat tax regimes partly to manage housing pressure; should anyone expect this to work?**

That is the real question. The literature-gap framing is serviceable but second-tier.

#### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?
Not cleanly. Right now, a smart economist might say: “It’s a paper on Portugal’s NHR abolition using cross-country house-price DiD, and basically it finds no effect but with bad pre-trends.” That is not a memorable contribution.

What you want them to say instead is: “It asks whether expat tax subsidies really matter for housing affordability, and the answer seems to be: much less than public rhetoric suggests.”

#### What would make this contribution bigger?
Several concrete possibilities:

1. **Move from national prices to local exposure.**  
   This is the biggest upgrade. If NHR beneficiaries were concentrated in Lisbon, Algarve, and Porto, then national HPI is too blunt. A municipality- or metro-level treatment-intensity design would make the question substantively sharper: did exposed places cool relative to less-exposed places?

2. **Look at transaction volumes/composition, not just prices.**  
   If prices don’t move because supply is inelastic or because the affected segment is thin, the adjustment may show up in transactions, high-end sales, foreign-buyer share, or listing times. That would transform the story from “no effect” to “the effect was on quantities/composition rather than aggregate prices.”

3. **Focus on luxury/localized market segments.**  
   A null in the national average is much more interesting if paired with evidence that the policy mattered in premium submarkets but did not diffuse to the broader market.

4. **Reframe the contribution around political economy of scapegoating.**  
   If the author cannot deepen the empirics, the paper could become more interesting by explicitly positioning NHR abolition as an example of a policy that is symbolically powerful but quantitatively weak in addressing affordability.

5. **Compare abolition to other demand-side housing policies.**  
   The broader claim becomes stronger if the paper places expat tax termination alongside golden visa restrictions, short-term rental regulation, and other attempts to cool demand.

As it stands, the contribution is plausible but too small for AER.

### How should the paper position itself relative to those neighbors?
Mostly **build on and connect**, not attack.

- Relative to the tax-mobility literature: “You showed these policies move people; I ask whether that mobility translates into housing-market effects.”
- Relative to housing-demand literature: “You showed migration and foreign demand can matter for housing; I test one concrete policy margin that governments actually use.”
- Relative to policy discourse: “This is a reality check on a popular affordability narrative.”

The paper should not claim to overturn migration-housing work. It is too aggregate and too case-specific for that. Its strength is narrower: a prominent policy reversal apparently did not budge aggregate national prices.

### Is the paper currently positioned too narrowly or too broadly?
Paradoxically, both.

- **Too narrowly** in the empirical setup: one country, one policy, one national outcome.
- **Too broadly** in some claims: “the broader lesson” language outruns what a noisy national panel can support.

It needs a tighter but more consequential lane: not “housing effects of migration policy changes” in general, but **whether tax subsidies for wealthy mobile residents are a meaningful housing-affordability lever**.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?
It needs more engagement with:
- foreign buyers / second-home demand,
- investor migration / golden visa programs,
- tourism and Airbnb as housing demand shocks,
- political economy of housing blame assignment,
- spatial equilibrium / segmented housing-market effects.

There is also a missing conversation with the growing literature on **salient but weak policy tools**—interventions that get political attention because they target unpopular groups, not because they move market fundamentals much.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?
Not quite. The current conversation is mostly: “here’s a clean-ish policy reversal related to tax competition and housing.” The more impactful conversation is:

**When governments try to improve affordability by restricting demand from politically visible outsiders, do they actually affect the market?**

That framing reaches housing, public finance, migration, and political economy simultaneously.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Portugal, like many destination countries, experienced a housing affordability crisis. The NHR regime became a symbol of a state policy that privileged foreign, often wealthier residents while locals faced rising prices.

### Tension
The public and policymakers often assert that tax-favored expats materially inflate housing prices. But this is not obvious. The beneficiaries may be too few, too geographically concentrated, too focused on a luxury segment, or too small relative to broader forces like supply constraints and tourism.

### Resolution
After Portugal ended the NHR regime, aggregate house prices did not slow relative to peers. The raw post-period divergence is positive, but much of that reflects preexisting trends; the substantive takeaway is not that abolition raised prices, but that it did not visibly cool the aggregate market.

### Implications
Policymakers should be cautious about expecting affordability gains from abolishing expat tax perks. If these policies affect housing at all, the effects may be local, segment-specific, gradual, or swamped by more fundamental drivers of prices.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
It has the **ingredients** of a narrative arc, but not a fully successful one. The paper currently reads somewhat like a competent empirical note organized around estimates, diagnostics, and caveats. The story is there, but it is not disciplined enough.

The main problem is that the paper oscillates among three stories:
1. a policy evaluation of NHR abolition,
2. a null result on housing prices,
3. a methodological confession that the DiD is not clean.

Those can coexist, but the current version lets the third crowd out the first two. This is not a referee memo, so I won’t dwell on identification. Strategically, the issue is that the introduction gives away too much uncertainty and too little stakes. It sounds like the paper wants credit for honesty more than attention for insight.

### What story should it be telling?
The paper should tell this story:

> “A politically popular housing narrative says that tax privileges for wealthy foreigners are a major cause of unaffordability. Portugal’s abolition of NHR offers a revealing test. In aggregate national prices, the answer is no: removing the tax break did not cool the market. That suggests policymakers may be targeting a highly visible but limited source of demand, and that housing affordability cannot be solved by symbolic demand-side gestures alone.”

That is a real story. Right now, the paper only half-commits to it.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?
“I’d lead with: Portugal abolished one of Europe’s most famous expat tax breaks to help housing affordability, and aggregate house prices did not slow.”

That is the dinner-party line.

### Would people lean in?
Initially, yes. The policy is salient and the result is mildly surprising. But the next question comes fast, and the paper is vulnerable there.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Almost certainly:

**“Okay, but did it matter in Lisbon or the Algarve, or just not in the national average?”**

That is the fatal follow-up, because the current paper cannot answer it. And that means the result, while interesting, does not yet feel decisive.

Other likely follow-ups:
- “How large was the affected demand share really?”
- “Was the policy too gradual because incumbents were grandfathered?”
- “Maybe prices didn’t move, but did transactions or luxury segments move?”
- “Is this really about tax competition or about scapegoating foreigners in a supply-constrained market?”

### Is the null result itself interesting?
Yes, **conditionally**. Nulls are interesting when:
1. the policy was large and salient,
2. theory or rhetoric strongly predicted an effect,
3. the paper can convincingly rule out first-order effects on the relevant margin.

This paper has (1) and partly (2), but only weakly has (3) because the outcome is broad and the likely effects are localized. So the null is potentially interesting, but at present it risks feeling like a null generated by an insensitive outcome measure.

The paper does make some case that “X doesn’t work” is valuable, but not forcefully enough. It should say more clearly:

- this was enacted as an affordability policy,
- the public debate treated NHR as a major culprit,
- if the effect is absent in aggregate data despite the policy’s salience, that itself is informative,
- but the likely action is in local/high-end margins.

That combination could make the null respectable. Without that, it feels too much like a failed attempt to detect something small with the wrong lens.

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

### What would make it read better?

#### 1. Shorten the methodological throat-clearing in the introduction
The introduction currently gets into coefficient values, p-values, event-study diagnostics, and placebo details too early and too numerically. For strategic positioning, that is a mistake. The first two pages should sell the question and headline result, not replicate the results section.

#### 2. Move most numeric detail out of paragraph two
The second paragraph should say:
- here is the design,
- here is the headline,
- here is the main interpretation.

It should not read like a table note.

#### 3. Collapse the three “contributions” paragraphs into one sharper positioning section
The contribution section is too segmented and somewhat generic. Replace “First, Second, Third” with a single concise paragraph that says:
- this paper connects tax-mobility policy to housing-market consequences,
- uses a salient policy reversal,
- and offers a cautionary lesson about the limits of outsider-targeted affordability policy.

#### 4. Bring the back-of-the-envelope calibration forward
This is arguably one of the most interesting parts of the paper. It explains why a large political controversy might have a small market effect. That logic belongs in the introduction or immediately after the main result, not deep in Discussion.

#### 5. Cut or demote the “methodological contribution” claim
The claim that the paper contributes “methodologically” by using a cross-country panel is not persuasive and weakens the framing. It sounds like compensation for the fact that the design is blunt. Better to be modest and substantive.

#### 6. Put the strongest substantive interpretation in the main results
The paper’s best line is essentially: “the termination did not interrupt Portugal’s preexisting momentum.” That should be foregrounded immediately after the event study figure/table.

#### 7. Tighten the conclusion
The conclusion mostly summarizes. It should end on a sharper implication:
- If governments want affordability relief, target supply constraints or large demand margins, not just politically salient foreign tax niches.
That is more memorable.

### Is the paper front-loaded with the good stuff?
Partly, but not optimally. The good substantive hook is front-loaded; the best interpretive content is not. The calibration and “why no aggregate effect is plausible” should appear much earlier.

### Are there results buried in robustness that belong in the main text?
Yes:
- the trend-adjusted attenuation,
- the placebo logic,
- and especially any evidence or discussion suggesting the effect is too small for aggregate prices but potentially relevant for localized markets.

### Is the conclusion adding value?
Some, but not enough. It should do more than restate findings; it should crystalize the broader lesson about policy salience versus quantitative relevance.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

Be honest: in current form, this is **not close** to an AER paper. The main gap is not only framing. It is also scope and ambition.

### What is the gap?

#### Framing problem
Yes. The paper needs a much sharper world-question framing and less “I ran a DiD on Portugal” energy.

#### Scope problem
Definitely. AER would want more than a national null from a single policy reversal. The obvious missing margin is local exposure or market segmentation.

#### Novelty problem
Somewhat. The basic exercise is not novel enough on its own. “Tax change + housing prices + DiD + null” is not top-journal novelty absent a deeper conceptual payoff.

#### Ambition problem
Yes. The paper is competent but safe. It asks the smallest feasible version of the question and gets the unsurprising answer that aggregate national prices are hard to move. AER papers usually either:
- answer the question more decisively,
- generalize more broadly,
- or reveal a surprising mechanism.

This paper currently does none of those at the necessary level.

### What would excite the top 10 people in this field?
One of the following:

1. **A local treatment-intensity design** using municipality-level exposure to NHR beneficiaries.
2. **A market-segmentation paper** showing no effect on average prices but meaningful effects in luxury, foreign-buyer, or high-exposure markets.
3. **A broader comparative paper** across countries with expat tax regimes or similar policy reversals.
4. **A strong political-economy angle** showing that governments target visible outsiders even when quantitative effects on affordability are small.

### Single most impactful advice
If the author can change only one thing:

**Get subnational exposure data and reframe the paper around whether expat tax incentives affect the specific local housing markets where beneficiaries actually buy, rather than national average prices.**

That would transform the paper from “aggregate null with caveats” into a serious contribution.
