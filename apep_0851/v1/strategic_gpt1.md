## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether abolishing Portugal’s highly visible tax break for foreign residents reduced house prices, as politicians and commentators claimed it would. Using cross-country house price data around the 2023 termination of the Non-Habitual Resident regime, the paper’s substantive message is that aggregate housing prices did not slow; if anything, Portugal kept outpacing peers, suggesting that expat tax incentives may matter much less for national housing prices than public debate assumes.

A busy economist should care because this is really a paper about a broader world question: when governments target affluent mobile foreigners to relieve housing pressure, do housing markets actually notice? That is a policy-relevant question at the intersection of tax competition, migration, and housing affordability.

**Does the paper articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Partly, but not well enough. The opening is promising, but the second paragraph immediately dives into coefficients, significance levels, and pre-trends. That is too technical, too fast, and it obscures the real hook. The paper’s most interesting feature is not “a DiD coefficient of 0.311”; it is that a politically salient anti-expat housing reform appears not to have moved aggregate prices at all.

**What the first two paragraphs should say instead:**

> Across Europe, policymakers increasingly blame wealthy foreign residents for local housing crises. Portugal became the clearest test case: in 2023 it abolished the Non-Habitual Resident tax regime, one of Europe’s most generous tax breaks for foreign residents, explicitly arguing that the policy had fueled unaffordable housing. Did ending this “expat subsidy” actually cool the housing market?
>
> This paper shows that, in aggregate national data, the answer appears to be no. Portugal’s house prices did not decelerate relative to comparable European countries after the reform; instead, they continued along the country’s pre-existing upward trajectory. The broader lesson is that even politically salient tax incentives for affluent migrants may be too small, too localized, or too gradual in their effects to move national housing prices against larger forces such as supply constraints, tourism, and macro housing momentum.

That is the paper’s real pitch. Lead with the world question and the surprising substantive takeaway, not the regression output.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution in one sentence:**  
The paper argues that terminating Portugal’s preferential tax regime for foreign residents did not measurably reduce aggregate house prices, implying that expat-targeted tax policy may have limited effects on national housing affordability.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?
Not yet. The introduction cites broad literatures on tax competition, housing, and migration, but the differentiation is still hazy. Right now the contribution reads like: “existing work shows taxes affect migration; I look at housing.” That is directionally right, but too generic. The author needs to identify the exact missing link:

- We know preferential tax regimes can attract high-income or high-skill migrants.
- We know foreign demand is often blamed for house price pressure.
- We do **not** know whether removing such tax incentives actually cools housing markets at the aggregate level.

That is the new margin. The paper should present itself as the first evidence on the **housing incidence of preferential tax regimes for mobile affluent residents**, not just another policy evaluation.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a gap in a literature?
It is mixed, but too often framed as filling a literature gap. The stronger frame is plainly about the world:

- **Strong framing:** “Do tax breaks for affluent foreigners meaningfully raise housing prices, and can repealing them improve affordability?”
- **Weak framing:** “The downstream housing effects of preferential tax regimes remain underexplored.”

The introduction currently leans too much on the second. AER wants the first.

### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what’s new?
At present, they could say something like: “It’s a DiD paper on Portugal’s NHR repeal and housing prices, and it mostly finds no aggregate effect.” That is not bad, but it is still too close to “another DiD paper about X.”

What they should be able to say is: “It tests one of the most common claims in European housing politics—that tax-favored rich foreigners bid up prices—and finds that ending the policy didn’t cool national house prices.” That version is memorable.

### What would make this contribution bigger?
Several possibilities:

1. **Local rather than national housing outcomes.**  
   The paper itself admits the likely action is in Lisbon, Algarve, and Porto. National HPI is a blunt object. If the author wants this to be a top-field contribution, the most obvious path is to move from aggregate national prices to subnational treated markets where NHR exposure was concentrated.

2. **Transactions, composition, or segment-level outcomes.**  
   If prices do not move, perhaps transaction volumes, luxury segment activity, foreign-buyer shares, or listing times do. “No aggregate price effect, but clear volume/composition effects” would be a much richer contribution.

3. **A broader framing around policy overreach.**  
   The paper could become bigger if it is not just about Portugal, but about why governments overestimate the housing role of affluent foreign inflows relative to structural supply constraints. The current discussion hints at this but does not fully claim it.

4. **A more comparative angle.**  
   If the author can connect Portugal to similar European anti-foreign-buyer or anti-expat policies, the paper becomes less a one-off case study and more a lesson about a class of interventions.

As written, the contribution is interesting but too narrow and too aggregate to feel field-defining.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Across many housing-stressed places, policymakers and voters believe wealthy foreigners or tax-favored expats are a major driver of unaffordability. Portugal’s NHR regime became an emblematic case because it was large, visible, and politically contested.

### Tension
There is a sharp disconnect between political rhetoric and economic uncertainty. Preferential tax regimes may attract affluent residents, but it is unclear whether that demand is quantitatively large enough to move aggregate housing prices. Ending such a regime is therefore a clean public test of a widely repeated claim.

### Resolution
After Portugal terminated NHR, aggregate house prices did not slow relative to peer countries. The market kept rising along its prior trajectory, suggesting no visible aggregate cooling effect.

### Implications
Policymakers may be overestimating how much housing affordability can be improved by targeting expat tax incentives alone. If foreign-affluent demand is small, localized, or slow-moving, national price relief requires attention to supply and other larger market forces.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
It has the ingredients, but the story is not yet fully disciplined. The paper currently feels like:

- a politically interesting setup,
- followed by a result,
- followed by a long disclaimer that the design cannot cleanly isolate causality,
- followed by a plausible calibration.

That makes the paper read as somewhat defensive and somewhat like a collection of exercises around a null claim.

### What story should it be telling?
The story should be:

1. Policymakers blamed tax-favored foreigners for rising housing costs.
2. Portugal gave us a real-world test by abolishing the flagship policy.
3. Aggregate prices did not cool.
4. Therefore, even salient migration-tax policies may have surprisingly limited aggregate housing incidence.

That is a coherent AER-style narrative. Right now the paper lets the econometric caveats occupy too much narrative space relative to the substantive lesson.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?
“Portugal abolished one of Europe’s most famous tax breaks for foreign residents to relieve housing pressure, and national house prices did not slow.”

That is a good lead.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
They would lean in initially, because the policy is concrete and the claim is salient. But the next question comes quickly: “Is that because the policy truly didn’t matter, or because the data are too aggregate to see where it mattered?” If the speaker cannot answer that, attention drops.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Almost certainly:  
**“What happened in Lisbon and the Algarve?”**

That is the paper’s central vulnerability in terms of strategic positioning, quite apart from econometrics. The paper asks a question that is inherently spatially concentrated and answers it with a national index. That may still be publishable somewhere, but it limits how far the result can travel.

### If the findings are null or modest: is the null itself interesting?
Yes, potentially very much so. The null is interesting because the policy was politically prominent and explicitly justified on housing grounds. Learning that such a salient repeal did not cool aggregate prices is useful. But the author needs to lean harder into why this null matters:

- It disciplines public claims about who drives housing crises.
- It distinguishes visible scapegoats from quantitatively important forces.
- It suggests housing policy may be chasing small demand margins while neglecting larger structural constraints.

At present, the null is interesting, but the paper does not extract the full intellectual value from it. It still reads a bit like “we looked and didn’t find much” rather than “here is a revealing failure of a politically popular housing theory.”

4. **Promote the calibration / demand-share argument.**

5. **Demote or delete the “methodological contribution” language.**  
   “Contributes methodologically” via a cross-country panel is not convincing at AER level and invites the wrong standard of evaluation.

6. **Rework the conclusion.**  
   The current conclusion mostly summarizes. It should instead do one thing: generalize the lesson. Why should economists update their beliefs about housing politics and tax policy from this case?

### Is the paper front-loaded with the good stuff?
Yes and no. The question is front-loaded, which helps. But the real substantive value—the interpretation that NHR likely represented too small a share of demand to move aggregate prices—is buried later. That should come much sooner.

### Are there results buried in robustness that should be in the main results?
Yes, conceptually: the placebo-timing result and the trend-adjusted attenuation are central to the narrative, not just robustness. They are crucial for interpreting the baseline. They should be presented as part of the main story, not as auxiliary checks.

### Is the conclusion adding value?
Only modestly. It summarizes sensibly, but it does not elevate the paper. It should end on the broader proposition: politically salient anti-outsider housing policies may have weak aggregate price effects because they target a small slice of demand.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The gap is mainly a **scope and ambition problem**, with some framing issues.

### Framing problem?
Yes, somewhat. The paper undersells the world question and oversells the specification. It should be framed as a test of a widely believed housing mechanism, not as a country-case DiD with careful caveats.

### Scope problem?
Yes, strongly. This is the main issue. The paper’s core result is at the national level, but the mechanism is likely local and segmented. For a top general-interest journal, the paper needs either:
- subnational exposure-based evidence,
- richer outcomes beyond aggregate prices,
- or broader comparative evidence across similar policies/countries.

### Novelty problem?
Moderate. The exact setting is novel enough. But the empirical object—one-country policy change, DiD, aggregate housing outcome—is not intrinsically novel enough for AER unless it yields a broader, sharper lesson.

### Ambition problem?
Yes. The paper is competent and surprisingly candid, but it is also safe and small. It answers the narrowest version of the question with the coarsest plausible data. That may be enough for a field journal note; it is not yet enough for a flagship general-interest paper.

### The single most impactful piece of advice
**Rebuild the paper around subnational or segment-specific housing exposure to NHR beneficiaries, so the paper tests where the policy should have mattered rather than where aggregate noise is largest.**

If the author can only change one thing, that is the thing. If such data are impossible, then the fallback is to radically reframe the paper as a broader lesson about the limits of politically salient demand-side housing policies and support that claim with more comparative or non-price evidence.
