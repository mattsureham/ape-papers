**SUBJECT:** Strategic Positioning – "The Integration Premium: Thirty-Five Years of Mandated Ethnic Mixing and Housing Prices in Singapore"

---

### 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
Can a government "legislate away" racial prejudice through housing? This paper uses Singapore’s unique 35-year experiment with mandatory ethnic quotas to test the "contact hypothesis" in a high-stakes market. It asks whether decades of forced mixing have narrowed the price gap between majority and minority neighborhoods, or if ethnic preferences remain a fixed feature of urban sorting.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this well, specifically in the third paragraph of the introduction. However, the first two paragraphs are a bit "policy-heavy."
**The pitch it should have:** "Economists have long debated whether ethnic segregation is a fixed equilibrium driven by immutable preferences or a malleable state that policy can disrupt. We exploit the world’s most aggressive residential integration policy—Singapore’s EIP, which has mandated ethnic mixing since 1989—to measure the long-run evolution of the 'minority price discount.' By analyzing over 200,000 transactions, we provide a rare look at whether 35 years of forced contact actually changes the market's valuation of diversity."

### 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
The paper identifies a significant (33%) decline in the minority neighborhood price penalty over the last decade, providing market-level evidence that mandated integration can gradually shift ethnic preferences.

*   **Differentiation:** It differentiates itself from Wong (2013) by using modern administrative data and, crucially, by testing *temporal convergence* which Wong could not do.
*   **Framing:** It is currently framed as a "policy evaluation" of the EIP. To be AER-quality, it needs to be framed as a fundamental test of the **Contact Hypothesis vs. Schelling Sorting**.
*   **The "Smart Economist" Test:** A reader would likely say, "It’s a paper showing that Singapore’s quotas are slowly working." To avoid being "just another DiD/Hedonic paper," it needs to emphasize the *duration* (35 years) as a unique window into preference formation.
*   **Bigger Contribution:** The paper would be stronger if it could decompose the "constraint" effect from the "preference" effect more cleanly. Are prices converging because people *like* living together more, or because the quotas have reached a steady state where they no longer bind as painfully?

### 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
*   **Neighbors:** Wong (2013, ReStud) is the direct predecessor. Bayer et al. (2007) and Card et al. (2008) on sorting/tipping. Chetty et al. (2016) on neighborhood effects.
*   **Positioning:** It should "build on" Wong but "challenge" the static view of preferences in the US-centric sorting literature (Schelling/Bayer).
*   **Missing Conversations:** The paper needs to engage more with the **Social Identity** literature (e.g., Akerlof and Kranton) or **Cultural Transmission** (Bisin and Verdier). Is the "Integration Premium" a change in culture or just a change in market search frictions?

### 4. NARRATIVE ARC
*   **Setup:** Large-scale mandatory integration in Singapore.
*   **Tension:** If preferences are immutable, the price discount for minority areas should persist forever as a "tax" on those neighborhoods. If contact works, the discount should vanish.
*   **Resolution:** The discount is shrinking, but it’s still massive (10-15%).
*   **Implications:** Policy works, but the "half-life" of prejudice is measured in generations, not years.
*   **Evaluation:** The arc is clear. It avoids the "collection of results" trap by centering everything on the convergence test.

### 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
*   **The Lead Fact:** "After 35 years of forced integration, living in a minority neighborhood still costs you 12% of your home value."
*   **Reaction:** People will lean in because it suggests that even the "gold standard" of integration policy is struggling to erase the ethnic gradient.
*   **Follow-up:** "Is it actually about race, or is it about school quality?" (The paper needs a tighter answer to the school/amenity exclusion in Section 4.4).

### 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
*   **Front-loading:** The RDD results (Section 5.3) are currently an afterthought. Since the RDD offers "sharper identification," it should be elevated.
*   **The "2026" Data:** The paper lists data through 2026. This is a red flag for a "current" paper unless it's using projections or I've misread the date. (Assuming 2024 is the true current date).
*   **Appendix:** The "Standardized Effect Sizes" (Table 5) are excellent; move the core of this logic into the main text to help the reader understand the "economic magnitude."

### 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The gap is the **"Preference vs. Constraint"** problem. If the minority discount is shrinking, is it because Singaporeans are becoming more tolerant (Allport), or because the Chinese majority is simply running out of "Chinese-only" blocks to buy into, forcing them into minority-heavy blocks?

**Single Biggest Advice:** Use the RDD at the 10% Indian threshold more aggressively to show that the *discontinuity itself* is shrinking over time. If the "jump" at the quota is getting smaller, that is the "smoking gun" for preference change rather than just shifting city-wide demographics.
