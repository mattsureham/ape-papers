**Idea Fidelity**  
The paper implements the Florida quota liquor license lottery idea outlined in the manifest. It focuses on the population-based threshold to construct exogenous entitlement variation and links it to county-level alcohol employment outcomes, as promised. However, the lottery component only appears indirectly: while the manifest envisioned exploiting within-county randomization across applicants, the submitted paper relies solely on mechanically computed license entitlements and never uses applicant-level lottery winners versus losers. This means the promised two-stage identification (population threshold for licenses entering a county and lottery for who receives them) is only partially realized. **Summary**  
The paper studies Florida’s quota liquor license system, exploiting statutory population thresholds to identify when extra full-liquor licenses become available to counties. Using county-year QCEW data, the author finds that marginal license entitlements do not increase drinking-place employment or establishments but do compress average weekly wages. A complementary RDD corroborates the null employment effect, while robustness checks (binary treatment, population controls, cumulative stock) reinforce the view that population growth—not license availability—drives employment changes.

**Essential Points**  
1. **Treatment measurement conflates entitlement with actual license activity.** The identified “new licenses” are mechanically derived entitlement increments, not observed lotteries, issuances, or openings. If counties take time to claim licenses or winners do not immediately open establishments, the estimated β is an intention-to-treat on entitlement—not on new outlets. This distinction matters for interpretation (rent redistribution vs. output creation). The authors should either present evidence linking entitlement to actual license issuance/openings (e.g., DABT winners per year per county, secondary market transactions, or timing of establishment openings) or explicitly frame the estimand accordingly and discuss the size of the compliance issue.

2. **Population growth confounding is inadequately addressed.** The main concern—counties crossing thresholds because of faster population growth—pervades the paper. While the robustness exercise adds population controls and shows the license coefficient flips sign, the fact that the placebo (restaurant employment) is strongly negative suggests the identifying variation is dominated by growth dynamics. The identifying assumption that threshold crossings, conditional on fixed effects, are as good as random is not credibly defended. The paper needs to present stronger evidence that threshold crossings contain exogenous variation separate from linear population trends: for instance, exploit the deterministic 7,500 rule in an event-study around thresholds with flexible controls for population or growth, or show that counties crossing thresholds are not systematically different in pre-trends (beyond the McCrary and current year balance tests). Without this, the null result could simply reflect the fact that larger, faster-growing counties both gain licenses and add restaurant/bar employment through other channels.

3. **Absence of the promised lottery-based identification limits causal credibility.** The manifest’s second stage—comparing lottery winners and losers—was supposed to provide within-county randomization and ensure the marginal entrant is randomly assigned. The paper fails to implement this stage, forfeiting the clearest source of exogenous variation. Relying solely on the threshold ignores the opportunity to verify that, among applicants, winners indeed increase local employment relative to losers. Without this, the paper leans on the weaker assumption that the timing of crossings is exogenous rather than on the stronger (and more novel) claim of randomized winners. The authors should either incorporate the lottery data or explain why doing so is infeasible, and discuss the implications for identification.

If additional essential issues exist beyond these three, I would recommend rejecting the paper until they are addressed.

**Suggestions**  
- **Clarify the estimand and the path from entitlement to outcomes.** Given the existing data, explicitly state that the estimates are at the entitlement level (counties earning the right to additional licenses) and not at the winner/establishment level. Provide descriptive evidence (perhaps from a subset of counties) showing how many entitled licenses are actually claimed, the lag between entitlement and issuance, and how many new establishments open per license. If possible, obtain DABT annual award lists or secondary-market transaction data to link entitlement to realized entry; if not, discuss in the limitations that the estimates capture a latent effect of marginal entry rights rather than realized openings.

- **Strengthen the identification strategy around the threshold.** The power of the threshold-based experiment is currently undermined by the correlation with population growth. Consider an event-study that compares counties just below and just above threshold crossings over several years, controlling for county-specific trends, to show that the treatment effect does not simply reflect growth-driven demand. Alternatively, exploit the modular distance to the threshold in a difference-in-differences setup that includes flexible controls for population (e.g., cubic splines) or interact the threshold indicator with linear population trends to isolate the non-linear jump. Presenting graphical evidence (e.g., plots of drinking-place employment against the modular distance) would help assess whether the threshold generates a discontinuity beyond growth.

- **Revisit the placebo strategy.** The restaurant employment placebo suggests that new license entitlements are picking up broader county-level growth. To interpret the null as evidence against job creation by marginal entrants, the authors need to demonstrate that the quota mechanism, rather than macro growth, is driving the variation. One approach is to instrument the entitlement variable with the threshold crossing indicator, which should be exogenous conditional on smooth covariates; this two-stage approach would help isolate the discontinuous component. Another possibility is to use non-quota license counts (e.g., beer-and-wine or SRX licenses) as falsification outcomes or to show that their coefficients are null, buttressing the claim that the behavior is specific to the quota mechanism.

- **Leverage county heterogeneity.** The manifest emphasized county lotteries and the variation afforded by the number of applicants (e.g., Miami-Dade vs. rural counties). Consider splitting the sample or estimating heterogenous effects: do counties with more intense lottery competition (more applicants per license) show different employment/wage responses? Does the wage effect differ in smaller vs. larger counties, or before/after the onset of a treatment when a county first crosses a threshold? This would not only add nuance but also bolster the narrative about labor-market competition.

- **Explain the missing welfare implications or health outcomes.** The introduction mentions DUI, arrests, and health, but the paper never returns to these outcomes. If data limitations preclude their use, briefly note this in the introduction or institutional background to avoid raising expectations. Alternatively, if such data are accessible, consider adding even preliminary regressions showing whether the quota threshold affects DUI arrests or liquor-related hospital visits—this would align the paper more closely with the manifest’s broader public health framing.

- **Discuss policy implications with care.** The concluding claim that quotas “destroy public surplus” rests on the assumption that zero employment gain implies pure rent creation. Yet if the entitlement mainly reallocates scarce workforce hours or stimulates larger establishments without hiring more workers, the welfare effect may be more complex (e.g., better consumer choice or quality). Adding a short paragraph that situates the null in the broader literature on entry barriers (e.g., explaining why zero employment gain does not rule out other benefits/costs) would temper overstatement and connect more thoroughly to past work such as Seim & Waldfogel (2013).

- **Provide more detail on the wage compression mechanism.** The wage result is the one statistically significant finding. It would be helpful to explore whether this effect persists across different definitions (e.g., median wages) or whether it is driven by certain counties (urban vs. rural). Presenting a simple theoretical or graphical framework explaining why new entrants would depress wages without changing employment would tie the empirical finding to the narrative.

- **Address power concerns explicitly.** The RDD is underpowered (as acknowledged), and the FE estimates have wide confidence intervals. Reporting minimum detectable effects or conducting a formal power analysis (especially for the main employment outcome) would help readers interpret the null. If the study is simply underpowered to detect small effects (say, 5–10 workers per license), this should be clearly stated, along with the implications for policy.

These suggestions aim to help the authors clarify the causal claims, strengthen the identification, and deliver a narrative that more closely aligns with the original vision of exploiting Florida's quota lottery.
