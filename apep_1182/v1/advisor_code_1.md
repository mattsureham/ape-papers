**Idea Fidelity**

The submitted paper partially pursues the idea articulated in the manifest, but it does not deliver the core identification strategy promised there. The manuscript indeed exploits HyADS/HYSPLIT to construct county-level coal-attributable PM$_{2.5}$ and studies its impact on labor-market outcomes. However, the manifest emphasized using the physics-based HyADS prediction as an instrument for observed pollution in a causal IV framework (building on Henneman et al. and the physics-based dispersion identification). In the submitted version, HyADS exposure enters directly as the explanatory variable in reduced-form panel regressions; no instrumental-variable strategy is implemented, nor are the additional data sources (e.g., EPA monitors) or outcomes (establishment births/deaths) mentioned in the manifest leveraged. Thus the paper falls short of the original aspiration to elevate the identification strategy to a full physics-based IV. If the intent was instead to treat HyADS as “observed” exposure, the manifest should have been updated accordingly.

**Summary**

The paper estimates the relationship between HyADS-modeled coal-attributable PM$_{2.5}$ and county-level employment and wages over 2014–2019. Using county and year fixed effects (and a first-differenced specification), the author finds a negative elasticity of employment with respect to coal PM$_{2.5}$ (about –0.012 to –0.024) concentrated in rural counties, while wages remain unchanged. The leading interpretation is a spatial-equilibrium story: pollution deters workers from locating downwind, reducing employment without affecting wages.

**Essential Points**

1. **Identification of Causal Effects is Unclear.** The HyADS measure is a model-based prediction rather than an exogenous instrument, and the paper treats it as the “treatment” in an OLS panel regression. This raises concerns that the variation in HyADS exposure may still be correlated with local economic shocks—especially if plant-level emission decisions are responsive to regional factors correlated with employment (e.g., local demand for electricity, regulatory flexibility, or complementary industry declines). Without additional assumptions or an IV strategy, the estimates cannot be interpreted as causal. The paper therefore needs to either (a) explicitly argue and provide evidence that HyADS’s modeled exposures are as-good-as-random (e.g., by showing plausibly exogenous variation in plant emissions after controlling for major confounders) or (b) implement the promised IV strategy with HyADS as an instrument for observed PM$_{2.5}$ and demonstrate the relevance and exclusion restrictions.

2. **Limited Time Window and Outcome Harmonization.** The empirical sample is restricted to 2014–2019 despite the manifest promising 1999–2020 coverage and the rich history of coal decline. This short window may amplify concerns about regional trends and state-level shocks, as seen with the attenuation when adding state×year fixed effects. Extending the labor data backward (QCEW is available pre-2014) would substantially increase identifying variation and allow for event-study diagnostics around major regulatory milestones (e.g., CSAPR implementation). Without it, it is hard to distinguish pollution effects from broader restructurings tied to coal retirements.

3. **Mechanism Evidence is Thin.** The claim that pollution affects employment via worker sorting (extensive margin) rather than productivity is based on null wage effects alone. Yet wages per worker are an imperfect measure of firm-level labor productivity, and the null finding could reflect measurement error or insufficient variation. The paper should provide direct evidence on migration, labor-force participation, or establishment entry/exit to substantiate the spatial equilibrium channel, especially since the policy punchline hinges on renters/owners moving away from polluted areas.

**Suggestions**

- **Implement the Physics-Based IV or Articulate a Credible Reduced-Form Strategy.** The manifest strongly featured HyADS as an IV built on atmospheric transport physics; the paper should realize that strategy. One way is to combine HyADS (which predicts coal-attributable PM$_{2.5}$ based on emissions and transport) with observed PM$_{2.5}$ from EPA monitors in a two-stage least squares design: first stage regresses observed county PM$_{2.5}$ on coal-specific HyADS exposure (potentially interacted with plant-level regulation timing), and the second stage uses the fitted values to predict employment. This would leverage the exogenous variation in upwind plant activity while still measuring the actual pollutant intake. Alternatively, if HyADS is treated as the treatment variable, provide a much stronger justification for its exogeneity, such as showing that changes in HyADS exposure are orthogonal to local economic indicators once controlling for major confounders, and document that plant emissions are not responsive to local employment shocks through placebo tests (e.g., using future employment, industry-specific outcomes that should be unaffected, or lead covariates). In either case, explicitly discuss the exclusion restriction and any necessary assumptions.

- **Broaden the Temporal Coverage.** Extend the sample back to at least the early 2000s, ideally 1999, to align with the manifest’s promise and to capture the bulk of coal retirements. QCEW provides annual employment and wage data earlier than 2014. A longer panel would allow the inclusion of pre-trends, better control for local linear trends, and potentially event studies around CSAPR (2011) or MATS (2012). This would also increase the variation exploited by the first-differenced estimator, improving statistical precision and robustness checks (e.g., estimating dynamic leads and lags).

- **Enhance Mechanism Evidence.** To support the spatial-equilibrium explanation, include outcomes such as county-level population, migration flows, labor-force participation, and establishment entry/exit (CBP contains births/deaths). Showing that population or net migration responds to pollution, while wages remain flat, would make the “worker sorting” story more convincing. Additionally, consider heterogeneity by housing prices/rental markets or amenities to see if pollution-induced disamenities drive location choices differently across regions.

- **Address Regional Trends with Flexible Controls.** The attenuation of the employment coefficient when adding state×year fixed effects suggests some variation is driven by regional shocks. To address this, the paper could (i) interact region dummies with year trends, (ii) control for observable time-varying covariates such as natural gas prices, electricity demand growth, or coal plant retirements within the region, and (iii) use more granular fixed effects (e.g., commuting zone×year) if feasible. Also consider adding plant-level controls (capacity utilization, dual-fuel capability) if they can be mapped to downwind counties.

- **Clarify the Interpretation of HyADS Exposure.** Since HyADS is a model output rather than an observed concentration, carefully explain how it maps to actual exposure and what assumptions are needed for causal interpretation. Consider presenting validation exercises showing that HyADS correlates with observed PM$_{2.5}$ even after controlling for county characteristics, and discuss whether HyADS predicts only coal-attributable PM$_{2.5}$ or also captures broader pollution patterns that may confound the analysis. If measurement error is a concern, consider using errors-in-variables correction or discussing attenuation bias.

- **Explore Dose–Response and Threshold Effects.** The elasticity estimates are relatively small. Presenting nonlinear specifications (e.g., dividing counties into quantiles of exposure or using splines) could reveal whether there are thresholds beyond which pollution has stronger effects. This also offers intuition for policymakers about which counties benefit most from further coal reductions.

- **Strengthen External Validity and Policy Implications.** The paper’s central policy message is that coal retirements yield “hidden employment dividends.” To cement this claim, consider a back-of-the-envelope welfare calculation translating the estimated elasticities into employment changes for high-exposure counties, perhaps comparing to the mortality benefits estimated in the Henneman literature. Additionally, discuss the broader implications for interstate pollution regulation (e.g., how much employment loss could be prevented through interstate compensation or stricter transport controls).

- **Improve Presentation of Robustness.** The paper currently reports limited robustness checks. Adding placebo tests (e.g., using pollution from unrelated sources or future employment as an outcome), alternative clustering (e.g., county), and reporting first-stage F-statistics (if implementing IV) would increase confidence. Also, given that the HyADS panel is generated from plant-level data, consider randomization inference or permutation tests that resample plant emissions to assess how much of the variation is driven by a small set of coal units.

- **Address Potential Spillovers and Interference.** Since pollution moves across borders, counties near high-emitting plants may both affect and be affected by neighbors. Discuss how the model accounts for spatial spillovers—is there cross-border interference beyond what HyADS captures? A spatial lag model or including neighboring counties’ exposure could test whether the estimated effects are localized or distributed.

Implementing these suggestions would significantly enhance the credibility of the causal claims and the contribution to the literature on pollution, labor markets, and energy transitions.
