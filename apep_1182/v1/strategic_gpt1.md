## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether pollution from coal plants depresses employment not just near the plant, but in the rural counties hundreds of miles downwind that receive the pollution. Using a physics-based model that links emissions from individual coal units to county-level exposure, it argues that the decline of US coal pollution may have delivered a previously overlooked labor-market dividend in exposed rural places.

Why should a busy economist care? Because if pollution transport affects where people work and firms locate, then the benefits of environmental regulation are not just health gains but also spatial economic gains—and those gains may accrue far from the regulated source.

### Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Not quite. The current introduction is competent, but it leads with atmospheric transport mechanics and a literature gap rather than a sharp, memorable economic question. The phrase “downwind tax” is potentially strong, but the introduction does not fully cash it out as a major economic idea. The first two paragraphs should more directly tell the reader: coal pollution is an interregional distortion; the key question is whether it suppresses labor-market activity in downwind places; and the paper’s novelty is source-specific, transport-based exposure linked to labor outcomes.

### The pitch the paper should have

“Coal plants impose costs far beyond the counties that host them: most of their pollution is carried downwind to other communities. This paper asks whether that transported pollution acts like a tax on local economic activity, reducing employment in the counties that receive it. Combining a plant-to-county atmospheric transport model with county labor-market data, I show that declines in coal-attributable PM\(_{2.5}\) are associated with higher employment—especially in rural counties—suggesting that the coal transition generated not only health benefits but also labor-market gains in places far from the source.”

That is the story. It is more about the world, less about “there is a gap in the literature.”

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper’s contribution is to show, using source-specific and transport-based exposure from coal plants, that declines in downwind coal pollution are associated with higher county employment—especially in rural areas—even when wages do not respond.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?

Only partially. Right now, the paper says it contributes to air pollution and health, productivity, spatial equilibrium, and coal retirement policy. That is too diffuse relative to what the actual result is. The closest contrast is not with broad health papers, but with:

1. papers on air pollution and mortality,
2. papers on air pollution and worker productivity/labor supply,
3. papers on environmental amenities and spatial equilibrium.

The paper needs to more crisply say: existing work mostly studies health or within-job productivity; this paper studies local employment and economic geography using source-specific transported exposure. That’s the distinctive move.

But it still risks sounding like “another reduced-form pollution paper with county panel regressions,” because the introduction does not sufficiently foreground why *coal transport* and *downwind labor markets* are a new object.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or filling a gap in a LITERATURE?

Mixed, leaning too much toward literature-gap framing. The stronger framing is about the world:

- Coal pollution crosses jurisdictional boundaries.
- Those externalities may distort the geography of economic activity.
- The US coal decline may therefore have reshaped labor markets in downwind rural America.

That is a world question. The introduction should stay there.

### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what's new?

At present, they might say: “It’s a county-panel paper linking modeled coal PM to employment, with bigger effects in rural areas.” That is not yet enough. You want them to say: “It’s about whether interstate pollution transport from coal plants acts like a hidden tax on downwind local economies, and it uses plant-to-county dispersion data to measure that.”

Right now the paper has novelty in the data and potentially in the framing, but the introduction doesn’t make either feel indispensable.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Specific ways to make it bigger:

- **Shift from average county employment to spatial reallocation outcomes.** Migration, establishment entry/exit, business formation, or sectoral composition would make the “where economic activity locates” claim much more powerful.
- **Exploit sectoral heterogeneity.** If the main effect is in outdoor, tourism/amenity-sensitive, or pollution-sensitive sectors, the story becomes much sharper.
- **Make the “downwind” aspect central.** A decomposition into host-county versus non-host/downwind counties would enlarge the policy significance.
- **Connect more directly to interregional incidence of environmental regulation.** Who gains when coal declines? Not just neighbors of plants, but distant receivers. That is a bigger policy point than “pollution affects employment.”
- **Use a more ambitious welfare framing.** If the claim is that coal retirements created labor-market gains in rural places, the paper should frame this as part of the regional incidence of the energy transition.

The biggest gain would come from outcomes that directly speak to reallocation rather than reduced-form employment levels alone.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Coal plants generate pollution that mostly harms downwind communities rather than host communities. The US has seen a massive decline in coal pollution due to regulation and fuel-market shifts.

### Tension

We know a lot about the mortality burden of pollution, but much less about whether transported pollution suppresses local economic activity in the places that receive it. If pollution changes where people want to live and where firms want to operate, then the coal transition may have had broader regional economic consequences than usually recognized.

### Resolution

Counties experiencing declines in coal-attributable PM\(_{2.5}\) see modest employment gains, concentrated in rural counties, with no parallel wage response.

### Implications

Environmental policy may generate labor-market benefits in downwind communities, implying that the gains from coal decline are geographically broader and economically richer than standard host-county or health-only analyses suggest.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

Serviceable, but not strong. It currently feels somewhat like a collection of plausible regressions attached to an interesting dataset. The atmosphere model is interesting; the employment finding is interesting; the rural heterogeneity is interesting. But they do not yet lock into a memorable story.

The missing story is:

- pollution transport creates an **interregional distortion**;
- the coal transition relaxed that distortion;
- the gains showed up in **downwind rural labor markets**.

That is the arc. Right now the paper instead reads as:
“Here is a source-specific pollution measure; here are county regressions; here are some coefficients; here is a spatial equilibrium interpretation.”

That is results-first, story-second.

### What story should it be telling?

It should be telling the story of **hidden regional beneficiaries of the coal transition**. Not just fewer deaths. Not just cleaner air. But better labor-market outcomes in places that were never part of the coal industry and may not even realize coal plants were affecting them.

That is vivid, intuitive, and policy-relevant.

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

### What fact would I lead with?

“I’d lead with: the collapse of US coal pollution appears to have raised employment in rural counties downwind of coal plants, meaning the energy transition may have created labor-market gains far from the plants themselves.”

That is the dinner-party line.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

Some would lean in—especially environmental, labor, urban, and public economists—but only if you lead with the geographic-incidence angle. If you lead with “I estimate an elasticity of employment with respect to coal PM\(_{2.5}\),” they will reach for their phones.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Most likely: “What exactly is the mechanism—workers move in, firms enter, or incumbent workers get healthier and work more?”  
And second: “Are the gains really downwind and outside the source regions, or are you picking up broader regional economic changes tied to coal decline?”

Those are exactly the questions the paper should anticipate in the framing, even before referees get into design.

### If the findings are modest: is that okay?

Yes, modest effects can still be interesting here because the object is new: source-specific transported pollution and county labor markets. But the paper must make the case that even modest annual employment elasticities, when applied to a 96% collapse in coal PM, imply meaningful cumulative reallocation.

Right now the paper half-makes this case. It should do it more confidently and more concretely. Otherwise the result risks feeling like a slightly noisy reduced-form exercise rather than a meaningful finding about the economy.

The null wage result can also be interesting, but only if it is used to sharpen the interpretation rather than merely noted. At present, it is suggestive but underdeveloped.
