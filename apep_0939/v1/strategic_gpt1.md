## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple, policy-relevant question: when Oklahoma sharply curtailed wastewater injection to stop induced earthquakes, did the regulation destroy local jobs in the communities most exposed to it? The answer, according to the paper, is no: a major environmental/safety regulation appears to have reduced seismic hazard without measurable employment losses, suggesting that some regulations aimed at risky byproducts may be much less costly than standard “jobs versus regulation” narratives imply.

That is a potentially good AER pitch. But the paper does **not** currently deliver it in the first two paragraphs as clearly or forcefully as it should. The introduction opens with vivid institutional detail, but the broader economic question emerges only gradually. The first two paragraphs should not read as “here is an Oklahoma episode and here is a missing literature niche.” They should read as “economists think environmental regulation often kills jobs; here is a clean and surprising case where a dramatic regulation may not have.”

### The pitch the paper should have

A stronger opening would say something like:

> Environmental regulation is often judged by a familiar tradeoff: safer environments at the cost of fewer jobs. But that tradeoff may depend critically on **what** regulation constrains. This paper studies Oklahoma’s response to induced earthquakes, where regulators sharply reduced wastewater injection volumes at disposal wells—an intervention that dramatically lowered seismic activity but, I show, had no detectable effect on local employment.
>
> The case matters beyond Oklahoma. Unlike classic pollution rules that raise the cost of production itself, seismicity regulation targeted the disposal of a byproduct of oil and gas production. This allows the paper to ask a broader question: when regulation restricts a dangerous ancillary activity rather than core output, can it deliver large risk reductions without the employment losses emphasized in the environmental-regulation literature?

That is the world-question. Only after that should the paper explain Oklahoma, the treatment waves, and the specific data.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:**  
The paper argues that Oklahoma’s earthquake-mitigation regulation—large, geographically targeted cuts in wastewater injection—reduced seismic risk without measurable local employment losses, suggesting that regulations aimed at byproduct disposal may have much smaller labor-market costs than regulations aimed at production.

This is a decent contribution, but right now it is only **partly** differentiated from neighboring literatures.

### Is it clearly differentiated from the closest papers?
Not enough. The paper says it is “the first” on employment effects of seismicity regulation, which may be true, but that is not enough for AER. “First” in a narrow niche is rarely decisive. The contribution needs sharper contrast against at least 3–4 literatures/papers:

1. **Environmental regulation and jobs**: Greenstone (2002), Walker (2013), Curtis (2018).
2. **Shale boom/local economic effects**: Feyrer, Mansur, and Sacerdote (2017); Bartik et al. (2019); Allcott and Keniston (2018).
3. **Induced seismicity / local impacts**: Muehlenbachs, Krupnick, and Noailly (2015) on housing; possibly seismology papers documenting the causal chain from injection to earthquakes.
4. Potentially **risk regulation** more broadly: cases where regulating an externality-producing margin does or does not meaningfully contract employment.

At present, the paper reads a little too much like: “there is no paper on this exact Oklahoma policy and employment, so I wrote one.” That is publishable somewhere, but it is not yet an AER contribution.

### World question or literature gap?
The paper moves between the two, but too often defaults to the weaker formulation: “the literature has not studied X.” The stronger framing is clearly available: **When does environmental regulation impose large employment costs, and when does it not?** Oklahoma is then a test case that reveals something general about regulation of byproducts versus regulation of production.

That world framing is much stronger.

### Could a smart economist explain what is new?
Right now, a smart economist might say:  
“It's a DiD on Oklahoma injection caps showing no employment effect.”

That is not enough. What you want them to say is:  
“It shows that a very salient environmental/safety regulation can have near-zero labor-market costs when it targets disposal rather than production, which changes how we think about the jobs-regulation tradeoff.”

The paper is not yet making that leap cleanly.

### What would make the contribution bigger?
Most importantly: **make the paper about the economics of regulating byproducts, not just about Oklahoma jobs**.

Specific ways to make it bigger:
- **Different framing:** Contrast “regulation of production” vs. “regulation of disposal/byproducts” as the conceptual distinction.
- **Different outcomes:** If possible, connect labor nulls to more economically meaningful margins—establishments, earnings, reallocation across sectors, commuting, or business dynamism. Right now “no employment effect” risks feeling narrow and incomplete.
- **Better mechanism evidence:** The positive/marginally positive mining-support results are potentially the most interesting fact in the paper. If regulation caused operational adaptation rather than contraction, that is the mechanism that makes the paper conceptually bigger.
- **Different comparison:** The paper would be much stronger if it explicitly positioned Oklahoma against the classic environmental-regulation studies: not “another null,” but “a different kind of regulation with different economic incidence.”
- **Broader stakes:** CCS/geothermal is mentioned, but currently feels tacked on. If the author can convincingly argue that Oklahoma is an early empirical example of the economics of subsurface risk regulation, the paper becomes more forward-looking.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

### Closest neighbors
The closest conversation appears to involve:

- **Greenstone (2002)** — environmental regulation and manufacturing employment.
- **Walker (2013)** — earnings/labor-market effects of environmental regulation.
- **Curtis (2018)** — employment effects of NOx regulation/cap-and-trade.
- **Feyrer, Mansur, and Sacerdote (2017)** — local economic effects of shale/oil and gas activity.
- **Allcott and Keniston (2018)** or **Bartik et al. (2019)** — local boom effects/resource shocks.
- **Muehlenbachs, Krupnick, and Noailly (2015)** — induced seismicity/housing market impacts.
- Seismology papers like **Keranen et al.**, **Weingarten et al.**, **Langenbruch et al.** as institutional/scientific background, but these are not the main economics neighbors.

### How should the paper position itself?
Mostly **build on** the environmental-regulation-and-jobs literature, while using the induced-seismicity setting as a way to sharpen the theory of when labor-market costs arise.

It should not “attack” Greenstone/Walker/Curtis. Those papers concern regulations that affect production costs more directly. The right stance is:

- Those papers show that some regulations have real labor-market costs.
- This paper shows a boundary condition: when regulation targets a byproduct-disposal margin with compliance flexibility, those costs may be much smaller or absent.

That is an intellectually useful synthesis.

### Too narrow or too broad?
Currently, it is **too narrow in topic and too broad in aspiration**.

Too narrow because much of the intro is basically Oklahoma institutional detail.  
Too broad because the paper gestures at “environmental regulation,” “shale,” and “CCS” without fully committing to one main conversation.

The paper needs one primary audience. It should be: **economists interested in the incidence of environmental and risk regulation**. Shale and induced seismicity are the empirical setting, not the main contribution.

### What literature does it seem unaware of?
It seems under-engaged with a broader literature on:
- **Regulatory incidence and adjustment margins** — how firms adapt to regulation through reorganization rather than contraction.
- **Risk regulation / safety regulation** — not only pollution regulation. There may be analogies in workplace safety, financial regulation, or transport safety where the margin regulated is ancillary rather than core output.
- **Local adjustment and reallocation** — if the paper’s explanation is that labor was reallocated within the oilfield-services ecosystem, it should say so with more awareness of labor reallocation literature.

### Is it having the right conversation?
Not quite. The most promising conversation is not “the first paper on induced seismicity regulation and jobs.” It is:

> “What determines whether environmental/safety regulation actually destroys jobs?”

That is the conversation AER readers care about. The induced-earthquake setting is unusual enough to be memorable, but the paper must extract a general lesson from it.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Before this paper, the natural prior is that a major environmental regulation imposed on a fossil-fuel-adjacent industry should reduce employment, especially in already exposed local labor markets.

### Tension
But Oklahoma’s seismicity regulation is unusual: it sharply restricted wastewater disposal, not oil production per se. That creates a puzzle. If disposal is an essential complement to production, job losses should follow. If disposal can be reorganized, substituted, or absorbed operationally, maybe not.

### Resolution
The paper finds no detectable employment decline overall, and even hints that directly exposed mining-support employment did not fall and may have relatively outperformed placebo sectors.

### Implications
The implication is that the jobs-versus-regulation tradeoff may depend crucially on the margin being regulated. Policymakers may be able to sharply reduce certain hazards—especially those associated with waste disposal or risky ancillary processes—without large local employment damage.

### Does the paper have a narrative arc?
Only **partially**. The pieces are there, but they are not assembled into a compelling story. Right now it still reads somewhat like a collection of standard empirical sections attached to a null result.

The paper should be telling this story:

1. Environmental regulation is often presumed costly in labor-market terms.
2. Oklahoma provides a stark test because the regulation was dramatic and salient.
3. The distinctive feature is that the regulation constrained **waste disposal**, not output itself.
4. The surprising finding is no employment loss.
5. Therefore the paper uncovers an economically important distinction between regulating production and regulating ancillary externality-generating activities.

Without that, the paper is just “null effects in an unusual setting.”

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?
I would lead with:

> Oklahoma cut wastewater injection volumes by roughly 40 percent in the counties at the center of the induced-earthquake crisis, earthquake rates collapsed, and local employment did not.

That is the dinner-party fact. It is concrete and surprising.

### Would people lean in?
Yes, initially. The earthquake angle is vivid, and “big regulation with no jobs effect” is counter to instinct.

But the next 30 seconds matter a lot. If the presenter then says, “we estimate a null in county-quarter QCEW,” attention will fade. If instead they say, “this suggests the cost of regulation depends on whether you regulate production itself or the disposal of a byproduct,” economists will lean in.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
They would ask one of two questions:
1. **Why no job loss?**  
2. **What does this tell us beyond Oklahoma?**

Those are exactly the questions the paper must answer better. Right now, the mechanism discussion is tentative and the external relevance is gestural.

### Is the null interesting?
Yes—but only if framed correctly. The null is interesting because:
- the policy was large,
- the hazard was salient,
- the regulated activity was economically central to a politically important sector,
- and the result potentially revises a broader prior about regulation and jobs.

The paper mostly makes the first two points, but not the last one strongly enough. Otherwise it risks feeling like a competent failed hunt for negative effects.

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

### What would make it read better?

**1. Rewrite the introduction around one big question.**  
The introduction currently spends too much early space on institutional detail and on “the literature hasn’t studied this.” Move faster to the broader economic claim.

**2. Front-load the surprising result and why it matters.**  
The best fact in the paper appears in paragraph five. It should arrive earlier: big regulation, big hazard reduction, no employment losses.

**3. Shorten standard empirical throat-clearing in the introduction.**  
The paragraph on the principal threat to identification is too detailed for an intro. That belongs later. For editorial positioning purposes, it slows the narrative and makes the paper sound method-first rather than question-first.

**4. Elevate the conceptual distinction.**  
The disposal-vs.-production distinction is currently buried in the contribution/discussion language. It should become a recurring organizing idea: intro, results motivation, discussion, conclusion.

**5. Either strengthen or trim the industry-by-industry table march.**  
Right now the reader gets a sequence of total employment, mining support, extraction, retail, food, etc. This feels incremental. If the core point is “no overall employment loss and evidence of adaptation in directly exposed sectors,” then organize the results around that. Do not let placebo industries take equal narrative weight.

**6. Wages section may be too thin to stand alone.**  
As currently written, the wage section does not add much conceptually. It can stay, but it should be integrated as part of a broader statement: “no job loss, no wage loss, hence little evidence of meaningful labor-market distress.”

**7. Conclusion should do more than summarize.**  
The conclusion is competent but mostly repetitive. It should end by sharpening the general lesson: regulations targeting dangerous byproducts may have very different labor-market incidence from regulations targeting output. That is the memorable takeaway.

### Are good results buried?
Yes. The most strategically important result may actually be that the directly exposed sector does **not** show the expected decline. That is more narratively interesting than some of the placebo material. The paper should make more of that fact, while being careful not to overstate.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

At present, the gap is mainly **framing and ambition**, with some scope concerns.

### Is it a framing problem?
Yes, strongly. The science may be serviceable, but the paper is framed as a niche policy evaluation rather than as evidence on a major economics question.

### Is it a scope problem?
Also yes. The paper wants to speak to environmental regulation broadly, but the evidence base is somewhat narrow: one state, one policy, mainly employment/wages, and limited mechanism evidence. For AER, the author probably needs either:
- a much stronger conceptual framing that makes the single setting do more work, or
- somewhat broader evidence on adjustment mechanisms or economic margins.

### Is it a novelty problem?
Partly. The exact setting is novel. The underlying empirical design is not. That means the burden is on the paper to make the insight, not the method, feel new.

### Is it an ambition problem?
Yes. The current paper is careful and competent, but safe. It is trying to establish “first evidence on X” rather than making a bolder claim about how economists should think about the incidence of regulation.

### Single most impactful advice
**Reframe the paper around a general claim: environmental regulations that constrain dangerous byproduct disposal, rather than production itself, may sharply reduce risk without the labor-market losses found in classic regulation settings—and use Oklahoma as the flagship example of that distinction.**

That is the one change that could most alter the paper’s trajectory.
