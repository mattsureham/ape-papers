## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple and policy-relevant question: when Oklahoma sharply restricted wastewater injection to stop induced earthquakes, did it kill local jobs? Using the staggered rollout of county-level injection caps, the paper argues that a regulation that dramatically reduced seismic hazard appears to have imposed little or no detectable employment cost, suggesting that some environmental regulations aimed at byproduct disposal may be much less economically costly than regulations that directly constrain production.

That is a potentially interesting pitch for a busy economist. But the paper does **not** currently deliver it as cleanly as it should in the first two paragraphs. The introduction starts with vivid background and quickly moves into “first estimates” and literature gaps; it takes too long to state the core economic question in big-picture terms. The paper should lead less with Oklahoma-as-case-study and more with the broader economic tension: environmental regulation is often argued to cost jobs, but that presumption may depend critically on *what* the regulation constrains.

### The pitch the paper should have

“Do environmental regulations always cost jobs, or does it matter whether they restrict production itself or only the disposal of harmful byproducts? This paper studies Oklahoma’s response to induced earthquakes, where regulators imposed large, geographically targeted cuts in wastewater injection volumes. I show that these rules sharply reduced seismic risk but had no detectable effect on local employment, suggesting that regulations aimed at hazardous disposal margins can deliver large environmental benefits with little labor-market cost.”

Second paragraph:

“This distinction matters far beyond Oklahoma. Many of the most contentious energy and climate policies—from wastewater disposal to carbon sequestration and geothermal operations—regulate injection and waste management rather than output directly. If the employment effects of such regulations are small, then a central political argument against environmental protection is weaker in precisely the sectors where future regulation is likely to expand.”

That is the AER-facing version of the paper. Right now the paper’s opening is competent, but too local, too descriptive, and too eager to catalog literatures before locking in the bigger question.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper’s contribution is: **it provides evidence that a high-profile environmental regulation targeting wastewater disposal rather than production substantially reduced induced seismicity while generating no measurable local employment loss.**

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest papers?

Only partially. The paper distinguishes itself from:
- induced seismicity/housing papers,
- seismology papers,
- environmental regulation and jobs papers.

But the differentiation is still a bit mechanical. The reader understands that “no one has estimated this exact thing before,” but not yet why this exact thing changes how we think about regulation more broadly. “First estimates of Oklahoma seismicity regulation on employment” is a literature-gap contribution. “Employment costs of environmental regulation depend on whether regulation bites on production or on a flexible disposal margin” is a world-question contribution. The latter is much stronger.

### World question or literature gap?

Currently it is framed too much as **filling a literature gap**:
- first estimates,
- existing literature focuses on housing and seismology,
- contributes to three literatures.

That is fine for a field journal. For AER, it needs to be framed more as answering a question about the world:
- When do environmental regulations destroy jobs?
- Are labor-market costs intrinsic to environmental protection, or specific to regulations that constrain output?
- Can hazard mitigation in extractive industries be economically cheap because firms can reoptimize along disposal margins?

### Could a smart economist explain what is new after reading the introduction?

Right now, they might say: **“It’s a DiD paper on Oklahoma wastewater regulations and county employment, and they find a null.”** That is not enough.

What you want them to say is: **“It shows an important boundary condition on the jobs-versus-environment tradeoff: regulations on byproduct disposal can slash environmental harm without the employment losses seen when regulation hits production directly.”**

That is much better, and it is available in the current material, but the paper does not yet foreground it.

### What would make the contribution bigger?

A few specific ways:

1. **Integrate environmental benefit and economic cost into one central object.**  
   Right now the “hazard reduction” is background and the “employment null” is the result. The paper would feel much bigger if the headline were explicitly: *large environmental gains, no labor-market losses*. Even descriptive evidence on the hazard side should be elevated as part of the core result, not left as institutional backdrop.

2. **Show more directly why disposal regulation differs from production regulation.**  
   The paper gestures toward substitution margins—alternative formations, recycling, logistics, compliance work—but does not make that mechanism the organizing idea. Even without new causal mechanism tests, the framing should be that this is a test of a broader conceptual distinction.

3. **Expand the economic margin beyond employment counts.**  
   If feasible, the paper would be bigger with stronger evidence on:
   - establishment counts,
   - payroll/wages,
   - reallocation across sectors,
   - perhaps commuting or neighboring-county shifts if available.  
   Right now “no jobs lost” could still sound narrow. “No jobs, no wage, no establishment contraction” is more persuasive as a statement about local labor-market costs.

4. **Frame CCS/geothermal as a motivation, not a brief afterthought.**  
   This is one of the best routes to general interest. Induced seismicity from injection is not just Oklahoma history; it is a live issue for decarbonization infrastructure. The paper currently notes this too late and too briefly.

   - The seismology papers cited: Ellsworth (2013), Keranen et al. (2014), Weingarten et al. (2015), Langenbruch et al. (2018)

### How should the paper position itself relative to those neighbors?

It should mostly **build on and connect** them, not attack them.

- Relative to Greenstone/Walker/Curtis:  
  The paper should say those studies established that regulations can impose substantial labor-market costs **when they constrain production or emissions-intensive activity directly**. This paper examines a different class of regulation—hazardous disposal constraints—where firms may have more flexible compliance margins. So the contribution is not “those papers were wrong”; it is “their tradeoff is not universal.”

- Relative to induced-seismicity papers:  
  The paper should say that prior work documents the physical and asset-market consequences of induced earthquakes, but we know much less about the labor-market incidence of the policy response. That is a natural extension.

- Relative to shale papers:  
  The paper should say most of that literature studies booms and busts from prices/production shocks; this paper studies the economic effects of a targeted safety/environmental intervention within that sector.

### Is the paper positioned too narrowly or too broadly?

At present, it is oddly both:
- **Too narrowly** in the empirical presentation: a county-level Oklahoma case with one main reduced-form result.
- **Too broadly** in the contribution section, where it lists three literatures without really anchoring itself in one central conversation.

The right audience is not “everyone interested in Oklahoma earthquakes.” It is economists interested in:
- environmental regulation and incidence,
- energy and climate infrastructure,
- local labor-market adjustment,
- and the political economy of regulation.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?

A few gaps in positioning, if not necessarily in citation:
- **Porter hypothesis / induced innovation / flexible compliance** traditions: not because the paper tests Porter directly, but because the “why no job loss?” story is about margins of adjustment and adaptation.
- **Risk regulation / disaster mitigation / environmental externalities with local economic consequences**: the paper could speak more to regulation of localized hazards, not just pollution.
- **Energy transition infrastructure constraints**: CCS, geothermal, siting, permitting, subsurface risk. This is a forward-looking policy literature that could give the paper much more contemporary relevance.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?

Not quite yet. It is currently having the conversation:  
**“Here is an under-studied policy episode in Oklahoma.”**

It should be having the conversation:  
**“What kinds of environmental regulations are economically costly, and what kinds are surprisingly cheap?”**

That reframing would make the paper more legible to general-interest readers.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Environmental regulation is politically and economically contested because it is widely believed to impose meaningful employment costs. At the same time, modern energy systems generate harms not only through production, but also through waste disposal and subsurface operations.

### Tension

We know a fair amount about job losses from regulations that constrain production, and we know induced seismicity became a major hazard in Oklahoma. But we do **not** know whether a major, high-salience regulation aimed at stopping that hazard destroyed jobs in the communities it affected. That is the puzzle: can regulators mitigate a serious environmental risk in an extractive industry without triggering the classic local employment backlash?

### Resolution

The paper’s answer is that Oklahoma’s injection caps appear to have sharply reduced seismicity while leaving local employment essentially unchanged, even in sectors most directly exposed to disposal activity.

### Implications

The implications are potentially important:
- the jobs-versus-environment tradeoff may be overstated for regulations that target flexible byproduct-disposal margins;
- politically, some environmental rules may be easier to implement than conventional wisdom suggests;
- practically, this matters for future regulation of injection-based technologies like CCS and geothermal.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the ingredients, but not a fully realized arc. Right now it feels a bit like **a competent collection of reduced-form results attached to a plausible story**. The narrative is there, but underpowered.

The biggest problem is that the paper does not fully exploit its own best tension:
- policy dramatically reduced earthquakes,
- but did not reduce employment,
- despite acting in the middle of an oil-producing region,
- which cuts against the default expectation of costly regulation.

That is a strong story. The paper should lean into the surprise more.

### What story should it be telling?

The story should be:

1. Environmental regulation often appears costly because it restricts production.
2. But some regulations target a harmful byproduct margin where substitution is easier.
3. Oklahoma’s seismicity rules are an unusually clean and important test of that distinction.
4. The result: large hazard reduction, no labor-market damage.
5. Therefore, economists and policymakers should distinguish more sharply between **production-constraining** and **disposal-constraining** environmental rules.

That is an actual AER-style narrative. The current version is too close to “policy evaluation of one state episode.”

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?

“I’ve got a paper showing that Oklahoma cut wastewater injection enough to collapse earthquake incidence—but local employment didn’t budge.”

That is the fact.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

A subset would lean in—especially environmental, labor, and energy economists—but general economists may not, because the current framing doesn’t yet make clear why this is a broad lesson rather than an Oklahoma curiosity.

The issue is not the result itself; it’s the packaging. “No job loss from earthquake regulation in Oklahoma” is a medium-interest fact. “Evidence that some environmental regulations can deliver large benefits with no detectable labor-market cost because they target disposal rather than production” is a high-interest fact.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Probably: **“Why were there no job losses?”**

And second: **“Is this because the regulation was genuinely low-cost, or because the oil bust swamped everything?”**

You told me not to referee identification, so I won’t go there. But strategically, the paper should realize that the audience’s first instinct will be to ask whether the null reflects economics or muddled exposure. That means the paper needs to make the conceptual interpretation sharper and more disciplined.

### If the findings are null or modest, is the null itself interesting?

Yes—*if* the paper sells the null correctly.

A null is interesting here because:
- the policy was large and salient,
- the environmental benefit was large,
- the regulated activity was directly tied to the local extractive economy,
- and the default political narrative would predict job losses.

So this is not a failed experiment. It is potentially evidence against a commonly presumed tradeoff. But the paper needs to explicitly make that case and avoid sounding apologetic about the null. At present it still reads a bit like, “we found nothing, but maybe that itself is informative.” It should read more like, “the absence of labor-market damage is the central fact to be explained.”

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The gap is mostly **framing plus ambition**, with some **scope** concerns.

### Is it a framing problem?

Yes, substantially. The paper has a potentially publishable idea, but it is currently written like a respectable field-journal policy evaluation. It has not yet claimed the larger conceptual territory available to it.

### Is it a scope problem?

Also yes. The current design delivers one central local employment null with a few sectoral and wage results. That may be enough for a solid journal, but for AER the paper would feel more complete if it did more to show the broader incidence of the policy:
- jobs,
- wages,
- establishments,
- perhaps a richer accounting of sectoral reallocation,
- and a clearer integration of the environmental benefit side.

### Is it a novelty problem?

Somewhat. “Another DiD about regulation and employment” is always a risk. The novelty here is not the method; it is the policy setting and the broader implication. If the authors fail to elevate that implication, the paper will look incremental.

### Is it an ambition problem?

Yes. The paper is careful but safe. It does not yet make a bold enough claim about what economists should learn from the case. The result itself invites a bolder interpretation than the paper is willing to own.

### The single most impactful piece of advice

**Reframe the paper around a general economic claim: environmental regulations that target hazardous byproduct disposal, rather than production itself, may deliver large benefits with little local labor-market cost—and use Oklahoma as the leading test case of that claim.**

That is the one change that would most improve its odds. Everything else is secondary.
