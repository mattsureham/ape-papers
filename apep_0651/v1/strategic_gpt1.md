## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple and potentially important question: when a workplace death occurs during a crowded news cycle, does the regulator respond less aggressively because nobody is paying attention? Using U.S. mine fatalities and variation in competing disaster news, the paper finds no such effect: unlike disaster relief and other discretionary government responses, mine-safety enforcement appears insulated from media salience, plausibly because it is governed by statutory mandates.

A busy economist should care because this is not really a paper about mines. It is a paper about when government behavior is shaped by attention and when institutional design shuts that channel down. If true, that is a useful boundary condition on a large and influential “media accountability / salience” literature.

### Does the paper articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Mostly, but not optimally. The introduction is competent and readable, and the opening anecdote does useful work. Still, the paper gets to “mine fatalities + hurricanes + enforcement” before fully clarifying the bigger issue: this is a test of whether media-driven accountability affects regulatory enforcement, and more importantly, whether mandates can neutralize that force.

Right now the introduction risks sounding like a niche application of Eisensee-Strömberg to mines. The first two paragraphs should more sharply foreground the general question and the contrast between discretionary and rule-bound state action.

### The pitch the paper should have

Here is the version the paper should be selling:

> Governments often respond more strongly to events that receive more media attention. But does that pattern extend to regulatory enforcement, or can legal mandates insulate agencies from the news cycle?  
>   
> This paper studies whether mine-safety enforcement weakens when mine fatalities coincide with major competing news events. Using variation from disaster-driven news competition, I find no effect on inspections, violations, or penalties after a death. The broader implication is that media accountability matters less when the state is constrained by automatic, statutory enforcement obligations than when officials retain discretion.

That is the AER-facing version. It makes the paper about state capacity, discretion, and accountability rather than about one regulator in one industry.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper’s contribution is to show that a canonical media-salience mechanism that affects discretionary government responses does not appear to affect mandated mine-safety enforcement, suggesting an important boundary condition for the political economy of attention.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?

Only partially. The paper does differentiate itself from Eisensee-Strömberg by emphasizing the null and the role of mandates, but the differentiation is still too design-centric (“I apply the competing-news design to a new setting”) and not conceptual enough (“I identify where media accountability breaks down, because discretion is constrained”).

The current contribution statement leans on novelty of application:
- first use of this design in occupational safety regulation,
- no prior study of media attention in mine safety.

That is not enough for AER. “First use of a design in a new setting” is almost never the right frontier. The paper needs to say more clearly: prior literature shows that attention distorts discretionary policy; this paper shows that a legally mandated regulatory regime is a limiting case where that mechanism does not operate.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or filling a gap in a LITERATURE?

Right now it is split, and too much of it is “gap in literature.” The stronger frame is a question about the world:

- When does media attention shape state behavior?
- Are regulators hostage to the news cycle?
- Can institutional design make enforcement attention-proof?

That is much better than “no one has used Eisensee-Strömberg for mine safety.”

### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what’s new?

They could, but many would still summarize it as: “It’s another competing-news paper, this time on mine safety, and the effect is zero.”

That is the danger. To avoid that, the introduction must hammer the broader interpretation: **this is a boundary-condition paper**. The null is not “nothing happened”; it is “one widely documented mechanism does not generalize to a setting with automatic legal obligations.”

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Several things could make it materially bigger:

1. **Show variation in discretion within the same institutional environment.**  
   This is the single most obvious way to elevate the paper. If inspections after fatalities are mandatory, then compare:
   - mandatory versus discretionary enforcement actions,
   - actions at the fatality mine versus peer mines in the same district/industry,
   - immediate fatality investigation margins versus subsequent follow-up or broader compliance sweeps.

   That would turn the paper from “null in a mandated setting” into “attention matters exactly where discretion lives.”

2. **Bring media coverage itself into the narrative more directly.**  
   Even if the paper remains reduced-form, it would help strategically if the reader saw clear evidence that competing disasters actually crowd out coverage of mine fatalities. Otherwise the paper reads as a null reduced form looking for interpretation.

3. **Broaden the outcomes toward public-relevance or institutional significance.**  
   Inspections/violations/penalties are administrative outputs. Bigger outcomes would include:
   - speed of investigation,
   - severity of citations,
   - mine closures,
   - subsequent accident risk,
   - spillovers to nearby mines.

   Not for referee-level “please add ten more regressions,” but because those outcomes would make the stakes larger.

4. **Frame the comparison as a test of institutional design, not just of one agency.**  
   The paper needs a more explicit conceptual contrast:
   - discretionary responses: FEMA, prosecutorial action, some environmental enforcement;
   - mandated responses: MSHA fatality investigations, perhaps certain OSHA/MSHA statutory obligations.

   The paper’s current interpretation does this rhetorically, but not yet as the centerpiece of the contribution.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Prior work shows that public attention affects government action. Disasters that get less coverage get less relief; politicians under less press scrutiny face weaker accountability. It is natural to suspect that regulators too may respond more strongly when the public is watching.

### Tension

But regulatory enforcement is not always discretionary. Some agencies operate under automatic, legally mandated procedures. So there is a genuine puzzle: does media pressure still matter when the regulator is bound by statute, or are such agencies insulated from the attention cycle?

### Resolution

In mine safety, the paper finds essentially no effect of competing news pressure on post-fatality inspections, violations, or penalties.

### Implications

The implication is not simply “media doesn’t matter.” It is narrower and more interesting: media salience may matter mainly where public officials retain discretion. Institutional design — especially mandatory enforcement obligations — can blunt or eliminate attention-driven distortions.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has a **serviceable arc**, but it is not yet fully disciplined. The raw ingredients are there, but the paper still reads somewhat like a collection of:
- one design,
- one null result,
- several robustness exercises,
- then an interpretation.

The stronger story is:

> Attention distorts government action when officials choose whether to act. Mine-safety fatalities offer a test of whether the same is true when action is mandated. The answer is no, suggesting that legal automaticity can substitute for media scrutiny.

That should be the story from the first page through the conclusion. Right now the paper occasionally slips back into “I study mines because this is an unexplored application.”

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?

I would say:  
**“When a miner dies on a day dominated by hurricanes or other big news, the mine regulator does not pull back. Unlike disaster relief, the enforcement response is basically unchanged.”**

That is a decent opening fact.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

Initially, some would lean in, because the contrast with Eisensee-Strömberg is intuitive and potentially important.

But they would only stay engaged if the presenter quickly made clear that this is about **discretion versus mandate**. If it remains at the level of “a null in mine safety,” many will disengage.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

The immediate follow-up is obvious:

**“Is that because MSHA is special, or because the relevant margin is mandatory? Can you show that attention matters for a more discretionary enforcement margin?”**

That is the strategic question the paper needs to answer, or at least explicitly organize itself around.

### Is the null itself interesting?

Yes, potentially. But only if the paper leans hard into why this null is theoretically informative.

A null is interesting when:
- theory predicts a nonzero effect,
- prior evidence in nearby domains shows a nonzero effect,
- the null reveals a meaningful boundary condition.

This paper has the ingredients for that. It should not apologize for the null; it should frame it as a comparative institutional result.

What would make it feel like a failed experiment is if the paper does not establish why one should have expected an effect here in the first place, and why the absence of an effect updates beliefs. Right now it is close to making that case, but it needs sharper framing.

### What would make it read better?

   The intro moves fairly quickly into the instrument and specifications. For editorial positioning, that is too early. The first two pages should sell the question, the stakes, and the key result. The design can be stated briefly and then elaborated later.

2. **Front-load the comparative insight.**  
   The line “Unlike disaster relief, mine safety enforcement operates through statutory mandates that insulate regulators from the news cycle” is the most important sentence in the abstract. A version of that should appear even earlier and more forcefully in the introduction.

3. **Compress the “three contributions” paragraph.**  
   It currently reads like a standard field-journal introduction. For AER, this should become one sharper paragraph centered on the world question, with the literature serving that question.

4. **Move some defensive material out of the main text.**  
   The balance tests, randomization inference details, leave-one-out, minimum detectable effect discussion — all useful, but some of this can be pushed back or streamlined. The paper currently spends a lot of pages proving the null is careful. That is fine for referees; it is not what wins editorial excitement.

5. **Strengthen the discussion section by making it more comparative and less speculative.**  
   The discussion is directionally right, but it jumps quickly to broad claims about other regulators. It would be better to say:
   - what this setting teaches,
   - what the key margin of discretion is,
   - what future work should compare.

6. **Conclusion should do more than summarize.**  
   The current conclusion is clear but predictable. It should end on the broader lesson: if the policy goal is equal enforcement independent of salience, automaticity may outperform attention-based accountability.

### Is the paper front-loaded with the good stuff?

Reasonably, but not enough. The main result is on page 1 of the intro, which is good. But the broad interpretation is still not sufficiently front-loaded. A reader should not have to wait until the discussion section to understand why the null matters.

### Are results buried that should be in the main results?

The heterogeneity by coal vs. metal/nonmetal is suggestive, but not yet central. The potentially more important buried point is the idea that **mandatory margins may be insulated while lower-salience/discretionary margins may not be**. If the paper has any evidence even loosely in that direction, that should be elevated conceptually.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

Be honest: in its current form, this is **not yet an AER paper**. The main issue is not competence. The paper is tidy, coherent, and knows its immediate comparison set. The issue is that the current package still looks like a narrow application of a known design producing a null in a specialized setting.

### What is the gap?

Mostly:

- **Framing problem:** The science may be fine, but the story is still too much “mine safety + competing news” and not enough “when does media accountability shape the state?”
- **Scope problem:** The paper studies a setting where the main margin is plausibly mandated, but then only measures outcomes at that mandated margin. To make the lesson big, it needs either variation in discretion or a more compelling comparative architecture.
- **Ambition problem:** It is careful but safe. The paper identifies a plausible boundary condition but does not push hard enough to show where the mechanism does and does not operate.

### What would excite the top 10 people in this field?

A paper that could say one of the following:

1. **Media attention affects discretionary government responses but not legally mandated ones — here is evidence within the same regulatory setting.**
2. **Attention has no effect on the fatality-mine response, but it does affect spillover enforcement, follow-up intensity, or other discretionary margins.**
3. **Institutional automaticity causally insulates enforcement from salience, and here is a direct comparison across agencies or policy margins.**

Any of those would be much bigger than the current version.

### Single most impactful piece of advice

**Reframe and, if possible, redesign the paper around discretion versus mandate rather than around mine safety versus disaster relief.**

That is the one thing. If the authors can show — or at least organize the paper around — differences across margins with more or less discretion, the paper becomes much more than a null application. It becomes a paper about the architecture of accountability.
