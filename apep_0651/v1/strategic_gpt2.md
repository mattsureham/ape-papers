## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple and potentially important question: when a workplace death occurs during a crowded news cycle, does the regulator respond less aggressively because the event gets less media attention? Using mine fatalities and variation in competing disaster news, the paper finds no such effect for MSHA, and argues that statutory enforcement mandates insulate mine-safety regulation from the media-attention channel documented in other domains like disaster relief.

A busy economist should care because the paper is trying to answer a broader question than mine safety: when does government responsiveness depend on media salience, and when do institutional rules substitute for public attention?

**Does the paper articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Mostly, but not optimally. The current introduction opens with a vivid anecdote and then moves quickly into the “growing literature” frame. That is serviceable, but the paper’s real comparative advantage is not “here is a new setting for an existing design.” It is “here is a test of a boundary condition on a major idea in political economy and public economics: regulation by attention.” The first two paragraphs should lead with that world-facing question and the contrast with discretionary government response.

### The pitch the paper should have

> Governments often respond more forcefully to crises that capture public attention. But does that logic extend to regulatory enforcement, or can legal mandates insulate agencies from the news cycle?  
> 
> This paper studies U.S. mine fatalities and shows that when competing disasters crowd the national news, MSHA enforcement after a death does not weaken. The result suggests an important limit to “governing by salience”: media attention matters for discretionary state action, but much less where statutes require regulators to act.

That is the paper’s best version. Right now the introduction hints at this, but still sounds too much like “an application of Eisensee-Strömberg to mine safety.”

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:**  
The paper argues that media-salience shocks that affect other forms of government response do not affect post-fatality mine-safety enforcement, implying that mandatory statutory enforcement can insulate regulators from the news cycle.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?
Only partly.

The paper differentiates itself from Eisensee and Strömberg by emphasizing the null contrast with disaster relief. That is the right instinct. But it does not yet sharply distinguish itself from the broader “media affects politics/government behavior” literature in a way that makes the reader immediately see why this is an important boundary case rather than just another transplantation of a known design.

The paper needs to state more explicitly:

- **Eisensee and Strömberg (2007):** discretionary aid allocation is salience-sensitive.
- **This paper:** mandatory enforcement is not.
- Therefore the object of study is not mine safety per se, but **how institutional discretion mediates media effects**.

That would make the contribution feel conceptually larger.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a gap in a literature?
It is mixed, but currently leans too much toward the literature-gap framing: “no prior study applies this design to occupational safety regulation.” That is not an AER contribution statement. Many papers are first in a setting; that alone is not enough.

The stronger frame is about the world:

- Do public agencies need media scrutiny to act?
- Or can formal mandates substitute for attention?

That is the version that belongs in AER territory.

### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what’s new?
Right now they might say: **“It’s an Eisensee-Strömberg-style competing-news paper on mine-safety enforcement, and it finds a null.”**

That is not fatal, but it is not where you want to be.

What you want them to say is: **“It shows that media-salience effects are not universal; they seem to depend on whether government action is discretionary or mandated.”**

That is much stronger and more memorable.

### What would make this contribution bigger?
The paper’s biggest limitation is scope, not technical execution. Specific ways to make the contribution bigger:

1. **Exploit variation in discretion within the enforcement process.**  
   The current story is that “mandates insulate.” Then show that the null is strongest on the most mandatory outcomes and, if possible, weaker on more discretionary margins:
   - timing/intensity of follow-up inspections beyond the statutory minimum,
   - citation severity,
   - proposed penalties,
   - spillovers to peer mines in the district/region,
   - public communications or reopening decisions if observable.

2. **Create an internal or external comparison between mandatory and discretionary state action.**  
   This is the paper the authors want to be writing. A stronger design would compare:
   - fatality-site enforcement versus discretionary inspections elsewhere,
   - MSHA versus another regulator with more discretion,
   - mandated investigation tasks versus optional enforcement escalations.

3. **Show the first-order substantive fact in a broader frame.**  
   The interesting claim is not just “MSHA doesn’t respond to news competition.” It is “legal mandates can substitute for media accountability.” To support that, the paper needs evidence around the discretion margin.

4. **Move beyond mine safety if possible.**  
   If the authors can add even one other agency or context, the paper jumps substantially in ambition. As is, it is a single-setting case study with a well-argued null.

It should instead be having the conversation: **“When does public attention discipline the state, and when do formal rules do that work instead?”**

That is the right conversation for impact.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
There is now substantial evidence that media attention affects government behavior. Public agencies may react more when an issue becomes salient, because media coverage creates political pressure.

### Tension
But that logic may not hold everywhere. Some agencies operate under hard statutory mandates rather than broad discretion. So the puzzle is: **does attention still matter when the regulator is legally required to act?**

### Resolution
In mine safety, using competing-news variation around fatalities, the paper finds no meaningful effect of crowded news cycles on post-fatality enforcement.

### Implications
The paper implies that media accountability is not universally necessary for government responsiveness. Institutional design—specifically, mandatory rules—can insulate some forms of enforcement from salience shocks.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
It has the ingredients of a clear arc, but it is not yet fully disciplined. At times it reads like:

- a design paper,
- then a mine-safety paper,
- then a media-and-politics paper,
- then an institutional-design paper.

Those are related, but the hierarchy is not stable.

The story it should be telling is:

1. **Known fact:** media salience distorts discretionary government response.
2. **Open question:** do mandates neutralize this distortion?
3. **Test case:** post-fatality mine-safety enforcement is highly rule-bound.
4. **Finding:** no salience effect.
5. **Interpretation:** institutional constraints shape whether attention matters.

That is the clean narrative. Right now the paper gets there, but too gradually and with too much emphasis on specification and empirical housekeeping relative to conceptual stakes.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?
“When a miner dies during a week dominated by hurricanes and other major disasters, mine-safety enforcement doesn’t appear to change at all—unlike disaster relief, which is highly sensitive to media competition.”

That is the best fact. The second sentence is essential; without it, the result is just a null in a niche setting.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
A mixed outcome.

- If presented as **“here’s a null in mine-safety enforcement”**, phones.
- If presented as **“here’s evidence that media effects depend critically on institutional discretion”**, people lean in.

The current draft is closer to the first than the second.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Probably: **“What margin is actually discretionary here?”**

That is the key question the paper must anticipate. If everything important is mechanically mandated, then the result may feel overdetermined rather than revealing. The paper needs to show that there are meaningful margins on which attention could have mattered, or at least explain more concretely why a null remains informative despite the mandates.

### Is the null itself interesting?
Potentially yes. But null papers only work when they convincingly establish that the null adjudicates between meaningful competing views of the world.

Here, the paper has the right ingredients for that:
- strong prior from the media-attention literature,
- a setting where politics plausibly could matter,
- a reason to think mandates might suppress the effect.

But it needs to make the case more forcefully that this is not just a failed search for an effect. It is a test of a theory’s scope condition.

At present, the null is **interesting but not yet fully sold**.
