**Idea Fidelity**

The paper takes the core theme of the idea manifest—applying an Eisensee-Strömberg competing-news design to MSHA enforcement—but departs from the original operationalization. The manifest promised to instrument for media salience using weekly GDELT news-competition volume and then estimate whether higher media coverage of a mine fatality leads to more enforcement. Instead, the paper estimates reduced-form correlations between FEMA disaster declarations in the fatality week and enforcement outcomes, without ever demonstrating the assumed first-stage (that disasters reduce fatality coverage) nor attempting to link media salience directly to enforcement. The paper therefore bypasses the central causal chain laid out in the manifest: (i) fatalities receive endogenous coverage, (ii) disasters crowd out that coverage, and (iii) regulators respond to media attention. The current version effectively tests whether contemporaneous macro “disaster pressure” (measured by FEMA declarations) is associated with enforcement, which weakens the manifest’s promised identification strategy. Key components of the original research question and empirical strategy—particularly the use of GDELT to proxy news attention and a first-stage on fatality coverage—are absent.

**Summary**

The paper asks whether MSHA enforcement intensity following a mine fatality is sensitive to competing news events. Using FEMA disaster declarations as a proxy for media competition, it finds no statistically significant effect on post-fatality inspections, violations, or penalties, and interprets the null as evidence that mandatory statutory mandates insulate MSHA from media attention. The contribution is positioned as contrasting disaster-relief work, where media salience shapes discretionary responses, with a regulated setting where enforcement is claimed to be “deaf” to the news cycle.

**Essential Points**

1. **Identification strategy is incomplete and weak.** The manifest promised an Eisensee-Strömberg instrument that operates through news coverage, yet the paper reports only reduced-form estimates of disaster declarations on enforcement. There is no evidence that FEMA disasters actually reduce media coverage of mine fatalities (first stage), nor is there an exogenous variation in media salience exploited. Without a first-stage or measure of coverage, the exclusion restriction is untested, and the paper is effectively regressing enforcement on an arguably endogenous aggregate shock. The authors should either (a) supply the first-stage demonstrating the variation in media attention induced by FEMA disasters and proceed with an IV estimate, or (b) reframe the paper explicitly as a reduced-form test about FEMA disasters rather than news competition and justify why that still answers the policy question.

2. **Proxy mismatch undermines the media-attention interpretation.** The theoretical discussion explicitly ties disaster declarations to “competing news” that crowds out media coverage, yet the empirical proxy is simply weekly FEMA disaster counts. Without connecting those declarations to actual measures of media salience (e.g., GDELT coverage of the mine fatality), it is unclear whether the instrument captures the intended mechanism or instead reflects unrelated supply-side shocks (e.g., resource reallocation during disasters, inspection scheduling disruptions due to disaster response). The exclusion restriction—that FEMA disaster declarations affect enforcement only through media attention—is therefore unpersuasive in its current form and needs either stronger support (by showing, for example, that fatality coverage declines in high-declaration weeks) or a redefinition of the causal story.

3. **Causal interpretation of the null is overstated without power against the implied effect.** While the paper reports tight confidence intervals, the interpretation hinges on the assumption that FEMA disaster counts generate substantial variation in media salience of mine fatalities (cf. Eisensee-Strömberg). Without establishing that link, the conclusion that “mandatory inspections insulate MSHA from media attention” is speculative. In addition, the randomized-inference and placebo tests are informative, but they do not address the possibility that the instrument is simply weak (i.e., disaster declarations have little effect on coverage). The authors should either furnish evidence on coverage or expressly qualify their claims as descriptive associations.

**Suggestions**

- **Demonstrate or proxy the first-stage.** The paper’s contribution would be much stronger if it could show that FEMA disaster weeks indeed reduce media coverage of contemporaneous mine fatalities. Consider (i) directly measuring mine-fatality coverage using GDELT event counts or LexisNexis article counts, (ii) exploiting Google Trends or Lexis search intensity, or (iii) presenting evidence that GDELT counts for mine fatalities drop when FEMA disaster declarations rise. Even a reduced-form relationship between disaster declarations and media mentions of the fatality would validate the instrument and make the causal interpretation credible. If such data are unavailable, the paper should be forthright that it lacks a media-attention measure and treat the FEMA declaration variable as a policy shock rather than as a stand-in for the Eisensee-Strömberg instrument.

- **Explore alternative mechanisms or direct channels.** If the null result is driven by statutory mandates rather than attention, the paper should provide additional evidence supporting that institutional explanation. For example, can enforcement be shown to vary less with disaster pressure in mines operating under stricter inspection mandates (underground vs. surface) or after MINER Act implementation? Alternatively, examining discretionary enforcement outcomes (e.g., peer mines, supplemental inspections) could help differentiate between statutory versus attention-based channels. If direct disaster effects (e.g., inspectors redeployed to disaster zones) are present, they should be estimated or ruled out by showing that FEMA declarations geographically distant from the mine have similar effects.

- **Clarify the estimation approach and address potential biases.** The paper currently interprets estimates of FEMA disaster counts as if they were instrument effects. If the goal is reduced-form, explicitly state that, and discuss possible omitted variable bias: Could weeks with many disaster declarations also coincide with other national shocks (e.g., fiscal constraints) that affect MSHA budgets? Year-quarter fixed effects mitigate this, but more discussion or controls (e.g., national budget indicators, labor market conditions) would strengthen the argument. If the intent is IV, report first-stage regression(s), F-statistics, and justify the exclusion restriction more thoroughly.

- **Revisit sample selection and timing.** The manifest envisioned comparing enforcement at the fatality mine and peer mines within narrow windows. The paper focuses solely on the fatality mine and uses relatively long windows (up to 365 days) for the main results. Consider (i) narrowing the time window to emphasize short-run responses, (ii) analyzing neighboring mines to test for spillovers or peer-monitoring effects, and (iii) checking whether enforcement responds differently in weeks immediately following the fatality versus longer horizons. These augmentations would better align the empirical approach with the research question about immediate attention-driven responses.

- **Improve balance and placebo statistics.** The reported balance table is informative, but the description is confusing (mentions six tests but only displays four). Ensure the table matches the text and consider presenting standardized differences for key covariates. For placebo tests, estimate the effect on outcomes that should not plausibly be affected by competing news (e.g., enforcement after non-fatal accidents, no-fatality mines) to further demonstrate exogeneity.

- **Tone down overgeneralized conclusions.** The discussion currently frames the null as evidence that “mandatory mandates insulate MSHA from the news cycle.” While that may be true, the empirical strategy does not isolate media attention; it simply shows no association between disaster pressure and enforcement. Emphasize that the paper documents a lack of correlation, interpret institutional claims cautiously, and highlight that the null contrasts with prior evidence in discretionary settings but does not irrefutably rule out attention effects within MSHA.

- **Consider using alternative attention proxies.** The IDEA indicated availability of GDELT weekly data; even if not used for the main instrument, perhaps the authors can construct a direct measure of national media attention to mine fatalities and use FEMA declarations as an instrument for that measure. Doing so would align the paper with the original manifest and provide a more defensible causal chain.

Overall, these additions would align the empirical approach more closely with the stated research question and reinforce the credibility of the identification strategy.
