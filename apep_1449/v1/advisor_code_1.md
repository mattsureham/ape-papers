**Idea Fidelity**

The paper closely follows the manifest idea. It exploits the deterministic lunar productivity cycle for squid jigging, uses Global Fishing Watch data, compares Chinese (subsidized) against Korean/Taiwanese/Japanese (unsu­bsidized) fleets, and tests for effort responses (monthly constancy, spatial shifts, placebo trawlers) to evaluate intertemporal substitution and subsidy persistence. All key elements—perfect foresight shock, panel structure, lunar illumination treatment, and the welfare/policy motivation around WTO subsidy negotiations—are present. The manifest’s secondary tests (e.g., falsification on light-independent gears) are reported, though the paper could more transparently discuss the spatial substitution test that was mentioned as “key” in the manifest.

**Summary**

The paper studies whether industrial squid jigging fleets adjust effort in response to the perfectly predictable lunar illumination cycle, exploiting Global Fishing Watch AIS data for China, South Korea, Taiwan, and Japan (2020–2022). It finds essentially zero intertemporal substitution—daily fishing hours change by only 3.1% between new and full moons even for the massive Chinese fleet—and no significant differential response tied to subsidies. The null is robust to various specifications and a placebo on non-light-dependent gear.

**Essential Points**

1. **Identification beyond moon-calendar correlation.** The assumption is that lunar variation is exogenous and orthogonal to other determinants of effort once flag and year-month fixed effects are included. Yet lunar cycles are highly regular and may correlate with unobserved calendar effects (e.g., crew pay cycles, fuel deliveries, or religious holidays) that operate on similar ~week-to-week patterns. The paper should more rigorously demonstrate that no such confounders remain—e.g., by conditioning on day-of-week or by showing that de-trended lunar spinoffs remain null (beyond the already-included month FE). Without ruling this out, the near-null finding could mask a mis-specified seasonal pattern rather than genuine inertia.

2. **Aggregated treatment measurement and spatial heterogeneity.** The manifest envisioned spatial substitution tests (“darker waters”), but the empirical section only briefly notes that flag-day aggregation may mask it. Since CPUE drops to near zero, effort could be reallocated spatially or by time-of-day to mitigate moonlight, and the current aggregation would not detect it. A key threat is that Chinese fleets might shift latitudes/longitudes with different moonrise times or use deeper convergence zones. The paper needs to operationalize the spatial/temporal substitution test initially promised—e.g., by examining cell-level intensity changes, latitudinal shifts, or night-time hours versus dawn/dusk across lunar phases. Without that, an unchanged total does not imply “no substitution,” and the policy conclusion about subsidy persistence is incomplete.

3. **Power to detect economically meaningful effects and interpretation of null.** The primary interaction of interest (subsidy effect) is imprecise (SE=0.126 on a coefficient of 0.104). Although the point estimate is small, the 95% CI spans ±0.26 log points, meaning large subsidy-induced distortions cannot be ruled out. The manuscript should calculate minimum detectable effects or translate the CI into hours/vessels to demonstrate whether the study is sufficiently powered to speak about the policy question. As written, the strong claims ("effort inertia dominates") risk overstating what a statistically noisy estimate can deliver.

**Suggestions**

- **Strengthen identification analysis.** While lunar cycles are exogenous, their regular periodicity can pick up residual weekly or turning-of-the-month dynamics. Add additional robustness tests: (i) include day-of-week (already done in robustness but highlight more prominently), (ii) exploit variation within a cycle by focusing on deviations from a smoothed lunar “trend” (e.g., high-frequency residual), or (iii) compare effort across two lunar cycles that fall in the same calendar month to ensure month FEs do not inadvertently soak up the effect. A placebo using a shifted “fake moon” schedule (e.g., offset by 7 days) could also demonstrate that the coefficient is indeed capturing the astronomical cycle rather than a secular pattern.

- **Operationalize the spatial substitution test.** The manifest promised a “spatial substitution to darker waters” check, which is policy-relevant given the concern that subsidies might enable fleets to seek out marginal grounds rather than exit. Use the rich cell-level data to test whether fleets relocate to higher latitudes, depths, or lower moonlight intensity cells as illumination increases. This could involve measuring the average cell illumination (from lunar geometry) or depth and interacting it with the lunar fraction. Alternatively, analyze whether the **variance** of effort across cells increases during full moons (greater dispersion as fleets search for dark spots). These analyses would substantiate (or refute) the hypothesis that effort is reallocated rather than curtailed.

- **Clarify margin of substitution being studied.** The paper interprets the null as evidence against intertemporal substitution, but the regression uses log fishing hours aggregated at the flag-day level. Since CPUE collapses to zero, the binding constraint might be that entire fleets cannot stop fishing (due to contractual obligations), yet they may still vary the **number of vessels** fishing, shift crew hours, or change trip duration. Consider decomposing effort into (i) number of vessels active, (ii) average daily hours per vessel (column 4 attempts this but could be expanded), and (iii) intensive margins at the cell level (fishing hours per cell). This would better separate mechanisms (e.g., fixed capital vs. crew rigidity) and align with the labor-supply framing.

- **Address measurement error concerns in fishing hours.** The manuscript notes that GFW may misclassify transit hours as fishing, especially when catch is low. If false positives are correlated with illumination (e.g., during full moons boats make more dead time around lights), this could bias the estimated lunar effect toward zero. Consider leveraging the distinction between “fishing hours” and “hours present” (available in the dataset) to show that the ratio behaves differently across the lunar cycle—if the ratio falls, it would imply misclassification; if it stays flat, it reassures the reader.

- **Enhance policy framing with quantitative magnitudes.** The WTO policy discussion hinges on whether subsidies keep fleets fishing through the moon. Translate the bounds into policy-relevant terms: what would a subsidy-induced 10% reduction mean in terms of extra vessel-days per cycle, fuel burned, or catch forgone? Similarly, contextualize the 3.1% reduction (approx. 100 hours) in terms of actual economic losses—are these days still profitable despite low catch? Such magnitudes would help policymakers decide whether the detected inertia matters relative to other subsidy effects.

- **Discuss alternative theories for the null.** The Discussion section mentions fixed costs, crew contracts, and spatial substitution. Consider complementing this with more direct evidence—e.g., if vessel deployments are long (multi-week), show that average trip length is much longer than a lunar phase, supporting the sunk-cost narrative. If data permit, document average wages or crew contracts to substantiate labor rigidity. Alternatively, discuss whether fleets may be “moon-aware” but constrained by coordination costs—are captains able to cancel fishing days quickly, or do they have to commit before knowing the next phase? Including such operational detail (even if qualitative) strengthens the interpretation of inertia as structural.

- **Expand robustness for the core null.** The paper already reports quadratic specs and day-of-week FE. Additional checks could include: (i) using instrumental variables that shift illumination (e.g., actual moonrise times, which vary by latitude), (ii) focusing on the 3-4 days around the full moon where illumination is highest to test for nonlinear effects, and (iii) investigating whether other gear types with intermediate light dependence (e.g., purse seiners with spotlights) show intermediate results.

- **Clarify theoretical expectations.** The introduction sets up perfect foresight and the expectation of substitution. It might help to reference a simple model (or previous theory) showing that, absent constraints, effort should rise during high-CPUE periods to smooth income (i.e., positive substitution elasticity). A brief equilibrium sketch would help readers understand why a nearly flat response is surprising, thereby highlighting the theoretical contribution.

Overall, the project is promising and well-aligned with the manifest. Addressing these points—especially the spatial substitution test and the power of the main interaction—will strengthen both the empirical credibility and the paper’s policy relevance.
