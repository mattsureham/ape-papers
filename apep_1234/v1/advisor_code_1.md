**Idea Fidelity**

The paper closely follows the manifest: it exploits Panama’s distinct bank license types to construct a within-country difference-in-differences, uses the SBP monthly financial indicators and bank listings, and targets the question of whether FATF grey-listing hurts internationally oriented banks relative to domestically focused peers. Key elements of the identification strategy (license-type treatment/control definition, grey-listing timing, pre- and post-periods including the de-listing reversal, and mitigation of the COVID confound) are present. The main deviation is a stronger emphasis in the paper on the null result and the “compliance illusion” framing than on any precise quantification of flow effects, but this difference flows naturally from the underlying data and does not undermine factual fidelity to the manifest.

**Summary**

The paper studies whether Panama’s June 2019 FATF grey-listing differentially affected the profitability of International License banks (100% cross-border) versus Panamanian Private General License banks (domestic deposit base). Using 122 months of SBP monthly indicators and a two-unit panel difference-in-differences with Driscoll–Kraay standard errors, it finds no statistically significant decline in ROA, ROAA, ROE, or capital adequacy for the treated group during the grey-listing window; robustness checks, event studies, placebo tests, and de-listing reversals sustain this null. The authors interpret the result as evidence of a “compliance illusion”: heightened scrutiny does not translate into differential profitability losses for the most exposed banks.

**Essential Points**

1. **Inference with Two Cross-Sectional Units.** The design relies on only two bank-type aggregates (International vs. Panamanian Private) observed over time. While the long time series helps, any DiD drawing on two units risks being entirely driven by unobserved time-varying factors that differentially affect the aggregates (e.g., differential shifts in portfolio composition, regulatory actions, or business cycles). Relying solely on Driscoll–Kraay standard errors does not solve this fundamental issue because it does not restore identification—it only adjusts the variance estimate. The authors should provide stronger justification (or evidence) that no contemporaneous shocks besides grey-listing differentially affected the two groups; otherwise the null may be driven by omitted time-varying confounders.

2. **Pre-Trend Evidence Suggests Anticipation.** The event-study appendix shows a statistically significant negative coefficient on ROA (and ROE) in the $-24$ to $-18$ month bin, coinciding with the FATF mutual evaluation visit. This timing suggests banks were already adjusting before June 2019, which violates the parallel-trends assumption. Although the main text argues the effect is limited to ROE and an anticipation window, the significance and size of the coefficient for ROA ($-0.64$ pp, $p<0.05$) undermines that defense. If banks changed behavior before the official treatment date, the canonical DiD estimate conflates grey-listing with anticipatory adjustments. The authors need to more rigorously account for this by either redefining treatment timing, explicitly modeling anticipation, or showing that the pre-trend disappears once observable covariates (e.g., global compliance risk indicators) are included.

3. **Aggregate-level data mask heterogeneity and mechanisms.** The paper’s conclusion—no differential profitability cost—is based on aggregated bank-type averages, which may conceal offsetting movements across individual banks (exit of marginal players, composition shifts toward higher-margin assets). Without access to bank-level data, it is difficult to rule out substantial internal reallocation that still leaves the means unchanged. Moreover, the primary claim is about causal effects on the “most exposed” banks, yet aggregate averages over 19 International License banks do not guarantee that the targeted banks (e.g., the smaller ones reliant on correspondent lines) experienced no harm. The authors should either obtain finer-grained data or demonstrate that the aggregate mask cannot conceal meaningful heterogeneity (e.g., by reconstructing a pseudo-panel using quarterly balance sheet shares or assets to proxy for composition changes).

Given these issues, particularly the threat to parallel trends and the reliance on only two aggregates, I am hesitant to endorse the causal interpretation as-is. If substantial additional evidence is not forthcoming, the paper may fall short of AER: Insights standards.

**Suggestions**

1. **Strengthen identification through additional units or dimensions.** Consider exploiting the third available license category—foreign private General License banks—as a separate cross-section and possibly using a triple-difference (International vs. Panamanian vs. foreign) to add variation. Alternatively, break the International License group into large vs. small banks if data permit, or incorporate other counterfactual groups (e.g., representative offices’ profitability proxies) to enrich the cross-sectional dimension. This would mitigate concerns about relying on two units.

2. **Model anticipation explicitly.** The significant pre-treatment dip suggests that grey-listing effects started before the official date. Introduce a lead indicator in the DiD specification or redefine the treatment period to begin at the FATF evaluation visit. Present event studies with leads and lags plotted (not just table coefficients) to visualize the dynamics. If anticipatory effects are substantive, discuss whether the estimand is the total effect of grey-listing momentum or the discrete post-listing shock.

3. **Investigate quantity and composition channels.** Since the outcome is a ratio (ROA/ROE), it is crucial to examine whether numerator and denominator moved offsettingly. Use the quarterly balance-sheet data to assess trends in total assets, deposits, loans, and correspondent banking exposures by license type. If international banks reduced assets but maintained ROA through higher margins, that is an important complement to the profitability null. Show whether asset growth (or shrinkage), deposit flows, or loan composition changed differentially during grey-listing.

4. **Assess heterogeneity within the aggregated groups.** While individual bank data may not be published, the monthly “Listado de Bancos” can be used to reconstruct bank counts and, potentially, start dates. Use these to proxy for entry/exit composition effects: e.g., did the average size (assets) of International License banks change (use publicly available balance sheets or total sector aggregates divided by counts)? If some banks exited, the average could stay stable even as the marginal banks were impacted. Providing such evidence would bolster the claim that the null is not driven by offsetting heterogeneity.

5. **Enhance robustness and inference.** Beyond Driscoll–Kraay, complement with permutation inference (e.g., randomly assign the “International” label to one of the control categories) to assess how unusual the observed coefficient is. Present sensitivity analyses to alternative bandwidths, but also to different definitions of the grey-listing window (e.g., excluding the first 3–6 months after listing if compliance costs ramped up over time). Report the estimated effects on ROA/ROE across multiple tangible specifications (e.g., weighted averages, winsorized series) to ensure the null is not an artifact of outliers or measurement error.

6. **Clarify the interpretation of null results.** The paper already notes that the null does not imply no economic cost, but it could further discuss statistical power. Provide minimal detectable effects given the sample variance and Driscoll–Kraay SEs. This will help readers understand whether the data are capable of rejecting moderately sized effects. If the confidence interval is wide (e.g., contains economically meaningful negative values), the policy takeaway should be more cautious.

7. **Tie mechanisms to observable implications.** The three proposed mechanisms (portfolio rebalancing, selective de-risking, cost diffusion) could each generate testable predictions. For example, portfolio rebalancing would imply differential asset mix shifts (toward trade finance vs. other assets) that could be checked in the quarterly detail. Selective de-risking might show up as concentration changes (Herfindahl index) or changes in correspondent banking counts. Cost diffusion could lead to similar compliance cost indicators across groups (e.g., staff costs, compliance expenditures if available). Working through at least one of these would make the “compliance illusion” story more concrete.

8. **Situate the null in the broader literature carefully.** Existing studies document aggregate flow reductions. Clarify how the null observed here complements or contrasts with those findings. For example, if aggregate flows declined but profitability stayed stable, it suggests quantity effects with margin adjustments—highlighting that the “cost of grey-listing” may manifest differently across margins. This would strengthen the paper’s contribution beyond just “no effect on profitability.”

Implementing these suggestions would markedly improve the paper’s causal credibility and explanatory power.
