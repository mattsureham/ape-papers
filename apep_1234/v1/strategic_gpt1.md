## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple, policy-relevant question: when the FATF grey-lists a country, do the banks most exposed to cross-border compliance pressure actually suffer? Using Panama’s unusual banking structure, the paper compares internationally licensed banks—whose business is almost entirely cross-border—to domestically oriented banks, and finds little evidence that grey-listing differentially reduced the profitability of the internationally exposed banks.

A busy economist should care because FATF grey-listing is routinely treated as a serious sanction in policy circles, yet we know surprisingly little about who actually bears its costs inside a country. If the most exposed intermediaries appear resilient, then either the standard story about how grey-listing works is wrong, or the economically relevant effects lie somewhere other than bank profitability.

**Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Pretty well, but not quite sharply enough. The current introduction is competent and readable, but it still leans too much on “I exploit within-country variation” and “the literature uses cross-country comparisons.” That is useful, but it is not the real hook. The hook is that a widely presumed enforcement mechanism may not operate as advertised.

**What the first two paragraphs should say instead:**

> FATF grey-listing is widely believed to damage a country’s international financial sector by raising compliance costs, straining correspondent banking relationships, and deterring cross-border activity. But does grey-listing actually hurt the intermediaries it is supposed to pressure? Despite strong policy claims, there is little direct evidence on which institutions inside a listed country bear the costs.
>
> This paper studies that question in Panama, where banking licenses create a clean contrast between banks devoted to international business and banks with domestic funding bases. If grey-listing works through cross-border compliance pressure, internationally licensed banks should be the first place to see it. Yet I find little evidence that Panama’s 2019–2023 FATF grey-listing differentially reduced their profitability. The broader implication is that grey-listing may generate visible compliance activity without imposing the targeted financial pressure that policymakers often assume.

That version leads with the world question and the puzzle, not with the method.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution in one sentence:**  
The paper provides within-country evidence from Panama that FATF grey-listing did not differentially reduce the profitability of the banks most exposed to cross-border compliance pressure, challenging the common assumption that grey-listing works by directly penalizing international intermediaries.

### Evaluation

**Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?**  
Only partially. The paper says the literature is mostly cross-country and aggregate; that is useful, but still too generic. To feel distinctive, it needs to name the exact contrast more crisply:

- Existing papers estimate average country-level effects of grey-listing on capital flows, FDI, or macro-financial aggregates.
- This paper asks an incidence question within the banking system: who bears the burden?
- Existing de-risking/correspondent banking papers emphasize cross-border retrenchment but do not show whether exposed banks lose profitability.
- This paper’s novelty is not “another setting” but “a different margin of adjustment.”

Right now a reader can still summarize it as “a DiD on bank profitability in Panama after grey-listing.” That is too small.

**Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or filling a gap in a LITERATURE?**  
It starts with the world, which is good, but quickly retreats into literature-gap language: “existing empirical literature relies entirely on cross-country comparisons.” That is not the strongest version. The better framing is: **How do international financial sanctions-like designations actually bite? Through which institutions, and on which margins?** That is a world question.

**Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what's new?**  
Some could, but many would still say: “It’s a DiD using Panama’s bank license categories to show no profitability effect of grey-listing.” That’s too method-forward and too narrow. The introduction needs to make “the presumed mechanism fails at the intended target” the memorable takeaway.

**What would make this contribution bigger? Be specific.**  
The biggest issue is scope. Profitability is probably too downstream and too equilibrium-laden to carry a top-journal contribution by itself. To make the contribution larger, the paper would need at least one of the following:

1. **A more direct outcome variable:**  
   Cross-border payment volumes, correspondent banking links, foreign liabilities, trade finance, offshore deposits, fee income, noninterest income, or asset composition. If the claim is about de-risking and compliance burden, profitability is an indirect proxy.

2. **A sharper mechanism:**  
   Show whether international banks preserved profits by shrinking assets, repricing services, increasing spreads, shifting portfolios, or relying on preexisting compliance capacity.

3. **A stronger comparative framing:**  
   Compare Panama to other grey-listed financial centers with similar multi-license banking systems, or benchmark Panama’s bank-level incidence against country-level aggregate effects documented elsewhere.

4. **A reframed question:**  
   Instead of “does grey-listing hurt banks,” ask “why do aggregate sanctions-like designations sometimes have visible political salience but muted incidence on targeted intermediaries?” That is broader and more important.

If the paper stays with only profitability ratios, the contribution will remain modest.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

Economics is a conversation, and this paper is not yet having the most valuable one.

### Closest neighbors

Based on the cited papers and topic, the nearest literatures seem to be:

1. **FATF grey-listing / AML-CFT macro effects**
   - IMF or policy studies on grey-listing and capital flows, such as Nkusu et al. and Collin et al.
   - Masciandaro and coauthors on AML/CFT enforcement and financial outcomes.
2. **Correspondent banking / de-risking**
   - Erbenova et al. (IMF SDN) on the withdrawal of correspondent banking relationships.
   - Johannesen and Zucman-style work on offshore finance / tax haven responses, though not exact neighbors.
3. **Bank regulatory adaptation**
   - Acharya, Laeven, Dagher-type papers on how banks adjust portfolios or business models under regulatory pressure.

### How should it position itself relative to those neighbors?

**Build on them, then narrow the disagreement.**  
The paper should not “attack” the macro grey-listing literature; those papers answer different questions. It should say:

- Cross-country papers show aggregate declines in flows or broad economic costs.
- Those findings do not tell us where inside a financial system the burden lands.
- De-risking papers imply cross-border intermediaries should be especially exposed.
- This paper tests that incidence prediction within one country.

That is a constructive bridge. The current paper edges toward claiming something stronger—almost that the de-risking narrative is overstated altogether. With the current evidence, that is too ambitious. What it can credibly say is: **profitability incidence does not line up neatly with the standard narrative.**

### Is the paper currently positioned too narrowly or too broadly?

Oddly, both.

- **Too narrowly** in empirical framing: Panama, one event, one banking system, profitability ratios.
- **Too broadly** in rhetoric: “The compliance illusion” sounds like a general theory of FATF enforcement from one case study.

The title and discussion promise a broad conceptual intervention; the evidence is much more case-specific. That mismatch will bother top readers.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?

It should speak more explicitly to:

- **Sanctions and regulatory incidence**: who actually bears compliance shocks?
- **Reputational regulation / soft law / international organizations**: FATF is not just financial regulation; it is transnational governance.
- **Offshore finance and financial centers**: Panama is not just “a country with banks”; it is a specific type of international financial center.
- **Misallocation of regulatory burden / pass-through**: when regulations target one margin but costs diffuse system-wide.

The most impactful version of the paper might connect FATF grey-listing to a broader literature on **how international monitoring regimes create compliance theater, political pressure, and aggregate reputational effects without necessarily imposing concentrated costs on intended private actors**.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?

Not fully. It is currently having a somewhat technical conversation about identification relative to cross-country FATF studies. The more interesting conversation is about **mechanisms of international regulatory pressure**. That conversation has a wider audience.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Grey-listing is widely believed to raise compliance burdens and damage international finance, especially in countries reliant on cross-border banking. Panama is an especially relevant case because it is a major international banking center and its banking licenses create stark differences in exposure.

### Tension
If the standard de-risking/compliance story is right, internationally licensed banks should be hit hardest. Yet we do not know whether the burden actually shows up where theory and policy rhetoric say it should.

### Resolution
The paper finds little evidence that Panama’s internationally licensed banks suffered a differential decline in profitability during the grey-listing episode.

### Implications
Either the burden was absorbed elsewhere, diffused across the system, offset by adaptation, or manifested in outcomes other than profitability. That matters for how we understand FATF as a policy tool.

### Evaluation

The paper has the skeleton of a narrative arc, but it is not fully developed. Right now it is halfway between a story and a specification sheet.

What weakens the arc:

- The paper quickly becomes a design paper: within-country heterogeneity, 41 pre months, delisting reversal, placebo.
- The “compliance illusion” language is evocative, but the evidence provided is narrower than the phrase suggests.
- The mechanisms are speculative rather than integral to the narrative.

So the paper is **not** just a random collection of results, but it is also **not yet a fully satisfying story**. The story it should tell is:

> “Grey-listing is supposed to work by putting pressure on cross-border banks. Panama gives us a rare chance to test that mechanism inside one banking center. The striking fact is that the most exposed banks do not show the expected profitability damage. That does not mean grey-listing is harmless; it means its incidence and mechanism are more subtle than policymakers assume.”

That is cleaner and more defensible than “grey-listing is a compliance illusion.”

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

### What fact would I lead with?
“I’d lead with: during Panama’s FATF grey-listing, the banks whose business model was most dependent on cross-border intermediation did not see a clear relative profitability decline.”

That is the dinner-party fact.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
Among international finance, development, and banking economists, some would lean in—because FATF and de-risking are policy-relevant and under-studied. But many top economists would reach for their phones unless the framing becomes broader and the outcome becomes more direct. Profitability nulls in one country are not automatically gripping.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Almost certainly: **“If not profitability, then where did the costs show up?”**  
That is the central issue. The paper currently does not have a strong answer. A second likely question: **“Is Panama special because banks had already adapted after the earlier listing and Panama Papers?”**

### If findings are null or modest: is the null interesting?
Potentially yes, but the paper needs to work harder to justify it. A null is interesting when it overturns a strong prior on an outcome that should have moved. Here, the paper does have a strong prior story. But to make the null compelling, it must do three things more explicitly:

1. Show that profitability is a meaningful place where one would truly expect the burden to show up.
2. Argue that Panama is not just a weird, fully adapted repeat-listing case.
3. Reframe the null as evidence on **incidence and adjustment**, not simply “no effect.”

As written, it is close to interesting but still risks reading like a failed hunt for an expected negative coefficient.

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

Without rewriting the paper, several changes would make it read much better.

### 1. Front-load the conceptual contribution
The introduction currently reveals the empirical result reasonably early, but it still spends too much effort selling the design. Move the conceptual point ahead of the method:

- First: conventional belief
- Second: why this is a test of mechanism/incidence
- Third: the headline result
- Fourth: only then explain why Panama is useful

### 2. Shorten the identification/inference prose in the introduction
AER readers do not need, in paragraph 3 of the introduction, the full architecture of the pre period, post period, reversal test, etc. That is valuable later. The introduction should spend that space on why the result changes how we think about grey-listing.

### 3. Pull the strongest “what this does and does not mean” paragraph earlier
The discussion section contains one of the paper’s most important points: the null on profitability does **not** mean grey-listing had no economic effect. That caveat should appear much earlier, probably in the introduction, because it sharpens rather than weakens the contribution.

### 4. Reconsider the title
“The Compliance Illusion” is catchy, but it oversells. The evidence is about relative bank profitability in one country. A title that is slightly more concrete would better fit the paper’s actual contribution. For example:

- “Who Bears the Cost of FATF Grey-Listing? Evidence from Panama’s Banking System”
- “FATF Grey-Listing and the Incidence of Compliance Pressure: Evidence from Panama”

You can still use “compliance illusion” as a phrase inside the paper if desired.

### 5. Move some defensive detail to the appendix
The paper occasionally reads like it anticipates skepticism sentence by sentence. That is understandable, but it clutters the narrative. Some of the longer inferential and design-defense material can be compressed in the main text and shifted to an appendix discussion.

### 6. Bring any mechanism-relevant descriptive evidence into the main text
If there is any available descriptive evidence on asset composition, bank counts, or business model adjustment, that belongs in the main paper, not buried in appendices or discussion. Right now the mechanism section is mostly conjectural.

### 7. The conclusion should do more than summarize
The paper ends with a standard implications paragraph. It would be stronger if the conclusion distilled one changed belief:
- not “grey-listing has no costs,”
- but “grey-listing may not discipline international finance through the institutions policymakers think it does.”

That is the intellectually useful takeaway.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

Be honest: in its current form, this is **not an AER paper**. The main gap is not just polish. It is strategic.

### What is the core problem?

Mostly a **scope + ambition problem**, with some framing issues.

- **Not mainly a framing problem:** the paper is actually written fairly clearly.
- **Partly a novelty problem:** within-country FATF incidence is new-ish, but not obviously important enough on its own.
- **Mainly a scope problem:** one case, one episode, one indirect outcome.
- **Also an ambition problem:** the paper tests a narrow implication rather than using Panama to answer a larger question about international regulatory enforcement.

The result is competent and potentially publishable in a good field journal, but top-field excitement usually requires either:
1. a much bigger question,
2. much richer evidence,
3. or a finding that more decisively changes beliefs.

This paper is closest to having (1), but it has not fully claimed it.

### What is the gap between current form and a paper that would excite the top 10 people in this field?

A top paper would need to show not just that profitability did not move, but **where the costs went instead**. For example:

- fewer correspondent banking relationships,
- lower cross-border volumes,
- compositional shifts in assets,
- higher spreads/fees,
- exit of smaller institutions,
- or diffusion of compliance costs to all banks.

Without that, the reader is left with “interesting null, unclear mechanism, uncertain generality.”

### Single most impactful piece of advice

**If the author can change only one thing: broaden the paper from a null on bank profitability to a paper about the incidence and adjustment margins of FATF grey-listing.**

Concretely: add at least one more direct outcome that sits closer to the de-risking mechanism, and rewrite the paper around the question “where do the costs of grey-listing actually land?” rather than “is the DiD coefficient on ROA significant?”

That would materially change the paper’s ceiling.
