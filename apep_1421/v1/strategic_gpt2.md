## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple, policy-relevant question: when India forced mining firms to channel a share of royalties back to mining-affected districts, did those large earmarked transfers produce visible local economic development? Using district-level nightlights, the paper’s headline claim is no: despite billions of dollars in mandated revenue redistribution, mining districts do not show detectable post-reform gains, and in the most mineral-intensive states may even do slightly worse.

A busy economist should care because this is not just a paper about one Indian policy. It speaks to a broader question: can resource revenue sharing actually compensate places that bear the costs of extraction, or do transfers fail without state capacity and implementation capability?

### Does the paper itself articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Mostly yes, but not optimally. The current introduction is competent and readable, but it leads with institutional detail before fully crystallizing the broader economic question. The first paragraph is vivid, but the second paragraph gets quickly into percentages, acronyms, and program rules. For AER purposes, the opening should more aggressively foreground the big issue: governments around the world promise that resource revenues will be shared with affected communities; this paper tests whether that promise translates into development.

### The pitch the paper should have

“Governments increasingly respond to the political costs of extraction by promising to share mineral revenues with local communities. But do earmarked resource transfers actually improve local development, or does the combination of weak implementation, capture, and fiscal fungibility break the link between revenue and welfare? This paper studies India’s 2015 creation of District Mineral Foundations—the world’s largest mandatory benefit-sharing program in mining—and finds that districts more exposed to the resulting revenue flows experienced no detectable increase in economic activity, as measured by satellite nightlights. The broader lesson is that getting resource money to local governments is not enough; institutional capacity to convert funds into development may be the real binding constraint.”

That is the opening this paper wants.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper’s contribution is to show that one of the world’s largest earmarked mineral revenue-sharing reforms generated no detectable improvement in local economic activity in India’s mining districts, suggesting that revenue redistribution alone does not ensure local development.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest papers?

Partially, but not sharply enough. The paper identifies Aragón and Rud (Peru’s canon minero) as the main contrast, and that is the right instinct. But right now the differentiation is still too generic: “Peru found positive effects; India finds null effects.” That is not yet a deep contribution unless the paper is very explicit about what differs conceptually:

- unrestricted transfers to existing local governments vs. earmarked funds through newly created district institutions,
- fiscal capacity vs. low administrative capacity,
- transfer receipt vs. transfer conversion,
- local compensation promises vs. actual development outcomes.

The paper should make clear that it is not merely another local-resource-curse paper and not merely another transfer paper. Its central novelty is the disconnect between **formal benefit-sharing** and **realized development**.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a gap in a literature?

It is mixed, leaning too much toward literature-gap framing. The stronger version is clearly a question about the world: *when states promise local compensation from resource extraction, does that promise work?* That is much better than “I contribute to three literatures.” The three-literature paragraph is serviceable, but it has the familiar “slotting” feel of a paper trying to justify itself by bibliography rather than by importance.

### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what’s new here?

At present, maybe, but not confidently. They might say: “It’s a DiD using nightlights showing India’s mining royalties didn’t help.” That is not enough. You want them to say: “It shows that even very large, earmarked local resource transfers can fail completely when institutions for spending are weak—so the bottleneck is not revenue assignment but state capacity.”

Right now the paper risks reading like “another reduced-form policy evaluation with a null.”

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Several specific possibilities:

1. **Measure the transfer more directly.**  
   The biggest upgrade would be district-level DMF collections and expenditures, not mining employment as a proxy. That would let the paper become about the effect of actual fiscal inflows and spending, rather than exposure to a reform. Strategically, that is the difference between an interesting national policy study and a paper with broader public-finance bite.

2. **Move beyond nightlights to outcomes closer to the policy’s stated objectives.**  
   The program was meant to fund water, health, schools, roads, environmental remediation. A null on nightlights is useful, but not fully decisive. If the paper could show no improvement in roads, health facility access, schooling infrastructure, or water-related outcomes, the result would become much more persuasive and policy-relevant.

3. **Demonstrate the mechanism through spending failure, not just speculate it.**  
   The paper invokes underutilization, elite capture, and fungibility. As written, these are plausible stories, but still stories. If the authors can document low spending rates, project delays, composition of spending, or substitution out of state expenditure, the paper becomes much larger.

4. **Exploit institutional heterogeneity.**  
   The most compelling framing may be comparative within India: where did DMFs work better, if anywhere? Did effects differ by baseline district capacity, state administrative quality, preexisting panchayat strength, or mining intensity? That would turn a flat null into a more general lesson about when revenue-sharing works.

In short: the current contribution is respectable, but a bigger paper would connect **resource transfers → spending/institutions → development outcomes**, rather than stopping at the first and last nodes.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

### Closest neighbors

The closest neighbors appear to be:

1. **Aragón and Rud (2013)** on Peru’s canon minero and local welfare effects.
2. **van der Ploeg / resource curse and local resource effects**—including local Dutch disease and political distortions.
3. **Litschig and Morrison (2013)** or similar papers on intergovernmental transfers and local public goods.
4. **Reinikka and Svensson (2004)** and **Olken (2007)** on leakage, local capture, and implementation failure in public spending.
5. Possibly **Asher et al. / SHRUG-related work** using spatial Indian data and nightlights, though that is more data/method than substantive neighbor.

Depending on how the field is defined, one might also include work on decentralization and fiscal federalism in developing countries, and papers on compensating communities harmed by extraction.

### How should the paper position itself relative to those neighbors?

Mostly **build on and contrast**, not attack.

- Relative to **Aragón and Rud**, the paper should say: “prior work shows that formula-based mineral revenue transfers can improve welfare under some institutional conditions; this paper shows those conditions are not automatic.” That is stronger than implying one result invalidates the other.
- Relative to transfer/leakage papers, it should say: “we bring the logic of public-finance implementation failure into the extractives context.”
- Relative to the resource-curse literature, it should be careful: the paper is not really identifying a resource curse per se; it is identifying a failure of a compensatory transfer mechanism. That distinction matters.

### Is the paper currently positioned too narrowly or too broadly?

It is currently positioned a bit **too broadly and too mechanically**. The “three literatures” paragraph feels like it is trying to tick boxes. Yet in substance the paper is actually narrower: it is about whether mandated local mineral revenue sharing translates into development under weak institutional capacity.

The right audience is the intersection of:
- development public finance,
- political economy of resource extraction,
- decentralization/state capacity,
- and applied policy evaluation.

That is a coherent and important conversation. The paper should lean into that, rather than stretching to “nightlights literature” as a contribution category. The fact that it uses nightlights is not itself a major contribution.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?

Two gaps stand out:

1. **Benefit-sharing / social license / extractive governance literature**  
   The paper should connect more directly to work on how governments and firms try to compensate communities affected by extraction. That is the most natural policy conversation.

2. **State capacity / implementation literature**  
   If the claim is that money arrived but development did not, then state capacity is central, not peripheral. The paper currently gestures at this but does not really anchor itself there.

3. **Fiscal incidence / fungibility in decentralization**  
   The fungibility angle is promising but underdeveloped. There is a larger intergovernmental transfers literature that could sharpen the paper’s broader relevance.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?

Not quite yet. Right now it is having the conversation, “here is a null reduced-form estimate on an Indian mining policy.” The more impactful conversation is: **why do compensatory resource transfers fail to compensate?** That is the conversation AER readers are more likely to care about.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Before this paper, the world looks like this: resource-rich places often remain poor despite generating large rents, and governments increasingly try to address that by earmarking mining revenues for local communities. India’s DMF reform is a huge real-world test of that idea.

### Tension

The core tension is strong and should be emphasized more: if the political problem with extraction is that affected communities do not share in the gains, then a massive mandatory benefit-sharing regime should visibly improve local conditions. But there are reasons to doubt that—weak administrative capacity, delayed spending, capture, and substitution could sever the link between transfers and development.

### Resolution

The paper’s resolution is that there is no detectable positive local development effect in aggregate, despite very large transfers, with suggestive negative effects in the most exposed states.

### Implications

The implication is potentially important: assigning revenues locally is not sufficient; institutional capacity and spending effectiveness matter at least as much as the transfer itself. More broadly, the political promise of “resource revenue sharing” may be much easier to legislate than to implement.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the ingredients, but the arc is not fully disciplined. The paper does have a real story, but it occasionally drifts into “collection of results looking for a story,” especially because the mechanisms are not directly shown and because the literature discussion is somewhat list-like.

The story it should be telling is:

1. Mining communities are promised compensation through mandated revenue sharing.
2. India creates the largest such program in a developing country.
3. This is a crucial test of whether earmarked resource transfers solve the local resource curse / compensation problem.
4. They do not, at least not in measurable local economic activity.
5. Therefore the binding constraint is likely not access to resource revenue, but the institutional ability to spend it productively.

That is a clean story. The paper is close to it, but should strip away some of the secondary framing and tell that story more relentlessly.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?

“I’d lead with this: India routed more than $6.5 billion in mining-related funds to affected districts, and the paper finds no detectable increase in local economic activity.”

That is the attention-grabber.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

Some would lean in—especially development, public finance, and political economy economists—because the policy scale is large and the finding is counterintuitive. But the reaction depends heavily on how the result is framed.

If the presentation is “we ran a DiD on nightlights and found a null,” phones come out quickly.  
If the presentation is “the world’s largest attempt to compensate mining communities appears not to have generated local development,” people lean in.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Immediately: **why not?**  
And second: **is nightlights the right margin?**

Those are the paper’s central strategic vulnerabilities. The paper cannot stop at “no detectable effect” and then offer three speculative explanations. It needs to make the “why not” answer more concrete, even descriptively.

### If the findings are null or modest, is the null itself interesting?

Yes, potentially very interesting. But only if the paper convincingly frames the null as informative about a first-order policy debate. This is not a failed experiment if the paper can persuade readers that:

- the policy was large enough that meaningful effects were plausible,
- the outcome should have moved if development had materialized at scale,
- and the absence of movement teaches us something important about the constraints on compensatory transfers.

At present, the paper is halfway there. It has the scale and policy relevance. What it lacks is stronger conversion of the null into a broader lesson.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The gap is mostly a combination of **framing problem**, **scope problem**, and a bit of **ambition problem**.

### Framing problem
The science may be fine, but the story is still a little too much “policy evaluation of India’s DMF reform” and not enough “a general lesson about the failure of compensatory resource transfers under weak institutional capacity.”

### Scope problem
The paper currently relies on a single broad outcome and inferred mechanisms. For AER, this is likely too thin. A null on nightlights alone is rarely enough unless the conceptual contribution is overwhelming. The paper needs either:
- richer outcomes closer to the policy’s objectives, or
- direct fiscal/spending data, or
- much sharper evidence on mechanism and heterogeneity.

### Novelty problem
The core result is interesting, but a skeptical reader may say: “another paper showing transfers don’t do much under weak governance.” To rise above that, the paper must make the institutional design of DMFs the central novelty: *earmarked local benefit-sharing through newly created district bodies can fail even at large scale*.

### Ambition problem
The paper is competent and clean, but currently safe. It doesn’t yet fully exploit the scale and uniqueness of the setting. The title and abstract promise a big statement; the body still reads like a careful first-pass evaluation.

### Single most impactful advice

If the author can only change one thing, it should be this:

**Rebuild the paper around the broader question of whether local resource revenue-sharing can compensate affected communities, and add direct evidence on the missing link between transfers and outcomes—ideally district-level DMF collections/spending or policy-targeted outcomes—so the paper explains not just that development failed to appear, but why.**

That is the move that could turn this from a solid field paper into something that top people in development/public finance/resource economics would genuinely discuss.
