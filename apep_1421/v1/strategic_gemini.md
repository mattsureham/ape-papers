## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
The paper evaluates India’s 2015 mining reform, which mandated the redistribution of $6.5 billion in royalties to mining-affected districts. Using satellite nightlights and a continuous DiD design, it finds that despite massive revenue inflows, there was no detectable increase in local economic activity—and in the most mineral-rich areas, activity potentially declined. This matters because it challenges the assumption that "benefit-sharing" is a panacea for the local resource curse, suggesting that institutional capacity is a more binding constraint than capital.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this well in the first paragraph but drifts into institutional minutiae in the second. It should stay at the "big picture" level longer.
**The Pitch the paper should have:** "While economists have long debated the 'resource curse' at the national level, policy has pivoted toward local benefit-sharing as a solution. This paper evaluates the world's largest experiment in mandated local mineral royalty redistribution—India's 2015 reform—to ask: can billions of dollars in earmarked transfers actually transform the economic trajectory of mining communities? The answer is a precise 'no,' suggesting that without institutional capacity, revenue channels become 'transfer traps' rather than development engines."

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**Contribution:** The paper provides the first causal evidence that massive, earmarked resource-revenue transfers to local governments can fail entirely to generate measurable economic growth in a developing country context.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It is well-differentiated from **Aragón and Rud (2013)** by highlighting the "unrestricted vs. earmarked" and "existing vs. new institutional" divides. 
*   **Framing:** It is framed as a question about the **WORLD** (does benefit sharing work?), which is its strongest asset.
*   **Clarity:** A smart economist would call this "The Indian Mineral Null."
*   **What would make it bigger?** To reach the AER, the contribution needs to move from a "precise null" to a "documented mechanism." The "So What?" currently relies on a 2021 audit report. If the author could use district-level budget data or project-level data to *show* the money sitting in bank accounts or being diverted to non-productive uses, the paper moves from an empirical observation to a fundamental lesson in public economics.

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
*   **Neighbors:** **Aragón and Rud (2013)** (Local resource curse); **Caselli and Michaels (2013)** (Oil and local spending in Brazil); **Gadenne (2017)** (Tax vs. transfers in India); **Asher and Novosad (2020)** (Mining and local development in India).
*   **Positioning:** It currently "adds to" these literatures. It should instead **complicate** them. It should position itself as the "Institutional Capacity" counterpoint to the "Resource Windfall" literature.
*   **Unexpected Connection:** The paper could benefit from connecting to the **"Flypaper Effect"** literature. Why didn't the money stick? Is it because it's earmarked (mental accounting at the government level)?

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC
*   **Setup:** Developing nations adopt "benefit-sharing" to pacify mining regions.
*   **Tension:** India pours $6.5B into these regions—the "best case scenario" for local redistribution.
*   **Resolution:** Nothing happens. Luminous intensity remains flat.
*   **Implications:** Policy design that focuses on *revenue* without *capacity* is doomed.

**Evaluation:** The narrative arc is strong and "clean." However, the "Resolution" is currently a bit of a letdown because we don't see the gears of the failure. The paper feels like a "black box" null.

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
*   **The Lead Fact:** "India gave $6.5 billion back to its poorest mining districts, and you can't even see a single new lightbulb from space."
*   **Reaction:** People will lean in. They will ask: "Where did the money go?"
*   **The Follow-up:** "Was it stolen, or is the Indian bureaucracy just that slow?" 
*   **The Null:** This is a "Golden Null." It is interesting because it contradicts the naive "more money = more growth" model in a high-stakes setting.

---

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
*   **Front-loading:** The results are clearly presented early (Table 2). This is good.
*   **Appendix:** The DMSP calibration is vital for the pre-trend argument but belongs in a technical appendix to keep the main narrative focused on the 2015–2021 period.
*   **Discussion:** Section 6 is too speculative. It needs to be shorter OR backed by a bit more "suggestive" data (e.g., correlations between the null and measures of district-level governance).

---

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The paper is currently a "very high-quality empirical note." To make it a full AER article, it needs **Ambitious Mechanisms.** 

AER reviewers will be frustrated by the "we don't know why" conclusion. The author needs to hunt for *any* data that sheds light on the "Implementation Gap" vs. "Elite Capture" debate. Even if it's only for a subset of districts, showing that "Districts with higher corruption scores (or lower administrative capacity) saw lower spending rates" would transform this from an India-specific evaluation to a general theory of "The Transfer Trap."

**Single Biggest Piece of Advice:** Move beyond the nightlights "black box" by incorporating whatever data is available on the *actual spending* or *composition of projects* within the DMFs to prove why the money failed to generate growth.
