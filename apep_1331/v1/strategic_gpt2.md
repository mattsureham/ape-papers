## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper studies a simple and important question: when regulators ban a blatantly conflicted compensation scheme in financial advice, do consumers actually end up better off? Using UK ombudsman data around the 2020 ban on contingent charging for defined-benefit pension transfer advice, the paper argues that the ban did not reduce complaint volume but did increase the share of complaints resolved in consumers’ favor, suggesting an improvement in the quality or merit of disputes reaching redress.

A busy economist should care because this is, at least in aspiration, a paper about whether conflict-of-interest regulation changes real consumer harm rather than just changing market structure, prices, or sales patterns.

### Does the paper articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Not quite. The introduction is competent, but it leads with institutional detail and then frames the paper as “filling a gap” left by an FCA review. That is too small and too bureaucratic a pitch for AER. “The regulator didn’t study consumer outcomes, so I do” is not a strong opening claim. The paper’s best version is not about completing a post-implementation review; it is about the welfare consequences of banning conflicted advice.

### What the first two paragraphs should say instead

Something like:

> Financial advisers often face compensation schemes that reward recommendations benefiting the adviser rather than the client. Economists have long worried that these conflicts distort advice, but evidence on whether banning such incentives actually reduces consumer harm is surprisingly limited. Most studies show effects on sales, fees, or adviser exit; far fewer can observe whether consumers ultimately fare better when disputes arise.
>
> This paper studies the UK’s 2020 ban on contingent charging for defined-benefit pension transfer advice, one of the cleanest modern examples of a regulator eliminating a direct conflict of interest. I show that after the ban, complaints about DB transfer advice were no less common, but they were substantially more likely to be upheld by the Financial Ombudsman Service. The central implication is that conflict-of-interest regulation can improve consumer welfare through the composition and merit of cases reaching redress, even when headline complaint volumes do not fall.

That is the story. Start with the broad economics question, then the clean policy setting, then the headline finding, then the implication.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper’s contribution is to argue that banning contingent-fee compensation in UK pension transfer advice improved consumer redress outcomes by increasing the share of complaints upheld, revealing a “quality” channel of conflict-of-interest regulation not captured by standard market-structure metrics.

### Is this clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?

Only partially. The paper distinguishes itself from work on adviser incentives, commission bans, and misconduct by saying prior work focuses on adviser behavior, product sales, or market structure rather than complaint adjudication. That is a real distinction, but it is still presented somewhat mechanically. Right now, the novelty reads as “same question, different dataset.” For AER, it needs to read as “different margin of welfare that changes the substantive conclusion.”

The paper should differentiate itself more forcefully from:
- broker/adviser misconduct papers documenting conflicts and bad recommendations,
- studies of commission bans or fiduciary rules emphasizing product choice, fees, or adviser supply,
- institutional papers on dispute resolution or complaints data.

The key line should be: **existing work shows conflicts distort advice; this paper asks whether removing those conflicts changes realized consumer harm as seen in redress outcomes.**

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or filling a gap in a LITERATURE?

Right now it is too often framed as filling a literature/policy-review gap. The stronger framing is world-facing:

- Weak: “The FCA review did not examine consumer outcomes, and complaint data are rare.”
- Strong: “Regulators often ban conflicted compensation without knowing whether consumers are actually better protected. This paper tests whether such a ban changes realized harm.”

The latter belongs in a top journal conversation; the former sounds like a useful policy note.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the intro?

At present, they might say: “It’s a DiD on a UK commission-ban setting using ombudsman data, and uphold rates go up.” That is not enough. The author needs the reader to say something sharper:

> “It shows that eliminating conflicted pay changed not how many complaints were filed, but how meritorious the complaints were. So conflict regulation can work through a quality-of-harm channel rather than a quantity-of-activity channel.”

That is much better.

### What would make the contribution bigger?

Most importantly, the paper needs a broader welfare framing and possibly broader outcome architecture.

Specific ways to make it bigger:
1. **Move from complaints to consumer harm more directly.** If possible, bring in awards, redress amounts, case severity, or resolution timing. “Upheld” is interesting, but still an intermediate legal/administrative outcome.
2. **Separate extensive and intensive margins of harm.** Did the ban reduce bad advice, alter which consumers seek redress, or change adjudication standards? The current story assumes “quality of complaints,” but that mechanism is not nailed down in the framing.
3. **Link to underlying market activity.** If transfer volumes fell while uphold rates rose, that is a different welfare story from constant transfer volumes with better advice.
4. **Make the result speak to a general theory of professional conflicts.** Right now it is a UK pensions paper with a clever outcome. It could instead be a paper about how conflict regulation reshapes the distribution of disputes in expert markets.

The biggest upgrade would be to frame the paper as a test of **how conflict-of-interest regulation changes the composition of realized consumer harm**, not as a study of FOS complaint categories.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Before the paper: economists and regulators believe contingent compensation can distort advice. Existing evidence mostly shows distorted recommendations, transfer rates, fees, or adviser exit. But whether banning those incentives actually improves realized consumer outcomes remains less directly observed.

### Tension

There are two competing possibilities:
- banning contingent charging reduces unsuitable advice and therefore consumer harm;
- or it creates an “advice gap,” reducing access without improving outcomes, or at least making harms less visible.

A deeper tension is that headline complaint volumes may be a poor welfare metric: fewer complaints could mean less harm, less access, less awareness, or less redress.

### Resolution

The paper finds no decline in complaint volume but an increase in the consumer-uphold rate for DB transfer complaints after the ban.

### Implications

The implication the paper wants is that conflict-of-interest regulation can improve consumer welfare by changing the composition and merit of disputes, not just the number of transactions or complaints. Regulators should not evaluate these policies only with market-activity metrics.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the ingredients, but the arc is not yet fully disciplined. At times it feels like a collection of plausible results arranged around an ex post label, “quality dividend.” The phrase is memorable, but the paper has not completely earned it conceptually. The core issue is that the paper jumps quickly from higher uphold rates to a fairly specific mechanism: “the ban filtered out low-merit cases.” That is one interpretation, but not obviously the only one.

So the story should be tightened:

- **Setup:** conflicts in advice are pervasive; evidence usually stops upstream.
- **Tension:** a ban could either improve welfare or reduce access, and complaint counts alone cannot tell us which.
- **Resolution:** counts do not move, but adjudicated success shifts toward consumers.
- **Implication:** the policy changed the composition of disputes in a way consistent with improved underlying advice quality or case merit; therefore redress outcomes provide a distinct lens on regulatory effectiveness.

That version is stronger and more careful.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?

“After the UK banned advisers from being paid only when clients transferred out of defined-benefit pensions, the number of complaints didn’t fall, but the share of cases won by consumers rose substantially.”

That is the dinner-party line.

### Would people lean in?

Some would, especially people in household finance, regulation, and applied micro. But many would need one more sentence to see why it matters. The missing sentence is:

> “So the standard way regulators judge these reforms—looking at volumes, activity, or market exit—may miss the actual welfare channel.”

That gets attention.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Immediately: **Why did uphold rates rise?**  
Was it because advice improved, because only stronger cases got filed, because the ombudsman changed standards, or because the stock of legacy bad cases persisted while new low-merit cases disappeared?

That follow-up question is not a weakness per se; it is the natural question. But the paper must embrace it as the central interpretive issue rather than overstate that it has fully answered it.

### If the findings are modest or partly null, is the null interesting?

Yes, the null on complaint volume is interesting if framed correctly. “The ban did not reduce complaints” is not itself exciting. But “the ban did not reduce complaints even while shifting complaint success toward consumers” is interesting because it shows that quantity and quality margins can move differently.

The paper should make a more explicit argument that **null effects on quantity can coexist with meaningful welfare gains on composition**. That is the genuinely publishable insight.

Be honest: in current form, this is not yet an AER paper. It feels like a neat, competent, policy-relevant applied paper with one interesting outcome variable and a clean institutional setting. That can go somewhere good. But AER requires either bigger ambition, sharper conceptual novelty, or a finding that reorients a literature.

### What is the main gap?

Mostly a **framing and ambition problem**, with some **scope risk**.

- **Framing problem:** the paper undersells the broad question and oversells the bureaucratic gap it fills.
- **Ambition problem:** it settles too quickly for “first causal evaluation using ombudsman data” rather than asking what economists should learn more generally about conflict regulation.
- **Scope problem:** one product category, one adjudication outcome, one country-specific reform. That can still work if the conceptual payoff is larger than the institutional footprint.

I do not think the main problem is novelty in the narrow sense; the setting and outcome are interesting. The problem is that the paper has not yet converted that novelty into a broader claim economists will care about.

A version that makes one of these stronger claims convincingly:

1. **Conflict-of-interest bans work through complaint composition, not just market activity.**
2. **Administrative redress data reveal welfare effects that transaction-level data miss.**

Any of those could travel. But the current paper only partially gets there.
