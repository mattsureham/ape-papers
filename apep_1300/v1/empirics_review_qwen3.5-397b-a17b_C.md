**2. Summary**

This paper investigates whether Amazon's fulfillment center expansion generated a "racial dividend" by disproportionately increasing Black employment in local logistics markets. Using county-level QWI data and a staggered difference-in-differences design, the author finds that FC entry increases Black warehousing employment by 0.49 log points, significantly outpacing the 0.28 log point increase for White workers. The results suggest that sectoral demand shocks can narrow racial employment gaps when the expanding sector relies on labor pools where minority groups are already over-represented, though average earnings compress slightly due to the entry-level nature of the new jobs.

**3. Essential Points**

1.  **Inference on the "Racial Dividend" (Covariance Structure):** The paper estimates the "racial dividend" as the difference between the Black and White ATT ($\hat{\beta}_B - \hat{\beta}_W$). The text states inference is based on the "square root of the sum of squared standard errors," which assumes $\text{Cov}(\hat{\beta}_B, \hat{\beta}_W) = 0$. This is econometrically incorrect. Since both estimates are derived from the same counties with the same treatment timing, the error terms are almost certainly correlated (e.g., county-specific shocks affect all warehousing employment). Ignoring the covariance term ($\text{Var}(A-B) = \text{Var}(A) + \text{Var}(B) - 2\text{Cov}(A,B)$) risks misstating the precision of the dividend. You must estimate the covariance, ideally by stacking the data or using a bootstrap that respects the county-level clustering across race groups.
2.  **Magnitude and Log-Point Interpretation:** The reported ATT for Black employment (0.49 log points) implies a roughly 63% increase in employment ($e^{0.49} - 1$). Given Table 1's pre-treatment mean of 1,803 Black workers in treated counties, this implies an increase of ~1,100 workers. While plausible for a large FC, this magnitude is at the upper bound of what a single facility typically employs (usually 1,000–1,500 total workers, not just Black workers). You need to explicitly reconcile the ATT magnitude with the physical capacity of the facilities. Is the effect driven by net new jobs, or is it capturing a compositional shift where workers are reclassified into NAICS 48-49 from retail (NAICS 44-45) due to Amazon's presence?
3.  **Control Group Comparability:** The summary statistics reveal a massive baseline disparity: treated counties have 10,534 average warehousing workers versus 1,570 in control counties. While county fixed effects control for time-invariant heterogeneity, the parallel trends assumption relies on the *growth rates* of these vastly different economic environments being comparable. A large metropolitan county (treated) may have different cyclical sensitivities than a rural county (control). You should provide stronger evidence that the *trends* in warehousing employment were parallel prior to the staggered adoption, perhaps by weighting the control group to match the pre-treatment employment size distribution of the treated counties.

**4. Suggestions**

*   **Refine the Standard Error Calculation:** As noted in the Essential Points, the independence assumption for the racial dividend test is the most critical technical fix. A practical way to handle this in the Callaway-Sant'Anna framework is to estimate a single specification with interaction terms (e.g., $Y_{crt} = \alpha_c + \gamma_t + \sum \beta_r \text{FC}_{ct} \times \mathbb{1}[\text{Race}=r] + \epsilon_{crt}$) or to use a bootstrap procedure that resamples counties (preserving the within-county correlation between Black and White employment) to generate the distribution of the difference statistic. This will ensure your p-values for the "dividend" are robust.

*   **Contextualize the "Log Points":** For an *AER: Insights* audience, translating log points into "number of jobs" is crucial for economic intuition. If the ATT is 0.49, and the average treated county has 1,800 Black warehousing workers, the effect is ~1,100 jobs. Does this align with the average size of an FC opening in your sample? If the average FC brings 1,000 total jobs, and the local Black labor share is 20%, a 1,100 Black job increase suggests significant indirect effects or displacement of non-Amazon logistics workers into the "warehousing" classification. Adding a back-of-the-envelope calculation comparing your ATT to the average facility employment size (from your MWPVL data) would bolster the plausibility of the mechanism.

*   **Address Spatial Spillovers and Commuting Zones:** Amazon FCs draw labor from commuting zones (CZs), not just counties. If an FC opens in County A, it might employ workers residing in County B. Your current design attributes all employment gains to County A (where the facility is), potentially biasing the effect if the QWI counts workers by workplace (which it does) but the labor supply shock is regional. Consider aggregating the data to Commuting Zone levels or including a measure of "nearby treatment" (e.g., FCs in adjacent counties) to rule out spatial contamination of the control group.

*   **Clarify the Earnings Mechanism:** The finding that earnings decline (-0.036 log points) is fascinating but requires careful framing. You argue this is a composition effect (entry-level jobs diluting the average). However, QWI earnings are average monthly earnings for *stable* jobs. If Amazon has higher turnover (as you note), the QWI "stable job" filter might be selecting for a different subset of workers than in incumbent firms. Consider checking if the *hire rate* earnings (if available in QWI OEWS or similar) tell a different story, or explicitly discuss how the QWI definition of "earnings" interacts with Amazon's high-turnover model.

*   **Reconcile Manifest vs. Paper Timeline:** In the Introduction or Data section, briefly explain the deviation from the 2010–2022 window proposed in early planning (Manifest) to the 1997–2016 window used here. Readers familiar with the "warehouse boom" narrative expect the post-2010 acceleration. A sentence clarifying that reliable facility-level data is constrained to this earlier period (or that you are focusing on the *initial* shock to avoid COVID-era noise) would improve transparency.

*   **Visualizing the Dividend:** The paper relies heavily on tables. For an *Insights* format, a single, clear event-study plot showing the divergence between Black and White employment paths (the "dividend" over time) would be powerful. Plot the cumulative ATT for Black and White workers on the same graph
