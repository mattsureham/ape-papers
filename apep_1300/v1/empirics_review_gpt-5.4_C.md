## 1. **Idea Fidelity**

The paper broadly pursues the original idea, but in an importantly narrowed and somewhat inconsistent way. The manifest proposed a 2010–2022 county-level staggered DiD using the Azure QWI race panel and roughly 350 treated counties, with attention to Black employment, earnings, hires/separations, and a triple-difference exploiting baseline Black workforce share. The paper instead studies 63 treated counties, ends treatment exposure effectively by 2016/2017, uses a different county count than the manifest, and drops the proposed triple-difference entirely. Those are not minor implementation details: they materially change both the identifying variation and the external relevance of the result.

More concerning, the paper never really explains why the realized treatment sample is so much smaller than the one described in the manifest. If the underlying MWPVL source supports many more facilities/counties through 2022, limiting the analysis to 63 counties through 2016 risks a selected-sample design. The paper therefore captures the spirit of the original question, but it misses key elements of the intended identification strategy and does not justify those departures.

## 2. **Summary**

This paper asks whether Amazon fulfillment center entry increased warehousing employment disproportionately for Black workers. Using county-level QWI data by race and a staggered DiD design, it reports large increases in warehousing employment after FC entry, with Black employment rising more than White employment and modest declines in average monthly earnings.

The question is interesting and potentially important. But in its current form, the paper overstates what the design can establish, and several features of the magnitudes, inference, and interpretation need substantially tighter scrutiny before the main “racial dividend” claim is credible.

## 3. **Essential Points**

**1. Identification is not yet persuasive because treatment timing and site selection are plausibly endogenous at the county level.**  
The paper repeatedly asserts that Amazon timing is driven by logistics optimization or state tax nexus laws “rather than local labor market conditions,” but that is too quick. Even if tax policy matters at the state level, the empirical design is at the county-year level, and Amazon chooses counties with favorable highway access, land availability, large labor pools, and often growing logistics demand. Table 1 itself shows treated counties are dramatically different from controls in baseline scale. County fixed effects absorb levels, not differential trends in large logistics hubs. Event-study pre-trends close to zero help, but with only 63 treated counties and annualized data, they do not settle the concern. You need a more convincing design-based case: e.g., conditioning on pre-treatment county size/growth, comparing only counties in the same commuting zone or metro ring, or using matched controls. As written, I am not convinced the paper isolates Amazon entry from pre-existing county-level logistics growth.

**2. The treatment effect magnitudes are large enough that they require validation against facility capacity and sector totals.**  
A 0.35 log-point increase in county warehousing employment is huge; 0.49 for Black employment is larger still. In treated counties, pre-treatment mean warehousing employment is about 10,500, so the implied increase is on the order of 3,500–4,000 workers if interpreted literally. That is large relative to the staffing of a typical FC, especially for a “first opening” treatment. If the treatment captures multiple subsequent openings, general equilibrium spillovers, or broader logistics clustering, say so clearly—but then the estimand is no longer simply the effect of one FC entry. The same issue appears for Black employment: with baseline Black employment around 1,800, a 0.49 log-point effect implies roughly 1,100 additional Black warehousing jobs, which is economically possible in some large counties but not obviously typical. These are not impossible numbers, but they are big enough that the paper must benchmark them carefully against facility-level employment capacity, county QCEW changes in warehousing, and the timing of subsequent Amazon expansions.

**3. The inference for the “racial dividend” is not correctly handled, and some outcome definitions raise comparability concerns.**  
The paper computes the standard error for Black minus White effects as the square root of the sum of squared standard errors. That is generally wrong because the two estimates are obtained from the same counties and years and are almost certainly positively correlated. Depending on covariance, your SE could be too large or too small, but in any case it is not valid. You need joint estimation or a county-level bootstrap that preserves covariance across race-specific outcomes. More broadly, the race-specific employment series are not independent samples, and inference should reflect that. I also worry about logging race-specific county employment cells with many small or zero values, especially for Asian employment and possibly Black employment in smaller counties. The paper needs to state the transformation used for zero cells and show robustness to levels, inverse hyperbolic sine, and employment shares.

## 4. **Suggestions**

The paper is promising, but it needs to become much more disciplined empirically. My main advice is to scale back the rhetoric and strengthen the measurement.

First, clarify the treatment dataset. The paper alternates between 60, 63, and 93 facilities/counties, while the manifest suggests a much larger universe through 2022. That makes it hard to know what sample is actually analyzed. Provide a transparent treatment-construction appendix: raw Amazon sites, exclusions by facility type, geocoding method, first opening year by county, and a map/histogram of cohorts. If the analysis is intentionally restricted to “classic” FCs before 2017, explain why that is the relevant policy variation rather than the full warehouse boom. Right now the title and framing imply Amazon’s modern nationwide expansion, but the estimation sample is much narrower.

Second, benchmark the magnitudes directly. This is essential. For each treated county, report pre-treatment warehousing employment, estimated post-treatment increase, number of Amazon facilities eventually opened, and total listed facility capacity if available. A simple table comparing implied employment gains to plausible FC staffing would go a long way. If average implied gains exceed what one FC could account for, that is not fatal—but then you need to reinterpret the result as an equilibrium county-level logistics expansion induced by Amazon entry. Similarly, compare your estimates with QCEW warehousing employment totals, if only for a subset of counties. At present, the estimates read as surprisingly large without enough economic grounding.

Third, I would strongly recommend re-estimating the main specifications in levels and shares, not only logs. AER: Insights readers will want to know: how many additional Black warehousing jobs does an FC generate? How much does the Black share of county warehousing employment rise? The current “75% faster than White employment” formulation is rhetorically strong but economically ambiguous, because it compares percentage changes on different baselines. A rise in Black employment levels and a rise in Black employment share are different claims. The latter is closer to the headline “racial dividend,” and you should show it explicitly.

Relatedly, the most natural heterogeneity outcome here is the Black share of warehousing employment or a stacked race-by-county panel with race interacted with treatment. Estimating separate models by race and subtracting coefficients is cumbersome and, as noted, complicates inference. A cleaner design is to stack race groups and estimate a saturated specification with county-race and year-race fixed effects, allowing direct inference on whether treatment shifts Black employment relative to White employment within county-year. That would also align better with the paper’s core comparative claim.

Fourth, devote more effort to comparing treated counties to plausible controls. The current control group is essentially “all other counties,” which is hard to defend when Amazon almost never considers many rural counties with tiny logistics sectors. I would like to see at least three alternatives: (i) only counties above some pre-treatment population or warehousing-employment threshold; (ii) matched controls based on pre-treatment warehousing levels and trends, metro proximity, interstate access, and demographics; and (iii) controls restricted to counties in states or commuting zones that eventually receive Amazon facilities. If the racial dividend survives these more demanding comparisons, the paper will be much more credible.

Fifth, the event-study evidence needs to be shown, not merely described. Given how central pre-trends are to the design, a figure with simultaneous confidence bands is indispensable. I would also encourage reporting cohort-specific dynamics or at least separating early from late cohorts graphically. With only 63 treated counties, average dynamic effects may hide substantial heterogeneity. Since you already note larger post-2010 effects, show whether the Black-White differential is similarly concentrated in later, larger facilities.

Sixth, the earnings interpretation needs tightening. The paper says earnings fall because Amazon’s entry-level wage floor compresses the sector distribution, but this is speculative given the data. QWI average monthly earnings for stable jobs can move for several reasons: composition toward new/younger workers, incumbent displacement, changes in hours, or shifts across subindustries within NAICS 48–49. The current explanation is plausible, but not demonstrated. A more cautious interpretation would say that average earnings for stable jobs decline modestly, consistent with composition effects, and then show supporting evidence if possible: changes in hires, separations, age mix, or subindustry composition. I would avoid the stronger “wage floor pulls down the average” language unless you can document it.

Seventh, exploit the rich QWI structure more fully. The manifest envisioned hires and separations, and the paper only lightly touches hires. Show separations too. If Amazon operates through high turnover, then employment, hires, and separations should move together in an informative way. In particular, if Black employment rises more because Black hires rise more, that is very different from Black workers simply having lower separation rates post-entry. Those are distinct mechanisms with distinct welfare implications.

Eighth, the placebo section should be sharpened. A 0.07 log-point effect in NAICS 54 is not tiny, especially relative to many county-level labor-market papers. Calling it “statistically weak and economically modest” may be true, but it also suggests your treatment captures broader county growth. I would add placebo industries that are less likely to respond to local multiplier effects and more likely to reveal spurious differential trends—say finance or utilities—or, better, test within logistics-adjacent versus unrelated sectors. You might also examine pre-treatment leads in these placebo sectors. If those also move, that would signal a broader confounding trend.

Ninth, address measurement concerns in the QWI race data. County-race-industry cells can be noisy, suppressed, or small, and annual averaging does not remove all of that. Please report how many county-year-race cells are zero or missing, how logs are constructed, and whether results change under asinh transformations. For Black and especially Asian employment, this matters a lot. The Asian estimate is implausibly large relative to the paper’s narrative and may simply reflect small-cell volatility.

Tenth, I would temper the language of causality and welfare. “Racial dividend” is a vivid phrase, but in the current draft it overstates what is established. The paper shows differential employment growth in a sector where Black workers were already relatively concentrated. That is interesting. But it does not yet establish an unambiguous welfare gain for Black workers overall, because county-level net employment in warehousing is not the same as worker-level gains, and the earnings results are slightly negative. Framing the contribution as documenting differential sectoral employment incidence would be more defensible unless you can bring stronger evidence on worker welfare.

Finally, the paper would benefit from one additional specification much closer to the manifest’s original insight: interact treatment with the county’s pre-treatment Black share in logistics or in the local less-college labor force. That would test the “compositional amplifier” more directly than simply comparing Black and White outcome regressions. If counties with higher ex ante Black logistics presence experience larger post-entry Black employment gains, that would provide cleaner evidence for your proposed mechanism and move the paper beyond a reduced-form heterogeneity exercise.

In short, there is a potentially publishable paper here, but not yet in AER: Insights form. The question is good, the outcome data are valuable, and the headline pattern may well be real. But the design needs tighter control-group logic, the effect sizes need economic validation, and the inference on racial differences must be redone properly. If the authors address those issues, the paper will be substantially stronger and much more convincing.
