## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple, policy-relevant question: did mine-safety regulation after the 2006 Sago disaster kill jobs in coal country, or was coal’s employment collapse mainly driven by market forces? Using variation in counties’ preexisting dependence on mining, the paper argues that the 2006 MINER Act did not reduce local employment, while the later post-2010 decline looks much more like the consequence of cheap natural gas than of safety enforcement.

A busy economist should care because this is a test of a very common political claim: when an industry declines after a regulatory intervention, was regulation actually the cause? Coal is an especially salient setting because “the war on coal” became a major public narrative, so disentangling regulation from market competition has broader relevance for how economists think about declining places and the employment costs of regulation.

**Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Mostly, but not sharply enough. The current opening is vivid and readable, but it takes a bit too long to get to the real stakes. The paper should get to the core dispute faster: *Was coal-country decline caused by safety regulation or by shifts in energy markets?* Right now the first paragraph is more journalistic than strategic.

**What the first two paragraphs should say instead:**  
> Coal’s decline is often blamed on regulation. After two deadly mine disasters, Congress and federal regulators imposed the most important mine-safety changes in decades, and these interventions became part of a broader political narrative that safety and environmental rules destroyed jobs in coal communities. But coal employment was also hit by a massive contemporaneous market shock: the shale gas boom sharply reduced coal’s competitiveness in electricity generation.  
>   
> This paper asks which force mattered more. Using county-level variation in preexisting coal dependence, we study two distinct post-disaster policy episodes: the 2006 MINER Act and the post-2010 enforcement crackdown. We find no evidence that the 2006 safety mandates reduced employment in mining-intensive counties, while the post-2010 decline appears in western as well as Appalachian coal regions, pointing to market competition rather than regulation as the main driver of coal-country job loss.

That is the pitch. It is cleaner, more general-interest, and makes the world-question explicit.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Coal communities lost jobs, and regulation became a central explanation in public and political discourse. Two mine disasters led to major safety-policy responses during a period when coal was also becoming less competitive due to cheap natural gas.

### Tension
When a regulated industry declines, it is hard to know whether regulation caused the decline or merely coincided with it. Coal is the canonical case where this distinction matters politically, economically, and rhetorically.

### Resolution
The paper finds no detectable employment effect from the 2006 MINER Act, while later declines after 2010 are geographically widespread in ways more consistent with fuel-market competition than with Appalachian-centered enforcement.

### Implications
The coal jobs narrative may be backward: safety regulation was not the main destroyer of coal employment. More broadly, policymakers should distinguish between decline caused by regulation and decline caused by market substitution; the policy response differs radically.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
**Yes, but it is not yet fully disciplined.** There is a real story here, but the paper sometimes becomes a collection of findings:
- no effect in 2006,
- negative effect after 2010,
- regional split,
- some earnings movement,
- some hiring/non-mining results,
- salience/media discussion.

The core story should be:

1. Coal decline was widely blamed on regulation.  
2. The 2006 safety law offers a clean test of that claim.  
3. It did not reduce employment.  
4. The later collapse aligns better with market competition than enforcement.  
5. Therefore, the standard narrative about what killed coal jobs is wrong.

Everything else should support that spine. The “media salience” angle is colorful but secondary. It should not compete with the main story.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?
“Coal-country job collapse is often blamed on mine-safety regulation, but in this paper the big 2006 safety law has basically no employment effect; the later collapse looks much more like shale gas competition than regulation.”

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
**Lean in, initially.** The topic is politically loaded and economically familiar. But they will only stay engaged if the paper is presented as overturning a widely held causal story, not as a narrow county-level exercise on one statute.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Probably one of these:
- “How convincingly can you separate regulation from the gas shock?”
- “Is this about coal specifically, or about safety regulation more generally?”
- “Why should the 2006 law have had low employment costs despite real compliance costs?”
- “What happened to workers and communities if total employment didn’t fall right away?”

That last question is important. The paper would become more interesting if it better anticipated the obvious audience reaction: *if not jobs, then what margins adjusted? wages? hiring? mine entry/exit? migration? local spillovers?*

### If findings are null or modest, is the null itself interesting?
Yes, **but only if aggressively framed as a rejected conventional wisdom**.

A null on the MINER Act is interesting because the baseline public claim is not “maybe regulation had zero effect”; it is “regulation killed coal jobs.” The paper must make the reader feel that the null is informative because it rules out a popular and policy-relevant explanation.

At present, the paper does this somewhat, but it could do it more forcefully. The null should feel like a substantive result, not a failed attempt to detect an effect.
