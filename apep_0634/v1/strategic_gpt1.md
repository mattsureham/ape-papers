## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a politically salient question: did federal mine-safety regulation kill coal jobs, or was coal country’s collapse driven mainly by market forces? Using the 2006 MINER Act and the post-2010 Upper Big Branch enforcement episode, the paper argues that the first major safety law did not reduce employment in mining-intensive counties, while the later employment collapse was broad enough geographically to look more like competition from cheap natural gas than regulation.

A busy economist should care because this is not really just about coal. It is about a general question: when industries decline after a salient regulatory intervention, how much of the decline is actually regulation versus preexisting technological and market change? That is an important question for regulation, political economy, and regional adjustment.

### Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Partly, but not cleanly enough. The introduction is vivid and readable, but it opens in a journalistic mode and takes too long to get to the economic question. The first paragraph is memorable, but it risks making the paper seem narrower and more rhetorical than it is. The actual pitch only emerges by paragraph 2-4.

### What the first two paragraphs should say instead

The paper should open much more directly with the economic debate it is trying to settle:

> Coal country’s decline is often attributed to regulation. But when employment falls after a highly salient safety law, it is hard to know whether regulation caused the losses or whether regulation merely coincided with deeper market forces already eroding the industry. Distinguishing between these explanations matters for both the economics of regulation and the design of policy responses to regional decline.
>
> This paper studies two shocks to U.S. coal mining: the 2006 MINER Act, passed after the Sago disaster, and the post-2010 enforcement escalation after Upper Big Branch. Using cross-county differences in preexisting mining dependence, we show that the 2006 safety law did not reduce employment or earnings in mining-intensive counties. The large declines after 2010 were at least as strong in Western coal regions far from the enforcement epicenter, pointing to competition from natural gas rather than mine-safety regulation as the main driver of coal-country job loss.

That is the paper’s real hook. Start there.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### Contribution in one sentence

The paper’s contribution is to argue that a prominent case of disaster-driven safety regulation did not itself reduce coal-community employment, and that the subsequent collapse of coal jobs is better explained by energy-market competition than by mine-safety enforcement.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest papers?

Only somewhat. The paper makes a distinction from mine-level work on the MINER Act and from broad regulation-employment papers, but the differentiation is not yet sharp enough. Right now the reader can still summarize it as: “another exposure-based DiD on regulation and local labor markets.” The introduction names some literatures, but it does not yet crisply explain why this setting changes what we know rather than simply replicating known patterns in a new industry.

The three closest comparators seem to be:

1. **Walker (2013)** on the labor-market costs of environmental regulation.
2. **Greenstone / Greenstone, List, and Syverson-era Clean Air Act work** on regulation and local employment in regulated sectors/counties.
3. **Li (2022)** or analogous mine-level work on the MINER Act.
4. On the market-forces side, **Hausman and Kellogg / Hausman and coauthors** on the shale gas revolution and energy-market effects.

The current paper differentiates itself most plausibly by combining:
- a salient safety-regulation episode,
- community-level rather than plant-level incidence,
- and a direct attempt to separate regulation from market competition.

That triad is potentially interesting. But the introduction does not yet make it feel like a clean new fact rather than a narrow application.

### Is the contribution framed as a question about the world or a gap in the literature?

It is mostly framed as a question about the world, which is good: “Did safety regulation kill coal jobs?” That is much stronger than “we provide first county-level estimates.” Unfortunately, the introduction then slips back into gap-filling language: “first county-level estimates,” “the only prior economic analysis…” That is useful supporting material, but it should not be the headline.

The world-question should dominate:
- **What caused coal country’s decline?**
- **How costly are salient safety regulations in equilibrium?**
- **What should policymakers infer when a declining industry blames regulation?**

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?

Not quite confidently. They could say:
- “It says the MINER Act didn’t hurt employment and later coal losses were probably gas, not regulation.”

That is decent. But they might also say:
- “It’s a county-level DiD on coal regulation with a regional split after 2010.”

That second version is too method-centered and too incremental. The introduction needs to do more work to force the first interpretation.

### What would make the contribution bigger?

Most importantly: **connect the employment-cost result to the benefits or stakes of the regulation.** Right now the paper only says “the cost was not what critics claimed.” But an AER-level regulation paper usually changes beliefs more if it also informs the cost-benefit margin or at least the incidence margin in a deeper way.

Specific ways to make it bigger:

1. **Add safety outcomes or accident outcomes if at all possible.**  
   If the paper could show “no job loss, and meaningful safety gains,” it becomes much more than a debunking paper. Then it speaks directly to the welfare economics of regulation, not just attribution.

2. **Lean harder into the decomposition of coal decline.**  
   If the true big question is “what killed coal jobs?”, then the paper should look less like a narrow MINER Act study and more like a paper on competing explanations for regional industrial decline.

3. **Develop mechanism evidence on why employment didn’t fall after the MINER Act.**  
   Was compliance mostly fixed-cost capital spending? Was it absorbed in rents? Passed through? Offset by productivity or reduced disruptions? Right now the explanation is suggestive, not persuasive.

4. **Broaden implications beyond coal.**  
   The paper hints at “disaster-driven regulation may be less costly than critics assume.” That could be a real statement about salient regulation generally—but right now it is too speculative to carry much weight.

At the moment, oddly, both.

The paper could speak more to:

2. **Political economy of regulation scapegoating**  
   The “regulation killed us” narrative is not just an empirical claim; it is a political claim. If the paper wants to use the word “scapegoat” in the title, it should engage more with political economy.

3. **Compensating differentials / worker safety / labor regulation**  
   If safety investments do not reduce jobs, why not? This could connect to labor economics and workplace-safety literatures.

4. **Regional adjustment and declining places**  
   Kline/Moretti are cited, but the paper could more directly connect to work on what local labor markets look like when tradable sectors contract.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?

Not fully. The paper thinks it is in the “employment costs of regulation” conversation. That is one conversation, but it may not be the best one.

The more interesting conversation may be:
- **how to distinguish regulatory blame from market-driven decline in politically salient industries**.

That is a stronger and timelier conversation. It connects regulation, energy economics, and regional decline in a way that is more distinctive than “another regulation-and-jobs paper.”

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

A declining industry and its political defenders often blame regulation for job loss. In coal, two highly salient mine disasters produced major federal responses, and these responses became convenient explanations for coal country’s decline.

### Tension

The timing is confounded: safety regulation intensified around the same time broader energy-market conditions were turning sharply against coal. So observed job loss after regulation does not tell us whether regulation caused the losses.

### Resolution

The paper finds no detectable employment decline after the 2006 MINER Act in more mining-dependent counties. The large declines occur after 2010 and are just as strong in Western states far from the enforcement focal point, which points toward natural gas competition rather than mine-safety enforcement.

### Implications

If coal decline was mainly market-driven, deregulation would not revive coal communities. More generally, salient safety regulations may impose smaller employment costs than their political opponents claim.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has a **serviceable** arc, but it currently feels like **two papers awkwardly joined**:

1. Paper A: “Did the MINER Act reduce employment?”  
2. Paper B: “What explains post-2010 coal decline: enforcement or natural gas?”

Paper B is actually the more interesting one. Paper A produces a null. The current draft tries to make the null carry the paper and then uses Paper B as corroboration, but readers may experience the reverse: the post-2010 story is the real action, and the MINER Act result is a useful but narrower piece of evidence.

### What story should it be telling?

The story should be:

> Coal country’s decline is widely attributed to regulation, but that attribution is wrong. A major national safety law imposed little if any labor-market cost, and the large later collapse looks geographically like market competition, not regulation. The paper uses two disaster-linked policy episodes to separate a politically convenient explanation from the economically relevant one.

That is a cleaner story than “we study two shocks.”

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?

“Congress passed the biggest mine-safety law in 30 years after Sago, and we find no evidence it killed coal-country jobs; the big employment collapse comes later and looks more like shale gas than regulation.”

That is the dinner-party line.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

They would **lean in initially**, because the question is politically loaded and easily understood. But then the follow-up becomes crucial. If the paper cannot move beyond “we don’t find job loss,” attention may fade. The topic is salient; the current payload is modest.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

The first follow-up question is obvious:

**“So what did the regulation actually buy in safety terms?”**

The second is:

**“How sure are we that the post-2010 pattern is gas rather than some broader coal-specific regulatory environment?”**

As editor, I would note that the first of these is the strategic weakness. The paper wants credit for showing that regulation was not the villain, but it does not yet show what the hero accomplished.

### If the findings are null or modest, is the null itself interesting?

Yes, potentially. A null can be interesting here because the public claim it overturns is strong and consequential: “safety regulation destroyed coal jobs.” Learning that a major, costly, salient safety mandate had little equilibrium employment effect is meaningful.

But the paper must lean harder into why this null matters:
- it overturns a central political narrative,
- it changes the policy response one should favor,
- and it suggests caution in inferring job effects from coincident industry decline.

Right now the null is interesting, but the paper has not fully monetized it.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

At present, the gap is mainly a mix of **framing problem**, **scope problem**, and a bit of **ambition problem**.

### Framing problem

The paper undersells its best question. “Did the MINER Act reduce employment?” is a respectable field-journal question. “Did regulation actually cause coal country’s decline, or was regulation a scapegoat for market-driven collapse?” is much bigger and better.

### Scope problem

The paper wants to make a broad statement about regulation and welfare, but only measures one side of the ledger. Without safety outcomes, productivity margins, or stronger mechanism evidence, the paper remains a well-aimed debunking exercise rather than a transformative regulation paper.

### Novelty problem

Moderate, not fatal. The basic empirical architecture is familiar. The novelty has to come from the question and interpretation. That means the writing and positioning need to do real work.

### Ambition problem

The paper is competent but somewhat safe. It shows no employment effect from one regulation and attributes later decline to gas. That is good, but the ambition needs to rise either by:
- tying the result to broader regulatory incidence,
- or by more decisively answering “what did the regulation do?” not just “what it didn’t do.”

### Single most impactful advice

**Reframe the paper around the larger question—what actually caused coal-country job loss—and make the MINER Act result one piece of evidence in a broader argument that regulation was politically blamed for a market-driven decline.**

If the authors can do one more substantive thing, it would be:
**add evidence on safety benefits or another mechanism showing why employment costs were limited.**  
That would materially raise the paper’s ceiling.
