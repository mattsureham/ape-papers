**SUBJECT:** Strategic Positioning of "The Referendum Floor: Employment Effects of the World’s Highest Minimum Wages"

---

### 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
This paper exploits a unique policy experiment in Switzerland where five cantons enacted the highest statutory minimum wages in the world (up to $26/hr) via direct democracy. Using administrative census data and modern staggered DiD estimators, the author finds a "bounded null": even at these extreme levels, there is no evidence of meaningful job destruction in high-bite sectors. This suggests that the "frontier" of minimum wage policy is much higher than previously thought, or that direct democracy selects for "binding-but-not-destructive" wage floors.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this very well. The opening hook about the Geneva-Vaud border is classic *AER* style—tangible, stark, and immediately illustrative of the identification strategy.

---

### 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
The paper’s contribution is providing the first comprehensive evidence of employment effects at the global "frontier" of minimum wage levels (CHF 19–24) using high-quality administrative data and heterogeneity-robust estimators.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It differentiates itself well from the US/UK-centric literature (modest wages) and the Hungarian case (large increase, but in a middle-income transition economy).
*   **Framing:** It is framed as a question about the WORLD (can very high wages exist without job loss?), which is strong.
*   **Clarity:** A smart economist would definitely understand the "newness": it's the "extreme value theorem" of minimum wage papers.
*   **Scaling Up:** The contribution would be bigger if it explored *why* the null exists. Is it price pass-through? Is it monopsony? Without those mechanisms, it risks being "just another DiD paper," albeit with a very cool setting.

---

### 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
The paper sits at the intersection of Labor (minimum wage) and Political Economy (direct democracy).

*   **Closest Neighbors:** Card & Krueger (1994) for the "classic" debate; Cengiz et al. (2019) for modern US evidence; Harasztosi & Lindner (2019) for large increases; and Callaway & Sant’Anna (2021) for the methodology.
*   **Positioning:** It builds on the "New Minimum Wage" literature but should perhaps "attack" the external validity of the US-centric consensus. If $26/hr doesn't hurt jobs in Geneva, why are we debating $15/hr in New York?
*   **Conversation:** It should lean harder into the **Political Economy** of the referendum. Does "The Referendum Floor" imply that voters are better at finding the optimal wage than technocrats? That is a very *AER* type of question.

---

### 4. NARRATIVE ARC
*   **Setup:** The global consensus is that modest minimum wages don't kill jobs.
*   **Tension:** Switzerland implements wages that are *not* modest—they are world-record highs. Standard theory predicts a "cliff."
*   **Resolution:** There is no cliff. The employment effects remain null.
*   **Implications:** Either the "bite" is still not high enough, or institutional factors (Swiss labor markets) or political factors (referendums) fundamentally change the trade-offs.

**Evaluation:** The arc is clear, but the "Resolution" section needs more work on the "Why." Currently, the discussion of mechanisms (monopsony vs. pass-through) is speculative.

---

### 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
*   **Dinner Party Fact:** "Switzerland has a $26 minimum wage and it didn't kill a single job."
*   **Reaction:** People will lean in. The follow-up will be: "Is it because Switzerland is rich/special, or because the US is wrong?"
*   **The Null:** This is a "Goldilocks Null." It isn't a failed experiment; it’s a challenge to the standard downward-sloping demand curve for labor at the intensive margin.

---

### 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
*   **The "Methodological Lesson":** The comparison between TWFE (-2.9%) and Callaway-Sant'Anna (-0.3%) is a great "teachable moment" for the profession. It should be elevated from a robustness check to a primary result.
*   **Data:** The STATENT data is excellent. I would move some of the sector-specific disaggregation (Table 3, Panel C) into the main results to show the consistency across industries.
*   **Length:** The institutional background on specific cantons is a bit long. This could be condensed to make room for more "Why" analysis.

---

### 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The gap between "Good Applied Paper" and "AER Paper" here is **Ambition**.

Right now, it’s a very clean, very competent evaluation of a policy change. To be an *AER* paper, it needs to move from "evaluating a policy" to "testing a theory of the labor market."

**Single Biggest Advice:** Shift the focus from "Does it work in Switzerland?" to "Why does it work at the frontier?" The author needs to dig into the **Referendum** aspect. Is there a difference in outcomes between wages set by the people versus those set by decree? If the author could find a way to compare the "Referendum Floor" to a "Legislative Floor," that would be a Top 5 paper instantly.
