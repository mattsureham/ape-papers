### 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
This paper examines the "reversibility" of environmental conservation by looking at what happens when the US government stops paying farmers to keep land idle. Using the 2014 Farm Bill’s massive reduction in the Conservation Reserve Program (CRP) cap as a natural experiment, the author finds that rather than remaining as grassland, expired acres are disproportionately converted into corn—the most profitable and nitrogen-intensive row crop. This suggests that conservation benefits in agriculture are "rented, not purchased," and evaporate as soon as subsidies cease.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this well, but it buries the "rented vs. purchased" hook in the conclusion. The first two paragraphs are currently a bit dry on the policy mechanics.
*   **The pitch the paper should have:** "Environmental conservation programs often assume that temporary payments can induce long-term land-use changes. This paper shows that for 7 million acres of US farmland, conservation was merely a temporary lease: once the 2014 Farm Bill ended their subsidies, farmers immediately converted the land to high-value corn production. This 'corn conversion' reveals that the environmental gains of the largest US conservation program are entirely contingent on perpetual payments."

### 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**Contribution:** This paper provides the first large-scale causal evidence of how land-use transitions respond to the expiration of conservation contracts, moving beyond existing simulations to show a selective shift toward high-intensity corn production.

*   **Differentiation:** It moves past the "enrollment" literature (Sullivan et al., 2004) and the "satellite-only" descriptions (Lark et al., 2015) by linking a specific policy shock (the 2014 cap reduction) to economic choice (crop selection).
*   **Question vs. Literature:** It frames itself as answering a question about the **WORLD** (Are conservation gains permanent?), which is its greatest strength.
*   **Clarity:** A smart economist would understand this as a "leakage/reversibility" paper. It is more than "another DiD" because the policy shock is massive (7 million acres).
*   **What would make it bigger?** The contribution would be massive if the author could link this "corn conversion" directly to a secondary environmental outcome—specifically, water quality (nitrate runoff) or carbon sequestration loss—rather than just implying the harm based on crop type.

### 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
*   **Closest Neighbors:** Ferraro (2011) on PES; Roberts & Schlenker (2013) on crop supply response; Jayachandran et al. (2017) on "Cash for Carbon" reversibility.
*   **Positioning:** It should position itself as the **domestic, industrial-scale counterpart** to the developing-world PES literature (like Jayachandran). It shows that even in a high-institutional-capacity country like the US, the "permanence" of conservation is an illusion.
*   **Unexpected Connection:** The paper should speak more to the **Industrial Organization of Agriculture**. The fact that it’s *corn* and not just "crops" suggests that federal crop insurance or ethanol mandates (the "Renewable Fuel Standard") might be the silent partners making conversion so attractive.

### 4. NARRATIVE ARC
*   **Setup:** The CRP is a cornerstone of US green policy, idling land for decades.
*   **Tension:** In 2014, the "quiet dismantling" (author's words) began. Does the land stay green because farmers have learned to love conservation, or does it revert?
*   **Resolution:** It reverts to the most "aggressive" use possible: corn.
*   **Implications:** Conservation is a subscription service, not a one-time investment.

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong. However, the "Tension" could be heightened by emphasizing the massive public investment ($80/acre for 15 years) that was effectively "undone" in a few seasons.

### 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
*   **Lead Fact:** "We spent billions renting 7 million acres of nature, and the moment the check stopped, it all turned into cornflakes."
*   **Reaction:** Lean in. It’s a cynical but powerful result that speaks to the "additionality" and "permanence" debates in carbon markets.
*   **Follow-up:** "Was it the same land that was low-quality before? Or did the CRP actually make the soil *better* for corn, meaning the government subsidized future corn yields?" (This is a crucial question the author should address).

### 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
*   **Front-loading:** The result is clear, but Table 2 (the main result) is a bit thin. I want to see the "Corn vs. Wheat" trade-off visualized in a coefficient plot, not just a table.
*   **Appendix:** The "leave-one-state-out" is great; keep it in the appendix.
*   **Discussion:** Section 6 is the best part of the paper. Expand the discussion on "perverse pro-cyclicality."

### 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The paper is currently a very high-quality *Journal of Environmental Economics and Management (JEEM)* or *American Journal of Agricultural Economics (AJAE)* paper. To make it **AER**, it needs to move from "here is what happened to the corn" to "here is what this tells us about the **optimal design of conservation contracts**."

**Single biggest improvement:** Address the "Endogenous Productivity" story. Did the 10-20 years of idling the land under CRP actually increase the soil's productivity (fallow effect), thereby making the eventual conversion to corn *more* profitable? If the government is paying to "rest" the land only to have that rest subsidize a future carbon-intensive corn boom, the policy irony is much deeper and more AER-worthy.
