**Referee Report**

**Paper:** The Walking Dead of the Nonprofit Sector: Mass Revocation and the Illusion of Creative Destruction

**1. Idea Fidelity**
The paper closely adheres to the original research proposal. It executes the proposed continuous-treatment DiD design using county-level revocation intensity, leverages all the specified data sources (IRS revocation lists, EO BMF, QWI, SOI), and directly tests the central hypothesis of creative destruction versus collateral damage. The novelty—applying the zombie firm concept to nonprofits and providing a causal estimate of mass revocation effects—is clearly the paper's focus. One minor deviation is the extension of the post-period to 2020 (beyond the initially suggested 2024 endpoint in the manifest), which is a logical and beneficial data-maximizing choice. The identification strategy, empirical specifications, and core outcomes (formations, employment) are faithfully implemented.

**2. Summary**
This paper provides the first causal evidence on the local ecosystem effects of the IRS’s mass automatic revocation of tax-exempt nonprofits. Exploiting cross-county variation in revocation intensity, it finds a striking temporal pattern: an initial, short-lived burst of new nonprofit entry is followed by a sustained decline in both new formations and sector employment. The authors conclude that the collateral damage from severing community and donor networks outweighs any creative destruction benefits, challenging the policy rationale for automatic, non-discretionary revocation.

**3. Essential Points**
The following three issues are critical and must be convincingly addressed for the paper to be suitable for publication.

*   **Treatment Timing and Heterogeneity:** The treatment variable (`RevocationIntensity`) is defined as the share of pre-2010 nonprofits revoked between 2010 and 2012. However, the "first massive wave" and primary policy shock occurred in **June 2011**. This conflates pre-wave, wave-year, and post-wave revocations into a single, time-invariant measure. This is problematic for three reasons. First, it muddies the interpretation of the event study's "t=0" (2011) effect, as the treatment measure itself includes outcomes from that year and the next. Second, it assumes the effects of revocations in 2010 (perhaps early-enforcement or truly defunct orgs) and 2012 (post-wave, potentially different types of orgs) are identical. The authors must justify this aggregation or, preferably, reconstruct the analysis using a time-varying measure of cumulative revocation intensity (e.g., the share revoked by the end of the prior year). This would more cleanly align treatment intensity with the timing of the shock.
*   **Threats to Parallel Trends and Anticipation:** The event study shows a statistically significant negative coefficient at t = -2. While the authors note it is "modest," a pre-trend, even a short-lived one, directly challenges the parallel trends assumption central to the DiD design. The suggested explanation—"anticipation" of the 2011 wave—is plausible but needs rigorous investigation. If organizations were applying for reinstatement or delaying new filings in 2009-2010 in anticipation, this behavior is itself a causal effect of the *policy threat*, not a violation of parallel trends. The authors must: (1) test for and report results using the `t = -3` or `t = -4` year as the reference period to see if the pre-trend persists; (2) directly examine pre-period reinstatement or application data, if possible, to validate the anticipation mechanism; and (3) discuss how this anticipatory behavior, if real, influences the interpretation of the post-2011 effects (e.g., is the post-2012 decline partly a reversal of pre-2011 suppression?).
*   **Cross-Sectional Interpretation of Charitable Giving:** The analysis of charitable deductions (Table 4, columns 1-2) is presented as a result but is fundamentally cross-sectional (comparing high- vs. low-intensity counties *after* the shock) due to the lack of pre-2011 SOI data. The authors correctly note the "absence of pre-period giving data limits causal interpretation," but this disclaimer is buried and the results are still discussed alongside the causal DiD estimates. This is misleading. The charitable giving analysis should be either moved to a purely descriptive section in the appendix with explicit caveats, or, better yet, the authors should attempt to find a proxy for pre-period charitable activity (e.g., religious adherence rates, historical foundation assets) to create a DiD-style design for this outcome. Presenting OLS estimates alongside DiD estimates in the main results table implies a comparable identification strategy, which is not warranted.

**4. Suggestions**
The paper is built on a clever and important idea with rich data. The following suggestions aim to strengthen the analysis, interpretation, and presentation.

*   **Clarify the Treatment and Its Variation:** The narrative focuses on the 2011 "single day" event, but the treatment measure spans three years. The introduction and institutional background should more precisely frame the "mass revocation" as a process beginning in 2010. A figure mapping the monthly or quarterly count of revocations from 2010-2012 would visually establish the shock's timing and duration. Furthermore, the authors should characterize the geographic variation in `RevocationIntensity`. What county-level factors (e.g., rural/urban, poverty rate, sector composition like share of 501(c)(3) vs. (c)(4)) predict higher intensity? A descriptive table or discussion would help readers assess whether treatment intensity is plausibly as-good-as-random conditional on fixed effects or if it proxies for other economic trends.
*   **Deepen the Mechanism and Heterogeneity Analysis:** The proposed mechanism—damage to "institutional infrastructure"—is compelling but under-explored. The paper could strengthen this narrative by testing for heterogeneous effects. For example, does the negative effect on formations concentrate in counties with fewer large "anchor" institutions (e.g., hospitals, universities) that might stabilize the ecosystem? Does the effect differ for subgroups of nonprofits (e.g., using NTEE codes from the BMF to separate arts, human services, education)? If some revoked organizations were quickly reinstated, does their presence mitigate the negative effects? Exploring these margins would move the paper from establishing a *what* to explaining a *why*.
*   **Address Potential Mean Reversion and Functional Form:** The outcome variable, new formations per capita, likely exhibits mean reversion. Counties with a historically high density of nonprofits (which may also have a high share of "zombies") might naturally see slower growth. The county-specific linear trends (column 3 of Table 2) are a good start, but the coefficient becomes imprecise. The authors should consider an alternative specification suggested in the literature: instead of a simple interaction `Intensity x Post`, use `Intensity x I(t >= 2011) + Pre-2010 Formation Rate x I(t >= 2011)`. This more flexibly controls for differential post-trends based on pre-shock activity. The robustness check in Table 5, column 4, is a version of this and strengthens the result—this should be highlighted and perhaps promoted to a preferred specification.
*   **Improve Visualization and Transparency:** The event study plot is described in text but not shown. A coefficient plot with 95% confidence intervals for the event study in **Figure 1** is essential. It would immediately convey the inverted-U pattern and the pre-trend issue more effectively than Table 3. Furthermore, the appendix should include a balance table showing pre-2010 county characteristics (e.g., population, income, nonprofit density, employment) by treatment intensity quartile to assess the exogeneity of the variation.
*   **Refine the Policy Discussion:** The conclusion that maintaining dormant organizations is "costless" may be too strong. The policy was motivated by administrative and transparency costs (cluttered registry, donor confusion). The paper provides evidence of one cost of revocation (ecosystem damage) but does not measure the potential benefits (reduced IRS administrative burden, improved donor information). A more nuanced policy conclusion would be that the ecosystem costs appear significant and should be weighed against the administrative benefits, perhaps arguing for a more tailored approach (e.g., a "dormant" status instead of revocation) rather than abandoning registry cleanup entirely.
*   **Data and Sample Clarification:** The sample description notes excluding counties with fewer than 10 pre-2010 nonprofits. The rationale (avoiding extreme ratios) is sound, but the authors should report how many counties are dropped and verify that this exclusion does not systematically bias the sample (e.g., towards more urban counties). A footnote or appendix table summarizing the sample selection flow would be helpful. Additionally, for the employment outcome, the use of NAICS 813 is appropriate but imperfect, as it includes for-profit professional organizations. The authors should note this as a limitation and, if possible, mention if alternative data (e.g., from the Nonprofit Quarterly Census) were considered.
