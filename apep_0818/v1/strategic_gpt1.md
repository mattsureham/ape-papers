## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper studies a striking administrative shock: in 2011 the IRS automatically revoked the tax-exempt status of roughly 275,000 nonprofits that had failed to file for three consecutive years. Using cross-county variation in how intense this purge was, the paper asks whether clearing out “zombie” nonprofits stimulates new organizational entry via creative destruction, or instead weakens the local nonprofit ecosystem; its headline claim is that any short-run burst of entry is followed by lower formation and lower nonprofit employment.

A busy economist should care because this is not really about IRS paperwork. It is about a broader question: when does removing inactive incumbents improve allocative efficiency, and when does organizational culling destroy complementarities, legitimacy, and infrastructure that support entry and activity?

### Does the paper articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Partly. The opening is vivid and memorable, but the first two paragraphs are too institutional before the paper has earned that detail. The paper gets to the big economics question by paragraph three, which is too late for a top-journal introduction. Right now the opening reads like an interesting policy episode in the nonprofit sector; it should instead immediately read like a paper about organizational cleansing, creative destruction, and ecosystem fragility.

### What the first two paragraphs should say instead

The paper should open with the economic question first, then present the IRS shock as an unusually clean setting to answer it. Something like:

> Policymakers often assume that removing inactive organizations improves the health of a sector: culling “zombies” should free up attention, resources, and institutional space for more productive entrants. But in sectors sustained by networks, legitimacy, and complementarities rather than pure market competition, the opposite may be true: eliminating dormant incumbents may damage the ecosystem that supports new entry.
>
> This paper studies that question using a large, sharp administrative purge in the U.S. nonprofit sector. In 2011, the IRS automatically revoked the tax-exempt status of roughly 275,000 nonprofits—about one quarter of all registered organizations—after a new filing requirement exposed widespread noncompliance among small entities. I show that counties more exposed to this mass revocation experienced a brief increase in new nonprofit entry followed by a larger decline in formation and lower nonprofit employment, suggesting that organizational cleanup can produce collateral damage rather than creative destruction.

That is the pitch. The institutional details can come after.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### Contribution in one sentence

The paper claims that mass administrative removal of dormant nonprofits does not revitalize the sector through creative destruction; instead, after a short-lived spike, it reduces subsequent nonprofit entry and employment, implying that organizational culling can weaken local ecosystem capacity.

Not yet, at least not sharply enough for AER. The contribution is directionally clear, but the paper does not yet distinguish itself with enough force from four adjacent literatures:

1. zombie-firm / creative-destruction papers in IO and macro,
2. nonprofit entry and organizational ecology papers,
3. administrative burden / compliance / state-capacity papers,
4. deregistration / formalization / legal-status papers outside the U.S. nonprofit setting.

Right now the paper says, in effect, “there is no prior study of this exact policy in this exact sector.” That is true but not sufficient. Top journals want to know what general lesson for economics emerges from this setting.

It starts to answer a world question, which is good: does removing inactive organizations help sectors renew themselves? But it repeatedly slides back into “this contributes to the nonprofit literature” and “this extends the zombie literature to nonprofits.” Those are secondary. The strongest frame is not “no one has studied revocation before”; it is “creative-destruction logic can fail in sectors where organizations generate ecosystem externalities.”

Some could, but many would still summarize it as “a DiD paper on nonprofit deregistration.” That is a warning sign. The introduction currently has an interesting event and a set of results, but the general conceptual novelty remains blurrier than it should be.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Several options, in descending order of impact:

- **Reframe around ecosystem externalities, not nonprofits per se.** The bigger claim is about when institutional cleanup backfires in sectors with network complementarities and legitimacy spillovers.
- **Strengthen the mechanism evidence.** The paper asserts damage to “donor networks, legitimacy, community awareness, grant pipelines,” but this remains mostly verbal. Even one or two cleaner mechanism proxies would make the paper feel much larger.

Most concretely: the paper would be bigger if it could show not just that entry fell, but **why**—for instance through donor behavior, volunteer mobilization, grantmaking, or the disappearance of local umbrella infrastructure.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Policymakers often believe that cleaning out inactive organizations is healthy: removing deadwood should improve information quality, reduce clutter, and create room for new activity.

### Tension

That logic may fail where organizations are embedded in networks and local civic infrastructure. Dormant nonprofits may still carry legitimacy, social ties, volunteer pathways, donor relationships, and community visibility. So a mass purge may either liberate space or damage the ecosystem.

### Resolution

The paper finds an initial rise in new nonprofit formations after revocation in high-exposure counties, but this quickly reverses; medium-run formation falls, and nonprofit employment also declines.

### Implications

Administrative cleanup is not necessarily benign. In sectors built on complementarities, registry enforcement can reduce ecosystem capacity rather than sharpen competition.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the ingredients of one, but the arc is not yet fully disciplined. The paper currently feels like: interesting shock → formation result → event study pattern → employment result → some cross-sectional giving evidence → discussion. That is competent, but still a bit like a collection of related findings orbiting an intuitive story.

The story it **should** be telling is tighter:

1. **Common presumption:** cleanup improves organizational vitality.
2. **Alternative theory:** in networked sectors, dormant entities may still support ecosystem function.
3. **Natural test:** the 2011 IRS purge.
4. **Key fact:** apparent short-run creative destruction is a mirage; medium-run collateral damage dominates.
5. **General lesson:** cleansing policies have equilibrium effects that depend on organizational interdependence.

That should be the spine of the paper.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?

“In 2011, the IRS erased about one quarter of the registered nonprofit sector in one administrative sweep—and counties hit harder did not end up with more new nonprofits; after a brief spike, they ended up with fewer.”

That is a good lead. It is concrete, surprising, and memorable.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

They would lean in at the opening fact. But whether they stay engaged depends on what comes next. If the follow-up is “the effects are statistically significant but economically small, and the mechanism is speculative,” attention will drift. If the follow-up is “this reveals a broader failure of cleansing logic in sectors with strong ecosystem complementarities,” then it becomes much more interesting.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Probably one of three:

1. “Were those organizations actually dead?”
2. “Why would removing inactive entities reduce future entry?”
3. “Is this really about nonprofits, or about administrative burdens and registry quality more generally?”

The current paper has decent instincts on these questions, but not yet decisive answers.

### If findings are modest, is the modesty itself interesting?

The average effect sizes are not large, and the paper should stop trying to rescue that with repeated references to “small negative standardized effects.” For AER positioning, effect-size taxonomy tables are not helping. The interesting result is the **dynamic reversal**—an apparent cleansing boom that becomes a contraction. That is the part economists will remember. The paper should lean into that, not into classifying coefficients as “small negative.”
