## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether clearing out “dead” nonprofits helps the nonprofit sector renew itself or instead damages the local ecosystem that sustains entry and activity. Using the IRS’s 2011 mass auto-revocation of roughly 275,000 tax-exempt organizations, the paper argues that high-revocation counties saw a brief spike in new nonprofit formation followed by a larger decline in entry and lower nonprofit employment, suggesting collateral damage rather than creative destruction.

A busy economist should care because this is, in principle, a sharp test of a broad economic idea: whether removing inactive incumbents improves allocative dynamism, or whether in networked sectors exit can reduce future entry.

Does the paper itself articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs? Not quite. The opening is vivid and concrete, which is good, but it spends too much time on institutional detail before stating the big question in a way that general economists will recognize. The first two paragraphs should do less “here is a policy” and more “here is a general economic question, and here is a rare setting where we can see it.”

### The pitch the paper should have

“Does removing inactive organizations make an economy more dynamic, or can it destroy the ecosystem that supports new entry? This paper studies that question in the nonprofit sector using the IRS’s 2011 automatic revocation of tax-exempt status for 275,000 organizations—roughly one-quarter of all registered nonprofits in a single administrative purge.

I show that counties hit harder by revocation experience a short-run increase in nonprofit entry, but this quickly reverses: new formations fall and nonprofit employment declines. The episode suggests that in sectors where organizations are linked through donors, volunteers, and local legitimacy, clearing out ‘zombies’ may weaken rather than strengthen organizational dynamism.”

That is the AER version of the story: not “an IRS event happened,” but “this is a clean case of the economics of exit, dynamism, and ecosystem spillovers.”

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:**  
The paper claims that mass administrative exit of nonprofits, unlike standard creative-destruction logic, reduces medium-run organizational dynamism because nonprofits are embedded in local ecosystems with complementarities rather than purely competitive relationships.

This is a potentially interesting contribution, but it is not yet clearly differentiated from neighboring literatures.

### Is it clearly differentiated from the closest papers?
Only partially. The paper gestures at “zombie firms,” nonprofit organizational dynamics, and IRS enforcement, but it does not sharply say what prior work would lead us to expect, and why this setting overturns or qualifies that expectation. Right now, the contribution reads as: “Here is a DiD on an unusual nonprofit policy.” It needs to read as: “Here is evidence that the consequences of exit depend on whether organizations compete for market share or jointly produce local organizational capacity.”

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a literature gap?
Mostly the latter, with some movement toward the former. The stronger framing is clearly about the world: **When does removing inactive organizations increase dynamism, and when does it damage local capacity?** The current introduction still lapses into “this contributes to the literature on nonprofit organizational dynamics” language too early. That weakens it.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the intro?
Not confidently. They could probably say, “It’s a paper about IRS revocations and nonprofit entry.” They may not come away with the sharper takeaway: “This shows that creative destruction may fail in sectors with network complementarities and local legitimacy externalities.” That distinction matters enormously for AER positioning.

### What would make the contribution bigger?
Several possibilities:

1. **Bigger framing:**  
   Lean harder into a general theory-of-sector angle: creative destruction works differently when incumbents create positive spillovers for entrants.

2. **Better mechanism evidence:**  
   The paper currently infers “ecosystem damage” from the dynamic pattern. That is too interpretive. A bigger paper would show which part of the ecosystem breaks: donor participation, volunteering, local density of affiliated organizations, grant flow, religious/civic associations, or re-registration frictions.

3. **Sharper heterogeneity:**  
   The paper would gain a lot by distinguishing sub-sectors where complementarities are likely strongest: religious congregations, mutual aid, youth sports, volunteer fire departments, neighborhood associations, grantmaking nonprofits. If the effect is concentrated in locally networked organizations, the story gets much more compelling.

4. **Comparison to for-profit organizational exit:**  
   The “creative destruction fails here” claim would be bigger if there were a parallel or benchmark showing that sectors without these complementarities look different.

5. **A more consequential outcome:**  
   Entry counts are useful, but AER-level ambition would benefit from outcomes that speak more directly to welfare or service provision: local charitable activity, service availability, civic participation, volunteering, or public-good provision.

At present, the paper has a plausible contribution. It does not yet have a maximally legible one.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

### Closest neighbors
The paper seems to sit near several conversations:

   - Harrison and Laincz (2010) on nonprofit entry/crowding/competition

   - The older sociology/organizational ecology literature is relevant even if not standard econ canon

3. **Tax-exempt regulation / nonprofit compliance / nonprofit finance**
   - Work on IRS oversight, reporting requirements, and donor response to nonprofit disclosure and status
   - Possibly the nonprofit accountability/disclosure literature, including work on Form 990 and donor behavior

4. **State capacity / administrative burden / registration regimes**
   - There is a broader public economics / political economy conversation about administrative enforcement that this paper could tap more effectively

### How should the paper position itself?
**Build on, not attack.** It should not set itself up as “the zombie firm literature is wrong.” That is too strong and not credible. It should say: the paper identifies a class of environments—nonprofits and perhaps other networked sectors—where exit has different spillovers than in standard market-cleansing models.

The right framing is:
- build on the creative-destruction literature by showing boundary conditions,
- connect to organizational ecology by giving quasi-experimental evidence,
- connect to public economics by showing unintended consequences of administrative enforcement.

### Is it positioned too narrowly or too broadly?
At the moment, oddly both.
- **Too narrowly** in that much of the draft reads like a paper for nonprofit scholars or public administration readers.
- **Too broadly** in the sense that “creative destruction fails” is asserted at a high level without enough structure around when and why.

The right audience is not “people interested in nonprofits.” It is economists interested in **dynamism, entry, exit, and administrative state effects in sectors with complementarities.**

### What literature does it seem unaware of?
It seems under-connected to:
- the broader entry/exit/reallocation literature in IO and macro,
- administrative burden/state capacity work,
- place-based local public goods / civic capital literatures,
- organizational ecology beyond the few nonprofit cites,
- possibly religious economy / civic association literatures if many revoked entities are congregations or local associations.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?
Not yet. The most impactful framing is not “IRS policy for nonprofits.” It is:
**What happens when the state forcibly removes a large stock of low-activity organizations from a sector where organizations generate local complementarities?**

That is a much better conversation.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
There is a common intuition in economics and policy that clearing out inactive or low-quality incumbents frees up space and resources for more productive entrants.

### Tension
That logic may not hold in nonprofits, where organizations may support one another indirectly through donor norms, volunteer networks, legitimacy, and civic infrastructure. So does culling “zombies” cleanse the sector or damage it?

### Resolution
The paper finds a short-run burst of new entry after mass revocation, followed by a larger drop in formations and lower nonprofit employment.

### Implications
Exit can reduce rather than enhance dynamism when organizations are tied together by complementarities rather than pure competition. Administrative cleanup may therefore impose real ecosystem costs.

This is a good arc in principle. The problem is that the paper only intermittently tells it. Too often it reads like:
1. here is the policy,
2. here is the design,
3. here are several outcomes,
4. here is an interpretation.

That is competent, but not memorable. The story should be much tighter:
- **Question:** Does forced exit increase dynamism?
- **Setting:** A massive administrative purge of nonprofits.
- **Key fact:** Entry initially rises, then collapses.
- **Interpretation:** The sector has ecosystem spillovers.
- **Implication:** Cleansing logic is context-dependent.

Right now the paper has a story, but it is not disciplined enough in repeatedly returning to that story. It still feels somewhat like a collection of results searching for a general-interest narrative.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party?
“In 2011 the IRS wiped out about a quarter of registered nonprofits in one shot, and the places hit hardest got an initial burst of new nonprofits that quickly turned into fewer new nonprofits and lower sector employment.”

That is a good opening fact. People would lean in at first, because the policy shock is dramatic and unusual.

### Would they keep listening?
They would keep listening if the presenter quickly pivoted to the general lesson: “This tells us creative destruction can fail when organizations create local spillovers.” They would reach for their phones if it stayed at the level of “county-level nonprofit counts moved around after a policy.”

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Almost certainly:
- “What exactly is the mechanism?”
Then:
- “Were these actually dead nonprofits, or just noncompliant ones?”
- “Why would removing inactive entities reduce future entry?”
- “Is this about donor confidence, network loss, administrative hassle, or measurement?”

Those follow-up questions reveal the paper’s current weakness. The central empirical pattern is interesting. The causal narrative around that pattern is still too asserted relative to what is shown in the paper.

### If findings are modest, is the modesty itself okay?
Yes, but only if framed correctly. The average effect sizes are not huge; the dynamic reversal is the main interesting result. The paper should stop overselling tiny standardized effects in the conclusion. “Small negative standardized effect” is not an AER sentence. The striking thing is not the average effect size metric; it is the dynamic reversal after a massive organizational purge. That is the memorable fact.

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

   The detailed walkthrough of filing rules and reinstatement can be compressed by a page or more.

2. **Method exposition** should be leaner in the main text.  
   This is not the paper’s comparative advantage. Get to the facts faster.

3. **Repeated interpretation** should be cut.  
   The “false spring” idea is nice language once; repeated restatements make it feel more speculative than grounded.

### What should be longer?
1. **Introduction** should be longer and more strategic.  
   Not in word count necessarily, but in intellectual architecture: broader question, sharper conceptual framework, clearer statement of why nonprofits are a hard test of cleansing logic.

2. **Mechanism/interpretation section** should be more developed.  
   Even without adding entirely new data, the paper needs a structured discussion of plausible channels and predictions, not just one umbrella phrase like “ecosystem damage.”

3. **Literature positioning** should be more precise.  
   Right now the cites are somewhat scattershot.

- The appendix table on standardized effect sizes adds little and actually hurts the paper. It invites the reader to conclude the effects are tiny. For a top-journal pitch, this is exactly the wrong emphasis.
- Some robustness tables can go to the appendix unless they are central to the narrative. The reader should not encounter all the plumbing before fully understanding the main claim.

### Is the paper front-loaded with the good stuff?
Reasonably, but not enough. The key dynamic result should appear immediately in the introduction, ideally with one sentence that says: “The average post effect is negative, but the important fact is a pronounced positive-then-negative reversal.” That is the hook.

### Are important results buried?
Yes: the dynamic reversal is the real paper, but the write-up still gives too much visual and rhetorical space to the average DiD coefficient. That is backwards.

### Is the conclusion adding value?
Only modestly. It mainly summarizes. A better conclusion would do one thing: state clearly the broader lesson about exit, ecosystem complementarities, and administrative enforcement.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

At present, this is **not yet an AER paper**, though it has the seed of one.

### What is the gap?
Mostly:
- **A framing problem**
- **A mechanism/scope problem**
- Some **ambition problem**

Less a novelty problem, because the setting is genuinely unusual. The issue is that the paper has not yet converted the unusual setting into a broad, field-shaping claim.

### More specifically
1. **Framing problem:**  
   The paper needs to become a paper about the economics of exit in networked sectors, not just nonprofit IRS enforcement.

2. **Scope problem:**  
   The paper currently documents reduced entry and lower employment, but the mechanism remains abstract. Without richer heterogeneity or direct ecosystem outcomes, the “collateral damage” interpretation feels underdeveloped.

3. **Ambition problem:**  
   The draft is satisfied with competent reduced-form evidence plus a plausible story. For AER, it needs to say something that changes how economists think about reallocation and administrative cleansing.

### What would excite the top 10 people in this field?
A version of this paper that convincingly showed:
- when incumbent exit helps versus hurts,
- why the nonprofit sector is a boundary case for standard creative destruction,
- and which local complementarities are severed by mass deregistration.

The top people would want either:
- stronger mechanism evidence,
- more conceptually disciplined heterogeneity,
- or a much sharper conceptual framework tying this episode to general theories of organizational ecology and reallocation.

### Single most impactful advice
**Reframe the paper around a general proposition—forced exit can reduce dynamism in sectors with local complementarities—and reorganize the evidence to support that proposition, especially through heterogeneity and mechanism rather than average treatment effects.**

That is the one change that could most alter the paper’s trajectory.
