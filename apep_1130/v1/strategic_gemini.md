## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
The paper asks whether expanding the definition of a "small business" in federal procurement causes contract dollars to cluster in fewer locations. Using staggered policy changes across industrial sectors, it finds that raising eligibility thresholds allows mid-sized firms in urban hubs to displace smaller firms in rural or "thin" markets, significantly reducing the number of counties that participate in the $98 billion set-aside program.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this well in the second paragraph, but the first paragraph is too descriptive of the institutional mechanics. 
**The pitch it should have:** 
"Does a policy designed to help small businesses actually hurt small towns? By expanding the definition of 'small' to include larger firms, the U.S. Small Business Administration may be inadvertently concentrating federal wealth in metropolitan hubs. This paper demonstrates that when eligibility thresholds rise, the geographic footprint of federal procurement shrinks, as mid-sized urban incumbents crowd out genuinely small firms in peripheral counties."

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**Contribution:** The paper identifies a "spatial crowding-out" effect of size-dependent regulations, showing that expanding eligibility for small-business status leads to a 17% contraction in the number of counties receiving federal contracts.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It differentiates itself well from Denes et al. (2024). While Denes looks at firm-level displacement, this paper looks at the *spatial* map of that displacement.
*   **World vs. Literature:** It currently leans toward "filling a gap," but it has the bones of a "question about the world" (specifically, the unintended geographic consequences of industrial policy).
*   **Clarity:** A smart economist would get it: "Raising the bar for 'small' moves money from the country to the city."
*   **Bigger Contribution:** To make this an AER-level contribution, the author needs to move beyond just counting counties. They need to show **welfare or local labor market effects.** If a county loses its only federal contractor, do local employment rates drop? Does the "multiplier" of federal spending vanish?

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
*   **Neighbors:** Denes et al. (2024) [SBA thresholds]; Garicano et al. (2016) [size-dependent regs]; Suarez Serrato & Wingender (2016) [fiscal federalism/spending incidence].
*   **Positioning:** It should **synthesize** the size-dependent regulation literature with the geographic inequality literature. Currently, it sits too comfortably in "SBA policy evaluation."
*   **Missing Conversations:** The paper is missing the "Spatial Economics/Urban-Rural Divide" conversation. It should speak to the literature on "Place-Based Policies" (e.g., Neumark & Simpson). If SBA set-asides are a de facto place-based policy for rural areas, this paper shows that threshold increases are a "de-tuning" of that policy.
*   **Framing:** Connect to the "Geography of Discontent." Are these policies contributing to the economic hollow-out of specific regions?

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC
*   **Setup:** The government spends $100B to help small firms, partly to ensure broad economic participation across the US.
*   **Tension:** The SBA keeps raising the definition of "small." In theory, this helps more firms; in practice, it might only help the "biggest of the small" who live in big cities.
*   **Resolution:** When the threshold goes up, the number of participating counties drops by 85. The "small business" program becomes a "metropolitan mid-sized business" program.
*   **Implications:** Bureaucratic "bracket creep" in definitions has the power to reshape the economic map of the US without a single vote in Congress.

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong but the "Implications" section is currently too dry. It needs to emphasize the political economy of these threshold shifts.

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
*   **The Lead Fact:** "When the SBA expands the definition of a small business, one in six participating counties gets wiped off the procurement map."
*   **Reaction:** Lean in. It’s a provocative result that suggests a "hidden" mechanism for regional inequality.
*   **Follow-up:** "Does this matter for local GDP, or do those small rural firms just find other work?" (This is the question the author must answer to clear the AER bar).

---

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
*   **Front-load:** The event study plots (which show the gradual displacement) need to be the center of the paper.
*   **Appendix:** The sector-by-sector descriptions can move to the appendix. 
*   **Robustness:** The "Total Procurement" placebo result (Table 3, Panel B) is a major red flag. If total procurement is falling, it suggests a sector-wide shock, not just an SBA policy effect. This needs to be addressed in the main text with a more robust decomposition.

---

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The distance is currently **Medium-to-Far**. The paper is a very competent "Policy Evaluation," but it lacks the "General Economic Insight" required for AER.

**The Gap:** It feels like a "reduced form" result without a deep dive into the *why* or the *so what*. 
1.  **The Why:** Use the County Business Patterns (CBP) data more aggressively. Show that the counties that lose out are exactly the ones where the "old" small firms were the only game in town.
2.  **The So What:** Link the loss of contracts to county-level economic health (employment, wage growth).

**Single Biggest Advice:** Move from a "counting counties" exercise to a "regional economic impact" study. If you can show that SBA threshold increases actually lead to measurable economic decline in rural counties, you have a top-five paper.
