**EDITORIAL MEMO: "Moving Births to Facilities: Evidence from India’s National Rural Health Mission"**

## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper evaluates India’s National Rural Health Mission (NRHM)—the largest community health worker (CHW) deployment in history—to determine if it successfully shifted childbirth from homes to hospitals and why this massive behavioral change failed to yield an obvious "inflection point" in neonatal survival. Using a 27-year panel with newly incorporated pre-treatment data, the author quantifies a massive 25-percentage-point increase in facility births in high-focus states, ultimately arguing that demand-side success (getting women to the clinic) was undermined by supply-side failure (the quality of care at those clinics).

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this clearly by the end of the first page, but the very first two paragraphs are a bit "policy-heavy." They emphasize the size of the program and the existing literature disagreement. 

**The pitch it should have:**
"Does moving millions of births from homes to clinics actually save lives in the developing world? This paper exploits the staggered rollout of India’s National Rural Health Mission—the largest health intervention of its kind—to show that while a bundle of cash incentives and health activists moved one in four births into facilities, the expected 'survival dividend' was likely lost to the abysmal quality of the clinics themselves. By resolving a decade-long debate with superior long-run data, the paper shifts the policy conversation from 'improving access' to 'fixing the destination.'"

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**The Contribution:** The paper provides the most robust causal evidence to date that India’s NRHM successfully transformed the "geography of birth" in its poorest states, while highlighting the "facility quality paradox" where massive utilization gains don't match mortality declines.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It differentiates well from Lim et al. (2010) and Powell-Jackson (2015) by using a much longer panel (DHS 1993-2020) and modern DiD diagnostics (parallel trends tests) that previous studies lacked.
*   **Framing:** It is currently framed as a mix of "resolving a literature dispute" and a "question about the world." The latter (the quality paradox) is the AER-level story; the former is more suited for a field journal.
*   **Smart Economist Test:** They would say: "It’s a robust DiD that confirms the program worked on behavior but hints at a major supply-side bottleneck."
*   **Bigger Contribution:** To make this "AER-big," the author needs to move beyond "suggestive" mortality evidence. If they could causally link the *lack* of mortality improvement to specific local facility quality measures (e.g., electricity/staffing levels at the district level), it would be a definitive statement on the limits of demand-side development.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

*   **Closest Neighbors:** Lim et al. (2010, *Lancet*); Powell-Jackson et al. (2015, *J. Dev. Stud.*); Gertler (2004, *AER* - Progresa); Alatas et al. (2012, *AER*).
*   **Positioning:** It currently "synthesizes" the literature by saying "Study A found a birth effect, Study B found no mortality effect; I show they are both right and explain why."
*   **Missing Conversations:** The paper should speak more to the **Political Economy of Public Service Delivery**. Why did the government invest billions in moving people to clinics but fail to fund the clinics themselves? Is there a "visibility" bias in performance-based CHW models?
*   **Right Conversation?** Yes, but it needs to pivot slightly away from "NRHM evaluation" and toward "The Limits of Demand-Side Incentives in the Face of Weak Institutions."

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

*   **Setup:** Global consensus pushes CHWs and cash transfers to solve maternal mortality.
*   **Tension:** We’ve been "grading on a curve" by measuring clinic visits instead of lives saved. India did the biggest experiment ever, and the results are confusingly mixed.
*   **Resolution:** The program was a behavioral triumph (25pp shift) but a health stalemate.
*   **Implications:** You can't "incentivize" your way out of a broken infrastructure.

**Evaluate:** The arc is strong. It isn't just a collection of results; it has a clear "Facility Quality Paradox" hook that keeps the reader engaged.

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

*   **The Fact:** "India spent billions to move 4 million births a year from homes to hospitals, but they sent them to clinics where only 16% met basic standards."
*   **Reaction:** Lean in. It’s a classic "Development Fail" story that challenges the WHO/World Bank orthodoxy of CHW-led models.
*   **Follow-up:** "If the survival gains weren't there, was the $30 billion a waste, or is the behavioral shift a necessary first step?"

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The gap is **Causal Mortality Evidence**. 

Right now, the mortality piece is "descriptive" and "suggestive." To clear the AER bar, the author needs to find a way to make the mortality result as robust as the delivery result. If they can show that in districts where clinic quality was high (using the 2012 assessment), the NRHM *did* save lives, while in low-quality districts it didn't, the paper becomes a landmark study on the **complementarity of demand-side and supply-side investments.**

**Single Piece of Advice:** Move from a "national descriptive" mortality story to a "within-India heterogeneity" story that causally links the 25pp delivery surge to mortality *only* when facility quality exceeds a certain threshold.
