### 1. Idea Fidelity
The paper partially pursues the original idea manifest but deviates substantially in execution, missing several core elements of the proposed identification strategy, data sources, and research question. The manifest promised a Callaway-Sant'Anna (CS) staggered DiD on monthly BLS LAUS and quarterly Census QWI data (2014-2025), supplemented by CPS ASEC microdata for race-by-age-by-education heterogeneity to test statistical discrimination (e.g., widening Black-White gaps, young low-skilled men). It emphasized two treatment definitions (enactment vs. implementation), robustness to alternative DiD estimators (Sun-Abraham, de Chaisemartin-D'Haultfoeuille, synthetic DiD), permutation inference, COVID controls, BTB interactions, and mechanism tests (industry/firm-size heterogeneity, dose-response by scope, hires/separations/earnings dynamics, decomposition of barrier removal vs. discrimination).

The paper retains the CS staggered DiD framework (as a robustness check) and tests aggregate employment plus Black-White gaps using 12 states' staggered adoption (enactment timing), but simplifies to annual state-year panels from BLS LAUS (aggregated) and ACS 1-year estimates (not CPS ASEC or QWI). It omits all promised heterogeneity/mechanisms (no age/education, industry, firm size, hires/separations, earnings composition, dose-response, or BTB main interactions), uses TWFE as baseline (problematic in staggered designs), shows no event studies/plots, and provides limited robustness (e.g., no full permutation results, synthetic DiD, or Roth pre-tests). The research question shifts from testing "intensified statistical discrimination widening racial gaps" (manifest H_A) to claiming barrier removal dominates, but without supporting empirics. Treatment excludes IL (as never-treated) and VA/NJ partially due to implementation delays, but does not systematically compare enactment vs. implementation. Overall, the paper captures the aggregate equilibrium motif and policy contrast to BTB/Agan et al. but executes a stripped-down version, undermining the manifest's novelty claims.

### 2. Summary
This paper examines the aggregate labor market effects of "clean slate" automatic criminal record sealing laws adopted by 12 U.S. states (2018-2023) using state-year difference-in-differences designs (TWFE and Callaway-Sant'Anna) on BLS LAUS unemployment/employment and ACS race-stratified employment-to-population ratios. It finds that these laws increased unemployment rates by ~0.3 pp (interpreted as labor force expansion), raised Black employment-to-population ratios by 1.3 pp, and narrowed White-Black gaps by 1.3 pp, suggesting barrier removal outweighs statistical discrimination (contra ban-the-box findings). Results are presented as robust to COVID exclusion, implementation timing, and leave-one-out tests, with policy implications for federal expansion.

### 3. Essential Points
The paper has potential but requires major revisions to establish credible identification and alignment with the research question. Three critical issues must be addressed; failure to do so warrants rejection.

1. **Parallel trends and staggered DiD validity are not convincingly demonstrated.** No event study plots or coefficients are shown (only a brief Sun-Abraham mention without details), despite promising 4+ pre-periods and Roth (2022) tests. With annual data, short post-periods (e.g., 1 year for NY/MN 2023 cohorts), and only 12 treated units, TWFE is biased (as acknowledged implicitly via CS); yet CS estimates are imprecise/noisy (e.g., racial gap CI includes zero and opposite sign). State-year aggregation loses power from promised monthly/quarterly data. Authors must provide event studies for all outcomes (CS/Sun-Abraham preferred), formal pre-trend tests (e.g., Roth/Huber conditional), and discuss cohort-specific ATTs/dynamics, or switch to a cleaner design (e.g., PA as single pre-2020 treatment).

2. **Core data sources and heterogeneity are omitted, crippling mechanism tests and research question match.** The manifest's key innovation—testing statistical discrimination via CPS ASEC race-age-education gaps, QWI hires/separations/earnings (for composition/LF effects), and industry/firm-size/dose-response—is entirely absent. ACS 1-year data (small cells, 2020 missing, excludes small states) is a poor substitute for CPS microdata or QWI; racial gaps use broad 16-64 population (not young low-skilled men à la Doleac-Hansen). Unemployment rise lacks LF/employment confirmation (log employment insignificant). Authors must replicate with promised CPS/QWI (publicly accessible per manifest), add Black-White gap decomposition by age/education/race-sex, and test mechanisms (e.g., QWI industry hires), or explicitly justify narrowing scope (but this guts novelty vs. Agan/Doleac).

3. **Treatment timing and controls are inconsistently handled, biasing enactment effects.** Enactment-year coding assumes immediate anticipation, but many states (e.g., NY 2027 implementation) have no sealing during the sample, conflating enactment with effects; robustness to "implementation" shrinks estimates (0.15 pp unemployment). No systematic Spec A vs. B comparison, BTB interactions (only mentioned), or never-treated purity (e.g., IL 2025 excluded arbitrarily). COVID overlap for 4 states unaddressed beyond exclusion. Authors must use event-time variation around implementation dates (restricting to 6-7 states with confirmed starts, per manifest), add BTB×post interactions and state stringency controls, and clarify eligible records sealed by 2023 (e.g., table NJ/VA petition-only issues).
