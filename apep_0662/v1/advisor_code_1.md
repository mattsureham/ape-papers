**Summary**  
This paper studies whether clean slate automatic record sealing laws narrow or widen the Black–White employment gap by exploiting staggered state adoption between 2018 and 2023. Using state-level BLS unemployment data and race-specific ACS employment–population ratios within a TWFE and Callaway–Sant’Anna framework, the author finds that clean slate laws raise Black employment by about 1.3 percentage points, leave White employment unchanged, and thereby narrow the racial employment gap—suggesting barrier removal dominates any statistical discrimination response.

**Essential Points**

1. **Parallel trends and pre-treatment dynamics for the ACS gap are under-documented.** The key identification assumption for the racial employment gap relies on only annual ACS data with relatively few pre-treatment years for the 2018–2023 adopters. The paper states that Sun–Abraham event studies “show no pre-treatment trend,” but these estimates are neither presented nor discussed in detail. Without plotted coefficients or formal tests, it is hard to assess whether the parallel trends assumption holds, particularly when the treated states differ systematically (e.g., PA, CA, MI) and when the White–Black gap has idiosyncratic year-to-year volatility. Please present the event study coefficients (including confidence intervals) for the racial gap and discuss their stability, and consider augmenting the specification with flexible time trends or leads to mitigate concerns about differential pre-trends.

2. **Treatment definition and timing may not accurately capture when records become sealed.** The baseline uses enactment years, but many states implemented clean slate policies only later (and some have not yet reached automation). This matters because employers cannot change behavior until implementation. Using enactment as treatment risks attributing effects to years in which the law was not operative, especially for the 2023 adopters (MN, NY). The robustness table shows weaker effects when switching to implementation timing, but the implementation specification is not systematically used for the racial gap outcome. Please re-estimate all main outcomes using implementation dates (and/or a “first actual sealing” indicator) to ensure the treatment timing aligns with the policy’s operative period. If implementation dates vary substantially across eligible counties or judges, consider instrumentalizing with readiness measures.

3. **Potential confounders and complementary policies are insufficiently controlled.** States adopting clean slate laws may also undertake other criminal justice reforms (e.g., ban-the-box policies, sentencing reforms) or labor market programs that impact employment or racial gaps. The paper references ban-the-box only briefly, reporting an insignificant interaction, but it does not control for the presence of other policies or state-level trends in incarceration, policing, or economic shocks. Without these controls, the estimated gap narrowing risks conflating clean slate effects with co-movements in policy or macro shocks, especially since the paper finds that unemployment rises simultaneously. Please include controls for major contemporaneous policies (BTB adoption, minimum wage changes, Medicaid expansions, etc.), add state-specific linear trends or time-varying covariates (GDP growth, incarceration rates), and explicitly discuss how these adjustments affect the main estimates.

**Suggestions**

1. **Present fuller diagnostics of the staggered DiD design.** The manifest emphasised modern staggered DiD methods (Callaway–Sant’Anna, Sun–Abraham, synthetic DiD, permutation inference), but the current paper only briefly mentions CS and an event study without figures. Display the group-time treatment effects and aggregated dynamic effects for both unemployment and the racial gap. Plot the event-study coefficients with confidence intervals so readers can visually inspect pre-trends and post-treatment trajectories. Provide the aggregated CS ATT with weights and explain the heterogeneity across cohorts; if some cohorts (early adopters like PA) drive most of the effect, clarify why.

2. **Expand data usage to capture the mechanism more directly.** The original plan included QWI hires/separations/earnings and CPS heterogeneity to test statistical discrimination. To better discriminate between barrier removal and statistical discrimination, incorporate at least some of these planned data sources. For instance:
   - Use QWI earnings and hires (even if only a subset of industries) to test whether employment gains for Black workers coincided with wage declines or increased hiring. This would help adjudicate whether the employment gains are driven by low-wage record holders entering the market or by broader labor demand shifts.
   - Use CPS ASEC microdata to construct race-age-education employment gaps and test whether the effects concentrate among the Doleac–Hansen targeted group (young non-college Black men). This would strengthen the claim that the barrier removal channel dominates and align more closely with the manifest’s heterogeneity claims.
   - If QWI industry stratification is feasible, show that sectors with high background check usage (finance, health) exhibit stronger effects, supporting the background-check mechanism.

3. **More carefully reconcile the unemployment and employment gap findings.** Table 3 reports a rise in the unemployment rate alongside rising Black employment. This can happen via labor force expansion, but the narrative should more explicitly link these pieces: Are newly re-entering workers classified as unemployed because they are searching but not yet employed? Could measurement error in the ACS (e.g., missing 2020) bias the gap estimate? Consider adding labor force participation as an outcome, or using the ACS to compute the labor force participation gap, to show that the rising unemployment partly reflects re-entry. This will help reassure readers that the unemployment increase is not indicative of a negative labor market shock offsetting the racial gap improvement.

4. **Clarify the role of never-treated states and later adopters.** The manifest distinguished enactment cohorts (13 states) and noted that some states (IL, MN, NY) only recently enacted laws with implementation into 2025–2027. The paper currently treats IL as never-treated because implementation lies outside the sample, but this may bias the control group if IL is similar to treated states. Consider reclassifying Illinois (and others whose implementation lies just beyond the sample) as treated in a “future treatment” specification to check whether anticipation or omitted adoption affects the estimates. Alternatively, implement an “event time” horizon that censors states once they adopt, so later-adopting states contribute to the control group only until their treatment year.

5. **Strengthen discussion of potential mechanisms beyond statistical discrimination.** The conclusion posits that permanent information destruction reduces discrimination because employers cannot learn records later. While plausible, this mechanism warrants more empirical support. Could you test whether explicitly measured background check intensity (e.g., from industry composition or state law on private background checks) moderates the effect? Or examine whether the narrowing of the gap is limited to occupations that traditionally rely on these checks? Adding such evidence, even in a limited form, would make the proposed mechanism more convincing.

6. **Revisit the presentation of standardized effect sizes and interpretability.** The standardized effect size table is interesting but may bewilder readers who expect raw percentage point changes. Consider moving it to an appendix and focusing in the main text on raw changes with clearer policy interpretations (e.g., “1.3 pp increase in Black employment equals X additional individuals per 100,000”). When discussing percentage reductions in the racial gap, note that the mean gap is only 1.9 pp, so the percentage change is large but occurs from a modest baseline; clarify whether this is economically meaningful in absolute terms.

7. **Address sample limitations due to ACS coverage.** ACS 1-Year is unavailable for 2020 and for small populations (WY, VT). A brief discussion of how these omissions affect inference—especially since 2020 coincides with COVID and falls between early and mid adopters—would strengthen credibility. You might also re-estimate using ACS 5-Year estimates (which include 2020) as a robustness check to ensure the findings are not driven by the missing year or by variations in sampling error.

8. **Ensure transparency around permutation and inference procedures.** The robustness table reports a permutation p-value of 0.12 but does not explain how these permutations were constructed (e.g., were treatment assignments reshuffled while keeping cohort sizes fixed?). Add a short appendix describing the permutation procedure, the handling of unbalanced cohorts, and whether the permutations respect the timing structure. This will help readers assess the strength of the evidence given the moderate number of treated states.

By addressing these points—particularly strengthening the identification narrative, expanding the data to test mechanisms, and aligning treatment timing with actual sealing—the paper can deliver a much more compelling and policy-relevant contribution.
