### 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper exploits the Supreme Court’s 2018 PASPA decision to test whether the rapid legalization of online sports betting led to an increase in suicide mortality. Using high-frequency (weekly) CDC provisional data and modern staggered DiD estimators, the author finds a precise null result, suggesting that the expansion of gambling access has not (yet) translated into population-level mortality. Economists should care because this addresses a major "externality" concern in the multi-billion dollar gambling industry and challenges the clinical intuition that increased access to addictive goods inevitably leads to extreme public health outcomes.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this well, but it leads with a $57 billion wagering figure. While interesting, the "AER pitch" should lead with the tension between individual clinical risk (15x suicide rate) and population-level policy. 

**The pitch the paper should have:**
"Clinical research indicates that individuals with gambling disorders are 15 times more likely to attempt suicide, yet the population-level consequences of the largest expansion of gambling access in U.S. history remain unknown. This paper uses the staggered legalization of online sports betting across U.S. states to provide the first quasi-experimental evidence on whether making gambling accessible on every smartphone increases suicide mortality. I find a precise null result across multiple specifications, suggesting that the documented financial and mental health costs of gambling have not scaled into a mortality crisis in the short run."

---

### 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution:** The paper provides the first quasi-experimental, population-level estimate of the impact of online sports betting legalization on suicide mortality.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It differentiates itself well from Humphreys & Ruseski (2024) and Hollenbeck (2025) by moving from "intermediate" harms (mental health/financial distress) to the "ultimate" harm (death).
*   **Framing:** It is framed as a question about the WORLD, which is strong.
*   **Clarity:** A smart economist would understand this immediately. It is "the mortality paper" in the sports betting literature.
*   **How to make it bigger:** To be a "big" AER paper, it needs more than just a null on one variable. It needs to bridge the gap between the documented financial distress (Hollenbeck) and this null mortality. Are people going broke but not dying? Is the "treatment" of legalization actually just a shift from illegal (bookies) to legal (apps), meaning the net change in access is smaller than we think? Exploring the *mechanism* of why the link breaks would elevate this.

---

### 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

*   **Closest Neighbors:** Case & Deaton (2015/2020) on "Deaths of Despair"; Humphreys & Ruseski (2024) on gambling and mental health; Hollenbeck (2025) on gambling and debt.
*   **Strategy:** The paper currently "builds" on them. It should arguably "tension" itself more against them. If Hollenbeck finds massive financial ruin, why don't we see suicides? That tension is where the AER interest lies.
*   **Missing Conversations:** The paper should speak more to the **Industrial Organization** and **Regulation of Addictive Goods** literatures (e.g., alcohol, tobacco, opioids). Is gambling "different" because of the lack of physical toxicity?
*   **Right Conversation?** Yes, but it could be framed more broadly as a test of the "availability-harm" hypothesis in public health.

---

### 4. NARRATIVE ARC

*   **Setup:** The 2018 SCOTUS ruling opens a floodgate for a new, highly accessible form of gambling.
*   **Tension:** Clinical data says gambling is lethal; the market is exploding; but we don’t know if "more gambling" = "more death."
*   **Resolution:** A precise null. No detectable increase.
*   **Implications:** Concerns about a "suicide epidemic" from gambling may be overstated, or the harms are manifesting in ways that don't reach the level of mortality.

**Evaluation:** The arc is clean. However, the "Pre-COVID" finding of a *decrease* in suicides is a narrative distraction. If the author wants to use it, they need a theory (e.g., does gambling provide a "third space" or social utility that *lowers* despair? Unlikely, but a story is needed). Otherwise, it feels like a "collection of results" in Section 5.2.

---

### 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

*   **Lead Fact:** "Legalizing online sports betting has zero detectable effect on suicide rates, despite it being a $90 billion industry."
*   **Reaction:** Lean in—because it’s counter-intuitive given the headlines about "gambling addiction." 
*   **Follow-up:** "How long is the lag? Does it take 5 years of losing money before someone hits rock bottom?"
*   **Null Result Value:** The null is highly valuable here because the *prior* (held by many policymakers) is that this is a public health catastrophe. Ruling out large effects is a major policy contribution.

---

### 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

*   **Section 2 (Institutional Background):** Keep it tight. We don't need a history of PASPA as much as we need a table/figure showing the *intensity* of the marketing/ads that followed.
*   **Front-loading:** The "back-of-the-envelope" calculation in Section 4.3 (page 8) is actually one of the most important parts of the paper. It tells us what effect we *should* have expected. Move that into the Introduction or the Discussion to frame the null.
*   **The Appendix:** Move the "NFL Season" interaction to the main results (it’s already in Table 3) but explain *why* it matters—it’s a test of "dosage."

---

### 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The gap is **Ambition/Scope.** Right now, it’s a very clean, very competent "Note" or a short paper. To make it an AER flagship:

1.  **Heterogeneity by Vulnerability:** Can you look at counties with high poverty or high baseline "deaths of despair"? A null in the aggregate might hide a crisis in the "bottom 10%."
2.  **The "Why":** Address the Hollenbeck contradiction. If betting causes financial ruin, why no suicide? Is it because the "new" bettors are wealthier/more resilient than the "old" casino gamblers?
3.  **Longer Horizon:** If the data exists, add 2022/2023. 20 months is short for a "path to ruin."

**Single biggest improvement:** Explicitly bridge the "Hollenbeck-Humphreys Gap"—reconcile the fact that while people report being more miserable and in more debt because of these apps, they aren't dying from them.
