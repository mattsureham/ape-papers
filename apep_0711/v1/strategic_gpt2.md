## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether the rapid legalization of online sports betting in U.S. states increased suicide mortality. That is a question economists should care about because sports betting has expanded at extraordinary speed, the policy debate is active, and the paper connects a large new consumer market to one of the most consequential possible social harms.

The paper does articulate the basic pitch reasonably clearly, but not optimally. The current opening spends too much time on clinical motivation and not enough on the broader economic and policy stakes of mass-market mobile gambling. It also leads with “one outcome has gone unexamined: death,” which is vivid but slightly tabloid; for AER, the better hook is that this is the first evidence on whether a huge expansion in a newly legal, app-based consumer finance/gambling product translated into extreme social harm at the population level.

### The pitch the paper should have

After *Murphy v. NCAA*, states rapidly legalized mobile sports betting, turning sports gambling from a niche activity into an app-based mass market. Policymakers and researchers now know this expansion affects betting, household finances, and self-reported mental health, but we still do not know whether it moved the most severe outcome of all: suicide mortality. Using the staggered rollout of online sports betting across U.S. states, this paper shows that—even against strong clinical priors linking gambling disorder to suicidality—the population-level effect on suicide deaths is close to zero in the short to medium run.  

That version makes the world-question sharper: did a major consumer-market liberalization produce detectable mortality harm?

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:**  
The paper provides the first quasi-experimental evidence on whether the legalization of online sports betting affects population suicide mortality, finding no detectable short-run population-level increase.

This contribution is fairly clear, but it is not yet sharply differentiated from the nearest papers. Right now the introduction signals: “here is a new outcome added to an emerging literature on sports betting.” That is a competent contribution, but it risks sounding like “another policy DiD, now on suicide.” The paper needs to work harder to explain why suicide is not just one more outcome, but the key test of whether gambling-related harms scale from individual pathology to aggregate public-health damage.

### Relative to closest papers
The contribution is differentiated from papers on:
- sports betting and mental health,
- sports betting and household financial distress,
- sports betting and spending/consumption,

but the paper does not yet articulate the distinction crisply enough. The distinction should be: prior work shows intermediate harms or distress; this paper asks whether those harms were large enough, prevalent enough, and immediate enough to shift mortality.

### World question vs literature gap
The paper mostly frames the contribution as a **world question**, which is good: does legal online betting kill? But it occasionally slips into literature-gap language (“one outcome has gone unexamined”; “contributes to three literatures”). The stronger framing is not “no one has estimated this yet,” but “this is the key policy margin if we want to know whether the social costs of legalization include extreme harms.”

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new?
A smart economist could roughly explain it, but the risk is they would say:  
“It's a staggered-adoption paper on online sports betting and suicide, and it finds a null.”

That is not enough for AER-level excitement. They should instead be able to say:  
“This paper asks whether the massive post-PASPA expansion of mobile betting generated detectable mortality harm. Despite severe clinical concern and emerging evidence of financial and mental-health damage, the aggregate suicide effect is basically zero.”

### What would make the contribution bigger?
Specific ways to make it bigger:

1. **Stronger framing around aggregation from micro harm to macro harm.**  
   The big question is not just gambling and suicide; it is whether large individual-level risk among a small subgroup translates into population mortality when access expands dramatically.

2. **Sharper heterogeneity or exposure margins.**  
   If feasible, the paper would be much more interesting if it could say where one would expect effects to be largest:
   - among men,
   - prime-age males,
   - during major betting seasons,
   - in states with larger betting handle per capita,
   - in areas with higher preexisting gambling participation or financial fragility.  
   Even a null that is robust in high-exposure groups becomes more informative.

3. **A more explicit link to market scale.**  
   The paper should convert the estimate into something economically legible: despite a near-zero-to-mass-market increase in legal online betting, suicide mortality did not move detectably. That contrast is the result.

4. **A less generic methodological claim.**  
   “We apply modern staggered DiD to provisional mortality data” is not an AER contribution. If the design is to matter strategically, it should be because the policy setting is first-order, not because the estimator is modern.

Mostly **build on**, not attack.

- Humphreys/Hollenbeck/Baker document intermediate outcomes.
- This paper asks whether those intermediate harms translated into the most severe population outcome.
- The answer appears to be no, at least over the available horizon.

That is a valuable synthesis: strong micro/clinical concern, some evidence of distress, but no aggregate mortality effect.

### Too narrow or too broad?
Currently it is **slightly too narrow in question, slightly too broad in claimed significance**.

- Too broad because invoking “deaths of despair” may oversell what the paper can speak to. This is not really a paper about the evolution of despair mortality in the Case-Deaton sense; it is about whether one prominent new addictive/financially risky technology generated detectable suicide effects.

A better audience is: economists interested in gambling, regulation of addictive digital products, and the translation of individual behavioral risk into social cost.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?
It should speak more to:
- **casino and lottery legalization** literature,
- **sin goods / addictive goods** literature,
- **consumer credit / fintech / digital platform access** literatures where the broader question is whether easier access to risky products causes severe downstream harm,
- possibly **health economics on suicide and economic shocks**.

The unexpected but potentially powerful conversation is not just with gambling papers. It is with the broader economics of **digital access to risky products**: when mobile technology dramatically lowers friction, do extreme harms increase?

That framing could widen the audience meaningfully.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
A major legal shock transformed sports betting from a largely prohibited or geographically limited activity into an easily accessible mobile product. Clinical evidence suggests gambling disorder is strongly associated with suicidality, and recent economics papers show online sports betting affects financial distress and mental health.

### Tension
If online betting expands access to a risky and potentially addictive activity, did that expansion generate extreme social harm at the population level? Or are the harms too concentrated, too rare, too offset by substitution from illegal markets, or too slow-moving to show up in mortality?

### Resolution
The paper finds no detectable increase in suicide mortality following legalization; if anything, the point estimates lean negative.

### Implications
The expansion of legal online sports betting may have produced financial and psychological harms without generating short-run aggregate suicide mortality effects. That matters for regulatory priorities and for how economists think about translating high-risk subgroup effects into population-level consequences.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
It has a **serviceable** arc, but not yet a compelling one. The current paper is a bit too much “clinical fact, design, null, robustness, discussion.” The story it should tell is more conceptual:

> The world saw an enormous expansion in access to mobile gambling. The key unresolved question is whether severe individual risk scales into aggregate mortality. We study that scaling question, and the answer appears to be no in the short run.

That is a cleaner narrative than simply “we study an unexamined outcome.”

The weakest part of the current narrative is that the paper overstates decisiveness (“The answer is no”; “decisive null”) while also acknowledging that clinically plausible effects are within the confidence interval. That creates tension in the wrong direction. Strategically, the paper should present itself as **evidence against large immediate mortality effects**, not as having closed the case.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?
“I’d lead with: the post-2018 explosion of legal mobile sports betting in U.S. states does not appear to have increased suicide mortality, despite strong clinical concerns and evidence of financial distress.”

That is the dinner-party fact.

### Would people lean in?
Yes, at least initially. The topic is salient, the outcome is high-stakes, and the contrast between alarming clinical evidence and a population null is interesting.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Almost certainly:
- “Is that because there’s truly no effect, or because suicide is too rare / the horizon is too short?”
- Then: “What about the groups most exposed?”
- Then: “What about financial distress, bankruptcy, overdoses, or domestic violence?”

Those follow-up questions reveal the paper’s current strategic issue: the main result is interesting, but readers will immediately want either a stronger exposure test or a broader implication.

### Is the null interesting?
Yes, but only if framed correctly. AER readers will not care about “we found nothing” unless the paper convinces them that:
1. there was a strong ex ante reason to expect an effect,
2. the policy change was economically large,
3. the confidence interval rules out effects large enough to matter for policy.

The paper gets (1) and (2), but undersells and oversells (3) at different moments. It says “precise null” and “decisive null,” but later admits clinically plausible effects are within the interval. That inconsistency weakens the strategic case. The paper should instead say:

- it rules out **large short-run increases** in suicide mortality,
- it does **not** rule out smaller clinically plausible effects,
- therefore the contribution is to discipline alarmist claims, not to prove zero harm.

That is a much stronger null-result posture.

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

### What would make it read better?

1. **Rewrite the introduction to front-load the actual tension.**  
   The first page should establish:
   - mobile sports betting expanded rapidly,
   - prior work finds intermediate harms,
   - the unresolved question is whether those harms rose to mortality,
   - this paper’s answer is no detectable short-run increase.

2. **Shorten the institutional background.**  
   Much of Section 2 is standard and could be compressed. The reader does not need several paragraphs on PASPA history and market scale before getting to the core argument.

3. **Cut or demote the “methodological contribution.”**  
   It reads like filler. AER readers will not be persuaded by “we use modern staggered DiD on provisional mortality surveillance data” as a main contribution.

4. **Bring interpretation closer to the main result.**  
   The paper should explain on the results page itself what the estimate means in economic terms:
   - relative to the baseline suicide rate,
   - relative to the size of the betting-market expansion,
   - relative to what policy fears implied.

5. **Handle the pre-COVID negative finding more carefully.**  
   Right now it is presented as “provocative but fragile.” Fine, but the paper gives it perhaps too much oxygen. Strategically, this is not the headline; it is a side note. Don’t let a fragile negative estimate hijack the narrative.

6. **Move some robustness material out of the main text if needed.**  
   The leave-one-out table is useful but not intrinsically interesting. The main text should privilege:
   - the central estimate,
   - event-study shape,
   - perhaps one economically meaningful heterogeneity test if available.

7. **Strengthen the conclusion.**  
   The current conclusion mostly summarizes. It should instead make one clean substantive point: aggregate mortality is not where the social costs of sports betting show up, at least so far; economists and policymakers should look more to financial distress and other margins.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

At present, the gap is mostly a mix of **framing problem** and **ambition problem**, with a bit of **scope problem**.

### Framing problem
The science may be adequate, but the paper is not yet telling the biggest version of its story. The right story is not “first paper on gambling legalization and suicide.” That is too incremental. The right story is:

> a massive digital-market liberalization with plausible severe harms did not produce detectable aggregate mortality effects.

That is broader, more interesting, and more AER-relevant.

### Ambition problem
The paper currently feels safe: one treatment, one grave outcome, one null. That can work only if the framing is extraordinary or the implications are broader. Right now neither is fully there.

### Scope problem
To get closer to AER territory, the paper likely needs either:
- sharper heterogeneity along exposure or susceptibility margins, or
- a broader set of extreme outcomes / related harms, or
- a more convincing conceptual contribution about when micro-level pathology does or does not aggregate into macro-level harm.

### Novelty problem
Moderate, not fatal. The exact outcome is novel, but the policy setting is already crowded. So novelty must come from the question’s importance and the paper’s interpretation, not just from being “first on suicide.”

### Single most impactful advice
**Reframe the paper around the aggregation question—why a dramatic expansion in access to a risky digital good did not translate into detectable population mortality—and reorganize the introduction and discussion around that conceptual contribution rather than around being the first paper on this specific outcome.**

That single change would improve the paper more than any cosmetic addition.
