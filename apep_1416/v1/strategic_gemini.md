### 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
The paper asks whether granting legal status to asylum seekers impacts local housing markets, using the quasi-random assignment of immigration judges as a natural experiment. It finds that while legal status provides critical access to formal housing tools (credit, subsidies, work permits), marginal changes in grant rates have no detectable effect on county-level rents or home values. This suggests that the "immigration effect" on housing documented in previous literature is driven by physical volume (people moving) rather than the legal credentials they hold.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this well, but it buries the "So What?" (the distinction between volume and status) in the second paragraph. The first paragraph is a bit too descriptive of the mechanism.

**The pitch the paper should have:**
"Does the legal right to work and borrow actually change where and how immigrants live, or is housing demand simply a function of 'heads on beds'? By exploiting the 'refugee roulette' of judge leniency, this paper provides the first causal evidence that legal status—independent of population flow—has zero marginal impact on local housing prices. This result suggests that the well-documented inflationary effect of immigration on housing is a pure supply-and-demand story of population volume, unmediated by the formalization of legal status."

---

### 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**One-sentence contribution:** The paper identifies the "legal status premium" in housing markets as being zero at the margin, effectively de-coupling the legal channel from the volume channel in the immigration-housing literature.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It is clearly differentiated from Saiz (2007) and Howard (2020) by its focus on *status* rather than *stock*. It builds on the judge-leniency literature (Kling, 2006; Maestas et al., 2013) by applying a proven IV to a new, macro-relevant outcome.
*   **Question vs. Literature:** It frames itself as answering a question about the world ("Does status move markets?"), which is a strength.
*   **"Another DiD?":** A smart economist would see this as a "Clean IV for a messy problem" paper.
*   **Making it bigger:** To be a "big" AER paper, it needs to address the "Scale" problem mentioned in the discussion. If the N is too small to move a county, can the author look at **highly localized** outcomes (e.g., ZIP codes near the court) or **specific segments** (e.g., the bottom quintile of the rental market) where 40 grants might actually matter?

---

### 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
The paper sits at the intersection of Urban Economics (Housing) and Labor Economics (Migration).

*   **Closest neighbors:** Saiz (2007) on immigration/rents; Ramji-Nogales et al. (2007) on judge leniency; and perhaps Borjas (2017) on the integration of asylees.
*   **Positioning:** It should "pivot" from the neighbors. It shouldn't attack Saiz; it should say, "Saiz found a large effect; we show that the *status* component of that effect is surprisingly small."
*   **Missing conversations:** The paper needs to speak to the **Informal Economy** literature. The "null" is only interesting if we believe unauthorized immigrants are already housed. Connecting this to the literature on the "shadow economy" or "informal housing" would broaden the appeal.

---

### 4. NARRATIVE ARC
*   **Setup:** Immigration is known to raise rents.
*   **Tension:** We don't know if this is because they are people (volume) or because they get papers (status). Papers unlock credit and subsidies, which *should* theoretically spike demand more than just "arriving" does.
*   **Resolution:** It doesn't. The "status" effect is a precise zero.
*   **Implications:** Policy debates about the "burden" of asylees on local housing costs are likely focused on the wrong margin.

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong. It’s a "The Dog That Didn't Bark" story.

---

### 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
At a dinner party, I'd lead with: *"Giving an asylum seeker a work permit and a social security number doesn't actually change the local rent, which means they were probably already finding a place to sleep without them."*

**Verdict:** People lean in. The follow-up is: *"Is it because they're already in 'informal' housing, or because there just aren't enough of them to matter?"* This is the "Scale" problem. The paper needs to prove the null isn't just a lack of power.

---

### 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
*   **Front-loading:** The paper is well-structured.
*   **The "Scale" Discussion:** Section 6 (Discussion) is currently a defensive list of reasons why the result is null. This should be transformed into a **Mechanism** section. 
*   **Move to Appendix:** Table 5 (Standardized Effect Sizes) is actually very useful for the "Precise Null" argument and should be more prominent in the main text.

---

### 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The gap is **Ambition vs. Scale**. AER reviewers will likely say: "You found a null at the county level, but the treatment (40 people/year) is a drop in the bucket for a county. Of course it's a null."

**The single most impactful change:**
The author must move from **County-level** analysis to **Sub-market** analysis. If they can show that even in the specific neighborhoods where these asylees settle (using ACS migration data to identify "enclaves"), there is *still* no effect, the paper becomes a major statement on the resilience of informal housing markets.
