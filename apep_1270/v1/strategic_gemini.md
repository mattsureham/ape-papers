### 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
This paper asks whether carbon taxes—the "gold standard" of climate policy—actually drive the transition to zero-carbon technology or merely nudge consumers toward "less-bad" fossil fuels. Using a decade of Swiss data, the author finds that the primary result of rising carbon levies was a shift from heating oil to natural gas, with almost no accelerated adoption of heat pumps. This suggests that price signals alone can create a "gas bridge trap" that locks in fossil infrastructure for decades rather than solving the decarbonization problem.

**Evaluation:** The paper does a commendable job in the first two paragraphs. It immediately sets up the tension between economic theory (taxes = efficiency) and the practical reality of capital stock transitions. However, the first paragraph is a bit heavy on Swiss context and light on the broader economic stakes.
**Revised Pitch:** *While carbon taxes are theorized to induce a transition to clean technology, their real-world impact depends on the relative costs of intermediate substitutes. I show that in the building sector, carbon pricing can backfire by incentivizing "fossil-to-fossil" substitution—replacing dirty oil with slightly cleaner gas—thereby locking in carbon emissions for another capital cycle. This "gas bridge trap" suggests that without complementary mandates, price signals may fail to bridge the cost gap to frontier green technologies.*

### 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**The Contribution:** The paper provides empirical evidence that carbon pricing directs technical change toward intermediate fossil fuels rather than green frontiers when the cost gap between them remains large.

*   **Differentiation:** It moves beyond the "does the tax reduce emissions?" question (Andersson, 2019; Metcalf, 2019) to the "what technology replaces the old one?" question. It distinguishes itself from Cattaneo et al. (2022), who found null effects on Swiss oil consumption, by looking at the long-run technology mix rather than short-run demand.
*   **Framing:** It is framed as answering a question about the **WORLD** (the efficacy of the energy transition), but leans heavily on "filling a gap" in the Swiss-specific literature in the intro.
*   **Smart Economist Test:** They would say: "It’s a paper showing that carbon taxes aren't enough to get people to buy heat pumps; they just buy gas boilers instead because they're cheaper."
*   **Making it Bigger:** To truly move the needle for the AER, the author needs to formalize the "trap" logic. Is the gas switch *welfare-reducing* in the long run because of the 25-year lock-in? A simple back-of-the-envelope model of the "option value of waiting" vs. the "cost of switching to gas" would elevate this from a Swiss case study to a general theory of path-dependent decarbonization.

### 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
The paper sits at the intersection of **Environmental Economics** (Carbon Pricing) and **Directed Technical Change** (Acemoglu/Aghion).

*   **Neighbors:** Andersson (2019) on Swedish CO2 taxes; Acemoglu et al. (2012) on green innovation; Aghion et al. (2016) on path dependency in autos; Jacobsen et al. (2023) on second-best environmental regulation.
*   **Strategy:** The paper should **synthesize** the environmental and growth literatures. It currently "contributes to three literatures" in a list format. It should instead argue that the Building Sector is the *best* place to test the theories of path dependency found in the auto/innovation literature because of the extreme longevity of the capital stock.
*   **Missing Conversations:** The paper needs to speak to the **Industrial Organization** of the heating market. Who provides the gas? If the cantons with gas infrastructure are the ones switching, this is as much a story about "complementary infrastructure" as it is about "price signals."

### 4. NARRATIVE ARC
*   **Setup:** Switzerland has a world-leading carbon tax and a desire to go net-zero.
*   **Tension:** Despite the high tax, heat pump adoption is lagging. Where is the "missing" carbon reduction going?
*   **Resolution:** It’s going to gas. The tax is high enough to kill oil but not high enough to make heat pumps competitive against gas.
*   **Implications:** Carbon taxes need "friends" (subsidies, bans) to work in sectors with high capital costs.

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong but the "Implications" section is currently the weakest. It feels like a policy brief. To be an AER paper, the implication needs to be about the *failure of the price mechanism in the presence of intermediate substitutes and lumpy investment.*

### 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
*   **Lead Fact:** "Switzerland raised its carbon tax by 10x, and all it got was a bunch of new gas boilers."
*   **Reaction:** Lean in. Every country is currently debating "gas as a bridge fuel." This paper says the bridge is actually a trap.
*   **Follow-up:** "Does this happen because gas is actually cleaner, or because the gas companies are better at marketing than heat pump installers?"

### 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
*   **Front-loading:** Move the "Mechanism" (the cost comparison of CHF 10k for gas vs 30k for heat pumps) earlier. It explains the results before they even appear.
*   **Robustness:** The "Placebo Treatment" (initial gas share) in Table 3 is actually very interesting. It shouldn't be buried. It shows that infrastructure *pathway* is the mediator.
*   **Data Limitation:** The 21-year gap between 2000 and 2021 is a major hurdle for a top-tier journal. The author must find *any* intermediate data (even if non-standard) to show the trend wasn't already happening in 2005-2010.

### 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
**The Gap:** Currently, it is a very competent "Applied Micro" paper about a specific policy in a small country. The distance to the AER is the "Generalizability Gap."

**Single Most Impactful Advice:**
Develop a simple theoretical framework of **"Path-Dependent Substitution"** where an agent chooses between three technologies (Dirty, Intermediate, Clean) with different entry costs and carbon intensities; show that a monotonic increase in carbon price can *optimally* lead to the "wrong" long-term choice (Gas) due to capital lock-in, then use the Swiss data as the empirical validation of this specific theoretical trap.
