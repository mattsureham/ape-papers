### 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
This paper examines how a 2018 USDA regulation tripling the required "depth-of-stock" for SNAP retailers unintentionally reduced program participation by pricing out small-format stores (convenience stores). Using county-level variation in retail composition, it finds that high-exposure counties saw a modest, temporary decline in SNAP enrollment, particularly in high-poverty areas, suggesting that supply-side compliance costs can inadvertently create barriers to safety-net access.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this well in the abstract, but the first two paragraphs of the introduction are slightly too focused on the "how" of the regulation rather than the "why it matters" of the economic trade-off. 
*   **The pitch the paper should have:** "When governments mandate quality improvements in the provision of public goods through private retailers, they face a fundamental trade-off: better offerings versus reduced access. This paper documents a 'compliance trap' in the SNAP program, where a tripling of stocking requirements led to the exit of 15,000 small retailers and a subsequent decline in household participation. I show that while the retail market eventually adapts, the short-run costs of regulatory 'upgrading' are borne most heavily by high-poverty communities."

### 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
The paper identifies a supply-side friction (regulatory compliance costs) that affects the take-up of a major social safety net program (SNAP).

*   **Differentiation:** It differentiates itself from the "food desert" literature (e.g., Allcott et al. 2019) by focusing on regulatory *exit* rather than market-driven *entry*. It differs from the SNAP administrative burden literature by focusing on the *retailer* as the point of friction rather than the *household*.
*   **Framing:** It is currently framed as answering a question about the **WORLD** (unintended consequences of USDA rules), which is a strength. 
*   **Clarity:** A smart economist would understand the "Compliance Trap" immediately. However, they might dismiss it as "another DiD on a niche regulation" unless the magnitude of the 15,000-store exit is centered.
*   **Making it bigger:** To make this an AER-level contribution, the author needs to move beyond "did participation drop?" to "who was marginalized?" Specifically, linking this to store-level scanner data or household-level mobility data to prove that the *distance* to the nearest store actually increased for these households would turn a "suggestive" result into a definitive one.

### 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
*   **Closest Neighbors:** Allcott et al. (2019) on food deserts; Hoynes and Schanzenbach (2016) on SNAP's history/impact; Djankov et al. (2002) on the regulation of entry.
*   **Strategy:** The paper should **synthesize** the "Administrative Burden" literature (usually focused on forms and interviews) with the "Industrial Organization of Retail" literature. 
*   **Narrow vs. Broad:** It is currently a bit narrow (Agricultural/Policy). It should speak more broadly to **Public Economics** regarding the efficiency of indirect benefit transfers (using private vendors to deliver public aid).
*   **Right Conversation?** Yes, but it should explicitly engage with the "optimal regulation" literature. Is the nutritional gain of having 36 items instead of 12 worth a 0.1pp drop in participation? That welfare calculation is the "AER-level" conversation.

### 4. NARRATIVE ARC
*   **Setup:** SNAP relies on a vast network of small stores to reach the "last mile" of poor households.
*   **Tension:** Regulators want to improve diet quality by raising standards, but small stores are capital/space constrained.
*   **Resolution:** The 2018 rule caused a mass exit of small stores, which measurably (though temporarily) lowered SNAP participation in the most vulnerable counties.
*   **Implications:** Supply-side resilience is real, but "shocking" the retail network has immediate human costs.

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong, particularly the "fade-out" result. It turns a potential weakness (a disappearing effect) into a finding about market resilience.

### 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
*   **Lead Fact:** "The USDA tried to make corner stores sell more broccoli, and it accidentally kicked 15,000 stores out of the SNAP program overnight."
*   **Reaction:** People lean in. It's a classic "unintended consequences" story.
*   **Follow-up:** "Did the people who stayed on SNAP actually eat better?" (This is the missing link).

### 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
*   **Front-load:** Table 5 (the aggregate decline) should be Figure 1 in the introduction. It establishes the "smoking gun" of store exits before the econometrics begin.
*   **Shorten:** The detailed breakdown of the 2016 Final Rule (Section 2) can be condensed.
*   **Appendix:** The "Standardized Effect Sizes" (Table 6) are helpful for the AER's emphasis on magnitude, but could be integrated into the main results text.

### 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The gap is **Ambition/Scope**. Currently, it's a very clean, competent evaluation of a policy change. To be an AER paper, it needs to tackle the **Welfare Trade-off**. 
*   **The single most impactful change:** Incorporate (or at least proxy) the *benefit* side. If participation dropped by 0.1pp, but the remaining 99.9% of households significantly improved their dietary quality because their local corner store now stocks more varieties, the regulation might be a success. Without the "benefit" side of the ledger, it's half a story.
