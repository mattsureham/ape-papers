## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple, policy-relevant question: when England made it much easier to convert offices into housing, did that actually expand housing supply enough to improve affordability? Using cross-area variation in preexisting office stock, it argues that the reform generated many office-to-residential conversions but did not measurably increase total housing supply, while prices in more exposed places rose faster.

A busy economist should care because this is exactly the kind of test people want from the “just deregulate housing supply” argument: a large, salient, real-world planning liberalization in a high-cost country, with a concrete read on whether deregulation translated into affordability.

**Does the paper itself articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Not quite. The current opening is decent, but it oversells “first causal estimate” and moves too quickly into design language. The strongest pitch is not “I use a Bartik design”; it is “here is a major national deregulation that created 130,000 units, yet affordability did not improve.” The first two paragraphs should lead with the policy experiment and the substantive puzzle, not the econometric machinery.

**What the first two paragraphs should say instead:**

> Housing policy debates often presume that relaxing land-use regulation will increase supply and thereby lower prices. England’s 2013 office-to-residential permitted development reform offers an unusually sharp test of that proposition: it removed planning permission requirements for one class of housing production and ultimately generated more than 130,000 dwellings. The central question is whether a deregulation large enough to produce substantial new units also made housing markets more affordable.
>
> This paper studies how the reform changed local housing quantity and prices across English local authorities. Areas with more preexisting office stock experienced far more conversion opportunities and, in fact, saw many more office-to-residential projects. But the broader market response is strikingly limited: I find little evidence of an increase in total housing supply, while prices rose faster in more exposed places. The paper’s contribution is to show that planning deregulation can generate visible housing production without delivering affordability gains when it operates in high-demand markets and mainly reshuffles the composition of supply.

That is the pitch the paper should have.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution in one sentence:**  
The paper claims that England’s large office-to-residential planning deregulation generated substantial conversion activity but did not measurably expand aggregate local housing supply or reduce prices, implying that deregulation alone may be insufficient to improve affordability in high-demand markets.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest papers?
Only partially. The paper distinguishes itself from descriptive UK work on permitted development and from broad housing-supply/regulation papers, but it does not yet carve out a sufficiently sharp intellectual niche. Right now the contribution reads as:

- “first causal estimate of this reform,” and
- “another paper on planning deregulation and prices.”

That is not enough for AER positioning unless the reform is framed as *the* cleanest real-world stress test of a central housing claim, or unless the findings dramatically revise prior beliefs.

The closest neighboring literatures are:
1. UK planning constraints and housing supply elasticity.
2. Place-based studies of zoning/planning reform.
3. Studies of new housing supply and market outcomes.
4. Descriptive work on office-to-residential conversion quality.

The paper needs to say much more clearly: **what should we believe about deregulation after seeing this case that we did not know before?**

### Is the contribution framed as a question about the world, or a gap in the literature?
Mostly as a literature gap: “the impact remains unquantified in the academic literature,” “first causal estimate,” “contributes to several literatures.” That is weaker.

The stronger framing is a question about the world:  
**Can a large, targeted deregulation of housing production deliver affordability when it expands a politically easy margin of supply rather than the full supply frontier?**

That is a much bigger and more interesting question than “there is no paper on this reform yet.”

### Could a smart economist explain what is new after reading the introduction?
At present, probably not crisply enough. They would likely say:  
“It's a DiD/Bartik paper on England’s office-to-residential reform; it finds conversions happened, but total supply effects are weak and prices still rose.”

That is not bad, but it still sounds like “another policy-evaluation paper,” not “a paper that changes how we think about housing deregulation.”

### What would make this contribution bigger?
Several options:

1. **Reframe around the margin of supply, not the reform itself.**  
   The big idea is that not all “supply deregulation” is equally consequential. Office conversions expand housing on a narrow, high-demand, composition-shifting margin. That could explain why “more units” does not equal “lower prices.” This is the most promising conceptual upgrade.

2. **Push harder on composition and incidence.**  
   If the core story is that the reform increased small, low-quality units without changing broader scarcity, then the paper should foreground composition more centrally. Right now the flat/terraced price-gap result feels tacked on; it should be part of the main contribution. A bigger paper would ask: what kind of housing got created, for whom, and with what equilibrium relevance?

3. **Make the paper about affordability transmission rather than unit counts.**  
   A stronger framing would distinguish between:
   - gross unit creation,
   - net supply expansion,
   - affordability effects.
   
   That ladder is conceptually rich and widely relevant.

4. **Connect to the political economy of “easy” deregulation.**  
   Office conversions are attractive precisely because they bypass the hardest political fights over new construction. The paper could become a statement about why politically feasible supply reforms may produce visible output but modest affordability gains.

If the author could enlarge the contribution, it should be along one of those dimensions rather than by merely adding more tables.

### How should the paper position itself relative to those neighbors?
**Build on them, not attack them.**  
The paper should not present itself as disproving the broad claim that supply matters. That would invite obvious resistance and overstate what the design can support. Instead, it should say:

- Existing work shows restrictive planning systems raise prices and that new supply can reduce rents/prices.
- This paper studies a specific kind of deregulation: one that expands supply on a narrow conversion margin.
- The finding is not “supply doesn’t matter”; it is “not every deregulation margin is quantitatively important for affordability.”

That is a much more credible and interesting position.

### Is the paper currently positioned too narrowly or too broadly?
Oddly, both.

- **Too narrowly** in the empirical branding: “first causal estimate using a Bartik design with administrative data covering all English LAs.”
- **Too broadly** in the claims: “challenge the prediction that relaxing planning controls should increase supply and moderate prices.”

The current framing oscillates between “this is a case study of one reform” and “this speaks to whether deregulation works at all.” The paper needs a middle level: **this is evidence about the limits of a specific, politically salient deregulation margin.**

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?
At a strategic level, it should speak more directly to:

1. **The literature on the effects of new housing supply on nearby or citywide affordability.**  
   Right now Mast and Asquith appear only in the discussion. They should be in the introduction as the natural comparison class.

2. **Urban redevelopment / adaptive reuse / commercial-to-residential conversion literature.**  
   Even if thinner in economics, related urban studies/planning work matters because this reform is about adaptive reuse, not generic zoning.

3. **Political economy of housing reform.**  
   The paper is implicitly about why governments pursue “permissionless” conversion reforms. That conversation could widen the audience.

4. **Measurement of housing quality and composition.**  
   If the paper’s interpretation leans on low-quality/small units, it should acknowledge work on quality-adjusted supply and housing services, even outside the exact UK niche.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?
Not yet. It is having the conversation “does this reform have effects?” The more impactful conversation is:

**What kinds of housing supply reforms meaningfully change equilibrium affordability, and why do some highly visible deregulatory reforms fail to do so?**

That is the conversation top housing economists care about.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
England has a severe housing affordability problem, and many economists argue that restrictive planning is a central cause. The 2013 permitted development reform removed planning barriers for office-to-residential conversion, creating a large, nationally visible deregulatory experiment.

### Tension
A reform can generate lots of new units and still fail to change affordability if it operates on the wrong margin: too narrow, too concentrated in already-hot places, too compositional, too small relative to demand, or too detached from the real bottleneck. The puzzle is that 130,000 units sounds large enough to matter, but it is unclear whether it affected total supply or prices.

### Resolution
The reform clearly increased conversion activity where office stock was high, but there is little evidence that it raised total housing supply at the local-authority level, and more exposed places saw faster price growth.

### Implications
Headline deregulation and visible unit creation are not sufficient evidence of affordability gains. Policymakers should distinguish between reforms that create housing on paper and reforms that materially relax scarcity in equilibrium.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
It has the ingredients, but the arc is not yet fully disciplined. Right now it reads somewhat like:

- institutional background,
- design,
- one null-ish supply result,
- one positive price result,
- then post hoc interpretation.

That feels a bit like a collection of results looking for a story.

### What story should it be telling?
The paper should tell this story:

> England ran a large natural experiment in “planning without permission.” It succeeded on the visible metric—conversions—but failed on the economically deeper one—affordability. The reason is that easing one narrow conversion margin is not the same thing as expanding effective housing supply in a market where demand pressure is intense and the added stock is compositionally unusual.

That story is much stronger than “I estimate the effect of a reform and here are the coefficients.”

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?
“England let offices be turned into housing without planning permission, created more than 130,000 dwellings that way, and the places most exposed didn’t see lower prices—in fact, prices rose faster.”

That is a decent opening fact. People will not reach for their phones immediately.

### Would people lean in?
Yes, initially. The policy is concrete, the setting matters, and the finding cuts against a simplistic reading of “deregulate and prices fall.”

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Almost certainly:  
**“So does this mean supply deregulation doesn’t work, or just that office conversions are too narrow a margin and concentrated in places where demand dominates?”**

That follow-up question is the entire ballgame. The paper must be written to answer it. At present, it leaves too much ambiguity and sometimes flirts with the broader, less defensible claim.

### If the findings are null or modest, is the null itself interesting?
Yes, potentially very interesting. A null effect on total supply is valuable here because the policy generated enough visible output that one might expect an aggregate quantity response. Learning that 130,000 conversion units did not obviously translate into broader supply expansion is important.

But the paper needs to make the null result feel like a substantive lesson, not an imprecisely estimated disappointment. The way to do that is to emphasize the distinction between:
- **created units**
- **net additional supply**
- **market affordability**

That is a meaningful conceptual contribution. Without that framing, the paper risks feeling like a failed attempt to find a supply effect.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

At present, the gap is mainly a **framing and ambition problem**, with some **scope** issues.

### Framing problem
The paper undersells its most interesting idea and oversells its generic empirical novelty. “First causal estimate” is not an AER argument. “A major deregulation can create many units yet fail to move affordability because it expands the wrong margin of supply” is much closer.

### Scope problem
The paper’s current outcome set and interpretation do not yet fully support the bigger conceptual claim. It has:
- conversions,
- total additions,
- prices by type.

That is enough for a strong field-journal paper if very well framed. For AER-level excitement, it probably needs a richer articulation of mechanism—especially composition, location, or the distinction between gross and net supply. The paper has hints of that but not a fully developed mechanism.

### Novelty problem
Moderate. The broad question—do planning reforms affect supply and prices?—is obviously well-trodden. The setting is distinctive, but the paper must turn that distinctiveness into a general lesson. Otherwise it remains an informative UK case study.

### Ambition problem
Yes. The paper is competent but safe. It reports coefficients, notes prices rose, and concludes deregulation was insufficient. The more ambitious version would use this episode to discipline a much bigger debate: **which forms of deregulation matter quantitatively for affordability, and why?**

### Single most impactful piece of advice
**Rewrite the paper around one big claim: office-to-residential deregulation increased visible housing production on a narrow margin but did not relax effective housing scarcity, so “more units” and “more affordability” are not the same policy accomplishment.**

That is the one change that would most improve its chances.
