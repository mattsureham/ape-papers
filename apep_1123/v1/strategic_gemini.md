### 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
This paper examines how mandatory disclosure laws affect the production and dissemination of scientific evidence. By exploiting a phase-based exemption in the FDA Amendments Act of 2007 (FDAAA 801), the author estimates that legal mandates increased results reporting by 10 percentage points overall—but 22 percentage points for industry sponsors who face real regulatory consequences—while finding no effect for academic researchers. This suggests that "sunlight" alone is an insufficient disinfectant for the file-drawer problem; transparency requires enforcement "teeth."

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this pitch reasonably well, but the first two paragraphs focus heavily on the medical literature (GSK lawsuit, Turner et al. 2008). 
**The pitch it should have:** "The market for scientific knowledge suffers from a fundamental information asymmetry: researchers choose which results to publish, and which to bury in the 'file drawer.' While the economics of science has long called for pre-registration as a solution, we have little evidence on whether mandates actually work. This paper exploits a natural experiment in clinical trial regulation to show that mandates only change behavior when backed by credible institutional enforcement, offering a sobering lesson for the movement toward open science in economics and beyond."

### 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**Contribution:** The paper provides the first causal evidence that transparency mandates in science are effective only when coupled with high-stakes regulatory oversight (industry vs. academia).

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It is well-differentiated from the *medical* literature (which is purely descriptive). In the *economics* literature, it moves beyond the theory of pre-analysis plans (Olken 2015) to empirical evaluation.
*   **World vs. Literature:** It currently straddles the two. It would be stronger if framed as a study of **incentives in the production of knowledge** rather than a study of "compliance with FDAAA 801."
*   **Clarity:** A smart economist would understand the "Industry vs. Academic" gradient immediately. That is the "hook."
*   **Bigger Contribution:** To make this an AER paper, the author needs to dig into the *quality* of what is being reported. If people are forced to report, are they just "p-hacking" the mandated report? The negative result on "number of primary outcomes" (Table 3) is a great start—it suggests a strategic narrowing of focus.

### 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
*   **Closest Neighbors:** Dranove et al. (2003) on healthcare report cards; Jin and Sorensen (2005) on disclosure; and the "Credibility Revolution" meta-science (Brodeur et al. 2020).
*   **Positioning:** The paper should **attack** the notion that voluntary registration (like the AEA registry) is a panacea. It should position itself as the "enforcement" chapter of the open-science conversation.
*   **Missing Literature:** It needs more on the **Economics of Science** (Stephan, Dasgupta & David). Is this a "rent-seeking" story for industry? Or a "cost of compliance" story for academics?
*   **Right Conversation?** Yes, but it should lean harder into the "Regulatory Capture" or "Incentive Design" angle to broaden its appeal beyond health economists.

### 4. NARRATIVE ARC
*   **Setup:** Scientific results are selectively reported, biasing the evidence base.
*   **Tension:** Laws were passed to fix this, but they have no real fines. Do "soft" laws work?
*   **Resolution:** They work for industry (who fear the FDA for other reasons) but fail for academics (who have no "boss" to answer to).
*   **Implications:** Pre-registration in economics is likely failing because we lack an "FDA" to punish non-reporters.

**Evaluation:** The arc is clear but the "Pre-trend" section (5.4) is a narrative speed bump. The author is too honest about the failing parallel trends. They need to pivot: the pre-trend *is* the story—it shows that the "natural" market for results was already diverging, making the mandate even more necessary.

### 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
*   **The Fact:** "Mandating results reporting had zero effect on academic trials but a 22% effect on Big Pharma."
*   **Reaction:** Lean in. Every economist has a strong opinion on whether our own pre-analysis plans are a waste of time.
*   **Follow-up:** "Does this mean the AEA registry is useless?" That is the question that makes this an AER paper.

### 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
*   **Front-loading:** The "Industry-Academic" split (Table 4) is the most interesting result. It should be moved earlier or highlighted more in the intro.
*   **Appendix:** The detailed legal history of FDAAA 801 could be streamlined.
*   **Outcome Pre-specification:** This is a "hidden gem" in the paper. The finding that mandates lead to *fewer* pre-specified outcomes is fascinating—it suggests researchers are "hedging" against future disclosure. This deserves a deeper dive.

### 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The gap is **ambition**. Currently, it feels like a very high-quality health policy paper. To be an AER paper, it must be an **Economics of Science** paper.

**Single Biggest Improvement:** Explicitly link the findings to the "Replication Crisis" in social sciences and provide a formal or semi-formal model of a researcher’s decision to report a result as a function of (a) result significance and (b) the cost of regulatory penalty.
