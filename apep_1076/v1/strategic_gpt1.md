## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether laws banning conversion therapy for minors improve adolescent mental health, and especially whether they improve outcomes for LGB youth. Using staggered state adoption of bans and YRBSS data, it argues that these laws reduce sadness and suicidality, with the largest effects concentrated among LGB adolescents, suggesting that the policy matters not only by blocking a harmful practice but by signaling social validation and reducing stigma.

A busy economist should care because the paper is really about a broader question: can targeted civil-rights or professional-regulation policies generate measurable population health gains through norm change, even when the directly regulated practice is relatively rare?

### Does the paper articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Not quite. The current introduction gets to the topic quickly, but it moves too fast into “single prior study / modern DiD / sexual identity data” mode. That is a literature-and-method pitch, not a world-and-economics pitch. The paper’s best idea is the “destigmatization dividend,” but that concept arrives too late, after the reader has already been trained to think of this as a narrow policy-evaluation exercise.

### What the first two paragraphs should say instead

The paper should open with the larger economic question: when governments regulate stigmatizing practices aimed at minority groups, do such laws matter only for the people directly protected, or do they also change beliefs, norms, and mental health more broadly? Conversion therapy bans are a sharp setting because the directly regulated practice is uncommon, yet the symbolism is powerful: the state officially declares that sexual minority identity is not a pathology to be “treated.”

Then the paper should say that it studies the staggered adoption of these bans across U.S. states and finds two things: measurable declines in adolescent mental distress overall, and especially large declines in suicidality among LGB youth. That framing makes the paper about policy as social signal, not merely about one more state-law DiD.

A stronger opening pitch would be:

> Governments often pass minority-protective laws whose direct reach is narrow but whose symbolic content is large. This paper asks whether conversion therapy bans for minors improve adolescent mental health not only by preventing a harmful clinical practice, but by changing the social meaning of sexual minority identity. Using the staggered adoption of bans across U.S. states and YRBSS data, I find that bans reduce sadness and suicide planning among adolescents overall, with much larger reductions in suicidality for LGB youth. The results suggest a broader “destigmatization dividend”: laws can improve health partly by signaling legitimacy and reducing ambient stigma.

That is an AER-style pitch. The current version is more like a strong field-journal pitch.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

LGB youth face substantially worse mental health outcomes than heterosexual peers. States have passed bans on conversion therapy for minors, but the practice itself is relatively uncommon and the population-level consequences of banning it are unclear.

### Tension

If the directly treated activity is rare, why should a ban matter at the population level? Either the law is mostly symbolic and therefore perhaps inconsequential, or symbolic law is actually consequential because it shifts stigma, identity validation, and social norms.

That is the real tension. The paper only partially exploits it.

### Resolution

The paper finds declines in sadness and suicide planning overall, and much larger declines in suicidality among LGB youth, which it interprets as evidence of a destigmatization channel.

### Implications

Minority-protective laws may generate welfare gains beyond their direct enforcement margin. Policies with small direct exposure can still have meaningful health effects if they alter social meaning and reduce stigma.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the pieces, but they are not fully assembled. Right now it reads somewhat like a collection of estimates with an appealing mechanism label attached. The narrative should be tighter:

1. **Puzzle:** conversion therapy is rare, so why would banning it matter?
2. **Hypothesis:** because the law changes what the state says about minority identity.
3. **Evidence:** average improvements plus larger benefits for the targeted group.
4. **Implication:** policy can affect health through expressive and normative channels.

That is the story. The current draft spends too much of its scarce introduction real estate on estimation architecture and too little on the puzzle.
