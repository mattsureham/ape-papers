## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether symbolic-but-concrete LGBTQ-protective legislation improves youth mental health. Using the staggered adoption of state conversion therapy bans, it argues that these laws reduce adolescent distress overall and substantially reduce suicidality among LGB youth, with the key claim being that the main benefit comes less from shutting down a rare practice than from changing social norms and reducing stigma.

A busy economist should care because the paper is trying to make a broader point: law can affect health not only through direct treatment or resources, but through recognition, signaling, and social legitimacy. That is potentially an AER-type idea if convincingly framed.

Does the paper itself articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs? Not quite. The current opening is competent, but it starts too much as “there is a policy, here is one prior paper, here is my design.” That is journal-article prose, not top-journal positioning. The first two paragraphs should make the world-level claim first: that governments may be able to improve mental health by publicly repudiating stigmatizing institutions, even when the directly regulated behavior is rare. Only then should it introduce conversion therapy bans as the test case.

### The pitch the paper should have

“Can laws improve health by changing what society publicly endorses? This paper studies conversion therapy bans for minors as a test of whether legal repudiation of a stigmatizing practice can improve adolescent mental health, especially among sexual minority youth. I find that when states ban licensed providers from offering conversion therapy, measures of adolescent distress fall, and the sharpest declines in suicidality occur among LGB youth—suggesting that the payoff from these laws may lie not only in direct protection, but in the social signal that sexual minority identity is legitimate rather than pathological.”

That is much stronger than “here is a staggered DiD with 22 states.”

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper’s contribution is to argue that conversion therapy bans improve adolescent mental health—especially suicidality among LGB youth—and to interpret this as evidence that protective legislation generates a “destigmatization dividend” beyond its direct regulatory effect.

### Is this clearly differentiated from the closest papers?

Partially, but not sharply enough. The paper differentiates itself from the one directly adjacent study on conversion therapy bans by saying it has more treated states and sexual-identity heterogeneity. That is useful, but too incremental as currently framed. “Six times more treatment variation” is not, by itself, an AER contribution. The paper needs stronger differentiation from the broader literature on LGBTQ policy and health: same-sex marriage, anti-discrimination protections, school climate, minority stress. Right now it risks sounding like “another policy-shock paper showing mental health effects for LGBTQ youth.”

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world or filling a literature gap?

It starts with a world question, which is good, but then quickly slips into literature-gap language: only one prior study, limited power, staggered timing, no sexual identity data. That is second-tier framing. The stronger version is: **Do laws that repudiate stigma change mental health, even when they regulate a small number of direct interactions?** That is a world question. The conversion-therapy setting is then the clean case.

### Could a smart economist explain what is new after reading the introduction?

Right now, maybe, but the explanation would be something like: “It’s a DiD paper on conversion therapy bans and teen mental health, with heterogeneity by sexual orientation.” That is not enough. You want the colleague to say: “It shows that legislation can improve mental health mainly through social recognition and norm-setting, not only through direct exposure reduction.”

### What would make the contribution bigger?

Three concrete ways:

1. **Make the mechanism claim more central and more comparative.**  
   The paper’s big idea is not “bans help”; it is “the main returns to bans come through destigmatization because direct exposure is too small to explain the magnitude.” That should be front and center and quantified more aggressively.

2. **Connect outcomes more directly to social climate or institutional behavior.**  
   If the paper could show changes in counselor behavior, school support climate, help-seeking, disclosure, or family acceptance proxies, the contribution would feel less like a reduced-form policy paper and more like a paper about how norms move health.

3. **Frame the comparison against other LGBTQ policy shocks.**  
   The bigger claim is not merely that these bans matter, but that they are a particularly revealing test because they regulate a rare practice while sending a strong symbolic signal. That distinction could make the paper more conceptually interesting than prior same-sex-marriage/anti-discrimination papers.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Sexual minority adolescents suffer dramatically worse mental health than their heterosexual peers. Governments increasingly pass LGBTQ-protective laws, but it is unclear whether such laws affect health through material exposure, symbolic recognition, or both.

### Tension

Conversion therapy bans regulate a practice that is widely condemned but probably rare in prevalence. So why might banning it matter at the population level? If effects are large, that would suggest a broader destigmatization channel; if effects are absent, it would imply that symbolic laws may matter less than advocates claim.

### Resolution

The paper finds population-level declines in distress and much larger reductions in suicidality among LGB youth after bans. It interprets this pattern as consistent with a norm-shifting, stigma-reducing mechanism.

### Implications

The implication is that legal recognition and public repudiation of stigmatizing practices can generate meaningful health benefits, potentially at low fiscal cost. More broadly, lawmakers may affect mental health by altering what institutions legitimize.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the ingredients, but the arc is not fully disciplined. Right now it sometimes reads like:
1. Here’s the policy.
2. Here’s the dataset.
3. Here are the estimates.
4. Here’s a mechanism label.

That is weaker than a true narrative. The paper should more deliberately tell the story:

- **Before:** sexual minority youth face high distress; conversion therapy is one extreme manifestation of a broader stigmatizing environment.
- **Puzzle:** banning a rare practice should not, mechanically, move population mental health very much.
- **Answer:** it does, especially for the targeted group.
- **Interpretation:** the law changes social meaning, not just clinical exposure.
- **Broader lesson:** the expressive content of law has health consequences.

That is the story the paper should be telling from page 1.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?

“I’d lead with: when states ban conversion therapy for minors, suicide attempts among LGB youth fall by roughly one-fifth.”

That is a strong dinner-party fact. It is legible and morally salient.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

They would lean in initially. The topic is salient and the effect size, as presented, is striking. But the next reaction from economists will be immediate skepticism about whether the paper is really about conversion therapy bans per se or about broader pro-LGBT state environments. Since I am not to referee identification, I’ll stay away from adjudicating that. Strategically, though, the paper has to preempt the “isn’t this just blue states being different?” reaction by framing itself as a paper about legal recognition in policy bundles rather than overclaiming clean isolation.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

“Is this actually about the ban, or is the ban just a marker for a broader shift in social acceptance and institutions?”

That question is not a weakness; it is actually the path to a bigger paper. If the answer is “exactly—that broader shift is the object of interest, and this law is a salient legal expression of it,” then the paper has a larger payoff. If the answer is “no, it’s literally just the clinical ban,” the story becomes less plausible and smaller.

### If findings are modest or null, is that okay?

Here the findings are not null, but some are uneven. That is fine. The key is that the paper should stop trying to make every coefficient heroic. The interesting fact is the targeted heterogeneity and the mismatch between a likely thin direct pipeline and nontrivial population effects. That is enough.

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

1. **Rewrite the introduction around the big idea, not the estimator.**  
   The first page currently spends too much time on “single prior study / staggered timing / modern DiD.” That belongs later and in lighter doses.

2. **Move most methodological throat-clearing out of the opening.**  
   AER readers do not need the first two paragraphs to be a methods seminar. They need the question, the puzzle, and the punchline.

3. **Shorten the robustness discussion in the main text.**  
   The paper currently spends too much narrative energy walking through event studies, alternative estimators, placebo outcomes, and leave-one-out exercises. Those are important for referees, but in terms of reading experience they crowd out the central idea. One concise paragraph in the main text, more detail in appendix.

4. **Bring the mechanism argument forward.**  
   The “direct pipeline is likely thin” point is one of the most interesting conceptual moves in the paper. It should appear earlier—arguably before the results—so the reader knows what would make the findings surprising.

5. **Trim the contribution paragraph.**  
   The current “contributes to three literatures” paragraph is conventional and forgettable. Replace it with one sharper paragraph about the conceptual contribution.

6. **Rethink the conclusion.**  
   The conclusion currently edges into advocacy (“for the 28 states without bans…”). For AER positioning, the conclusion should be less policy-brief, more intellectually expansive: what does this teach us about law, identity, and health?

7. **Be careful with labels like “placebo-like test” and “key mechanism test.”**  
   Those are oversold in editorial terms. Strategic positioning improves when the paper sounds intellectually controlled rather than eager to certify itself.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

This is mostly a **framing and ambition problem**, with a secondary **scope problem**.

The underlying result is interesting enough to merit serious attention. But in current form, the paper feels like a strong field-journal paper: a competent policy evaluation of a timely law using standard reduced-form tools, with nice heterogeneity. That is not yet an AER story.

### What is the gap?

- **Framing problem:** The paper undersells the big idea and oversells the design details.
- **Ambition problem:** It settles for “we study conversion therapy bans” when it should be arguing “we identify a broader channel through which law affects health.”
- **Scope problem:** The mechanism discussion is still more asserted than developed. To excite the top people in the field, the paper needs either richer mechanism evidence or a more persuasive conceptual framing that makes the reduced form itself illuminating.

### Is it a novelty problem?

Somewhat. Policy-and-mental-health papers in LGBTQ settings already exist. So the paper’s novelty cannot just be “another protective law improves outcomes.” Its novelty has to be the **expressive-law / destigmatization** angle and the fact that this setting is especially informative because direct exposure is rare.

### Single most impactful advice

**Reframe the paper as evidence on the expressive and destigmatizing effects of law—using conversion therapy bans as the sharpest test case—rather than as a larger-sample update to a niche policy-evaluation literature.**

If they only change one thing, it should be the introduction and conceptual positioning around that claim.
