## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether state-level bans on "conversion therapy" for minors actually improve adolescent mental health. Exploiting the staggered rollout of these bans across 22 states and using data on over 700,000 students, the author finds that these laws significantly reduce persistent sadness and suicide planning—not just for the targeted LGB youth, but at a population level—suggesting the laws work more as a "normative signal" that reduces social stigma than as a simple clinical restriction.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this pitch very well in the first two paragraphs. It immediately hooks the reader with the high-stakes reality of LGB suicide rates and then pivots to the policy instrument. 

**The pitch the paper should have (refined):**
"LGB youth attempt suicide at three times the rate of their heterosexual peers, yet we know little about which policies actually close this gap. This paper provides the first comprehensive evaluation of conversion therapy bans, finding they reduce LGB suicide attempts by nearly 20%. Crucially, the effects extend beyond the directly protected group to the general adolescent population, suggesting that legislative 'signaling' can generate a broad destigmatization dividend for public health."

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**State of the contribution:** This paper provides the first large-scale, multi-state causal evaluation of conversion therapy bans, identifying a "destigmatization" mechanism that improves mental health outcomes for both the targeted minority and the population at large.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It clearly differentiates itself from Overhage (2025) by increasing the "niche" sample size sixfold and adding the critical heterogeneity analysis (LGB vs. heterosexual).
*   **World vs. Literature:** It is framed as answering a question about the **WORLD** (how to stop kids from killing themselves) rather than just filling a gap in a literature. This is a major AER-level strength.
*   **Clarity:** A smart economist would immediately understand the "what's new": it's the first high-powered evidence that these specific bans have a "social signaling" effect beyond the doctor’s office.
*   **Making it bigger:** To make the contribution "top-tier," the paper needs to prove the *signaling* mechanism more aggressively. Right now, "destigmatization" is a plausible label. To be a "Big" AER paper, it needs to rule out other concurrent shifts (e.g., changes in school climate or local media sentiment) more convincingly.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

*   **Closest Neighbors:** Raifman et al. (2017) on marriage equality; Carpenter et al. (2021) on LGBTQ+ outcomes; Gruber and Hungerman (2008) on adolescent mental health policy.
*   **Positioning:** The paper builds on Raifman/Carpenter but occupies a more specific policy niche (conversion therapy) that is currently a hot-button legislative issue. 
*   **Conversation:** It is currently having a "Public Health/Applied Micro" conversation. To reach the AER level, it should lean harder into the **Political Economy/Information** conversation: how do laws change social norms? (e.g., Benabou and Tirole, or Acemoglu and Jackson). 
*   **Niche vs. Broad:** It is positioned well, though it risks being seen as "too specific to one policy." Connecting it to the broader theory of "Expressive Law" would widen the audience.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

*   **Setup:** Adolescent mental health is in crisis, with a massive disparity for LGB youth.
*   **Tension:** States are passing bans on conversion therapy, but many skeptics argue these are symbolic "virtue signaling" with no teeth since the practice is already rare.
*   **Resolution:** The "symbolism" is actually the point—the laws work *because* they signal social acceptance, leading to a measurable "destigmatization dividend."
*   **Implications:** Policy can improve mental health at "zero fiscal cost" by shifting social norms.

**Evaluation:** The narrative arc is very strong. It takes a potential weakness (the rarity of the practice) and turns it into the paper's central theoretical contribution (the signaling effect).

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

*   **Lead Fact:** "Passing a ban on conversion therapy reduces LGB suicide attempts by 20% and makes *all* kids in the state less sad."
*   **Reaction:** People will lean in. It is a "clean" result on a "messy" and emotional topic.
*   **Follow-up:** "Is it just the ban, or is the ban a proxy for the state becoming more liberal?" (This is the "Identification" hurdle the author needs to clear).

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

*   **The "CS-Gap":** The discussion on why the Callaway-Sant'Anna estimator yields insignificant results (page 9) is a red flag for an AER editor. It feels defensive. The author needs to move the best-performing "modern DiD" results to the front or explain the power issue more cleanly.
*   **Mechanisms:** Section 6 (Discussion) needs to be a full Section 6 (Mechanisms). Use data on "attitudes toward LGBTQ people" (e.g., from the GSS or CCES) to show that the bans actually move the needle on stigma.
*   **Front-loading:** The paper is well-structured. The summary statistics in Table 1, Panel B are a "killer" descriptive result that should be highlighted even more.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The paper is currently a "very high-quality applied micro paper." To make it an **AER paper**, it needs to move from "Policy X worked" to "Here is a generalizable truth about how Law and Norms interact."

**Single Most Impactful Advice:**
Incorporate external data (like Google Trends or social survey data) to show a "break" in social stigma/discourse in a state *immediately following* the ban's passage; this would transform the "signaling" argument from a compelling story into an empirical fact.
