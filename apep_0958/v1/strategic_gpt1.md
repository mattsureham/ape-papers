## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether a major environmental court ruling in the Netherlands generated support for a new agrarian-populist party by creating localized economic damage, or whether it mainly activated pre-existing rural/agricultural identity. The headline claim is that the geography of BBB support aligns much more with where agriculture was already central to local life than with where the nitrogen ruling appears to have caused differential disruption in construction activity.

Busy economists should care because this is, in principle, a sharp political-economy question about the backlash to green regulation: when environmental policy triggers populist revolt, is the mechanism material loss or identity threat? That question travels well beyond the Netherlands.

**Does the paper articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Mostly yes, but not in the strongest possible way. The current intro is vivid and readable, but it leans too quickly into the Dutch episode and not enough into the general economic question. It also overstates what the evidence can support by posing the question as “did the ruling cause BBB’s rise?” and then answering with a non-causal decomposition for voting and a narrow proxy for economic disruption.

**What the first two paragraphs should say instead:**

> Environmental regulation is increasingly colliding with electoral politics. When green policies provoke backlash, the central question is whether voters are responding to material losses created by regulation or to a perceived threat to the identity and status of affected communities. That distinction matters for policy design: if backlash comes from concentrated economic harm, compensation may work; if it comes from identity-based grievance, compensation alone may fail.
>
> This paper studies that distinction in the Dutch nitrogen crisis. After a 2019 court ruling invalidated the national nitrogen permit regime, a newly formed agrarian-populist party, BBB, went from nonexistence to becoming the largest party in every Dutch province by 2023. I show that BBB’s support is much more strongly associated with municipalities’ pre-existing agricultural base than with spatial exposure to the ruling, and I find little evidence that more exposed municipalities saw larger declines in building permits. The episode suggests that environmental-policy backlash may be driven less by newly created local losers than by the political mobilization of communities whose economic identity feels under attack.

That is the pitch the paper should have. Cleaner, more general, and more defensible.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution in one sentence:**  
The paper argues that the Dutch nitrogen ruling did not primarily produce agrarian-populist support through localized economic disruption, but instead mobilized pre-existing agricultural identity.

That is a plausible contribution, but at present it is **not yet sharply differentiated** from adjacent work, partly because the paper straddles three claims without fully choosing one:

1. a paper on environmental regulation and political backlash,
2. a paper on identity versus material loss,
3. a paper on the rise of BBB specifically.

Right now, the contribution is somewhat fuzzy because the empirical pieces do not all operate at the same level. The permits analysis is quasi-causal and narrow; the voting analysis is descriptive/decompositional and broad. The paper wants to conclude something structural about mechanisms of backlash, but the evidence currently supports something more modest: **the geography of BBB support looks more like the geography of agricultural identity than the geography of measured local permit disruption.**

### Is the contribution clearly differentiated from the closest papers?
Not enough. The paper cites big classics or near-classics—Autor et al. on China shock, Fetzer on austerity/Brexit—but those are not really the closest neighbors in design or object. Those papers are about large economic shocks and political response. This paper is about a policy/legal shock, spatial exposure, and the rise of a sector-specific populist party. It needs a tighter set of comparators and a more explicit “here is how I differ.”

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a gap in a literature?
Mostly the former, which is good. The best version is: **What causes political backlash against environmental regulation?** That is a world question. The weaker version is: “there is little evidence on judicial environmental rulings and political consequences.” The paper should lean harder into the first and treat the second as supporting context.

### Could a smart economist explain what is new after reading the introduction?
At present they would probably say: **“It’s a paper about the Dutch nitrogen ruling and BBB; the main point is that rural/agricultural identity predicts BBB better than local exposure.”** That is not bad, but it is still a bit close to “another geography-of-populism paper with a DiD and a cross-section.”

### What would make the contribution bigger?
Most importantly: **broaden and sharpen the measure of material disruption.** If the paper wants to claim “identity rather than economic loss,” building permits are too narrow and construction-centric relative to the story, which is at least as much about farming as about construction. To make the contribution bigger, I would want one of the following:

- **Agricultural outcomes**: farm exits, land prices, investment, buyouts, livestock reductions, farm credit, machinery purchases, ammonia-intensive production, etc.
- **Political comparison over time**: show that BBB support is not just rural conservatism relabeled, but a specific shift after the nitrogen crisis relative to prior party support.
- **Mechanism evidence**: text, protests, media consumption, survey data, manifesto content, or candidate geography that more directly links the ruling to identity activation.
- **Comparison to other populist parties over time**, not just one cross-sectional placebo.

The paper’s ambition should be: **not merely that agriculture predicts BBB, but that green backlash is electorally strongest where threatened livelihoods are socially constitutive, even absent large localized short-run economic losses.**

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Environmental regulation is becoming stricter, and economists increasingly worry that green policy may trigger political backlash that undermines implementation.

### Tension
The standard intuition is that backlash comes from concentrated economic pain. But in many salient green conflicts, what appears to be “economic interest” may actually be bound up with identity, status, and place. The Dutch nitrogen crisis offers a clean episode because a visible legal shock was followed by a dramatic electoral realignment.

### Resolution
The paper finds little evidence that more exposed municipalities experienced greater declines in building permits, while BBB support is strongly associated with pre-ruling agricultural employment and related rural characteristics.

### Implications
If backlash is not tightly linked to localized measured economic disruption, then compensation for direct losses may not be enough; environmental policy design may need to account for identity, legitimacy, and symbolic recognition.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
**Serviceable, but not fully disciplined.** There is a real story here, but the paper still feels somewhat like **a collection of suggestive findings being asked to carry a larger claim than they can comfortably support.** The title and abstract are punchy; the results are coherent; but the inferential leap from “permits don’t move differentially” and “agriculture predicts BBB” to “identity, not material loss” is still too abrupt.

### What story should it be telling?
The story should be:

1. Green transitions create political risk.
2. That risk is often assumed to come from direct economic losses.
3. This episode lets us compare the geography of measured disruption with the geography of political response.
4. Those geographies do not line up cleanly.
5. The places that rallied to BBB were places with strong agricultural identity, suggesting that green backlash can be politically mediated through threatened community identity.

That is a stronger and more careful story than “green courts created brown politics.”

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?
“Within four years of a court ruling on nitrogen permits, a brand-new farmer party became the largest party in all twelve Dutch provinces—and the places that voted for it were not especially the places where measured permit disruption was largest, but the places where agriculture already defined local economic life.”

That is a pretty good dinner-party fact. People would likely lean in.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
They would lean in at the start. The Dutch episode is vivid and surprising. But the second question would come quickly: **“How do you know it’s identity rather than just badly measured economic exposure?”** If the answer remains “because building permits don’t move differentially, and agriculture predicts votes,” interest will soften. The paper absolutely lives or dies on how persuasively it handles that question in framing.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Likely one of:
- “What happened to farm-level outcomes?”
- “Was BBB support just where CDA/FvD/rural conservatives already were?”
- “Can you show an actual political shift rather than a cross-sectional correlation?”
- “Why should building permits be the relevant measure of material loss for an agrarian backlash?”

Those are not referee-style nitpicks; they are strategic-positioning questions. The paper needs to preempt them.

### If the findings are modest or partly null, is the null interesting?
Yes, **if sold correctly.** The null on differential building permits is interesting only as part of a broader contrast: visible national disruption, but weak local incidence in the obvious administrative channel. On its own, it feels narrow. Paired with the political geography, it becomes more interesting. The paper should explicitly make the value of the null result be: **the local political backlash did not map onto the local incidence of the most visible measurable policy disruption.**

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

At present, the gap is a mix of **framing problem, scope problem, and ambition problem.**

### Framing problem
The science is somewhat there, but the paper needs a more disciplined and defensible claim. It should stop sounding like it has definitively separated identity from economics, and instead emphasize that the political geography of backlash aligns poorly with measured local disruption and strongly with pre-existing agricultural communities.

### Scope problem
The paper wants to answer a large mechanism question with one narrow material-outcome proxy and one cross-sectional political outcome. For AER, that is thin. The paper needs either:
- more measures of material disruption, especially in agriculture,
- stronger evidence of political realignment over time,
- or direct mechanism evidence on identity activation.

### Novelty problem
The broad claim that rural/agricultural identity matters for populist support is not new. What is potentially new is the connection to a specific, salient environmental legal shock and the argument that backlash to green policy may operate through identity rather than immediate local economic losses. That novelty needs sharpening.

### Ambition problem
The current paper is careful and competent, but a bit safe. It documents an interesting event with suggestive decomposition evidence. To be an AER paper, it needs to do more than document; it needs to **change how economists think about the political constraints on environmental policy.**

### Single most impactful advice
**If the author changes only one thing, it should be to reframe the paper around the broader question of green-transition backlash and substantially strengthen the “material loss” side beyond building permits—especially with agricultural economic outcomes or a cleaner measure of local economic incidence.**

That single change would do the most to convert this from an interesting case study into a paper with field-defining potential.
