**Idea Fidelity**  
The paper largely follows the manifested idea: it leverages the May 2019 Dutch nitrogen ruling, exploits municipality-level variation through Natura 2000 proximity and sectoral employment to quantify exposure, and compares economic disruption (building permits) with the rise of BBB. The data sources described in the manifest (CBS building permits, Kiesraad election results, PDOK shapefiles) are indeed those used. However, the empirical focus shifts from estimating a continuous-treatment DiD effect of disclosure-related disruption on political outcomes (as originally suggested) to two separate analyses: a DiD on building permits and a cross-sectional regression on BBB vote share. The manifest promised a continuous-treatment DiD identifying the causal path from the ruling to BBB votes, whereas the paper settles for a decomposition that attributes BBB support to agricultural identity with only suggestive links to the ruling. A direct, joint causal pathway linking exposure → disruption → votes is not fully pursued, which is a deviation from the original idea’s ambition.

**Summary**  
The paper studies whether the 2019 Dutch nitrogen court ruling caused agrarian populist support for BBB through economic disruption. Using municipality-level building-permit data, it finds no differential decline for high-exposure areas, suggesting the ruling did not disproportionately harm those near Natura 2000 sites. A cross-sectional analysis then shows that BBB vote share is strongly correlated with pre-existing agricultural employment shares, not with spatial exposure measures, implying that identity rather than economic losses drove the rise of BBB.

**Essential Points**  
1. **Threat to the DiD identification** – Equation (2) relies on a continuous-treatment DiD, but the paper does not present graphical or statistical evidence supporting the parallel-trends assumption. Given that exposure is time-invariant and municipal outcomes exhibit strong seasonality and secular trends, an event-study or lead coefficient plot is essential to show that high- and low-exposure municipalities were on similar trajectories before 2019Q3. Without this, the null result is hard to interpret: it could reflect pre-trends offsetting treatment effects rather than a genuine absence of heterogeneity.  
2. **Mismatch between exposure measure and economic outcome** – The treatment intensity combines Natura 2000 share with an employment weight, yet the outcome is residential building permits. It is not clear why construction permits should respond mechanically to agricultural employment shares; the specification therefore places substantial weight on a composite whose components are themselves correlated with the outcome through alternative channels (e.g., rural municipalities have fewer permits irrespective of the ruling). The authors need to motivate (and ideally test) why their exposure measure isolates the ruling’s effect on construction activity, or else construct an exposure that is plausibly exogenous to permit trends (e.g., share of Natura 2000 land within a fixed buffer).  
3. **Causal claim on BBB vote share unsupported** – The cross-sectional regressions are presented as evidence that agricultural identity, not the ruling, explains BBB support. But BBB did not exist pre-2019, and the analysis lacks an identification strategy that exploits the timing of the ruling. Agricultural employment is almost certainly correlated with unobserved rural characteristics (culture, media markets, historical voting) that directly affect BBB votes. Without an exogenous shock to agricultural identity or a difference-in-difference comparing pre- vs post-ruling vote shares (even if only the party’s predecessor votes exist), the analysis cannot separate a policy effect from long-standing rural partisanship. Given this, the inference that the nitrogen crisis was merely a catalyst rather than a cause is overstated.

**Suggestions**  
1. **Strengthen the DiD credibility**  
   - Provide event-study figures for the building permits panel, estimating dynamics of the Exposure × Post interaction in the quarters before and after the ruling. This helps assess the parallel-trends assumption and whether any pre-trend exists.  
   - Consider normalizing the exposure measure by pre-trends: e.g., residualize exposure on pre-treatment permit levels to reduce correlation with baseline rurality. Alternatively, work with a binary treatment defined by proximity to the five Natura 2000 sites with the most permits frozen and show that high-proximity municipalities did not diverge post-ruling.  
   - Discuss (and, if possible, model) confounders such as national economic cycles or COVID-related construction shocks. Adding municipality-specific linear time trends or interacting exposure with time effects might help isolate differential responses.

2. **Improve the treatment-outcome link**  
   - Justify why the composite exposure index applies to residential building permits. If permits were frozen primarily for infrastructure or agricultural buildings, a more relevant outcome might be construction employment, permit valuation, or sector-specific investments. If such data are unavailable, acknowledge this limitation explicitly and discuss how measurement error in the economic channel could attenuate the estimated effect.  
   - Explore other outcomes that capture the ruling’s direct economic impact on agriculture (e.g., farm investment, nitrogen reduction investments, farm closures) or broader municipal fiscal strain. This would clarify whether the ruling affected local economies, even if building permits did not change differentially.

3. **Pursue a quasi-experimental design for political backlash**  
   - To credibly tie the ruling to BBB’s rise, exploit variation in timing and exposure across municipalities by combining the cross-sectional support data with municipality-level electoral history (e.g., votes for agricultural or rural parties in 2015/2019 provincial or national elections). A difference-in-differences comparing vote swings between the BBB-supporting municipalities and similar municipalities with low exposure could trace the ruling’s effect. If BBB only emerged in 2019, consider using turnout or protest participation as intermediate outcomes linked to the ruling.  
   - If a cross-sectional design is unavoidable, implement control function or instrumental variable strategies that isolate exogenous variation in agricultural identity due to the ruling (perhaps exploiting exogenous Natura 2000 delineations established before the ruling). Demonstrating that the ruling sharpened the relationship between agriculture and political preferences (e.g., by interacting post-2019 indicators with agricultural shares in a panel of multiple elections) would bolster causal claims.

4. **Broaden robustness and mechanism exploration**  
   - Provide robustness checks that use alternative definitions of exposure (e.g., distance to legal Natura 2000 boundaries, share of agricultural land overlapping the buffer). Show that the key null in the DiD is not driven by the particular functional form of the composite measure.  
   - Investigate whether local media coverage, protest activity, or farmer association membership mediates the relationship between the ruling and BBB support. Mapping such channels (even qualitatively) would make the identity-vs-economic-loss argument more concrete.  
   - Assess whether the BBB strength is concentrated in municipalities with both high agricultural employment and early exposure to nitrogen reduction measures (e.g., buy-outs or permit refusals) to differentiate identity-based mobilization from material losses. If data constraints prevent this, clearly outline which data are missing and how future work could address the gap.

5. **Clarify interpretation and limitations**  
   - The narrative concludes that “green courts create brown politics” through identity threats. To avoid overclaiming, explicitly state that the null effect on building permits does not rule out other economic harms (e.g., delayed farm expansion, depressed land prices). Emphasize that the paper shows the ruling’s differential impact on permits was minimal, but the ruling may still have economic and political consequences through other channels.  
   - Provide more detail on how agricultural employment share is measured (e.g., whether it includes seasonal labor) and how sensitive the results are to measurement choices, as this variable is central to the identity claim.  
   - Discuss alternative explanations for BBB’s rise, such as national-level media narratives or party organization, and explain why the spatial analysis still offers unique evidence.  

By addressing these identification, measurement, and causal interpretation issues—and by expanding the range of robustness checks and potential mechanisms—the paper can more convincingly speak to the question of whether environmental regulation triggered agrarian populism through economic disruption or identity mobilization.
