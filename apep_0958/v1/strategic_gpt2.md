## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Governments are moving toward stricter environmental regulation, and standard political-economy intuition says backlash should come from places that bear concentrated economic harm.

### Tension
The Dutch nitrogen ruling triggered a dramatic agrarian-populist surge. But was that surge driven by direct localized losses from the ruling, or by activation of a deeper rural/agricultural identity that the ruling symbolically threatened?

### Resolution
The paper finds little evidence that municipalities more exposed to localized permit disruption saw differential declines in building permits, while BBB support is strongly concentrated in municipalities with higher pre-existing agricultural employment.

### Implications
If backlash to environmental policy is organized through identity and status rather than narrowly measured local economic pain, then technocratic compensation alone may be insufficient. Politically durable green policy may require a different approach to constituency-building, framing, and transition design.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
Yes, but it is not yet fully disciplined. The paper has the skeleton of a strong arc, but the execution still feels a bit like **two empirical exercises joined by interpretation**:
- one null on permits
- one cross-section on votes
- then a strong identity conclusion

That is not quite the same as a fully integrated story.

### What story should it be telling?
It should tell one clean story:

> The Dutch nitrogen crisis is a revealing case because it separates two channels of backlash that are usually conflated: direct economic incidence and identity-based mobilization. The evidence suggests that the political geography of backlash follows the social geography of agriculture more than the spatial geography of regulatory exposure.

That story is better than “green courts create brown politics,” which is catchy but somewhat op-ed-ish and perhaps too flamboyant for the evidentiary payload.
