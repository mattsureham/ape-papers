**Subject:** Strategic Assessment – "No Workers Were Harmed: The Null Employment Effect of the CFPB Payday Lending Rule"

### 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper investigates whether the CFPB’s 2017 "ability-to-repay" rule—the most significant federal intervention in the payday lending market—caused the "massive job losses" predicted by industry groups. Using a continuous difference-in-differences design based on pre-existing county-level payday establishment density, the author finds a precise null effect on credit-sector employment.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this pitch quite clearly. The first two paragraphs effectively set the stakes (150,000 jobs at risk) and identify the gap (the literature focuses on borrowers, not workers). The "pitch the paper should have" is almost exactly what is written, though it could be even punchier by emphasizing the *political* importance of the findings: "Regulatory debate often pits consumer protection against job preservation; this paper provides the first empirical test of that tradeoff in the $38 billion payday lending industry."

---

### 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution:** The paper provides the first empirical evidence that major federal consumer finance regulation had no detectable impact on industry employment, countering industry projections of 60-70% contraction.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It is well-differentiated from Bhutta (2015) or Morse (2011). Those papers are about credit supply/welfare. This is a labor/political economy paper.
*   **World vs. Literature:** It frames itself as answering a question about the WORLD (Do regulations kill jobs?), which is its strongest suit.
*   **Newness:** A smart economist would say: "This is the paper that checks if the 'job-killer' rhetoric about the CFPB was actually true."
*   **Making it Bigger:** To move from "solid null" to "AER blockbuster," the paper needs a more granular outcome. Using NAICS 522 (broad credit intermediation) is a major limitation—the signal is diluted. If the author could get 6-digit NAICS or firm-level data, the contribution would be undeniable.

---

### 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

*   **Closest Neighbors:** Morse (2011) and Melzer (2011) on payday impacts; Greenstone & Gallagher (2012) on regulatory employment effects; Autor et al. (2007) on labor market mandates.
*   **Positioning:** It builds on the payday literature by adding the "missing" producer-side labor dimension. It synthesizes the "regulatory cost" literature with consumer finance.
*   **Niche vs. Broad:** Currently a bit niche. It speaks to "payday lending" people. It should speak more to "regulation" people generally.
*   **Conversation:** The paper is having the right conversation, but it misses a chance to connect to the "Political Economy of Lobbying" literature—specifically how industry groups use exaggerated employment projections to stave off regulation.

---

### 4. NARRATIVE ARC

*   **Setup:** Payday lending is a massive, controversial industry. The CFPB finally steps in.
*   **Tension:** Industry predicts a bloodbath (70% volume loss, thousands of layoffs). Pro-regulation groups claim it's a win for consumers.
*   **Resolution:** The data shows... nothing happened to the workers.
*   **Implications:** The "job loss" argument is a paper tiger, or the industry is more resilient/adaptable than it admits.

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong and classic. It’s a "Myth-Buster" story. The author correctly identifies the COVID-19 confound as a secondary narrative arc (a "cautionary tale" for DiD researchers), which adds methodological value.

---

### 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

*   **The Fact:** "The CFPB tried to effectively shut down 70% of the payday market, and not a single statistically significant job was lost."
*   **Reaction:** Lean in. It’s a counter-intuitive finding given the severity of the 2017 rule.
*   **Follow-up:** "Why didn't they lose jobs? Did they just change the name of the loans to 'installment loans' to bypass the rule?" (The author touches on this in the discussion, but more evidence here would be the "hook").

---

### 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

*   **Front-loading:** The paper is well-structured. The "COVID confound" diagnosis on page 3 is excellent—it prevents the reader from dismissing the result as a messy 2020-2022 trend.
*   **Buried Treasure:** The power calculation (Section 4.2) is vital for a null result. It should perhaps be moved earlier or given more prominence to head off the "you just don't have enough data" critique.
*   **NAICS Dilution:** The 3-digit NAICS (522) is the paper's "Achilles heel." The author should move heaven and earth to find a subset of states with 6-digit data or use a different data source (e.g., Infogroup/Data Axle) to validate the storefront counts.

---

### 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The gap is **Signal-to-Noise.** Because the author uses NAICS 522 (which includes banks and mortgage brokers), a critic will say the "null" is just the payday signal being drowned out by the rest of the financial sector.

**The single most impactful piece of advice:** Use an alternative data source (like Google Maps API, Yelp, or Business Directories) to track *actual* storefront closures in high-density vs. low-density counties. If the author can show that the *number of stores* didn't even drop, the employment null becomes a "slam dunk" instead of a "noisy maybe."
