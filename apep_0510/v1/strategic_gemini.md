## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether state-level supply-side interventions in the opioid crisis—specifically mandatory Prescription Drug Monitoring Programs (PDMPs)—yield downstream benefits for human capital formation. Using an institution-level panel and staggered difference-in-differences, it finds that while these mandates are intended to curb misuse, they have no detectable impact on college retention or degree completion. This suggests that the determinants of student success are largely insulated from the "prescribing" dimension of the drug market.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this pitch fairly well in the first two paragraphs, effectively linking the macro-crisis (Case & Deaton) to the micro-level institutional outcomes (IPEDS). However, it leans heavily on "filling a gap." 

**The pitch it should have:** 
"A standard justification for aggressive drug supply regulation is the protection of the next generation's human capital. We test this link by evaluating the most significant opioid policy of the last two decades—PDMP mandates—against the outcomes of 3.5 million students entering college at the peak age of risk. Our finding of a precise zero suggests that the 'pill-to-diploma' channel is significantly weaker than policymakers assume, shifting the burden of student success back toward institutional and financial factors."

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:** The paper provides the first comprehensive causal evaluation of how mandatory prescription drug monitoring affects college persistence and completion, documenting a precise null effect.

- **Differentiation:** It is well-differentiated. While Buchmueller & Carey (2018) look at prescribing and Alpert et al. (2018) look at substitution/mortality, this is the first to connect these dots to education.
- **Question vs. Gap:** Currently, it is framed about 50/50. It needs to pivot more toward the **WORLD**: "Can we regulate our way to better educational outcomes by restricting drug access?"
- **"Just another DiD":** A smart economist would likely say "it's an IPEDS DiD on a health policy." The "null result" framing is the danger zone here—it risks being seen as a "failed" exploration rather than a "precise" discovery.
- **Bigger Contribution:** To make this "AER big," the paper needs to prove *why* it is a zero. Is it because students substitute to illicit drugs (the "substitution hypothesis" mentioned in Section 5.4)? If the authors could definitively show that PDMPs fail to help students because they just trade Oxy for Heroin, the paper becomes a high-stakes story about the unintended consequences of supply-side policy.

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

- **Closest Neighbors:** Buchmueller & Carey (2018) on PDMP efficacy; Alpert et al. (2018) on substitution; Zuo & Ruhm (2022) on opioids/attainment (descriptive); and Bound et al. (2010) on completion determinants.
- **Positioning:** It should **synthesize** the first two (health) and **challenge** the assumptions of the third (education). It currently builds on them, which is too polite.
- **Audience:** The current audience is narrow (health/labor). It should speak to **Public Economics** more broadly: the efficiency of multi-stage causal chains in policy.
- **Missing Conversations:** The paper needs to engage more with the "Geography of Despair" literature. If college outcomes are "insulated" (as they claim), does that mean colleges are safe havens, or does it mean the students most at risk never made it to the IPEDS sample?

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

- **Setup:** The opioid crisis is a generational catastrophe.
- **Tension:** States passed mandates (PDMPs) to fix the supply, but we know users often switch to more dangerous street drugs. Does this "fix" actually help the kids trying to get through college?
- **Resolution:** No. Institution-level metrics don't move. 
- **Implications:** Supply-side "prescribing" tweaks are too far removed from the actual mechanics of student retention (money, prep, mental health).

**Evaluation:** The arc is "serviceable" but lacks the "substitution" tension as a primary driver. It feels like a series of robustness checks looking for an effect, then settling on a null. The narrative should be: "The Policy Chain is Broken."

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

**Dinner Party Fact:** "We looked at every college in America for 20 years and found that the biggest drug policy of our time had zero impact on whether kids dropped out."

**Reaction:** People lean in for the "why" but reach for their phones if the answer is just "the data is noisy." The "precise zero" (ruling out even small effects) is the hook.

**The Null:** The null is interesting *if and only if* the authors can convince the reader that the "prescribing" channel was a plausible lever in the first place. They need to emphasize how much 18-25 year olds were actually getting these pills.

---

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

- **Front-load the "Why":** Section 5.7 (Mechanisms) is the most interesting part of the paper. It should be hinted at much earlier.
- **Appendices:** Move the "TWFE vs. CS-DiD" methodological hand-wringing to the appendix. In 2026 (the paper's date), we take CS-DiD as the standard; we don't need a tour of the Goodman-Bacon decomposition in the main text.
- **Visuals:** Figure 3 (Mortality) is vital but messy because of the pre-trend. This needs to be cleaned up or framed as a "Motivation" figure rather than a "Result" figure.

---

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The gap is **Ambition**. Currently, it feels like a very high-quality *Economics of Education Review* paper. 

To reach the AER, it needs to address the **Extensive Margin** (Section 6.5, Limitation 1). If PDMPs don't affect *retention* (those already in college), do they affect *who gets to go*? If the authors could find a way to look at state-level college-going rates (CPS data?), they could tell a complete story of human capital.

**Single Most Impactful Advice:** Shift the focus from "measuring a policy" to "testing the limits of supply-side intervention." Make the "Substitution Hypothesis" the heart of the paper, not a footnote to the null.
