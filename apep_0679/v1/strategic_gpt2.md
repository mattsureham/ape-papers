## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether the UK Apprenticeship Levy, which sharply shifted apprenticeship spending toward older workers and higher-level credentials, also reshaped local training markets by crowding out apprenticeship opportunities in places with more levy-paying firms. Using variation across English Local Authorities in exposure to large employers, the paper finds no evidence of local crowd-out: the Levy’s disruption appears to have happened mainly within firms, not through geographically local training-market spillovers.

Why should a busy economist care? Because this is a policy-relevant test of a broader question: when governments subsidize or mandate firm training, do the distortions show up as market-wide reallocation or as within-firm relabeling/upskilling? That is a real economics question about incidence, equilibrium adjustment, and policy design—not just a UK institutional footnote.

**Does the paper itself articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Mostly, but not optimally. The opening has energy and a concrete fact pattern, but it gets too quickly into UK institutional detail and provider-capacity intuition without first nailing the broader economics question. The current introduction is stronger than average, but it still reads somewhat like “here is a UK reform and a test” rather than “here is a general economic question illuminated by a salient reform.”

**What the first two paragraphs should say instead:**

> Training levies are meant to increase human-capital investment, but they may also distort who gets trained and where training capacity goes. When large firms are given strong incentives to spend on subsidized training, does that simply change their own training mix, or does it pull scarce training resources away from smaller firms and new labor-market entrants in surrounding local markets?
>
> This paper studies that question using the UK’s 2017 Apprenticeship Levy, a large payroll-tax-financed training mandate that triggered a dramatic shift from entry-level apprenticeships toward expensive degree-level apprenticeships. I test whether Local Authorities with greater exposure to levy-paying firms saw larger declines in total apprenticeship starts, as would be expected if large employers crowded out local training provision. They did not: the Levy changed the composition of training within firms, but not the local volume of training across places.

That is the pitch the paper should have. Start from the general equilibrium/incidence question; then introduce the UK reform as a sharp setting.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution in one sentence:**  
The paper shows that the UK Apprenticeship Levy’s dramatic shift in apprenticeship composition did not translate into geographically differential changes in total apprenticeship starts across local labor markets, implying that the reform’s main distortion operated within firms rather than through local crowd-out.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest papers?
Only partly. The paper says firm-level work has already documented the shift toward higher-level apprenticeships, and that its contribution is to test a geographic spillover those papers cannot see. That is valid, but the differentiation is still a bit thin. Right now the novelty is “same policy, different unit of observation.” That is not yet a big contribution unless the paper persuades readers that the geographic margin is first-order and theoretically important.

The closest comparison is not just “papers on the Levy,” but “papers showing compositional shifts under training mandates/subsidies.” The paper needs to sharpen that its added value is to distinguish **within-firm substitution** from **market-level displacement**. That is the conceptual contribution.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or filling a gap in a LITERATURE?
It is mixed, but too often framed as filling a literature gap (“firm-level analyses cannot address this”). That is weaker. The stronger framing is world-facing:

- When training is subsidized or tax-financed, do firms absorb the subsidy internally?
- Or do they reallocate scarce local training capacity away from other firms/workers?

That is a question about how training markets work.

### Could a smart economist explain what is new after reading the intro?
Not confidently enough. Right now many readers would summarize it as: “It’s a DiD/Bartik paper on whether the Apprenticeship Levy affected local apprenticeship starts, and it finds no effect.” That is not enough. The introduction should make them say: “It separates two possible channels of distortion—within-firm reallocation versus local market crowd-out—and shows the former dominated.”

### What would make this contribution bigger?
Specific ways to enlarge it:

1. **Outcome variable:**  
   The current outcome—total apprenticeship starts—is too blunt. If the paper could show that high-exposure areas did not lose **youth / Level 2 / new-entrant** apprenticeships differentially, that would be much more consequential. The current null on totals leaves open a lot of offsetting composition.

2. **Mechanism:**  
   The paper’s story is provider-capacity crowd-out, but it does not directly observe providers. If the authors could show whether provider entry/exit, provider concentration, or provider-level specialization changed more in high-exposure areas, the paper would feel more like economics and less like a reduced-form null.

3. **Comparison:**  
   Compare local effects on SME-reliant training versus large-firm training, or on tradable vs nontradable local training supply. That would make the “local market” claim more concrete.

4. **Framing:**  
   Place the result in a broader class of policies with “use-it-or-lose-it” spending incentives. The big idea is not apprenticeships per se; it is how earmarked training funds shape equilibrium.

At present, the paper has a real but modest contribution. To be AER-level, it needs to be more than “a useful null at a different aggregation level.”

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
The Levy was introduced to increase employer-funded training. Instead, it coincided with a dramatic shift from low-level, entry-oriented apprenticeships toward expensive degree-level apprenticeships, often for incumbent workers.

### Tension
That observed compositional shift can be interpreted in two very different ways:
1. a within-firm substitution story, or
2. a broader local-equilibrium crowd-out story where large firms absorb scarce provider capacity and displace training for SMEs and youth.

The existing literature documents the first set of symptoms but does not settle which interpretation is right.

### Resolution
Using local exposure to levy-paying firms, the paper finds no differential decline in total apprenticeship starts in more exposed areas.

### Implications
The reform’s main distortion was not geographic displacement of training supply across places; it was a change in what large firms chose to buy with their training funds. Policy design should therefore focus more on spending incentives and eligibility rules than on local capacity protection.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
It has the ingredients of one, but not fully the discipline. At moments it feels like a collection of empirical outputs organized around a null result. The story is there, but it needs to be told more cleanly as a **horse race between mechanisms**.

Right now the paper sometimes lapses into:
- institutional background,
- empirical setup,
- result,
- robustness,
without continuously reminding the reader what conceptual uncertainty is being resolved.

**The story it should be telling:**
- We know the Levy changed apprenticeship composition.
- What we do not know is whether the damage was internal reclassification/upskilling, or external crowd-out in local training markets.
- That distinction matters for both theory and policy.
- I test that distinction and find evidence for the first, not the second.

That is a coherent narrative. The paper should hammer it.

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?
“Despite the UK Apprenticeship Levy dramatically shifting apprenticeships from youth entry-level training toward degree-level training for incumbent workers, places with more levy-paying firms did not see larger declines in total apprenticeship starts.”

That is a pretty good lead fact.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
Some would lean in, especially labor/public economists who know the UK policy debate. But many would ask: “Interesting—but is total starts the right margin?” That is the immediate vulnerability. A total-volume null is only engaging if it adjudicates a meaningful mechanism. Otherwise it sounds like a fairly local non-result.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Almost certainly:
- “Okay, but what about composition locally—young workers, Level 2, SME apprenticeships?”
Secondarily:
- “Why should local provider capacity be expected to bind at the LA level rather than regionally or nationally?”
And:
- “Is the null informative enough to rule out economically meaningful crowd-out?”

### If the findings are null or modest: is the null itself interesting?
Yes, but only conditionally. The null is interesting because it discriminates between two competing stories about what the Levy did. If the paper can foreground that mechanism distinction, the null becomes informative. If not, it risks reading like a failed search for local effects.

At present, the paper is not far from making the null interesting, but it still needs to do more work to show that “no local effect” is a substantive lesson, not just an absence of significance.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

Be honest: in its current form, this is **not an AER paper**. The main gap is not basic competence; it is ambition and scope.

### What is the main problem?
Mostly a **scope/ambition problem**, with some framing issues.

- **Not mainly a framing problem:** The authors already have a plausible story.
- **Not mainly a novelty problem:** There is some novelty in testing geographic spillovers.
- **Mainly a scope problem:** The core outcome is too aggregated, and the mechanism is too indirectly observed.
- Also an **ambition problem:** The paper asks a sensible question, but not yet on a scale that would excite the top people in labor/public economics.

### What is the gap between current form and an AER paper?
An AER version would likely need at least one of the following:

1. **A more decisive mechanism test**  
   Show that high-exposure areas did not differentially lose the kinds of apprenticeships most plausibly crowded out: entry-level, youth, SME, first-time apprentices, or specific provider-dependent categories.

2. **Provider-side evidence**  
   If the crowd-out theory is about provider capacity, show provider behavior: reallocation, entry/exit, specialization, pricing, capacity, or geography of provision.

3. **A broader conceptual contribution**  
   Use the UK setting to make a general point about earmarked corporate training funds and the incidence of quasi-mandated benefits.

4. **A more surprising comparative result**  
   For example: no local crowd-out in totals, but strong within-area compositional shifts; or no crowd-out in urban thick markets but crowd-out in thin/rural provider markets.

Right now the paper establishes an informative null on one broad margin. That is publishable somewhere solid, but it does not yet have the layered contribution expected at AER.

### Single most impactful advice
**If the author can only change one thing, they should replace or complement total apprenticeship starts with a sharper, policy-relevant local outcome—especially youth/Level-2/SME-oriented starts—so the paper directly tests whether the Levy displaced the workers and training types that matter most.**

That one change would immediately make the null far more interpretable and the contribution much bigger.
