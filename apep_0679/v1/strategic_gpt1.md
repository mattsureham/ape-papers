## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether the UK Apprenticeship Levy, which pushed large firms away from entry-level apprenticeships and toward higher-level training, also harmed local training markets by crowding out apprenticeship opportunities for smaller firms in places with more levy-paying employers. That is a potentially important question because the policy debate is not just whether the levy changed what firms bought, but whether it also reduced training access for young workers through local equilibrium effects.

The paper does articulate a pitch reasonably clearly in the opening paragraphs, and better than many papers do. But the current introduction leads with institutional detail and descriptive facts before fully clarifying the economic question. It risks sounding like “another policy evaluation of a UK training reform” rather than “a test of whether firm-level distortions spill over into local training market equilibrium.”

What the first two paragraphs should say instead:

> The Apprenticeship Levy dramatically changed who receives apprenticeship training in England: large firms shifted away from entry-level apprenticeships for new labor market entrants and toward expensive higher-level apprenticeships for incumbent workers. The natural concern is that this was not merely a within-firm compositional change: if training providers are capacity constrained, then demand from levy-paying firms could crowd out apprenticeship provision for smaller firms in the same local labor markets.
>
> This paper tests that local-equilibrium hypothesis. Using variation across English local authorities in pre-reform exposure to large employers, I ask whether places more exposed to the levy saw larger declines in total apprenticeship starts after 2017. The answer is no: the levy appears to have reshuffled training within firms, not displaced training across local labor markets.

That is the paper’s real pitch. It is sharper, more general, and more obviously economic.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:**  
The paper shows that the UK Apprenticeship Levy’s well-known shift from low-level youth apprenticeships to higher-level incumbent-worker training did not produce detectable crowd-out in local training volumes in areas more exposed to levy-paying firms.

This is a real contribution, but it is not yet differentiated sharply enough from adjacent work.

### Is it clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?
Only partially. The paper says prior work documented the compositional shift at the firm/national level and that this paper tests the “geographic dimension.” That is true, but “geographic dimension” is not yet a compelling differentiator by itself. The author needs to define the exact unanswered question more forcefully:

- Existing work: Did the levy change the composition of apprenticeship spending?
- This paper: Did those firm-level changes spill over through provider capacity constraints into local training-market outcomes?

That distinction is present, but it needs to be foregrounded more clearly and repeatedly.

### Is it framed as a question about the world or filling a literature gap?
Mostly a question about the world, which is good. The strongest framing is: **Do distortions in subsidized employer training markets remain inside firms, or do they propagate through local training supply?**  
That is much stronger than “firm-level analyses cannot address local authority variation.”

### Could a smart economist explain what is new after reading the intro?
Not confidently enough. Right now they might say: “It’s a DiD/Bartik paper on whether the UK apprenticeship levy changed local starts.” That undersells it badly. They should instead say: “It asks whether the levy’s compositional distortions created local crowd-out through training providers, and finds the distortion stayed within firms.”

### What would make the contribution bigger?
Several possibilities:

1. **Use more targeted outcomes.**  
   Total apprenticeship starts is a blunt outcome for the story being told. The crowd-out hypothesis is specifically about **SME-facing, entry-level, youth-oriented training**. If the paper could show null effects for those margins specifically, the contribution would become much more convincing and much more important.

2. **Show the mechanism more directly.**  
   The paper’s story runs through provider capacity. But the current evidence is one step removed. Results on provider entry/exit, provider concentration, or local mix of apprenticeship levels would make the contribution much larger.

3. **Reframe as a general equilibrium question.**  
   The biggest version of this paper is not “did English LAs with more big firms lose starts?” It is “when training subsidies distort employer demand, do those distortions transmit through local training supply?” That is a broader and more durable contribution.

4. **Explore heterogeneity where crowd-out should be strongest.**  
   Urban markets, areas with concentrated providers, areas initially heavy in lower-level apprenticeship provision, or sectors where providers are likely to be capacity constrained. If the average effect is null but the paper identifies the places where one might have expected an effect and still finds none, that sharpens the message.

At present, the contribution is interesting but modest.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

### Closest neighbors
The nearest neighbors appear to be:

- **Patrignani and coauthors / CVER-style work** on levy effects and apprenticeship composition.
- **Dougherty and coauthors** on “use-it-or-lose-it” training incentives.
- More broadly, papers on **training subsidies / employer-sponsored training** such as **Acemoglu and Pischke (1999)** and **Leuven and Oosterbeek (2004/2005 review tradition)**.
- If the local-market framing is emphasized, the paper also sits near work on **spatial equilibrium / local spillovers / provider capacity constraints**, though it currently does not exploit that enough.

### How should the paper position itself?
**Build on**, not attack. The paper is not overturning the prior literature; it is narrowing and refining what kind of distortion the levy created. The stance should be:

- Prior work established a dramatic within-firm compositional shift.
- This paper asks whether that firm-level change propagated outward into local training-market crowd-out.
- It did not, at least on total local volumes over the short-run post period.

That is a useful complement, not a challenge.

### Is it too narrow or too broad?
Currently **too narrow in data, but too broad in aspiration**.

- Too narrow because the empirical object is a subset of English local authorities, one policy, one aggregate outcome.
- Too broad because the prose occasionally implies broad lessons about training levies and market equilibrium without quite having enough evidence on mechanism or outcome composition to support that ambition.

### What literature is it unaware of?
The paper needs stronger engagement with:

1. **Vocational education / training-market design literature**, especially on provider behavior and capacity constraints.
2. **Public finance of earmarked payroll taxes / tax-benefit links** beyond generic payroll tax incidence.
3. **Local market spillover literature**—not because it is a local-labor-market paper per se, but because its core claim is about the absence of local equilibrium effects.
4. **Education production / supply-side constraints** literatures. The mechanism is not just employer incentives; it is provider allocation.

The current payroll-tax literature citations feel somewhat generic and not central to the paper’s actual value proposition. The more natural conversation is with labor/training policy and local supply responses.

### Is it having the right conversation?
Not quite. The paper wants to be in the conversation about **whether targeted subsidies change market-wide opportunity sets or just private spending patterns**. That is a better conversation than generic payroll-tax effects. The payroll-tax angle is technically true but not where the intellectual action is.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Before this paper, we know the Apprenticeship Levy transformed apprenticeship composition in England: fewer lower-level starts, many more higher-level degree apprenticeships, especially among levy-paying firms.

### Tension
It is unclear whether this was only a within-firm reshuffling or whether it also reduced local apprenticeship opportunities for smaller firms and young entrants by pulling scarce provider capacity toward large, lucrative contracts.

### Resolution
The paper finds no detectable differential decline in total apprenticeship starts in local authorities more exposed to levy-paying firms.

### Implications
The levy’s main distortion appears to have operated through **firm incentives over who and what to train**, not through local crowd-out in training markets.

This is a real narrative arc. The paper does have one. That is to its credit.

But the arc is not fully maximized because the outcome variable does not perfectly match the tension. The tension is about crowd-out of **specific kinds of training**. The resolution is about **total starts**. That makes the conclusion slightly less satisfying than the setup promises. The reader feels a mismatch: “You said youth/SME entry-level crowd-out; you showed total starts.”

So this is **not** a random collection of results. It is a coherent paper. But it is a somewhat underpowered story, conceptually, because the narrative is narrower than the introduction implies.

The story it should be telling:

> The levy changed the composition of apprenticeship demand. The key policy concern is whether that demand shift generated local supply spillovers. Using local exposure to levy-paying firms, I test for those spillovers and find none in aggregate training volume. That pushes our interpretation of the reform away from local crowd-out and toward within-firm relabeling/upskilling.

That is a clean story. The paper should avoid overclaiming beyond that.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party?
“England’s apprenticeship levy massively shifted training from low-level apprenticeships for new entrants to higher-level programs for incumbent workers—but places with more levy-paying firms did not see bigger drops in total apprenticeship starts.”

That is decently interesting. Economists would not reach for their phones immediately. But they would lean in only if the second sentence comes fast: **“So the distortion stayed within firms rather than spilling over through local provider capacity.”**

### Would people lean in?
A subset would. Labor/public/education economists, yes. General-interest economists, maybe, but not enthusiastically. The paper has a respectable “so what,” but not yet an AER-grade one.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Almost certainly:

- “But what about starts for young workers or lower-level apprenticeships specifically?”
- “How do you know provider capacity was the operative mechanism you’re ruling out?”
- “Could total starts mask a big local compositional shift?”

Those are exactly the questions the current version invites and cannot fully answer.

### If findings are null or modest, is the null interesting?
Yes, in principle. This is not a failed experiment if framed correctly. Nulls can be very valuable when they adjudicate between two plausible stories:

1. the levy distorted training purely within firms, or  
2. it also crowded out local training markets.

The paper’s null matters because many reasonable observers would expect local crowd-out when providers are capacity constrained. So there is a valid scientific use for the null.

But to make the null land, the author needs to make the counterfactual expectation sharper. Why should we have expected local crowd-out? Which policymakers, reports, or theories implied it? The more plausible the crowd-out prior, the more informative the null.

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

### What would make it read better?

1. **Shorten the methods exposition in the introduction.**  
   The introduction currently spends too much space on design labels and robustness inventory. For editorial positioning, that is the wrong use of early real estate.

2. **Move most of the robustness parade out of the introduction.**  
   “Randomization inference, leave-one-out, alternative exposure measures…” belongs later. Early on, the reader wants the question, the answer, and why it matters.

3. **Front-load the conceptual contribution more aggressively.**  
   The first page should say: prior work documents within-firm substitution; I test local spillovers; there are none.

4. **Cut generic literature review from the introduction.**  
   The Acemoglu/Pischke, Leuven, payroll-tax incidence discussion is too diffuse. It makes the paper sound more derivative and less focused. The intro should cite only the directly relevant levy and training-market papers.

5. **Discussion and conclusion are repetitive.**  
   The conclusion mostly restates. It should instead do one of two things:
   - either state the paper’s boundary conditions very crisply, or
   - connect the result to a broader policy-design principle.

6. **Bring limitations closer to the main result.**  
   Especially the key one: total starts are not the same thing as youth/SME/Level-2 starts. That limitation is central to strategic positioning, not an afterthought.

### Is the good stuff front-loaded?
Partly. The opening descriptive facts are good, but the actual high-level insight is not stated crisply enough soon enough.

### Are results buried in robustness that should be in main results?
Not exactly. The main issue is not buried results; it is that the paper spends too much narrative energy certifying a null instead of making clear why the null changes how we interpret the policy.

### Is the conclusion adding value?
Only modestly. It summarizes, but does not elevate. It should be used to sharpen the broader claim: distortions in subsidized training systems may reshape **who gets trained** without reducing local training volume, so policy design should focus on spending incentives, not only funding levels.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

At present, this is not an AER paper. The gap is mostly **scope and ambition**, with some **framing** issues.

### What is the main problem?
Not primarily framing alone. The framing can be improved, but even with perfect framing the paper remains somewhat small because:

- the outcome is very aggregate relative to the mechanism,
- the post period is short,
- the sample is partial,
- and the contribution is a null on one plausible spillover channel.

That is a solid field-journal paper architecture, not yet an AER architecture.

### Is it a novelty problem?
Somewhat. The broad fact that the levy changed apprenticeship composition is already known. This paper’s novelty is the absence of local spillovers. That is new, but modestly so.

### Is it a scope problem?
Yes, most of all. To be AER-relevant, the paper would need to do more than show no effect on total starts across local authorities. It would need to pin down whether **the relevant margins of opportunity**—young entrants, low-level apprenticeships, SME training access, provider allocation—were insulated from the levy, not just aggregate starts.

### Is it an ambition problem?
Yes. The paper is competent but safe. It asks a reasonable extension question and answers it cleanly. But AER papers usually either change the object we care about, reveal a surprising mechanism, or provide a result with broader transportability. This one is not there yet.

### Single most impactful advice
**Make the paper about whether the levy changed access to apprenticeship opportunities for the groups and training types policymakers actually care about—young entrants, lower-level apprenticeships, and SME-facing provision—not merely total local starts.**

That is the one change that could most alter the paper’s trajectory. If the author cannot do that with current data, then the paper should narrow its claims and target a field audience rather than trying to sell an aggregate null as a major general-interest contribution.
