This paper studies whether the downstream labor-market costs of the 2018 U.S. steel and aluminum tariffs were distributed differently by education level. Its core claim is that in more steel-exposed downstream manufacturing counties, college-educated workers experienced higher separation rates after Section 232, even though net employment and earnings effects were mostly nil; a busy economist should care because the paper speaks to who actually bears the incidence of protectionist policy once one looks beyond the protected sector.

The paper does articulate a decent version of this pitch in the first two paragraphs, but it does not yet land the punch hard enough. The current introduction starts with institutional detail and then pivots to the literature before fully clarifying why the central fact is surprising and important. The first two paragraphs should say, more directly: “Steel tariffs were sold as protection for blue-collar workers, but most workers touched by the policy are in downstream manufacturing, where steel is an input rather than an output. This paper asks a simple incidence question: when input protection raises downstream costs, which workers inside those firms bear the adjustment burden? Using administrative data by education group, I show that the adjustment shows up not in headline employment losses but in higher turnover among more-educated workers in more exposed places.” That is the pitch the paper should have.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

The contribution in one sentence: the paper argues that the downstream labor-market incidence of Section 232 tariffs was not skill-neutral, showing relatively higher separations for more-educated workers in more exposed downstream manufacturing areas.

This is differentiated from the closest tariff papers, but only partially. The paper clearly separates itself from work on aggregate price effects, aggregate employment effects, and retaliation. But the differentiation is still a bit mechanical—“they study average effects, I study heterogeneity by education”—rather than conceptual. The paper needs to state more sharply what new economic question it answers: not just whether tariffs reduce downstream employment, but how firms reallocate adjustment across worker types when a trade shock works through input costs.

Right now the contribution is framed somewhere between a world question and a literature gap. The stronger version is the world question: when governments protect upstream industries with input tariffs, who inside downstream firms pays? That is much better than “the literature has not decomposed downstream effects by education.” The latter sounds incremental; the former sounds like incidence.

A smart economist reading the current introduction could probably say, “It’s a DDD paper on education heterogeneity in the Section 232 tariffs.” That is not enough. They should instead be able to say, “It shows that input protection changes the composition of adjustment inside downstream firms, with turnover concentrated among white-collar or more-educated workers rather than the workers tariff rhetoric emphasizes.” That is more memorable.

What would make the contribution bigger? Most importantly, moving from “education-specific reduced form” to “who in the organization adjusts” would help enormously. Education is a noisy proxy. Occupation, worker function, manager/professional versus production, or wage quantiles within firms would make the contribution feel much more economic and less demographic. Short of that, stronger mechanism evidence on whether the separations are concentrated in engineering/administrative-heavy industries, or in places with high white-collar shares, would make the story more compelling. A second way to enlarge it would be to frame the paper as about incidence of input protection in downstream labor markets generally, using Section 232 as the case study, rather than as one more paper about the Trump tariffs.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

Closest neighbors include:

- Amiti, Redding, and Weinstein (2019) on the incidence of the 2018 tariffs and domestic price effects.
- Flaaen and Pierce (2019) on downstream manufacturing effects of steel tariffs.
- Fajgelbaum et al. (2020) on the aggregate welfare and retaliatory costs of the trade war.
- Cavallo et al. (2021) on tariff pass-through to prices.
- More distantly, Autor, Dorn, and Hanson (2013, 2014) and Dix-Carneiro and Kovak (2017) on heterogeneous labor-market adjustment to trade shocks.

Relative to those neighbors, the paper should mostly build, not attack. It should say: we now know tariffs raised domestic input prices and imposed downstream costs; what we do not know is how downstream firms distribute those costs across workers. That is a natural next question. There is no need for a combative stance toward the existing Section 232 literature.

The current positioning is somewhat too narrow. It is written as a small extension to the Section 232 literature plus a nod to trade-and-labor adjustment. For AER-level positioning, the audience has to be broader: trade economists, labor economists, and political economy readers interested in incidence and the constituency of protection. The real conversation is not just “what did Section 232 do?” but “how do input-cost shocks transmit through firms to different workers?”

The paper also seems under-engaged with adjacent literatures on:
- firm adjustment to cost shocks,
- labor hoarding and cyclical adjustment across occupations,
- organizational capital and white-collar versus blue-collar adjustment,
- task-based or occupational exposure to trade/cost shocks,
- incidence of industrial policy and place-based policy.

If the paper connected to work on firm boundaries, organizational restructuring, or occupational adjustment, it would have a better shot at escaping the “Trump tariff episode paper” box. That is likely the right conversation. The unexpected literature connection that could matter most is not classic trade exposure but personnel economics / firm organization: when margins compress, which parts of the organization get cut first?

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

The setup is clear enough: tariffs were intended to protect upstream metal producers, but because steel is an input, the economically relevant effects run downstream as well.

The tension should be: aggregate downstream effects may miss the true incidence because firms can adjust on margins other than net headcount, and those margins may differ across worker types. Put differently, tariff politics is about “protecting workers,” but the relevant workers are not homogeneous.

The resolution is: in more exposed downstream places, post-tariff adjustment appears in higher separations among more-educated workers, with little change in aggregate employment or earnings.

The implications are: first, input protection may reallocate costs within firms rather than simply destroy jobs overall; second, the political story of tariffs as blue-collar protection is incomplete because much of the burden may be borne elsewhere in the workforce.

The paper has a serviceable narrative arc, but not a fully convincing one. It still reads somewhat like a collection of tariff regressions with one interesting coefficient. The problem is that the narrative currently overpromises relative to the findings. It gestures at restructuring, replacement, skill gradients, political economy, and irony, but the empirical core is one modest separation-rate result plus a set of mostly null employment and earnings estimates. The story should therefore be narrower and cleaner: “the main labor-market margin affected downstream was turnover, not employment, and that turnover was concentrated among more-educated workers.” That is the story supported by the current evidence. The paper gets into trouble when it tries to infer too much about organizational restructuring from that alone.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

At a dinner party of economists, the lead fact would be: “The steel tariffs didn’t show up much in downstream net employment, but in more exposed manufacturing areas they did raise separations among college-educated workers relative to less-educated workers.”

Would people lean in? Some would, but only briefly. Trade and labor people might ask a sharp follow-up; many others would see it as a somewhat niche heterogeneity result from a heavily studied policy episode. The finding is interesting, but it is not yet one of those instantly gripping AER facts.

The natural follow-up question would be: “Why the college-educated workers? Is this engineers/managers being cut, or is it voluntary mobility, or is education just proxying for something else?” And that question is exactly where the paper is currently weakest strategically. The memo need not evaluate whether the evidence is sufficient, but from a positioning standpoint, the paper’s headline invites a mechanism question it cannot yet answer.

The null and modest results are not fatal, but the paper needs to embrace them correctly. If the real message is that the shock appears in churn rather than levels, the paper should make the value of that distinction explicit. Right now, the null employment results feel a bit like “the main outcomes didn’t move, but here is one margin that did.” To avoid that impression, the paper should frame turnover as the conceptually primary outcome from the outset, not as the consolation prize after null employment estimates.

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

The introduction should be shorter on institutional recap and literature cataloging, and quicker to the central fact. The first page should give the reader the headline result and the reason it matters. Right now the paper takes a bit too long to explain what is at stake.

The institutional background can be compressed substantially. For this audience, a full section on what Section 232 is and what downstream exposure means is not carrying much marginal value. Much of that can be shortened or folded into the introduction and data section.

The empirical strategy section is over-explained relative to the strategic value of the paper. Since this is not a methods contribution, the introduction and results should dominate, not the mechanics of fixed effects. AER readers should not have to wade through a methodological defense before they know the substantive payoff.

The best result is reasonably front-loaded, but the paper still underplays it conceptually. If turnover is the key margin, the paper should organize the results around that, not around the standard sequence of employment / separations / earnings. Put the turnover result first in both text and perhaps even table order.

There are also interpretive claims in the discussion that should be toned down or moved later unless supported better. “Organizational restructuring” and “inverted skill-hoarding” are suggestive hypotheses, not yet the main finding. The conclusion should not overstate them.

Finally, the conclusion is mostly summary. It should either broaden to a stronger general lesson about input protection and labor-market incidence, or be trimmed.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

In its current form, this is mostly a framing-plus-ambition problem, with some scope issues. The science may be competent, but the paper currently reads like a careful heterogeneity extension of an already crowded policy episode. That is not enough for AER.

The paper’s biggest strategic weakness is that the headline contribution is narrower than the prose suggests. “Education-specific downstream separation effects of Section 232” is a field-journal contribution. For AER, the paper needs to become a broader statement about how input-cost shocks are transmitted within firms or within local labor markets, with Section 232 as a compelling setting.

So what is the gap? The paper needs either:
1. a much stronger framing around the incidence of input protection across worker types, or
2. broader evidence that pins down the adjustment margin more concretely, ideally beyond education alone.

If I had to classify the issue: primarily ambition and framing, secondarily scope. Novelty is moderate but not negligible; the question is not answered already, but it is adjacent to papers that have already mined this episode intensively.

The single most impactful advice: stop selling this as “the first education-specific DDD on Section 232” and instead make it a paper about the internal labor incidence of input protection—then add whatever evidence you can to show that the key margin is white-collar/organizational adjustment rather than simply a noisy education split.
