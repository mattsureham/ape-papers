**2. Summary**  
This paper exploits the 2018 Section 232 steel/aluminum tariffs to examine how downstream input‑cost shocks affect workers of different education levels in manufacturing. Using a triple‑difference design that leverages within‑county, between‑education variation in exposure, the author finds that higher‑educated workers experience a modest but statistically significant increase in separation rates (≈0.4 pp of the quarterly rate) in high‑exposure counties, while overall employment and earnings effects are indistinguishable from zero.

**Overall assessment**  
The paper tackles an interesting and policy‑relevant question and makes clever use of the QWI education breakdown. The identification strategy is promising, but the current version lacks crucial validation of the parallel‑trend assumption and uses an inference clustering that appears too optimistic. The effect, while statistically significant under the baseline specification, is economically tiny and disappears under more appropriate standard errors. Strengthening the pre‑trend evidence, adopting correct clustering (or robust inference), and contextualising the magnitude will determine whether the contribution is compelling enough for publication. If those issues are addressed, the paper would make a valuable, data‑driven addition to the trade‑and‑labor literature.
