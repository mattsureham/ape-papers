## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
This paper examines the distributional consequences of the 2018 Section 232 steel and aluminum tariffs on downstream manufacturing. It asks whether the "working class" protection intended by the policy backfired on higher-skilled (college-educated) workers through increased turnover and restructuring. A busy economist should care because while the *aggregate* effects of these tariffs are well-documented, we know little about the *internal* labor market reallocation they triggered, which has profound implications for the political economy of trade.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this reasonably well, but the second paragraph gets bogged down in literature citations too quickly. 
**The pitch the paper should have:** 
"In 2018, the U.S. imposed steel tariffs to protect blue-collar jobs, yet steel is a primary input for 6.4 million downstream manufacturing workers. This paper investigates whether the resulting cost shocks were skill-neutral or if they forced firms to disproportionately shed high-skill 'overhead' labor. Using administrative QWI data and a triple-difference design, I find that the burden of adjustment manifested not as mass layoffs of production workers, but as a significant spike in separation rates for college-educated staff—the very group the policy was never intended to affect."

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**Contribution:** The paper identifies a specific "skill gradient" in labor market adjustment to input tariffs, showing that higher-educated workers face increased turnover (separations) while net employment remains stable.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It differentiates well from Flaaen & Pierce (2019) by moving from industry-level aggregates to education-specific flows.
*   **Framing:** It is currently framed as "filling a gap in a literature" (extending the 2018 tariff studies). To be AER-worthy, it needs to be framed as a question about the **World**: "How do firms restructure their human capital when faced with permanent intermediate input shocks?"
*   **What would make it bigger?** The "separation" result is the "cleanest," but it's also the most ambiguous. A major contribution would show *where* these workers go. Do they leave manufacturing? Do they move to less-exposed counties? The paper currently lacks the "second act" of the labor transition.

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
The paper sits at the intersection of Trade (Amiti et al., 2019; Fajgelbaum et al., 2020) and Labor Adjustment (Autor et al., 2013). 

*   **Closest Neighbors:** Flaaen & Pierce (2019) on 2018 tariffs; Bloom et al. (2019) on trade and management; Hummels et al. (2014) on offshoring and skills.
*   **Strategy:** The paper should **synthesize** the trade-shock literature with the "Skill-Biased Technical Change/Restructuring" literature. It currently misses the connection to the literature on "Inverted Skill Hoarding" or how firms treat fixed vs. variable labor costs during trade uncertainty.
*   **Narrow vs. Broad:** Currently too narrow (U.S. 2018 tariffs). It should speak to the broader theory of the firm’s response to cost-push shocks.

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC
*   **Setup:** Protectionist tariffs are back in style, aimed at saving blue-collar jobs.
*   **Tension:** These tariffs raise costs for downstream firms. Standard theory suggests firms might "hoard" high-skill overhead labor and cut production staff.
*   **Resolution:** The opposite happens. Production workers are relatively stable, but college-educated turnover spikes.
*   **Implications:** Protectionism causes "invisible" churn among high-skill workers, potentially undermining long-term R&D or managerial stability in manufacturing.

**Evaluation:** The arc is "Serviceable." The "tension" needs to be heightened: why is the result surprising? The paper suggests it’s surprising because it's "ironic," but it's more interesting if it's "theoretically counter-intuitive" (reversing the standard skill-hoarding story).

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
*   **The Lead Fact:** "Trump's steel tariffs actually made college-educated engineers and managers more likely to lose or leave their jobs, while production workers were largely spared."
*   **The Dinner Party Reaction:** People would lean in because it subverts the "working class vs. elite" narrative of the trade war.
*   **The Follow-up:** "Did they get fired, or did they quit because they saw the writing on the wall?" The paper’s inability to distinguish voluntary from involuntary separations is a significant hurdle for an AER-level "So What?"

---

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
*   **Front-load:** The distinction between "overhead" (fixed) and "production" (variable) labor should be in the Introduction, not page 4.
*   **Appendix:** The state-level results (Table 2, Col 1-3) are noisy and add little; move them to the appendix. Focus entirely on the county-level triple-diff.
*   **Mechanism Section:** The paper needs a more robust discussion/test of *why* this is happening. Are these firms automating? Are they cutting R&D? Even suggestive evidence from Compustat or patent data would bridge the gap.

---

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The paper is currently a very competent "Applied Micro" piece, but it lacks the **ambition** of an AER paper. It reports a localized fact about a specific policy. 

**Single biggest improvement:** Shift the focus from "The 2018 Tariffs" to **"The Sensitivity of Overhead Labor to Intermediate Input Shocks."** If the author can show that this "inverted skill-hoarding" is a general feature of how modern manufacturing firms handle margin compression (perhaps by looking at other price shocks), it becomes a landmark paper on the nature of the firm.
