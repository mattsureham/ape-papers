**Idea Fidelity**

The paper largely pursues the manifest’s ambition: it leverages the QWI administrative panel with education-specific outcomes for manufacturing, constructs downstream steel exposure from pre-tariff employment shares, and implements a triple-difference strategy that exploits time, exposure, and education variation. That said, the exposition blurs the original emphasis on a county × 3-digit NAICS × education × quarter panel—Table 2, the robustness checks, and the identification discussion rely heavily on state-level variation (and standard errors clustered at the state level), with county-level specifications relegated to secondary tables. The manifesto suggested a strong county-level design with county-time fixed effects as the workhorse. In the paper, the state-level results take center stage and the county-level analysis is less thoroughly developed: the county triple-difference is reported mainly for separations/employment/earnings, but no detailed discussion of three-digit industry heterogeneity or additional county-level diagnostics appears. Clarifying why the county panel receives only supporting status and ensuring the main story matches the manifesto’s granular county-level emphasis would improve idea fidelity.

**Summary**

The paper studies the downstream labor incidence of the 2018 Section 232 steel/aluminum tariffs, exploiting education-specific QWI data to estimate a triple-difference in which high versus low-educated workers in high-exposure counties are compared before and after the tariff. The most compelling result is a statistically significant increase in separation rates for higher-educated workers in exposed counties, while employment and earnings show null effects. The finding is interpreted as evidence that downstream adjustment operated through higher turnover among college-educated workers, challenging the narrative that protection uniformly shields “working-class” manufacturing jobs.

**Essential Points**

1. **Parallel Trends and Event Study Evidence**: The identifying assumption rests on the claim that, absent the tariffs, the education gap in outcomes would have evolved similarly across high- and low-exposure counties. The paper lacks any event study or placebo evidence for this key assumption. Without visualizing pre-trends disaggregated by education × exposure, it is difficult to assess whether the differential separation rate effect simply reflects pre-existing divergence. Constructing event-study graphs for each education group (or at least for the high-low education gap) and testing for pre-period coefficients would greatly bolster credibility.

2. **Choice of Clustering and Inference for County-Level Results**: The main separation-rate result relies on county-level data but reports clustering at the state level, resulting in a statistically significant coefficient (p = 0.03). Switching to county clustering renders the effect insignificant (p = 0.49), as reported in the robustness table. Given the heavy reliance on county-level units for identification, state-level clustering is likely too coarse, and county-level clustering is more appropriate. The authors should commit to a single clustering approach justified by the level of variation and report results accordingly, perhaps using wild bootstrap methods if the cluster count is too high for conventional inference.

3. **Mechanism and Interpretation of Separation Rate Increase**: The separation-rate result is interpreted as higher-educated workers bearing the adjustment cost, yet the QWI cannot distinguish between involuntary layoffs and voluntary quits, nor can it rule out reallocation to other sectors. Without additional evidence, the causal interpretation of the separation-rate gradient as a “burden” on high-skilled manufacturing workers is weakened. The authors should explore supplementary margins: for instance, can QWI-based hiring rates or job churn be used to show that separations are not offset by new hires of equally educated workers? Alternatively, linking to auxiliary data (e.g., UI receipts or BEA) that differentiate layoffs from quits would clarify whether the effect reflects distress or mobility. At minimum, the discussion should more sharply acknowledge the limits of attributing separations to adverse outcomes.

**Suggestions**

1. **Event-Study Visualization and Tests**: As noted, the identification hinges on the common trend assumption for the differential education effects. I recommend plotting the high-education versus low-education separation-rate gap across exposure terciles over time, centered around the tariff implementation. Ideally, include interactions of exposure with leads and lags of the post indicator in the regressions to estimate dynamic effects and test for pre-trend violations. Reporting the same event study for the employment and earnings margins would also demonstrate whether the null effects are stable or hide delayed responses.

2. **Precise Definition of Education Groups**: The paper treats “High Edu” as some college or higher, collapsing E3 and E4. Yet Table 3 suggests richer heterogeneity. It would strengthen the paper to clarify why this binning is chosen and to explore whether the separation-rate effect is driven primarily by the bachelor’s group versus some college. If the effect is concentrated among E4, that is a sharper statement about the skill-air burden. Conversely, if E3 also contributes, this nuance should be highlighted in the narrative.

3. **Exposure Measurement and Robustness**: The exposure variable is constructed from 2016Q1 employment shares, which is sensible, but it would help to document the degree of pre-treatment variation within counties over time—if exposure shifts significantly post-2016, the assumption that it is exogenous may be compromised. Consider demonstrating the stability of the exposure share (e.g., correlations between 2015, 2016, 2017 shares) or re-estimating with alternative pre-treatment years or with plant-level shipping data if available. Additionally, exploring whether the results are sensitive to excluding large manufacturing metros (say, auto-heavy counties) would show that the effect is not driven by a few influential locales.

4. **County-level Fixed Effects in Practice**: The paper touts the strength of county × time fixed effects, but much of the main analysis is at the state level. If the county specification is intended to be the main identification strategy, it should be more fully developed—describe the fixed-effects estimation in detail, report balance statistics, and show that the county-level sample yields similar patterns once the sample is restricted to counties with sufficient education-specific observations. If computational constraints limit county-level estimation, make that explicit and justify why state-level aggregates are used instead.

5. **Placebo on Non-Downstream Industries**: A placebo using non-downstream manufacturing industries is a useful falsification, but the paper currently implements it only for employment. Extending the placebo to the separation rate (the main outcome) would strengthen the interpretation that the observed differential is specific to steel-using industries. Similarly, consider an outcome where no effect is expected (e.g., services or non-manufacturing separations) to rule out county-level confounders that could mimic the skill gradient.

6. **Interpretation of Null Employment/Earnings Effects**: The transition from separation effects to the conclusion that tariffs incur “costs” on college-educated workers is somewhat abrupt. To cement this argument, discuss how increased churn might affect career progression, job quality, or the productivity of downstream firms. Are there any complementarities between skilled labor and capital such that higher separation rates could lead to broader declines in firm performance? Even if the economic magnitudes are modest, connecting the empirical separation results to a broader economic mechanism (e.g., disruption of R\&D teams) would provide more traction for the paper’s political-economy claim.

7. **Discussion of Alternate Channels**: Could the separation-rate increase reflect higher safety concerns or voluntary mobility rather than layoffs? If higher-educated workers have better outside options, they may leave exposed industries for less volatile sectors when tariffs signal uncertainty. The authors mention voluntary mobility but do not test it. Collecting data on net employment or flows to other industries (possible with LEHD longitudinal links) might clarify whether the tariff prompted upward moves or forced exits.

8. **Policy Implications and Graduated Exposure**: The conclusion infers that downstream tariffs are “regressive.” It could be more precise: is the skill gradient economically large enough to offset the direct benefits to low-skill steelworkers? A back-of-the-envelope calculation—multiplying the estimated separation rate increase by the number of high-educated workers in downstream counties and translating it into job-years lost—would help readers judge the policy significance. Additionally, exploring whether the effect varies with the intensity of the tariff (e.g., counties with more exposure to steel versus aluminum) could illuminate whether the skill gradient scales with the policy’s bite.

Overall, the paper addresses an important question with novel data, but strengthening the identification diagnostics, clarifying the county-level implementation, and providing richer interpretation of the separation result would greatly improve its contribution.
