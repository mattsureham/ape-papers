## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
This paper asks whether policy shocks travel through social networks rather than just geography. It finds that workers in low-minimum-wage counties (like in Texas) experience significant increases in earnings and employment when their socially connected "neighbors" in distant, high-wage states (like California) receive a minimum wage hike. This suggests that a worker’s "outside option" is not just the shop down the street, but the wage expectations set by their digital and social network.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this very well in the first two paragraphs, specifically using the El Paso vs. Amarillo contrast to ground the abstract concept in a concrete geographic reality. It avoids the "I do X" trap by leading with the "California to Texas" question.

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
The paper’s contribution is demonstrating that social network exposure to high-wage labor markets causally reshapes local labor market equilibria through information transmission rather than physical migration.

- **Differentiation:** It differentiates itself from the "SCI literature" (Bailey et al.) by introducing **population-weighting** (scale vs. probability) and from the "Minimum Wage literature" (Dube et al.) by moving beyond contiguous-county geographic spillovers to network spillovers.
- **Framing:** It is framed as a question about the world: *How do workers form the outside options that determine their local bargaining power?*
- **Explainability:** A smart economist would say: "It’s the paper that shows workers in Texas get a raise when California raises its minimum wage because their Facebook friends tell them how much more they’re making."
- **Making it bigger:** To make it even bigger, the authors could link this to **inflationary expectations**. If social networks propagate wage shocks, do they also propagate price-setting behavior? However, for a labor paper, the "information-as-mechanism" is already a high-bar contribution.

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
The paper sits at the intersection of Network Economics and Labor Economics.

- **Neighbors:** Bailey et al. (2018) on SCI; Dube, Lester, and Reich (2010) on MW borders; Jäger et al. (2024) on worker beliefs; Adao et al. (2019) on shift-share.
- **Positioning:** It **synthesizes** these by using the SCI toolset to answer a classic Labor question. It effectively "attacks" the narrow geographic focus of previous spillover papers.
- **Unexpected connection:** The paper wisely connects to the literature on **"Reference-Dependent Preferences"** and **"Salience."** It’s not just about the *fact* of the wage, but the *noise* of the wage signal in the network.

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC
- **Setup:** Labor markets are traditionally seen as local or geographically contiguous.
- **Tension:** We observe labor market movements that don't align with local policy; meanwhile, we know workers have vast, non-local social networks.
- **Resolution:** Out-of-state MW shocks "bleed" into local markets through these network ties.
- **Implications:** Policy evaluation must look at the "network catchment area," not just the jurisdictional border.

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong. It isn't just a collection of coefficients; it's a test of a theory (Information vs. Migration).

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
At a dinner party: "Did you know that when California raises the minimum wage, it actually increases employment in parts of Texas that have a lot of family in LA?" 
- **The Hook:** The 9% employment multiplier. It’s "too big," which is exactly why people will lean in (and why referees will sharpen their knives).
- **The Follow-up:** "Wait, if the labor cost goes up because workers want more, why does employment go *up*?" The paper answers this via the "Search and Participation" channel (drawing people into the labor force).

---

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
- **Front-loading:** The "Population vs. Probability" weighting (Section 3.2 and 5.2) is a methodological gem. It should be highlighted even more in the intro as a "best practice" for researchers using SCI data.
- **Appendix:** The "Policy Diffusion" (Section 9.3) is a great "null" result to prove the channel is economic, not political. It’s well-placed.
- **Magnitudes:** Section 11.1 (Assessing the 9% magnitude) is defensive but necessary. It might benefit from a more explicit "Back of the Envelope" calibration.

---

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The gap is the **"Believability vs. Magnitude"** trade-off. A 9% employment increase from a distant $1 change is a massive multiplier.

- **The Single Biggest Improvement:** The authors need to bridge the gap between their **Information Model** (Appendix A) and the **Equilibrium Multipliers**. They need to show that the *volume* of information (signals) is high enough to plausibly change the reservation wage of a whole county. If they can provide any corroborating evidence from job search platforms or "Search Intensity" (Google Trends for "minimum wage" in high-connectivity counties), the paper becomes an "Open and Shut" case for AER.
