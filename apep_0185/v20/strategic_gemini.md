### 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether minimum wage increases in one state "travel" through social networks to affect labor markets in distant, low-wage states. Using Facebook’s Social Connectedness Index, the authors show that counties with dense social ties to high-minimum-wage hubs (like California) see significant increases in local earnings and employment, driven by information transmission that raises workers' reservation wages and search intensity. It suggests that a worker’s "outside option" is defined not just by their local geography, but by the wage signals they receive from their national social network.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this well in the opening anecdote (El Paso vs. Amarillo). However, the first two paragraphs lean heavily on the "minimum wage" literature. To better fit the AER, the pitch should lead with the fundamental insight about **market-level search and bargaining**.

*Revised Pitch:* "Standard models of labor market equilibrium assume workers' outside options are determined by local conditions and jurisdictional policy. We show that these options are actually network-weighted: a $1 increase in the minimum wage of a worker's distant social contacts raises local earnings by 3.4% and employment by 9% in their home county. These effects are driven by information transmission rather than migration, suggesting that social networks geographically decouple the reference wages that shape local labor supply."

---

### 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:** The paper demonstrates that socially-transmitted information about distant policy shocks serves as a market-level multiplier that reshapes local labor market equilibria.

*   **Differentiated?** Yes. It moves beyond "networks help people find jobs" (Beaman, 2012) to "networks transmit price signals that change local bargaining." It also differentiates itself from geographic spillover papers (Dube et al., 2010) by showing effects persist over thousands of miles.
*   **Question about the WORLD?** Strongly so. It changes the definition of a "local" labor market.
*   **Explanation:** A smart economist would say: "It’s a paper showing that if your friends in California get a raise, you start demanding more at your job in Texas, even if you don't move."
*   **Bigger contribution:** To make this "AER-big," the paper needs to lean harder into the **methodological innovation of population-weighting SCI**. The finding that *scale* (the number of signals) matters more than *probability* (the share of the network) is a major insight for anyone using social network data in economics.

---

### 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

*   **Neighbors:** Jäger et al. (2024) on outside options; Bailey et al. (2018) on SCI; Dube et al. (2010) on MW spillovers; and Kramarz & Skandalis (2023) on network job access.
*   **Positioning:** It should **synthesize** the SCI literature with the search-and-matching literature. Currently, it feels a bit like a "minimum wage paper." It should frame itself as a "Labor Market Friction" paper where the minimum wage is merely the instrument used to provide a clean price shock.
*   **Missing Conversations:** The paper should speak more to the **Reference-Dependent Preferences** literature. Is this just information, or is it "Keeping up with the Joneses" across state lines?

---

### 4. NARRATIVE ARC

*   **Setup:** Labor markets are local; policy effects stop at the border.
*   **Tension:** In a hyper-connected world, workers in low-wage El Paso know exactly what workers in high-wage Los Angeles earn. Does this "virtual migration" affect local wages?
*   **Resolution:** Yes, network exposure acts as a significant positive shock to local wages and employment via increased churn and search.
*   **Implications:** Policy evaluation is currently under-counting the reach of state-level interventions.

**Evaluate:** The arc is strong. The "Population vs. Probability" section (7.4) provides a great "Aha!" moment that resolves why previous network studies might have found smaller effects.

---

### 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

*   **The Fact:** "If California raises its minimum wage, employment in specific Texas counties *increases* by 9% because of Facebook friends."
*   **Reaction:** Lean in. It sounds counterintuitive (MW usually hurts employment in standard models) until the mechanism (increased labor supply/participation via higher reservation wages) is explained.
*   **Follow-up:** "Does this lead to inflation in those local counties?" or "Does it force low-productivity firms to exit?"

---

### 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

*   **Front-load:** The "Population-vs-Probability" divergence is the most intellectually interesting part. Move it earlier to justify the specific weighting choice.
*   **Appendix:** The Policy Diffusion analysis (Section 9.3), while rigorous, is a "null" result that distracts from the main labor market story. Move it to the appendix.
*   **Expand:** The "Job Flow Analysis" (Section 9.1) is the smoking gun for the mechanism. It needs more prominence and perhaps a figure showing the churn.

---

### 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The paper is technically competent and has a "sexy" data source (SCI). To clear the AER bar, it needs to move from being a "Minimum Wage Spillover" paper to a **"Theory of Networked Labor Markets"** paper.

**The Gap:** The current magnitudes (9% employment increase for a $1 network wage shift) are *very* large. To excite top people, the authors must prove this isn't just a "complier" story for a few weird counties.

**Single Biggest Advice:** Spend less time on minimum wage institutional details and more time on the **General Equilibrium implications**. If workers everywhere are updating their wages based on the highest-wage city in their network, what does that mean for national wage convergence and the "Great Convergence" vs. "Great Divergence" debate?
