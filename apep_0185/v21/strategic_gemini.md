## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
This paper asks whether a minimum wage increase in one state (e.g., California) affects labor market outcomes in a distant, unconnected jurisdiction (e.g., Texas) via social networks. Using Facebook’s Social Connectedness Index, the authors show that counties with dense social ties to high-wage areas see significant increases in local earnings and employment, driven by information transmission that reshapes worker reservation wages and search intensity. It suggests that "outside options" are defined by who you know, not just where you live.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this pitch exceptionally well. The El Paso vs. Amarillo opening is a classic "top-five" hook—it grounds a complex network identification strategy in a simple, intuitive geographic puzzle. 

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
The paper’s contribution is the demonstration that policy shocks propagate through social networks to create general equilibrium effects in geographically distant labor markets, mediated by information rather than physical migration.

*   **Differentiation:** It moves beyond the "geographic spillovers" of Dube et al. (2010) and the "direct search" of Kramarz & Skandalis (2023) by looking at market-level equilibrium multipliers.
*   **Framing:** It is framed as a question about the world (how do workers form outside options?) rather than a gap in literature.
*   **Clarity:** A smart economist would call this "the paper that shows California’s $15 wage matters for Texas because of Facebook friends."
*   **What would make it bigger?** The "industry gradient" results (Section 9.3) are currently the weakest link. The finding that high-wage sectors respond more than "high-bite" sectors is counter-intuitive. Finding a way to reconcile this—perhaps by looking at "bellwether" effects on general inflation expectations or local service demand—would turn a "minimum wage paper" into a "general macro-labor paper."

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
The paper sits at the intersection of Social Networks (Bailey/Stroebel), Labor Search (Jäger/Schoefer), and Minimum Wage (Dube/Cengiz).

*   **Closest Neighbors:** Bailey et al. (2018a) on SCI; Jäger et al. (2024) on worker beliefs; Dube et al. (2014) on spillovers.
*   **Positioning:** It builds on Bailey’s SCI infrastructure but pivots the application to a high-stakes policy debate. It synthesizes the "misperceived outside options" literature with "spatial equilibrium" models.
*   **Conversation:** It is having the right conversation. It elevates the "minimum wage" debate into the "information and search frictions" debate, which is much more AER-friendly.

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC
*   **Setup:** Outside options are local and determined by geography/jurisdiction.
*   **Tension:** If information flows freely across borders via social ties, why do we assume labor markets are geographically contained?
*   **Resolution:** Counties with high network exposure to high-wage policies behave as if they are in high-wage states, even if they aren't.
*   **Implications:** Policy evaluation that ignores network spillovers undercounts the true impact (and potential distortion) of jurisdictional laws.

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong. However, the "Information vs. Migration" tension could be sharper. The authors should more aggressively kill the "migration" strawman earlier to let the information story breathe.

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
*   **The Fact:** "A $1 increase in the minimum wage of your Facebook friends' states increases your local county employment by 9%."
*   **Response:** People lean in. That magnitude is large enough to be provocative (and perhaps controversial).
*   **Follow-up:** "Wait, if employment goes UP in Texas when California raises wages, does that mean Texas firms are forced to compete for labor they didn't know they were losing?" This leads directly to the core "outside option" mechanism.

---

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
*   **Front-loading:** The distinction between "population-weighted" and "probability-weighted" (Section 3.2) is brilliant but technically dense. It should be simplified to ensure the "scale matters" intuition isn't lost.
*   **Appendix:** The detailed "Policy Diffusion" (Section 10.3) is necessary for identification but slows the narrative. Move the bulk to the appendix.
*   **Visuals:** Figure 1 (the map) is the paper's strongest asset. I would suggest a second map or scatterplot that explicitly shows the "residual variation" (Figure 3) next to the "raw variation" to prove the within-state story more clearly.

---

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The paper is currently a "Home Run" on identification and a "Triple" on narrative. To make it an AER paper, it needs to address the **Ambition Problem** in the mechanism.

The current "Information" story is a bit of a black box. **The single most impactful piece of advice:** Use the industry heterogeneity (which currently looks "puzzling") to build a theory of **Labor Market Dynamism.** If the effect is strongest in "mining and information" (high dynamism) rather than "retail" (binding MW), this isn't just a minimum wage story—it’s a story about how social networks reduce search frictions and increase churn across the entire economy.
