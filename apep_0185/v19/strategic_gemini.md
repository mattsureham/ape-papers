## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
This paper asks whether policy shocks in one state—specifically minimum wage hikes—spill over to distant labor markets through social connections rather than geography. Using Facebook’s Social Connectedness Index (SCI) weighted by destination population, the authors find that a $1 increase in a county's "network minimum wage" raises local earnings by 3.4% and employment by 9%, driven by information-led changes in reservation wages and job search dynamism rather than physical migration.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this pitch remarkably well. The opening contrast between El Paso and Amarillo is a "textbook" AER hook—it uses a concrete geographic puzzle to illustrate a latent social mechanism. The second paragraph clearly defines the "Fight for $15" context and the information-sharing mechanism. It does not need a rewrite.

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**The Contribution:** The paper establishes that social networks serve as a high-conductance channel for policy transmission over long distances, demonstrating that the *scale* (population) of network ties is a critical and previously overlooked dimension of network exposure.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It differentiates itself well from Dube et al. (2010/2014) by moving from "contiguous borders" to "social borders." It also nuances the SCI literature (Bailey et al.) by proving that probability-weighted measures (the current standard) are insufficient compared to population-weighted "signal breadth" measures.
*   **World vs. Literature:** It frames itself as answering a question about the world: "Why does a raise in California create jobs in Texas?" This is a strong, ambitious framing.
*   **What would make it bigger?** The current 9% employment multiplier is quite large. To make the contribution "unassailable," the authors could explore the *firm* side more deeply. If workers have higher reservation wages, why are firms hiring more? They lean on "dynamism/churn," but a more explicit link to firm vacancy posting or search-and-matching theory would bridge the gap between "labor supply information" and "equilibrium employment."

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
The paper sits at the intersection of **Labor Economics (Minimum Wage)** and **Social Networks/Information.**

*   **Closest Neighbors:** Dube, Lester, & Reich (2010); Bailey et al. (2018a); Jäger et al. (2024) [Beliefs about outside options]; Moretti (2011) [Local multipliers].
*   **Strategy:** It builds on Bailey's data and Dube's policy context but "attacks" (politely) the standard geographic-spillover assumption.
*   **Missing Conversations:** The paper could benefit from connecting to the **Reference-Dependent Preferences** literature (e.g., Card et al., 2012). If I see my friend in LA making $15, is it just "information," or is it "envy/relative utility" that shifts my reservation wage? This would broaden the appeal to behavioral economists.

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC
*   **Setup:** Minimum wage research focuses on local/state effects; social networks are known to exist but are usually treated as channels for migration or referrals.
*   **Tension:** State minimum wages have diverged drastically, yet we treat counties with identical state laws as identical units, ignoring their radically different social "outside options."
*   **Resolution:** Socially connected counties "import" the minimum wage shock of their network neighbors, leading to higher wages and higher churn/employment.
*   **Implications:** Outside options are social, not local. Policy evaluation that ignores these "long-distance" spillovers misses a massive part of the general equilibrium.

**Evaluation:** The arc is exceptionally strong. It moves logically from a geographic puzzle to a methodological innovation (population weighting) to a mechanism test (migration vs. information).

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
At a dinner party: "A $1 raise in California's minimum wage actually increases employment in Texas counties that have high Facebook connectivity to LA."
*   **Reaction:** Lean in. It is counterintuitive (minimum wages usually "kill" jobs in the old model, and usually don't travel 1,500 miles).
*   **Follow-up:** "Why doesn't the firm just go out of business if workers demand more?" (This is the "tension" the paper needs to ensure it resolves convincingly).

---

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
*   **The "Distance-Credibility" section (8.1) is excellent.** It anticipates the "it's just a local shock" argument and defeats it by showing results get *stronger* as you exclude nearby counties.
*   **Appendix A (Formal Model):** Consider moving the core logic of Equation 9 (Extreme Value Theory/Signal Breadth) into the main text. It is the most compelling theoretical justification for why population weighting matters.
*   **Policy Diffusion (9.3):** This is a "nice to have" but potentially a distraction. If the authors find a null result on political feedback, keep it brief so it doesn't dilute the labor market story.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The paper is already highly "AER-shaped." It takes a classic topic (Minimum Wage), a modern dataset (SCI), and produces a surprising, large-magnitude result.

**The Gap:** The 9% employment multiplier is the "elephant in the room." It is very large for a $1 network-average shift. Critics will worry about the Exclusion Restriction—that California's minimum wage is just a proxy for "The Coastal Tech Boom" or "Blue State Cultural Trends" that also affect Texas.
**Single Most Impactful Advice:** Focus everything on the **Information vs. General Economic Shocks** horse race. The placebo tests with GDP/Employment (Table 13) are your most important exhibits. Move them from the "Robustness" mindset to the "Main Proof" mindset to convince the reader that this is truly about the *policy signal* and not just "California doing well."
