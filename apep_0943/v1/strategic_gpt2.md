## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether the failure of national climate policy can backfire by spurring more aggressive policy at lower levels of government. Using the 2021 Swiss referendum that rejected the federal CO2 Act, it argues that cantons with stronger support for the failed law were much more likely to adopt their own climate legislation afterward, suggesting that national policy defeat can reallocate climate action geographically rather than stop it.

A busy economist should care because this is a sharp political-economy question about federalism: when national policymaking breaks down, does policy die, or does it migrate? That is potentially important well beyond Switzerland, especially for climate policy in federal systems.

**Does the paper articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Mostly, but not quite. The opening has a nice intuition and a memorable label (“boomerang ballot”), but the paper quickly muddies the main contribution by mixing two claims: (i) referendum failure induced cantonal climate legislation, and (ii) this may have affected building activity. The first is the real headline; the second is, in the current form, a secondary and somewhat awkward proxy result.

**What the first two paragraphs should say instead:**

> National climate-policy failures are usually treated as dead ends. But in federal systems, a national defeat may instead shift policymaking downward, inducing states, provinces, or cantons to act on their own. This paper studies that possibility using Switzerland’s 2021 referendum rejecting the federal CO2 Act.
>
> I show that cantons where voters were more supportive of the failed federal climate law were far more likely to adopt their own climate legislation in the following two years. The central message is that national policy failure need not reduce policy ambition overall; it can decentralize it, producing compensatory subnational action and a more polarized geography of climate governance.

That is the pitch the paper should own. The building outcome can then be introduced as an exploratory implication, not as co-equal with the main contribution.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
The world before this paper: economists and policy observers tend to read national climate-policy failure as delay or reversal. Federal systems do allow subnational action, but whether national defeat actually triggers compensatory local policymaking is less clear.

### Tension
A failed national law could have two opposite effects:
- discourage climate action everywhere, because the national coalition lost;
- or activate supportive subnational governments, because they now have stronger incentive to act on their own.

That is the real tension. The paper has it, but it needs to foreground it more cleanly.

### Resolution
The paper’s central resolution is that Swiss cantons that more strongly supported the failed CO2 Act were much more likely to pass their own climate laws afterward. That is the real payoff.

The construction result is an attempted extension from policy response to real activity. It may be suggestive, but in narrative terms it currently muddies the ending because the reader is not fully convinced this is the best manifestation of climate-relevant adjustment.

### Implications
The implications are:
- national climate defeat need not mean policy stasis;
- federal systems can redirect ambition downward;
- the result may be more fragmented and polarized policy across space.

Those are interesting and believable implications.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
**Serviceable, but not yet fully coherent.** It has a story, but it is trying to tell two stories at once:
1. failed federal policy causes local legislative compensation;
2. failed federal policy affects building modernization.

The first story is strong. The second feels like a result searching for a rationale.

### If it is a collection of results looking for a story, what story should it be telling?
The story should be:

> National climate-policy defeat does not simply reduce ambition; in a federal system, it can reallocate ambition to subnational governments that reveal support for the failed policy. Switzerland’s 2021 referendum provides a clean case showing that policy demand, once blocked nationally, resurfaced locally and produced a more polarized regulatory landscape.

Everything else should serve that story.
