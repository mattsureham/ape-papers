## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether the failure of a national climate policy can perversely accelerate climate action at the subnational level. Using the 2021 rejection of Switzerland’s CO2 Act, it argues that cantons that were more supportive of the failed federal law were much more likely to pass their own climate policies afterward, with suggestive evidence of downstream effects on building activity.

A busy economist should care because the paper is really about a broader question: when does political defeat at the center redirect policy effort rather than kill it? That is a first-order question for climate policy, federalism, and the political economy of reform.

**Does the paper articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Not quite. The current opening is decent, but it drifts too quickly into the Swiss episode and the “boomerang ballot” label before fully stating the general question in a way that feels important beyond Switzerland. The first two paragraphs should more sharply foreground the big idea: **national policy failure may reallocate policy production across levels of government**. Right now the intro reads like an interesting case study; it needs to read like a paper about a general mechanism in federal systems, with Switzerland as the clean test.

**The pitch the paper should have:**

> In federal systems, failed national reforms are usually treated as policy setbacks. But they may also reallocate political effort downward, prompting subnational governments to substitute for the center. This paper studies that mechanism in the context of climate policy, exploiting the unexpected 2021 rejection of Switzerland’s CO2 Act to test whether cantons that most favored the failed national law responded by adopting their own climate policies.
>
> I show that they did: cantons with stronger support for the rejected federal law were substantially more likely to enact climate legislation afterward, with suggestive evidence of corresponding changes in building-sector activity. The paper’s core contribution is to show that national policy failure can generate **compensatory federalism**—not merely slowing reform, but changing where reform occurs.

That is the version that belongs in AER territory. Start with the world question, not the Swiss anecdote.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:**  
The paper claims to provide evidence that the failure of a national climate policy can trigger compensatory subnational policymaking, and that this reallocation of policy effort has observable real effects.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?
Only partially. The paper says “this is the first causal evidence” of compensatory federalism after national policy failure, but the differentiation is still too hand-wavy. It cites broad literatures on climate federalism, direct democracy, and policy diffusion, but it does not yet clearly say:

- what prior papers showed,
- what they could not show,
- and why this design gets at something different.

Right now the newness is something like: “another paper on subnational climate policy, but with a referendum shock and Swiss data.” That is not enough. The author needs a more precise claim: e.g., prior work documents subnational climate leadership in periods of weak federal policy; this paper isolates the **triggering role of a discrete national policy defeat** and ties it to revealed geographic demand for the blocked policy.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or as filling a literature gap?
Mixed, but too often as a literature gap. The strongest version is a world question:

- **When national climate policy fails, is action delayed or displaced?**

That is much stronger than:

- “the compensatory federalism mechanism is well theorized but rarely causally identified.”

The latter is fine as secondary framing, but not as the lead.

### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what's new?
Not yet cleanly. At present they might say:  
“It's a Swiss DiD paper showing pro-climate cantons did more after the CO2 referendum failed.”

That is not fatal, but it means the paper’s novelty is not landing as a general conceptual contribution.

What they should be able to say is:  
“It shows that national policy failure can induce substitution across levels of government, and it measures that with a clean preference shock from a failed referendum.”

### What would make this contribution bigger?
Several possibilities, in descending order of importance:

1. **A stronger outcome tied directly to decarbonization.**  
   The biggest strategic weakness is that the behavioral outcome is new residential construction, which is only loosely linked to climate policy and even the paper admits it is an imperfect proxy. If the paper had direct measures of heat pump adoption, fossil heating replacement, building retrofits, permit composition, emissions, or energy use, the contribution would jump materially.

2. **A clearer mechanism linking referendum support → cantonal law adoption → downstream real outcomes.**  
   Right now the policy-adoption result is more compelling than the construction result. The paper should lean into that and, if possible, build a cleaner mechanism chain. For example: what kinds of laws were passed, how quickly, and through what sectors? Did building-related laws drive the building results, or is this a generic pro-growth/pro-urban pattern?

3. **A broader comparative framing.**  
   The paper would feel bigger if it were more explicitly framed as a model for understanding state responses in the U.S., EU member-state substitution, or multi-level governance after central deadlock. A one-country case can still be AER-worthy if it illuminates a broad mechanism. The paper needs to insist on that.

4. **A sharper distinction between policy substitution and spatial polarization.**  
   The current draft mentions polarization, but somewhat as an afterthought. It might actually be the bigger idea: failed national action can increase policy divergence across places. That has larger welfare and political economy stakes than the construction estimate itself.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Before this paper, the standard intuition is that national policy failure delays or blocks progress. In climate policy especially, failed national reform is usually coded as a setback.

### Tension
But in federal systems, failure at one level of government may induce substitution at another. The puzzle is whether defeat at the center kills policy momentum or redirects it to subnational governments—especially where local demand for the blocked policy is strongest.

### Resolution
In Switzerland, cantons that more strongly supported the failed CO2 Act were much more likely to adopt their own climate laws afterward, with suggestive evidence of increased building activity in those places.

### Implications
National political defeats may not settle policy conflict; they may decentralize it. That changes how economists should think about federalism, climate governance, and the consequences of direct democracy. It also implies that failed national action can deepen geographic polarization rather than produce a uniform pause.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
**Serviceable, but not fully disciplined.** The paper has the ingredients of a strong story, but it is still a bit of a collection of related findings:

- adoption regressions,
- building activity panel estimates,
- placebo referendum,
- placebo outcome,
- discussion of polarization.

The story should be more hierarchical:

1. **Main fact:** national policy failure triggered subnational legislative substitution in pro-policy cantons.
2. **Mechanism:** revealed local demand predicts where substitution occurs.
3. **Consequence:** this substitution has downstream real effects and increases cross-canton divergence.

Right now the paper sometimes acts as though the construction result is the centerpiece. Strategically, I think that is wrong. The **policy-adoption result is the cleaner and more compelling result**; the building outcome is supporting evidence and should be handled as such.

So the story this paper should be telling is not “the referendum increased construction.” It is “the referendum relocated climate policymaking from the federal level to the cantonal level.”

That is a much better story.

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

### What fact would I lead with?
I would lead with:

> “When Switzerland narrowly rejected a national climate law, the cantons that had most supported it were dramatically more likely to pass their own climate laws within two years.”

That is the cleanest and most intuitive fact in the paper.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
They would lean in at first, because the “defeat causes action” angle is inherently interesting and counterintuitive. But interest may fade if the conversation quickly turns into “and then there’s a modest effect on residential building counts.” That second move is weaker and more contestable as a matter of economic significance and interpretation.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Probably one of these:

1. “Is this really about climate policy, or just that green cantons are different in all kinds of ways?”
2. “Did the cantonal laws actually change emissions-relevant behavior?”
3. “How general is this beyond Switzerland?”
4. “Is the real contribution the substitution in policymaking, or the building effect?”

Those are strategic tells. The paper should be organized to answer them.

### If findings are modest: is the null/modest result itself interesting?
The construction result is not null, but it is strategically modest because the outcome is indirect. The paper should not oversell it. It should say: the downstream outcome evidence is suggestive, not definitive, and the central contribution is about policy substitution. That would actually make the paper more credible and sharper.
