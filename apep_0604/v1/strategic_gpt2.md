## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple, important question: when a civil war ends, does human capital recover—and if so, where does recovery show up first? Using Colombia’s 2014–2016 FARC ceasefire/peace process and cross-municipality variation in prior FARC exposure, the paper argues that secondary school enrollment rose in the most conflict-exposed places, suggesting that education may be an early peace dividend, especially when paired with state presence.

Why should a busy economist care? Because the broader question is not “did peace reduce violence?” but “what margins of economic and social life actually restart after conflict?” If the answer is that education rebounds even when local economic activity does not, that is potentially a useful and publishable fact.

**Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?** Not quite. The opening is readable and polished, but it spends its best real estate on scene-setting and a generic literature review rather than the sharpest version of the paper’s claim. The paper’s most promising angle is not “conflict hurts schooling and I study the reverse”; it is: **peace may generate gains first in state-delivered human capital rather than in market activity, and Colombia lets us see that.**

### The pitch the paper should have

A better first two paragraphs would say something like:

> When civil wars end, what recovers first: private economic activity or public human capital formation? Colombia’s FARC ceasefire provides a useful test because the end of violence was national, but exposure to FARC control and attack intensity was highly uneven across municipalities.  
>  
> This paper shows that municipalities with concentrated pre-ceasefire FARC violence experienced meaningful increases in school enrollment after the peace agreement, even as recent work finds little evidence of local economic recovery on margins like nightlights, firm entry, or formal employment. The core message is that peace dividends may appear first in education—especially where the state subsequently expands its presence—rather than in private-sector activity.

That is a much stronger AER-style setup: world question, empirical setting, contrast, and implication.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution in one sentence:**  
The paper’s contribution is to show that in Colombia’s former FARC territories, the end of conflict is associated with increased school enrollment in the most heavily exposed municipalities, suggesting that education may be an early and state-mediated peace dividend.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?

Only partially. The paper differentiates itself from the conflict-and-education literature by focusing on recovery after conflict rather than destruction during conflict, and from **Fajardo et al. / Fajardo (2023)**-type peace-dividend work by emphasizing education rather than economic activity. That is sensible. But the differentiation is still thinner than it needs to be.

Right now, a reader could summarize the paper as: “It’s another reduced-form paper on the Colombian peace process, but with education outcomes.” That is not enough. The introduction needs to distinguish more sharply between:

1. **Conflict harms education** papers  
2. **Peace dividends on economic activity** papers  
3. **State capacity / post-conflict reconstruction** papers  

The current version gestures at all three, but does not fully integrate them.

### Is the contribution framed as a question about the world, or a gap in the literature?

Mostly the former, which is good—but it keeps sliding back into literature-gap language (“first municipality-panel evidence,” “dataset unavailable to earlier researchers,” “methodological point about concentrated violence”). Those are supporting details, not the main event.

The strongest framing is about the world:

- Does peace automatically restore schooling?
- Are peace dividends sector-specific?
- Does the state have to show up for recovery to happen?

That is much stronger than “there is no municipality panel yet.”

### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what’s new?

Not cleanly enough. They might say: “It studies the FARC ceasefire with municipality data and finds enrollment went up in heavily affected places.” That is fine, but not memorable. The paper needs a sharper takeaway, something like:

> “Peace in Colombia did not revive local economic activity, but it did revive schooling in the hardest-hit municipalities—suggesting public human capital may recover before markets do.”

That is a sentence a reader can carry around.

### What would make the contribution bigger?

Several possibilities:

1. **A stronger contrast outcome.**  
   The comparison to null economic effects is potentially the paper’s most valuable framing device, but it is currently outsourced to another paper. It would be much stronger if this paper itself directly showed sectoral heterogeneity: e.g., education recovers, while some local economic outcome in the same data environment does not.

2. **Mechanism evidence.**  
   The current PDET discussion is suggestive, but too soft to bear much narrative weight. The paper would feel bigger if it could distinguish among:
   - return migration,
   - improved school safety,
   - school reopening/supply,
   - teacher presence,
   - targeted public investment.

3. **Learning, not just enrollment.**  
   Enrollment is an okay outcome, but not a top-journal endpoint by itself unless the mechanism or implication is very strong. If the paper could speak to **test scores, grade progression, attendance, age-grade distortion, or completion**, the contribution would feel more substantial.

4. **General framing beyond Colombia.**  
   The paper should be more explicit that this is about **post-conflict recovery of state services** rather than one country/event. AER papers need to feel like they revise how we think about a class of settings.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

### Closest neighbors

The nearest literatures/papers seem to be:

1. **Fajardo (2023)** or related work on the Colombian FARC ceasefire and economic outcomes  
2. **León (2012)** on political violence and education in Peru  
3. **Akresh and de Walque / Akresh et al.** on conflict and schooling in Rwanda  
4. **Shemyakina (2011)** on civil war and girls’ education in Tajikistan  
5. Broader synthesis pieces like **Blattman and Miguel (2010)** on civil war consequences, and perhaps work on state capacity in post-conflict settings such as **Acemoglu, Robinson, and coauthors**

Depending on the exact bibliography, it may also want to engage more directly with papers on:
- displacement and return migration,
- public service delivery in weak states,
- peacebuilding and state presence,
- school access under insecurity.

### How should it position itself relative to those neighbors?

**Build on, don’t attack.** The right positioning is:

- Against the conflict-and-education literature: “We know conflict destroys schooling; much less is known about whether peace reverses that damage.”
- Against the peace-dividend literature: “Existing work often looks for recovery in private economic activity; we show recovery may instead emerge first in state-delivered services.”
- Against the state-capacity literature: “This is consistent with a broader view in which peace is necessary but not sufficient; public recovery depends on state re-entry.”

The paper should not overstate novelty or imply others missed something obvious. It should instead claim that it **connects two conversations that have largely been separate**.

### Is the paper positioned too narrowly or too broadly?

At the moment, **too narrowly in evidence, too broadly in implication**.

- Too narrow because much of the paper is organized as a municipal-level Colombian education study.
- Too broad because it occasionally sounds like it has identified the role of state capacity in post-conflict reconstruction, when what it really has is suggestive timing evidence.

It needs a more disciplined middle ground:
> “This paper provides evidence from Colombia that education can be an early peace dividend, with suggestive indications that complementary state investment matters.”

That is ambitious but credible.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?

The paper seems underconnected to at least two important literatures:

1. **Displacement, return migration, and demographic composition.**  
   The discussion mentions this, but not as a central competing explanation. If enrollment rose because displaced families returned, that is still interesting—but it is a different story from school-system recovery.

2. **Public service delivery and state building in weak/insecure areas.**  
   This is probably the highest-upside literature for the paper. The manuscript hints at it via PDET and Acemoglu/Collier, but it should speak much more directly to questions like:
   - what does the state restore first after conflict?
   - are schools a leading indicator of state re-entry?
   - why might public goods recover faster than private investment?

### Is the paper having the right conversation?

Almost, but not fully. The highest-value conversation is **not** “education in Colombia” per se. It is:

> **What kinds of social and economic recovery does peace produce, and what role does state capacity play in determining the sequence of recovery?**

That framing gives the paper a broader audience: development, political economy, labor/public economics of education, and conflict.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Before this paper, we know civil conflict damages schooling and broader human capital. We also know that many hoped-for “peace dividends” do not immediately show up in conventional economic indicators.

### Tension
What happens after violence ends is less clear. Does schooling simply bounce back once insecurity recedes? Or does recovery require active state rebuilding? And why might education recover even if local economies do not?

### Resolution
The paper finds that school enrollment rises in municipalities with concentrated prior FARC violence, with suggestive evidence that gains are larger after the onset of targeted state investment.

### Implications
The implication is that peace dividends are heterogeneous across sectors, and that education—because it is state-delivered and highly sensitive to local safety—may recover earlier than private economic activity.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

**Serviceable, but not yet fully coherent.** There is a good story in here, but the paper currently reads somewhat like a collection of sensible empirical exercises:

- main DiD,
- binary threshold,
- two-shock decomposition,
- violence first stage,
- robustness table,
- comparison to Fajardo.

The paper needs to commit to one central story and organize everything around it.

### What story should it be telling?

The best story is:

1. Conflict ends.
2. Not all margins recover equally.
3. In Colombia, schooling rebounds in the hardest-hit places.
4. This likely reflects the combination of restored safety and state re-engagement.
5. Therefore, peace dividends are sector-specific and state-mediated.

That story is stronger than “I test whether peace affects education.”

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?

I would say:

> “In Colombia, the FARC peace process seems to have increased school enrollment in the hardest-hit municipalities, even though other work finds basically no local economic recovery.”

That is the hook.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

Some would lean in—**if** the contrast with null economic effects is made front and center. Without that contrast, this risks sounding like a niche paper on schooling in conflict areas. With it, the paper becomes about the anatomy of post-conflict recovery.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Immediately:  
**“Is this real educational recovery, or just returning families re-enrolling children?”**

That is the paper’s key substantive follow-up question. A second one would be:
**“Why education and not jobs or firms?”**

Both are exactly the questions the paper should be organized to answer.

### If findings are modest: is the modest result itself interesting?

Yes, but only under the right framing. A 4.5 percentage point increase in enrollment in a small set of high-conflict municipalities is not earth-shattering on its own. It becomes interesting if cast as evidence that:

- peace dividends are selective,
- state services may recover before markets,
- and the relevant margin is concentrated exposure, not diffuse conflict incidence.

Without that framing, it can feel like a modest positive result from a standard design.

Not enough. The best fact is not front-loaded hard enough. The paper should not make the reader wait to understand that the interesting thing is the contrast between **education recovery** and **economic non-recovery**.

Possibly the **PDET-only restriction** or anything that sharpens the sectoral/state-capacity interpretation, if it is genuinely informative. But more important than moving tables is clarifying the conceptual role of each result.

Mostly a **scope + ambition problem**, with some **framing problem**.

One of two upgrades:

1. **Mechanism upgrade:** show whether the enrollment response comes from return migration, school reopening, teacher allocation, reduced recruitment risk, or targeted public investment.

As written, the paper hints at both but fully delivers neither.

If the author can change only one thing:

> **Reframe the paper around the broader claim that peace restores state-delivered human capital before it restores private economic activity, and reorganize all evidence around that contrast.**

That is the version with a shot at wider interest.
