## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether peace restores human capital in places scarred by civil conflict, using the 2014–2016 FARC ceasefire and peace agreement in Colombia. The core claim is that education recovered in municipalities with concentrated prewar FARC violence, and that the gains appear larger once peace was paired with targeted state investment—suggesting that “peace dividends” in human capital are not automatic, but mediated by state capacity.

A busy economist should care because this is a broad question, not a Colombia-only one: when violence ends, what rebounds first, and under what conditions? That question matters for conflict, development, public economics, and state capacity.

**Does the paper articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?** Not quite. The current opening is reasonably strong, but it quickly slips into a literature-summary mode and only later reveals the sharper claim: that the paper is really about whether peace alone is enough to rebuild schooling, or whether peace needs the state. That is the interesting hook. The current intro also foregrounds design details too early.

**What the first two paragraphs should say instead:**

> Civil wars do not only destroy lives and assets; they interrupt the accumulation of human capital. But we know much less about the reverse question: when conflict ends, does schooling recover on its own, or only when peace is followed by an effective state presence? This is a first-order question for post-conflict policy, because governments and donors routinely assume that once violence falls, children will return to school.
>
> This paper studies that question in Colombia after the FARC ceasefire and peace agreement. Using municipality-level variation in pre-ceasefire FARC presence, I show that secondary enrollment rose in Colombia’s genuine conflict zones after the guns fell silent, with suggestively larger gains after targeted post-conflict state investment began. The broader message is that peace can generate a human-capital dividend, but the dividend is concentrated where violence had truly constrained schooling and may depend on the state converting security into service delivery.

That is the pitch the paper should have. It leads with the world question, the stakes, the empirical setting, and the main takeaway.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:**  
The paper argues that the Colombian peace process increased school enrollment in municipalities heavily exposed to FARC violence, and that educational recovery may have been amplified by subsequent targeted state investment.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?
Only partly. The paper does one thing well strategically: it differentiates itself from work showing limited economic peace dividends in Colombia, especially Fajardo et al./fajardo2023peace. But beyond that, the contribution is still a bit mushy. Right now it reads as a combination of:
1. a Colombia application,
2. a new municipal panel,
3. a peace-versus-investment decomposition,
4. a point about concentrated versus diffuse treatment.

Those are not all equally important. The paper needs to decide which is the real contribution.

My view: the strongest contribution is **not** “first municipality-panel evidence” and not “methodological point about thresholds.” Those sound incremental. The strongest contribution is the **substantive claim about the nature of post-conflict recovery**: education can recover after peace even when conventional economic indicators do not, and this recovery may require the state to translate security gains into actual service provision.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a gap in a literature?
It starts with a world question, which is good, but then repeatedly retreats into gap-filling language: “first municipality-panel evidence,” “dataset unavailable to earlier researchers,” “complements X.” That weakens the ambition. AER papers usually ask readers to update their beliefs about how the world works, not just about what one more dataset allows.

The paper should frame itself as answering:

- **Does peace rebuild human capital?**
- **Are peace dividends sector-specific?**
- **Is violence reduction enough, or does recovery require active state capacity?**

Those are world questions. The “new panel” is a means, not the contribution.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the intro?
Right now, maybe, but not crisply. They might say: “It’s a DiD on Colombian peace and schooling with some suggestive evidence on PDET.” That is not enough. You want them to say: “Interesting—economic recovery after conflict may stall, but schooling rebounds, especially when the state follows peace with targeted investment.”

That version is memorable.

### What would make this contribution bigger?
Several possibilities:

1. **Lean harder into sectoral heterogeneity in peace dividends.**  
   Right now the comparison to null economic effects is one paragraph. It could be the paper’s central organizing idea: peace did not revive markets, but it did revive schooling. Why? Because education is a state-delivered good, while investment depends on private confidence and institutions. That comparison makes the paper larger.

2. **Sharpen the state-capacity mechanism.**  
   The PDET discussion is suggestive but thin. As currently presented, it is not persuasive enough to carry a major mechanism claim, but it is central to the paper’s story. Either strengthen it conceptually and empirically, or demote it. In strategic terms, the paper is trying to get a lot of mileage out of a fuzzy second act.

3. **Focus on educational recovery, not just enrollment.**  
   Enrollment gains are useful, but not by themselves a top-journal hook unless they speak to a broader conceptual point. The approval-rate decline hints at a quantity-quality tradeoff; that could be important if developed. If the paper could show that peace changed not just enrollment but actual participation, progression, age-grade distortion, or school inputs, the contribution would feel more substantive.

4. **Reframe “concentrated violence” less as a methodological point and more as a conceptual one.**  
   The meaningful peace dividend occurs in places where armed rule actually constrained daily life. That is a substantive insight about conflict exposure, not just a coding choice.

That is the unexpected but valuable bridge: conflict economics + public economics of service delivery + state capacity.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Conflict destroys schooling. Colombia endured a long insurgency, and the FARC ceasefire created a major national break in violence. Policymakers often assume peace should generate dividends.

### Tension
But peace dividends are empirically elusive, especially on conventional economic metrics. So the motivating puzzle is: if economic activity does not rebound after violence ends, does anything important recover? And is peace itself enough, or must the state actively rebuild?

### Resolution
The paper finds that school enrollment rises in municipalities with concentrated prior FARC violence, with suggestively larger effects after targeted state investment begins. Education appears more responsive to peace than standard local economic indicators.

### Implications
Post-conflict recovery may be sector-specific. Human capital can recover even when private economic activity does not, and recovery may depend on converting security gains into public service delivery. That matters for post-conflict policy design and for how economists conceptualize “peace dividends.”

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
It has the ingredients, but the arc is still loose. Right now it feels like:
- a conflict-and-education paper,
- plus a Colombia peace paper,
- plus a PDET note,
- plus a treatment-threshold point.

That is too many partially developed stories.

**The paper should tell one story:**
> Peace does not generate a uniform local boom. In Colombia, the first meaningful dividend of peace in former conflict zones was educational recovery, and that recovery appears strongest where reduced violence was followed by state presence.

Everything else should support that story.

The current manuscript is closest to a collection of sensible results looking for one dominant frame. It needs a more disciplined hierarchy:
1. Main result: schooling recovers in hard-hit places.
2. Contrast: unlike economic activity, education responds.
3. Interpretation: public-service recovery is a distinct peace dividend.
4. Secondary mechanism: state investment may magnify it.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?
“After the FARC ceasefire, school enrollment rose in Colombia’s hardest-hit municipalities even though other papers find little local economic recovery.”

That is the hook.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
They would lean in—**if** presented that way. “Another DiD on Colombian municipalities” is phone-reaching material. “Peace didn’t revive firms, but it did bring children back to school” is much better.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Almost certainly: **Why education and not the economy?**  
And then: **Is it because schools are public goods and the state stepped in, or because families simply returned?**

That is exactly where the paper should want the conversation to go.

### If the findings are modest, is the result itself interesting?
Yes, but only if framed properly. A 4.5 percentage point increase in secondary enrollment in the most affected municipalities is not earth-shattering on its own. Its importance comes from what it says conceptually: some post-conflict gains show up first in public-service utilization, not in market activity.

The PDET evidence is modest and imprecise. That is fine if presented as an interpretive extension, not as a headline causal mechanism. Right now the paper wants the reader to believe something fairly strong about state capacity from evidence that is, by its own admission, suggestive. That overreach creates avoidable skepticism.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

At present, the gap is mainly **framing and ambition**, with a secondary **scope** issue.

### Is it a framing problem?
Yes, substantially. The paper has a potentially interesting message but is presenting itself as a careful Colombia application rather than a paper about how post-conflict recovery works. That is underselling the contribution.

### Is it a scope problem?
Also yes. The main result is a bit narrow as currently packaged. To feel AER-level, the paper needs either:
- a stronger conceptual mechanism,
- a broader set of educational outcomes that clearly illuminate recovery,
- or a sharper comparative frame that makes the Colombia result teach us something general.

### Is it a novelty problem?
Moderately. Conflict affects schooling is old news. Peace affecting schooling is newer, but still not enough by itself. The novelty has to be the **type of peace dividend** and the role of public service delivery/state presence. Without that, it risks feeling like “the reverse sign of an existing literature.”

### Is it an ambition problem?
Yes. The paper is competent but safe. It seems content to establish a positive enrollment effect and call it a day. To interest the top people in the field, it needs to make readers feel that this changes how we think about post-conflict recovery—specifically, that the earliest or most plausible gains from peace may show up in publicly provided human capital rather than private economic activity.

### Single most impactful advice
**Reframe the paper around a broader claim: peace dividends are sector-specific, and schooling is an early margin of recovery because peace reduces insecurity and allows the state to re-enter—then organize the entire introduction and discussion around that claim rather than around the existence of a new municipal panel.**
