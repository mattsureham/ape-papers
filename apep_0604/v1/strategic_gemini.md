## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
The paper examines the "peace dividend" in education following the 2016 Colombian peace agreement, using municipality-level variation in historical conflict intensity to identify effects. It finds that while aggregate economic indicators often remain stagnant after conflict (the "elusive peace dividend"), secondary school enrollment significantly increases in former conflict hotspots, particularly when initial safety is followed by targeted state investment.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates the "what" clearly, but the "why care" is currently framed as a narrow response to a single 2023 paper (*Fajardo-Gonzalez & Steinhauser*). 
**The Pitch the paper should have:** "Economists have long documented the human capital costs of war, yet we know surprisingly little about the speed and conditions of recovery after peace. This paper shows that while private investment may stay on the sidelines for decades, state-led educational recovery can begin almost immediately—provided the state 'shows up' with both security and infrastructure. It provides a blueprint for understanding when and where peace dividends actually materialize."

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**One-sentence contribution:** The paper identifies that education, as a state-provided service, serves as a leading indicator of post-conflict recovery in areas where private economic activity remains stagnant.

- **Differentiated?** Yes, by moving from the "cost of war" literature to the "recovery from war" literature and focusing on the interaction between peace and state capacity.
- **World vs. Literature?** Currently leans too heavily on "filling a gap in the FARC literature." To be AER-worthy, it must frame itself as a study of **State Capacity and Reconstruction.**
- **DDA or Smart?** Currently risks being seen as "another DiD on FARC."
- **Make it bigger:** The "Two-Shock Decomposition" is the paper's most AER-like feature. To make it bigger, the author should focus less on the 4.5pt increase and more on the **cross-sectoral divergence** (Why does education recover while firms do not?).

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
The paper sits at the intersection of Development and Political Economy.
- **Neighbors:** *León (2012)*, *Akresh & de Walque (2012)*, *Acemoglu et al. (2015)*, and *Fajardo-Gonzalez & Steinhauser (2023)*.
- **Strategy:** It should move from "complementing" Fajardo-Gonzalez to "reconciling" a broader tension in development: the lag between institutional change (peace) and economic outcomes.
- **Missing Literature:** It needs more on **State Capacity**. It cites Acemoglu (2015) but doesn't fully engage with the theoretical tension of whether the state *can* effectively provide services in "captured" territories.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC
- **Setup:** War destroys human capital.
- **Tension:** Peace agreements are signed, but "peace dividends" (nighttime lights, GDP) are often invisible in the data. Is peace a failure, or are we looking at the wrong variables?
- **Resolution:** Education recovers because it is a state-led public good, whereas the economy lags because it requires private trust.
- **Implications:** Reconstruction policy should prioritize state-led service delivery as a bridge to private sector confidence.

**Evaluation:** The arc is present but buried in the results section. The "Two-Shock" story (Peace vs. PDET investment) should be the stars of the show, not just an exploratory decomposition.

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
- **The Lead Fact:** "When the guns fell silent in Colombia, schools filled up long before the lights came on or the factories opened."
- **Reaction:** People lean in. It challenges the "elusive dividend" narrative and offers a specific mechanism (State vs. Private).
- **Follow-up:** "Is it just people returning from displacement, or are the schools actually better?" This is where the paper needs to be bulletproof.

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
- **Front-loading:** The distinction between "Safety" (2016-17) and "Investment" (2018+) is the most interesting part but appears late. This should be integrated into the main conceptual framework.
- **Appendix:** The "Standardized Effect Sizes" (Table 6) are very helpful for cross-study comparison; keep these prominent.
- **The "Approval Rate" decline:** This is a fascinating result (quantity-quality tradeoff). It deserves its own subsection in the Discussion rather than being a footnote to the enrollment results.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The paper is currently a very high-quality *Journal of Development Economics* (JDE) paper. To move to AER, it needs to be more than a program evaluation of the FARC peace deal. 

**The Gap:** Ambition. It treats the Colombia case as the end goal. 
**The Advice:** Elevate the "Two-Shock Decomposition" from a secondary result to the core theoretical contribution. Frame the paper as a study on the **Hierarchy of Recovery**: identifying which public goods respond to "negative shocks" (the absence of violence) versus "positive shocks" (the presence of the state).
