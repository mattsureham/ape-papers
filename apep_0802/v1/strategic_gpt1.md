## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper studies a distinctive housing-tax reform in New Zealand: mortgage interest deductibility was removed for existing rental properties but retained for new builds. The paper asks whether this tax wedge changed what gets built—shifting construction away from stand-alone houses and toward denser multi-unit housing—and argues that targeted tax policy can reshape the composition of housing supply even if it does not raise total construction.

A busy economist should care because the paper is really about a broader question: can tax policy redirect housing investment toward the kinds of units policymakers say they want more of? That question travels well beyond New Zealand.

### Does the paper articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Not quite. The current introduction starts with institutional detail and only gradually gets to the actual economic question. The paper knows what it is about, but it does not present the question in the sharpest, most portable way soon enough. The first paragraphs currently read like “here is a reform” rather than “here is a big economic question for which this reform is an unusually informative test.”

### What the first two paragraphs should say instead

Something like:

> Housing policy often aims not just to increase how much gets built, but to change what gets built: fewer detached homes, more dense housing suitable for renters and growing cities. Yet we know surprisingly little about whether tax policy can redirect housing investment along this composition margin, as opposed to simply raising or lowering aggregate activity.
>
> This paper studies a sharp test of that question from New Zealand. In 2021, the government eliminated mortgage interest deductibility for existing rental properties while preserving it for new builds, creating a large tax advantage for newly constructed rental housing. I show that this policy shifted the composition of new housing supply toward multi-unit dwellings, and that this shift partially reversed when deductibility was later restored. The broader implication is that tax incentives can reallocate housing production toward denser forms of supply.

That is the pitch. It starts with the world, not the statute book.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### Contribution in one sentence

The paper shows that a tax preference for newly built rental properties can reallocate housing construction toward denser multi-unit dwellings, even without clear evidence of an increase in total housing supply.

Only partially. The paper distinguishes itself from broad work on mortgage interest deductions and housing demand, but the differentiation is still mostly at the level of setting and policy novelty (“first evidence from New Zealand”) rather than at the level of economic insight. Those are reasonable selling points, but they do not yet clearly establish why this changes what we know about housing economics in a first-order way.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or filling a gap in a LITERATURE?

Mixed, but too often the latter. The strongest version is world-facing: **Can tax incentives push private capital toward denser forms of housing supply?** The weaker version is literature-gap framing: **There is no causal evidence yet on New Zealand’s reform.** The paper leans too much on the second.

For AER, the contribution must be: this tells us something general about how housing markets work, not merely that one reform in one country had one effect.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?

They could explain it, but they might still summarize it as “a DiD paper on a New Zealand tax reform that shifted permits toward multi-unit housing.” That is competent, but not memorable. The introduction does not yet force the reader to see this as a paper about **the composition margin in housing supply** and **tax design as a tool for changing urban form**.

### What would make the contribution bigger?

Several possibilities:

1. **Connect composition to socially meaningful outcomes.**  
   Right now the key outcome is building consents by dwelling type. That is one step removed from the big stakes. The contribution would be much bigger if the paper could show implications for:
   - total dwelling counts rather than structures,
   - bedroom counts / floor area,
   - completions rather than consents,
   - rental stock additions,
   - rents or affordability,
   - spatial density in high-demand locations.

2. **Clarify whether this is about density, rental supply, or investor demand.**  
   The paper currently blends these together. A bigger paper would pin down which margin matters:
   - denser housing,
   - more rental-oriented housing,
   - more units per lot,

3. **Make the comparison economically richer.**  
   “Multi-unit vs stand-alone houses” is useful but somewhat blunt. A stronger paper would distinguish apartments, townhouses, small infill, etc., and show whether the response is concentrated where planning constraints and investor demand actually intersect.

4. **Frame the question around policy design.**  
   The bigger question is not “did this reform matter?” but “when policymakers want more housing, should they subsidize new supply rather than subsidize housing demand broadly?” That is a more ambitious conversation.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Governments want more housing and often say they want denser, more rental-oriented housing in particular. But evidence on whether tax policy can steer supply toward those forms is limited.

### Tension

Most work on housing tax policy focuses on prices, homeownership, or total activity—not the composition of supply. New Zealand’s reform created a rare policy wedge favoring new rental construction over existing rental assets, potentially changing what gets built. But it is not obvious ex ante whether such a tax wedge would matter once macro conditions and planning constraints dominate.

### Resolution

The paper finds that during the period when new builds had preferential tax treatment, multi-unit consents rose relative to stand-alone houses, and this relative shift partially unwound when deductibility was restored.

### Implications

Tax policy may be able to reallocate housing production toward denser forms of development. But the paper also implies limits: this is composition more than quantity, and perhaps temporary rather than transformational.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the ingredients, but the arc is only **serviceable**, not compelling. Right now the paper is a bit too much “policy happened, here’s a design, here are some results.” It is not yet telling a sharp story with a real intellectual payoff.

The story it should be telling is:

> Housing policy has a neglected composition margin. New Zealand offers a clean test of whether targeted tax advantages for new rental housing can push developers/investors toward denser housing forms. They can—but mainly by reallocating within the housing pipeline, not obviously by expanding the total frontier of supply.

That is the story. Everything should serve it.

At present, parts of the paper still feel like a collection of empirics:
- main DiD by dwelling type,
- dosage version,
- cross-TA null,
- reversal episode,
without a fully unified narrative about which margin of economic behavior is central.

The cross-TA null in particular reads more like “we also tried this” than an essential part of the story.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?

“When New Zealand stopped landlords from deducting mortgage interest on existing rentals but exempted new builds, the mix of permitted housing shifted sharply toward multi-unit dwellings.”

That is the headline fact.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

Some would lean in, especially urban and public finance economists. But many would still ask: “Okay, but did it actually increase housing supply, rents, or affordability?” If the answer remains “not shown,” attention will fade quickly.

This is the core strategic problem. The current main result is interesting, but it is one margin removed from the outcome most economists care about.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Almost certainly one of these:
1. Did total housing supply rise, or did builders just swap houses for townhouses?
2. Did this affect rents or the rental stock?
3. Was the effect bigger in constrained or high-demand places?
4. Are multi-unit permits really a good proxy for investor-oriented rental supply?

If the paper cannot answer any of these, it needs to foreground why the composition margin itself is substantively important.

### If the findings are modest: is the result itself interesting?

Yes, but only if the paper makes the case better. A null on total construction combined with a strong compositional effect could still be publishable in a good field journal because it teaches us that tax policy changes the type of housing more readily than the quantity. That is informative.

But for AER, the paper needs to make the null-or-modest side of the story feel like a substantive lesson, not a limitation. Right now it still feels somewhat like the paper is studying composition because quantity is harder to show. That is dangerous. The paper has to persuade the reader that composition is the economically central margin, not the available one.

Primarily a **scope and ambition problem**, secondarily a **framing problem**.

- **Not mainly an identification problem** for present purposes.

- The deeper issue is that the paper currently offers a neat result on a fairly narrow policy/outcome pair: one country, one reform, one compositional measure.

For AER, the top people in this field would want one of two things:

1. **A much bigger empirical payoff**  
   e.g., evidence that the reform changed total unit creation, rental supply, urban density, or rents.

2. **A much sharper conceptual payoff**

Right now the paper is in between: more than a policy note, less than a field-defining statement.

Yes, in part. The paper undersells the broad question and oversells the policy novelty. Better framing would help a lot.

Yes, substantially. The main outcome—consent composition—is too narrow by itself for AER unless linked to a larger welfare-relevant margin.

Somewhat. The exact setting is novel, but the general idea that tax incentives change investment composition is not. So the paper needs either stronger evidence or a more original conceptual angle.

### Is it an ambition problem?

Yes. This is the biggest issue. The paper is careful and sensible, but it feels safe. It asks a modest question and answers it cleanly. AER papers usually either ask a bigger question or use a narrower question to overturn a broader belief.

**Reframe and expand the paper around the economically central question—whether targeted tax incentives change not just what housing is permitted, but the amount and welfare-relevant form of supply—and bring at least one more consequential outcome to the forefront.**

If they can only change one thing, that is it. Either make composition feel first-order, or connect it to a first-order outcome.
