## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper examines how a massive, temporary tax wedge—the removal of mortgage interest deductibility for existing rental properties but not for new builds—impacts the *composition* of housing supply. Using New Zealand’s 2021 reform and its subsequent 2024 reversal, the author finds that developers shifted production toward investor-friendly multi-unit dwellings (apartments and townhouses) by 42% relative to stand-alone houses. This is a rare, symmetric natural experiment showing that tax policy can effectively "steer" the housing pipeline toward density, though the effect partially unwinds when the incentive vanishes.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this well in paragraphs 1 and 3, but paragraph 2 gets bogged down in NZ-specific institutional details (600,000 properties, specific dates).
**The "Better" Pitch:** The first two paragraphs should lead with the universal economic tension: *Can tax policy shift the physical composition of a city’s housing stock without impacting the aggregate quantity? Most research on the Mortgage Interest Deduction (MID) focuses on homeownership rates; this paper exploits a unique "new-build exemption" in New Zealand to show that targeting the tax wedge at the supply-entry point can rapidly pivot a national construction pipeline toward higher density.*

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**The Contribution:** The paper identifies a high elasticity of supply *composition* (rather than just aggregate quantity) in response to differential tax treatment of new versus existing capital.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It stands apart from Hilber & Turner (2014) or Glaeser & Shapiro (2003) because it’s not about "to buy or not to buy," but "what to build." It differentiates from the "supply elasticity" literature (Saiz, 2010) by focusing on the *type* of unit.
*   **Framing:** It is currently framed as "answering a question about the world" (Composition vs. Quantity), which is strong.
*   **Clarity:** A smart economist would say: "It’s a DiD on unit types showing that if you tax old rentals but not new ones, you get a ton of townhouses."
*   **Bigger Contribution:** To make this a "Top 5" paper, it needs to address **capital misallocation.** Does this tax-induced shift toward multi-units result in "deadweight density"—buildings that wouldn't exist without the tax dodge—and does that lead to lower-quality or poorly located housing?

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

*   **Closest Neighbors:** Glaeser & Gyourko (2005) on supply constraints; Hilber & Turner (2014) on MID; Poterba (1984) on tax-subsidized housing.
*   **Strategy:** The paper should **synthesize** the "Public Finance/Tax" literature with the "Urban/Supply" literature. Currently, it sits more in Public Finance. It needs to speak more to the Urbanists about how tax policy acts as a "shadow zoning" tool.
*   **Missing Conversations:** The paper misses the "Investment-led Gentrification" or "Institutional Investor" literature. Since the mechanism is "investor demand," it should link to how tax policy facilitates the "corporatization" of the rental stock (e.g., Garriga et al.).
*   **The Right Conversation:** The most impactful framing is: **Tax policy as Urban Planning.** If the government wants density, they don't just need to change zoning; they can change the tax-deductibility of the mortgage.

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

*   **Setup:** Housing supply is usually viewed as a monolithic block constrained by geography or zoning.
*   **Tension:** Governments want to increase density and supply without blowing up the fiscal budget or facing NIMBY backlash against zoning changes.
*   **Resolution:** NZ created a massive tax advantage for new multi-unit builds. Construction shifted 42% toward these units almost overnight.
*   **Implications:** Tax policy is a highly surgical tool for urban form. However, because the effect reversed when the policy did, it suggests these aren't permanent shifts in developer "learning-by-doing," but a transient response to a price signal.

**Evaluation:** The arc is clear. It’s a "clean" story, which is its greatest strength.

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

*   **The Lead Fact:** "New Zealand changed one tax rule and saw the multi-unit share of its national construction pipeline jump from 47% to 57% in two years."
*   **Reaction:** People will lean in. It’s a massive effect size for a tax change.
*   **Follow-up:** "Did it actually make rent cheaper, or did it just change *who* owned the houses?" This is the paper's weak point—it lacks the rental price data.

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

*   **Front-loading:** The "Dosage" specification (Table 2, Col 2) is actually more interesting than the binary DiD because it shows the "marginal" effect of the tax wedge. It should be elevated.
*   **Appendix:** The Cross-TA results (Table 3) are a null result. While "economically interpretable," they break the momentum of the supply-shift story. Move them to the appendix.
*   **Mechanisms:** The "Discussion" (Section 6) needs to be a "Mechanism" section. Use the 2024 reversal more aggressively to prove it was the tax and not a secular trend in "living in townhouses."
