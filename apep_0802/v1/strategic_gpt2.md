## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper studies a New Zealand tax reform that removed mortgage interest deductibility for existing rental properties while exempting new builds, thereby favoring newly constructed rental housing. The core claim is that this tax wedge shifted the composition of housing supply toward denser, multi-unit dwellings and that part of this shift reversed when deductibility was restored.

A busy economist should care because the paper speaks to a first-order policy question: can tax policy change not just how much housing gets built, but what kind of housing gets built? That is a live issue in housing, public finance, and urban economics, especially where governments want more units without directly relaxing land-use constraints.

**Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Not quite. The current opening is competent, but it spends too much time narrating the reform before landing the economic question. The sharper pitch is not “New Zealand changed a tax rule,” but “Can tax incentives tilt the housing production margin toward denser forms?” That should be the lead.

**What the first two paragraphs should say instead:**

> Housing policy debates usually ask whether tax incentives raise house prices, homeownership, or total construction. But many governments care just as much about the *composition* of supply: do policies produce more stand-alone houses, or more apartments and townhouses that add units in constrained cities? This paper asks whether a targeted tax incentive can shift housing production toward denser, rental-oriented forms of new construction.
>
> I study New Zealand’s 2021–24 mortgage interest deductibility reform, which removed deductibility for existing rental properties while preserving it for new builds. This created a sharp tax advantage for newly built investment properties. Using within-region comparisons of multi-unit versus stand-alone housing permits, I show that the reform substantially increased the relative share of multi-unit construction, and that this shift partly unwound when deductibility was restored. The broader implication is that housing tax policy can reallocate supply across dwelling types even when aggregate construction is moving for other macroeconomic reasons.

That is the paper’s strongest version of itself.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution in one sentence:**  
The paper argues that a tax preference for new-build rental properties can reallocate housing production toward denser, multi-unit construction rather than stand-alone houses.

That is a legitimate contribution. The problem is that it is not yet differentiated sharply enough from adjacent literatures, and the paper sometimes frames itself as “the first paper on this New Zealand reform” rather than as answering a broader question about how housing tax policy shapes the form of supply.

### Is the contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?
Only partially. The paper cites broad neighbors, but the differentiation is still vague. Right now the novelty sounds like a bundle of modest claims:
- first causal evidence on this NZ reform,
- focus on composition rather than total quantity,
- within-country design rather than cross-country comparisons.

That is fine as a seminar introduction, but for AER the reader needs a cleaner “this paper changes what we know” statement.

The key differentiator should be:

1. **Most prior tax papers ask about prices, ownership, or aggregate activity.**
2. **This paper isolates a specific, policy-relevant supply margin: the type of housing produced.**
3. **The reform creates unusually direct variation between old and new rental investment, making it possible to observe compositional supply responses.**

That is stronger than “there is no paper on New Zealand yet.”

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a gap in a literature?
Too much as a literature gap. “First causal evidence on New Zealand” is intrinsically local and weak. The stronger framing is world-facing: **when governments subsidize new-build investment, they may not raise total construction much, but they can redirect the housing pipeline toward higher-unit forms.**

That is a real claim about the world.

### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain what’s new?
At present, maybe, but not cleanly. They would likely say:  
“It's a DiD on a New Zealand tax reform showing more multi-unit consents relative to houses.”

That is not enough. You want them to say:  
“It shows that tax treatment affects the composition of housing supply, not just prices or total quantity, and that this margin is economically important in constrained housing markets.”

### What would make this contribution bigger?
Several possibilities:

1. **Link composition to actual unit creation.**  
   The paper hints at this but does not cash it out. If multi-unit means more dwelling units per parcel, then the contribution becomes much bigger: tax policy changes *effective housing capacity*, not merely style.

2. **Show who changed behavior: developers, investors, or locations.**  
   Right now the mechanism is inferred from the dwelling-type comparison. Stronger evidence on investor-facing channels would enlarge the contribution.

3. **Connect to urban form and welfare.**  
   The paper says welfare is ambiguous. True, but that also lets the contribution shrink. If it can say something concrete about unit counts, density, affordability, or land efficiency, it becomes a much more consequential paper.

4. **Generalize beyond New Zealand.**  
   The reform is local; the question is global. The paper needs to emphasize that many countries debate negative gearing, landlord deductibility, or new-build incentives. The NZ case is valuable because it cleanly reveals a margin that likely exists elsewhere.

The highest-return upgrade is probably not another specification; it is **making “composition of supply” economically bite harder** by tying it to unit counts, density, or rental stock implications.

The right audience is not “everyone interested in taxation” nor only “New Zealand housing policy people.” It is:

Almost, but not quite. Right now it is having a “tax reform in New Zealand” conversation. The better conversation is:

**How do tax incentives interact with housing market frictions to shape the form of new supply?**

That is a better AER conversation because it links public finance and urban economics. The unexpected but fruitful connection here is not to another narrow policy evaluation, but to the broad question of **how tax systems shape real allocation when direct quantity responses are muted.**

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Governments often use housing tax policy to influence housing markets, but existing evidence mostly focuses on prices, ownership, or aggregate construction. Meanwhile, cities care deeply about whether new supply comes as stand-alone houses or higher-density units.

### Tension
Aggregate construction is buffeted by macro forces, and it is hard to tell whether tax policy changes total supply. But the New Zealand reform created a sharp and unusual distinction: new rental investment remained tax-favored while existing rental property did not. The question is whether that changed the composition of what got built.

### Resolution
The paper finds that multi-unit consents rose relative to stand-alone house consents during the period when the tax wedge was in place, and that the effect partially reversed when deductibility was restored.

### Implications
Tax policy can shape housing form, not just volume. That matters because many policymakers care about unit density, rental-oriented supply, and the ability to expand housing capacity in constrained markets.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
It has the ingredients, but the arc is not yet fully disciplined. At times it reads like a competent set of results with several plausible framings:
- first causal evidence on NZ reform,
- tax policy and housing supply composition,
- a nice reversal design,
- a within-market DiD,
- an investor-demand story.

These are all related, but the paper has not decided which is the main story. For AER, it needs one dominant narrative:

**When total housing activity is noisy, tax policy may still reshape the composition of supply—and that margin may be one of the most policy-relevant ones.**

Everything else should serve that story.

The cross-TA null result is fine, but it currently feels like an auxiliary result in search of purpose. If included, it should explicitly reinforce the narrative: **the relevant margin is form, not geography.** Otherwise it risks looking like a failed second design.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?
“New Zealand created a tax advantage for newly built rental properties, and multi-unit housing permits jumped relative to stand-alone houses—then partly fell back when the tax advantage disappeared.”

That is a decent opening fact.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
A subset would lean in—especially urban, housing, and public finance people. The average economist might not, unless the framing becomes more universal. “NZ permits shifted by dwelling type” is not, by itself, an AER-level dinner-party hook. “Tax policy can change the form of supply even when it doesn’t clearly raise aggregate supply” is much more interesting.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Immediately:  
“Does that mean more housing units overall, or just fewer detached homes and more apartments?”  

That is the key question because it goes directly to economic significance. The paper anticipates it but does not answer it. That is strategically the biggest vulnerability in the current version.

If the answer remains unknown, the paper must make the case that the composition margin is independently important:
- for unit counts,
- for rental availability,
- for land use efficiency,
- for urban affordability.

Without that, the result risks sounding like a relabeling exercise.

### If findings are modest or partial
The findings are not null, but they are still modest in the sense that the paper does not show a total supply effect. That is okay—**if** the paper embraces that this is a paper about reallocation, not expansion, and argues why reallocation is first-order. Right now it sort of does, but not forcefully enough.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

Bluntly: in its current form, this is **not yet an AER paper**. It is a competent housing/public finance policy evaluation with a plausible empirical design and a clean institutional setting. But AER needs either a bigger question, a sharper conceptual contribution, or stronger evidence that the result changes how economists think.

### What is the gap?

#### Mostly a framing problem
The science may be fine, but the story is too small. “New Zealand tax reform changed permit composition” is field-journal material. “Tax policy changes the form of housing supply, which is a central margin for affordability and urban density” is closer to top-journal material.

#### Also a scope problem
The paper stops one step too early. It shows a compositional shift, but not why that shift matters quantitatively in terms economists care about. If multi-unit means more units, lower land consumption, more rental-oriented stock, or different affordability effects, the paper must do more to demonstrate it.

#### Some novelty problem
The identification may be locally clean, but the conceptual novelty is incremental unless composition is shown to be economically central. Otherwise readers will say: “Okay, investors favored townhouses over detached houses when tax rules changed.” That is believable, but not obviously transformative.

#### An ambition problem
The paper is cautious and tidy, but safe. It accepts a modest contribution rather than pushing hard on the larger implication. For AER, the author needs to be more ambitious about the question.

### Single most impactful advice
**Reframe the paper around the economically consequential margin—how tax incentives change housing capacity through supply composition—and then provide direct evidence or stronger argument that the shift toward multi-unit construction meaningfully alters unit production, density, or rental supply.**

That is the one thing. If the author can make the compositional shift matter in a first-order way, the paper becomes much more than a New Zealand policy note.
