## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
This paper exploits a unique policy experiment—Mexico’s 2022 abolition of Daylight Saving Time (DST) with specific border exemptions—to test whether an extra hour of evening darkness increases crime. Using a triple-difference design that compares neighboring municipalities on different clocks, the author finds a precise null effect across all crime categories, directly challenging the established "darkness-enables-crime" consensus from U.S. data. This is a high-stakes external validity test for a canonical finding in the economics of crime.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this pitch very clearly. The first two paragraphs effectively set up the "bedrock finding" (Doleac & Sanders) and the "unique institutional feature" that allows for a cleaner spatial test than previous temporal studies. The pitch is already at an AER level of clarity.

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**One-sentence contribution:** The paper provides a well-powered, quasi-experimental null result showing that the ambient-light-crime relationship does not generalize to a middle-income country dominated by different criminal structures (organized crime).

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It differentiates itself well from Doleac & Sanders (2015) by shifting from temporal to spatial variation and from a U.S. to a Latin American context.
*   **Framing:** It is framed as a question about the **WORLD** (does light matter everywhere?) rather than just filling a gap.
*   **Clarity:** A smart economist would immediately understand this as "The 'Darkness-Crime' result fails in Mexico."
*   **Growth Potential:** To make the contribution "bigger," the paper needs to move beyond "it didn't happen" to a more rigorous exploration of **why**. Specifically, demonstrating that the share of crime attributable to organized vs. opportunistic actors drives this difference would elevate the paper from a "failed replication" to a "new theory of context."

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
The paper sits at the intersection of the **Economics of Crime** (Doleac & Sanders, 2015; Chalfin et al., 2022) and **Development Economics/Latin American Crime** (Dell, 2015; Blattman et al., 2021).

*   **Closest Neighbors:** Doleac & Sanders (2015); Chalfin et al. (2022); Dell (2015); Blattman et al. (2021).
*   **Strategy:** It uses a "Boundaries of the Phenomenon" approach. It doesn't attack the U.S. findings but demonstrates that the mechanism is context-dependent.
*   **Missing Conversations:** The paper could lean more into the **Logistics of Crime**. If organized crime follows "shipment schedules," as the author posits, is there literature on the "industrial organization of cartels" that would support this?

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC
*   **Setup:** We think darkness causes crime because it provides anonymity.
*   **Tension:** Mexico abolished DST, creating a massive "darkness" treatment, but in a region (the border) where crime is systemic and organized, not just opportunistic.
*   **Resolution:** A precise zero. The "law" of ambient light breaks down.
*   **Implications:** Policy shouldn't use "crime reduction" as a universal justification for DST, and researchers shouldn't assume U.S. behavioral parameters apply to middle-income, high-violence settings.

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong. It is not a collection of results; it is a targeted strike on a specific hypothesis.

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
**Dinner Party Fact:** "In Mexico, we gave the 'bad guys' an extra hour of darkness every evening, and they didn't take the bait—crime didn't budge."
**Reaction:** Lean in. It's counter-intuitive and challenges a "common sense" economic finding.
**Follow-up:** "Is it because they're already so violent that an extra hour doesn't matter, or because the cartels operate on a 9-to-5 schedule?"
**The Null Result:** This is a "Gold-Standard Null." It is interesting because it contradicts a highly-cited literature, not because the data was noisy (the CIs are tight).

---

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
*   **Front-loading:** The paper is well-structured. The triple-diff logic is explained early.
*   **Appendices:** Move some of the functional form robustness (Table 4, Panel A) to the appendix.
*   **Expansion:** The "Discussion" section (Section 6) is actually the most important part for an AER audience. It should be expanded into a more formal analysis if possible. Can the author proxy for "organized crime presence" at the municipality level to show that the null is *more* null where cartels are stronger?

---

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The gap between this and an AER publication is the **Mechanism**. Currently, it says "it's zero, maybe because of cartels." An AER paper proves it's the cartels.

**The Single Most Impactful Advice:** The author needs to "interact the null." Split the sample or use a continuous measure of organized crime intensity (e.g., drug-trafficking presence as in Dell 2015). If the author can show that the darkness effect *exists* in peaceful Mexican towns but *disappears* in cartel-heavy border towns, the paper becomes a landmark study on how market structure changes behavioral responses to environmental cues.
