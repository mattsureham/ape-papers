**Idea Fidelity**

The submitted paper departs substantially from the original idea manifest. The project promised a province-level continuous-treatment DiD exploiting variation in rice intensity and post-collapse consolidation outcomes, drawing on Thai administrative data plus provincial land-census information. Instead, the paper aggregates to the national level and estimates the effect of the collapse relative to other Asian countries using augmented synthetic control and a cross-country DiD. As a result, key elements of the identification strategy are missing: there is no within-country variation in treatment intensity, no focus on land consolidation outcomes, and the analysis does not exploit the heterogeneous exposure (rice intensity) highlighted in the manifest. The empirical approach thus addresses a different research question and should be judged on its own merits rather than as a faithful realization of the original idea.

**Summary**

The paper estimates the impact of Thailand’s 2013–14 rice pledging scheme collapse on cereal production and structural transformation by comparing Thailand to 13 other Asian countries using augmented synthetic control (ASCM) and a country-level difference-in-differences. The authors find a roughly 21-point drop in Thailand’s cereal production index relative to the synthetic counterfactual, persistent declines in yields and agriculture employment, and a reallocation of GDP shares toward services. They interpret these findings through a “subsidy withdrawal trap” whereby overinvestment induced by the subsidy becomes stranded when the support ends.

**Essential Points**

1. **Identification challenges in the cross-country comparison.** The causal interpretation of the ASCM and cross-country DiD hinges on the assumption that, absent the subsidy collapse, Thailand’s agricultural trajectory would have followed that of the synthetic control/donor pool. Yet Thailand experienced a major political rupture (the 2014 coup), distinct fiscal turmoil from the rice program, and perhaps other macro shocks during 2014–2015 that likely differ systematically from the donor pool. The paper needs to demonstrate that these concurrent events did not drive the observed divergence—e.g., by controlling for or ruling out other shocks, showing robustness to excluding years close to the coup, or presenting placebo tests using other sectors unaffected by the subsidy collapse. Without such evidence, the national-level ASCM estimate may conflate the policy collapse with broader political/economic disruptions.

2. **Lack of direct measurement of the policy-caused mechanism.** The compelling “subsidy withdrawal trap” narrative rests on the idea that rice-specific overinvestment and debt dynamics collapsed with the subsidy. However, the outcomes analyzed (national cereal production, sectoral GDP shares) are highly aggregated and include other cereals/agricultural activities. It is therefore unclear whether the decline reflects rice-specific capacity destruction or broader demand/supply shifts, especially since cereal production includes maize and wheat, which were not subsidized. The empirical strategy should provide more direct evidence—e.g., disaggregating rice production/output, leveraging any available Thai rice-specific series, or showing that other staple crops did not experience similar declines—to support the mechanism claim.

3. **Inference and precision concerns with a single treated unit and small donor pool.** The ASCM relies on 13 donor countries with varied pre-treatment agricultural structures. Thailand’s RMSPE ratio is large, but the permutation p-value is only 0.071, and the point estimates from ASCM are imprecise (SEs almost as large as the point estimate). This uncertainty should be acknowledged more fully, and the paper should explore whether the donor pool is adequate for the SCM requirements. For example, the authors should report how sensitive the results are to excluding large donors (e.g., China, India) or to alternative weighting schemes, and whether pre-treatment predictors (beyond cereal production) are well balanced.

Given these shortcomings, the paper is not yet ready for publication; addressing them is essential to establish credible identification.

**Suggestions**

1. **Clarify and defend the timing assumption in the presence of the 2014 coup.** The coup and broader political transition likely affected trade, investment, and farm behavior independently of the rice scheme collapse. Present evidence that such shocks were either concurrent with the collapse or were shared across the donor pool. For instance, provide placebo tests that treat other countries’ political shocks as “treatments” to show they cannot explain the estimated effect, or restrict the post-period to years less affected by the coup (e.g., 2015–2020) to see if the main result holds.

2. **Disaggregate the cereal outcome or supplement with rice-specific data.** If possible, use Thai administrative (e.g., FAO rice production statistics) or national account data to isolate rice output from other cereals. If that is not feasible, at least show that other cereals (maize, wheat) in the donor pool do not drive the effect by running SCM on each composition separately. Alternatively, include rice acreage or rice-specific yield as additional outcomes; if these series are unavailable nationally, explain the limitation and discuss how it affects the interpretation.

3. **Strengthen the pre-treatment match and balance checks.** The augmented SCM relies on a good pre-treatment fit. Provide diagnostics such as plots comparing time-series of key predictors (GDP per capita, agriculture share, rice prices) between Thailand and the synthetic counterpart, and compute pre-treatment RMSPE for multiple outcomes. If the ASCM fit is heavily driven by cereal production, consider augmenting the predictor set with slow-moving covariates to ensure that pre-treatment balance is maintained across dimensions likely related to the counterfactual path.

4. **Deepen the robustness analysis to address donor pool concerns.** The current leave-one-out and ASEAN-only estimates are helpful but insufficient. Consider (a) re-estimating ASCM after excluding the largest donors (India/China) to check whether the weights concentrate on a few countries; (b) using alternative donor pools that match Thailand on key covariates (e.g., initial GDP per capita, agricultural share); and (c) applying a Bayesian structural time-series or other counterfactual method to see whether results are similar. Discuss explicitly how the donor pool choice affects inference, given there are only 13 countries.

5. **Triangulate structural transformation claims with micro-level evidence if available.** The finding that services GDP share increased while industry declined is intriguing. To substantiate this narrative, incorporate additional data such as employment or output shares by sub-sector (e.g., agro-processing vs. other manufacturing) or use regional variation within Thailand (if accessible) to show that areas more reliant on rice experienced sharper service-sector growth. Even simple trends (e.g., exports of rice vs. services) could help link macro shifts to the subsidy mechanism.

6. **Address the “subsidy withdrawal trap” narrative with more evidence on credit/land dynamics.** The trap hypothesis rests on stranded investments and debt. While the current dataset cannot capture this, the paper could cite or incorporate evidence from farm debt statistics, land consolidation trends, or interviews/surveys documenting defaults. Alternatively, the authors can temper the narrative by noting the limitations of the available data and framing the trap as a plausible but not fully verified channel.

7. **Be explicit about the comparability of the donor pool to Thailand.** Table 1 summarizes pre-treatment means but masks heterogeneity; you might provide visualizations (e.g., scatterplots) showing how close each donor country is to Thailand on key dimensions. Highlight countries that receive large ASCM weights and justify why they are reasonable counterfactuals. This transparency will reassure readers about the plausibility of the synthetic control.

8. **Consider complementary within-country checks if feasible.** While the paper does not exploit provincial variation, the idea manifest emphasizes such heterogeneity. Even if full data are unavailable, discuss whether the national-level findings could be supported by partial subnational evidence—e.g., compare rice-producing provinces to others before and after the collapse using publicly available statistics. If this is not possible, explain why, but make it clear that such within-country variation would be a valuable future extension.

By addressing these suggestions, the authors can make a stronger case that the observed cereal production decline is truly attributable to the rice pledging scheme’s collapse and better motivate the broader “subsidy withdrawal trap” claim.
