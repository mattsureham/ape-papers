## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple, policy-relevant question: when Schengen countries reintroduced internal border controls after 2015, did the border regions exposed to those controls become economically weaker? Using regional data across European border areas, the paper’s central claim is that the answer is basically no at the level of annual regional GDP: the scary macro rhetoric about the economic costs of these “temporary” controls appears overstated for the actual, limited checks that were implemented.

Why should a busy economist care? Because this is a clean test of a broader question about what part of “economic integration” really matters: do passport checks themselves meaningfully depress local economic activity, or are the big gains from integration really coming from deeper institutions like regulatory harmonization, labor mobility rights, and the single market?

### Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Not quite. The current introduction is competent and vivid, but it takes too long to reveal the paper’s actual intellectual payload. The first two paragraphs set up a policy concern, but the paper’s sharper and more interesting claim is not just “do border controls hurt regions?” It is: **the marginal economic cost of reintroducing soft internal borders appears much smaller than the public debate and simulation literature suggest.**

Right now the introduction sounds like a standard policy evaluation paper about Schengen. It should instead foreground the broader economic question: **which border frictions matter?** The paper is potentially interesting because it distinguishes between “hard” borders and “soft” checks, and between the symbolism of border reinstatement and its measurable aggregate economic effects.

### The pitch the paper should have

Here is the pitch the first two paragraphs should deliver:

> European policymakers often treat the reintroduction of internal Schengen border controls as economically costly, and simulation studies of a “return of borders” project large losses for trade, commuting, and growth. But those projections typically model something close to a hard reversal of integration, not the selective identity checks actually implemented since 2015. The key economic question is therefore not whether borders matter in general, but whether these **soft internal border controls** materially reduce economic activity in the places they touch most directly.
>
> This paper provides evidence from the post-2015 Schengen reintroductions. Using variation across affected and unaffected European border regions, I find little evidence that temporary internal border controls reduced annual regional GDP per capita, even though some trade- and transport-exposed activity may have shifted. The broader implication is that the economic gains from European integration may depend much less on the absence of passport checks per se than on the deeper market integration that Schengen sits on top of.

That is a stronger AER-style opening because it frames the paper as answering a question about the world, not merely filling a missing empirical slot in a Europe-policy literature.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper’s contribution is to show that the post-2015 reintroduction of **soft internal Schengen border controls** appears to have had little detectable effect on annual aggregate economic activity in exposed border regions, implying that the marginal economic cost of these controls is much smaller than “border reversal” simulations suggest.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest papers?

Only partially. The paper does identify a gap—most prior work studies Schengen creation, trade flows, or simulation exercises—but the differentiation is still too mechanical. The introduction says, in effect, “no one has done a quasi-experimental regional DiD on this exact setting.” That is true, but it is not, by itself, a top-journal contribution.

The paper needs to distinguish itself conceptually from at least three nearby strands:

1. **Studies of Schengen’s original integration effects**  
   Those ask whether removing border frictions helped integration. This paper asks the reverse, but in a much weaker form: what happens when some checks return but deeper market institutions remain intact?

2. **Simulation papers on the costs of Schengen collapse**  
   Those model hard or system-wide border reinstatement. This paper estimates the realized effect of the much softer, selective checks actually observed.

3. **Classic border-effect papers**  
   Those quantify the full bundle of frictions associated with borders. This paper isolates a much thinner friction: identity-control-type barriers inside an otherwise integrated market.

That conceptual differentiation is available in the draft, but not sharply enough. The paper still reads too much like “first DiD estimate of X.”

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or as filling a gap in a LITERATURE?

Mixed, but too often the latter. The stronger version is clearly about the world:

- How economically important are soft border checks inside an integrated market?
- Which components of “border openness” actually matter for local economic performance?
- Are policymakers conflating symbolic border reinstatement with economically meaningful barriers?

Those are strong world-facing questions. The paper should lean harder into them.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?

A smart economist could probably say: “It’s a DiD paper on Schengen border controls that mostly finds no aggregate GDP effect.” That is not enough. The worry is exactly that this gets filed mentally as “another staggered DiD with a null.”

The introduction should make it easier for the reader to say something more distinctive, such as:

> “It shows that reinstating passport checks inside Europe doesn’t seem to dent annual regional GDP much, so the economically meaningful part of integration may be deeper than the visible border regime.”

That is a memorable takeaway.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Several possibilities:

- **Different outcome variable:** The current main outcome, annual NUTS3 GDP per capita, is broad and sluggish. The paper itself practically admits the action is likely in trade, commuting, logistics, tourism, and border crossing intensity. A more compelling paper would center outcomes that match the mechanism: freight flows, commuting, retail sales in border towns, tourism nights, firm entry/exit in logistics, or travel-time-sensitive industries.
- **Different framing:** The paper should frame itself less as “the cost of Schengen reversal” and more as “what part of border removal matters economically.” That makes the null substantively interesting rather than disappointing.
- **Different comparison:** The paper could do more to contrast soft internal checks with settings involving hard border frictions. Even if only as framing, an explicit comparison to customs barriers, Brexit-style frictions, or historical border openings/closures would enlarge the paper.
- **Different mechanism emphasis:** The sectoral result is potentially the most interesting part of the paper, because it suggests compositional effects without aggregate contraction. If that pattern is real, it is a better paper than a pure null paper.

If the author can only enlarge one dimension, I would enlarge the **mechanism/outcome match**. Right now the paper asks a high-frequency friction question with low-frequency aggregate outcomes.

That is the conversation more economists would care about, including those who do not specialize in Europe.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Europe removed internal border checks and built a common market; after 2015, several countries partially reinstated them. Public debate and policy simulations treated this as economically dangerous, especially for border regions.

### Tension

But it is unclear whether the actual controls that returned—selective identity checks without tariffs, customs, or regulatory fragmentation—were economically meaningful enough to dent regional activity. The observed policy change is politically salient, but economically ambiguous.

### Resolution

The paper finds little evidence of a meaningful decline in annual regional GDP per capita attributable to these controls. Any apparent aggregate losses are swamped by national trends, while some narrower sectoral effects may exist.

### Implications

The visible act of “bringing borders back” may not be the economically important margin. The large gains from integration may reside mainly in deeper institutional features of the single market rather than in the absence of passport checks alone.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the ingredients, but the narrative is currently diluted by two things:

1. **Methodological self-consciousness**
2. **A surplus of institutional detail**

The paper often reads like it is trying to prove it knows the recent DiD literature and the Schengen legal code. That is fine for credibility, but not for narrative. The story is there, but it is buried under implementation.

At present, it is somewhat a **collection of estimators and heterogeneity tables looking for a bigger idea**. The bigger idea should be explicit from page 1:

> Not all borders are economically equal. Reintroducing selective internal identity checks inside an integrated market may have much smaller aggregate consequences than the term “border controls” suggests.

That is the story.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?

I’d say:

> “Europe brought back internal border checks after 2015, but annual GDP in the affected border regions barely moved. So maybe passport checks are not the economically important part of integration.”

That is the hook.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

A few would lean in; many would need the second clause. “Another null on regional GDP” is phone-risky. “This changes how we think about what borders do” is lean-in material.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Probably one of these:

- “Then what *does* matter—trade, commuting, logistics?”
- “Are you just using too blunt an outcome?”
- “Does this mean Schengen doesn’t matter economically, or just that passport checks don’t?”
- “How does this compare to Brexit or customs barriers?”

Those are exactly the questions the paper should anticipate and use to sharpen its framing.

### Is the null itself interesting?

Yes, but only if framed correctly. A null result is interesting here if it teaches us that:

- the marginal friction created by these controls is small;
- public rhetoric about “the return of borders” overstates short-run aggregate economic costs;
- the economically meaningful parts of integration are deeper than checkpoint removal.

If instead the paper is framed as “I tested whether Schengen controls lower GDP and found nothing,” it will feel like a failed experiment. The paper must work harder to make the null **diagnostic** rather than merely **non-significant**.

The author is close to doing that, but not quite there.

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

### What would make it read better?

#### 1. Cut the methodological throat-clearing in the introduction
The intro currently spends too much space announcing estimators and sample counts. An AER-caliber intro should first establish the question, then the surprising answer, then why the answer matters. The precise estimator menu can wait.

#### 2. Shorten the institutional background substantially
The legal notification history is overlong relative to its payoff. For strategic positioning, readers do not need a miniature treatise on Articles 25–35. Keep only what is essential to understand why the policy variation exists and why the treatment is persistent rather than a one-off shock.

#### 3. Move some of the DiD-literature signaling out of the main text
The paper is overinvested in saying it uses modern estimators. Good. Do it cleanly, then move on. The contribution is not “I know Callaway-Sant’Anna.” That is table stakes.

#### 4. Front-load the conceptual punchline
The reader should learn by page 2:
- the policy concern,
- the distinction between soft and hard borders,
- the main finding,
- and the bigger implication for how to think about integration.

Right now that punchline arrives in pieces.

#### 5. Elevate the sectoral/compositional result if it is credible
The trade/transport decline is more interesting than much of the robustness material. If the aggregate story is “no GDP effect, but a reallocation away from border-intensive activity,” that is an actual result with texture. It should not feel secondary.

#### 6. Trim repetition around national trends
The paper repeatedly says the naïve estimate is driven by France vs. Germany trajectories. Say it once clearly, show it, and move on.

#### 7. Rework the conclusion
The conclusion currently adds some value, especially with the “speed bump vs wall” intuition, but it is too long and somewhat essayistic. It should close on the main substantive lesson: **visible border controls are not the same thing as economically consequential border fragmentation.**

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

### What is the gap between current form and an AER paper?

Primarily:

### 1. Framing problem
The science may be adequate, but the story is not yet ambitious enough. The current version reads as a careful niche Europe-policy paper. An AER paper would make readers feel they learned something general about borders, integration, and which frictions matter.

### 2. Scope problem
The paper’s main outcome is too aggregate for its mechanism. If the treatment is modest and localized, annual regional GDP is a hard place to expect movement. That makes the central result vulnerable to feeling unsurprising or underpowered, even if the paper says otherwise. The scope should shift toward outcomes where one would actually expect the policy to bite.

### 3. Ambition problem
The paper is competent but safe. It says, roughly, “we don’t find much.” A stronger paper would say either:
- “we don’t find much in aggregate, and here is decisive evidence showing why—because economic agents adapted and the damage is concentrated in commuting/trade margins,” or
- “the visible return of borders is mostly symbolic economically unless it is paired with deeper policy frictions.”

### 4. Novelty problem, but only partly
The exact setting is novel enough. The issue is not lack of novelty in data or setting; it is lack of novelty in the current *interpretation*. “First quasi-experimental evidence on X” is not enough for AER unless X is itself a first-order question.

### Single most impactful advice

**Reframe the paper around a bigger, general question—what components of borders actually matter for economic activity—and reorganize the empirical presentation so the answer is “soft internal border checks have little aggregate effect, but may reallocate border-intensive activity.”**

If the author does only one thing, it should be this. That change would affect the intro, the literature review, the choice of which results to emphasize, and the conclusion. It would transform the paper from a Schengen policy note into a paper about the economics of border frictions inside integrated markets.
