### 1. Idea Fidelity
The paper largely pursues the original idea manifest but with some deviations that weaken its scope and fidelity. It faithfully implements the core identification strategy—a Callaway-Sant'Anna (CS) staggered DiD for aggregate effects combined with a material-level triple-difference (DDD) exploiting targeted (plastic, metal) vs. non-targeted materials (paper, wood)—using Eurostat cei_wm020 data on recycling rates by material. The research question on DRS causal effects on material-specific recycling matches, as does the emphasis on staggered adoption, never-treated controls, and policy relevance to the EU PPWR mandate. However, key misses include: (i) reducing treated units from 13 (manifest: up to Poland 2025) to 10 (ending at Romania 2023, excluding future adopters like Ireland/Austria/Poland and some 2022 cases like Slovakia/Latvia in counts), justified by data availability but shrinking variation; (ii) omitting env_waspac (packaging waste tonnes) despite manifest mention, relying solely on cei_wm020 recycling rates; (iii) excluding glass from baseline controls (manifest included it as untreated), using it only in robustness; and (iv) no explicit dose-response by deposit amount in baseline (only robustness). These changes make the design credible but less ambitious than promised, with reduced power from fewer treated units.

### 2. Summary
This paper evaluates the causal impact of staggered deposit return schemes (DRS) on packaging waste recycling rates across 10 EU/EEA countries using 2000–2023 Eurostat data. Employing a Callaway-Sant'Anna staggered DiD for aggregate effects and a material-level triple-difference comparing targeted (plastic, metal) vs. non-targeted materials (paper, wood), it finds precisely estimated null effects on total recycling (-0.13 pp) and insignificant positive differential effects on targeted materials (+4.03 pp). The results challenge DRS efficacy at the system level, attributing nulls to infrastructure displacement rather than consumer incentives, with timely implications for the EU's 2029 PPWR mandate.

### 3. Essential Points
1. **Low statistical power from limited clusters undermines credibility of null results.** With only 10 treated countries and standard errors clustered at the country level (effective df ~9 post-FE), the DDD minimum detectable effect (MDE) exceeds 10 pp (two-sided α=0.05, 80% power), far larger than the 4 pp point estimate or industry-claimed effects (~5–10 pp gains in beverage containers). The paper acknowledges power limits but does not quantify MDEs or conduct power simulations; without this, the "precise null" claim is overstated, as CIs are wide (e.g., DDD: [-6.2, 14.3] pp). Authors must add power calculations and consider alternatives like wild cluster bootstrapping or pre-registration of MDEs to credibly interpret insiginificance.

2. **Parallel trends violations in aggregate CS DiD threaten identification, insufficiently addressed in DDD.** The paper notes 3/16 pre-treatment event-time coefficients significant at 10% (long horizons), attributing to noise, but provides no formal tests (e.g., joint pre-trend F-test) or event-study plots. The DDD relies on material-specific parallel trends within countries, but no event-study equivalent is shown; material-year FEs absorb EU shocks, but country-year FEs may overcontrol if DRS affects aggregate infrastructure. Authors must provide DDD event studies by relative time and material, plus placebo tests on leads, to validate trends—failure here justifies rejection.

3. **Mismatch between narrow DRS scope and aggregate outcomes biases toward null, unaddressed measurement error.** DRS targets single-use beverage containers (~10–20% of plastic/metal packaging), yet outcomes aggregate all packaging waste; dilution implies understating true effects on covered items, but the paper interprets nulls as "no system-level improvement" without bounding substitution (e.g., via env_waspac tonnes). Manifest promised env_waspac integration—its absence misses a key robustness. Authors must incorporate waste generation/tonnes data to decompose recycling rates (recycled tonnage / generated tonnage) and test for displacement.

### 4. Suggestions
The paper is well-written, concise, and policy-timely for AER:Insights, with strong institutional detail, modern DiD methods, and a compelling "deposit illusion" narrative. The DDD is novel and elegantly addresses staggered biases/endogeneity, making it a genuine contribution despite nulls. To elevate to publishable form, prioritize feasibility and transparency given data constraints.

**Data and sample enhancements (20% effort):** Incorporate manifest-promised env_waspac for tonnes recycled/generated, enabling falsification on total waste (should not rise) and decomposition of rates. Extend to 2024–2025 provisional Eurostat data for late adopters (Hungary/Ireland), boosting treated units to 12+ and power. Clarify sample: manifest's 13 treated/14 controls vs. paper's 10/18 (incl. not-yet-treated); recode not-yet-treated as controls only pre-2029, or use CS multi-period extension (e.g., Borusyak et al. 2024). Balance panel strictly (e.g., drop early years with sparse coverage) and report attrition by treatment status.

**Empirical robustness expansions (30% effort):** Generate full event-study figures: (i) CS group-time ATTs with 95% CIs shaded; (ii) DDD dynamic effects (DRS_lead_k × Targeted) stacked by material. Test pre-trends formally (e.g., Roth et al. 2023 estimator). For spillovers, estimate full DDD interactions for each material separately (as in tab:material, but with FEs). Dose-response: baseline continuous deposit (0.10–0.25 EUR, country-specific), interacted with Targeted; plot binned scatter. Placebo tests: (i) invert controls (targeted as placebo pre-DRS); (ii) synthetic controls à la AbiRafeh et al. (2024) for small-N robustness. Wild bootstrap SEs (Roodman et al. 2019) for low df.

**Power and interpretation refinements (20% effort):** Compute MDEs explicitly (e.g., via simstudy R package) for DDD/CS under baseline variance/covariate balance, reporting "fails to reject effects <X pp." Add standardized effect sizes prominently (appendix tab:sde is good; move to main). Heterogeneity: interact DRS×Targeted with baseline recycling (low vs. high), adopter size (Germany drop already done), or EPR stringency indices. Discuss ceiling effects quantitatively: regress post-DRS levels on pre-levels to bound convergence bias.

**Broader improvements (20% effort):** Strengthen lit review: cite EU-specific DRS evals (e.g., Bohm 2022 Lithuania microdata) and contrast with US (Ashenmiller 2009). Mechanism tests: proxy infrastructure (RVM counts from Reloop/TOMRA, Google Trends "recycling") or litter (Eurostat env_waslit). Policy section: simulate PPWR costs (€3bn) vs. implied recycling gains under point estimates/CIs. Figures: add trends plot (treated vs. control by material, normalized pre-DRS). Abstract: quantify precision ("rules out >3 pp aggregate gains"). Overall, these yield a tighter, more convincing null—valuable amid PPWR hype—positioning for top-field publication with minor revise.
