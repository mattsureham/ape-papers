### 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether the global death of bank secrecy actually matters for where money is held. By exploiting the staggered bilateral activation of information exchange between Liechtenstein and 25 countries, the author finds that "turning on the lights" causes a massive (42–57%) exodus of offshore banking positions.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this well, but it leads with a historical anecdote about Liechtenstein’s GDP ratio. While interesting, an AER pitch should focus on the *global* policy shift. 

**The pitch the paper should have:** 
"Does the Automatic Exchange of Information (AEOI) effectively dismantle offshore tax havens, or does wealth simply hide in the shadows of remaining secrecy? Using a bilateral 'natural experiment' in Liechtenstein, this paper provides the first high-frequency evidence that bilateral transparency triggers a massive, immediate repatriation of offshore assets. These findings suggest that the modern international tax architecture has successfully eliminated the 'secrecy premium' that once defined global offshore finance."

---

### 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:** The paper provides the first bilateral, staggered-treatment evidence that Automatic Exchange of Information (AEOI) causes a significant contraction (over 40%) in offshore banking positions, a magnitude far exceeding previous estimates of tax transparency initiatives.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It differentiates itself by moving from *aggregate* financial center flows (Johannesen & Zucman 2014) to *bilateral* pairs. This is a significant identification upgrade.
*   **Question:** It is currently framed as answering a question about the WORLD (Does transparency work?), which is strong.
*   **Clarity:** A smart economist would understand this immediately. It is not "just another DiD"; it is the "definitive DiD on the end of bank secrecy."
*   **What would make it bigger?** The paper is currently a "case study" of Liechtenstein. To be a "slam dunk" AER paper, it needs to address the "Waterbed Effect" more aggressively—did this money go to Switzerland, or back to the home country?

---

### 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

*   **Closest Neighbors:** Johannesen & Zucman (2014), Menkhoff & Miethe (2019), and Alstadsæter et al. (2019).
*   **Positioning:** The paper should position itself as the *precision instrument* that confirms the broad trends observed by Zucman and others. It shouldn't "attack" them, but rather explain why their aggregate estimates were likely lower-bound undercounts.
*   **The Missing Conversation:** The paper needs to speak more to the **International Macro/Finance** literature on capital flows and home bias. Is this "repatriation" or just "relocation"?
*   **Unexpected Framing:** Connecting this to the literature on **Inattention/Procrastination** (as suggested in the discussion) is a brilliant move—it explains why there are no anticipation effects despite years of treaty talk.

---

### 4. NARRATIVE ARC

*   **Setup:** The world was once a place where 30x a country's GDP could sit in secret vaults.
*   **Tension:** Critics argued AEOI would be toothless or that money would move before the "switch" was flipped.
*   **Resolution:** The switch was flipped, and the money vanished almost instantly (at the bilateral level).
*   **Implications:** Bank secrecy was the only thing Liechtenstein was selling. Without it, the business model is dead.

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong. However, the "Sun and Abraham" results (showing a smaller, insignificant ATT) create a narrative "dip." The author needs to lean into why the TWFE is actually the *more* relevant narrative (the EU cohort is the "big reveal") rather than just a statistical artifact.

---

### 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

*   **The Fact:** "When Liechtenstein started sharing data with Germany, half the German money left within a year."
*   **Reaction:** People lean in. It confirms a suspicion but with a magnitude that is shocking (50% vs the 8% found in earlier studies).
*   **Follow-up:** "Where did the money go?" (This is the paper's current weakness).

---

### 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

*   **Front-load the EU result:** Table 2, Column 5 is actually your most "clean" result. Don't hide it behind the messy pooled Sun-Abraham ATT. 
*   **Appendix:** Move the "Methodological insights on staggered DiD" to the appendix. In an AER paper, the methodology should serve the result, not be a third-tier contribution.
*   **Mechanism:** If possible, include a small section or chart on *where* the money went (using the rest of the BIS data). Even a "null result" on the waterbed effect would be valuable.

---

### 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The gap is **Scope**. Currently, it is a very high-quality study of one small country. 

**Single most impactful advice:**
Broaden the "Waterbed" analysis—explicitly test whether the 42% decline in Liechtenstein was offset by a 5% increase in non-AEOI jurisdictions like the US (South Dakota/Delaware) or UAE. If you can show that the money *didn't* just jump to the next haven, you've moved from a paper about "Liechtenstein's decline" to a paper about "The Global Victory over Tax Evasion."
