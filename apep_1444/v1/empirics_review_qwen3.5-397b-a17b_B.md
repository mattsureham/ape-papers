1. **Idea Fidelity**

The paper largely pursues the core empirical strategy outlined in the manifest: exploiting the age-65 eligibility threshold for Ecuador's *Pensión Mis Mejores Años* (MMA) using ENEMDU microdata. However, there are significant deviations from the proposed design. First, the manifest promised a **dual-threshold design**, utilizing both the age-65 cutoff and the Registro Social score cutoff (score=28) to identify income effects. The paper entirely omits the secondary score-based RDD, citing lack of direct score data in ENEMDU, despite the manifest confirming its availability from 2014 onward. Second, the manifest specified the sample should be restricted to **individuals in poverty** (Registro Social <34.67) to ensure a clean sharp RDD. The paper instead runs the primary specification on the **full sample** of elderly (ages 55–75), using an income percentile proxy only for heterogeneity checks. This dilutes the first stage and complicates the "Sharp" RDD claim. Finally, the data period shifted from the proposed 2018–2021 to 2021–2023, introducing potential post-pandemic labor market confounders not addressed in the original feasibility assessment.

2. **Summary**

This paper provides the first causal estimates of Ecuador's non-contributory elderly pension on labor supply, exploiting the age-65 eligibility threshold. Using a regression discontinuity design on nationally representative survey data, the author finds that pension eligibility reduces labor force participation by 3.0 percentage points, driven entirely by urban workers exiting wage employment, while rural agricultural workers show no response. The findings highlight a "sectoral exit asymmetry," suggesting that uniform pension designs have heterogeneous behavioral effects depending on the structure of local labor markets (wage vs. subsistence).

3. **Essential Points**

1.  **Identification Classification (Sharp vs. Fuzzy):** The paper labels the design a "Sharp RDD" on age, but the first stage shows only a 6.7 percentage point jump in transfer receipt. Because the program is means-tested (Age 65 + Poverty), age alone does not determine eligibility for the full population. For the non-poor, the treatment probability is zero on both sides of the cutoff. This makes the design technically a **Fuzzy RDD** where age is the instrument for pension receipt, or a Sharp RDD only if restricted to the poor. The current specification dilutes the treatment effect by including ineligible (non-poor) individuals in the control and treatment groups. The authors must either restrict the main sample to the poor (using the best available proxy) to align with the manifest's "Sharp" claim or reframe the estimation as a Fuzzy RDD/IV strategy, scaling the reduced form by the first stage to recover the Local Average Treatment Effect (LATE) for the eligible poor.

2.  **Omission of the Secondary Identification Strategy:** The manifest's key methodological contribution was the **dual-threshold design** (Age + Registro Social Score). The paper drops the score-based RDD entirely, stating the score is "not directly observed." This contradicts the manifest's data audit, which confirmed Registro Social groups are in ENEMDU from 2014 onward. If the exact score is unavailable, the authors should attempt a fuzzy RDD using the available poverty quintiles/terciles near the cutoff or explicitly explain why the manifest's feasibility claim was incorrect. Dropping this element removes the ability to estimate the marginal income effect of the benefit intensification ($50 vs. $100), which was a central part of the proposed contribution.

3.  **Data Period and External Validity:** The analysis uses data from 2021–2023, immediately following the COVID-19 pandemic, whereas the manifest proposed 2018–2021. Elderly labor supply during this period may be influenced by differential health risks, sectoral recovery rates, or emergency transfers unrelated to MMA. The significant placebo effect at age 60 (p=0.002) noted in the robustness section is concerning; this often signals a confounding retirement norm or contributory pension eligibility spike. Given the post-pandemic context, the authors must discuss whether age-60 spikes are artifacts of the specific survey waves used or structural features of the Ecuadorian labor market that threaten the validity of the age-65 counterfactual.

4. **Suggestions**

**Reframing the Empirical Strategy**
The most critical improvement involves clarifying the identification strategy. Given the means-tested nature of MMA, the "Sharp RDD on Age" label is misleading for the full sample. I recommend two paths: (1) **Restrict the Main Sample:** Use the ENEMDU income variables to construct a stricter poverty proxy (e.g., bottom 40% or asset-based indices) and run the main RDD on this subsample. This aligns with the manifest's promise of studying the "poor elderly" and strengthens the first stage. If the first stage jumps from ~0 to ~0.5 in this subsample, the Sharp RDD claim holds. (2) **Embrace the Fuzzy Design:** If the full sample must be used for power, explicitly frame age-65 as an instrument for pension receipt. Report the IV estimate (LATE) prominently. The current text mentions the IV effect (-4.5 pp) in the introduction but bases the main tables on the reduced form. For policy purposes, the effect of *receiving* the pension (IV) is more relevant than the effect of *eligibility* (Reduced Form), especially when take-up is incomplete.

**Deepening the Mechanism Analysis**
The urban-rural heterogeneity is the paper's strongest finding, but the mechanistic explanation relies on broad assertions about "wage vs. subsistence" work. You can strengthen this by leveraging ENEMDU's occupation codes.
*   **Occupational Shifts:** Do urban leavers exit entirely, or do they shift to informal unpaid family work? Tabulate the transition rates by major occupation group (e.g., ISCO-08 1-digit codes). If urban workers move from "Services/Sales" to "Inactive," this supports the retirement narrative. If they move to "Unpaid Family Worker," the pension may be subsidizing household production rather than enabling leisure.
*   **Household Composition:** The manifest proposed analyzing household composition (coresidence). Even if not a primary outcome, include a table on whether pension receipt changes the probability of living with adult children. In Latin America, pensions often affect multigenerational living arrangements (e.g., allowing grandparents to live independently). If urban elderly exit labor *and* live independently, while rural elderly work *and* co-reside, this would powerfully support the "sectoral exit" narrative.
*   **Income Effects:** You report labor income is unchanged. This is consistent with exit, but consider looking at *total household income*. Does the pension crowd out other transfers (e.g., remittances from children)? If children reduce support because the parent now has a pension, the net welfare gain is smaller. ENEMDU typically captures remittances; adding this analysis would round out the welfare picture.

**Addressing the Secondary Threshold (Score RDD)**
Revisit the data availability regarding the Registro Social score. The manifest explicitly stated ENEMDU contains "Registro Social group variables (quintiles) from 2014 onward."
*   **If available:** Implement the secondary RDD as proposed. Even if the exact score (0–100) isn't there, the quintile boundaries can support a fuzzy design. This would allow you to estimate the elasticity of labor supply to benefit size ($50 vs. $100), a rare and valuable parameter.
*   **If unavailable:** Explicitly reconcile this with the manifest. State clearly that while group variables exist, the continuous score required for a clean cutoff is not public. Then, use the income percentile cutoff (bottom 40%) as a proxy for the score threshold and run a heterogeneity analysis there. Currently, the "Poor Subsample" results (Table 2, Panel B) show a null effect. This contradicts the main result and needs interpretation. Is it because the income proxy is noisy? Or do the poorest truly *not* exit labor (subsistence constraint)? If the latter, this is a profound finding: the pension helps the "near-poor" retire but traps the "extreme-poor" in work. This nuance deserves more space than the current brief mention.

**Robustness and Contextualization**
*   **The Age-60 Placebo:** The significant effect at age 60 requires more than a footnote. In many Latin American countries, age 60 is the standard retirement age for contributory systems or offers other benefits. If there is a spike at 60, the counterfactual trend at 65 might be biased. Consider using age 60–64 as a separate control group or detrending the data based on the 60–64 slope before estimating the 65 jump.
*   **Pandemic Controls:** Given the 2021–2023 window, include wave fixed effects interacted with age trends if possible, or at least discuss how pandemic recovery might differ by sector. Urban service jobs (high contact) may have recovered slower than rural agriculture, potentially confounding the urban-rural split.
*   **Take-Up Rates:** The 6.7% first stage implies low take-up among the eligible. Discuss *why*. Is it administrative friction? Stigma? Lack of information? If take-up is low, the IV estimate will be large but imprecise. Providing context on MIES enrollment campaigns during this period would help readers gauge whether the estimated effects are stable or transient.

**Presentation and Formatting**
*   **AER: Insights Style:** Ensure the paper fits the "Insights" format, which prioritizes brevity and policy relevance over technical appendix heavy-lifting. The current draft is well-structured but could be tightened. Move the robustness table to an online appendix if space is tight, keeping only the main RD plot and heterogeneity table in the print version.
*   **Visuals:** Include the canonical RD plot (bin scatter + fitted lines) for the main LFP outcome. The text describes the results well, but a visual of the urban vs. rural discontinuity would be compelling and immediately communicate the "sectoral asymmetry."
*   **Policy Implications:** Expand the conclusion to speak directly to the MIES. If urban workers exit but rural workers do not, should the benefit be higher in rural areas to achieve the same welfare gain? Or is the goal income support rather than retirement? Clarifying the policy objective helps position the paper as actionable advice rather than just academic evidence.

**Final Thought on Contribution**
The paper successfully identifies a gap in the Ecuador literature (elderly vs. working-age BDH) and provides clean evidence on labor supply. The urban-rural finding is novel and important for the broader development literature on informal labor markets. By addressing the identification clarity (Sharp vs. Fuzzy) and reconciling the data discrepancies with the manifest, this paper can make a robust contribution to *AER: Insights*. The key is to ensure the empirical claims match the institutional reality of a means-tested program.
