## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether a modest non-contributory pension lets poor older adults in Ecuador stop working, and finds that the answer depends sharply on where they live: urban elderly reduce labor force participation at the age-65 eligibility threshold, while rural elderly do not. A busy economist should care because the paper’s broader claim is not just “pensions reduce labor supply,” but that the same transfer has different behavioral effects depending on whether old-age work is wage employment with a clear exit margin or self-employment in subsistence agriculture.

The paper does **not** articulate this pitch as clearly as it should in the first two paragraphs. Right now the introduction starts as a country case study and only later reveals the more interesting idea: that labor supply responses to old-age transfers depend on labor market structure. That broader idea is the reason a general-interest reader might care; Ecuador alone is not.

### The pitch the paper should have

“Across developing countries, governments use non-contributory pensions both to reduce old-age poverty and to enable retirement outside the formal sector. But whether these pensions actually allow elderly adults to stop working may depend less on transfer size than on the kind of work older people do. Using Ecuador’s age-65 eligibility threshold, this paper shows that a non-contributory pension induces labor force exit among urban elderly but not among rural elderly, suggesting that pensions facilitate retirement where old-age work is discrete wage employment but not where it is tied to agricultural self-employment and household production.

This matters because it reframes a standard policy question. The key issue is not simply whether pensions reduce labor supply on average, but when cash transfers can buy genuine retirement and when they merely supplement continued work. That distinction has first-order implications for how economists think about social protection, labor supply at older ages, and the design of pension systems in low- and middle-income countries.”

That is the AER version of the pitch. It leads with the world question, not the Ecuador application.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper’s contribution is to show that a non-contributory pension’s effect on elderly labor supply is highly context-dependent: it induces labor force exit in urban wage-based settings but not in rural agricultural self-employment.

### Evaluation

**Is this clearly differentiated from the closest 3-4 papers?**  
Only partially. The paper names several neighboring studies, but the differentiation is still too much “first causal estimate for Ecuador” and not enough “here is the substantive margin previous papers have missed.” “First evidence from Ecuador” is not an AER-level contribution. “Transfer effects depend on the exit technology of work at older ages” could be.

**Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a gap in a literature?**  
Mixed, but too often the latter. The introduction says things like “adds Ecuador to the small set of countries with clean RD estimates.” That is a literature-gap framing, and a weak one. The stronger framing is about the world: when do old-age pensions purchase retirement?

**Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain what’s new?**  
Not confidently. They might say: “It’s an RD on a pension age cutoff in Ecuador and finds some labor supply effects with urban-rural heterogeneity.” That is not yet memorable. The paper needs a sharper conceptual object than “heterogeneity”: namely, that retirement responses depend on whether work is separable from household production.

**What would make this contribution bigger? Be specific.**
1. **Elevate occupation/sector, not geography, to the main object.** Urban-rural is a proxy. The more interesting claim is about wage employment versus agricultural self-employment, or discrete labor force exit versus blurred household production. If the data allow even coarse evidence by occupation, self-employment status, agricultural work, or informality, that would enlarge the paper substantially.
2. **Show that the mechanism is about the feasibility of retirement, not simply poverty or program access.** The current “poor subsample” result points in a different direction and muddies the story. A stronger contribution would compare settings where old-age work is more or less divisible.
3. **Connect to the economics of retirement, not just transfer incidence.** If the paper could frame this as evidence on retirement constraints outside formal pension systems, it becomes a more general labor/public economics paper.
4. **Emphasize a policy tradeoff.** If the same transfer produces retirement in cities and income support in rural areas, then universal old-age pensions are doing different jobs in different labor markets. That is more than a local treatment effect; it is a design insight.

Currently it is positioned **too narrowly in setting** and **too broadly in policy implication**. Too much Ecuador-specific institutional detail appears before the main conceptual payoff is clear. ## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Many elderly adults in developing countries lack contributory pensions and continue working into old age, often in informal sectors. Governments increasingly use non-contributory pensions to reduce old-age poverty and potentially enable retirement.

### Tension
But it is unclear whether these pensions actually let older adults stop working, especially when their work is informal, self-employed, or embedded in household production. Average effects may conceal a more important distinction: some workers can retire cleanly, others cannot.

### Resolution
Using Ecuador’s age-65 eligibility threshold, the paper finds a reduction in labor force participation overall, driven entirely by urban elderly; rural elderly do not reduce work.

### Implications
The behavioral effect of pensions depends on labor market structure. A pension may buy retirement where work is discrete wage employment, but function mainly as income support where work is inseparable from agricultural self-employment.

### Evaluation

There is **a plausible narrative arc here**, but the paper only partially realizes it. At present, it reads somewhat like a competent empirical paper that discovered an interesting heterogeneity result and then built the story around it. The “sectoral exit asymmetry” is the right candidate for the story, but the paper has not fully earned it conceptually.

Right now the narrative is:
1. Ecuador has a pension.
2. I estimate its effects.
3. There is urban-rural heterogeneity.
4. Here is a mechanism story.

The better narrative is:
1. Social pensions are supposed to reduce poverty and enable retirement.
2. Whether they can enable retirement depends on the nature of old-age work.
3. Ecuador offers a clean case to test that distinction.
4. The evidence supports a sharp divide between settings with discrete exit and settings with household-production work.
5. Therefore, average estimates obscure a key constraint on retirement in developing economies.

That story is stronger, cleaner, and much more publishable.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?

“I’d lead with: the same old-age pension causes urban elderly to leave the labor force, but leaves rural elderly still working.”

That is the memorable fact.

### Would people lean in?

Some would, but only if presented correctly. If the pitch is “an RD in Ecuador finds a 3 percentage point labor supply effect,” many economists will reach for their phones. If the pitch is “social pensions only buy retirement when older workers can actually separate from work,” they may lean in.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Probably: **“Is this really about urban versus rural, or about wage work versus self-employment/agriculture?”**  
And then: **“How general is this beyond Ecuador?”**

Those are exactly the questions the paper needs to anticipate and make central.

### If findings are modest

The average effect is modest; the heterogeneity is the real result. The null in rural areas could be interesting, but only if the paper frames it as informative about constraints on retirement rather than as “we found nothing there.” The paper is close to making that case, but it still feels somewhat like post hoc interpretation of subgroup results rather than a fully developed conceptual argument.

The null result among the poor is especially awkward for the current narrative. If the core claim is subsistence constraints, then that result may be useful, but as written it muddles rather than clarifies. The paper needs to decide which heterogeneity dimension is central and organize around it.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

At present, the gap is mainly a mix of **framing problem**, **scope problem**, and **ambition problem**.

- **Framing problem:** The science may be serviceable, but the paper is not selling the right question. “What is Ecuador’s pension effect?” is not enough. “When can social pensions purchase retirement?” is better.
- **Scope problem:** The headline mechanism rests on urban-rural heterogeneity, but that is one step removed from the real economics. The paper needs more direct evidence on type of work or labor market structure.
- **Ambition problem:** The paper is careful but safe. It presents an estimate and some subgroup splits. AER papers usually either change how we think about a question or offer evidence on a first-order issue at a scale or level of generality that forces attention. This draft does not yet do that.

I see less of a pure novelty problem than a problem of **insufficient conceptual elevation**. The result could be novel enough if framed properly, but “another pension RD with heterogeneity” is not.

### Single most impactful advice

**Reframe the paper around a general economic claim—that social pensions enable retirement only where older workers face a discrete exit margin—and then marshal every piece of the paper around that claim, ideally with more direct evidence on employment type rather than geography alone.**

That is the one change that would most improve its chances.
