## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple, policy-relevant question: when poor older adults become eligible for a non-contributory pension, do they actually retire, or do they keep working and simply add the transfer to earned income? Using Ecuador’s age-65 pension cutoff, the paper argues that the answer depends sharply on local labor market structure: urban elderly reduce labor force participation, while rural elderly do not, suggesting that pensions enable exit from wage work but not from subsistence-oriented self-employment.

A busy economist should care because the broader issue is not “does this Ecuadorian program work?” but “when do cash transfers to the elderly buy retirement, and when do they merely supplement continued work?” That is a first-order question for aging, informality, and social protection in developing countries.

### Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Not quite. The current introduction is competent, but it leads with Ecuador-specific institutional description and then moves too quickly into the design. It tells me **what the author does** before fully clarifying **what world-level question the paper answers**. The best part of the paper—the urban/rural asymmetry tied to sector of employment—is there, but it arrives as a result rather than as the animating question.

### What the first two paragraphs should say instead

The paper should open with something closer to this:

> Across developing countries, non-contributory pensions are often justified as a way to let poor elderly adults stop working when formal retirement systems do not cover them. But whether these transfers actually buy retirement is unclear: for some older workers, especially those in wage jobs, a modest pension may be enough to trigger labor market exit; for others, especially in subsistence agriculture or self-employment, the same transfer may simply supplement continued work. The key question is therefore not only whether non-contributory pensions reduce labor supply, but **for whom and in what labor market environments** they do so.

> This paper studies that question using Ecuador’s age-65 eligibility threshold for a non-contributory old-age pension. I show that eligibility reduces labor force participation overall, but this average masks a sharp divide: the effect is concentrated in urban areas and absent in rural areas. The central contribution is to show that the labor-supply effect of old-age pensions depends on the form of work available to the elderly—discrete exit from wage employment versus continued engagement in agricultural self-employment.

That is the pitch. It makes the paper about the world, not about “the first causal estimate for Ecuador.”

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### Contribution in one sentence

The paper’s contribution is to show that a non-contributory pension in Ecuador reduces elderly labor force participation only in urban areas, consistent with pensions enabling exit from wage employment but not from rural agricultural self-employment.

### Is this clearly differentiated from the closest papers?

Only partially. Right now the paper differentiates itself mostly by country and method (“first causal estimates for Ecuador,” “sharp age cutoff,” “joins Mexico, Bolivia, Chile…”). That is not enough for AER-level positioning. Country-level novelty is rarely sufficient unless Ecuador is doing something conceptually distinctive.

The differentiating contribution should instead be framed as:

- existing papers estimate average labor supply effects of old-age pensions;
- this paper argues that the key margin is **sector-specific exit feasibility**;
- the same transfer can induce retirement in settings with discrete wage work and no retirement in settings where work is inseparable from household production.

That is a real idea. But the paper currently under-sells it by presenting “urban-rural heterogeneity” as just one heterogeneity table rather than the conceptual core.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or filling a gap in a LITERATURE?

Mostly as a literature gap and a country gap. That is weaker.

“Here is the first causal estimate for Ecuador” is a journal-club contribution.  
“Whether old-age pensions purchase retirement depends on whether older workers can actually exit production” is a world question.

The paper needs much more of the latter.

### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what’s new?

At present, they would probably say: “It’s an RD on Ecuador’s pension cutoff; average effects are modest; there’s some urban-rural heterogeneity.”

That is not enough. You want them to say: “The paper argues that old-age pensions only reduce labor supply where elderly workers are in jobs with a clean exit margin. In subsistence agriculture they don’t buy retirement.”

That is more memorable and portable.

### What would make the contribution bigger?

Specific ways to enlarge it:

1. **Make sector, not geography, the main heterogeneity.**  
   Urban/rural is only a proxy. The paper’s real idea is about wage employment versus self-employment/agriculture. If the data allow, reorganize the results around baseline occupation/sector/job type, not residence.

2. **Show margins of adjustment more directly.**  
   If elderly urban workers are exiting wage employment, can the paper show a decline in wage work specifically, rather than just LFP? That would materially sharpen the mechanism.

3. **Connect to retirement, not just labor supply.**  
   Right now the paper risks sounding like a small labor-supply paper. If the framing is “do non-contributory pensions create meaningful retirement among informal workers?”, the stakes rise.

4. **Reframe the null in rural areas as substantive.**  
   The rural zero is potentially the most interesting fact in the paper, but only if interpreted as evidence that cash cannot substitute for household production at older ages.

5. **Think beyond Ecuador.**  
   The contribution gets bigger if the paper explicitly states a general proposition: pension incidence and behavioral response depend on the production technology of old-age work.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

In countries with limited contributory pension coverage, many elderly adults continue working late in life. Governments use non-contributory pensions partly to alleviate poverty and partly, implicitly or explicitly, to permit dignified retirement.

### Tension

But it is unclear whether these pensions actually enable retirement. Existing evidence is mixed, and the key reason may be overlooked: older workers face very different work technologies. Some can stop showing up to a wage job; others remain tied to farms, livestock, or household enterprises where “retirement” is not a discrete choice.

### Resolution

Using Ecuador’s age-65 cutoff, the paper finds a modest average decline in labor supply, but the effect is concentrated entirely in urban areas. The paper interprets this as evidence that pensions facilitate exit from wage employment but not from rural agricultural self-employment.

### Implications

The same pension can function as a retirement-enabling transfer in one context and pure income support in another. That matters for both theory and policy: one should not expect uniform labor-supply responses to old-age transfers in economies with large informal and agricultural sectors.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the ingredients, but the arc is not fully assembled. Right now it reads somewhat like:
- here is the program,
- here is the RD,
- here are the average results,
- here is a heterogeneity table,
- here is a mechanism story.

That is backwards. The urban/rural asymmetry is doing all the narrative work, so it should be introduced much earlier as the central puzzle and contribution. As written, the paper feels a bit like a collection of estimates with an ex post label (“sectoral exit asymmetry”).

### What story should it be telling?

The story should be:

> Old-age pensions are often assumed to buy retirement. But in informal economies, retirement is only possible when work has an exit margin. Ecuador shows this clearly: pensions reduce urban wage work but do not move rural agricultural labor. Therefore, the labor-supply effects of social pensions are not a scalar program parameter; they are mediated by labor market structure.

That is the story.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?

“I’ve got a paper showing that an old-age pension in Ecuador reduces work only in cities, not in the countryside—the same cash transfer seems to buy retirement for wage workers but not for subsistence farmers.”

That is the hook.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

If presented that way, some would lean in. If presented as “the first RD estimate of Ecuador’s non-contributory pension,” phones come out.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Probably one of these:

- “Is it really urban-rural, or is it wage work versus self-employment/agriculture?”
- “Why is the first stage so small?”
- “What does this imply for how we think about retirement in informal economies?”
- “Is the rural null because the transfer is too small, or because the work margin is fundamentally different?”

The strongest follow-up is the first one, and it points directly to the paper’s biggest strategic need: move from geography to labor market structure.

### If the findings are modest, is the modesty itself interesting?

The average effect is modest. On its own, that is not especially exciting. But the **pattern behind the average** is interesting. The paper should stop apologizing for the modest pooled effect and instead foreground that the average is uninformative because it combines two qualitatively different labor market environments.

The rural null can be very interesting if framed as:
- “cash transfers do not automatically purchase retirement,”
- “retirement is not a well-defined margin in many rural informal settings,”
- “labor supply responses to pensions depend on whether work is separable from household production.”

If not framed this way, it risks feeling like a failed attempt to find larger effects.
