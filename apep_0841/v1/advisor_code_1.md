**Idea Fidelity**

The paper does pursue several elements of the original manifest: it studies Poland’s 2019 universalization of the Family 500+ program, targets female employment as the outcome, and seeks to leverage regional heterogeneity in exposure to the “first-child shock.” However, it departs from the key identification strategy outlined in the manifest. In contrast, the submitted paper anchors its main specifications on a Poland-versus-CEE difference-in-differences (with the reference group being 35 NUTS2 regions in neighbouring countries) and only introduces the “treatment intensity” channel in a triple-difference that relies on the inverse TFR as a proxy for the share of one-child families. **Summary**

This short paper studies the July 2019 universalization of Poland’s Family 500+ child benefit, which removed the income test for first children, and asks whether the policy reduced female employment. Using a difference-in-differences design that compares Polish NUTS2 regions to several Central and Eastern European comparators, augmented by a gender-gap outcome and a triple-difference with a treatment intensity based on the inverse total fertility rate, the author finds no statistically significant decline in female employment (the largest negative point estimate is −1.4 pp against Visegrad controls). The paper interprets the null as evidence of a “universality discount,” whereby removing the prior means test may have offset the income effect through a simultaneous reduction in implicit participation taxes.

**Essential Points**

1. **Credibility of the Poland vs. CEE DiD is doubtful.** The central DiD comparison pools multiple countries that were experiencing very different macroeconomic and labor market trajectories prior to 2019. The male employment placebo is significant, implying that Poland was already diverging from the control group, which casts doubt on the parallel trends assumption. The event studies are only presented for the within-Poland intensity specification, so we cannot assess pre-trends for the main DiD. You need to demonstrate that the Poland-control difference is not driven by broader cross-country convergence/divergence—either through a more convincing set of comparators (e.g., using synthetic control or tighter “Visegrad-only” matching), through differential trends controls, or by pivoting to a within-Poland design where the identifying variation comes from pre-existing regional shares of one-child families, as originally envisaged.

2. **Treatment intensity proxy is insufficiently precise.** The paper claims to exploit variation in the share of one-child families, but the actual measure used— the standardized inverse of the total fertility rate—does not isolate the policy-relevant exposure. The TFR mixes changes in fertility behaviour and does not directly map to the number of previously ineligible one-child households. Without a direct measure, the interpretation of the triple-difference coefficient as the effect on newly eligible families is tenuous. Please construct a treatment intensity variable based on the actual cross-sectional share of one-child families (from GUS/Eurostat household or family statistics), or alternatively, use the share of families just above the income threshold if that data is available. This will also allow you to implement the within-Poland DiD proposed in the manifest, strengthening the causal claim.

3. **Inference and heterogeneity need bolstering.** With only 17 treated regions, clustering inference is fragile, and the paper’s permutation/LOO exercises (which are mentioned but not shown in the main text) should be reported more transparently. Moreover, the null result on average could mask responses concentrated among specific subpopulations (e.g., low-educated mothers, rural households, single parents). Without any heterogeneity or microdata exploration, it is hard to conclude that the universalization had “no effect.” Please present the exact permutation distribution, report cluster-robust p-values explicitly, and—if data permits—explore heterogeneity using finer-level aggregates (e.g., NUTS3/powiat data or education-based employment rates). If microdata are unavailable, consider alternative outcomes (e.g., female inactivity rates, part-time employment) to triangulate the mechanism.

**Suggestions**

1. **Refine the control group and show parallel trends explicitly.** Given the limitations of the cross-country DiD, consider re-estimating the main specification using a synthetic control that matches Poland’s pre-2019 female employment path (or its gender gap) more closely. Alternatively, restrict the control group further to the Visegrad countries and add region-specific linear trends, then present the associated event studies. At minimum, provide event studies for the baseline Poland vs. CEE DiD to convince readers that there were no pre-existing diverging trends.

2. **Construct and justify a more precise treatment intensity.** Use GUS family data or Eurostat’s `demo_fam` tables to compute the fraction of households with exactly one child in each Polish region before 2019. This will allow you to mimic the manifest’s design and to speak directly to the question of newly eligible families. If such data are unavailable, at least provide a detailed justification for why inverse TFR proxies the policy exposure, including correlations between TFR and the share of one-child families. You could also consider using birth order distributions or the share of households ineligible under the income test as alternative proxies.

3. **Expand the discussion of mechanisms with supplementary evidence.** The “universality discount” narrative is compelling but would benefit from additional backing. For example, examine female labor force participation separately from employment to detect whether mothers shifted into inactivity or part-time work. If possible, use regional poverty or consumption data (as mentioned in the manifest) to test whether the cash transfer’s primary effect was income smoothing rather than labor supply adjustment. Additionally, present evidence on employment participation taxes (e.g., the share of households near the 800 PLN threshold) to support the claim that removing the means test mitigated a participation tax.

4. **Clarify robustness analyses and inference.** Incorporate the permutation and leave-one-out results into the main tables or appendices with full detail (e.g., reporting the distribution of placebo coefficients and their p-values). When discussing COVID-19 robustness, specify whether the 2020–2021 exclusion also affects the post-treatment window for the event study and whether the standard errors change materially. Provide sensitivity of coefficients to alternative clustering (e.g., two-way clustering by country × year) and report the number of clusters used for inference.

5. **Be explicit about the research design’s limitations.** The current draft mentions limitations in passing but does not integrate them with the identification discussion. Strengthen the narrative around why a null result can still be policy-relevant, while acknowledging that the design cannot isolate family-level responses. Highlight the external validity constraints (e.g., whether more generous transfers in a looser labor market would produce different effects) and the potential for offsetting mechanisms, such as childcare consumption or labor demand shocks.

6. **Leverage microdata if feasible.** The manifest noted the possibility of EU-LFS microdata; if these data are obtainable (perhaps at the micro-regional level via Eurostat’s microdata access procedure), use them to directly track mothers of one child, distinguishing between those just above/below the income threshold. Even if the full microdata are unattainable, see if aggregated statistics (e.g., employment rates of women with children by age) are available at the country or regional level. This would allow you to more directly test the labor supply model’s predictions.

Implementing these suggestions would help restore fidelity to the original manifest’s identification strategy, strengthen causal interpretation, and offer readers greater confidence in the null result’s policy implications.
