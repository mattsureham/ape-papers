## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

**The Pitch:** While existing research focuses on how the Florida "pill mill" crackdown reduced the *volume* of opioids, this paper argues that the policy fundamentally changed the *composition* of the market. By analyzing 178 million DEA transactions, the author shows that high-dose pills—the primary currency of illegal diversion—collapsed by nearly 40% while legitimate low-dose prescribing remained stable. This "dosage shift" provides a new empirical fingerprint to distinguish between medical use and criminal diversion, offering a potential early-warning system for future drug epidemics.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this reasonably well, but the first two paragraphs are a bit "literature-heavy." They spend too much time listing citations (Rutkow et al., Alpert et al.) and not enough time on the economic intuition.
**The revised pitch should be:** "Traditional supply-side evaluations of the opioid crisis treat all pills as equal. However, a 5mg pill for post-surgical pain and a 30mg 'blue' used for snorting serve two different economic markets: legitimate medicine and illicit diversion. This paper uses transaction-level data to show that the Florida crackdown didn't just 'tax' the whole market; it selectively dismantled the diversion channel by shifting the dosage composition of shipments, a result that is invisible in aggregate data."

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**The Contribution:** The paper identifies "dosage-strength composition" as a novel empirical object that successfully separates the illicit diversion channel from the legitimate medical market.

- **Differentiation:** It differentiates itself well from Alpert et al. (2018) and Evans et al. (2019) by moving from *volume/substitution* to *internal market structure*.
- **Question vs. Literature:** It currently sits somewhere in between. It frames itself as "filling a gap" (no one has looked at dosage), but the stronger version is the "Question about the WORLD": *How do we identify a shadow market when it is hidden inside a legal supply chain?*
- **AER Potential:** To make this bigger, the author needs to lean harder into the "early warning" policy implication. If this metric had been used in 2008, could we have prevented 100,000 deaths? That is the AER-level stakes.

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

- **Closest Neighbors:** Alpert et al. (2018, AEJ: Policy); Evans et al. (2019, REStat); Schnell (2017, Working Paper/Molly Schnell's broader work on physician behavior).
- **Positioning:** The paper builds on these but should more aggressively "synthesize" them. It provides the empirical proof for the "secondary market" theories proposed by Schnell.
- **Narrow vs. Broad:** It is currently a bit narrow (Florida health policy). To reach the AER audience, it should speak to the broader literature on **"Optimal Regulation of Dual-Use Goods"** (goods that have both high social value and high diversion/abuse potential).
- **Missing Conversations:** It should reference the literature on "Alchian-Allen effects" (the "Third Law of Demand"). Usually, when you tax a good (crackdown), you shift consumption toward *higher* quality/potency. This paper finds the *opposite*. Explaining why the "Potency Effect" goes the other way here is a high-level economic puzzle.

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

- **Setup:** Florida was the "Wild West" of opioids, where doctors dispensed 30mg oxycodone like candy.
- **Tension:** Did the 2011 crackdown just stop people from getting medicine, or did it specifically hit the "bad guys"? If it hit everyone equally, the *average* dose wouldn't change.
- **Resolution:** The average dose plummeted. The high-dose share vanished in high-volume counties but didn't budge in rural ones.
- **Implications:** Regulators shouldn't just count pills; they should watch the *mix*. A shift toward high-potency units is the "smoke" that signals a diversion "fire."

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong. The "Asymmetric Boom-Bust" (Table 3) is a great narrative device. It shows the market "building up" and then "being dismantled."

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

- **The Lead Fact:** "In 2010, 98 of the top 100 oxycodone doctors were in Florida, and when the law hit, the 'street-preferred' 30mg pill share dropped by 40% while the 'medicine' pills stayed the same."
- **Reaction:** Economists will lean in because it solves a "signal-to-noise" problem. It proves we can see the "invisible hand" of the black market in the official data of the legal market.
- **Follow-up:** "Does this happen in other states? Can we use this to find the next fentanyl-like surge in other substances?"

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

- **Front-load the "Why":** Move the "Why composition matters" logic from page 3/4 to the very first page. 
- **The "Pill-Weighting" Argument:** This is currently a bit defensive in the text. Make it a centerpiece: "Unweighted results are the wrong question; pill-weighted results tell us what's happening to the *market*."
- **Appendix to Main:** The "Permutation Test" in the appendix (rotating the treatment to GA and AL) is a very strong visual for the AER. It should be a figure in the main text.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

**The Gap:** Currently, it's a very high-quality "Policy Evaluation." To be AER, it needs to be a "Market Discovery" paper.

**Single Biggest Improvement:** Explicitly test the **Alchian-Allen "Potency" hypothesis.** Why did the crackdown lead to a *decrease* in potency (the "Dosage Shift") rather than the traditional "Iron Law of Prohibition" (where crackdowns lead to *more* concentrated forms, like moving from beer to liquor)? If the author can explain why this specific regulatory structure (PDMPs/Clinic registration) reversed the standard economic intuition of drug potency, it becomes a "must-cite" paper for all of regulatory economics.
