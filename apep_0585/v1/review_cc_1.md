# Internal Review (Claude Code) - Round 1

**Reviewer:** Claude Code (self-review)
**Date:** 2026-03-10
**Paper:** The Dog That Didn't Bark: EU Medical Device Regulation and Short-Run Production Effects

## Summary
This paper estimates the causal effect of the EU Medical Device Regulation (MDR) on aggregate medical device production using a difference-in-differences design comparing NACE C325 to control manufacturing sectors. The paper finds no statistically detectable effect across multiple specifications, with the preferred estimate of 3.8 index points (SE = 7.7, p = 0.63) and wild cluster bootstrap p = 0.63. Three hypotheses are offered for the null: staggered transition deadlines, volume-versus-variety compositional effects, and anticipatory front-loading.

## Assessment

### Strengths
1. **Important, timely question** — First causal analysis of MDR's production effects
2. **Clean identification** — Country-by-year FE absorb all country-level shocks
3. **Comprehensive robustness** — Wild bootstrap, 2022-as-post, placebos, LOO, RI, alternative controls
4. **Honest framing** — Acknowledges limitations (volume ≠ variety, few clusters, short post-period)
5. **Strong institutional detail** — Notified body bottleneck narrative is compelling

### Weaknesses
1. **Six treated countries** — Fundamental power limitation
2. **Control-sector sensitivity** — Pairwise estimates range from -7.8 to +14.6
3. **Production ≠ innovation** — Outcome captures volume, not product variety or market entry
4. **Short post-period** — Most binding MDR constraints haven't materialized yet (2027-2028 deadlines)
5. **DDD relies on Turkey** — Single non-EU comparator for treated sector

### Verdict
The paper addresses a policy-relevant question with a credible design, though power limitations inherent in the data structure constrain what can be claimed. The framing revisions (from "innovation decline" to "short-run production effects") appropriately calibrate the contribution.

**DECISION: CONDITIONALLY ACCEPT**

The paper is ready for publication as a policy-relevant working paper, with the understanding that the contribution is necessarily constrained by data availability.
