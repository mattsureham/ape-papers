## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
The paper examines whether OSHA’s new mandate for detailed, public electronic injury reporting (the 2023 Rule) incentivizes firms to improve workplace safety or merely to manipulate their size to avoid disclosure. Using a difference-in-discontinuities design at the 100-employee threshold, the paper finds that firms strategically "dodge" the requirement by bunching just below the threshold, while those that do comply show no improvement in actual injury rates.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this very clearly. The first two paragraphs use a compelling narrative hook (the warehouse worker in Alabama) and immediately pivot to the "sunlight vs. blinds" metaphor. The pitch is already at an AER-level of clarity.

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**The Contribution:** The paper documents that "regulation by information" can be completely undermined by strategic threshold manipulation, proving that firms prioritize avoiding the "blinds" of disclosure over the "sunlight" of safety improvements.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It is well-differentiated. While bunching is common in tax (Kleven) and size-based regulation (Garicano), applying it to a *pure information mandate* is novel.
*   **World vs. Literature:** It is framed as a question about the world (Does sunlight disinfect?). This is a major strength.
*   **What would make it bigger?** To reach the "Top 5" status, the paper needs to move beyond just documenting the "dodge" and more deeply explore the **welfare implications** of the dodge. Are firms actually firing people, or just using staffing agencies? If they use agencies, does safety actually get *worse* because of the lack of direct oversight?

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
*   **Closest Neighbors:** *Jin and Leslie (2003)* on hygiene grades; *Hamilton (1995)* on TRI; *Garicano et al. (2016)* on firm size distortions; *Johnson (2020)* on OSHA shaming.
*   **Positioning:** It should position itself as the "skeptical counterpoint" to the optimistic "regulation by information" literature. It suggests that information disclosure is not a low-cost panacea if the threshold is manipulable.
*   **Unexpected Connection:** The paper could connect to the **Contracting/Outsourcing** literature. If the "dodge" is achieved through sub-contracting, it links to the "Fissured Workplace" (Weil) narrative, suggesting that transparency mandates might accidentally accelerate the shift toward precarious, third-party employment.

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC
*   **Setup:** Regulatory agencies are moving toward "shaming" and transparency as a cheap alternative to physical inspections.
*   **Tension:** Transparency only works if firms are "caught" in the light. But if the light has a known boundary, firms can simply step back into the shadows.
*   **Resolution:** Significant bunching below 100 employees; zero impact on safety for those above 100.
*   **Implications:** Threshold-based transparency is a flawed design; mandates should be universal or based on non-manipulable criteria.

**Evaluation:** The arc is exceptionally strong. It moves from a micro-tragedy to a macro-market failure.

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
*   **The Fact:** "Firms are willing to forgo 3-5 full-time employees—costing them up to $250k in value-add—just to keep their injury logs secret."
*   **Reaction:** Lean in. It suggests the "reputational cost" of being known as a dangerous workplace is perceived as being massive by firms.
*   **Follow-up:** "If the reputational cost is so high, why didn't they just fix the safety issues?" This is the core tension the paper needs to emphasize.

---

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
*   **Front-loading:** The paper is well-structured. The "Triple Test" (treated vs. control, pre vs. post) is its strongest econometric asset and is presented early.
*   **Refinement:** The "Outcome Decomposition" (Section 5.5) is currently a bit weak because it's a null. I would move the "Donut Hole" robustness check closer to the main results, as it's the most honest way to handle the selection bias created by the bunching.

---

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The gap is **Mechanism and Welfare.** 
Currently, the paper proves firms manipulate. It doesn't prove *how* they do it or *what it costs the workers*. If the author can use supplementary data (e.g., County Business Patterns or job postings) to show a spike in "Staffing Agency" usage specifically in Appendix B industries near the 100-worker mark, the paper becomes a "slam dunk."

**Single biggest piece of advice:** Focus the revision on the "How" of the dodge. If you can show that this disclosure mandate actually pushed workers into the "shadow economy" of staffing agencies—where safety is often worse—you have a seminal paper on the unintended consequences of transparency.
