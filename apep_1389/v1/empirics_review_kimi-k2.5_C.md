 **Review: The Disclosure Dodge**

**1. Idea Fidelity**

The paper faithfully executes the research design outlined in the manifest. It correctly implements the difference-in-discontinuities (DiD) strategy at OSHA’s 100-employee threshold, leveraging the triple variation (cross-sectional threshold, temporal pre/post, and cross-industry Appendix B status). The analysis preserves the core insight: testing whether “regulation by information” induces compliance (injury reductions) or avoidance (bunching below the threshold). The authors appropriately prioritize the bunching analysis (McCrary tests) as the headline finding, while transparently reporting null results on injury rates. No essential elements of the original design are omitted; if anything, the paper deepens the analysis by engaging seriously with the selection problems induced by bunching.

**2. Summary**

This paper evaluates OSHA’s 2023 electronic injury reporting mandate using a difference-in-discontinuities design at the 100-employee threshold, documenting significant bunching just below the cutoff in treated industries (evidence of strategic avoidance) but finding no statistically significant reduction in injury rates among compliers during the first year of implementation. The results suggest that when disclosure obligations are tied to a size threshold, firms respond on the extensive margin (employee count manipulation) rather than the intensive margin (safety improvements).

**3. Essential Points**

1. **Severely Underpowered Design with Extreme Outcome Dispersion.** The DiD specification relies on a single post-treatment year (2024) to identify the triple-interaction coefficient, effectively reducing the design to a cross-sectional comparison of discontinuity changes. Compounding this, the outcome variables exhibit extreme overdispersion: Table 1 reports Total Case Rate (TCR) means of approximately 18–21 but standard deviations of 1,800–2,400 (ratios exceeding 100:1). This variance inflation renders the study dramatically underpowered. The reported DiD point estimate of −110.5 (SE = 104.6) for TCR is imprecise (95% CI: [−315, 94]), and with such noisy data, the null result is consistent with both a true zero effect and a failure to detect economically meaningful improvements (e.g., a 10% reduction). The authors cannot claim “no effect” with confidence; rather, they should report the minimum detectable effect given the observed variance.

2. **Invalid Parallel Trends and Pre-Trend Violations.** The event study (Figure 3 and Table 3) reveals a significant discontinuity in 2022 (estimate = 24.0, *p* = 0.017)—two years before the mandate—undermining the parallel trends assumption required for causal interpretation of the DiD. With only one post-treatment year, the design cannot distinguish between a true treatment effect in 2024 and random year-to-year fluctuation in the threshold discontinuity. The 2024 estimate (21.1) is statistically indistinguishable from the 2022 spike, suggesting the “null” result may simply reflect noise. The authors treat the 2022 estimate as a false positive, but with a single treated year, the specification lacks theover-identifying restrictions needed to validate this claim.

3. **Selection Bias from Bunching Invalidates the Injury-Rate Comparisons.** The McCrary test confirms manipulation (bunching) below 100 in treated industries post-2024. This creates a classic Lee (2009) selection problem: the firms remaining above 100 are positively or negatively selected (the paper suggests “adverse selection of stayers” where unsafe firms avoid reporting). This compositional shift invalidates the sharp RDD and contaminates the DiD if selection dynamics differ between Appendix B and non-Appendix B industries. The paper’s “donut hole” estimate (excluding 98–102 employees) shows a near-zero effect (−3.0, SE = 3.7), suggesting the main DiD estimates are biased by the manipulation window, yet the abstract emphasizes the noisy DiD results rather than this cleaner specification.

**4. Suggestions**

*Power and Specification:* Given the extreme right skew in injury rates (likely from small establishments with few hours worked generating spuriously high rates), report results using inverse hyperbolic sine (IHS) transformations or quantile regression (median effects) rather than means. Winsorizing at the 99th percentile is insufficient when the SD is 100 times the mean. Conduct formal power calculations: given the observed residual variance, what is the minimum detectable effect size (MDE) for a 80% powered test? Report this MDE alongside null results to clarify whether the data could have detected plausible effect sizes (e.g., a 5–10% injury reduction).

*Inference with One Treated Year:* Because the design has only one post-treatment period, conventional clustered standard errors may be anti-conservative (the number of “treated clusters” is effectively the number of industries in 2024). Implement randomization inference or permutation tests: randomly assign the “post” year to 2016–2023 and re-estimate the DiD coefficient to construct an exact null distribution. This would properly account for the fact that the 2024 “treatment” timing is not randomly assigned across years. Additionally, exclude 2020–2021 (COVID-19 disruption) from the pre-period to ensure the parallel trends assumption is not contaminated by pandemic-era volatility in employment and safety protocols.

*Address the 2022 Pre-Trend:* The significant 2022 discontinuity warrants investigation. Is it driven by a specific industry or data quirk (e.g., BLS updating Appendix B classifications)? Show an event study by industry or present the “placebo” DiD using 2022 as a fake treatment year; if the DiD coefficient is significant when pretending 2022 is treated, the design is compromised. Consider a sensitivity analysis dropping 2022 to verify robustness.

*Selection and Bounding:* The bunching analysis is the paper’s strongest contribution. Lean into this by making the donut-hole specification (excluding the manipulation window) the primary result for injury rates, relegating the full-sample DiD to a “naive” comparison. Explicitly model the selection problem: use the method of Gruber & Kleven or Diamond & Persson to estimate bounds on the treatment effect accounting for the observed bunching. Report Lee bounds that assume the “dodgers” have injury rates at the 5th and 95th percentiles of the observed distribution to bracket the true complier average treatment effect.

*Mechanism Evidence:* The paper hypothesizes that firms use staffing agencies or seasonal adjustments to manipulate headcounts, but provides no direct evidence. Link the ITA data to the BLS Quarterly Census of Employment and Wages (QCEW) or Census County Business Patterns to see if establishments near 100 exhibit differential growth in temporary help services employment or establishment splitting. If possible, identify “likely dodgers” (firms just below 100 in 2024 with large employment drops from 2023) and compare their pre-period injury trends to compliers—this would test whether dodgers are selected on pre-existing safety quality.

*Control Group Concerns:* The non-Appendix B control group has vastly different baseline injury rates (Table 1 shows means of 101 vs. 8.8, driven by outliers) and different industry composition. Show covariate balance plots for the DiD: do the non-Appendix B establishments near 100 look like the Appendix B establishments in terms of pre-period TCR trends, hours worked, and state fixed effects? If the parallel trends assumption is suspect, consider a synthetic control approach or restrict the control group to the subset of non-Appendix B industries with baseline TCR distributions overlapping the treated group.

*Dynamic Effects:* Acknowledge explicitly that 2024 is the first year of a potentially dynamic process. Regulatory disclosure may take years to affect injury rates through insurance pricing or worker sorting, while avoidance (bunching) is immediate. Frame the injury-rate results as “short-run effects” or “first-year effects” rather than definitive evidence of ineffectiveness. Propose a pre-analysis plan for 2025–2026 data updates to test for lagged effects, particularly if the public database becomes widely used by workers or investors.

*Heterogeneous Effects:* The industry heterogeneity analysis suggests manufacturing shows null effects while services show positive selection. Substantiate this with formal tests of interaction effects rather than separate regressions, and discuss whether the bunching manipulation is stronger in services (where staffing agencies are prevalent) versus manufacturing (where production needs may fix employment levels). This could illuminate which regulatory design features (industry-specific vs. universal) minimize avoidance.
