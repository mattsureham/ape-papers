## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper studies a striking policy shock: Nigeria abruptly closed all land borders in 2019, effectively shutting down formal overland food imports. The paper asks whether such a dramatic trade restriction raised food prices disproportionately in markets near the border, as standard spatial trade logic would suggest, and finds that it did not: price increases were broadly national rather than border-localized.

Busy economists should care because this is, in principle, a clean test of a basic prediction about the spatial incidence of trade barriers in a setting where informal trade, weak enforcement, and domestic arbitrage may matter more than textbook models assume.

**Does the paper articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?** Not quite. The current opening is vivid, but it takes too long to get to the actual economic question and overstates the policy drama relative to the conceptual contribution. The paper’s real hook is not “Nigeria did something extreme,” but “an extreme trade barrier did not generate the spatial price pattern standard models predict.”

**What the first two paragraphs should say instead:**

> Trade barriers are typically expected to have geographically uneven effects: when a border closes, markets closest to the disrupted trade route should experience the largest price changes. This prediction is central to spatial models of trade and to how policymakers monitor food-security shocks, yet there is little direct evidence from developing-country settings where informal trade and domestic arbitrage are pervasive.
>
> This paper studies Nigeria’s sudden 2019 closure of all land borders, a large and unexpected disruption to overland food imports. Using monthly market-level price data, I test whether staple food prices rose more in border markets than in interior markets. They did not. Prices increased nationally, but not disproportionately near the border, suggesting that domestic market integration, informal evasion, or expectations diffused the shock across space much more rapidly than standard spatial trade intuition would imply.

That is the pitch. Put the question first, then the result, then why it matters.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:**  
The paper shows that a major border closure in Nigeria did not produce the localized border-area food price increases predicted by standard spatial trade models, implying that trade shocks in this setting propagated nationally rather than spatially concentrating near the border.

### Evaluation

**Is this clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?**  
Only partially. The paper cites broad trade and market integration literatures, but the differentiation is still fuzzy. Right now, the contribution reads like: “another reduced-form study of a trade-policy shock with a null result.” The paper needs to be much sharper about what exact prediction from which literature it is testing, and why existing papers have not already answered that question.

**Is the contribution framed as a question about the world or a gap in a literature?**  
It is mostly framed as a question about the world, which is good. But it sometimes slips into “this contributes to several literatures,” which weakens the message. The strongest framing is: **When a porous developing-country state imposes an extreme trade barrier, where do the price effects show up?** That is a world question.

**Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what’s new?**  
Not confidently. They might say: “It’s a DiD on Nigeria’s border closure and they don’t find differential effects near borders.” That is not yet a compelling novelty statement. The introduction needs to convert the null into a conceptual surprise, not just a regression outcome.

**What would make this contribution bigger? Be specific.**
1. **Make the paper about incidence, not nulls.**  
   The key fact is not “no statistically significant effect.” It is “the incidence was national, not local.” The paper should foreground aggregate price increases plus flat spatial differentials. That is much more interesting than “null spatial gradient.”
2. **Show where the shock did appear if not near the border.**  
   If the paper can document stronger patterns along domestic distribution networks, production zones, or high-consumption urban centers, the story becomes much larger. Right now it says “not here,” but not convincingly “instead, there.”
3. **Deepen the mechanism comparison.**  
   Separate more clearly among domestic arbitrage, continued smuggling, expectations, and import substitution. Even reduced-form evidence that discriminates among these would enlarge the paper materially.
4. **Use the event to speak to a broader conceptual issue.**  
   For example: in weakly enforced states, formal trade policy may poorly predict actual spatial incidence. That is a bigger takeaway than “Nigeria is integrated.”
5. **Potentially add quantity/availability outcomes if available.**  
   If price effects were flat but stockouts or quality margins were spatially heterogeneous, the paper becomes much richer and avoids overinterpreting price equalization as welfare equalization.

- In formal, well-enforced settings, trade barriers may generate predictable local incidence.
- In settings with informal trade and domestic redistribution, the same formal shock may have very different spatial manifestation.

That is a useful boundary condition, not a refutation.

### Too narrow or too broad?
Currently **too broad in citation, too narrow in actual conversation**. The introduction lists many literatures, but the actual paper is about one thing: **spatial incidence of trade shocks under weak enforcement and high informality**. It should narrow to that.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?
Most notably:

- **State capacity and policy implementation**.
- Potentially **pass-through and incidence under distribution frictions**.
- More recent work on **African food markets and trader networks**.

The paper currently over-relies on canonical citations and under-engages the literatures that would make the setting intellectually distinctive.

### Is it having the right conversation?
Not yet. The most impactful framing may come not from “trade protection” per se, but from connecting trade policy to:
- **state capacity**,
- **informality/evasion**,

That is a much more modern and interesting conversation than “does trade protection raise prices?”

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
A large country with substantial food import dependence, extensive informal overland trade, and weak border enforcement suddenly closes its land borders.

### Tension
Standard spatial reasoning says markets nearest the disrupted border should experience the largest price increases. But in a setting with smuggling, domestic redistribution, and expectations, that prediction may fail.

### Resolution
Prices rose overall, but not more in border markets than in interior markets; the shock appears to have been transmitted nationally rather than locally.

### Implications
Formal trade barriers need not map cleanly into local incidence in low-capacity, high-informality settings. Policymakers and economists may misread who bears the burden of trade restrictions if they infer exposure from geography alone.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
**Yes, but it is diluted.** There is a real story here. The problem is that the paper repeatedly falls back on a laundry list of robustness checks and literature claims instead of tightening the core arc. At times it reads like a competent paper organized around a null estimate, rather than a conceptual paper organized around a surprising fact.

### What story should it be telling?
Not:  
“Here is a policy shock, and our DiD finds no significant spatial effect.”

But rather:  
“Here is a basic prediction economists would make about trade barriers. In this important setting, that prediction fails. Why? Because formal policy, actual enforcement, and domestic price transmission are not the same thing.”

That is the AER version of the story.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?
“Nigeria shut all land borders for fifteen months, rice prices rose, but they did **not** rise more near the borders.”

That is the fact.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
They would **initially lean in**, because the policy shock is dramatic and the result is surprising. But the second minute matters. If the follow-up is just “and here are many non-rejections,” phones come out. If the follow-up is “which means formal trade barriers can have nationally diffused incidence in informal economies,” they stay engaged.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Almost certainly:  
**“So where did the incidence come from, and what mechanism flattened the gradient?”**

That is the question the paper currently cannot answer strongly enough.

### If findings are null or modest, is the null itself interesting?
Yes—**conditionally**. The null is interesting because it contradicts a first-order theoretical prediction in a high-salience policy event. But the paper must stop talking about the null as if “null results are valuable too.” That is defensive and usually unpersuasive. The right stance is: **this is not a null in the ordinary sense; it is a surprising incidence pattern.**

At present, the paper does not fully make that case. It still feels somewhat like a failed attempt to find a gradient, retrofitted into a contribution. The fix is framing and mechanism.
