## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper studies Nigeria’s abrupt 2019 land-border closure and asks a simple, economically meaningful question: when a government suddenly cuts off overland imports, do prices rise most in markets near the border, as standard spatial intuition suggests, or do the effects diffuse nationally? Using market-level food price data, the paper’s headline result is that prices rose, but not disproportionately in border markets: the shock appears to have propagated broadly rather than locally.

A busy economist should care because this is potentially a paper about the incidence of trade barriers in a setting with weak enforcement, informal trade, and large domestic arbitrage networks. If true, it says something important not just about Nigeria, but about how protection works in low-state-capacity environments.

### Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Not really. The opening is vivid, but it spends too much time on scene-setting and too little time stating the economic question in a way that immediately signals why this matters beyond Nigeria. The introduction gets to the question by paragraph 3 and to the key result by paragraph 5; that is too slow for a top-journal pitch, especially for a paper with a null result.

### The pitch the paper should have

Nigeria abruptly closed all land borders in August 2019, effectively shutting down a major channel of food imports and informal re-exports. This paper asks whether such a trade shock has local incidence—raising prices most in border markets—or whether, in an economy with dense internal arbitrage and persistent smuggling, the shock is transmitted nationally. Using monthly market-level food prices, I find no detectable border-versus-interior price gradient for rice or other staples: the closure raised prices, but not more near the border. The broader implication is that in low-enforcement environments, the spatial incidence of protection may be determined less by formal policy geography than by domestic market integration and informal trade networks.

That is the paper’s best version of itself. Lead with the economic question, the surprising fact, and the broader implication.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper documents that Nigeria’s dramatic 2019 border closure did not generate the localized border-area food price increases that standard spatial intuition would predict, implying nationally diffused rather than locally concentrated price incidence.

### Is this clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?

Only partially. The introduction lists several literatures, but the paper does not sharply separate its contribution from its nearest empirical neighbors. Right now the paper risks sounding like “another reduced-form paper on prices after a trade shock,” except in an African setting and with a null result.

The nearest neighbors are not really the U.S. tariff-passthrough papers alone. Those are useful, but they are not the closest conceptual comparators. The more relevant neighbors are:

- papers on tariff/pass-through and incidence of trade shocks, especially **Amiti, Redding, and Weinstein (2019)** and **Fajgelbaum et al. (2020)**;
- work on spatial arbitrage and market integration, such as **Ravallion (1986)**, **Baulch (1997)**, **Aker (2010)**, and related African market integration papers;
- work on domestic trade costs and internal propagation, such as **Atkin and Donaldson (2015)**, **Donaldson and Hornbeck (2016)**, **Sotelo (2020)**, and **Allen and Arkolakis / Allen and Atkin-style spatial equilibrium work**.

The current draft gestures at all of these, but does not crisply say: here is what those papers establish, and here is the precise fact we add.

It is mixed, but too often framed as “contributes to several literatures.” That is weaker. The world question is stronger:

**When a government imposes a severe trade restriction in an economy where formal enforcement is incomplete and domestic arbitrage is active, where do the price effects show up geographically?**

That is a real-world question. The literature-gap framing should be subordinate.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?

Not cleanly. Right now they might say: “It’s a DiD paper using Nigeria’s border closure, and it finds no stronger effect near the border.” That is accurate, but not yet exciting.

What they should be able to say is: “It shows that in a setting with informal trade and market integration, a major border closure did not have local price incidence. That changes how we think about the geography of protection in weak-state-capacity environments.”

That requires sharper framing and much less literature-listing.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Several possibilities:

1. **Reframe the core question away from ‘testing textbook spatial models’ and toward ‘where protection bites in low-capacity states.’**  
   That is the biggest strategic upgrade. Right now the paper overstates the clash with theory and understates the institutional point.

2. **Show the aggregate price effect more centrally.**  
   The paper keeps saying “prices rose nationally,” but the main design only estimates the differential border-interior effect. For storytelling, the reader needs the two facts side by side: there was a real national food price increase, but no local incidence gradient. Without that contrast, the paper feels like “nothing happened,” when its claim is actually “something important happened, but not where you’d expect.”

3. **Bring in cross-border mirror outcomes or complementary outcomes if possible.**  
   The paper itself hints that prices in Benin/Niger border markets, quantities, stockouts, quality, or trader behavior would make the story much bigger. As written, the mechanisms section is almost entirely speculative. Even one additional outcome that distinguishes among market integration, smuggling, and expectations would materially raise ambition.

4. **Use the closure to say something general about de jure versus de facto trade barriers.**  
   The interesting contrast is between the formal policy being geographically sharp and the economic incidence being geographically flat. That could be a much bigger contribution than “a null spatial gradient in Nigeria.”

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Nigeria depends heavily on rice imports, much of it informally routed through neighboring countries. In 2019 it abruptly closed all land borders, seemingly creating a massive and geographically targeted trade shock.

### Tension

Standard intuition says border areas should be hit first and hardest. But in an economy with domestic arbitrage, trader expectations, and continued smuggling, that need not be true. So where do the costs of protection actually land?

### Resolution

Prices rose, but not more in border markets. The price effect appears spatially diffuse rather than localized.

### Implications

The geographic incidence of trade barriers may look very different in low-capacity, high-informality environments than in high-capacity settings. Policymakers and researchers should not assume that a geographically sharp policy generates geographically concentrated price effects.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the ingredients, but the arc is muddled by excess exposition and by an overextended “contributes to several literatures” introduction. The paper does have a story; it is not merely a pile of estimates. But it keeps interrupting its own story.

The main problem is that the paper toggles among three stories:

1. a trade policy paper,
2. a market integration paper,
3. a methodological defense of a null result.

All three are present, but none fully dominates. For AER purposes, it needs one central narrative with the others subordinated.

### What story should it be telling?

The cleanest story is:

**A government drew a hard line at the border, but the economy erased that line in prices. Why? Because protection in this environment is mediated by internal arbitrage and informal trade rather than by formal policy geography.**

That is memorable. That story can support the null result. The current title and some of the framing point in this direction, but the introduction then gets bogged down.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?

“Nigeria sealed all land borders overnight for more than a year, but rice prices did not rise more in markets near the border than in the interior.”

That is the lead.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

Initially, they would lean in. The setting is dramatic and the fact is surprising. But then the next question comes fast: “Interesting—so why not?” If the presenter cannot answer that with evidence rather than conjecture, attention fades.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Almost certainly: **Was the closure not actually binding near the border, or did arbitrage transmit the shock nationally?**  
And then: **Do quantities, stockouts, or prices across the border tell us which?**

This is where the current paper is vulnerable. It has a surprising fact, but not a decisive mechanism.

### Is the null result itself interesting?

Yes, potentially. This is not a failed experiment if framed correctly. The null is informative because:

- the policy shock is large and salient;
- theory and institutional intuition suggest a localized effect;
- the paper argues there was an aggregate food price increase, just not a local one.

That combination can make a null result important. But the authors need to work harder to convince the reader that the null is the central economic fact, not merely a failure to reject.

Right now the paper spends a lot of energy reassuring the reader that the null is not due to low power. That is necessary but not sufficient. The stronger move is to make the null substantively interesting by tying it to a sharp economic question about incidence.
