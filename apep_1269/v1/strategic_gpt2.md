## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether a light-touch form of compulsory national service reduces youth exposure to crime. Using Mexico’s military lottery, which assigns some 18-year-old men to Saturday morning training, it argues that part-time military service does not meaningfully reduce crime victimization, casting doubt on a prominent policy rationale for expanding the program.

A busy economist should care because this is, in principle, a clean test of a broad and important policy question: can structured, low-intensity state programs keep high-risk young men out of harm’s way? That question reaches beyond Mexico to the economics of crime, youth policy, national service, and the design of “incapacitation” interventions.

**Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Not quite. The introduction starts with the right raw ingredients—Mexico’s violence, the upcoming policy expansion, the military lottery—but then quickly gets pulled into literatures and design details. The first two paragraphs should do less literature review and more conceptual positioning: *why is this a meaningful test of a general proposition about youth policy?* Right now the paper risks reading like a niche Mexico policy note rather than a paper with broader stakes.

**The pitch the paper should have:**

> Many governments justify compulsory national service as a way to discipline young men and reduce crime, yet we know little about whether low-intensity service actually changes exposure to violence. Mexico’s military lottery offers a natural test: some 18-year-old men are assigned to roughly ten months of Saturday morning training, a program now slated for major expansion on crime-prevention grounds. This paper shows that such part-time service does not produce detectable reductions in crime victimization, suggesting that brief, structured occupation is not enough to alter the risks facing young men in high-crime environments.
>
> The broader question is not just about Mexico or military service. It is whether “keeping young men busy” works as a crime policy. The answer here appears to be no—at least not when the intervention is brief, nonresidential, and concentrated in low-risk hours.

That version gives the reader a world question, not just a policy-evaluation setup.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution in one sentence:**  
The paper’s contribution is to show that low-intensity, weekend military service—unlike full-time conscription—does not meaningfully reduce youth crime victimization, implying that the intensity and timing of structured state supervision matter for crime prevention.

**Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?**  
Only partially. The paper leans heavily on Galiani et al. on Argentine conscription, but the differentiation is still too thin. “This is part-time, not full-time” is a real distinction, but at present it feels like a variant rather than a strong standalone contribution. The contribution would be clearer if the paper more explicitly said: prior work studied **deep military immersion and later criminal behavior**; this paper studies **light-touch service and contemporaneous victimization exposure**. That is a different question, not just a different setting.

**Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or as filling a gap in a literature?**  
Mixed, but too often as a literature gap. The stronger framing is about the world: *Do brief, compulsory, structured programs keep young men safer?* The introduction currently slips into “first paper on part-time military service,” which is true but not enough for AER-level positioning.

**Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what’s new?**  
They could, but a nontrivial fraction would still summarize it as “another reduced-form paper on a military lottery and crime.” That is a problem. The paper needs a sharper sentence on why this is conceptually distinct from prior military-service papers and why victimization—not offending—is the relevant margin here.

**What would make this contribution bigger? Be specific.**
1. **Sharper mechanism framing around time use and risk exposure.**  
   The Saturday-morning nature of the intervention is the most interesting fact in the paper. The paper should lean hard into the mismatch between program timing and the temporal distribution of crime risk. Right now that point is made late and almost offhandedly. It could be the centerpiece.
2. **A stronger focus on violent victimization as the headline outcome.**  
   “Any crime” is broad and policy-relevant, but the broader intellectual question is about youth violence. If the most credible and conceptually central result is on violent victimization, lead there.
3. **Broader framing against the ‘keep them busy’ class of interventions.**  
   This could speak to after-school programs, youth employment, civic service, and other structured-time interventions. That would make the paper feel less like “Mexico military policy” and more like “what kinds of youth structure matter for crime?”
4. **A more explicit comparison between dosage/intensity margins.**  
   The paper already gestures at this; it should be elevated from discussion point to core contribution. The real contribution is not “null in Mexico,” but “light-touch service appears categorically different from immersive service.”

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

**Closest neighbors:**
1. **Galiani, Rossi, and Schargrodsky (2011)** on Argentina’s conscription lottery and later crime.
2. **Blattman, Jamison, and Sheridan (2017)** on CBT and crime among high-risk youth in Liberia.
3. **Draca, Machin, and Witt (2011)** on policing and crime deterrence.
4. Likely also work on **schooling / youth labor market participation / crime**, e.g. **Lochner and Moretti (2004)** and related papers.
5. Broader structured-time / incapacitation literature, potentially including after-school or summer youth employment papers.

**How should the paper position itself relative to those neighbors?**  
Mostly **build on and bridge**, not attack. The paper should say:

- relative to **Galiani et al.**: this is not a replication in another country, but a test of a different treatment margin—light-touch service rather than immersive conscription;
- relative to **incapacitation / structured-time papers**: this is a test of whether minimal compulsory occupation at a specific weekly time window can move crime exposure;
- relative to **youth intervention papers**: it speaks to dosage, timing, and mechanism, not just whether “interventions” matter.

**Is the paper currently positioned too narrowly or too broadly?**  
Somewhat too narrowly. It is presently positioned as Mexico + military service + crime. That is publishable in a field journal if the design is convincing, but it undersells the broader economics question. It should instead be positioned around **the effect of low-intensity structured supervision on youth crime risk**.

**What literature does the paper seem unaware of, or should speak to more directly?**
- **Structured time / routine activities / temporal crime risk** literature.
- **Youth development / after-school / summer jobs** interventions, especially those claiming reduced violence through supervision or schedule changes.
- Possibly **state capacity / civic service / social cohesion** literatures if the paper wants to preserve non-crime relevance.
- A broader literature on **victimization vs offending**. The paper treats victimization as a practical substitute for offending data, but strategically this is also a conceptual distinction: programs may affect exposure to violence even if they do not change criminal participation.

**Is the paper having the right conversation?**  
Not fully. It is having the “military service and crime” conversation, which is real but too small. The more impactful conversation is: **When does structured occupation reduce crime among young men?** The military setting is the vehicle, not the destination.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

**Setup:**  
Policymakers often believe that disciplined, structured programs for young men reduce crime. Mexico is expanding a military lottery program partly on those grounds.

**Tension:**  
Existing evidence on military service points in the opposite direction in some contexts, but those studies concern full-time, immersive conscription. We do not know whether low-intensity, part-time service might instead reduce crime through incapacitation or structured time use.

**Resolution:**  
The paper finds no meaningful reduction in victimization associated with lottery-based eligibility for Saturday service.

**Implications:**  
“Keeping young men busy” for a few hours on Saturday mornings is unlikely to be an effective anti-crime policy, at least in a high-violence setting; policymakers should not expect large safety gains from scaling such a program.

**Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?**  
It has the skeleton of one, but the arc is weakened by three things:

1. **Too much early emphasis on the literature and design rather than the conceptual question.**
2. **Too many contribution claims.** The paper says it contributes to part-time military service, informative nulls, and victimization-survey methods. That is one contribution too many, maybe two.
3. **The paper’s most interesting interpretive point—timing and dosage mismatch—is buried in the discussion.**

At present, it reads a bit like a collection of sensible results around a null finding. The story it *should* be telling is simpler:

> Policymakers believe structured occupation can reduce youth crime. Mexico’s Saturday military service is a natural test of that idea. It fails to move crime victimization, suggesting that low-dose, badly timed structure is not enough in a high-risk environment.

That is a story. The current draft is close, but not yet disciplined enough.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

**What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?**  
“Mexico is expanding a military lottery on the theory that Saturday training keeps young men out of crime—but the evidence suggests six hours a week of military structure does basically nothing for victimization.”

That would get some attention.

**Would people lean in or reach for their phones?**  
They would lean in initially because the policy is vivid and the setting is consequential. But if the presenter quickly shifts into “null result in a DiD around eligible ages,” attention will fade. The hook is strong; the current packaging is weaker than the hook.

**What follow-up question would they ask?**  
“Is the null because military service doesn’t matter, or because Saturday mornings are the wrong margin?”  
That is exactly the question the paper should want the audience to ask—and answer more forcefully.

**If the findings are null or modest: is the null itself interesting?**  
Yes, but only if framed correctly. AER will not care about “we didn’t find significance.” It may care about “a widely touted youth-discipline policy appears unable to produce even moderate crime reductions, implying that dosage and timing are central to how structured programs work.” The paper is trying to make this case, but it still sounds defensive, as if explaining away a failed experiment, rather than using the null to sharpen a broader economic claim.

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

1. **Shorten the institutional background.**  
   The mechanics of the lottery are important, but this section is a bit long for what the paper strategically needs. Compress the description of the program and move some institutional detail to an appendix.

2. **Move the key interpretive paragraph from the discussion into the introduction.**  
   Specifically: the point that Saturday mornings are low-risk hours and that the dosage is too low for deep socialization but maybe enough for incapacitation. That is the paper’s central conceptual hook.

3. **Front-load the best result and its meaning.**  
   By paragraph 3 of the introduction, the reader should know the main finding and why it matters. The current intro gets there, but too slowly and with too much setup.

4. **Drop or demote the “methodological contribution” claim about victimization surveys.**  
   That is not helping the paper’s strategic profile. It makes the paper feel smaller and more technical, not bigger.

5. **Be selective about robustness in the main text.**  
   The paper currently spends a lot of real estate reassuring the reader. For a top-journal audience, the main text should carry the headline results, the age-profile logic, and the central interpretation. Several specification variants can move to appendix unless they materially change the story.

6. **Rework the conclusion.**  
   The conclusion currently mostly summarizes. It should instead end with a sharper claim about what economists should learn: *structured time is not a monolith; intensity and timing matter*. That is the general lesson.

7. **Remove the meta/scaffolding elements that make it feel less like a finished scholarly product.**  
   The autonomous-generation acknowledgements are fine for transparency but strategically distracting in a submission to a flagship journal. This paper already faces a hill on novelty and positioning; it should not volunteer extra reasons for readers to view it as provisional.
