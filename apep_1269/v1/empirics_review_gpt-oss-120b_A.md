**1. Idea Fidelity**  
The submitted paper follows the original manifest closely. It exploits the random assignment created by Mexico’s *Sorteo Militar* to evaluate the effect of a short‐term, part‑time military service on youth crime. The authors use the ENVIPE victim‑survey (the primary data source mentioned in the manifest) and implement the male‑female difference‑in‑differences (DiD) design that was proposed. All key elements of the identification strategy—using males aged 18‑19 as the treated group, women (and older ages) as controls, and state‑by‑year fixed effects—are present. The only departure from the manifest is the omission of the second data source (SESNSP municipal crime statistics) from the core regressions; it is relegated to a descriptive role. Since the main identification relies on individual‑level victimization rather than aggregate homicide counts, this does not undermine the core exercise, but a brief discussion of why the municipal data are not used for causal estimation would have been helpful.

**2. Summary**  
The paper estimates the intent‑to‑treat effect of being lottery‑eligible for Saturday‑only military training on crime victimization among 18‑19‑year‑old Mexican men, using a DiD that compares male‑female gaps before and after the eligibility age. The main result is a precise null: the estimated reduction in any‑crime victimization is –1.2 percentage points (SE = 1.2 pp), allowing the authors to rule out reductions larger than about 3.6 pp. A significant negative effect on a fraud‑victimization placebo raises concerns about age‑by‑gender confounds, but the authors argue that the violent‑crime estimates are still credible.

**3. Essential Points**  

1. **Parallel‑Trends Assumption Not Directly Tested** – The DiD hinges on the male‑female gap evolving smoothly across the 18‑year boundary. Because ENVIPE does not observe ages 16‑17, the authors cannot present an event‑study graphic or formal pre‑trend test. The age‑profile analysis partially addresses this, but it does not substitute for a direct test of the key assumption. Without evidence that the gap would have been flat absent the lottery, the credibility of the ITT estimate remains questionable.  

2. **Fraud Placebo Indicates Unobserved Age‑by‑Gender Shocks** – The significant negative coefficient on fraud victimization (‑0.014, p < 0.01) suggests that the Male × Age 18‑19 interaction captures gender‑specific transitions at age 18 unrelated to the lottery (e.g., entry into formal financial markets). If such transitions affect fraud, they may also affect other outcomes that are correlated with financial activity (property crime, even some violent crime via “street robbery to obtain cash”). This threatens internal validity for the main outcomes and calls for additional robustness work.  

3. **Statistical Power May Be Insufficient for Policy‑Relevant Effects** – The authors correctly note the minimum detectable ITT effect (~3.4 pp) and the implied LATE (~8 pp). However, the policy discussion treats the null as evidence that the program “does not reduce crime.” Given that the design can only rule out relatively large effects, a more modest reduction (e.g., 1–2 pp among compliers) could still be policy‑relevant but would go undetected. The paper should temper its policy conclusions accordingly.  

If these three issues cannot be addressed satisfactorily, the paper should be **rejected** for lack of credible identification.

**Overall Assessment**  
The paper tackles a timely and policy‑relevant question with an elegant quasi‑experimental design that, in principle, aligns with the manifest. However, the credibility of the identification rests on an untested parallel‑trend assumption and is weakened by a significant placebo failure that hints at gender‑specific age‑18 shocks. The manuscript would benefit from additional robustness checks, a more nuanced discussion of statistical power, and deeper use of the municipal crime data that were part of the original proposal. If the authors can convincingly address the three essential points above, the paper would make a valuable contribution to the literature on national‑service programs and crime in high‑violence settings. As it stands, the paper should be **revised** before acceptance.
