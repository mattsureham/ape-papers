Using Mexico’s military lottery, which assigns some 18-year-old men to Saturday morning military training, the paper argues that part-time weekend service does not meaningfully reduce crime victimization—an important result because Mexico is expanding the program partly on crime-prevention grounds.

> Mexico is expanding compulsory Saturday military service for young men on the theory that structured discipline and supervised time will keep them out of crime. The answer is no detectable reduction in victimization. ## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:**  
The paper provides evidence that low-intensity, part-time military service—unlike the more immersive forms studied elsewhere—does not appear to materially reduce crime victimization among young men.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?
Somewhat, but not sharply enough. The paper knows its nearest comparator is Galiani et al. on Argentina, and it makes the “intensity margin” point. That is the right instinct. But right now the contribution still reads as “same broad question, different country and weaker dosage.” That is not yet a top-journal contribution on its own.

To differentiate itself, the paper needs to say more forcefully that the object of interest is not “military service” generically, but **whether low-dose compulsory structure can serve as crime prevention**. That puts it in conversation not only with conscription papers, but also with the economics of incapacitation, youth supervision, and routine activities. That move would make the contribution conceptually cleaner and larger.

At present it oscillates between the two. The stronger framing is clearly the world question:

- Governments believe part-time national service can reduce youth crime.
- Is that belief correct?

That is stronger than:
- There is little evidence on part-time military service relative to full-time conscription.

The latter is a literature gap. The former is a policy-relevant question about the world.

They could, but a nontrivial fraction would summarize it as “another reduced-form paper on military service and crime, with a null.” That is a danger sign. The introduction gives them ingredients, but not a crisp organizing idea.

Three possibilities:

   The paper’s latent idea is that six hours on Saturday morning is too little to matter. That is more interesting than “null result in Mexico.” The contrast should be between **immersive socialization** and **minimal incapacitation**, not merely Argentina versus Mexico.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Mexico faces severe youth violence, and policymakers are expanding Saturday military service as a crime-prevention tool.

### Tension
That policy rests on a plausible but untested premise: maybe giving young men disciplined, supervised weekend structure reduces crime exposure. But the prior evidence on military service comes mostly from very different, much more intensive settings.

### Resolution
The paper finds no detectable evidence that this low-intensity, weekend military obligation meaningfully reduces crime victimization.

### Implications
If governments want to use national service as anti-crime policy, light-touch versions may simply be too weak to matter. The paper therefore challenges a common policy intuition: structure alone, at low dose, is not enough.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
It has the ingredients, but the arc is still a bit loose. The introduction and discussion contain multiple candidate stories:

- military service and crime,
- incapacitation,
- informative null results,
- victimization surveys as measurement innovation,
- Mexico’s 2025 reform.

That is too many stories for one paper of this scope. The result is that the paper can feel like a competent collection of estimates searching for a top-journal narrative.

### What story should it be telling?
This one:

> Policymakers often assume that putting high-risk young men into structured compulsory activities will reduce crime. Mexico’s military lottery provides a test of that idea at low intensity. The answer is sobering: a few hours of compulsory structure per week do not appear to materially reduce youth victimization. The lesson is not about militaries per se; it is about the limits of light-touch state supervision as crime policy.

That story is stronger, cleaner, and more portable.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?
“Mexico is expanding compulsory Saturday military service to fight youth crime, but this paper suggests that six hours a week of military training does not materially reduce young men’s crime victimization.”

That is a good opening fact. It is concrete, policy-relevant, and legible.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
Initially, they would lean in, because the setting is unusual and policy-salient. But if the next sentence is “we use ENVIPE in a male-female age DiD,” some will reach for their phones. If the next sentence is instead “the striking point is that low-dose compulsory structure appears too weak to change violence exposure,” they stay with you.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Almost certainly:
- “Is the issue that military service doesn’t work, or that this version is too low-intensity to work?”
And then:
- “So what kinds of structured programs do work?”

Those are good follow-up questions. The paper should be written to invite exactly them.

### If the findings are null or modest: is the null interesting?
Yes, but only if the paper makes the null about a meaningful policy belief. At present it partly does. The null is interesting because:
- the policy is expanding now,
- the intervention is widely justified using crime-reduction rhetoric,
- and the null speaks to the limits of low-intensity compulsory service.

What would make the null feel less like a failed experiment and more like a finding?  
Stop apologizing for the null and instead **theorize it**. The paper should say: “A null is exactly what one should expect if the active ingredient is either deep resocialization or sustained incapacitation, neither of which this program provides.” That transforms the null from absence of evidence into evidence about mechanism and dosage.

A null result in a single-country reduced-form setting is not inherently top-journal material unless it resolves a larger debate. Right now the paper does not fully claim that larger debate.

If it presents itself as a substantive lesson about the limits of light-touch national service as crime policy, it has a chance to matter.
