## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
This paper tests whether a low-intensity, part-time military service requirement can reduce youth crime exposure in a high-violence environment. Exploiting the random lottery assignment of 18-year-old Mexican males to Saturday training, the author finds a precise null effect on crime victimization, suggesting that "light-touch" military programs lack the dosage necessary to trigger either the criminogenic peer effects or the protective incapacitation effects found in full-time conscription literatures.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this pitch quite well. The first paragraph sets the stakes (Mexico’s homicide crisis) and the second immediately identifies the theoretical tension (socialization vs. incapacitation). 

**The pitch the paper should have:**
The current pitch is strong, but to reach the AER, it needs to emphasize the *policy high-stakes* earlier. It should lead with: "In 2025, Mexico will double down on a decades-old military lottery to combat a historic crime wave, based on the untested premise that Saturday drills deter delinquency. This paper provides the first causal evidence that this 'dosage' of intervention is insufficient to move the needle on youth victimization, suggesting a massive misallocation of state resources."

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**One-sentence contribution:** The paper identifies the "intensity margin" of military service, showing that while full-time conscription affects crime, part-time service (6 hours/week) has a precisely estimated zero effect on youth victimization.

**Evaluation:**
- **Differentiation:** It differentiates well from Galiani et al. (2011) by focusing on the "part-time" vs. "full-time" margin. 
- **World vs. Literature:** It frames itself as answering a question about the world (Does Mexico's policy work?), but the empirical section leans heavily on "filling a gap" in the conscription literature.
- **Explainability:** A smart economist would say, "It’s a DiD/Lottery paper showing that Saturday-only military service in Mexico doesn't reduce crime."
- **Making it bigger:** To be a "big" AER paper, the author needs to move beyond the null and explore *why* it’s a null more aggressively. Are there heterogeneous effects by state-level violence? Is the null driven by the fact that the "incapacitation" happens during low-crime hours? (The author touches on this, but more data on crime-by-hour would be a "big" addition).

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
- **Closest Neighbors:** Galiani et al. (2011, AJAE), Blattman et al. (2017, AER), Dell (2015, AER), and perhaps the "summer jobs" literature (Heller 2014, Science).
- **Positioning:** It currently "complements" Galiani. It should "challenge" the broader assumption that any structured state intervention for at-risk youth is inherently beneficial.
- **Niche/Broad:** It is currently a bit niche (Mexico-specific). It needs to speak to the "state capacity" and "optimal intervention dosage" literatures more broadly.
- **Unaware of:** It should connect to the US literature on "Midnight Basketball" or summer youth employment programs (SYEP). These are the closest structural equivalents to "occupying youth on weekends/summers."

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC
- **Setup:** Mexico is in a bloodbath; the state uses a military lottery to try to fix it.
- **Tension:** Is this "military lite" enough to help (incapacitation) or enough to hurt (bad peers)? Or is it nothing at all?
- **Resolution:** It is a precise nothing.
- **Implications:** Mexico is about to waste a lot of money on the 2025 expansion.

**Evaluation:** The arc is clean but lacks "flavor." The "Resolution" is a bit flat. The "Implications" section needs to be the "hero" of the paper—calculating the opportunity cost of the 2025 expansion in terms of other proven interventions (like the CBT mentioned) would create a more compelling finale.

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
At a dinner party, the lead fact is: **"Mexico is expanding a military program to 95% of its youth to stop crime, but a random lottery shows it has exactly zero impact on whether those kids get robbed or killed."**
People would lean in, but the follow-up would be: "Is it because the program is a joke (6 hours of drills) or because the criminals are too powerful?" The paper needs to do more to answer the "why" of the null.

---

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
- **Front-load:** The "2025 Expansion" (Section 2.3) is the most compelling reason to read the paper. Move that into the Introduction to raise the stakes immediately.
- **The Fraud Placebo:** The failure of the fraud placebo is a "red alert" in the paper. Currently, it's buried. It needs to be handled with extreme care—perhaps by showing that for *violent* crime (the primary policy target), the identifying assumptions hold better.
- **Appendix:** Move the detailed JEL/BPCOD definitions to the appendix (as is done, but keep the main text lean).

---

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The gap is **ambition**. As a "precise null on a part-time program," it feels like a very high-quality *Applied Economics* or *REStat* paper. To make it AER:
1. **Mechanism Deep-Dive:** Use the time-use argument more formally. If the training is 7 AM - 1 PM on Saturday, show me that crime in Mexico *doesn't* happen then. Use the administrative data to show the "mismatch" between program hours and crime hours.
2. **The "2025 Expansion" Simulation:** Perform a back-of-the-envelope cost-benefit analysis. How many millions of dollars will the 95% expansion cost? What *could* that money buy in terms of CBT or policing?
3. **Address the Fraud Placebo head-on:** The fact that the "placebo" shows a significant result is a potential "desk reject" trigger for some editors. The author needs a more robust defense—perhaps a triple-difference (DDD) using women as the third layer of control across ages.

**Single most impactful advice:** Transform this from a "report on a null result" into a "critique of the 'low-dosage' policy trap," using the 2025 Mexican expansion as the primary exhibit of how states misspend on "ceremonial" interventions rather than effective ones.
