**Idea Fidelity**

The manuscript largely pursues the original idea, but several key components of the proposed manifest do not appear. The identification strategy is executed as the suggested male–female difference-in-differences at ages 18–19, and ENVIPE victimization data is the primary empirical source, but the paper never exploits the other data sets listed in the manifest (SESNSP municipal crime statistics, INEGI mortality statistics, or ENOE labor-market data). Nor does it incorporate the suggested mechanism tests (incapacitation, post-service persistence, or employment channels) beyond speculative discussion. As a result, the paper addresses only a subset of the original empirical research question and provides no evidence from the additional data sources that were meant to strengthen identification and interpretability.

**Summary**

The paper studies whether eligibility for Mexico’s Sorteo Militar Saturday training reduces crime victimization among young men. Using ENVIPE victimization microdata from 2021–2024 and a difference-in-differences strategy that compares the male–female victimization gap at ages 18–19 to older ages, it finds a precise null effect on any-crime victimization and explores robustness across outcome types, age windows, and placebos. The authors interpret the findings as indicating that part-time military service is too light to influence crime and caution against using crime reduction as the justification for the program’s 2025 expansion.

**Essential Points**

1. **Identification remains unconvincing given age-18 confounds.** The key identifying assumption—that the male–female victimization gap evolves smoothly across the lottery-eligibility window—is undermined by the significant fraud placebo and the absence of pre-treatment outcomes. Age 18 corresponds to multiple legal and labor transitions that are plausibly gender-differential, and without any direct observation of lottery assignment or pre-treatment trends, the Male × Age 18–19 interaction likely picks up these confounds. The paper needs either (a) an alternative source of exogenous variation, such as administrative data with actual lottery outcomes or sharper timing, (b) a focus on subpopulations where those transitions are less relevant, or (c) more convincing evidence that the fraud placebo and other confounds do not bias the main estimates (e.g., by exploiting heterogeneity across states with different age-of-majority transitions). As it stands, the credibility of the null interpretation is questionable.

2. **Lack of treatment intensity and compliance exploitation.** The manifest emphasized the lottery’s randomness and recommended deriving causal estimates by comparing lottery winners and losers or using compliance information. Here, the analysis stops at the coarse ITT via the Male × Age indicator; there is no attempt to harness the random lottery draw (e.g., by linking to administrative compliance rates across states or exploiting within-cohort variation over time). Given that only ~40% of eligible males receive active assignments and compliance is 60–80%, the ability to assess the actual impact of service is extremely limited without recovering even the first-stage variation. The paper should seek some way to approximate this—perhaps by combining ENVIPE with external data on assignment rates or by leveraging variation introduced by the 2025 expansion (if feasible). Without this, it is difficult to learn about the true effect of weekend service, as opposed to age-related transitions.

3. **Missed opportunity to triangulate with other data and mechanisms.** The original proposal envisioned integrating SESNSP municipal crime data, INEGI mortality records, and ENOE labor-market outcomes to provide complementary perspectives and mechanism validation (incapacitation, persistence, employment). The current draft confines itself to ENVIPE and does not exploit these rich administrative sources. This limits both the scope and credibility of the findings, especially since victimization surveys in high-violence contexts can be noisy. Incorporating at least one additional data source—e.g., analyzing homicide trends at the municipal level around age or cohort thresholds, or exploring employment transitions through ENOE—would bolster the argument and help differentiate mechanisms.

**Suggestions**

- **Address the fraud placebo more rigorously.** The significant effect on fraud is the clearest evidence that the identifying assumption fails. Instead of relegating it to the discussion, use it to explore gender-age transitions more explicitly. For example, examine whether the fraud gap spikes precisely at age 18 for men using flexible age trends (e.g., splines or second differences) or whether it differs by state factors such as financial access. If these patterns persist, they can inform a more credible specification—perhaps by demeaning outcomes by non-service-eligible ages or by adding interactions with observable transitions (labor law changes, school-leaving ages). Alternatively, identify an outcome that is more plausibly affected only by lottery exposure and less by broader adulthood transitions, and focus the main inference there.

- **Explore alternative strategies that mimic the lottery.** Since direct assignment status is unavailable in ENVIPE, the authors could attempt to link respondents to municipalities or cohorts with known lottery composition (if accessible from SEDENA releases). Even partial information—such as the proportion of “active” assignments by state or year—could be used in an instrumental-variables framework, interacting that proportion with the Male × Age indicator to recover a sharper first stage. Another direction is to exploit the time variation created by the 2025 policy change; perhaps the expansion of coverage differs by state or cohort in 2025, which could serve as an exogenous change in treatment probability. At minimum, the authors should demonstrate that the Male × Age effect correlates with any observed variation in assignment intensity, to show that the interaction captures the lottery rather than other age-related shocks.

- **Leverage SESNSP or mortality data for auxiliary evidence.** Even if victimization estimates remain central, supplementing them with official crime outcomes would strengthen the paper. Aggregate data on homicides or police-reported crimes could be analyzed using a regression discontinuity (age threshold) approach if age-specific counts are available, or at least used descriptively to confirm that there are no sudden shifts in male victimization near age 18. Mortality records could provide a hard outcome less subject to reporting bias. If the administrative data do not show any discontinuity either, it increases confidence in the null; if they do, it could point to measurement issues. Likewise, exploring labor-market outcomes via ENOE would help evaluate the proposed mechanism (structured occupation, employment prospects) and mitigate concerns that the null reflects insufficient dosage.

- **Discuss power in terms of policy-relevant effect sizes.** The current power section states that the design detects large effects but cannot rule out moderate ones, framing the null as “not detectable” rather than “unlikely.” To make the policy implications sharper, translate the minimum detectable effect into concrete crime-reduction scenarios (e.g., reductions in violent victimization per 1,000 youths). Compare these benchmarks to the effects that previous interventions achieved (the paper already cites some). If, for instance, the implied detectable LATE is 8.4 percentage points while credible policy targets lie around 2–3 points, the null is less informative. Explicitly acknowledging this nuance would temper the policy conclusion and guide future research toward designs that can detect smaller, but still consequential, effects.

- **Clarify the role of the 2025 expansion in the empirical strategy.** The manifest highlighted the 2025 reform (40% to 95% coverage) as an opportunity. If the paper cannot yet exploit it (perhaps because data post-2025 are unavailable), this should be explicitly stated, and the empirical design should outline how future work could take advantage of the policy shock (e.g., a pre-post comparison once new ENVIPE waves become available). If the expansion generates differential coverage across states or cohorts, even preliminary evidence from administrative logs or media reports could be used to motivate heterogeneous effects. This contextualizes the null result within a broader policy debate and clarifies why the current study focuses on the pre-expansion regime.

- **Expand the robustness analysis to include placebo outcomes less likely to suffer from lifecycle confounds.** Fraud is clearly problematic; a stronger placebo might be a crime type that should be wholly unaffected by age-based legal transitions but still subject to similar reporting behavior (e.g., criminal threats or kidnapping, which are unlikely to correlate with financial system entry). Alternatively, the authors could use non-crime outcomes (e.g., health emergencies) that capture survey response tendencies but not the treatment. Demonstrating that such placebos are null would increase confidence that the main estimates are not driven by unobserved age shocks.

- **Improve discussion of mechanisms and dosage.** The paper contrasts part-time service with full-time conscription, but it does not empirically explore the proposed channels (incapacitation, socialization, employment). Consider using ENVIPE’s questions about daily activities or combining with ENOE to see whether lottery-eligible men report different labor or schooling patterns. Even if survey data are limited, a detailed exposition of why six hours on Saturday mornings may fail to influence recruitment (e.g., if gang activity peaks at different times) would strengthen the policy argument. If possible, cite qualitative evidence or expert interviews on the content of the Saturday sessions to assess whether they plausibly affect risk factors for victimization.

- **Address potential clustering bias and functional-form choices.** With only 32 state clusters, state-level clustering risks over-rejection. Report alternative standard errors (e.g., wild bootstrap) to confirm the null. Additionally, linear probability models can produce problematic predictions; consider logit/Poisson versions or at least check that results are similar in nonlinear specifications. While the paper’s near-zero point estimates suggest these issues are minor, providing confirmation would enhance credibility.

By addressing these points, the paper would offer a much more compelling and policy-relevant assessment of Mexico’s Saturday military service as a crime-reduction tool.
