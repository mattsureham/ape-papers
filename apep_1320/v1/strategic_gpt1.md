## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether airports change what local economies do, not just how much they grow. Using the long-run legacy of WWII military airfield siting, it argues that airport access shifts counties away from manufacturing and toward professional services, especially in urban areas.

A busy economist should care because this is potentially a clean and intuitively important contrast with the canonical infrastructure story: railroads, highways, and ports are typically framed as helping goods production, while airports may instead help the movement of people, relationships, and ideas. If true, that is a useful contribution to economic geography, urban economics, and place-based policy.

### Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Not quite. The current introduction is competent, but it takes too long to say what the core surprise is. The paper opens with historical color and then broad infrastructure literature review before crystallizing the central fact. The best version of this paper should lead immediately with the unexpected result: airports do not create factory towns; they tilt local economies toward services.

Right now, the intro still reads a bit like “here is a historical IV using WWII airfields.” That is a method-led pitch. For AER, the pitch has to be question-led and world-led: **What kind of infrastructure supports which kind of local economy?**

### What the first two paragraphs should say instead

Something like:

> Economists know that transport infrastructure changes local economic development, but we know much less about whether different transport modes shape different kinds of production. Roads, railroads, and ports primarily move goods, so the classic infrastructure story is a manufacturing story. Airports are different: they mainly move people, business relationships, and time-sensitive activity. Do airports therefore reorganize local economies away from goods production and toward tradeable services?
>
> This paper shows that they do. Using the persistent legacy of WWII Army Air Forces training airfields to instrument for modern airport access, I find that counties with airport access have substantially lower manufacturing employment shares and higher professional-services employment, with effects concentrated in urban counties. The core implication is that air infrastructure does not simply raise local activity; it changes the sectoral composition of that activity.

That would give the paper a cleaner conceptual hook, a sharper contrast to existing infrastructure work, and a more memorable headline result.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### Contribution in one sentence

The paper’s contribution is to argue that airport infrastructure causally reshapes local economies toward professional services and away from manufacturing, implying that different transport modes have different sectoral consequences.

### Is this clearly differentiated from the closest 3-4 papers?

Only partially. The paper does a decent job distinguishing itself from broad transport-infrastructure work, but less well from adjacent airport and local-development literatures. The broad claim “air infrastructure is understudied” is not enough. The paper needs to be much more explicit that its novelty is **sectoral composition**, not airport effects per se, and **mode-specific economic restructuring**, not generic infrastructure.

The differentiation should be along these lines:

- Relative to railroad/highway/port papers: this is not another “transport lowers trade costs and raises output” paper; it is about how a mode that reduces travel costs for people affects the composition of local production.
- Relative to airport/agglomeration papers: this is not another airport-connectivity and employment/population/productivity paper; it is about the manufacturing-versus-services margin.
- Relative to structural transformation papers: this is not about national secular decline in manufacturing per se; it is about infrastructure as a local driver of sectoral reallocation.

At present, a smart economist might still summarize it as “an IV paper on airports and local sector shares.” That is not enough.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or filling a gap in a LITERATURE?

Mixed, but too often the latter. The strongest framing is world-oriented: **What kind of economic activity does air connectivity support?** The weaker framing is literature-oriented: **surface transport has been studied more than air transport.** The paper currently leans too much on the second.

For AER, the world question should dominate. The literature gap is a supporting point, not the main story.

### Could a smart economist explain to a colleague what’s new here?

They could, but only after some effort. The answer they should be able to give is:

> “It shows airports may have the opposite sectoral effect from classic freight infrastructure: they pull places toward business services rather than manufacturing.”

Right now, I worry many readers would instead say:

> “It’s a historical IV paper using WWII airfields to study airport access.”

That is a design summary, not a contribution summary.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Three possibilities, in descending order of impact:

1. **Lean harder into mode-specific theory and comparisons.**  
   The biggest version of this paper is not “airports affect manufacturing shares.” It is “transport infrastructure is not one thing; freight-oriented and people-oriented infrastructure have systematically different equilibrium effects.” The paper should more explicitly contrast airports with roads/rail/ports conceptually, even if not in new estimation.

2. **Show broader reallocation, not just one sector up and one down.**  
   The current outcomes are narrow: manufacturing, professional services, retail. That is suggestive but still feels like a selective trio. A more ambitious version would map the full employment reallocation across sectors, or at least a clearer decomposition of winners and losers. Even a compact figure of sectoral effects would make the story much bigger.

3. **Clarify the mechanism as business travel / idea-intensive activity rather than just “services.”**  
   “Professional services” is doing too much work. The paper would feel larger if it tied the result to the kinds of sectors where airport access should matter most: client-facing, time-sensitive, relationship-intensive, and high-skill tradable services.

Mostly **build on and reframe**, not attack.

- Existing transport papers mostly study modes that move goods.
- Existing airport papers mostly study aggregate growth, passenger traffic, or agglomeration.

That is a cleaner wedge.

### Is the paper currently positioned too narrowly or too broadly?

A bit both, strangely.

- **Too narrowly** in that it can read like a niche historical-IV county paper about WWII airfields.
- **Too broadly** in its repeated claims about “transport infrastructure causes growth,” which set up an overly generic contribution and invite comparison with much stronger literatures.

The right scope is: **economic geography of transport modes and sectoral specialization**. That is focused enough to be intelligible and broad enough to matter.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?

It seems under-engaged with at least four conversations:

1. **Airport connectivity and high-skill business activity**  
   There is likely a literature on airline service, headquarters location, business travel, conventions, knowledge spillovers, exporter performance, and urban productivity that should be brought in.

2. **Service-sector agglomeration and tradable services**  
   The paper mentions Moretti-style agglomeration, but it should more directly connect to work on urban sorting of sectors and the geography of tradable services.

3. **Structural transformation / deindustrialization**  
   If the claim is that airports help accelerate service transition locally, the paper should engage more seriously with structural transformation rather than just citing it in passing.

4. **Infrastructure heterogeneity / market access by mode**  
   The mode-specific idea is the paper’s conceptual value-add; it should explicitly connect to frameworks where different infrastructures reduce different types of trade costs.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?

Not fully. The current conversation is “historical military siting as IV for airport access.” That is the design conversation. The more important conversation is:

**How do different transport technologies shape the spatial division of labor between making goods and selling ideas?**

That is the conversation with AER potential.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

We know transport infrastructure matters for local development, and most classic evidence emphasizes rail, road, and port infrastructure helping goods production and market access. Airports are increasingly central to modern economies, but we do not know clearly whether they support the same kind of activity or a different kind.

### Tension

Airports could plausibly help manufacturing by improving connectivity, or they could instead disproportionately benefit business travel, high-skill coordination, and tradable services. The sectoral effect of air infrastructure is therefore ambiguous ex ante and economically important.

### Resolution

The paper finds that airport access is associated with a lower manufacturing employment share and higher professional-services activity, especially in urban counties. The headline is that airports appear to reallocate local economies toward services rather than boost manufacturing.

### Implications

If this result is right, policymakers should stop treating “infrastructure” as homogeneous. Airport expansion is not just more infrastructure; it is a different kind of infrastructure with different local winners. This matters for regional development strategy, urban specialization, and how economists think about market access in a service economy.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the bones of one, but it is not fully disciplined. There is a real story here, but the draft still feels somewhat like a set of regression tables wrapped around an interesting historical instrument.

The narrative weakness is that the paper oscillates between several possible stories:

- airports and manufacturing,
- airports and services,
- WWII path dependence,
- air infrastructure as understudied,
- policy relevance for local development.

All are related, but the paper needs one master story. The right master story is:

> As the economy shifts from moving goods to moving people and ideas, transport modes matter differently; airports tilt local economies toward tradable services and away from manufacturing.

Everything else should support that.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?

“I think this paper’s headline is: airports may do the opposite of what many local development officials say they want—they do not bring factories; they shift employment toward business services.”

That is a good line. It is crisp, a little counterintuitive, and easy to remember.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

Some would lean in. The result is counter to standard local boosterism and contrasts nicely with classic infrastructure narratives. But they will only lean in if the paper presents it as a broad idea about the modern economy, not a county-IV curiosity.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Almost certainly:

- “Why services specifically?”
- “Is this really about business travel and idea-intensive sectors?”
- “How general is this beyond the specific WWII-airfield counties?”
- “Is this a pure reallocation story or an aggregate growth story too?”

These are good questions. The paper should anticipate them in the introduction and discussion.

### If the findings are modest: is the modesty itself interesting?

The findings are not null, but some of the “mechanism” evidence is modest. Professional services effects are imprecise, and the heterogeneity evidence is suggestive rather than decisive. Strategically, the paper should be careful not to oversell these as fully nailed-down mechanisms. The mechanism discussion should be framed as “consistent with” rather than “demonstrates.”

That said, the main substantive result is interesting enough if framed properly. The paper does not need huge effects everywhere. It needs one durable, surprising fact and a coherent interpretation.

In current form, some distance. The core idea is promising and counterintuitive enough to matter, but the paper reads like a strong field-journal draft rather than an AER paper because it has not yet extracted the biggest idea from the evidence.
