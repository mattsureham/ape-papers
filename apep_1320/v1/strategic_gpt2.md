## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple and potentially important question: when places get airports, what kind of economy do they become? Using the legacy of WWII airfield placement to study persistent airport access, the paper argues that airports do not create more manufacturing; instead, they tilt local employment toward professional services, especially in urban areas.

A busy economist should care because this is a claim about how different transport modes shape structural transformation. If true, it says airports are not just another piece of infrastructure that “promotes growth,” but a specific technology that reallocates activity toward face-to-face, knowledge-intensive sectors.

**Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Not quite. The introduction is competent, but it gets to the real hook too slowly and too defensively. The current framing starts from “air infrastructure is underexplored,” which is a literature-gap framing. The stronger pitch is a world-question framing: *what do airports actually do to the production structure of places?* The surprising answer—*offices, not factories*—should appear immediately.

### The pitch the paper should have

“Economists know that transport infrastructure can transform local economies, but we know much less about whether different transport modes favor different sectors. This paper asks whether airports—an infrastructure designed to move people, ideas, and high-value time-sensitive goods—push places toward manufacturing or toward services. Using the persistent geography of WWII airfields as a source of variation in modern airport access, I find that airports reduce manufacturing employment shares and expand professional services, with the effect concentrated in urban counties. The core message is that air transport does not deliver a generic local growth dividend; it changes the kind of economy a place has.”

That is the opening. Only after that should the introduction situate the paper relative to railroads, highways, ports, and airport papers.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution in one sentence:**  
The paper claims that airport access causally shifts local employment away from manufacturing and toward professional services, implying that air infrastructure changes sectoral composition rather than simply raising local economic activity.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers in the literature?
Only partially. The paper differentiates itself from “surface transport” papers and from airport papers about passenger traffic or urban growth, but the differentiation is still broad-brush. Right now the novelty sounds like: “there is lots on roads/rail/ports, less on airports.” That is not enough for AER. The author needs to show more sharply what exact unanswered question remains after the nearest airport and infrastructure papers.

The paper should more explicitly say something like:  
- prior infrastructure papers show transport can increase market access and growth;  
- prior airport papers mostly study passenger demand, connectivity, traffic, or aggregate urban outcomes;  
- this paper isolates a **sectoral composition** effect and argues the sign differs across sectors because airports lower the costs of moving people and ideas more than bulk goods.

That is the clean differentiator.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or filling a gap in a LITERATURE?
It is mixed, but still too much “fills a gap.” The stronger version is clearly a world question:

- weak framing: “air transport remains underexplored”  
- strong framing: “places invest in airports expecting jobs and industrial development, but what sectors do airports actually favor?”

The latter is the AER version.

### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what’s new?
At present: maybe, but not crisply. They might say, “It’s an IV paper using WWII airfields to study airports and sectoral outcomes.” That is not enough. The colleague should instead say: “Interesting—this paper argues airports are fundamentally different from roads and rail: they help services, not manufacturing.”

That distinction needs to be the paper’s intellectual identity.

### What would make this contribution bigger?
Several possibilities:

1. **Move from sector shares to a broader theory of local specialization.**  
   Right now “manufacturing down, professional services up” is interesting but narrow. Bigger would be a more systematic demonstration that airports shift places toward sectors intensive in business travel, client interaction, or knowledge exchange.

2. **Use richer sectoral outcomes.**  
   Instead of just manufacturing / professional services / retail, show a gradient across sectors predicted by exposure to air connectivity: finance, management, legal, technical services, headquarters activity, perhaps tradable services vs goods. This would make the paper feel less like a single surprising coefficient and more like a coherent economic mechanism.

3. **Tie more directly to tradeable services and spatial equilibrium.**  
   The current “runway reallocation” phrase is decent, but the deeper contribution could be: airports alter local comparative advantage by lowering the cost of face-to-face interaction. That is more ambitious and more general.

4. **Show whether the effect is composition or scale.**  
   Even without new identification, the framing would improve if the paper more explicitly treated the issue as “reallocation across sectors within local economies” rather than “airports hurt manufacturing.” The latter invites skepticism and sounds too absolute.

If the author could do one substantive expansion, it would be a sector-by-sector pattern tied to a pre-specified economic characteristic, not just three outcomes.

   This is probably the most important missing conversation. If the mechanism is that airports lower the cost of in-person meetings, then the paper should lean into that literature.

2. **Tradable services geography**  
   The professional services angle wants stronger anchoring in the literature on tradable services, headquarters, business services clustering, and urban specialization.

3. **Airport economics beyond local growth**  
   There is likely a broader airport/connectivity literature in regional science, transportation economics, and urban economics than the introduction acknowledges.

4. **Structural transformation across places**  
   The paper implicitly speaks to why some places become more service-oriented. That literature could make the paper feel less like a transport curiosity and more like a contribution to how local economies evolve.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?
Not yet fully. The current conversation is “infrastructure and growth, but for airports.” The better conversation is:

**“Different transport technologies change local comparative advantage in different ways.”**

That is a much more interesting and more AER-relevant conversation. It connects transport economics to urban economics and structural transformation.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
We know transport infrastructure matters for local development. Roads, rail, and ports have been shown to increase market access and shape spatial production patterns.

### Tension
But airports are not just another transport mode. They primarily move people, meetings, management, and some high-value cargo. So the open question is whether airports create the same kind of local economy as roads and rail—or a different one. Policymakers often talk as if all infrastructure is growth-enhancing in the same way; that may be wrong.

### Resolution
The paper finds that airport access is associated with less manufacturing and more professional services, especially in urban counties.

### Implications
If this holds up, economists should think of airports as infrastructure that supports service-sector agglomeration and local specialization, not as generic industrial policy for manufacturing revival.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
It has the pieces, but the arc is only **serviceable**, not sharp. The current manuscript often reads like:
1. here is a historical instrument,
2. here are some balance concerns,
3. here is a surprising manufacturing result,
4. here are some placebo outcomes.

That is more a collection of empirical outputs than a fully disciplined story.

### What story should it be telling?
The paper should tell one story and tell it relentlessly:

**Airports are a different kind of infrastructure because they reduce the cost of moving people and ideas; therefore they reshape local economies toward sectors that depend on those flows.**

Everything should serve that story:
- why airports are conceptually distinct from roads/rail,
- why manufacturing is the wrong naive benchmark,
- why professional services should rise,
- why urban heterogeneity is expected,
- why retail is a useful contrast.

Right now the “runway reallocation” label is useful, but it comes a bit late and feels like branding pasted onto results rather than the paper’s organizing principle from page 1.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?
“I thought airports would help manufacturing. This paper says the opposite: airports seem to shift local economies out of manufacturing and into professional services.”

That is a good dinner-party fact. It is counterintuitive.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
They would **lean in briefly**, because the claim is surprising. But then the immediate follow-up would be: “Interesting—but is that really about airports, or just about cities that got airports?” In other words, the headline is strong enough to get attention, but the paper needs a larger conceptual payoff to sustain it.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Probably one of these:
- “So are airports just helping tradable services?”  
- “Is this really about business travel and face-to-face interaction?”  
- “Does this mean transport modes have systematically different effects on local specialization?”  
- “Why only professional services—what about finance, management, HQs, tech?”

The best version of the paper should anticipate exactly those questions and structure the evidence around them.

### If findings are modest or null, is the modesty itself interesting?
The paper’s positives are somewhat modest and imprecise, but the main negative manufacturing effect is not. The issue is not nullness; it is that the mechanism evidence is currently thinner than the headline. “Manufacturing falls” is interesting. “Professional services rise a bit” is directionally supportive but not yet overwhelming. So the paper must make the case that the broader pattern, not just a single manufacturing coefficient, is economically coherent.
