## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a fundamental question about economic geography: Does modern air infrastructure—the primary mode for high-value trade and business travel—reshape what a local economy actually produces? Using the historical accident of WWII training airfield locations as an instrument for contemporary airport access, the author finds that airports don't attract factories; they displace them, shifting local economies away from manufacturing and toward professional services.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this reasonably well, but the second paragraph gets bogged down in "filling a gap." The pitch should lead with the "Runway Reallocation" vs. "Runway Dividend" hook. 
**The pitch the paper should have:** "While we know that railroads and highways built the American manufacturing belt, the impact of the 20th century’s most vital transport mode—aviation—on industrial composition remains a mystery. This paper shows that contrary to the hopes of local policymakers, airports act as a catalyst for deindustrialization, pulling resources out of manufacturing and into high-value tradeable services."

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution:** The paper provides the first causal evidence that air infrastructure induces a sectoral shift from goods production to tradeable services, rather than simply acting as a general shifter of economic growth.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It is well-differentiated. Most literature (e.g., Brueckner 2003) looks at "how much" growth, while this looks at "what kind."
*   **Framing:** The abstract frames it as a question about the world, but the intro leans a bit too heavily on "this paper fills a gap."
*   **The "Smart Economist" Test:** A reader would successfully explain this as "the paper that shows airports kill local manufacturing to make room for lawyers and consultants." It avoids the "just another DiD/IV" trap because the result is counter-intuitive to the standard "infrastructure = factories" narrative.
*   **Bigger Contribution:** To make this an AER "Big Paper," the author needs to show the *general equilibrium* implications. Does the manufacturing move elsewhere, or does it disappear? Is this a zero-sum reallocation across space, or a net productivity gain for the US economy?

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

*   **Neighbors:** Donaldson (2018) on railroads; Duranton & Turner (2012) on highways; Blonigen & Cristea (2016) on air trade.
*   **Positioning:** It currently "extends" them. It should instead **contrast** with them. The story is: "Rail/Road = Goods; Air = Ideas/Services." This contrast is the most exciting part of the paper.
*   **Missing Conversations:** The paper needs to speak to the **Urban Economics** literature on "Consumer Cities" vs. "Producer Cities" (Glaeser et al.). Is the airport making a city more attractive for people to live/work in services, thereby driving up land rents and pushing factories out?
*   **Right Conversation?** Yes, but it could bridge more into the "Structural Transformation" literature (Herrendorf et al. 2014) to explain *why* this transition is happening.

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

*   **Setup:** The US airport network was born from a wartime exigency (flat land, clear skies).
*   **Tension:** Local mayors want airports to bring "good" manufacturing jobs. Economic theory suggests transport reduces trade costs, which should help goods.
*   **Resolution:** The data says the opposite. Factories leave; offices arrive.
*   **Implications:** Infrastructure isn't a monolithic "pro-growth" lever; it picks winners and losers among sectors.

**Evaluate:** The arc is strong. It’s a "Surprising Result" paper. However, the "Tension" section in the introduction is a bit weak—it needs to more forcefully state *why* we might have expected airports to help manufacturing (e.g., air freight of high-value components).

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

*   **Lead Fact:** "Building a major airport in your county reduces the manufacturing employment share by nearly 7 percentage points."
*   **Reaction:** People will lean in. The "Infrastructure is always good for industry" myth is strong.
*   **Follow-up:** "Wait, so is it just because airports are noisy/polluting (not-in-my-backyard) or because they make the land too expensive for low-margin factories?" (The author needs to answer this "Mechanism" question more deeply).

---

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

*   **Front-loading:** The paper is well front-loaded.
*   **Appendix:** The Wikipedia geocoding details are a bit "inside baseball"—keep that in the appendix. 
*   **Results:** The urban-rural split (Table 4) is actually *more* interesting than the main result. It should be elevated. It tells us that airports only "reallocate" when there is already a density of talent to work with.
*   **Conclusion:** Needs to move beyond a summary and discuss the *fiscal* implications. If airports kill manufacturing, do they also change the local tax base?

---

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The paper is currently a very "clean" IV paper, but it feels a bit "safe." To reach the AER, it needs **Ambition**.

**Single most impactful advice:** Transition from a "Local Labor Market" story to a "National Structural Transformation" story. The author should use the local shocks to estimate a simple spatial equilibrium model. This would allow them to say: "How much of the US shift from manufacturing to services since 1950 can be explained by the expansion of the air network?" 

Without that "Big Picture" counterfactual, it's a great *Journal of Urban Economics* paper. With it, it’s an AER paper.
