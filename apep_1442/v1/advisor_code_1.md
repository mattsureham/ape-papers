**Idea Fidelity**

The paper adheres closely to the manifest for “Planning Inspector Leniency and Housing Supply in England” in its basic components: it exploits the Planning Inspectorate’s inspector assignment process, constructs leniency measures from decision letters, and aims to evaluate causal effects of appeal outcomes on housing supply via an inspector leniency IV. However, the execution diverges substantially from the manifest’s stated empirical strategy and research question. Consequently, it does not estimate any treatment effects on housing outcomes, nor does it implement the two-stage, within-LPA identification that the manifest described. While this is a valuable contribution, the paper should more explicitly acknowledge that it departs from the original substantive goal of estimating housing effects and frame itself as a preliminary data-collection and diagnostics note rather than a full causal analysis.

**Summary**

The paper attempts to implement an inspector leniency IV using England’s planning appeals, scraping inspector identities from Planning Inspectorate decision letters. With just 860 appeals and 198 inspectors, the leave-one-out leniency instrument yields a negative first-stage coefficient and weak F-statistic, contrary to expectations—a result the author attributes to mechanical mean reversion when inspectors have very few cases per cell. Balance tests support quasi-random assignment, and lagged inspector allow rates are positive, leading the paper to conclude that the proposed design is promising in principle but requires a much larger dataset to work.

**Essential Points**

1. **Paper does not answer the stated research question.** The original idea aimed to estimate the causal impact of appeal outcomes on housing supply, prices, and development dynamics using the leniency IV. The submitted version stops after the first-stage diagnostics, providing no second-stage estimates or outcome analysis. If the primary purpose is now diagnostics about sampling requirements, the paper should explicitly reframe itself; otherwise it risks being judged as an incomplete empirical project. The authors need to either (a) commit to collecting the full population (or a large random sample thereof) and proceeding to the planned impact analysis, or (b) state clearly that the paper’s goal is methodological and provide a research note-style contribution focused on small-sample bias. Without this, the paper fails to deliver on the manifest’s promise.

2. **Limited data undermines the credibility of conclusions about the actual institution.** The analysis is conducted on <1,000 appeals scraped opportunistically, yet the authors make broader claims about the institutional “inspector lottery” and the implications for housing policy. These claims are unsupported because the sample is neither representative nor close to the size needed for the instruments to function. To support any inference about housing provision, the paper must dramatically expand its dataset. Since the manifest already documents that a much larger archive (≈120K appeals) is accessible, the current sample size is hard to justify. The authors should either scale up data collection or clearly label their current analysis as preliminary diagnostics uninformative for policy.

3. **First-stage diagnostics need further nuance.** The negative first stage is attributed to mechanical mean reversion due to small numbers, but the paper stops short of quantifying this effect rigorously. For example, one could simulate the expected bias under the observed inspector-case distribution or estimate the relationship between first-stage reliability and the number of cases per inspector. Without this, the argument remains qualitative. Additionally, the lagged-leniency test, while suggestive, does not fully establish that persistent inspector heterogeneity applies within the key cells (case-type × year). The authors should provide formal simulations or analytical bounds that clarify how many cases per inspector are needed to recover stable leniency scores and demonstrate that the observed sample is indeed in the problematic regime. This would strengthen the paper’s methodological contribution.

**Suggestions**

- **Scale up the dataset.** As noted in the manifest, the Planning Inspectorate’s records cover ~100,000 appeals and, with your extraction pipeline, inspector names are recoverable at high rates. The current sample (860 cases) is a small, convenience subset that does not exploit the manifest’s full feasibility. Completing the promised data collection would likely produce enough observations per inspector to recover a positive first stage and allow you to estimate second-stage effects on housing outcomes or prices. Even if full coverage is infeasible, aim for a large, clearly defined subsample (e.g., all appeals in a region and year range) so that the sample size per inspector is meaningfully higher than two. If resource constraints prevent this, explain them transparently and argue why the current sample is nevertheless informative.

- **Reframe the contribution if the substantive analysis cannot be completed yet.** If collecting the full sample is not possible within the current timeframe, consider presenting the paper as a methodological data note: “We attempted to replicate the inspector leniency IV with preliminary data and illustrate the severity of small-sample bias, providing heuristics for minimum case counts and releasing the extraction pipeline.” This would align expectations with deliverables and allow you to keep the valuable methodological insights. The current title (“The Inspector Lottery That Isn’t”) might then be modified to reflect the focus on diagnostics rather than policy conclusions.

- **Quantify the sampling noise via simulations or analytical bounds.** To bolster the claim that the negative first stage comes from mechanical mean reversion, simulate the distribution of leave-one-out estimators under the observed inspector-case counts (e.g., using bootstrap or analytical formulas). This would allow you to show that even if inspectors follow the same latent allow rate, the estimated instrument would still be negatively correlated with outcomes with high probability given the small sample. Such a quantitative demonstration would make the methodological lesson much more convincing than the current verbal argument.

- **Leverage additional sources of variation or aggregation strategies.** If the individual-level leave-one-out estimator is too noisy, consider constructing alternative instruments that aggregate over broader windows or similar inspectors to increase precision. For example, use the inspector’s cumulative allow rate from all appeals regardless of cell (with year fixed effects to control for time trends) or average over inspectors assigned to the same quarter–case type. Alternatively, the manifest’s idea of aggregating to the LPA-quarter level and instrumenting the share of appeals allowed with average leniency could still be explored even with the current data: aggregate the sample to LPA × quarter, compute the average leniency of inspectors assigned in that cell using all their other cases, and instrument the allow rate by that aggregate measure. These approaches might produce a first stage that is easier to interpret even with limited data.

- **Clarify balance and exclusion arguments.** While you include balance tests for filing lag and case type, the paper would benefit from a more systematic presentation of why the instrument satisfies exclusion. For instance, document whether the allocation rule truly prevents inspectors from being assigned based on case observables (perhaps via a description from PINS documentation or interviews). Also consider testing whether future-case characteristics predict current leniency (placebo) or whether inspector assignment correlates with LPA-level trends, strengthening the case that the instrument is as good as random despite data limitations.

- **Discuss the policy implications more carefully.** The conclusion currently extrapolates from the small-sample diagnostic to policy claims (“developer’s chances depend partly on which inspector is assigned”). These claims would be more credible if supported by a well-identified estimate of the effect of leniency on housing outcomes. Until that estimate is obtained, present policy implications more cautiously, noting that persistent heterogeneity appears to exist but that the current data cannot pinpoint its consequences for housing supply.

- **Make the data and code available.** Since your data collection pipeline is novel and potentially valuable for future researchers, consider releasing the scraping and PDF-extraction scripts along with metadata about the accessible fields. This would support replication, encourage others to scale up the dataset, and align with the paper’s methodological emphasis.

By addressing these suggestions, the paper can better align with its original research promise and sharpen its methodological lessons on leniency designs.
