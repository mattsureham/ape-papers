## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether places hit harder by the prescription-opioid boom subsequently trained more addiction counselors. Using ARCOS shipment data linked to IPEDS completions, it argues that local crises can induce colleges to create or expand occupation-specific training programs, generating a “demand-induced credential pipeline.”

A busy economist should care because the broad question is important: when local labor demand shifts sharply—especially in response to a public health shock—how quickly and through what institutions does training supply adjust? That is a first-order question in labor, education, health, and regional economics.

### Does the paper itself articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Not quite. The current opening is provocative but a little too clever (“the opioid crisis destroyed human capital—but did it simultaneously create it?”), and it risks sounding morally tone-deaf or conceptually confused. The interesting question is not whether the crisis “created human capital”; it is whether local education systems endogenously reallocated training capacity toward occupations suddenly in demand.

The first two paragraphs should say that directly.

### The pitch the paper should have

> The opioid epidemic sharply increased demand for addiction treatment in some places more than others. This paper asks whether local higher-education institutions responded by expanding the training of substance-abuse counselors, providing evidence on how quickly credential production adjusts to crisis-driven labor demand.
>
> Linking county-level opioid shipments from ARCOS to IPEDS completions, I show that areas more exposed to the prescription-opioid boom later produced substantially more substance-abuse counseling credentials. The paper’s broader claim is that local shocks do not just destroy health and employment; they can also reshape the occupational mix of postsecondary training.

That is the right story. It is cleaner, less gimmicky, and more obviously of general interest.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### Contribution in one sentence

The paper’s contribution is to document that counties more exposed to the prescription-opioid boom later produced more substance-abuse counseling credentials, consistent with local postsecondary institutions responding to crisis-induced occupational demand.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest papers?

Only partially. The paper distinguishes itself from the opioid-harms literature by focusing on institutional response rather than damage, and from the education-supply literature by studying a crisis-generated occupation. But the differentiation is still too fuzzy. Right now the contribution reads as “opioids affected yet another outcome.” For AER, it needs to read as “this teaches us something general about how training supply responds to localized demand shocks.”

The paper needs to be sharper about what exactly was unknown before:
1. We know shocks affect labor demand.
2. We know some colleges, especially for-profits and community colleges, respond to labor-market signals.
3. What we did not know is whether a geographically concentrated public-health shock can induce localized expansion in a very specific credential pipeline tied to treatment demand.

That should be the novelty claim.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a gap in a literature?

Mostly about the world, which is good. But it still slips too often into “this contributes to three literatures” mode. The stronger framing is: **How elastic is local credential production to sudden occupational demand?** The opioid episode is the setting, not the main event.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?

At present, maybe, but not crisply. Right now I suspect many would summarize it as: “It’s a county-level DiD showing that places with more opioids later had more addiction-counseling completions.” That is not enough. They should instead say: “It shows that higher ed training capacity appears to reallocate toward occupations created by local crises.”

### What would make this contribution bigger?

A few possibilities, strategically:

1. **Shift from one CIP code to a broader occupational adjustment margin.**  
   If the paper could show reallocation across related behavioral-health credentials—social work, psychiatric tech, peer-support certificates, mental-health counseling, nursing specializations—it would become about occupational supply adjustment, not one tiny program code.

2. **Show program creation, not just completions.**  
   New program entry is a much more compelling institutional response than growth in completions. “Counties hit by opioids saw new addiction-counseling programs open” is a stronger headline than “completions rose.”

3. **Tie to labor market outcomes.**  
   If the broader point is endogenous skill formation, readers will want to know whether these credentials translated into local workforce supply, wages, vacancy relief, or treatment access.

4. **Compare to other shocks.**  
   A paper that establishes a general mechanism often benefits from a second setting: e.g., COVID and respiratory therapy/nursing aides; cybersecurity threats and cyber credentials. Even one compact extension would make the “general mechanism” claim feel earned rather than asserted.

5. **Use a framing around adjustment speed and institutional incidence.**  
   Which sectors respond—community colleges, for-profits, public universities? If the action is mostly in for-profits, that is a much sharper and more consequential story.

   - Cellini on for-profit/program responsiveness
   - Deming, Goldin, Katz on the for-profit sector
   - Bound and Turner on supply constraints in higher education
   - Bettinger/Evans/Turner-type work on field choice and institutional adaptation

3. **Broader labor-market adjustment / endogenous skill formation**
   - Acemoglu on induced innovation / directed technical change
   - Autor on task demand and labor-market adjustment
   - Perhaps papers on local labor-demand shocks and educational supply response

### How should it position itself relative to those neighbors?

Mostly **build on and bridge**, not attack.

- Relative to the opioid literature: “This literature studies destruction; I study institutional adaptation.”
- Relative to the higher-ed responsiveness literature: “This literature shows schools respond to returns and student demand; I show they may also respond to crisis-driven local occupational demand.”
- Relative to broader human-capital theory: “This is a place-based case study of endogenous training supply.”

The paper should not oversell itself as overturning prior work. It is adding a new margin of adjustment.

### Is it positioned too narrowly or too broadly?

Paradoxically, both.

- **Too narrowly** in the empirical implementation: one CIP code in selected counties.
- **Too broadly** in the rhetoric: “crises generate workforce needs that higher education institutions accommodate” is an expansive claim that the current evidence does not fully support.

The right move is to **narrow the claim and broaden the conceptual conversation**. Say less about proving a universal mechanism; say more about using opioids as a lens on local credential-supply adjustment.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?

It should probably speak more to:

- **Health workforce economics**
- **Occupational licensing / credentialing**
- **Regional adjustment to shocks**
- **Institutional entry and program supply in higher education**
- Possibly **social policy implementation capacity**—who staffs treatment expansion after parity/ACA/Cures funding?

There is also a missed connection to the literature on whether local institutions can absorb and respond to shocks. This could attract a much broader audience than the current niche “opioids + IPEDS” framing.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?

Not quite. It is currently having a somewhat narrow “opioid crisis had another effect” conversation. The more interesting conversation is: **When demand for a specific occupation spikes locally, do education providers adjust?** That is a better AER conversation.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

A large literature shows the opioid epidemic devastated communities and reduced human capital. Separately, economists know that some education providers respond to labor-market demand.

### Tension

What we do not know is whether a sharply localized public-health shock induces a localized training response in the relevant occupation. Do colleges merely educate on slow-moving margins, or can they adapt quickly to urgent workforce needs?

### Resolution

The paper finds that counties with greater opioid pill exposure later produced more substance-abuse counseling credentials.

### Implications

If true, this suggests local education supply is more responsive and occupation-specific than we may think. Crises may create not just losses, but also institutional adaptation through credential production. That matters for workforce planning, public-health capacity, and the design of policy responses to shocks.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

Serviceable, but not strong. Right now it is halfway between a story and a bundle of regressions. The title and abstract imply a bold conceptual claim; the body often retreats to “suggestive descriptive association.” That mismatch weakens the arc.

The story it should be telling is:

1. A local shock sharply increased need for addiction treatment.
2. Treatment expansion required a specific type of credentialed worker.
3. Training institutions had varying capacity and incentives to respond.
4. The paper measures whether they did.
5. The answer informs a general question about the elasticity of occupational training supply.

That is the story. The current version instead spends too much time oscillating between headline ambition and defensive caveats.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?

“Places flooded with prescription opioids later trained many more addiction counselors.”

That is the dinner-party line.

### Would people lean in?

Some would, because it is counterintuitive and concrete. But the second sentence matters enormously. If the speaker follows with “which suggests colleges reallocate training toward occupations created by local crises,” people lean in. If the follow-up is just coefficient magnitudes from a county-level design, they check out.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Probably one of these:
- “Is that just because bigger places got more pills and produce more credentials of every kind?”
- “Did colleges actually open new programs, or is this just growth in a tiny existing field?”
- “Did this produce counselors who stayed local and improved treatment access?”

Those are not just referee questions; they reveal what is strategically missing from the paper’s current positioning.

### Are the findings interesting if modest?

Yes, but only if the paper leans into the novelty of the margin, not the size of the coefficient. This is not a paper where sheer treatment-effect magnitude will carry the day. The interesting thing is the institutional response margin. If the paper cannot fully establish causality, it can still matter as the first systematic documentation of a new adjustment channel—**provided** it is framed modestly and embedded in a broader conceptual question.

At present it sometimes feels like a clever descriptive fact looking for a grand theory. That is dangerous.

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

1. **Rewrite the introduction around one question.**  
   Right now there are too many layers: human capital destruction, labor market signaling, endogenous skill formation, induced innovation, crisis generalization. Pick one lead frame: local training-supply response to occupation-specific demand shocks.

2. **Front-load the core empirical fact earlier.**  
   The best descriptive evidence should arrive immediately: growth in this credential category, where it occurred, and why it maps onto opioid exposure. The introduction currently takes a bit too long to state the headline cleanly.

3. **Tone down the abstract and title rhetoric.**  
   “The opioid crisis destroyed human capital—but did it simultaneously create it?” is memorable but slightly strained. “Training the Healers” is fine as a title, but the subtitle should stress local training response to treatment demand rather than “demand-induced credential pipeline,” which sounds coined for this paper rather than discovered by it.

4. **Move some methodological throat-clearing out of the introduction.**  
   The introduction spends too much valuable real estate on IV weakness, placebo interpretation, and detailed coefficient comparisons. The introduction should sell the question, data, and main result. Save the qualification architecture for later.

5. **Promote the most persuasive descriptive institutional facts.**  
   If there is evidence on new program starts, sector composition, certificate versus degree growth, timing, or concentration in community colleges/for-profits, that belongs in the main text near the front.

6. **Demote generic literature paragraphs.**  
   The “three literatures” paragraph is conventional but flat. Better to have one or two sharper paragraphs situating the paper in a broader economic question.

7. **The conclusion should do more than summarize caveats.**  
   It should end on what economists learn about institutional adaptation, not mainly on what the design cannot establish.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

At present, the gap is substantial.

- **Framing problem:** The paper’s best idea is broader than its current presentation. It should be about how localized shocks reshape occupational training supply, not about the opioid crisis “creating human capital.”
- **Scope problem:** One CIP code in a selected sample is too thin to support the paper’s most ambitious claims.
- **Novelty problem:** In its current form, it risks reading like an interesting but narrow application of familiar reduced-form tools to a new outcome.
- **Ambition problem:** The conceptual ambition is high, but the empirical scope does not yet match it.

For AER, the paper needs to either:
1. become a broader paper on occupational-training response to local shocks, or
2. become much more institutionally rich within opioids—program entry, sector heterogeneity, workforce consequences, treatment capacity.

### Single most impactful advice

**Reframe the paper around the elasticity of local occupational training supply to crisis-driven demand shocks, and then expand the evidence beyond one narrow credential outcome so that the general claim is actually supported.**

That is the one change that could most alter the paper’s trajectory.
