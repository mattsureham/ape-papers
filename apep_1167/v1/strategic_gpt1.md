## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether the opioid crisis not only destroyed human capital, but also induced local higher education systems to produce more of a crisis-specific workforce: substance abuse counselors. Using ARCOS shipment data linked to IPEDS completions, it argues that places more exposed to prescription opioids subsequently generated more addiction-counseling credentials, consistent with colleges responding to local labor-market demand.

A busy economist should care because the broad question is important: when shocks create new skill needs, do educational institutions adapt endogenously? That is a real economics question about human capital supply, institutional responsiveness, and local adjustment to crisis.

Does the paper articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs? Mostly yes, but not optimally. The current introduction opens with the opioid crisis as destruction, then pivots to “did it also produce human capital?” That is clever, but the phrase risks sounding glating or gimmicky, and the paper’s real contribution is not “the crisis created human capital” in a welfare sense. The stronger pitch is about **how training institutions respond to localized demand shocks**, with opioids as the setting.

### The pitch the first two paragraphs should have

> Local labor-market shocks may change not only wages and employment, but also what colleges choose to teach. When a crisis creates concentrated demand for specific skills, postsecondary institutions may respond by creating or expanding programs that train the needed workforce. Yet we know little about whether higher education adapts in this way, especially when demand is generated by a public-health emergency rather than by technological change or industrial restructuring.
>
> This paper studies that question in the context of the U.S. opioid epidemic. Linking county-level opioid shipment data from ARCOS to IPEDS completions, I ask whether counties more exposed to the prescription-opioid boom subsequently produced more substance-abuse counseling credentials. The core result is that higher-opioid places saw substantially faster growth in these credentials, suggesting that colleges and vocational institutions form part of the local adjustment margin to crisis-driven skill demand.

That version makes the question legible immediately, elevates the paper from “another opioid paper” to “an endogenous human-capital supply paper,” and avoids overselling the welfare valence.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper documents that counties more exposed to the prescription-opioid boom subsequently produced more substance-abuse counseling credentials, proposing this as evidence that higher education institutions respond to localized crisis-driven labor demand by expanding relevant training.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest papers?

Not yet clearly enough. The paper cites broad literatures, but the differentiation is still mushy. Right now the contribution reads as:

- opioids paper + new outcome
- education-supply paper + unusual shock
- local labor demand paper + credential data

That is not nothing, but at AER level the reader needs to know exactly what prior work has and has not shown. Has anyone studied college program creation in response to local shocks? Yes. Has anyone studied educational responses to opioids? Perhaps indirectly. Has anyone studied crisis-specific credential expansion? Maybe not. The paper needs to state much more sharply: **the novelty is not “opioids affect X”; it is “localized public-health shocks change the composition of credential production.”**

### WORLD question or LITERATURE-gap question?

The paper is trying to be about the world, which is good. The world question is: **Do training institutions adjust to geographically concentrated skill demand created by crisis?** That is much stronger than “there is little evidence on CIP 51.15 completions.”

But the paper sometimes slides back into literature-gap framing (“first evidence of a geographic association consistent with…”). That weakens it. AER readers want to know what they should now believe about how economies adapt, not simply that one more relationship has been measured.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the intro?

A smart economist could probably say: “It’s a paper showing more opioid-exposed counties later produced more addiction-counseling credentials.” That is understandable.

But there is a real risk they would also say: “So it’s another reduced-form opioid paper with a novel outcome.” That is the danger. The paper’s current framing does not do enough to inoculate against that reaction.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Several possibilities:

1. **Shift the framing from one credential to institutional adaptation more broadly.**  
   If the paper could show not just completions, but program entry, program expansion, institution-type responses, and timing, it becomes a paper about educational adjustment rather than just one outcome variable.

2. **Show the mechanism more directly.**  
   The current mechanism is inferred. A bigger paper would connect opioid exposure to:
   - local treatment-sector employment growth,
   - counselor vacancy growth,
   - wage premia in behavioral health,
   - program openings by community colleges / for-profits,
   - geographic timing of credential creation relative to treatment demand.

3. **Use a more revealing comparison group.**  
   Engineering and business are weak placebos strategically. They mostly tell us bigger counties produce more degrees. The paper would feel larger if it compared SA counseling to:
   - adjacent health fields not directly tied to addiction,
   - mental-health counseling vs. unrelated counseling,
   - nursing/social work/psychology programs,
   - fields that require similar institutional capacity but were not opioid-specific.

4. **Make the object of interest the composition of local human capital, not the level of completions.**  
   If the main fact were that opioid shocks shift the mix of credentials toward addiction-related training, that is a more economic and less mechanical contribution.

The single biggest way to enlarge the contribution is to show that this is about **reallocation of educational supply toward crisis-specific skills**, not just more credentials in bigger or harder-hit counties.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

### Closest neighbors

The paper’s closest neighbors seem to come from three conversations:

1. **Opioid epidemic consequences**
   - Alpert, Evans, Lieber (2022) on triplicate prescription programs and OxyContin diffusion
   - Powell, Pacula, Jacobson and related opioid-policy papers
   - Cutler and coauthors on opioid mortality/economic consequences
   - Evans, Lieber, Power and related work on downstream opioid effects

2. **Higher education / program responsiveness to labor demand**
   - Cellini (2010) on for-profit supply responses
   - Deming, Goldin, Katz on for-profits and vocational responsiveness
   - Bettinger / Turner / Bound-type work on educational choice and local labor-market signals
   - Autor’s broader work on demand shifts and skill adaptation

3. **Human capital adjustment to shocks / endogenous skill formation**
   - Acemoglu on induced innovation / task demand
   - Ben-Porath / Becker / Heckman in a broad conceptual sense
   - Possibly literature on training responses to trade, technology, and industrial decline

### How should it position itself relative to those neighbors?

**Build on**, not attack.

The right stance is:

- The opioid literature has mostly emphasized destruction and downstream harms; this paper examines one adaptive institutional margin.
- The education-supply literature has shown colleges respond to labor-market demand from sectors and occupations; this paper studies whether they also respond to **public-health-crisis-induced occupational demand**.
- The adjustment literature studies labor and educational responses to economic shocks; this paper extends it to localized health shocks.

That is cleaner than the current “three literatures” section, which feels somewhat assembled rather than strategically chosen.

### Too narrowly or too broadly positioned?

Currently: both.

- **Too narrowly** in the sense that the outcome is a tiny CIP code and the sample is only counties with such institutions.
- **Too broadly** in the sense that the paper gestures at “opioids, COVID, cybersecurity threats” and grand endogenous-human-capital theories without having enough evidence to support such breadth.

It needs a tighter middle range: this is a paper about **how postsecondary institutions respond to localized occupational demand shocks caused by crisis**.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?

At minimum it should engage more seriously with:

- **Program entry / major supply / field-of-study composition** literature
- **Local public-sector and health-workforce shortages** literature
- **Behavioral health workforce** literature
- **Regional adjustment to shocks** literature
- Possibly **place-based policy and institutions** if the claim is that local educational infrastructure mediates crisis response

Right now the paper is under-connected to workforce-development and health-economics conversations. It knows the opioid literature and some education references, but it needs more direct neighbors.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?

Partly, but not fully. The most impactful conversation may not be “opioids” at all. The paper is stronger if it is framed as a paper on **institutional adaptation and endogenous workforce formation under localized shocks**. Opioids is the application that gives stakes and variation.

That is the unexpected literature bridge that could matter.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

The world before this paper: local crises create labor demand, but we know much more about their destructive effects than about whether training institutions adapt to supply the needed workforce.

### Tension

The opioid epidemic created geographically concentrated need for addiction treatment professionals. Did colleges and certificate providers actually respond on that margin, or were educational systems too slow, too rigid, or too disconnected from local need?

### Resolution

More opioid-exposed counties later produced more substance-abuse counseling credentials.

### Implications

If this pattern reflects real institutional adaptation, higher education is more responsive to localized demand shocks than many models assume. Crisis response may include not just government spending and labor-market adjustment, but also endogenous reshaping of the training pipeline.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has one, but only barely. The bones are there. The problem is that the narrative is constantly interrupted by defensive qualification and by a somewhat awkward “destroyed human capital / produced human capital” rhetorical contrast.

At present, the paper sometimes feels like a collection of regressions looking for a bigger story:
- main association,
- IV,
- panel,
- placebo,
- then a conceptual “credential pipeline.”

The better story is:

1. Crises create highly localized demand for specific skills.
2. Educational institutions might adjust, but we have little evidence on this margin.
3. The opioid epidemic is an ideal setting because the demand shock was sharp, persistent, and occupation-specific.
4. The paper documents that the educational response was real and geographically patterned.
5. This suggests postsecondary supply is part of local adjustment to crisis.

That story is cleaner and more AER-facing than “opioids destroyed but maybe also created human capital.”

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

### What fact would I lead with?

“I find that counties more exposed to the opioid-pill boom later generated much more addiction-counseling training, suggesting colleges respond endogenously to crisis-driven labor demand.”

That is the most interesting fact in the paper.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

Initially, they would lean in. The idea is surprising and intuitively interesting. It reverses the usual direction of opioid papers and points to a neglected institutional margin.

But the follow-up reaction would come fast:  
**“Is that just because bigger counties got more pills and also have more colleges?”**

And that is exactly the problem. The paper itself more or less concedes this concern repeatedly. Once the audience hears that, enthusiasm will cool unless the framing pivots from “causal proof” to “important descriptive fact about adaptation” and the evidence is reorganized around composition and mechanism.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Likely one of these:
- “Is the effect specific to addiction-related training, or just general degree growth?”
- “Did programs actually open, or did existing schools just scale up?”
- “Did counselor employment or treatment capacity rise too?”
- “Is this about per-capita opioid intensity or just county size?”

Those are healthy questions. The paper should be built around answering them as much as possible.

### If findings are modest or vulnerable, is the result still interesting?

Yes, but only if sold correctly. As currently written, the paper wants credit for a substantive positive finding while also disclaiming much of the identifying content. That is unstable.

The better version is: **even absent definitive causality, it is a novel and policy-relevant stylized fact that educational supply appears to move toward crisis-specific occupations in affected places.** That can be valuable if the paper leorients around descriptive geography, institutional timing, and compositional change.

Right now it sometimes feels like a paper that knows its own weaknesses too well and narrates them in the main text. That is intellectually honest, but strategically costly.

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

3. **Front-load the strongest descriptive facts.**

   - growth in SA counseling credentials,
   - geographic concentration,

4. **Reorganize results around the story, not estimator types.**  
   Instead of:
   - OLS
   - IV
   - panel

   Strategically, the IV does not help much. It is coarse, imprecise, and invites argument. If the paper is mainly descriptive/institutional, the IV should not get star billing.

6. **The conclusion should do more than summarize.**  
   It should answer: what should economists now think differently about local adjustment and educational supply? Right now the conclusion mostly restates findings and caveats.

### Is the paper front-loaded with the good stuff?

Somewhat, but not enough. The key interesting idea is there early, but it is quickly submerged in regression narration.

### Are important results buried?

Potentially yes: the most important result may actually be the “many counties had no such programs before” fact. If there is stronger evidence on program emergence, new institutional entry, or field-share changes, that belongs in the main text, not tucked away.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

Be honest: the gap is substantial.

This is not mainly a framing-only problem. The paper has an interesting idea, but in current form it feels too narrow and too vulnerable to the obvious “county size / generic growth” interpretation to excite the top people in the field.

### What is the main gap?

Mostly a **scope + ambition problem**, with some framing issues.

- **Framing problem:** The paper should be about endogenous educational adaptation to localized demand shocks, not mainly about opioid tragedy producing human capital.
- **Scope problem:** One tiny CIP code and a county-level association is too narrow for AER unless the mechanism is nailed down and the comparison is very sharp.
- **Ambition problem:** The paper stops at showing one novel correlation. An AER paper would show a broader institutional phenomenon—program creation, field composition, labor-market linkage, heterogeneity by institution type, timing, perhaps treatment-sector absorption.
- **Novelty problem:** The outcome is novel, but the empirical object is still “shock X predicts outcome Y.” That is not enough by itself.

### What would excite the top 10 people in this field?

A paper that convinces them of this broader claim:

> Local crises reshape the supply side of human capital: colleges and vocational institutions reallocate training capacity toward occupations made scarce by the shock.

To get there, the authors need to show more than one outcome and more than one specification. They need to show institutional adaptation on margins economists care about.

### Single most impactful advice

**Rebuild the paper around educational reallocation and program response—not around a single outcome correlation—by showing that opioid-exposed places shifted the composition of credential production specifically toward addiction-related training, ideally via program entry and institution-type expansion.**

That is the one change that could most improve both the paper’s credibility and its importance.
