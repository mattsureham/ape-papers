## 1. Idea Fidelity

The paper pursues the core of the manifest quite closely: it studies the eNLC, uses QWI county-quarter-industry data for healthcare subsectors, and frames identification around staggered adoption with a Callaway-Sant’Anna DiD. It also implements one of the manifest’s intended extensions—a cross-sector comparison between healthcare and non-healthcare sectors—and uses placebo sectors to probe whether the naïve DiD is picking up general state trends.

That said, several key elements of the original idea are either dropped or only partially implemented. Most importantly, the paper does not use the proposed IPEDS nursing completions data, which would have helped assess the “training pipeline” interpretation emphasized in the discussion. It also does not exploit the demographic/education decompositions in QWI in a serious way: the appendix mentions an education split, but this is not integrated into the main argument. Finally, the border-county spatial design suggested in the manifest is absent, even though that design is especially relevant for a policy whose clearest mechanism is cross-state mobility. So the paper is faithful to the broad research question, but it leaves out some of the most compelling pieces of the original identification and mechanism strategy.

## 2. Summary

This paper asks whether the Enhanced Nurse Licensure Compact increased healthcare employment by reducing interstate licensing barriers for nurses. Using county-by-quarter QWI data and staggered DiD methods, it finds that a simple healthcare-only DiD suggests modest employment growth in adopting states, but that this pattern disappears in a triple-difference comparison with non-healthcare sectors; the preferred estimate is essentially zero for employment, with suggestive declines in hiring and separation rates.

The paper’s main contribution is therefore negative but potentially useful: it argues that the eNLC did not expand healthcare employment, and may instead have reduced labor market churning. The central empirical insight is that naïve healthcare-sector DiD estimates appear contaminated by broader growth patterns in adopting states.

## 3. Essential Points

1. **The triple-difference design is not yet convincing enough to support the paper’s main conclusion.**  
   The key result rests on comparing healthcare to retail/accommodation within county over time, but the paper does not make a strong case that these sectors provide a valid counterfactual for healthcare-specific trends. Healthcare and retail/accommodation were affected very differently by both the business cycle and COVID, and even in the pre-COVID years they have very different secular dynamics. More importantly, equation (2) appears under-specified for a DDD: to absorb state-time shocks and industry-specific national shocks credibly, the specification should include a much richer set of fixed effects/interactions (e.g., county-industry FE, industry-quarter FE, and at least state/post or ideally state-time controls, depending on the exact stacked structure). As written, the DDD may still load on differential sectoral trends across adopting and non-adopting states. Since the entire paper turns on this comparison, the authors need a cleaner and more transparent specification, plus evidence on pre-trends for the DDD estimand.

2. **The paper over-interprets aggregate healthcare-industry QWI outcomes as nurse-specific effects.**  
   The treatment targets nurses, but the main outcomes are total employment, hires, and separations in broad healthcare industries (621/622/623), which include many workers whose licensing status is unaffected by the compact. This creates severe attenuation and interpretation problems: a null effect on total healthcare employment is not strong evidence of no effect on nurses. The appendix’s education split is too weak a proxy and is not incorporated into the central analysis. The paper therefore needs either (i) much stronger nurse-targeting in the outcome construction, or (ii) a substantial reframing of the contribution as an analysis of broad healthcare establishments rather than nurse labor supply per se.

3. **The policy timing, treatment definition, and institutional heterogeneity need to be handled more carefully.**  
   The paper treats eNLC adoption as a clean treatment shock, but in many founding states the eNLC replaced prior NLC participation. For those states, the relevant policy change in 2018 may be incremental rather than a new removal of interstate barriers. This is first-order for interpretation. The authors need to distinguish founding states previously in the original compact from genuinely new interoperability gains, and ideally separate “conversion from old NLC to eNLC” from “new compact entry.” Without that, the treatment bundles states experiencing very different institutional changes, and the null result could simply reflect that many “treated” states were already largely treated before 2018.

## 4. Suggestions

First, I think the paper is asking an important question, and the negative result could be publishable if the design is tightened. The broad message—that licensing reform may not expand employment even when it lowers administrative barriers—is interesting. But at present the argument outruns the evidence, especially because the preferred specification is doing so much work.

The most useful improvement would be to **reorganize the paper around mechanism and exposure** rather than around a single pooled average effect. The policy should matter most where interstate practice actually binds: border counties, commuting zones spanning state lines, and sectors with high nurse intensity or high reliance on agency/travel labor. If effects are truly absent even there, the null becomes much more persuasive. If effects appear there but wash out in the aggregate, that would also be a valuable contribution. The manifest itself anticipated this with a border-county design, and I strongly encourage the authors to implement it. A simple version would compare counties near borders where the neighboring state is compact-eligible versus not, before and after adoption; a richer version would use distance-to-border or border-pair fixed effects. For this policy, such a design is far more behaviorally grounded than a healthcare-vs-retail DDD.

Second, the paper should make much fuller use of the **QWI decomposition by education and demographics**. The current appendix note on E3 versus E4 is too underdeveloped. If nurse-targeted effects are present, they should be stronger in the associate’s/some-college category than in the bachelor’s-or-higher category, especially in NAICS 623 and parts of 622. I realize this is an imperfect mapping, but imperfect targeting is still better than using all workers. You could also look at gender-age cells given the composition of nursing employment, again not as definitive nurse measures but as higher-exposure groups. If the results remain null even in these more exposed categories, the paper’s interpretation would strengthen considerably.

Third, I would strongly recommend incorporating the **IPEDS nursing completions data**, which are mentioned in the manifest but omitted from the paper. Right now the discussion concludes that the “binding constraint is the training pipeline,” but no evidence in the paper actually speaks to that. At minimum, state-year nursing completions could be shown descriptively to establish whether adopting states experienced any differential expansion in supply. More ambitiously, completions could be used to proxy baseline nurse supply elasticity or training capacity: perhaps the eNLC only matters in states with larger nursing program output, where mobility barriers rather than headcount are the bottleneck. Even a simple heterogeneity exercise along those lines would convert a speculative concluding paragraph into an empirically anchored one.

Fourth, the authors should address the **original NLC/eNLC transition issue** much more directly. A clean way to do this would be to classify states into: (a) never in any compact, (b) original NLC participants transitioning into eNLC, and (c) states newly joining multistate recognition under eNLC-era rules. The strongest treatment contrast may be between newly joining states and never-treated states, not between founding states and never-treated states. Even if precision is lower, this would be informative. At a minimum, the paper needs a table listing each state’s prior compact status and the incremental legal change induced by eNLC implementation.

Fifth, the **event-study evidence should be brought into the main text**, not left as a one-sentence appendix claim. Given how different treated and untreated states are, readers need to see dynamic treatment effects and pre-trends. This is especially important because the paper’s own summary statistics show very large level differences between treated and control states. Event studies should be shown for the healthcare-only DiD and, if retained, for the DDD estimand as well. If pre-trends are noisy or non-flat, that should temper the causal claims.

Sixth, I would revisit the **choice of controls and the presentation of the comparison group**. The introduction says 10 never-adopted states, but the text elsewhere lists 11 states; the manifest lists 11 as well. This needs to be reconciled. More substantively, the never-treated states are disproportionately large, urbanized, high-cost states. That does not invalidate DiD, but it makes composition concerns acute. It would help to report weighted and unweighted estimates, and perhaps estimates excluding the largest control states one at a time. A leave-one-out for California and New York would be especially useful. Similarly, county weighting choices matter a lot here: are the estimates county-weighted, employment-weighted, or effectively dominated by small counties? The paper should make this explicit and show robustness.

Seventh, the **COVID discussion needs more nuance**. The pre-COVID restriction is helpful, but the paper continues to use retail and accommodation as placebo/comparison sectors even though these sectors experienced extraordinary pandemic shocks and differential recovery patterns. If the DDD is kept, I would prefer a pre-COVID main specification, with full-sample estimates demoted to robustness. Alternatively, use non-healthcare comparison sectors less distorted by pandemic-specific demand collapses. Professional services or administrative support may be better than accommodation; at minimum, justify the sector choice more carefully.

Eighth, the paper should be more cautious in describing some estimates as **“precisely estimated zero.”** A confidence interval of roughly ±3 percentage points around the DDD employment estimate is informative, but whether that is “precise” depends on the policy-relevant margin. For a large national reform, a 2–3 percent employment effect may still be economically meaningful. I would tone down that language and instead say the paper rules out large aggregate employment effects in the available data.

Ninth, I found the interpretation of lower hiring and lower separations as **“stabilization”** plausible but too speculative for the current evidence. Lower hires and separations could reflect many things, including slower growth or changing composition across industries. To support the stabilization interpretation, you should examine related margins already available in QWI—job creation, job destruction, turnover, or perhaps net employment growth decomposition. If reduced churning is the message, the evidence should be more direct and not rest on two p<0.10 coefficients.

Tenth, the paper would benefit from a clearer discussion of **what QWI is actually measuring** in these industries. For example, hospitals and some healthcare settings involve public-sector or quasi-public employment, and QWI coverage varies by state and sector. The paper says QWI covers over 95 percent of private-sector employment, but then makes broad statements about “healthcare employment” and “nurse shortages.” It would help to be precise about whether key hospital workforces are fully observed in all states, whether public hospitals are missing, and how suppression may differentially affect rural counties—the very places where eNLC effects might be largest.

Finally, I think the paper’s contribution would be sharper if the conclusion were narrowed. Rather than claiming that policymakers are “addressing the wrong constraint,” the evidence more securely supports a more modest statement: **the eNLC does not appear to have generated large aggregate increases in county-level healthcare employment in QWI data**. That is already useful. Stronger claims about nurse supply elasticity, training bottlenecks, or the welfare consequences of licensing reform need either direct nurse-level outcomes or stronger auxiliary evidence.

Overall, I see a promising paper with a potentially interesting null result, but it needs a more credible design for the healthcare-specific contrast, more attention to nurse-specific exposure, and tighter handling of the institutional treatment before its conclusions can be persuasive.
