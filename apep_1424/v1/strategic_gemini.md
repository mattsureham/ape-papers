## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
This paper uses the quasi-random assignment of asylum seekers to immigration judges to isolate the effect of granting legal status on local housing markets. While previous literature conflates the arrival of immigrants with their legal status, this study uses "judge leniency" as an instrument to ask whether the formal rights associated with status—work permits, credit access, and subsidies—actually drive up rents and home values. The paper finds a precise null: at the county level, the marginal legal status grant does not move the market.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this clearly. The first two paragraphs of the introduction are excellent. They move from the institutional "transformation" of the individual to the identification strategy and the gap in the existing "shift-share" literature. No rewrite is necessary.

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
The paper identifies the "legal status premium" in housing markets by isolating status from volume, finding that it is effectively zero at the county level.

- **Differentiation:** It is well-differentiated from Saiz (2007) and Howard (2020) by moving beyond the "total inflow" metric.
- **Framing:** It is framed as a question about the world (Do rights move markets?), but it relies heavily on being a "precise null."
- **Clarity:** A smart economist would immediately understand the "Judge IV" application to a new outcome (housing). 
- **Bigger Contribution:** To make this bigger, the paper needs to prove that the "null" isn't just a "small N" or "dilution" problem. The author currently admits in Section 9 that the treatment intensity might be too low. To be a top-tier contribution, the author needs to find a way to aggregate these effects or look at "asylum-heavy" micro-neighborhoods (ZIP codes) where the 40 grants per year might actually represent a significant percentage of *local* turnover.

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
The paper sits at the intersection of Urban Economics (Saiz, Howard) and the "Judge Leniency" IV literature (Kling, 2006; Maestas et al., 2013).

- **Neighbors:** Saiz (2007), Howard (2020), and Mueller-Smith (2015).
- **Strategy:** It builds on the IV methodology of the labor/crime literature to "correct" the findings of the urban immigration literature.
- **Missing Conversations:** The paper should speak more to the **Informal Housing** literature. If the result is null, is it because the "unauthorized" are already in the market? Connecting to work on the "shadow economy" in housing would make the narrative more sophisticated.

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC
- **Setup:** Immigrants are thought to drive up housing prices.
- **Tension:** Is it just their physical presence, or the legal rights they gain? If it’s rights (credit, Section 8), then status matters.
- **Resolution:** Status doesn't seem to matter at the county level.
- **Implications:** Fear-mongering about asylum grants driving local inflation is likely unfounded; the "action" in housing markets comes from volume/supply constraints, not the legal paperwork.

**Evaluation:** The arc is clean but suffers from a "deflationary" ending. The author essentially argues the result is null because the scale is too small. That is a "boring" reason for a null. The more "AER" story is the **Informal Participation** hypothesis (page 9): that status doesn't matter because the market already integrated these people while they were "illegal."

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
At a dinner party: "I found that giving 40 people a year legal status in a county of 200,000 doesn't change the rent." 
**Response:** "Well... obviously?"

The "So What?" currently fails because of the **Scale** argument. To make people lean in, the author must lead with: "Even though asylees get access to Section 8 and mortgages, their transition to legal status has zero impact on prices, suggesting the 'unauthorized' were already fully baked into the housing demand curve."

---

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
- **Front-load the Mechanism:** Move the "Scale" and "Informal Participation" discussion from the very end into the introduction or a dedicated "Conceptual Framework" section before the results.
- **The Appendix:** The standardized effect sizes (Table 5) are actually very helpful for the "precise null" argument; they should be integrated into the main Results section to prove the paper isn't just "underpowered."
- **Geography:** The author admits the "county" might be the wrong unit. If the data allows for a PUMA-level or ZIP-level analysis, that needs to be in the main paper, not "future work."

---

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The gap is **Ambition.** In its current form, it is a very competent "Note" or a solid *Journal of Urban Economics* paper. 

To be AER, it needs to solve the **Scale vs. Significance** problem. If the effect is null simply because the treatment is a drop in the bucket (0.02% of the stock), it’s a statistical certainty, not an economic discovery. 

**The Single Most Impactful Advice:** Shift the focus entirely to the **"Informality"** story. Conduct a heterogeneity test comparing "sanctuary" areas (where informal housing is easier) vs. "strict enforcement" areas. If the null holds in sanctuary cities but status *does* move the market in strict areas, you have a major paper about how legal institutions interact with shadow markets.
