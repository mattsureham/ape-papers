## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether giving asylum seekers legal status affects local housing markets. Using quasi-random variation in asylum grants generated by immigration judge leniency, it tries to isolate the effect of legal status itself—separate from immigrant inflows—and finds little detectable effect on county-level rents, home values, or homeownership.

A busy economist should care because the paper is taking on a live and policy-relevant question at the intersection of immigration, legal status, and housing: when immigrants gain formal access to work, credit, and regulated housing markets, does that move prices? More broadly, it asks whether “legal status” is an economically meaningful margin for local markets, as distinct from sheer population change.

**Does the paper articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Partly, but not well enough. The current introduction is too method-forward and too attached to “I have a credible instrument and a null.” That is not a top-journal opening. The opening should start from a world question and a real debate, not from the estimation strategy.

**What the first two paragraphs should say instead:**

> Immigration is often blamed for rising housing costs, but existing evidence mostly captures the effect of more people arriving, not the effect of immigrants gaining legal status. That distinction matters. Legal status changes access to jobs, credit, leases, mortgages, and housing assistance, so it could increase formal housing demand even if the number of people in a place does not change.  
>  
> This paper asks whether that legal-status margin is large enough to move local housing markets. I use quasi-random assignment of asylum cases to immigration judges to generate variation in asylum grant rates across U.S. immigration courts, and I link those shifts in legal status to housing outcomes in local areas. I find little evidence that higher asylum grant rates raise county-level rents, home values, or homeownership. The implication is that, at least at this geographic margin, legal status by itself does not appear to be a major driver of local housing prices.

That is the pitch the paper should have. It leads with the world question, explains why it matters, and only then introduces the design.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution in one sentence:**  
The paper claims to provide the first causal estimate of the effect of immigrant legal status—holding immigrant inflows fixed—on local housing markets, and it finds essentially no detectable county-level effect.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?
Not yet sharply enough. The paper says existing work conflates immigration volume and legal status, but it does not do enough to distinguish itself from:
1. immigration-and-housing papers estimating the effect of immigrant inflows on rents/prices;
2. legal-status papers on labor market or household outcomes;
3. the broader judge-leniency IV literature.

Right now the contribution risks sounding like: “another immigration paper with an IV, except the outcome is housing and the result is null.” The differentiation is there in substance, but it needs to be stated more crisply and repeatedly.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or filling a gap in a LITERATURE?
Mostly as a literature gap. That weakens it.  
The stronger framing is:

- **World question:** Does access to formal legality materially change housing demand in local markets?
- **Why now:** This matters for policy debates over asylum, regularization, and whether legal inclusion itself strains housing markets.
- **Then literature:** Existing studies identify inflow effects, not legal-status effects.

That order matters.

### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what's new?
Not confidently. They could probably say: “It uses judge leniency to look at asylum grants and finds no effect on county housing outcomes.” That is decent, but still close to “another DiD/IV paper about immigration and housing.”

What’s missing is a memorable sentence like: **“The paper separates the ‘more immigrants’ channel from the ‘more legal access’ channel.”** If a reader cannot say that instantly, the novelty is not landing.

### What would make this contribution bigger?
Several possibilities, in descending order of impact:

1. **Change the framing from housing prices to the economic meaning of legal status.**  
   Housing prices are currently the endpoint, but the deeper question is whether legal status changes market participation in a way that shows up in equilibrium outcomes. That is bigger and more interesting than a narrow housing-market exercise.

2. **Add outcomes closer to the mechanism.**  
   If the paper wants to say legal status does not move housing markets, it would be much stronger to show whether legal status changes:
   - residential mobility,
   - household formation,
   - overcrowding,
   - transitions into formal renting,
   - address stability,
   - mortgage take-up or banked status,
   - lease-holding or voucher use,
   - neighborhood sorting.
   
   Right now the paper jumps from legal status to aggregate county rents. That is a long leap. Even for an editorial memo that ignores identification, strategically this hurts ambition.

3. **Use more local spatial outcomes.**  
   The paper itself basically admits the county may be the wrong market. If the effects are plausibly highly localized, then the current design may be testing an attenuated object. A tract/ZIP/place-based outcome could make the contribution much more convincing and more substantively interesting.

4. **Reframe around “bounds” and scale.**  
   If the null is the point, the paper should make the contribution: legal-status shocks of this magnitude are too small to move aggregate local housing markets. That is more compelling than “we found nothing.”

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Public debate and much of the literature suggest immigration raises housing costs. Legal status plausibly matters because it opens access to formal jobs, credit, leases, mortgages, and public benefits.

### Tension
But existing studies mostly identify the effect of immigrant inflows, not the effect of legal status itself. So we do not know whether legal inclusion is an important demand channel, or whether the housing effects of immigration are mainly about headcounts and location choices.

### Resolution
Using variation in asylum grant rates induced by judge leniency, the paper finds little detectable effect of legal-status shocks on county-level housing outcomes.

### Implications
At the county level, asylum-based legal status changes do not appear to be a major source of rent or home-value pressure. This suggests either that the scale is too small, the spatial unit is too coarse, or unauthorized migrants were already participating in housing markets enough that legalization does not generate much additional demand.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
It has the ingredients, but the execution is uneven. The current paper is somewhat **a collection of results looking for a story**. The story appears only intermittently, then gets replaced by design talk, first-stage talk, placebo talk, and “null but informative” talk.

The story it should tell is simpler and stronger:

1. Immigration may affect housing through numbers or through legal incorporation.  
2. We know a fair amount about numbers; we know much less about legal incorporation.  
3. Asylum judge leniency isolates legal incorporation.  
4. The answer is: this margin does not move county-level housing prices much.  
5. Therefore, the legal-status channel is not the main equilibrium housing channel at this scale.

That should organize the whole paper.

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?
“I can separate immigrant legal status from immigrant inflows, and when asylum grants rise, county rents don’t visibly move.”

That is the right lead. It is cleaner and more intriguing than quoting coefficients.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
Some would lean in initially because the distinction between population inflows and legal status is genuinely interesting. But they will quickly ask whether the treatment is simply too small or too diffuse to matter. If the answer is obviously “yes,” attention drops fast.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Almost certainly:  
**“Is this telling me legal status doesn’t matter, or just that county-level housing markets are too coarse and the asylum flow too small?”**

That is the key strategic problem. The paper currently gives that objection away too easily. The discussion section essentially arms the skeptical reader with the best reason not to care.

### If the findings are null or modest: is the null itself interesting?
Potentially yes, but only if positioned correctly.

Right now it partly feels like a failed search for an effect. To make the null interesting, the paper must more forcefully argue that:
- this is the right margin to study;
- the estimated margin maps to a meaningful policy debate;
- the confidence intervals rule out effects large enough to matter for county-level housing policy;
- therefore the null is itself informative, not merely inconclusive.

The current “precise null” language oversells precision a bit and undersells interpretation. The more convincing pitch is not “nothing happened,” but **“whatever legal status does for individuals, it is not a quantitatively important county-level housing shock.”**

That is a useful fact.

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

### What would make it read better?

   The current background is efficient enough, but still too much of it reads like setup for the IV rather than setup for the question. AER readers do not need many paragraphs on asylum adjudication mechanics unless those mechanics speak directly to the economic question.

2. **Move most design-defense language later.**

3. **Front-load the substantive result and its interpretation.**  
   By the end of page 1, the reader should know:
   - why legal status could matter for housing;
   - why prior literature cannot isolate it;
   - what this paper does differently;
   - what it finds;
   - why that changes how we think about immigration and housing.

4. **Demote generic robustness discussion.**  
   The heterogeneity and placebo results are standard and not strategically central. If there is any result in the robustness section that helps interpretation—especially evidence on diffusion or lack of local population effects—that belongs in the main text. Boilerplate robustness should be compressed.

5. **Rework the discussion into interpretation rather than self-defense.**  
   The current discussion reads like “why my null is not a design failure.” That is understandable, but not attractive. It should read like “what the result teaches us about the margin under study.”

6. **The conclusion should do more than summarize.**  
   Right now it mostly restates the findings. The conclusion should end on a sharper conceptual claim: immigration’s housing effects are not automatically legal-status effects; formal inclusion need not generate large market-wide price pressure.

### Are there results buried in robustness that should be in the main results?
Yes: the null effect on noncitizen share / lack of detectable local population response is actually useful for interpretation and belongs closer to the headline findings. It helps explain why equilibrium housing outcomes may not move.

### Is the paper front-loaded with the good stuff?
Not enough. The best idea—the distinction between immigration volume and legal status—is present, but buried under identification language.

Yes, strongly. The paper has a potentially good question but introduces it like a methods note with a null result. AER papers usually make the world question unavoidable before they tell you the design.

#### 2. Scope problem
Yes. The paper is trying to make a broad claim from a narrow and possibly attenuated outcome definition: county-level host-county housing outcomes. That is too thin for the ambition of the claim.

#### 3. Novelty problem
Moderate. The legal-status-versus-volume distinction is genuinely novel enough to be interesting. But the current execution does not make it feel like a breakthrough rather than a tidy application.

#### 4. Ambition problem
Yes. The paper is competent but safe. It shows one design, one main null, and then explains why the null might arise. That is publishable in a field context, but top-journal papers usually either:
- answer a first-order question decisively, or
- open a much bigger conceptual window.

This one is not yet doing either.

### What would excite the top 10 people in this field?
A version that either:
1. **directly measures the housing-demand mechanisms of legal status** and shows they do or do not aggregate; or
2. **uses a more local geography** that is genuinely capable of capturing where effects should appear; or
3. **reframes the paper as a quantitative decomposition** of immigration’s housing effects into inflow vs. legal-incorporation channels.

The third option may be the most strategic if new data are limited.

### Single most impactful piece of advice
**Reframe the paper around the distinction between immigrant inflows and legal incorporation, and show why the estimated null is informative about that conceptual margin rather than just a weak county-level housing result.**

If the author can only change one thing, it should be that.
