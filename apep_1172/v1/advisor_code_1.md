**Summary**

This paper studies the impact of state-level cage-free egg mandates on in-state egg production by leveraging their staggered implementation across states and a robust Callaway–Sant’Anna difference-in-differences design. Using USDA NASS data on layers, total production, and per-hen productivity (2010–2026), it finds mandates reduce laying flocks and production by about 22–24 log points, with no significant effect on eggs per layer, suggesting displacement rather than in-place conversion. The effect is driven primarily by California’s Prop 12, with a growing post-treatment trajectory and robustness to alternative control groups.

**Essential Points**

1. **Limited Treated Sample & External Validity:** Only six of the ten mandate states appear in the main estimation because Massachusetts, Nevada, Arizona, and Rhode Island do not report monthly NASS data. This substantially restricts the empirical variation and raises concerns about whether the estimated displacement effect generalizes to the full mandate set, particularly states that are smaller producers or adopt later. The authors should explicitly assess how excluding these states might bias estimates—e.g., are the omitted states more or less likely to convert in place? If possible, incorporate alternative datasets (e.g., USDA annual census) or supplementary exercises to include these states, even at lower frequency, or at least demonstrate that their exclusion does not change the substantive conclusion.

2. **Composition of Control Group & Parallel Trends:** The control group is dominated by large-producing midwestern states (Iowa, Indiana, Ohio) that differ starkly from mandate states in size, industry structure, and possibly trends. While the event study suggests pre-trends are flat, the paper should more fully justify that controls are valid comparators—particularly given that mandate states are smaller producers and include some coastal or regulatory-progressive states. Can the authors show that key observable characteristics (e.g., pre-trend growth rates, poultry infrastructure) are similar? Would matching on pre-treatment levels or slopes alter the results? Alternatively, a synthetic control or reweighting to better align treated and control states might provide reassurance.

3. **Interpretation of Displacement Mechanism:** The policy conclusion rests on interpreting the negative layer and production effects, with null eggs-per-layer, as evidence of displacement. Yet the data cannot distinguish between (a) producers leaving the state and continuing conventional production elsewhere, (b) aggregate reduction in conventional production (e.g., exiting the industry entirely), or (c) reduction in data coverage (e.g., producers reclassifying or aggregating reporting). The authors should strengthen this mechanism claim by providing suggestive evidence: do non-treated states experience corresponding increases in layers/production (per the back-of-envelope in the discussion)? Can they track individual large firms’ geographic adjustments, use USDA slaughter/transfer data, or exploit shipment data? Lacking that, the narrative should be more cautious, or the paper should clarify that displacement is the most plausible interpretation but not confirmed.

If additional major issues arise beyond these three (e.g., standard errors, serial correlation, anticipation effects, placebo timing), the paper should be rejected. Otherwise, addressing these concerns should substantially bolster confidence in the results.

**Suggestions**

- **Augment Treated Sample via Alternative Data Sources:** To capture the omitted mandate states (MA, NV, AZ, RI), consider supplementing the monthly NASS data with annual Census of Agriculture figures or other state-level reports, even if at lower frequency. While these may lack the granularity of monthly data, a bridging analysis could show whether the displacement pattern extends to these states. If such data are unavailable, explicitly discuss how their exclusion might bias the treatment effect—are they more likely to exhibit null effects, thus potentially understating displacement, or are they mostly consumption-only states, in which case their exclusion may not alter the production story?

- **Balance Tests and Re-weighted Controls:** Present formal pre-treatment balance tests on levels and trends for layers and production between treated and never-treated states (e.g., standardized differences, placebo regressions). If substantial imbalance exists, explore re-weighting controls (e.g., entropy balancing, propensity scores) so that never-treated states resemble treated ones on observables. Alternatively, consider a difference-in-differences-in-differences (DDD) design exploiting within-control-state variation (e.g., comparing mandating states to similar non-mandating states within the same USDA region) or block-by-block matching, as used in environmental regulation studies.

- **Assess Spillovers & Spillback:** Since mandates target eggs sold rather than produced, producers could simply redirect conventional outputs outside mandate states, increasing production elsewhere. The paper briefly notes that non-treated states (e.g., Iowa) gained layers, but quantitative evidence would enrich the story. Offer a supplemental figure or table showing the trajectory of production in top non-mandate states relative to their pre-trends. Does the total national flock remain constant? If so, this strengthens the displacement interpretation; if not, it may suggest aggregate contraction or other forces. Similarly, examine whether mandate states import more eggs (if data allow) or shift to non-caged production within-state, plausible if productivity data were available.

- **Anticipation and Implementation Phases:** Address whether producers adjusted before the statutory effective dates (e.g., anticipatory transitions starting when legislation passed). Given that Prop 12 had a long rollout (with 2022 effective date) and lawsuits delayed compliance, enforcement timing may not align neatly with the effective date. Consider including leads of treatment or modeling adoption as a gradual treatment (e.g., cumulative compliance shares). This would also check for anticipation bias in the callaway event study.

- **Clustered Inference and Small-Treatment Group Issues:** With only six treated states, inference can be imprecise. The paper reports clustered standard errors and wild cluster bootstrap for TWFE, but similar bootstrap inference should be presented for the Callaway–Sant’Anna estimates as well (e.g., via percentile-t). Also, address whether the bootstrap uses states as clusters and whether treatments are balanced across the clustering dimension. If small-T issues persist, consider Bayesian hierarchical modeling or permutation tests as a robustness check.

- **Placebo Outcomes and Spillover Outcomes:** The eggs-per-layer placebo is informative, but consider adding the hatching egg data mentioned in the manifest as an additional placebo. If hatching eggs (which are exempt) do not exhibit treatment effects, this bolsters the claim that mandates target retail shell eggs. Additionally, the production displacement story could be strengthened by including outcomes that should not change (e.g., the number of housing units used for breeding hens if available, or feed usage) to ensure that the estimated effects are not driven by reporting artifacts.

- **Discussion of Policy Implications:** The discussion emphasizes that federal standards would prevent displacement, but this assumes that displaced conventional production remains caged. Could the authors elaborate on potential welfare trade-offs? For example, even if production moves out of mandate states, national cage-free production might still increase if consumers elsewhere choose higher-welfare eggs, or if firms reconfigure supply chains. Including a short simulation showing how much conventional output shifted nationally versus how much cage-free output increased would aid policymakers. Also, consider discussing potential future effects once later cohorts (e.g., RI) adopt mandates or if other states follow, and whether the displacement effect might saturate (i.e., once enough states mandate cage-free, there are no safe havens).

- **Transparency on Data Availability and Replication:** Given the importance of policy implications, mention whether the aggregated dataset (state-year or state-month) and code will be made publicly available, particularly as the paper references an autonomous generation. This encourages replication and use in policy analysis.

By addressing these suggestions, the paper can strengthen its identification claims, broaden its empirical reach, and provide more compelling evidence on how animal welfare mandates reshape production geography.
