## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether financial markets discipline firms after environmental disasters by punishing the whole sector or by selectively penalizing firms that are exposed to similar risks. Using global tailings dam failures and mining-firm stock returns, it argues that the market response is mostly cross-firm reallocation rather than indiscriminate contagion, and that this differentiation sharpens after the introduction of an industry governance standard.

A busy economist should care because the paper speaks to a broad question with relevance beyond mining: when regulation is soft or voluntary, can capital markets supply discipline by pricing risk correctly? That is potentially an important question in environmental economics, finance, and political economy.

**Does the paper articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Mostly, but not optimally. The current opening is vivid and competent, but it gets pulled too quickly into the tailings-specific institutional setting. The paper’s best hook is not “tailings dams are important”; it is “when catastrophic risk is governed partly through voluntary standards, do markets actually distinguish exposed from unexposed firms?” That is the general-interest question.

**What the first two paragraphs should say instead:**

> Environmental governance increasingly relies on market discipline. Investors, rating agencies, and voluntary standards are supposed to raise the cost of capital for risky firms, thereby creating incentives for better behavior even when governments do not regulate aggressively. But whether this mechanism works is an empirical question: after a major industrial disaster, do financial markets punish an entire sector indiscriminately, or do they selectively penalize firms that are exposed to similar risks?
>
> This paper studies that question in the global mining industry using tailings dam failures. I show that peer-firm stock-price responses are not mainly sector-wide contagion. Instead, investors reallocate toward firms with less operational exposure, and that differentiation becomes stronger after the 2020 Global Industry Standard on Tailings Management. The broader implication is that voluntary standards matter not because they replace regulation, but because they make risk legible to capital markets.

That is the pitch the paper should have. Start with the world question; then bring in the tailings setting as a sharp test case.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution in one sentence:**  
The paper claims that after environmental catastrophes, stock markets discipline firms through **risk differentiation across peer firms**, not blanket sectoral punishment, and that voluntary disclosure/governance standards strengthen this differentiation.

That is a real contribution. The problem is that it is only partly separated from adjacent literatures, and the paper sometimes frames itself as “first comprehensive dataset on X” rather than “here is a broader fact about how markets process environmental disaster risk.”

### Is the contribution clearly differentiated from the closest papers?
Not clearly enough. The introduction names many papers, but the differentiation is diffuse. Right now the reader gets: industrial disasters, voluntary disclosure, mining safety, ESG pricing, climate finance, self-regulation. That feels like literature coverage rather than intellectual positioning.

The closest neighbors seem to be:
1. **Lang and Stulz (1992)** on contagion vs competitive effects.
2. Papers on **market reactions to industrial/environmental disasters**: e.g. **Capelle-Blancard and Laguna (2010)**, **Humphrey, Lee, and Shen (2016)**, **Klassen and McLaughlin (1996)**.
3. Papers on **environmental information and stock prices**: **Hamilton (1995)**.
4. Papers on **investor engagement / ESG / voluntary standards**: **Dimson, Karakaş, and Li (2015)**, maybe **Flammer (2015)**.

The paper’s differentiation should be much tighter:
- Prior papers largely study **firm-specific disasters** or whether “bad environmental news moves stock prices.”
- This paper is about **peer-firm repricing within a sector** and about whether **voluntary standards sharpen the market’s cross-sectional sorting**.
- That combination is the novelty.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or filling a gap in a LITERATURE?
It begins as a world question, which is good, but then slides into literature-gap mode (“first comprehensive global dataset…”). For AER positioning, the world question is stronger:

- Weak framing: “There is little evidence on tailings failures globally.”
- Strong framing: “A lot of environmental governance rests on the idea that investors can price catastrophic operational risk. Do they?”

The latter is the real paper.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?
Not confidently. Right now they might say: “It’s an event study on mining disasters showing heterogeneous peer effects and some change after GISTM.” That is not bad, but it is too close to “another finance event study.”

The introduction needs to make it easier to say:

> “It shows that markets don’t just punish industries after disasters; they sort firms by exposure, and voluntary standards make that sorting sharper.”

That is memorable.

### What would make this contribution bigger?
Several possibilities:

1. **Upgrade the outcome from short-run returns to something closer to discipline.**  
   If the paper could connect the repricing to bond spreads, CDS, cost of capital, analyst expectations, insurance costs, institutional ownership, or subsequent financing, the “discipline” claim would become much larger. Right now it is a stock-price reaction paper. “Discipline” is one conceptual step beyond that.

2. **Measure exposure more finely.**  
   “Has tailings dams” versus “streaming company” is intuitive, but also coarse and potentially small-sample. A richer measure—number of dams, upstream construction, hazard classification, geographic risk, independent audits—would elevate the paper from a binary comparison to a genuine test of risk pricing.

3. **Make GISTM do more than provide a pre/post break.**  
   As currently framed, the paper suggests that standards improved screening, but the empirical object sounds like before/after timing. Strategically, this is not yet a big enough leap. The paper would be more ambitious if it distinguished firms by actual participation, disclosure quality, or compliance intensity.

4. **Sharpen the mechanism around information.**  
   If the claim is that standards help markets process information, then show that the response is strongest where information should matter most.

The most important scope expansion would be: **move from “returns react” to “capital markets become better at pricing operational environmental risk.”**

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

### Closest neighbors
The likely closest neighbors are:

- **Lang and Stulz (1992)** — contagion versus competitive effects.
- **Capelle-Blancard and Laguna (2010)** — stock market effects of industrial disasters.
- **Hamilton (1995)** — environmental information and market valuation.
- **Klassen and McLaughlin (1996)** — environmental events and abnormal returns.
- **Dimson, Karakaş, and Li (2015)** and perhaps **Flammer (2015)** — investor engagement / market punishment and corporate environmental behavior.
- Also possibly **Humphrey, Lee, and Shen (2016)** on Deepwater Horizon / sector spillovers.

### How should the paper position itself relative to them?
**Build on, not attack.**  
This is not a paper overturning those literatures. It should say:

- Lang-Stulz gives the organizing framework: contagion versus reallocation.
- Environmental-event papers show markets respond to bad news.
- ESG/governance papers suggest investor pressure can matter.
- This paper combines those threads by asking whether environmental disaster risk is priced **cross-sectionally among peers**, and whether voluntary standards improve that sorting.

That synthesis is respectable and potentially interesting.

### Is it currently positioned too narrowly or too broadly?
Paradoxically, both.

- **Too narrowly** in the empirical branding: tailings dams, mining, GISTM.
- **Too broadly** in the literature review: voluntary disclosure, self-regulation, climate finance, industrial disasters, ESG, mining safety.

The paper needs a narrower conceptual lane with broader relevance:
> “This is a paper on market discipline under environmental catastrophe risk, with tailings failures as the setting.”

That gives it a clear audience in finance/environment/public economics without pretending to solve all of ESG.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?
A few relevant conversations seem underdeveloped:

1. **Information economics of certification/standards.**  
   If GISTM matters because it creates a public signal, there is a literature on certification, disclosure, soft law, and standard-setting that could help.

2. **Climate/ESG asset pricing literature with firm-level heterogeneity.**  
   Not just “ESG matters,” but work on when and how investors distinguish firms by exposure.

3. **Corporate misconduct / scandals / peer spillovers.**  
   There is a broader finance literature on spillovers from scandals, accidents, recalls, fraud, and industry incidents. This could help normalize the paper beyond mining.

4. **Law and economics / regulation literature on private versus public enforcement.**  
   The paper’s policy claim is really about substitute/complement relationships between market discipline and regulation.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?
Almost, but not fully. The paper currently sounds like it is in a niche conversation about mining accidents. The more consequential conversation is:

> When can markets discipline environmental risk, and what kinds of institutions make that discipline more precise?

That is the right conversation for a top general-interest journal.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Environmental governance increasingly relies on private actors—investors, disclosure regimes, voluntary standards—to discipline firms when public regulation is incomplete.

### Tension
That model only works if markets can distinguish exposed from unexposed firms after bad events. If they simply punish everyone, or no one, then “market discipline” is rhetoric, not governance.

### Resolution
The paper finds that the average peer response is not strong sector-wide punishment; instead, investors differentiate across firms by operational exposure, and that differentiation becomes stronger after GISTM.

### Implications
Voluntary standards may matter not because they directly force behavior, but because they create information that lets markets price environmental catastrophe risk more selectively.

That is a solid narrative arc. The issue is that the current paper only intermittently tells it. Too often it becomes a tour through tables:
- overall mean,
- heterogeneity by tailings,
- heterogeneity by commodity,
- pre/post GISTM,
- severity,
- windows.

Those are components, not a story.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
**Serviceable, but not yet strong.**  
There is a story here, but the paper often reads like a collection of event-study outputs that the author has worked hard to interpret into coherence.

### What story should it be telling?
The paper should tell one story throughout:

1. **Market discipline is supposed to discipline environmental risk.**
2. **That requires differentiation, not blanket punishment.**
3. **Tailings failures provide repeated tests of whether such differentiation happens.**
4. **It does, and it gets sharper when a voluntary standard makes exposure more legible.**

Everything else should be subordinate. In particular:
- The positive overall average is interesting only as a foil.
- The commodity result matters only because it shows the market is sorting on operational risk, not simple product overlap.
- The severity result matters because it shows the sorting varies with the informativeness/salience of the shock.

Right now the “positive average CAR” is given too much rhetorical weight. It is not the paper’s big fact. The big fact is **differentiated repricing by exposure**.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party?
I would not lead with “average peer CAR is +0.23%.” That is too small, fragile, and forgettable.

I would lead with:
> “After mining disasters, investors don’t just dump mining stocks. They specifically penalize firms exposed to similar tailings risk, and that sorting gets stronger after an industry standard is introduced.”

That is the dinner-party fact.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
Some would lean in—especially economists interested in finance, environment, or governance—but only if the paper is pitched at the level above mining. If presented as “an event study of tailings failures,” many will indeed reach for their phones.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Probably one of these:
1. “Does that actually change firm behavior?”
2. “How do you know this is about information from the standard rather than just Brumadinho or heightened salience?”
3. “Are markets really disciplining risk, or are they just reallocating toward firms that avoid this particular exposure?”
4. “Is the effect economically meaningful beyond a short-run event window?”

Those are exactly the questions the framing should anticipate.

### If the findings are modest, is the modesty itself interesting?
Yes, but selectively. The modest aggregate result is only interesting because it masks offsetting forces. The paper correctly says the action is in heterogeneity. Good. But then it should stop spending so much time defending the unconditional mean. That mean is not what earns the paper attention.

   The background is competent but overlong for a paper whose real contribution is conceptual. We do not need a mini engineering manual. AER readers need just enough to understand why tailings failures are salient and why GISTM could alter information.

2. **Move most of the methodology section to an appendix or compress it.**  
   The market-model event study is standard. The detailed walk-through of Steps 1–3, assumptions, and threats reads like a methods paper or a workshop draft. For editorial purposes, it slows the narrative.

3. **Front-load the main fact better.**  
   The introduction should make clear much earlier that the key result is cross-sectional differentiation, not the overall average effect.

4. **Reduce the literature-review sprawl in the introduction.**  
   Three literatures are too many, and each paragraph is too encyclopedic. The introduction should cite fewer papers but position the paper more sharply.

5. **Bring the best figure/table forward conceptually.**  
   The within-event comparison between tailings-exposed firms and streaming/royalty firms is the most intuitive result. That should be the centerpiece, not one result among many.

6. **Demote some robustness material.**  
   The robustness section is long and often narratively repetitive. Many of these checks can be appendix material. In particular, leave-one-out exercises, benchmarking alternatives, and some of the placebo detail can move.

7. **Rework the conclusion.**  
   The conclusion is decent but overstates the policy implications slightly relative to the evidence. It should end on the narrower but stronger claim: voluntary standards may improve market pricing of catastrophe risk. The behavior-change and welfare implications should be presented as next questions, not implied achievements.

### Is the paper front-loaded with the good stuff?
Partly, but not enough. The abstract is actually quite good. The introduction is reasonably informative. But the paper then spends a lot of time on setup before returning to the central contribution. The reader should encounter the key intellectual move—“discipline requires differentiation”—immediately and repeatedly.

### Are there buried results that should be in the main text?
The most important “buried” idea is in the discussion and limitations: the paper does not really observe actual compliance or risk quality, only a coarse exposure proxy. That should not be hidden. It is central to what the paper is and is not claiming.

Also, if there is any stronger evidence on actual disclosure or standard adherence, that belongs in the main text and would materially improve the paper’s positioning.

### Is the conclusion adding value?
Some, but it mostly summarizes. It should do more interpretation and less recapitulation. Specifically:
- clarify the distinction between **pricing** and **disciplining**;
- state what the paper changes in our beliefs;
- avoid suggesting it has shown more about welfare or prevention than it actually has.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

At present, the gap is mostly a mix of **framing problem** and **ambition problem**, with some **scope problem**.

### Framing problem
The science may be decent, but the paper undersells the general question and oversells the setting. “Tailings dam failures” is a setting; “when does market discipline work for environmental risk?” is the paper.

### Scope problem
The evidence supports short-run repricing, but the paper wants to talk about discipline, governance, and voluntary standards. That conceptual reach is bigger than the current outcome measures. To carry that ambition in AER, the paper either needs:
- stronger evidence on the information channel and standard-based sorting, or
- a more modest title/claim centered on pricing rather than discipline.

### Novelty problem
Not fatal, but real. Event studies of disasters with heterogeneity are not new. The novelty has to come from the combination of:
- repeated global events,
- peer-firm differentiation by exposure,
- and voluntary-standard-induced sharpening of that differentiation.

That combination is potentially publishable. But it must be made to feel like a broader fact, not a competent application.

### Ambition problem
The paper is smart and careful, but it feels safe. It stops at “returns move differentially.” A top-field audience will ask for one more conceptual step:
- do markets get more accurate at pricing risk?
- do voluntary standards actually increase informational efficiency?
- does the repricing affect firms’ real choices?

Without some move in that direction, this is likely to be seen as a neat specialized finance/environment paper rather than a general-interest contribution.

### Single most impactful piece of advice
**Reframe the paper around a general proposition—voluntary standards make environmental catastrophe risk more legible to capital markets—and then either provide stronger evidence for that information mechanism or scale back the “market discipline” language to “risk pricing.”**

That is the one thing. Either deepen the mechanism so the big framing is earned, or tighten the claim so the evidence is fully credible. Right now the paper sits awkwardly between those two levels.
