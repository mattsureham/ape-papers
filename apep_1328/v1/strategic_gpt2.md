## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper studies Estonia’s e-Residency program, which lets foreigners create and manage an Estonian firm remotely, and asks whether removing administrative border frictions actually creates new economic activity. The headline claim is striking: digital access generates a large increase in firm registrations, but little detectable increase in domestic output, suggesting that digitizing entry creates “paper entrepreneurship” more than real local production.

A busy economist should care because this is a clean, policy-relevant test of a bigger question: when governments reduce cross-border frictions through digital public infrastructure, do they generate genuinely new economic activity or mainly reclassify where activity is booked?

**Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Not quite. The current opening is competent, but it starts too much as a border-costs literature review and too little as a punchy economic question. The strongest fact in the paper is not “McCallum showed borders matter,” but “for €100, a person anywhere can create an EU firm online, and the result is a surge in registrations without much output.” That needs to be up front. Right now the reader has to wait too long to learn the real tension.

**What the first two paragraphs should say instead:**

> Governments around the world are building digital systems that let people and firms access legal and administrative services without physical presence. Estonia’s e-Residency program is the most radical example: for about €100, a foreign entrepreneur can create and run an EU company entirely online, without ever entering the country. This policy sharply reduces one specific class of border friction—the administrative cost of forming a firm abroad.
>
> This paper asks a simple but important question: when digital government removes those border frictions, does it create real new economic activity or mainly shift firms on paper? I show that Estonia’s e-Residency program led to a large increase in business registrations, but no corresponding increase in GDP per capita. The implication is that digital public infrastructure can generate a large “registration dividend” without producing much local output—a useful distinction for economists thinking about entrepreneurship, border frictions, and the economic returns to digitization.

That is the AER-facing pitch. It is about the world, not about a design.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution in one sentence:**  
The paper argues that eliminating administrative border frictions through digital residency greatly increases firm registrations but does not meaningfully increase domestic output, implying that digital state capacity can create legal-form activity without much local production.

This is a real contribution, and it is more interesting than the paper sometimes makes it sound. But the contribution is only **partly** differentiated from the nearest literature.

### Is it clearly differentiated from the closest papers?
Only somewhat. The paper cites three buckets:
1. entry regulation and firm formation,
2. border effects,
3. digitization / digital governance.

The problem is that the introduction currently stacks these literatures rather than sharply distinguishing the paper from them. A reader can still come away with: “This is another policy-evaluation paper showing lower entry costs raise registrations.” What is new is not merely that Estonia digitized something. What is new is the combination of:
- **cross-border** rather than domestic entry frictions,
- a **government-provided digital identity / legal access** intervention,
- and the wedge between **registrations and output**.

That triplet should be the contribution.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world or filling a literature gap?
Mostly the former, which is good. The best version of this paper is: **What happens when a state sells legal incorporation access globally through digital infrastructure?** That is a world question. The paper occasionally lapses into “first causal evidence on…” language, which is weaker and more journal-seeking than idea-seeking.

### Could a smart economist explain what is new after reading the introduction?
At present: maybe, but not confidently. They might say, “It’s a DiD/synthetic control paper on Estonia’s e-Residency program finding more firm registrations.” That is not enough. The introduction needs to force the reader to say:  
**“It shows that digitally removing border frictions creates lots of firms on paper, but not much domestic production.”**

### What would make the contribution bigger?
A few concrete possibilities:

- **Better outcome framing:** The GDP null is potentially the paper’s most important result, but GDP per capita is a blunt, macro outcome. If the paper could speak to employment, wages, tax base composition, business-bank activity, exports of services, or domestic value added, the “hollow core” claim would become much more compelling and much less easy to dismiss as underpowered.
- **Mechanism discipline:** The ecosystem-spillover claim is suggestive but not yet central enough to carry weight. Either make it a serious mechanism with stronger evidence, or demote it. Right now it risks sounding like speculative frosting.
- **Sharper counterfactual:** The contribution would feel bigger if framed against a live policy belief: “digital business access can substitute for place-based investment policy.” If the paper can show why that belief is wrong, it becomes more than a one-country case study.
- **Broader framing of state capacity:** The paper could be larger if cast as evidence on how digital states monetize sovereignty—selling legal infrastructure to nonresidents. That is a more original framing than “firm formation paper.”

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

This paper sits at the intersection of entrepreneurship, international economics, and digital public economics / state capacity.

### Closest neighbors
The nearest papers/conversations appear to be:

- **Djankov et al. (2002)** on entry regulation
- **Bruhn (2011)** and **Kaplan, Piedra, and Seira (2011)** on business registration reform and entrepreneurship
- **McCallum (1995)** and **Anderson and van Wincoop (2003)** on border effects
- **Goldfarb and Tucker (2019)** on the economics of digitization
- Possibly broader work on digital public infrastructure / e-government, though economics has less canonical work here than adjacent fields

There is also a neighboring conversation the paper should probably engage more directly:
- tax competition / corporate domicile / legal arbitrage / profit shifting / shell-company formation
- platformized state services and regulatory competition
- geography of entrepreneurship and incorporation choices

### How should it position relative to those neighbors?
**Build on, don’t attack.** The paper is not overturning the entry-regulation literature; it is extending it to a new margin. It is not overturning border-effects work; it is isolating one border component—administrative incorporation frictions—and showing that relaxing that component alone has limited real effects. That is a useful refinement.

The strongest positioning line is:

- Entry-regulation papers show lowering domestic entry costs increases firm formation.
- Border-effects papers show national borders suppress economic activity.
- This paper shows that when one specific border friction—administrative legal access—is removed digitally, registrations jump, but production does not.

That is a clean triangulation.

### Is the paper currently positioned too narrowly or too broadly?
Paradoxically, both.
- **Too narrowly** in the empirical setup: it can read as a niche Estonia policy paper.
- **Too broadly** in some claims: “the digital border dividend” sounds like a grand concept, but the evidence is on one program in one country with one primary outcome.

The right balance is to make a **specific but important claim**: digitizing legal access to a jurisdiction is enough to create lots of firm entries in administrative data, but not enough to recreate the local real-economy effects economists often associate with entrepreneurship.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?
It seems underconnected to:
- **tax avoidance / legal arbitrage / offshore incorporation** literatures,
- **public finance / fiscal competition**,
- **state capacity and digital government**,
- possibly **law and economics of incorporation choice**,
- and some entrepreneurship work distinguishing firm counts from firm quality.

That gap matters. The paper’s most interesting implication is not simply “more firms, no GDP”; it is that states can attract legal entities without attracting production. That is a public finance / institutional competition result as much as an entrepreneurship result.

### Is it having the right conversation?
Not fully. It is currently having a respectable conversation with entrepreneurship and border-frictions work, but the more impactful conversation may be with:
- **how digital states compete for mobile legal activity,**
- **how sovereignty becomes an exportable service,**
- and **why firm creation statistics can become decoupled from real local development.**

That is the unexpected literature connection that could elevate the paper.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Borders create frictions. Forming a firm abroad is especially cumbersome because legal and administrative systems are territorial and often require physical presence. Estonia created a novel digital workaround.

### Tension
If those administrative border frictions are important, removing them should create a lot of entrepreneurial activity. But what kind of activity will that be? Real new production, or merely legal/incorporation activity detached from place?

### Resolution
Firm registrations rise dramatically after e-Residency, but domestic output does not. The program appears to generate administrative firms more than local productive firms.

### Implications
Digital public infrastructure can succeed on a fiscal or legal-services margin without generating traditional growth. Economists and policymakers should distinguish between lowering paperwork barriers and creating the deeper conditions for local production and agglomeration.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
**Serviceable, but not fully disciplined.** The core story is there, and in fact it is pretty good. But the paper intermittently slides away from its strongest arc into a collection of empirical subsections:
- main effect,
- decomposition,
- GDP null,
- robustness,
- discussion.

The main issue is that the “resolution” is split across two findings that are not yet integrated tightly enough:
1. registrations up a lot,
2. GDP doesn’t move.

The paper should tell one story, not two adjacent ones. The story is not “e-Residency increased registrations, and by the way GDP didn’t move.” The story is:
**e-Residency reveals that administrative entrepreneurship and productive entrepreneurship are separable.**

That is the narrative frame the paper should adopt consistently.

The ecosystem-spillover material is potentially useful, but right now it muddies the arc slightly. If the paper’s main message is “the firms are mostly virtual,” then emphasizing spillovers to local entrepreneurship needs stronger evidentiary grounding or more caution. Otherwise the paper sounds like it wants both: big spillovers and hollow output. That is not impossible, but it needs cleaner storytelling.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?
“For about €100, Estonia let anyone in the world create an EU company online. That caused a huge increase in firm registrations—but no detectable increase in GDP.”

That is the dinner-party line.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
Economists would lean in, at least initially. It is unusual, concrete, and policy-relevant. The Estonia setting is memorable. The distinction between legal activity and real activity is interesting.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Almost certainly:  
**“So what are these firms, then—tax vehicles, shell companies, freelancers, platform businesses, or something else?”**

That is the key follow-up. And it points to the paper’s strategic vulnerability: the paper has a strong reduced-form headline, but readers will want richer characterization of what these firms actually are. Without that, the paper risks feeling suggestive rather than definitive.

### If findings are modest or null, is the null itself interesting?
Yes, the null is interesting here—but only if sold correctly. “GDP per capita doesn’t move” by itself is usually too blunt and easy to shrug off. But in this setting, the null is substantively meaningful because the policy is explicitly marketed as an entrepreneurship and growth tool. The paper should lean harder into that. It should frame the null not as a failed experiment, but as evidence that:
- legal access and productive presence are different margins,
- digitization can scale one without the other,
- and firm-count metrics can seriously overstate economic substance.

That is valuable knowledge.

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

### What would make it read better?

1. **Front-load the registrations/output contrast.**  
   The introduction should give the main two-part result immediately: lots more firms, little output effect. Right now there is too much design detail too early.

2. **Shorten the empirical-strategy exposition in the introduction.**  
   The introduction currently spends too much real estate on ASCM, donor pools, conformal inference, few clusters, permutation p-values, etc. That is referee bait, not editorial bait. For AER positioning, the reader wants the question, the setting, the punchline, and why beliefs should change.

3. **Move some inferential detail and sensitivity discussion out of the main results.**  
   The leave-one-out range, permutation rank, and conformal p-values can be streamlined in the main text and expanded in the appendix or a shorter paragraph. The current presentation makes the paper feel defensive.

4. **Promote the decomposition only if it is central.**  
   Right now the decomposition is intriguing but somewhat awkwardly interpreted. Either make it a central contribution with stronger conceptual payoff—e.g., “most of the action is not direct take-up but induced ecosystem effects”—or scale it back as descriptive context. The current version risks over-reading aggregate arithmetic.

5. **The discussion section should do more conceptual work.**  
   This is where the paper should connect to the larger issue: the unbundling of legal jurisdiction from physical production. That’s the bigger idea. Right now the discussion is decent, but still somewhat policy-brief in tone.

6. **The conclusion should not merely summarize.**  
   It should end with a sharper takeaway: digital states can export legal infrastructure, but this is not the same as attracting real economic geography. That is a memorable final line.

### Is the good stuff front-loaded?
Partly, but not enough. The abstract is actually stronger than the opening paragraphs. The introduction needs to inherit the abstract’s confidence and clarity.

### Are important results buried?
Yes: the paper’s most conceptually interesting result—the decoupling of registrations from output—is not buried exactly, but it is still treated too much like an “alternative outcome” rather than the second half of the main result.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

At present, the paper feels like a good field-journal paper with one top-journal instinct: it has a memorable setting and a potentially important conceptual distinction. But it is not yet fully an AER paper.

### What is the gap?
Mostly a combination of:

- **Framing problem:** The science may be competent, but the paper still presents itself too much as an evaluation of a quirky Estonian program rather than evidence on a broader economic phenomenon.
- **Scope problem:** The paper wants to make a broad claim about “real” versus “illusory” economic activity, but the real-activity side is measured mainly with GDP per capita, which is too coarse for the ambition of the claim.
- **Novelty problem, partially:** Lower entry costs increasing registrations is not itself novel. The novelty lies in cross-border digitized legal access and the registrations/output wedge. That has to be the center of gravity.
- **Ambition problem:** The paper is intellectually safe. It documents a policy effect. The AER version would use Estonia to answer a bigger question about the nature of digital state competition and the meaning of firm formation in an era of virtual jurisdiction.

### What would excite the top people in the field?
A sharper claim along the lines of:
- digital public infrastructure allows countries to attract mobile legal entities without attracting place-based production;
- this uncouples entrepreneurship statistics from local development;
- and it changes how we think about border frictions, state capacity, and jurisdictional competition.

That is a top-tier idea. The current manuscript is adjacent to it but not fully there.

### Single most impactful piece of advice
**Reframe the paper around the separation between legal incorporation and real economic activity, and bring in richer evidence or sharper argument on what these new firms are if you want the “hollow core” claim to carry AER-level weight.**

If the author can only change one thing, it should be this:  
**Stop selling the paper as “the first causal study of e-Residency” and start selling it as evidence that digital states can create firms without creating local production.**
