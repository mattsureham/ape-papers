This is an intriguing paper with a genuinely good headline fact, but in its current form it reads more like a polished field-journal paper than an AER paper. The central strategic issue is not whether the estimates are right; it is that the paper has one striking empirical result, but has not yet turned that result into a broad economic question that top general-interest readers will feel they learned something fundamental from.

## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper studies whether making it possible for anyone in the world to remotely form a company in Estonia materially increases entrepreneurship, or simply creates more paper firms. It finds a large increase in business registrations after Estonia's e-Residency program, but little corresponding evidence of output gains, suggesting that digitizing legal access to a jurisdiction lowers administrative border costs for firm formation without generating much real local production.

Why should a busy economist care? Because it speaks to a broader question: when technology makes national legal institutions tradable across borders, what actually moves—real economic activity, or just legal form?

### Does the paper itself articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Partially, but not optimally. The current opening is competent and informed, but it starts from the border-effects literature in a somewhat mechanical way and then moves quickly into institutional detail. It does not immediately foreground the most interesting tension: the separation of legal incorporation from real economic activity. That is the part economists will remember.

The introduction should get to the core idea faster: Estonia turned company law into an exportable digital service; what happens when a country allows global entrepreneurs to buy access to its legal infrastructure? Do firms, production, tax base, and entrepreneurship all move together—or do they come apart?

### The pitch the paper should have

“Digital technology may allow countries to export legal infrastructure the way firms export goods: by selling remote access to incorporation, contracting, and tax administration. Estonia’s e-Residency program is the first large-scale test of whether removing the administrative border around firm formation creates real economic activity or mainly legal entities. I show that digitizing cross-border incorporation sharply increases business registrations, but with little detectable effect on domestic output, implying that digital states can attract firms on paper much more easily than production in place.”

That is the version that belongs in the first two paragraphs.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### Contribution in one sentence

The paper’s contribution is to show that making a country’s incorporation and business-administration infrastructure remotely accessible can dramatically increase firm registrations while generating little corresponding domestic output.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest papers?

Not clearly enough. The paper cites entry regulation, border effects, and digitization, but the differentiation is still a bit generic: “first causal evidence on digital governance and firm formation.” That is not a sufficiently sharp intellectual distinction for AER readers. “First causal evidence” is a weak frame unless attached to a much bigger underlying question.

Right now, a smart reader might summarize it as: “It’s a DiD/SCM paper on Estonia’s e-Residency program showing more registrations but no GDP effect.” That is accurate, but not exciting enough as a contribution statement.

What the paper should differentiate itself against is not just “other papers on digitization” but several nearby claims:

1. **Business-entry deregulation papers**: those show lower domestic registration costs can increase formal firms.
2. **Border-cost papers**: those show borders suppress cross-border economic activity.
3. **State capacity / digital public infrastructure papers**: those imply digital government can improve economic outcomes.

This paper’s distinct claim is stronger and more surprising: **when the state digitizes legal access for outsiders, formal firm creation responds strongly, but real local economic activity may not.** That is the novelty.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world or filling a gap in a literature?

Mixed, but too often as a literature gap. The stronger version is clearly world-facing:

- Can a country export its legal infrastructure?
- Are borders in entrepreneurship mainly administrative?
- When legal form becomes portable, what remains geographically sticky?

Those are AER-style questions. “There is no causal paper on e-residency” is not.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?

Sort of, but not crisply. They would likely say: “It studies Estonia’s e-Residency and finds lots more firms but no GDP effect.” That is a result summary, not a contribution statement.

A stronger intro would enable them to say: “It shows that digital state capacity can delink the location of incorporation from the location of production.” That is a memorable economic idea.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Three concrete routes:

1. **A broader outcome architecture.**  
   GDP is too blunt to carry the “hollow core” claim on its own. If the paper wants to argue that registrations rose without real local economic substance, it needs outcomes that map more directly to local production: employment, wage bill, taxable payroll, domestic investment, VAT base, exports of business services, bank lending, office/commercial real estate activity, or even corporate tax remittances if separable. The conceptual claim is bigger than “no GDP effect.”

2. **A stronger mechanism around what is moving.**  
   Are these firms choosing Estonia as a legal shell, a tax interface, an EU market-access platform, or a bundle of digital administrative services? The paper gestures at “ecosystem spillovers” and “virtual firms,” but those are asserted more than demonstrated. If the mechanism were nailed down, the paper would speak to the economics of the firm, taxation, and jurisdictional competition—not just entrepreneurship.

3. **A reframing around tradable institutions.**  
   The biggest version of the paper is not “Estonia’s program worked.” It is “digitalization makes some state functions internationally tradable, and legal incorporation is one of them.” That pushes it from a country case study to a paper about the changing nature of borders and the location of economic activity.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

### Closest neighbors

The paper’s closest neighbors appear to be:

- **Djankov et al. (2002)** on entry regulation  
- **Bruhn (2011)** and **Kaplan, Piedra, and Seira (2011)** on business registration reforms and entrepreneurship  
- **McCallum (1995)** and **Anderson & van Wincoop (2003)** on border effects  
- More broadly, **Goldfarb and Tucker / Goldfarb et al.** on digitization and the economics of digital technology  
- Potentially literature on **tax competition / corporate location / legal origin / regulatory arbitrage**, though the paper barely leans into this

Also relevant, and currently underplayed, are literatures on:

- **Jurisdictional competition**
- **Offshoring of legal and administrative functions**
- **Intangible capital and place**
- **Platformization of government services**
- **State capacity as a tradable input**

### How should the paper position itself relative to those neighbors?

Mostly **build on and redirect**, not attack.

- Relative to entry-regulation papers: “Those study domestic formalization when domestic entrepreneurs face lower entry costs; this paper studies what happens when the state lowers entry costs for the entire world.”
- Relative to border-effects papers: “This is a test of whether a specific administrative component of border frictions matters for firm formation.”
- Relative to digitization papers: “Digital public infrastructure changes not just efficiency inside the state, but the geographic reach of the state’s legal services.”

The paper should not pretend it overturns border-effects or deregulation literatures wholesale. It should instead isolate one margin—administrative/legal access—and show that this margin matters enormously for registrations but not much for local production.

### Is the paper positioned too narrowly or too broadly?

Paradoxically, both.

- **Too narrowly** in that it often sounds like a policy evaluation of one Baltic digital program.
- **Too broadly** when it suggests it resolves “the border effects literature” or says sweeping things about digital governance in general.

The right level is: a sharp case study used to answer a broad question about legal form versus real activity.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?

The most obvious missing conversation is with work on:

- **Tax havens / profit shifting / regulatory arbitrage**
- **Corporate inversions and place of incorporation versus place of activity**
- **The geography of intangible firms**
- **Platform and marketplace economics**, if Estonia is effectively a platform matching global entrepreneurs to a legal regime
- **Law and finance / corporate law competition**
- **State capacity / digital public infrastructure** in a more serious way than the current cursory references

That missing literature matters because the paper’s core finding—more firms, no output—is not just an entrepreneurship result. It is very close to a result about the unbundling of legal jurisdiction from production.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?

Not quite. It is currently having a conversation with “business registration reform” and “border effects.” Those are relevant but not sufficient. The more impactful conversation is with the economics of jurisdiction, place, and the portability of legal infrastructure in a digital economy.

That is the unexpected literature connection that could elevate the paper.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Before this paper, economists know that borders impede economic activity and that reducing business entry costs can increase firm formation. We also know governments are digitizing services, but we do not know whether digitizing legal access to a country for foreigners generates real economic activity or mostly administrative activity.

### Tension

Estonia created a remarkable institutional innovation: anyone can remotely form and manage an EU company there. This should lower cross-border entrepreneurship costs dramatically. But it is unclear whether that creates genuinely new productive activity, relocates legal entities, or simply manufactures paper entrepreneurship.

### Resolution

Firm registrations rise sharply after e-Residency. But broader output does not move much, suggesting that the reform changed where firms are legally organized more than where production occurs.

### Implications

Digitizing legal infrastructure can generate a large “administrative” entrepreneurial response, but that should not be confused with local growth. Policymakers should think of e-governance not only as reducing friction, but as creating exportable legal services whose incidence may fall on registrations, fees, and tax interfaces rather than jobs or output.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the ingredients, but not yet the clean arc. At present it still feels somewhat like a collection of empirical findings:

- big registration effect,
- decomposition,
- GDP null,
- some discussion of spillovers.

The story that should organize the paper is:

**Digitalization made Estonia’s legal infrastructure internationally accessible. That triggered a surge in legal entities, but not in local production. Therefore, reducing administrative border frictions changes legal organization much more readily than real economic geography.**

That is the story. Once that is the spine, every table has a role:
- Table 1: legal activity rises
- Table 2: not just direct users, broader ecosystem responds
- Table 3: but real local production barely moves
- Conclusion: digital states can export legal form, not necessarily economic substance

Right now the paper says this, but it does not fully structure itself around it.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?

“For about €100, Estonia let anyone on earth form an EU company online, and business registrations surged—but GDP didn’t.”

That is a good dinner-party line. People will lean in.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

Lean in initially. The setup is novel and memorable. But the second question will come fast: “So are these real firms, tax arbitrage vehicles, or just digital shell companies?” If the paper cannot answer that more directly, enthusiasm will fade.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Almost certainly one of these:

- “What exactly are these firms doing?”
- “Did this create tax revenue, jobs, or investment?”
- “Is this entrepreneurship, or regulatory arbitrage?”
- “Why GDP, and not a more direct real-activity measure?”
- “Did Estonia effectively become a platform for global microfirms?”

That follow-up question is revealing: the paper’s current form has a good headline fact, but readers will want a sharper answer about the nature of the firms and the margin along which the reform operated.

### If the findings are modest or null, is the null interesting?

Yes, potentially very interesting—but only if sold correctly. A null on GDP is not inherently exciting. It becomes exciting if it is presented as evidence that **legal location and economic location can be decoupled by digital state capacity**. Then the null is not a failed second-stage outcome; it is the key conceptual point.

At the moment, the paper does not fully earn that interpretation. It states it forcefully, but the evidentiary architecture is still light. So the null is interesting in concept, but not yet fully converted into a compelling contribution.

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

### What would make it read better?

1. **Shorten the methodological throat-clearing in the introduction.**  
   The intro spends too much valuable real estate on design details, p-values, and inferential caveats. For an editor deciding strategic fit, this is exactly the wrong emphasis. The first several pages should foreground the phenomenon and the main conceptual takeaway.

2. **Move most inference detail out of the introduction.**  
   The conformal inference/permutation/leave-one-out discussion is too prominent up front. Keep one sentence on approach, one sentence on design credibility, and move the rest later.

3. **Bring the decomposition and “hollow core” logic forward.**  
   The introduction should state, very early: “Most of the action is in registrations, not output, which suggests digital access to legal infrastructure affects legal form more than production.” That is the hook.

4. **Consider combining Results and Discussion more tightly.**  
   Right now the interpretation comes a bit late. The paper would read better if each major empirical result were immediately tied to the bigger conceptual point.

5. **Trim generic literature-claiming language.**  
   “First causal evidence” and broad three-part contribution paragraphs feel formulaic. Replace with sharper statements of what readers should update their beliefs about.

6. **The conclusion should do more than summarize.**  
   It currently restates the findings. It should end on a broader implication: digital public infrastructure changes the set of state services that are globally contestable. That is the memorable concluding thought.

### Is the paper front-loaded with the good stuff?

Only partly. The abstract is actually stronger than the introduction. The best idea—legal entities up, output not—is there, but the paper then becomes overly procedural. The reader should not have to wait for the Discussion section to see the paper’s true ambition.

### Are there buried results that should be in the main text?

Conceptually, yes: the paper’s most important “result” is not another coefficient but the distinction between administrative firm creation and productive activity. Anything that sharpens that distinction should be main-text material. If there are data on tax revenue, sector composition, firm survival, employment, or ownership geography, those should be central, not peripheral.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

At present, the gap is mostly a combination of **framing**, **scope**, and **ambition**.

### Is it a framing problem?

Yes, substantially. The paper has a good result but undersells the question. It should be framed as a paper about the unbundling of legal jurisdiction and economic activity in the digital economy, not as a clever evaluation of Estonia’s e-Residency program.

### Is it a scope problem?

Yes. For AER, one country-level program with registrations and GDP as the key outcomes is too thin. The paper needs a richer mapping from legal-form responses to real economic consequences. Right now the move from “registrations rose” to “these are largely virtual firms” is plausible but not yet big-paper convincing.

### Is it a novelty problem?

Somewhat. The institutional setting is novel, but the empirical template is familiar, and the question risks sounding niche unless expanded. Novelty of setting is not enough for AER; the paper needs novelty of economic insight.

### Is it an ambition problem?

Yes. The paper is competent but safe. It identifies a striking pattern and then mostly stops at documenting it. The more ambitious version would use Estonia as a laboratory for a much bigger claim about how digital technology changes the nature of borders, firms, and states.

### The single most impactful piece of advice

**Reframe the paper around the claim that digitalization makes legal incorporation portable across borders, and then build the evidence around distinguishing changes in legal form from changes in real economic activity.**

If the author can only change one thing, that is the thing. Everything else follows from it: literature, outcomes, mechanism, and why economists beyond this niche should care.
