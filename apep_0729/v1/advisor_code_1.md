**Idea Fidelity**  
The paper does not follow the manifesto’s central idea. The original proposal promised a causal estimate exploiting Norway’s precise eligibility discontinuity for produksjonstilskudd—specifically, a fuzzy regression discontinuity around the number-one/number-two market-share threshold and potential DiD reweighting after the 2022 reform. Instead, the submitted paper uses a simple cross-sectional comparison of municipalities with any subsidized paper in 2021 versus those without, without exploiting the threshold-based variation or the rich, multi-year newspaper-level data that the manifest outlined. As a result, the identification strategy switches from a credible quasi-experiment to an observational correlation, missing the key design that was supposed to yield causal inference. The data sources (SSB turnout, Medietilsynet grant lists, and population controls) are consistent with the manifest, but their empirical use diverges sharply from the promised RDD specification.

**Summary**  
The paper documents that Norwegian municipalities with subsidized “number two” newspapers exhibit 1.1–1.6 percentage point lower voter turnout than other municipalities, with larger gaps for local elections and in the pre-2017 period. The analysis relies on a pooled municipality-election panel (2001–2025) and uses OLS with log population, county, and year fixed effects. The author interprets the negative association as suggestive of selection into subsidized markets rather than a causal democracy dividend from the subsidies.

**Essential Points**  
1. **Lack of credible identification**: The paper explicitly admits that the treatment isn’t exogenous and proceeds with OLS estimates of subsidized versus non-subsidized municipalities. This approach doesn’t answer whether the production subsidy causes turnout changes. Without exploiting the sharp market-rank eligibility rule (the supposed RDD) or another quasi-experiment, the estimates conflate program effects with all sorts of unobserved municipal characteristics (e.g., economic structure, urbanization, political culture). Given the stated goal of testing the democracy dividend, this design is insufficient. The paper should either implement the promised regression discontinuity/DiD approach or be explicit that it only documents conditional correlations, in which case the policy claims must be tempered. As it stands, the causal inference is not credible.  
2. **Treatment construction is incomplete and potentially misspecified**: The manifest envisioned exploiting annual subsidy eligibility around circulation thresholds; the paper instead uses a 2021 cross-sectional indicator that a municipality has at least one subsidized newspaper. This ignores the dynamic nature of eligibility and confounds long-term treated municipalities with those that may never have been eligible. It also fails to use the detailed circulation and rank data necessary to construct the RD running variable (market share gap). The binary 2021 indicator captures differences in media market structure rather than subsidy exposure per se. The authors need to either (a) justify why the 2021 snapshot meaningfully proxies for the subsidy’s causal effect over the full panel, (b) exploit more granular newspaper-level data to create the running variable and treatment status as promised, or (c) clearly redefine the research question as descriptive.  
3. **Interpretation overreaches**: The discussion concludes that the absence of a positive association implies a modest democracy dividend, yet the analysis cannot rule out that subsidies still have positive effects that are masked by positive selection (e.g., subsidies targeted at declining turnout municipalities). Without proper causal identification, the observed negative correlation could reflect compensatory targeting rather than an absence of effect. Claiming that “subsidized municipalities have lower turnout” should remain tentative and contextualized as descriptive. If the authors cannot implement the promised RD, they should substantially tone down causal language and focus on documenting selection patterns rather than drawing policy conclusions about the program’s efficacy.

**Suggestions**  
1. **Implement the intended fuzzy RDD**: The manifest identified a clear source of exogenous variation—the sharp jump in subsidy eligibility when a newspaper loses the “number one” market position. The paper should construct the running variable (market share gap) using the newspaper-level circulation data from Medianorway (tracing circulation of leading and runner-up papers in each municipality-year) and the subsidy award data to identify the treated units. Applying the Calonico–Cattaneo–Titiunik bandwidth selection and local linear estimators around the zero gap will deliver the causal effect of subsidies on turnout, conditional on standard RD assumptions. Even if not all eligible firms take the subsidy (hence fuzzy), this approach is feasible and would fulfill the original research promise.  
2. **Leverage panel structure and reforms for DiD**: The manifest described the 2022–2023 formula reform as a secondary source of variation. If feasible, exploit municipalities whose subsidy eligibility changed because of the reform to construct a staggered DiD design (e.g., following Callaway and Sant’Anna). This would allow using richer time variation rather than relying on a single year. In combination with the RD, this would triangulate the effect and make the causal story sharper.  
3. **Clarify treatment dynamics and sample**: Since the current dataset spans 2001–2025, describe how treatment status evolves over time. If a municipality receives a subsidy in only some years, treatments should vary by year. A 2021 indicator misses temporal dynamics. If data limitations force a static treatment, clearly explain why and discuss the implications. Ensuring that the sample includes only municipalities with comparable newspaper market histories will reduce confounding.  
4. **Expand discussion of mechanisms and heterogeneity**: The observed stronger negative association in local elections is interesting. The paper could probe whether municipalities with subsidized papers differ on observable characteristics (e.g., industry composition, median age, education) that are themselves predictive of local election turnout. Presenting balance tables or incorporating controls for these observables would help assess selection. If the RD is implemented, these heterogeneities would be within a narrow bandwidth and hence more credible.  
5. **Preregister or share data/code for reproducibility**: Given the policy importance and the autonomy of the research process, sharing the code used to merge SSB and subsidy data (or providing a detailed appendix describing that merge) would help reviewers understand the construction of the treatment variable. This also makes it easier to trust future causal analyses derived from the same dataset.  
6. **Reframe policy claims**: Until a causal design is in place, avoid attributing the lack of a “democracy dividend” to the subsidy. Instead, emphasize that municipalities with subsidized outlets have lower turnout, but the direction of causality remains open. Reframe the paper more modestly as documenting selection into the subsidy program, which then motivates the need for causal analysis (e.g., the fuzzy RD). This shift would align the paper better with its current empirical content.
