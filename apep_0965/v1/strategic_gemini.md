### 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper investigates the labor market consequences of the 2018 EU retaliatory tariffs, which were surgically targeted at US industries in politically sensitive districts (e.g., bourbon, Harley-Davidson). Using administrative quarterly data, the author finds that while employment in targeted industries dropped significantly, total manufacturing employment in those counties remained stable because workers were absorbed into other local manufacturing sectors. This suggests that politically motivated trade "shots" act more as a temporary signaling device that forces costly reallocation rather than a driver of permanent regional economic decline.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this pitch quite well in the first two paragraphs. It immediately hooks the reader with the political narrative (McConnell, Ryan, the Rust Belt) and transitions to the identification strategy. 

*Suggested Pitch Improvement:* The current intro is strong, but to reach "AER-level" gravity, it should lean harder into the theoretical tension: Why does this trade shock result in reallocation when the "China Shock" resulted in decimation? The pitch should emphasize that this is a test of *local labor market flexibility* in the face of targeted vs. broad-sectoral shocks.

---

### 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution:** The paper demonstrates that targeted retaliatory tariffs cause significant industry-level job churn and separations but fail to reduce aggregate local manufacturing employment due to intra-sectoral reallocation.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It differentiates itself from Fajgelbaum et al. (2020) and Amiti et al. (2019) by moving from consumer prices to labor dynamics, and from Autor et al. (2013) by contrasting the "surgical" shock with the "broad" China shock. 
*   **Question vs. Literature:** It frames itself as answering a question about the world (how do trade wars affect workers?), which is a plus. 
*   **Clarity:** A smart economist would understand the "what’s new" immediately: it’s about the *granularity* of the shock and the *resilience* of the local market.
*   **How to make it bigger:** To make the contribution "top-tier," the author needs to dig deeper into the *characteristics* of the workers being reallocated. Are they moving to lower-wage manufacturing? Does the "signal" work if the voters (workers) don't actually lose their livelihoods, just their specific jobs?

---

### 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

*   **Closest Neighbors:** Autor, Dorn, and Hanson (2013); Fajgelbaum et al. (2020); Pierce and Schott (2016); Dix-Carneiro and Kovak (2017).
*   **Positioning Strategy:** The paper builds on the "Local Labor Market" literature but acts as a "corrective" or "nuance" to the China Shock narrative. It should position itself as a study of *adjustment frictions* rather than just *trade exposure*.
*   **Missing Conversations:** The paper could benefit from speaking to the **Search and Matching** literature. The finding of "elevated separations with stable hires" is a classic labor churn story that deserves more theoretical grounding than just a "trade" label.

---

### 4. NARRATIVE ARC

*   **Setup:** The 2018 trade war and the EU's "surgical" retaliation.
*   **Tension:** If the EU targeted these areas to cause political pain, did they actually cause economic pain? Or are local markets too fluid for surgical strikes to work?
*   **Resolution:** Targeted industries bleed workers, but the local manufacturing base absorbs them. There is "pain" (separations/churn), but not "ruin" (net job loss).
*   **Implications:** Politically targeted tariffs are effective signals of *intent* but ineffective tools for *economic destruction*.

**Evaluation:** The arc is clean. However, the "tension" is slightly undermined by the small dollar amount of the tariffs ($2.8B). The author needs to argue why $2.8B is a "feature, not a bug"—perhaps by framing it as a test of the *minimum threshold* required to trigger a political response.

---

### 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

*   **Lead Fact:** "The EU's tariffs on Mitch McConnell's bourbon successfully killed bourbon jobs, but those workers just walked across the street to other factories."
*   **Reaction:** Lean in. It challenges the "trade always destroys communities" trope that has dominated since ADH (2013).
*   **Follow-up:** "Do they earn less in the new jobs?" (The paper currently mentions earnings data in the appendix/data section but doesn't lead with it—this is a missed opportunity).

---

### 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

*   **Front-loading:** The "Leave-one-industry-out" result (Table 4) is actually one of the most interesting parts because it reveals the confounding role of US protectionism in the steel industry. This should be moved earlier or integrated into the main results discussion rather than treated as a "robustness" footnote.
*   **Appendix:** The earnings results (mentioned in the Data section) should be moved to the main text. If employment is stable but wages fell 10%, the "resilience" story changes significantly.

---

### 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

**The Gap:** Currently, the paper is a very high-quality "Policy Evaluation." To be an AER paper, it needs to be a "General Interest Economic Lesson."

**Single Biggest Improvement:** The author must analyze **wages/earnings**. If the story is "reallocation," we need to know the *cost* of that reallocation. If workers reallocated to same-wage jobs, the EU's political strategy is a failure. If they reallocated to 20% lower-wage jobs, the "political pain" is real even if the employment head-count is stable.
