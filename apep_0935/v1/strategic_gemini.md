### 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper evaluates the 2018 First Step Act's expansion of the "safety valve," which allowed federal judges to bypass mandatory minimum sentences for a new group of drug defendants. Using a difference-in-differences design and a unique "policy reversal" validation from a 2024 Supreme Court case, it asks whether giving judges more discretion reduces average sentence severity and whether that discretion narrows or widens racial sentencing gaps. This matters because it tests a fundamental trade-off in institutional design: whether rigid rules prevent bias or prevent the exercise of mercy that could correct for it.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates the "discretion vs. bias" tension well in the first paragraph, but it gets bogged down in statutory minutiae (U.S.C. codes) too quickly in the second.

**The "AER Pitch":**
> Mandatory minimums are designed to ensure uniformity, but they often mechanicalize inequality. This paper exploits a major expansion in judicial discretion—the First Step Act’s safety valve—to test whether giving judges the power to ignore sentence floors reduces racial disparities or allows judicial bias to flourish. By combining a large-scale natural experiment with a rare Supreme Court-mandated policy reversal, I provide a definitive account of how institutional flexibility reallocates the costs of incarceration across demographic groups.

---

### 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution:** The paper identifies the causal impact of the First Step Act’s safety valve expansion on sentencing outcomes and decomposes the resulting changes in racial disparities into mechanical and discretionary components.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It is well-differentiated from USSC descriptive reports and builds specifically on the Rehavi & Starr (2014) and Yang (2015) tradition of looking at the *mechanisms* of disparity.
*   **Framing:** It is currently framed as "answering a question about the world" (does discretion help?), which is high-level enough for the AER.
*   **Clarity:** A smart economist would see this as "The First Step Act paper."
*   **Expansion:** To make the contribution bigger, the author needs to lean harder into the **Pulsifer (2024) reversal**. Most papers have one shock; this has a "treatment" and a subsequent "withdrawal of treatment." This is a gold mine for testing the symmetry of policy effects—do sentences go up as fast as they came down?

---

### 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

*   **Closest Neighbors:** Rehavi & Starr (2014, JPE) on charging; Yang (2015, JLS) on *Booker*; Cohen & Yang (2019, AEJ: Policy) on judicial ideology; Tuttle (2019, AEJ: Policy) on plea bargaining.
*   **Positioning:** It should position itself as the "Post-Mandatory Minimum" paper. The 2010s were about how bad mandatories are; this paper should be about whether the *fix* (discretion) actually works.
*   **Missing Conversations:** The paper is light on the **Political Economy of the Judiciary**. It should mention the selection of judges (Appellate vs. District) or how judges respond to being "overruled" by the Supreme Court (Pulsifer).
*   **Framing:** Connecting to the "Incomplete Contracts" literature or "Rules vs. Discretion" (classic macro/IO framing) would elevate it from a "crime paper" to a "general interest paper."

---

### 4. NARRATIVE ARC

*   **Setup:** The federal system is a "bottleneck" of mandatory minimums.
*   **Tension:** We want to lower sentences, but we fear that "human" discretion is just a veil for "human" bias.
*   **Resolution:** Discretion lowers sentences overall (the "mercy" effect) but is applied unevenly (the "bias" effect).
*   **Implications:** Policy cannot just "add discretion"; it must "structure discretion."

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong but the "Resolution" section in the current draft (Table 2/3) looks statistically weak (p < 0.05 or p < 0.10). The story needs the results to be more robust or the narrative to focus more on the *heterogeneity* (why it worked in some districts and not others) rather than just the aggregate effect.

---

### 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

*   **The Lead Fact:** "When Congress gave judges a way out of mandatory minimums, judges saved thousands of years of prison time, but they gave the biggest 'discounts' to white defendants."
*   **Reaction:** People will lean in. It’s a "policy-relevant" finding that complicates the bipartisan consensus on the First Step Act.
*   **Follow-up:** "Did prosecutors just change what they charged to cancel out the judge's new power?" (The author needs a better answer to this "waterbed effect" than just "limitations.")

### 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

*   **Front-loading:** The *Pulsifer* reversal is the "coolest" part of the identification. It is currently buried on page 12. Move the Pulsifer logic into the main empirical strategy section.
*   **Condense:** The "Institutional Background" is too long. Economists need to know the *incentives*, not the section numbers.
*   **Appendix:** Table 2's Column 1 and 2 are redundant. Move them to the appendix; start with the most controlled model.

---

### 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

**The Gap:** Currently, the paper feels like a very high-quality "Policy Evaluation." To be an AER paper, it needs to be a "Theory-Testing" paper.

**Single Biggest Improvement:**
The author must move beyond "did sentences change" and provide a **structural decomposition of the racial gap**. Specifically, use the *Pulsifer* shock to see if judges "clawed back" the benefits from Black defendants faster than from white defendants. This would move the paper from "evaluating a law" to "uncovering the nature of judicial bias."
