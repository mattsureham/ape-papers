**Summary**

The paper studies the 2018 First Step Act expansion of federal drug safety valve eligibility, comparing defendants with criminal history categories II–IV (newly eligible) to category I (already eligible) in a difference-in-differences setup using USSC data from FY2016–FY2024. It finds that newly eligible defendants received shorter sentences and were more likely to benefit from the safety valve post-FSA, with some differential effects by race. The analysis includes robustness checks, including the pulsifer decision, and discusses implications for racial equity when judicial discretion expands.

**Essential Points**

1. **Identification needs to match the stated research question on judge discretion.** The manifest emphasized leveraging random case assignment to judges and estimating judge-level heterogeneity in safety valve usage, ideally via a judge-leniency instrument, but the paper never operationalizes this. If the goal is to assess how expanded judicial discretion through safety valve eligibility affects sentencing and racial disparities, it is critical to connect variation in sentencing outcomes to actual judge-level behavior. As written, the DiD measures the average effect of the reform on the newly eligible group, but it cannot distinguish whether the effects arise from judges’ discretionary decisions or from other margins (e.g., prosecutorial selection, plea bargaining). Please either implement the judge-leniency IV / triple-difference design described in the manifest or clearly justify why the simpler DiD suffices and adjust the framing away from claims about judicial discretion.

2. **Parallel trends and comparison group validity require stronger evidence.** The treatment group (CH II–IV) and control group (CH I) differ substantially in observable characteristics (see Table 1), and the paper does not present a convincing event-study showing pre-trends of the same outcomes. Without such evidence, the core DiD assumption is tenuous—especially because underlying sentencing practices, prosecutorial charging, or defendant selection may have evolved differently for higher criminal history defendants even absent the reform. Please present event-study estimates for sentence length and safety valve usage, ideally with placebo checks for other outcomes, and discuss how differential trends are addressed (e.g., via flexible controls, synthetic control, or bounding methods). Without this, the causal interpretation is weakened.

3. **Racial heterogeneity claims need clearer causal interpretation and mechanisms.** The paper reports race-specific effects and a triple-difference that suggests differential impacts for Black defendants, but it lacks a clear exposition of whether these differences stem from judges’ application of discretion versus compositional changes in the newly eligible pool. Moreover, the point estimates in Table 4 implying a +2.33 month change for whites but −8.00 for Black defendants raise questions about statistical reliability (e.g., are these from separate regressions or the same model?) and do not tie back to a specific mechanism. Please clarify the econometric specification, present standard errors for both coefficients, and provide evidence on whether the heterogeneity arises through the safety valve application channel (e.g., race-specific first-stage estimates, judge fixed effects, within-judge comparisons). Without this, the racial disparity discussion risks overstating what the data can support.

**Suggestions**

1. **Integrate judge-level variation into the empirical strategy.** If judge identifiers are available (as suggested by the manifest’s reference to JUSTFAIR), please exploit them. One strategy is to construct judge-specific safety valve utilization rates using pre-FSA observations and use the leave-one-out mean as an instrument for the probability of safety valve use. This would allow you to estimate how the reform’s expansion of discretion interacts with judge leniency, and to tie the mechanism to judicial behavior rather than average treatment effects. Even if the full IV framework is infeasible, consider at least including judge fixed effects (or random effects) and interacting the reform with judge leniency indicators (e.g., above/below-median pre-reform safety valve usage). Present summary statistics showing the distribution of judges’ pre-FSA leniency and how it relates to defendant race.

2. **Strengthen parallel trends evidence and robustness.** In addition to the event study, consider implementing the following robustness checks: (a) propensity-score–weighted event-study to balance observables between CH II–IV and CH I defendants; (b) a “triple-difference” using a secondary comparison group (e.g., defendants in offenses without mandatory minimums) to control for broader FSA-related changes; (c) analysis over narrower windows around the reform to reduce concerns about long-run trends; and (d) a bounded DiD using the Makarov-style decomposition into composition and effect changes. Reporting placebo regressions where the treatment timing is randomly assigned or where the treatment group is defined by a characteristic unaffected by the FSA would also strengthen credibility.

3. **Clarify the Pulsifer exercise and its interpretation.** The Pulsifer reversal is a valuable quasi-experiment, but the current description is vague (Table 5 simply shows a coefficient of 0.05 with a large SE). It would help to define precisely which defendants are affected—e.g., those who previously satisfied the expanded (disjunctive) criteria but fail one prong under the conjunctive interpretation—and then show DiD estimates comparing them to unaffected defendants before and after June 2024. Presenting a figure or table that traces safety valve usage and sentence length for the affected subgroup around the Pulsifer ruling would make the validity check more compelling. Also, provide a power calculation or sample size discussion to explain the imprecision.

4. **Enhance the racial disparity mechanism section.** The current narrative mixes policy discussion with empirical results. It would be helpful to decompose the total DiD effect into (a) a mechanical channel—the broader eligibility allowing all newly eligible defendants to qualify—and (b) a discretionary channel capturing differential safety-valve application by judges. You might do this by estimating the DiD on the probability of safety valve use and then modeling race-specific residuals. Alternatively, consider an IV approach where judge leniency is used to instrument for safety valve receipt, and the reduced-form or second-stage effects by race reveal whether judges’ discretion contributes to disparity changes. Finally, contextualize the racial patterns with descriptive statistics (e.g., share of recently eligible defendants by race, distribution of criminal history points). This will help the reader understand whether the observed heterogeneity is a result of differential treatment or differential composition.

5. **Improve table presentation and interpretation.** Some tables (e.g., Table 3) present coefficients without clear labels or context (e.g., column 4 in Table 2 is “NA”). Provide complete regression tables with consistent notation and significance stars. Include more informative table notes—for example, specify which fixed effects and controls are included per column. For the main sentence table, report effect sizes both in months and as a percentage of the baseline sentence to convey practical significance. Similarly, for the racial heterogeneity table, separate the race-specific coefficients and present their standard errors and sample sizes.

6. **Discuss potential prosecutorial responses.** The plausibility discussion notes that prosecutors may adjust charging strategies, but the paper does not provide empirical evidence. Even if these data are unavailable, consider arguing that the included covariates (e.g., drug quantities, offense levels) capture such strategic changes, or show descriptive trends in charge loads or plea rates. Alternatively, analyze a subset of cases where charges are less likely to change (e.g., only those with specific offense characteristics) to demonstrate robustness.

7. **Supplement with graphical evidence.** A plot showing the average sentence (or safety valve rate) over time for both treatment and control groups would help readers assess trends visually. Another plot could illustrate the distribution of criminal history points pre/post FSA by race to highlight the policy-relevant population shifts. If judge-level data are used later, graph the distribution of judge leniency and how it correlates with safety valve application.

8. **Clarify the policy message in the conclusion.** The conclusion currently states that expanding discretion “worked as intended” but leaves open whether this reform should be replicated given racial heterogeneity. Reframe the policy takeaway to emphasize under what conditions safety valve expansions reduce sentences equitably (e.g., when paired with oversight of discretionary choices). Suggest directions for monitoring or supplemental reforms (e.g., standardized reporting on race-specific discretionary decisions). This will make the policy implications more actionable.

With these refinements—especially incorporating judge-level variation and bolstering identification—the paper would make a stronger causal contribution to the literature on discretion, mandatory minimums, and racial equity in sentencing.
