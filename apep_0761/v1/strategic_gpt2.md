## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple, important question: when states ban abortion, what happens to the healthcare labor that used to provide those services? Using state-quarter industry employment data around *Dobbs*, the paper argues that reproductive-health employment did not visibly collapse in ban states but instead expanded in neighboring non-ban states, suggesting geographic reallocation of capacity rather than straightforward contraction.

A busy economist should care because this is not really just an abortion paper; it is a paper about how state policy shocks operate in an integrated national market. If true, the implication is that restrictive state policies may shift economic activity and labor across borders rather than eliminating it.

**Does the paper articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Not quite. The current introduction is competent, but it starts from the post-*Dobbs* literature and then identifies an overlooked margin. That is a literature-gap pitch. For AER, it needs to open with the world question: **when states ban a medical service, does supply disappear, adapt, or move?** The current “receiving-state perspective” is interesting, but the first two paragraphs still read like “here is another consequence of *Dobbs*” rather than “here is a general economic question revealed by *Dobbs*.”

### The pitch the paper should have

> State policies often aim to restrict access to a service within state borders, but in an integrated economy they may instead reallocate workers and capacity across space. The reversal of *Roe* created an unusually sharp test: when abortion bans took effect almost simultaneously across many states, did reproductive-healthcare labor contract, or did it move?
>
> This paper shows that after *Dobbs*, employment in family planning centers did not fall detectably in ban states, but rose sharply in neighboring non-ban states. The central implication is that abortion bans reorganized the geography of reproductive healthcare rather than simply shutting it down, highlighting a broader lesson about policy incidence in federal systems: legal restrictions in one place can create capacity booms just across the border.

That is the AER version of the story.

**One-sentence contribution:**  
The paper’s contribution is to show that the post-*Dobbs* abortion-policy shock appears to have geographically reallocated reproductive-healthcare employment toward bordering non-ban states, rather than producing an obvious net contraction in ban states.

Right now the novelty is essentially: “others study births, travel, and labor supply after *Dobbs*; I study provider employment.” That is a real distinction, but not yet a big one. A smart reader could still summarize this as “another reduced-form *Dobbs* paper, this time on family planning employment.”

To stand out, the paper needs to be much more explicit about what prior papers cannot tell us:

- Travel papers show where patients go, not whether provider capacity expands to absorb them.
- Birth and infant health papers show downstream effects, not the supply-side adjustment margin.
- Female labor supply papers show household consequences, not market-level reorganization of healthcare labor.
- Healthcare workforce papers study how regulation affects provider supply in general, but not how service bans trigger cross-border reallocation in a specific medical market.

Mostly as filling a literature gap. That weakens it. The stronger frame is:

- **World question:** What do service bans do in a federal system with mobile workers and cross-border demand?
- **Not:** “The post-*Dobbs* literature has overlooked workers.”

The current introduction says “one question has been almost entirely overlooked.” That is a classic gap-filling sentence. Fine for a field journal; not enough for AER.

That is understandable, but not memorable.

What you want them to say is:

### What would make this contribution bigger?
Several possibilities:

   The big question is whether subnational restrictions in integrated economies displace activity rather than suppress it.

- Patient-side studies document demand reallocation.
- This paper asks whether provider-side capacity followed.
- The broader implication is that subnational restrictions can shift supply geographically.

That is the right triangle: *Dobbs* outcomes → provider capacity → spatial policy incidence.

Paradoxically, both.

The paper should narrow the empirical claim and broaden the conceptual stake.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Before this paper, we know *Dobbs* sharply changed the legal geography of abortion access. We know patients traveled, births rose in ban states, and downstream outcomes changed. But we know less about whether the healthcare supply side shrank, adapted, or relocated.

### Tension
The obvious intuition is that bans should reduce abortion-related employment in ban states. But integrated markets create a competing possibility: demand and providers may shift geographically, especially to nearby states. So the puzzle is whether the policy reduced capacity or merely moved it.

### Resolution
The paper finds no detectable decline in family planning employment in ban states, but a substantial increase in neighboring non-ban states, with a stronger differential result relative to a placebo healthcare sector.

### Implications
The implication is that abortion bans changed the spatial organization of healthcare provision more than they changed aggregate labor demand in the sector. More broadly, state-level restrictions may generate cross-border supply responses that blunt local contraction while imposing travel and congestion costs elsewhere.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
It has the ingredients, but the arc is only **serviceable**, not fully convincing. The main weakness is that the paper is trying to tell two stories at once:

1. a provider-labor reallocation story, and  
2. a “national workforce expansion” story.

The first is plausible and interesting. The second is more ambitious than the evidence shown. That overreach weakens the narrative.

At moments, the paper feels like a set of results looking for a bigger story:
- null in ban states,
- positive in neighboring states,
- placebo DDD,
- some robustness.

The right story is simpler and stronger:  
**Dobbs offers a test of whether subnational bans suppress local healthcare supply or reallocate it across borders. The data point to reallocation.**

That should be the through-line of the paper.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Immediately:

And then:  
“Is this actual worker relocation, new entry, or just relabeling/service mix changes?”

That second question is the key strategic vulnerability of the paper’s current positioning. Referees can fight about evidence; as editor, I’m focused on whether the paper is centered on the right question. Right now, the paper’s own language invites mechanism questions it cannot fully answer.

### If the findings are null or modest, is the null interesting?
Yes, the null in ban states is potentially very interesting—but only if framed properly. “No employment decline in ban states” is not a boring non-result if the reader expects clinics to shut down and staff to disappear. The paper needs to sell this as a surprise to a widely held intuition.

But it must avoid making the null do too much work. The null is interesting as evidence against simple contraction, not as proof that nothing happened in ban states.
