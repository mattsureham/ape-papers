**Idea Fidelity**

The paper hews closely to the manifested idea. It exploits the Hill-Burton Act as a historical source of supply-side variation in hospital capacity, links that variation to contemporary measures of concentration (HHI), and studies the resulting implications for Medicare spending. The author also follows the manifest’s policy motivation and data sources—CMS Geographic Variation files for spending and CMS Hospital Compare for hospital counts—although the paper uses a proxy instrument (inverse 1950 state income) rather than the manifest’s preferred direct measure of Hill-Burton dollars per capita by county. In other words, the paper preserves the core research question and identification concept, but it relies on a coarser instrument than originally envisioned, missing the within-state bed-shortage variation emphasized in the manifest.

**Summary**

This paper documents that the observed negative cross-sectional relationship between hospital market concentration and Medicare spending is driven by rural, low-cost counties being mechanically concentrated, not by a protective effect of competition. Using inverse 1950 state per capita income as an instrument for today’s county-level HHI, the author shows that the IV estimate flips sign, suggesting higher concentration raises Medicare spending, while also acknowledging that the instrument likely violates the exclusion restriction. The resulting comparison between OLS and IV provides a diagnostic bracketing of the likely causal effect and highlights the magnitude of negative selection bias in naïve estimates.

**Essential Points**

1. **Credibility of the Instrumental Variable.** The identifying instrument—inverse 1950 state per capita income—has a plausible first-stage but a highly problematic exclusion restriction. As the paper itself concedes, the instrument predicts modern demographic, health, and socioeconomic characteristics that directly influence Medicare spending. The paper therefore lacks credible identifying variation that isolates the channel “Hill-Burton → hospital supply → current concentration → Medicare spending.” Instrument validity should not rest solely on the speculative “persistence of infrastructure” argument; it requires hard evidence that history affects spending only through concentration. Without a convincing exclusion argument (or at least a credible strategy that bounds the threat), the IV estimate cannot be interpreted inferentially, even as an upper bound. The mere fact that the instrument predicts covariates underscores the need for robustness to omitted variables, ideally through additional instruments or alternative research designs.

2. **Aggregation and Measurement of HHI.** The equal-share HHI constructed as \(10{,}000/N\) assumes symmetric hospital size and treats critical access hospitals and VA facilities as equivalent to full-service acute-care hospitals. This measurement likely compresses substantial variation in market structure—missing the fact that a county with one large tertiary hospital and several small critical-access hospitals is much less competitive than the equal-share measure suggests. The resulting measurement error is likely classical and biases the OLS estimate toward zero, but it may also contaminate the first stage and IV estimate in non-obvious ways. A more nuanced measure, perhaps combining hospital admissions (if available from AHRQ HMS) or using weighted counts by bed size, is essential to ensure the dependent and endogenous variables accurately represent market power.

3. **Interpretational Anchoring Between Bounds.** Positioning the OLS and IV estimates as bounds on the “true” effect is attractive, but the paper lacks a principled way to anchor the truth between them. If the IV is upward biased due to channels other than concentration, and the OLS is downward biased due to selection, the gap between them could be arbitrarily wide and depends on the magnitude and direction of those omitted channels. Without additional identifying assumptions or external evidence on the size of those biases, the bounds are not informative enough to support policy conclusions. The paper should either find stronger sources of plausibly exogenous variation (e.g., direct Hill-Burton grant exposure or within-state bed shortage allocations) or use bounding approaches that leverage observable covariates to shrink the interval (e.g., control function, partial identification, pre-trends).

**Suggestions**

1. **Leverage More Granular Hill-Burton Data.** One of the manifest’s promises was the availability of detailed Hill-Burton project data. The paper currently uses the inverse of 1950 state income as a proxy for those allocations. If feasible, assembling county-level Hill-Burton dollar amounts, bed grants, or presence of Hill-Burton hospitals would allow for a more localized treatment and stronger linkage to current hospital supply. Even a state-level instrument could be improved by interacting it with county-level indicators for Hill-Burton projects or bed shortages, exploiting within-state variation in the original formula. This would both strengthen the first-stage plausibility and dilute concerns that the instrument captures broader historical poverty rather than infrastructure creation per se.

2. **Explore Alternative Research Designs.** Given the exclusion threat, the paper could supplement the IV strategy with alternative designs that do not rely on the same instrument. For example, a difference-in-differences framework comparing changes in spending in counties that received more Hill-Burton-funded capacity growth relative to those that did not—using pre- and post-Medicare data—might help isolate the persistent effect of supply shocks. Alternatively, a regression discontinuity design exploiting the 4.5 beds per 1,000 threshold mentioned in the manifest (if historically binding) could provide quasi-experimental variation. At the very least, robustness checks such as controlling for current state-level trends in income, education, and public health infrastructure, or using placebo instruments (e.g., inverse 1950 income interacted with state fixed effects lagged by a decade) would illuminate how sensitive the IV is to alternative specifications.

3. **Strengthen the Discussion of Mechanisms.** The paper already discusses potential channels—volume, intensity, upcoding—but further fleshing out these mechanisms with empirical evidence would enhance the narrative. For instance, the author could examine whether concentration is more strongly associated with utilization-intensive subcategories (e.g., outpatient imaging, post-acute care) conditional on demographic controls. The placebos in Table 2 are helpful, but adding more hospital-specific outcomes (readmission rates, case mix index, hospital admissions per capita) would demonstrate that concentration affects hospital behavior and not just county-level demand. Linking the estimated effects to theoretical channels from the competition literature (e.g., Bain’s x-inefficiency, upcoding) would also add interpretive depth.

4. **Quantify the Direction and Magnitude of Selection Bias.** The sign reversal narrative is compelling, but it would benefit from a more quantitative investigation of the selection bias. For example, the author could apply Oster’s (2019) method to bound the treatment effect under different assumptions about unobservables, using comparisons between controlled and uncontrolled specifications. Alternatively, the paper could model the selection process explicitly—estimating a propensity to be a concentrated market and conditioning on that propensity—thereby isolating the bias that arises from observables and extrapolating to unobservables. Providing such quantitative diagnostics would make the argument that “OLS is biased downward by X percent” more persuasive than a qualitative statement.

5. **Clarify the Policy Implications.** The concluding claim—that neglecting selection leads policymakers to understate the cost of hospital concentration—rests on the IV estimate being upward biased yet still positive. This is a nuanced message, and it would help to state more explicitly how the bounds should inform policy. If the IV estimate is unreliable, what can antitrust enforcers learn from the OLS-IV gap? Should they treat the gap as evidence that competition reduces Medicare spending by at least a small magnitude (the OLS coefficient’s absolute value), or that it could be as large as the IV estimate minus the bias (which remains unquantified)? A clearer articulation of what these estimates say (and do not say) about mergers and FTC enforcement would improve the paper’s policy relevance.

6. **Address Sample Selection and External Validity.** The regression sample excludes counties without hospitals, reducing the sample to 1,620 observations. This selection may systematically exclude the most rural and poorest regions—the very areas where Hill-Burton may have had the largest impact. The paper should discuss how excluding zero-hospital counties affects the interpretation of the estimates, especially given that those counties are, by definition, infinitely concentrated. If data allow, including these counties with imputed HHIs (e.g., treating them as monopolies with a hypothetical hospital) or modeling the extensive margin separately could broaden the paper’s scope. Likewise, clarifying whether the results apply primarily to counties that “could” support hospitals versus all Medicare beneficiaries would help readers interpret the magnitude of the effects.

7. **Improve Presentation of IV Diagnostics.** The appendix currently reports first-stage $F$-statistics and balance tests in prose. Including a table summarizing the first-stage regression with coefficients, standard errors, partial \(R^2\), and overidentification tests (if multiple instruments are used) would allow readers to evaluate instrument strength and relevance more transparently. Similarly, tabulating the balance tests with the same format as the main regressions (e.g., coefficient, SE, \(t\)-stat) would quantify how correlated the instrument is with each covariate. If the instrument is strongly correlated with the HCC risk score, for example, the paper could show how leaving that control out affects the IV estimate—this sensitivity analysis would highlight how much of the IV shift is driven by observable imbalances.

By addressing these suggestions, the paper would retain its central insight about selection bias while substantially strengthening the credibility of its identification strategy, the precision of its concentration measure, and the clarity of its policy implications.
