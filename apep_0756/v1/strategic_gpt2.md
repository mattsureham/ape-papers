A busy economist should care because this is not just a paper about scheduling; it is a paper about how a modern labor regulation changes labor market dynamics in a large low-wage sector. ## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Before this paper, economists know that low-wage service-sector jobs are highly unstable and that workers dislike unpredictable schedules. Policymakers have responded by regulating scheduling practices, but it is unclear how firms adjust at the margin.

### Tension
The tension is whether fair workweek laws are a clean job-quality improvement or whether they also create adjustment costs that reduce hiring and change who benefits. In other words: do these laws improve jobs without tradeoffs, or do they create insider gains and outsider losses?

### Resolution
The paper finds that scheduling laws reduce separations and hiring while increasing employment. The interpretation is that firms retain incumbents more and hire less, producing a retention dividend for current workers and a thinner pipeline for entrants.

### Implications
The implication is that job-quality regulation is not distribution-neutral. It may stabilize low-wage work, but it can also reduce labor market access. That matters for how economists think about labor standards and how policymakers evaluate them.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
There is a usable arc here, but the paper currently only half-commits to it. Parts of the draft read like a collection of plausible results around a local ordinance rather than a paper driven by one sharp question.

The biggest narrative problem is that the paper keeps oscillating between three stories:
1. scheduling laws improve worker welfare,
2. scheduling laws affect labor market flows,
3. scheduling laws resemble employment protection.

These are related, but the paper needs to choose one as the spine. The best spine is **#3**, with #1 as motivation and #2 as evidence.

### What story should it be telling?
It should be telling this story:

- The service economy is increasingly regulated along nonwage margins.
- Scheduling laws are a particularly important example because they directly target firms’ ability to adjust staffing.
- That means they should affect labor market fluidity, not just worker experience.
- The data show exactly that: reduced churn, reduced hiring, higher employment.
- Therefore, job-quality regulation creates an insider-outsider tradeoff and should be analyzed as labor market regulation, not merely as worker protection.

That is coherent and memorable.

But the paper should be careful: modest estimates on their own do not make an AER paper. The case must be that even moderate changes in churn and hiring reveal something general about how firms respond to nonwage labor standards.
