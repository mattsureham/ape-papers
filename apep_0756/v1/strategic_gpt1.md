## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks what happens to labor market dynamics when cities require employers in retail and food service to provide predictable schedules. Its central claim is that fair workweek laws reduce turnover and reduce hiring, producing a more stable workforce for incumbents but a thinner entry margin for would-be workers.

A busy economist should care because this is not just another paper on “job quality”: it speaks to a broader question about whether labor regulation in high-churn service sectors reallocates opportunities from outsiders to insiders. If true, that is a general labor-market insight, not merely a niche result about scheduling.

### Does the paper articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Not really. The current opening is vivid, but it starts too small and too sociological. Paragraph 2 then frames the paper mainly as filling an understudied literature gap on scheduling. That undersells the paper. The strongest version is not “scheduling has been overlooked”; it is “a growing class of labor regulations may stabilize jobs by reducing labor market fluidity, and fair workweek laws provide a sharp test of that mechanism.”

### What the first two paragraphs should say instead

The introduction should open with the economic tradeoff, not the anecdote:

> Many labor regulations aim to improve job quality, but they may do so by changing who gets access to jobs. Fair workweek laws, which require large retail and food-service employers to post schedules in advance and compensate workers for last-minute changes, are a useful case because they target a defining feature of low-wage service work: employers’ reliance on just-in-time staffing and high turnover.
>
> This paper asks whether such laws make jobs better by stabilizing employment relationships, or whether they instead reduce firms’ willingness to bring in new workers. Using administrative data on hires, separations, and employment around staggered adoption of fair workweek laws, I show that the laws reduce both separations and hiring, while modestly raising employment. The core implication is that schedule regulation creates an insider-outsider tradeoff: incumbent workers gain stability, but new entrants face fewer openings.

That is the pitch. Right now the paper has the ingredients, but not the hierarchy.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### Contribution in one sentence

The paper’s contribution is to show, using administrative labor-flow data, that predictive scheduling laws in retail and food service appear to reduce both worker separations and new hires, implying that job-quality regulation can stabilize incumbent employment while narrowing entry opportunities.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest papers?

Only partially. The paper distinguishes itself from worker-survey studies of schedule predictability by emphasizing administrative data and employer-side flow outcomes. That is a real distinction. But the contribution is still framed too much as “first administrative-data estimates” and not enough as “evidence on the general equilibrium margin that previous work could not see.” “First” claims are fragile and don’t travel well; the conceptual distinction is what matters.

The paper needs a sharper contrast against at least three nearby strands:

1. **Worker welfare / schedule instability papers**: These show unpredictable scheduling harms workers and that reform may improve predictability.
2. **Employment protection / firing cost papers**: These show regulation can reduce both hires and separations.
3. **Labor market fluidity papers**: These document declining churn, but usually not from this regulatory channel.

The novelty is not just that this is about scheduling rather than wages. The novelty is that it interprets scheduling regulation as a labor market institution affecting churn and access.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a literature gap?

Too much the latter. The paper says scheduling is “underexplored” and the causal effects “have not been estimated at scale.” That is a literature-gap framing. It is serviceable, but weak for AER.

The stronger world question is:

- When policymakers make low-wage jobs more predictable, who benefits and who loses?
- Do job-quality mandates function like a mild form of employment protection?
- Does improving conditions on the intensive margin reduce opportunities on the extensive margin?

That is a much bigger conversation.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?

Right now, they would probably say: “It’s a DDD paper on fair workweek laws showing less turnover and less hiring.” That is competent, but not memorable enough.

The introduction should make them say instead: “It shows that schedule regulation creates an insider-outsider tradeoff by reducing churn in service-sector labor markets.” That is a more durable intellectual contribution.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Most importantly: **make the object of interest labor market access and insider-outsider redistribution, not schedule predictability per se**.

Specific ways to enlarge it:
- **Different outcome framing:** Push hires/separations as the main event, not ancillary outcomes.
- **Heterogeneity by likely outsiders:** young workers, low-tenure workers, women with childcare constraints, non-college workers, or demographic groups disproportionately entering retail/food service. If the paper can show that the hiring decline falls on first-job or marginal entrants, the contribution becomes much bigger.
- **Mechanism via internal labor substitution:** The “access to hours” provision is potentially central. If incumbents get more hours before firms hire new workers, that is a sharp mechanism linking job quality to reduced entry.
- **Comparison to other labor regulations:** Frame fair workweek laws as a new form of employment protection or labor-rationing mechanism in the service economy.
- **Welfare framing:** Even without a full welfare analysis, sharpen the idea that the law may improve conditions for those employed while reducing opportunities for those not yet employed.

As written, the contribution is solid but still feels like “another reduced-form labor regulation paper.” To become bigger, it needs to become a paper about how modern labor standards reshape labor market dynamism.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Before this paper, we know that service-sector workers often face unstable schedules, and that fair workweek laws are intended to improve predictability and worker well-being. We also know more generally that labor regulation can affect hiring and separation behavior, but we do not know whether scheduling mandates meaningfully alter labor market flows.

### Tension

The tension is strong and should be sharper: a policy designed to improve job quality for current workers may also reduce opportunities for prospective workers. That is a classic and important labor-market tradeoff. The paper has this idea, but it arrives too late and somewhat timidly.

### Resolution

The paper finds a pattern consistent with lower churn: separations fall, hiring falls, employment rises modestly, and earnings do not move. The interpretive resolution is that firms respond by stabilizing incumbent employment rather than expanding entry.

### Implications

The implication is not just “fair workweek laws matter.” It is that **nonwage labor standards may redistribute opportunities from outsiders to insiders.** That should change how economists and policymakers think about “good jobs” regulation: not only in terms of wages and employment levels, but also in terms of flows and access.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the pieces, but the arc is only **serviceable**, not strong. Right now it reads slightly like a collection of plausible results organized around a catchy label (“predictability premium”). The label helps, but it is not yet doing enough conceptual work.

### What story should it be telling?

The story should be:

1. Service jobs rely on high turnover and flexible scheduling.
2. Fair workweek laws make that operating model more costly.
3. Firms adapt by holding on to current workers and slowing replacement hiring.
4. Therefore, labor standards aimed at stability may create an insider-outsider tradeoff.

That is the narrative spine. Everything else should serve it.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?

I would say:

> “Fair workweek laws seem to reduce both quits/separations and hiring in retail and food service, so they make jobs more stable for incumbents but may reduce access for new entrants.”

That is the interesting fact.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

Some would lean in—especially labor economists—because the insider-outsider angle is real. But many would reach for their phones if the pitch stays at “schedule predictability in six cities.” The broader framing is what determines the reaction.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Almost certainly:
- “Who are the outsiders who lose?”
- “Is this basically mild employment protection?”
- “Does total labor input fall, or are firms just reallocating hours to incumbents?”
- “Is the employment increase real headcount growth or a measurement artifact of flows?”

That follow-up is revealing: the paper’s most natural audience wants the distributional and conceptual interpretation, not just the reduced-form estimate.

### If findings are modest, are they still interesting?

Yes, but only if framed correctly. The point estimates are not huge, and the paper should not oversell magnitude. What makes them interesting is the **sign pattern across flows**. The package of lower hires + lower separations + slightly higher employment is more interesting than any one coefficient.

If the author keeps emphasizing “first estimates” and moderate standardized effects, the paper will feel small. If the author emphasizes the pattern as evidence on how labor standards alter churn, it becomes much more interesting.
