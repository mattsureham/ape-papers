## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper studies a simple but important policy question: when governments ban one kind of real-estate development, does investment shift into more productive local uses, or does overall investment contract? Using Switzerland’s 2012 ban on new second homes in high–second-home municipalities, the paper argues that investment did not reallocate into hotels, commercial space, or tourism infrastructure; instead, construction activity broadly fell, especially in alpine resort economies.

A busy economist should care because the paper is trying to speak to a live policy claim far beyond Switzerland: restrictions on one margin of development are often sold not just as curbing excess, but as redirecting resources toward better uses. If that logic is wrong, then a whole class of place-based housing and tourism policies may be misframed.

**Does the paper articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Mostly yes—better than many submissions. The abstract and opening do get to the key idea quickly: the paper is about reallocation versus freeze, not merely about whether the ban reduced residential construction. That is the right instinct.

But the introduction overreaches in a way that weakens the pitch. It too quickly jumps from “Swiss second-home ban” to “construction bans proliferating from Barcelona to Amsterdam to New York,” which feels rhetorically inflated and somewhat imprecise. It also states the mechanism and conclusion too categorically before the reader has been persuaded that the broader lesson travels beyond this very specific alpine setting. The first two paragraphs should be a bit more disciplined: start with the general economic question, then introduce the Swiss policy as a clean test, then preview the core finding.

### The pitch the paper should have

Governments often justify restrictions on one form of development by claiming that land, capital, and local capacity will be redirected toward more productive uses. This paper tests that claim using Switzerland’s 2012 second-home ban, which sharply restricted vacation-home construction in municipalities with high second-home shares. Rather than inducing reallocation into hotels, commercial structures, or tourism infrastructure, the ban appears to have reduced construction investment broadly—suggesting that in specialized local economies, restricting one major use can shrink the entire development ecosystem rather than redirect it.

That is the paper’s best story. It is sharper, more general, and more credible than the current “global proliferation” framing.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:**  
The paper’s contribution is to show that a major place-based ban on second-home construction reduced not only targeted residential development but also other local construction sectors, casting doubt on the common policy claim that such restrictions reallocate investment toward more productive uses.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest papers?
Only partially.

The paper distinguishes itself reasonably well from papers on:
- the effect of the Swiss initiative on prices,
- permit counts / residential construction,
- and the broad housing-supply-regulation literature.

But it does **not yet sharply differentiate itself** from adjacent papers that an economist would naturally compare it to:
1. work on land-use or housing-supply restrictions and substitution across margins,
2. work on local general equilibrium effects of place-based shocks,
3. work on investment spillovers / complementarity across sectors within local economies.

Right now the contribution can sound like: “another policy evaluation of the Swiss second-home ban, but with more disaggregated outcomes.” That is not enough for AER positioning. The paper needs to insist that the novelty is not a new outcome table; it is a challenge to a widely invoked economic logic—**partial-equilibrium reallocation in a context where local production complementarities may dominate.**

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a gap in the literature?
It is closer to a **world question**, which is good: “When governments ban one type of construction, does freed capital flow elsewhere?” That is much stronger than “the literature has not yet studied sectoral reallocation.” The introduction already leans in the right direction.

That said, the paper still lapses into literature-gap language (“neither paper examines whether capital reallocated across sectors”), and that is weaker than the world-facing version. It should stay anchored in the empirical policy question.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?
A reasonably smart economist could say:  
> “It studies the Swiss second-home ban and claims the policy reduced commercial and other construction too, rather than shifting investment into those sectors.”

That is decent. But they might also say:  
> “It’s a DiD on Swiss municipalities with sector-specific construction outcomes.”

That is a danger sign. The paper’s novelty is not yet memorable enough.

### What would make this contribution bigger?
Most importantly, the paper needs to move from “another outcome” to “a broader economic mechanism.” Specific ways to enlarge it:

1. **Make the comparison explicitly about reallocation versus complementarity.**  
   Right now “reallocation hypothesis” is treated as a policy slogan. The bigger contribution is that local construction sectors may be technologically and organizationally complementary. The paper should present itself as evidence on whether local factor reallocation is frictionless or whether sector-specific restrictions propagate through local capacity.

2. **Focus more on tourism-relevant sectors if the policy rhetoric is about productive tourism.**  
   “Commercial” is too broad. Hotels, restaurants, leisure facilities, and tourism-linked infrastructure are the conceptually decisive outcomes. If the data can isolate even imperfectly related categories, those should be front and center.

3. **Strengthen the geography/mechanism framing.**  
   The alpine versus non-alpine contrast is potentially the most interesting result in the paper. That is the place where “ecosystem” has real bite. At present it is presented as heterogeneity. It might actually be central.

4. **Dial back aggregate welfare claims unless the outcomes support them.**  
   “Destroyed investment entirely” is too strong. “Reduced local construction investment broadly” is enough. Overclaiming makes the contribution feel smaller because readers start poking holes in the rhetoric instead of absorbing the idea.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Before this paper, the natural policy argument is that banning second-home development in resort municipalities could redirect scarce land, capital, and construction effort toward more productive tourism uses.

### Tension
We know such bans reduce targeted residential activity and affect prices, but we do not know whether non-targeted local investment expands, stays flat, or also contracts. The central puzzle is whether the local economy reallocates resources efficiently or whether the restricted sector was in fact supporting the broader construction ecosystem.

### Resolution
The paper’s core finding is that commercial and other construction investment fell, often by more than residential investment, with the contraction concentrated in alpine municipalities.

### Implications
The implication is that sector-specific development restrictions may not induce benign reallocation in specialized local economies; instead, they may reduce overall local investment capacity. That matters for how economists think about housing restrictions, tourism regulation, and place-based industrial composition.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
Yes, **more than most submissions**, but the arc is not yet fully disciplined. The paper has a real story. That is its strength. However, it occasionally slips into “collection of results” mode:
- main DiD,
- continuous intensity,
- alpine heterogeneity,
- RDD,
- placebo,
- robustness.

That sequence starts to feel like an econometrics checklist rather than a narrative.

### What story should it be telling?
The paper should tell one story only:

**A policy intended to redirect development away from low-value second homes instead appears to have contracted the local construction ecosystem, especially in specialized alpine markets.**

Everything should serve that story:
- main result: no positive nonresidential response;
- decisive sectoral outcomes: tourism/commercial/leisure;
- key heterogeneity: alpine versus non-alpine;
- mechanism interpretation: complementarity and ecosystem contraction;
- broader implication: targeted supply restrictions can have general-equilibrium local effects.

The RDD currently muddies rather than advances the story. As presented, it produces non-results or opposite-signed suggestive estimates and then gets rationalized away. That may be fine econometrically, but narratively it distracts from the core. For editorial positioning, I would not feature it prominently.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?
“Switzerland banned new second homes in resort municipalities, and commercial construction fell even more than residential construction.”

That is a good lead fact. It is surprising.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
They would **lean in initially**, because the result cuts against the obvious substitution story. The idea that restricting one use can depress ostensibly unrelated local investment is interesting.

But the next reaction would be immediate skepticism / curiosity:
- Is “commercial” really tourism infrastructure?
- Is this just alpine decline dressed up as a policy result?
- Is the paper about capital not reallocating, or about local demand and contractor capacity collapsing?

That is actually good news. Those are engaged questions. It means the paper has a hook.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
The main one:
**“What exactly is the mechanism—why didn’t hotels and tourism amenities expand?”**

A second:
**“Is this really about reallocation of capital, or about local complementarity / demand collapse?”**

The paper needs better answers to those questions in its framing, even if the final empirical mechanism evidence remains limited.

### If the findings are modest, is that a problem?
The findings are not null, but some are modest in levels and the paper may be overselling “investment collapse.” The better move is not to exaggerate the magnitude; it is to emphasize the **sign pattern**. For this paper, the intellectually interesting result is not that effects are huge. It is that the supposedly beneficiary sectors did not benefit.

That is a meaningful “so what” if framed properly.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

Bluntly: this is **not yet an AER story in current form**, but it has the outline of one.

### What is the gap?

#### 1. Framing problem
Yes. This is the biggest issue. The paper’s best idea is stronger than its current presentation. The real contribution is not “here is a ban that lowered commercial investment too.” It is:

- because local sectors can be complementary through shared capacity and ecosystem effects.

That is an AER-type conceptual framing if convincingly executed.

#### 2. Scope problem
Also yes. The outcome space is broad in one sense, but not yet deep in the most policy-relevant sense. If the policy rhetoric is about redirecting investment into productive tourism infrastructure, the paper needs those outcomes to be especially prominent. “Commercial” alone is too coarse.

#### 3. Novelty problem
Moderately. The setting is not new, and the empirical design is familiar. The paper’s route to novelty is the question and the pattern of sectoral responses, not the institutional setting. So the framing and mechanism need to carry a lot of weight.

#### 4. Ambition problem
Somewhat. The paper is competent and reasonably punchy, but still feels like a safe extension paper: take known policy shock, add sectoral data, document spillovers. To excite the top people in the field, it needs to stake out a more ambitious claim about how to think about local regulatory shocks.

### Single most impactful advice
**Reframe the paper around a general economic question—when sector-specific development restrictions generate reallocation versus ecosystem-wide contraction—and organize the evidence around that mechanism, with alpine heterogeneity and tourism-linked outcomes at the center.**

That is the one change that could most improve its strategic position. Right now the paper risks being read as “Swiss second-home ban, one more outcome table.” It needs to become “evidence that local investment is organized through complementarities, so targeted bans can contract rather than redirect economic activity.”
