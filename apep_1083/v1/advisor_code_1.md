**Summary**

This paper studies the Swiss 2012 Second Home Initiative to evaluate whether banning new second homes redirected investment toward commercial/tourism infrastructure or instead caused a general investment freeze. Using a municipality-level difference-in-differences design (with canton×year fixed effects) and continuous intensity measures, the author finds that treated municipalities experienced substantial declines in both residential (≈15%) and commercial (≈35%) investment, with no sector benefiting. The effects are concentrated in alpine municipalities, and an RDD at the 20% cutoff provides complementary (albeit imprecise) evidence consistent with the DiD results.

**Essential Points**

1. **Parallel Trends & Pre-trend Concerns**: The event-study pre-trend F-test is marginally significant even after canton×year fixed effects, suggesting residual differential trends. The paper currently defers to visual inspection, but does not provide a convincing argument that these deviations are innocuous. Please (i) report and discuss the magnitude of pre-trend coefficients relative to post-treatment effects, (ii) consider additional controls (e.g., time-varying municipal covariates or leads of the treatment intensity) to absorb remaining dynamics, and (iii) if possible, demonstrate that the DiD estimates are robust when re-weighting or re-matching treated and control municipalities to better align pre-trends (e.g., synthetic control or entropy balancing). Without these moves, the identification remains vulnerable to bias from differential growth patterns in alpine resorts.

2. **Treatment Definition Timing & Measurement Error**: Treatment status is assigned using the 2017 ARE inventory, several years after implementation, and the text acknowledges that some municipalities’ status may have changed (especially near the threshold). If post-treatment treatment status is used as though it were pre-determined, measurement error could bias the DiD toward zero or create heterogeneous bias (e.g., alpine municipalities below 20% in 2017 may still have been above in 2012). Please clarify the extent of such mismeasurement: how many municipalities shift categories between 2012 and 2017? Can the author reconstruct the 2012 designation (e.g., using earlier inventories or cabined data) or, failing that, show that results are insensitive to excluding municipalities whose treatment status is uncertain (e.g., those marked “under review” or status changes). This is crucial for the credibility of the identification.

3. **Mechanism & Alternative Explanations for Commercial Decline**: The “construction ecosystem” mechanism is plausible but under-explored. Could the commercial decline reflect demand-side shocks (e.g., the tourism slump after the vote, financial crisis dynamics, or zoning restrictions) rather than capacity constraints? Please provide additional evidence: for instance, do nearby (non-treated) municipalities with similar tourism profiles show different commercial trends, or do canton-level investment programs change after 2012? Alternatively, exploit the sectoral detail beyond “commercial” (e.g., subcategories such as hotels vs. office/retail) or use employment/construction firm-entry data to validate the ecosystem contraction story. Without stronger evidence, the finding could simply reflect a broader downturn in alpine tourism that coincided with the ban.

**Suggestions**

1. **Event Study Visualization and Diagnostics**: The paper describes the event-study but does not present the plots. Including a figure with confidence intervals (and a version with only pre-2012 data) would allow readers to judge the pre-trends directly. Consider augmenting the figure with local polynomial fits and showing how the inclusion/exclusion of canton×year FE affects the coefficients. Additionally, report the number of treated/control observations per year to illustrate balance over time.

2. **Placebo Outcomes and Spillovers**: The roads placebo is a good start. To strengthen the argument against spillovers and omitted factors, consider additional placebo outcomes (e.g., public non-residential construction, investment in non-tourism sectors, or nearby cantonal transfers). Similarly, test for heterogenous effects by distance to the nearest (non-treated) municipality with ample commercial construction. If spillovers exist, they could manifest in neighboring areas increasing investment, which would undermine the reallocation claim.

3. **Intensity Specification and Functional Form**: The continuous measure multiplies the second-home share by the post indicator. Please clarify the expected functional form: why is treatment intensity measured as the share (0–1) rather than a “gap above 20%”? Municipalities just above 20% face different constraints than those at 60%, but intensity measured by share conflates the two. Consider alternative specifications (e.g., (share−0.2)+ × Post) or spline regressions to capture the non-linear constraint effect. Similarly, the IHS transformation of investment may behave differently across sectors—report results using log(1+Y) vs. level and check whether outliers (e.g., Davos) drive the results.

4. **Heterogeneity by Municipality Size and Economic Structure**: The alpine/non-alpine split is intuitive, but treated municipalities also differ in size, median income, and tourism intensity. Provide additional heterogeneity checks (e.g., splitting by population size, share of tourism employment, proximity to ski areas) to show that the effect is not simply driven by a few large tourist hotspots. This also helps establish external validity: if only tiny alpine villages see the freeze, the policy implication for larger cities is weaker.

5. **RDD Power and Interpretation**: The RDD section reports insignificant (and oddly positive) estimates at the threshold. Since the DiD finds large negative effects, it would help to clarify whether the RDD is adequately powered and whether the apparent positive point estimate reflects small sample noise. Provide the bias-adjusted (Cattaneo et al.) confidence intervals, and explicitly state the size of the local average treatment effect implied by the DiD vs. what the RDD can reject. If possible, consider a “fuzzy” RDD that uses the 20% rule as an instrument for actual treatment (e.g., actual regulatory enforcement), which might address the measurement timing concern.

6. **Data Appendix Transparency**: Since the ARE inventory is only available from 2017, readers may worry about municipal mergers or coding inconsistencies. Please document (i) how many municipalities were dropped due to mergers; (ii) whether the matched sample overrepresents certain cantons; (iii) how municipal boundaries align between BFS and ARE; and (iv) whether the merger exclusions could bias results (e.g., if treated municipalities are more likely to merge). A table listing the cantonal composition of treated vs. control municipalities would help.

7. **Quantification of Aggregate Costs**: The abstract and discussion mention the CHF 3,400 per municipality-year loss in commercial investment but do not aggregate the macro implications. Provide back-of-the-envelope calculations (e.g., total CHF lost, share of municipal budgets, or employment implications) to contextualize the economic significance. This will help policymakers compare the “investment freeze” result to other costs (housing price increases, equity effects, etc.).

8. **Robustness to Alternative Control Groups**: Given the alpine/non-alpine differences, consider constructing a matched sample of control municipalities that are similar in observable pre-treatment trends (population, total investment, tourism share) but stay below the 20% threshold. This would increase confidence that the DiD is not conflating treatment with persistent geographic characteristics.

9. **Discussion of Policy Mechanisms Beyond Ecosystem**: The argument emphasizes supply-side capacity contraction but does not address demand-side behavior from developers, investors, or buyers. For instance, if expectations about future regulation reduced commercial demand and financing, that could also explain the decline. Incorporate evidence (or at least discussion) on credit conditions, lending to alpine municipalities, or developer announcements around 2012–2013 to strengthen the causal narrative.

10. **Clarify the Scope for Generalization**: The introduction states the finding undermines the reallocation justification for bans in Barcelona, Amsterdam, New York, etc. While provocative, the Swiss context (small alpine towns, direct democracy, unique tourism dynamics) differs from these cities. Temper the conclusion by acknowledging these contextual differences and by discussing the mechanisms that would make the result generalizable (e.g., in any market where construction relies on a tightly integrated ecosystem).

Overall, the paper addresses an important and timely question with rich data and a plausible identification strategy, but enhancing the diagnostics, clarifying treatment definition, and deepening the mechanism analysis will considerably strengthen the contribution.
