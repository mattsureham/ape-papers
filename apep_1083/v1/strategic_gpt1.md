## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple, policy-relevant question: when a government bans one kind of construction, does investment move into other local uses, or does it disappear? Using Switzerland’s 2012 second-home ban and municipality-level investment data by sector, the paper argues that the ban did not redirect resources into hotels or other productive tourism infrastructure; instead, it depressed construction broadly, especially in alpine municipalities.

A busy economist should care because the paper takes on a widely invoked but rarely tested policy logic: that selective housing or land-use restrictions can improve local economic composition by pushing capital toward “better” uses. If that logic is wrong, the welfare and political case for a whole class of urban and regional policies changes.

**Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Mostly yes. The paper does a better job than many submissions: it states the policy, the hypothesized mechanism, and the headline result quickly. But it overreaches in two ways that weaken credibility at the very moment it should be building reader trust: (i) it globalizes too fast (“central justification... from Barcelona to Amsterdam to New York”), and (ii) it declares the answer more strongly than the design seems ready to bear. The first two paragraphs should be a touch more disciplined and less slogan-forward.

**What the first two paragraphs should say instead:**

> Governments increasingly use targeted housing and construction restrictions to reshape local economies. The economic case for these policies often rests not just on reducing a disfavored activity, but on inducing reallocation: if second homes, short-term rentals, or luxury housing are restricted, land, capital, and developers may shift toward hotels, commercial space, or other locally productive investment.
>
> This paper studies that mechanism in the setting of Switzerland’s 2012 Second Home Initiative, which prohibited new second-home construction in municipalities above a statutory threshold. Using three decades of municipality-level construction investment data by sector, I ask whether the ban redirected investment toward commercial and tourism-related uses or instead reduced local investment more broadly. I find little evidence of reallocation; if anything, municipalities most exposed to the ban experienced declines across multiple construction sectors, with the contraction concentrated in alpine resort areas.

That version keeps the core insight, lowers the rhetorical temperature, and frames the paper around a big world question rather than a Swiss institutional curiosity.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution in one sentence:**  
The paper’s contribution is to test whether a major place-based housing construction ban causes cross-sector investment reallocation, and to show that in this setting the policy is associated with broad local investment contraction rather than compensating expansion in commercial construction.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?
Somewhat, but not sharply enough. The paper distinguishes itself from existing Swiss papers on prices and permit counts by saying “they study first-stage housing effects; I study sectoral reallocation.” That is a real distinction. But the introduction does not yet make crystal clear why this is a first-order conceptual advance rather than “same policy, new outcome.” Right now, the contribution sounds like an outcome extension.

To make it sharper, the paper needs to say:

- Existing work asks: **did the ban reduce second-home construction and raise prices?**
- This paper asks the more fundamental equilibrium question: **what happened to local investment composition after the ban?**
- That question matters because the standard defense of sectoral bans is not only that they stop one activity, but that they improve the allocation of real resources.

That is stronger than “no one has looked at sectoral data yet.”

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a gap in a literature?
It is partly framed as a world question, which is good, but it still slips into literature-gap mode too often (“neither paper examines whether capital reallocated across sectors”). The stronger frame is clearly the world question:

> When policymakers prohibit one type of development, do local economies adapt by reallocating investment, or do complementary local capabilities unravel?

That is much better than “the literature has not studied sectoral reallocation after this ban.”

### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what’s new?
Not quite reliably. A good reader would probably say:

> “It’s a DiD on the Swiss second-home initiative showing commercial construction fell too.”

That is not enough. The introduction needs to teach the reader the conceptual novelty: this is a paper about **whether selective development restrictions improve composition via reallocation or instead destroy local investment capacity because construction sectors are complementary.**

If the reader leaves with “another DiD paper about Swiss housing regulation,” the framing has failed.

### What would make this contribution bigger?
Several possibilities:

1. **Different framing:**  
   The biggest gain is not another regression but a reframing around **complementarities and local production ecosystems** rather than “reallocation mirage.” The interesting idea is that sector-specific restrictions can have negative general-equilibrium effects because the targeted activity is an anchor for shared local inputs.

2. **Different outcomes:**  
   The paper would be more important if it tracked not only investment by sector, but also the local economic objects that would reveal ecosystem contraction:
   - construction firm entry/exit,
   - employment in construction and tourism,
   - hotel capacity or openings,
   - business formation,
   - municipal tax base,
   - wages or local labor market adjustment.

   Right now the mechanism is plausible but mostly inferred.

3. **Different comparison:**  
   It would be stronger if the paper explicitly compared the Swiss setting to policies that are justified as compositional upgrades—e.g., short-term rental restrictions, tourist zoning, or anti-speculation policies. The paper hints at this, but currently the analogies feel a bit casual.

4. **Different mechanism articulation:**  
   “Capital didn’t reallocate” is weaker than “the targeted sector generated complementarities that supported the rest of the local construction market.” The latter is a larger and more exportable idea.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

### Which 3–5 papers are the closest neighbors?

- There is likely also a tourism / local development / land-use regulation literature the paper should engage more directly, even if not yet cited here.

### How should the paper position itself relative to those neighbors?
**Build on the Swiss policy papers, don’t attack them.**  
The right posture is: those papers established that the policy changed housing outcomes; this paper asks what happened to the broader local investment mix. That’s a natural and constructive extension.

**Do not oversell the uncertainty literature link.**  
The Bloom/Baker move currently feels somewhat opportunistic. This is not really a paper about aggregate uncertainty shocks. It is about local complementarities, place-based regulation, and sectoral spillovers. The “investment freeze” language is evocative, but if the paper leans too hard on macro uncertainty it may sound like it is borrowing importance from a literature it is not squarely speaking to.

### Is the paper currently positioned too narrowly or too broadly?
Interestingly, both.

- **Too narrowly** in the empirical sense: it can read like “the Swiss second-home paper with sectoral investment outcomes.”
- **Too broadly** in the rhetorical sense: the claims about Barcelona, Amsterdam, and New York overstate how directly this setting maps to global policy debates.

The sweet spot is: a paper in urban/public/regional economics with a Swiss natural experiment that speaks to a general question about **targeted development restrictions and local production complementarities.**

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?
It should likely engage more with:

- **Urban land-use regulation and zoning as industrial policy / composition policy**, not just housing supply restrictions.
- **Regional adjustment and local input complementarities**—papers on local multipliers, production networks, and place-based spillovers may offer a stronger conceptual home than uncertainty.
- **Tourism economics / resort-town development**—since the claimed mechanism concerns hotels, year-round activity, and resort economies.
- Possibly **misallocation / reallocation** work, if used carefully. The paper invokes reallocation repeatedly but doesn’t fully ground that term in the economics literature.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?
Not yet fully. The most impactful conversation is not “another paper on Swiss housing policy” and not really “policy uncertainty causes investment freezes.” It is:

> **Can targeted land-use restrictions improve local economic composition, or do they backfire because activities policymakers think are separable are actually technologically and commercially intertwined?**

That is the right conversation.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### What is the setup?
Policymakers often believe that restricting one kind of construction—especially speculative or low-social-value residential development—will redirect local resources toward more socially desirable uses like hotels, services, and productive tourism infrastructure.

### What is the tension?
That attractive intuition may be wrong if local construction sectors are complementary: the same contractors, suppliers, planners, and financing relationships support both residential and commercial projects. Then removing one activity may shrink local capacity rather than free it up.

### What is the resolution?
In the Swiss case, the paper finds little sign of positive reallocation into commercial or tourism-related construction. Instead, exposed municipalities—particularly alpine ones—see declines across multiple categories of construction investment.

### What are the implications?
The case for selective construction bans cannot rest on naive reallocation stories. Policymakers need to think about local complementarities, not just whether a targeted use is undesirable in isolation.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
It has the bones of one, yes. In fact, it has a better narrative than many technically competent papers. But the paper still sometimes reads like **results searching for a stronger conceptual frame**. The “reallocation mirage” title gives it a hook, but the real story is not merely “reallocation didn’t happen.” The real story is:

> **Banning a visible, politically unpopular activity can contract an entire local production ecosystem because the regulated and favored sectors share inputs and capabilities.**

That is the narrative the paper should own.

The paper’s current structure also interrupts the narrative by spending too much introductory real estate on empirical specification details and too little on the economic mechanism. An AER-caliber introduction should make the reader care about the phenomenon before it starts discussing fixed effects.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would you lead with at a dinner party of economists?
I would say:

> “Switzerland banned new second homes in resort municipalities to push development toward hotels and other productive uses. But in the places most exposed, commercial construction fell even more than residential construction.”

That is a genuinely good opening fact.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
They would lean in initially. The setup is intuitive and policy-relevant. The “commercial fell more than residential” contrast is strong. The risk is that interest fades if the paper cannot quickly answer the next question: **why?**

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Almost certainly:

> “Why would commercial investment fall if only second homes were banned?”

And then:
- Is it local contractor capacity?
- Financing?
- Resort demand collapsing?
- Anticipation or compositional change?
- Why only alpine places?

The paper has an answer—the ecosystem channel—but at present it is still more asserted than demonstrated. That is fine for a promising paper; it is not yet enough for a field-defining one.

### If the findings are modest: is the null itself interesting?
This is not a pure null paper, and that helps. The interesting result is not merely “no positive reallocation.” It is **negative spillovers to supposedly favored sectors.** That is stronger than a failed experiment. The paper does make this point, but it should emphasize that the economically relevant object is not the absence of a benefit; it is the presence of broader contraction.

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

### What would make it read better?

1. **Shorten the empirical strategy in the introduction.**  
   The introduction currently spends too much time on specification details, p-values, and the preferred FE structure. That is referee-report prose, not editorially effective prose. The introduction should foreground:
   - policy question,
   - mechanism,
   - headline facts,
   - why the result matters.

2. **Move a lot of design-defense material out of the main motivational arc.**  
   The long paragraph about canton-by-year fixed effects, placebo roads, and pre-trends belongs later. The introduction should not sound defensive before the reader is invested.

3. **Lead with the cross-sector comparison.**  
   The best fact in the paper is not “residential falls 15%.” Everyone expects that. The best fact is:
   - the sector meant to benefit falls more than the sector directly targeted.

   Put that front and center.

4. **Rethink the RDD section’s prominence.**  
   As written, the RDD muddies rather than clarifies the story. It produces weak or opposite-signed local estimates and then requires explanation. If the RDD is low-power and local to marginal municipalities, it should be deemphasized, shortened, or moved back unless the author can turn it into a substantive complement. Right now it distracts from the central contribution.

5. **The heterogeneity should arrive earlier conceptually.**  
   The alpine/non-alpine split is one of the most illuminating pieces because it points toward mechanism and scope. It helps explain when and where the ecosystem logic matters. That should be elevated in the narrative.

6. **Conclusion should do more than summarize.**  
   The current conclusion mostly restates findings. It should instead end with a broader economic lesson:
   - selective restrictions are often evaluated as if sectors are separable,
   - but in many local economies they are linked by common inputs,
   - so composition policy can reduce capacity rather than improve allocation.

7. **Delete or rethink some rhetorical flourishes.**  
   Phrases like “reallocation mirage” are fine in moderation, but repeated sloganizing can make the paper sound more certain than it should. AER papers can be vivid, but they also project control.

Also yes. The paper would feel more top-tier if it showed the mechanism more directly and broadened the consequence set. Yes. The paper is competent but slightly safe. It has a good headline result, but it does not yet build the larger economic argument with enough force or enough evidence. It needs to be less “here is an additional effect of the Swiss ban” and more “this case reveals a general mistake economists and policymakers make when they think about sector-specific restrictions.”

**Reframe the paper around local complementarities and ecosystem collapse—not the absence of reallocation—and organize every section around that bigger economic idea.**

That one change would improve the introduction, literature positioning, mechanism discussion, heterogeneity, and external relevance all at once.
