## 1. **Idea Fidelity**

The paper departs in a fundamental way from the original idea in the manifest. The manifest proposed an unusually strong design: exploit the **random assignment of quota licenses among applicants within county-year lotteries**, link winners to SunBiz and DBPR transfer/activation records, and use losers as the counterfactual to study whether the lottery creates entrepreneurs or simply transfers rents to flippers. That design would have directly answered the “who becomes an entrepreneur?” question and would have relied on actual randomization.

The submitted paper instead studies a different question: whether **county-level increases in quota-license supply**, inferred from population thresholds, affect county employment in NAICS 7224. That is not just a scaling-up of the original idea; it is a distinct empirical design with a much weaker identification strategy. The paper does not use winner/loser microdata, does not observe actual activation or transfers, and does not distinguish owner-operation from resale. Moreover, it uses **constructed treatment** from ACS-based threshold crossings rather than actual license allocations for almost all years, despite the manifest emphasizing that DBPR winner lists are available for many years. This means the paper misses the strongest data source and the strongest identification margin.

So: the paper does pursue the broad topic of Florida’s quota lottery and business creation, but it misses the key element that made the original idea compelling—**randomization at the applicant level using actual lottery outcomes**.

## 2. **Summary**

This paper studies whether additional quota liquor licenses in Florida increase county-level employment in drinking places. Using county-quarter QWI data and a county-level measure of new licenses derived from statutory population thresholds, the author reports a short-run positive association between new licenses and employment in NAICS 7224, but no effect of cumulative license stock.

The topic is interesting and potentially important, but the current paper’s identification strategy is substantially weaker than the institutional setting permits. As a result, the causal interpretation in the draft is not credible in its present form.

## 3. **Essential Points**

1. **The identification strategy is not persuasive as written.**  
   The treatment is driven by population growth crossing thresholds, and population growth is itself a direct determinant of bar employment. The paper acknowledges this concern, but the main estimate falls by roughly half and loses significance once log population is added. That is not a minor robustness issue; it suggests the baseline result is largely confounded. The restaurant placebo does not rescue the design, because bars and restaurants can respond differently to demographic change, tourism, income growth, neighborhood composition, or development patterns. More importantly, the identifying variation is not random: it is county growth. The paper should either (i) redesign around actual lottery randomization at the applicant level, or (ii) build a much more convincing threshold-based RD/RK or “close-to-threshold” design using the official population estimates that govern allocations.

2. **The treatment variable is measured with substantial error, and the validation exercise is too weak.**  
   The paper constructs “new licenses” from ACS 5-year population estimates and reports only a 0.64 correlation with actual allocations in one year and 17 matched counties. That is far from sufficient for a paper whose core regressor is this constructed series. Since actual DBPR winners/allocation data apparently exist for multiple years, the authors should use actual county-year allocations throughout, not a proxy. At present, it is hard to know whether the estimates reflect license supply, population trends, or noisy treatment coding. This issue is especially consequential for the paper’s headline interpretation of a “precise null” on cumulative licenses: attenuation from measurement error is an obvious alternative explanation.

3. **The empirical approach does not cleanly match the paper’s stated mechanisms and interpretations.**  
   The paper interprets the short-run effect as evidence that the quota constraint “binds,” and the long-run null as evidence that regulation does not determine equilibrium market size. But county-level employment in NAICS 7224 is a coarse outcome that cannot distinguish new establishment entry, incumbents expanding, seasonal hiring, substitution across counties, or delayed activation/transfer of licenses. Nor can it speak to the owner-operator versus flipper mechanism that motivates the paper. The event study is also not very informative: “first allocation” timing is endogenous to county growth, and staggered-treatment dynamics in this setting require more care than the current specification shows. The paper needs either a tighter interpretation or a design/outcome set that matches the mechanisms it claims to test.

## 4. **Suggestions**

My overall recommendation is to substantially rethink the empirical design around the institution’s strongest source of exogenous variation: the lottery itself. The current county-level panel can still play a supporting role, but it should not be the paper’s main causal engine unless the threshold design is rebuilt much more carefully.

First, I strongly encourage the authors to return to the original micro-level design. The paper repeatedly invokes the random drawing, but the regressions do not actually use that randomization. If the winner PDFs are available over a long span—as the manifest suggests—then the natural core design is applicant-level: compare winners and losers within county-year lotteries. This would immediately resolve the main identification problem. You could then study outcomes that are much closer to the motivating question: whether winners (a) activate a license, (b) transfer it quickly, (c) form a business entity, (d) survive as an operating business, and perhaps (e) generate subsequent employment if matched to establishments. Even reduced-form outcomes like SunBiz formations and DBPR transfer records would be far more informative than county-level NAICS employment. If the applicant universe is unavailable, a paper based only on winners is much less compelling; in that case, I would be explicit that the county-level analysis is a fallback design and far from the ideal.

Second, if the authors wish to keep the county-level question—“does expanding quota-license supply create jobs?”—then they need to use **actual county-year allocations**, not imputed allocations from ACS 5-year estimates. This is probably the single most actionable improvement. The annual winner lists should let the authors count actual licenses by county and year. Even a simple county-year panel based on observed allocations would be a large improvement over the current treatment measure. It would also allow the authors to assess first stage quality directly: how closely do official population increments map into actual allocations, timing, and activation? Right now, the reader cannot tell whether the treatment is truly observed policy variation or a noisy proxy.

Third, if the paper continues to leverage the population rule, the right design is not a generic TWFE panel but something closer to a **threshold-crossing design**. The institutional rule is one license per 7,500 residents. That suggests a regression-discontinuity-style or local comparison framework using counties close to the next threshold, ideally with the official population estimates used by DBPR. For example, compare county-years just above versus just below a threshold, controlling flexibly for population relative to the cutoff. At minimum, the paper should show the distribution of counties around thresholds, the frequency of crossing events, and whether pre-determined county characteristics evolve smoothly around the cutoff. The current specification assumes that, conditional on county and quarter FE, threshold timing is as good as random. That is not credible.

Relatedly, if the threshold design is pursued, the population measure must be the correct administrative one. ACS 5-year estimates are smoothed and backward-looking; they are poorly suited to pinpoint threshold crossings. The administrative mechanism seems to rely on official population estimates, not ACS. Using the wrong population series both weakens treatment measurement and muddies the interpretation of “crossing.”

Fourth, the placebo evidence needs to be handled more carefully. There appears to be a serious inconsistency in Table 2 and the text: the text says restaurant employment does not respond, but column 1 reports a positive and statistically significant coefficient of 0.0185***. That discrepancy must be fixed immediately. More broadly, I would avoid overstating the value of the restaurant placebo. Restaurants are not a clean control for county demand shocks if bars and restaurants serve different consumers and respond differently to tourism, nightlife demand, gentrification, age composition, or local development. A more convincing approach would be a stacked county-by-sector panel with transparent sector interactions, plus tests on several comparison sectors—not just one.

Fifth, the event-study design should be revisited. “First license allocation” is not a clean treatment onset in this setting. Counties receive treatment multiple times, treatment intensity varies, and the first allocation is mechanically related to county growth trajectories. Moreover, the monotonic post-treatment increase sits awkwardly beside the claim that cumulative effects are zero. That tension may simply be a symptom of dynamic misspecification. If treatment is staggered and repeated, I would recommend either a modern event-study estimator suited for staggered adoption with heterogeneous effects or, better, a direct dynamic distributed-lag model around actual county-year license additions. In any case, graphical presentation is essential; a table of coefficients is not enough.

Sixth, the paper should sharpen the interpretation of magnitudes. “Each new quota license increases county employment by 25 jobs” sounds surprisingly large, especially given that one license corresponds to one establishment-level regulatory asset and many establishments are modest in size. It would help to benchmark this against the average employment per drinking-place establishment in QCEW or County Business Patterns, or against the implied number of bars created. If one license corresponds to a 25-worker increase in a county, is that plausible as a new entrant effect? Or does it suggest broader equilibrium reallocation within the county? Without such calibration, the economic interpretation feels loose.

Seventh, I would significantly temper the long-run claims. A statistically small coefficient on cumulative licenses does not, in the current design, establish that regulation does not matter for equilibrium market size. It may instead reflect treatment mismeasurement, slow activation, secondary-market transfers across periods, noisy county-level outcomes, anticipation, or offsetting reallocation across incumbent and entrant firms. The “precise null” language is too strong given the data limitations. A more appropriate framing would be that the county-level reduced-form evidence does not show robust persistent employment gains.

Eighth, the discussion of mechanisms should be tied to observable outcomes wherever possible. The paper currently speculates about flipping, queue acceleration, and business failure, but none of these are measured. Even if the authors cannot implement the full winner/loser micro design, they may still be able to enrich the county-year panel with administrative outcomes: number of quota licenses activated, transferred, or newly issued establishments by county-year; perhaps even establishment counts from County Business Patterns or the QCEW. These would be much closer to entry than employment alone and would help distinguish extensive-margin from intensive-margin responses.

Ninth, the paper needs a more transparent data section. I would like to see: exactly which counties are missing in NAICS 7224 and when; whether suppression is related to treatment; how county-year treatment is mapped to quarter outcomes; the exact timing of the annual drawing relative to quarterly employment measurement; whether the treatment should affect only later quarters of the year; and how counties with multiple new licenses are coded. A timing-sensitive specification—e.g., treating the post-drawing quarters differently from pre-drawing quarters within the same year—could materially improve the design.

Finally, the current manuscript would benefit from a more disciplined contribution statement. The introduction currently gestures at entry regulation, occupational licensing, and entrepreneurship under capital constraints, but the present evidence is too indirect to contribute equally to all three. If the paper remains county-level, its most defensible contribution is narrower: descriptive/causal evidence on short-run sectoral employment responses to changes in quota-license supply. If the authors can implement the original winner/loser micro design, then the entrepreneurship and rent-transfer questions become much more compelling and publication-worthy.

In short, there is a promising paper here, but probably not yet in the form submitted. The institutional setting is excellent, and the original idea in the manifest is much stronger than the design currently used. I would encourage the authors to rebuild the paper around actual lottery outcomes and micro-level comparisons. If they do so, they could have a genuinely compelling contribution; if not, the current county-level threshold approach needs much stronger identification work before the causal claims can be sustained.
