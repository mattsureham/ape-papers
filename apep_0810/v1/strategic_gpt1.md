## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple, economically meaningful question: when the government relaxes a binding entry restriction in a local service market, does that actually create new economic activity, or merely reshuffle rents and timing? Using Florida’s population-based expansion of liquor-license quotas, the paper argues that new licenses increase bar-sector employment in the short run, but do not change long-run market size.

A busy economist should care because the paper is really about the incidence and real effects of entry regulation: are license quotas suppressing businesses, or are they mostly creating scarcity rents on a tradable regulatory asset?

### Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Not quite. The ingredients are there, but the introduction currently reads like an institutional setup plus empirical strategy. It takes too long to tell the reader what the big question is. The core idea is not “Florida has a quirky lottery”; it is “a famously valuable, tradable entry permit is expanded quasi-mechanically, letting us see whether relaxing entry barriers creates real activity or just reallocates rents.”

### What the first two paragraphs should say instead

Here is the pitch the paper should have:

> In many markets, governments restrict entry by rationing licenses, creating large private rents and potentially suppressing business formation. But whether such entry barriers actually reduce real economic activity—or instead mainly raise asset values and delay entry—remains surprisingly unclear.  
>  
> This paper studies that question using Florida’s liquor-license quota system, where licenses are scarce, valuable, and allocated as county population crosses statutory thresholds. I show that increases in license supply raise employment in drinking establishments in the short run, but do not appear to change long-run employment levels. The broader implication is that some entry restrictions meaningfully affect the timing of market activity without determining long-run market size.

That is the AER-relevant framing. The current version starts too close to the institutional weeds.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### Contribution in one sentence

The paper’s contribution is to show that relaxing a highly salient local entry restriction—Florida’s quota liquor-license cap—appears to increase bar-sector employment in the short run but not in the long run, suggesting that some entry barriers distort timing and rents more than equilibrium market size.

4. **Entrepreneurship and capital constraints**  
   - Evans and Jovanovic; Hurst and Lusardi

The paper gestures toward all four, but it has not decided which conversation is primary. That weakens the contribution.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a gap in a literature?

It is mixed, and it should be much more firmly framed as a question about the world.

The stronger world question is:

**When regulators expand the supply of scarce entry rights, do markets grow, or do rents just get capitalized and traded?**

That is much better than:

**There is little causal evidence on entry regulation, so I provide one more case.**

AER papers lead with the first kind of question.

### Could a smart economist explain what is new after reading the introduction?

Not cleanly. Right now they might say:

That is competent, but not memorable.

What you want them to say is:

> “It uses Florida’s liquor-license quota expansions to show that relaxing entry barriers creates short-run activity but not long-run market growth—so the regulation seems to matter more for timing and rents than for equilibrium quantity.”

That is a much more distinct takeaway.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Several possibilities:

- **Different outcome variable:** establishment counts, entry/exit, new firm formation, or ownership transitions would be much more direct than employment alone. “Does expanding quota supply create bars?” is the title question; employment is only a proxy.
- **Mechanism:** can the paper distinguish new entrant activation from resale to incumbents, chain expansion, or conversion of existing restaurants into full-liquor establishments? The secondary market is the whole economic point here; currently it is mostly discussed rather than analyzed.
- **Different comparison:** comparing bars to restaurants is sensible, but comparing counties near threshold crossings or using establishment-type composition could sharpen the economic interpretation.
- **Different framing:** the paper should be framed less as “liquor licensing” and more as “tradable entry rights.” That opens the door to broader implications for medallions, cannabis licenses, zoning-created scarcity, spectrum, and other administratively rationed permits.

The biggest upgrade would be: **show actual business creation, not just sector employment.** That would make the title finally match the evidence.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Governments often limit market entry through scarce licenses. In some cases these permits become extremely valuable, implying large entry rents. But it is not obvious whether those rents reflect suppressed output, delayed entry, or simply transfers among operators.

### Tension

A million-dollar liquor license looks like a major distortion. Yet because licenses are tradable and markets adapt, expanding supply may not actually increase long-run business activity. So what are these quotas doing in equilibrium: restricting the market, or just changing who gets to participate and when?

### Resolution

The paper finds a short-run increase in bar-sector employment following new license allocations, including a stronger effect for larger allocations, but little evidence that the cumulative stock of licenses raises long-run employment levels.

### Implications

The implication is potentially important: some entry regulations may impose real short-run costs and generate large rents without materially changing long-run market size. If true, that changes how we think about deregulation, incidence, deadweight loss, and the political economy of licensing.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the raw materials, but not a disciplined arc. At present it feels like a collection of estimates organized around an institution, not a paper relentlessly driving toward a single conceptual claim.

The story it **should** be telling is:

1. These licenses are valuable because entry is restricted.
2. If the restriction truly constrains output, expanding supply should create real activity.
3. If instead the main effect is rent capitalization and timing, long-run market size may barely move.
4. Florida lets us test which of these two worlds better describes reality.

That is a clean setup-tension-resolution structure. The current draft wanders into side conversations—occupational licensing, entrepreneurship lotteries, reduced-form identification—which dilute the story.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at dinner?

“In Florida, liquor licenses can sell for over a million dollars—but when the state expands license supply, bar employment rises only temporarily and long-run employment doesn’t move much.”

That is a good opening fact. Economists will understand immediately why it matters.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

They would lean in at first, because the institution is vivid and the result is mildly surprising. A million-dollar regulatory asset with no long-run quantity effect is interesting.

But the follow-up risk is serious: after the hook, people will ask whether the paper really shows business creation, or just temporary sectoral labor adjustment. If the answer is “employment only,” enthusiasm may cool.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Almost certainly:

**“So do the new licenses create new bars, or do they just get traded around and temporarily reshuffle activity?”**

That is the core follow-up question, and right now the paper cannot answer it. That is the main strategic limitation.

### If the findings are modest or null, is that still interesting?

Yes—**if** the paper leans into the null properly. The long-run null is not embarrassing; it is the point. But the paper must make the case that this null changes how we think about entry regulation.

Right now it says “maybe no long-run effect, maybe measurement error.” That is appropriately cautious, but strategically weak. If the paper’s central claim is the difference between short-run and long-run effects, the long-run result cannot be treated as an afterthought.

The paper needs to make the null economically interpretable:
- either scarce permits constrain timing more than steady-state quantity;
- or the private price of permits largely reflects rents and transfers rather than foregone output.

That is interesting. A null framed as “I couldn’t detect much” is not.

Yes, significantly. Yes. The title asks whether license expansion “creates businesses,” but the paper measures employment. For an AER audience, that mismatch is costly. The paper needs either:

**Reframe the paper around the economics of scarce, tradable entry rights—timing versus long-run market size—and either add direct evidence on establishment creation/transfer or stop claiming to answer whether licenses “create businesses.”**

That is the fork in the road. Right now the paper wants the conceptual upside of a business-creation paper without the corresponding evidence.
