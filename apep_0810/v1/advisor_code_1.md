**Idea Fidelity**

The paper stays largely within the scope of the original idea manifest: it studies Florida’s quota liquor license lottery as a regulatory natural experiment and uses county-level employment outcomes in drinking places to trace the short-run impact of license supply shocks. However, the execution departs from parts of the manifest that advertised linking lottery winners to corporate filings and transfer records to distinguish entrepreneurs from flippers. Instead, the paper constructs treatment from the statutory 7,500-person population thresholds and never exploits the denied–won comparison that the manifest envisaged, nor does it use the actual lottery winner and loser data beyond validating the computed treatment for a single year. That choice limits the paper’s ability to speak to the “who becomes an entrepreneur?” question at the individual level, which was a signature feature of the original proposal.

**Summary**

This paper exploits Florida’s population-threshold-driven quota liquor license system to estimate whether exogenously timed supply expansions affect drinking-place employment. Using county-quarter QWI data from 2012–2023, the author finds that each assignment of a new quota license increases drinking-place employment by about 4.8 percent in the allocation year, with a larger dose-response when counties receive multiple licenses. However, cumulative license stock has no detectable long-run effect, suggesting short-lived binding constraints. Placebo tests (restaurants) and event studies support the interpretation that the short-run effect is specific to the regulated sector.

**Essential Points**

1. **Treatment measurement and identification:** The treatment is constructed mechanically from ACS population estimates and statutory thresholds rather than the actual lottery outcomes the manifest highlights. The only validation is a 0.64 correlation for one year, raising concerns that measurement error is substantial and unknown. Since this treatment is the only source of variation, the paper must document in much greater detail how close the computed threshold-based treatment is to the real allocations in every year, or—ideally—should rely entirely on the administrative drawing results (which exist for multiple years) to ensure that the “random” variation the paper exploits is not contaminated by measurement error or endogenous forecasting of population thresholds.

2. **Endogeneity of population growth:** The identifying assumption is that crossing the 7,500-person threshold is as good as random conditional on county and time fixed effects. However, faster-growing counties are both more likely to receive licenses and to have rising demand for drinking places, so log employment and log population are mechanically correlated. The brief inclusion of log population as a control (Table 2, column 2) attenuates the flow effect to statistical insignificance, indicating this is a serious concern. The current placebo (restaurants) and dose-response regressions do not fully rule out that the observed effect is driven by demand shocks correlated with the computed treatment. A more credible strategy would exploit the discrete jumps in licenses at the thresholds (e.g., a regression discontinuity in population) or instrument license receipt with threshold indicators that are arguably exogenous conditional on smooth population trends. Without one of these approaches, the causal interpretation is fragile.

3. **Long-run null and treatment dynamics:** The interpretation that the long-run effect is zero relies heavily on the cumulative specification, yet this regression is also vulnerable to the same measurement error and omitted variable concerns as the flow specification. Moreover, the event-study focuses on the first-time license allocation in each county, which is an atypical treatment event and does not capture repeated entrants. Without clarifying what variation identifies the cumulative estimate, the null may simply reflect attenuation bias, especially given the acknowledged differential between ACS and DABT population counts. The authors should either show that the cumulative treatment is free of such bias (e.g., via external validation) or qualify the interpretation.

**Suggestions**

1. **Leverage the actual lottery data.** The idea manifest emphasizes using the quiz winners and losers directly. The paper should either obtain (or digitize) the DABT winner lists for every year they exist (the manifest notes 44 PDF lists from 2015–2024) and construct the treatment from those records, or at least demonstrate that the population-based treatment variable closely matches the true allocations throughout. A time series or scatter plot comparing computed versus actual licenses would help readers gauge the extent of measurement error. If winners can be joined to corporate filings, even better—it would allow disentangling activation versus flipping, making the entrepreneurship claim more credible.

2. **Strengthen identification.** Rather than relying solely on county fixed effects and trending controls, consider exploiting the discrete nature of the statutory rule. For example, estimate a regression discontinuity comparing counties just below and just above the 7,500-resident multiples, or implement a local projection approach that instruments new licenses with an indicator for crossing the next threshold (or the number of thresholds crossed) while controlling flexibly for population. That would mitigate bias from smooth population-related demand shocks. Alternatively, if the lottery assignment is genuine and random conditional on being in the pool, one could use the share of entrants who win (or the timing of drawing) as an instrument for employment, provided there is data on winners per county-year.

3. **Expand outcome and mechanism analysis.** The paper is currently limited to aggregate employment. To better speak to the entrepreneurship question, supply auxiliary outcomes: activation rates (e.g., number of drinking-place establishments created), license transfers (if licenses are flipped to incumbents), or wages/earnings. The manifest’s idea of linking to SunBiz could reveal whether new license recipients actually form businesses or just flip them. Even if this cannot be done perfectly, probing secondary-market activity would help differentiate between temporary boosts and sustained entry.

4. **Clarify placebo and robustness specifications.** The restaurant placebo in Table 3 is a useful sanity check, but the caption says the coefficient is 0.0185 with ***; this contradicts the paper’s statement that the placebo is a null. If that coefficient corresponds to the triple-difference specification instead, please clean up the table and text. Beyond that, use additional placebo sectors that are similar but unaffected by quota licenses (e.g., non-drinking nightlife or retail). Also, when showing dose-response (columns 5), control for population growth to ensure differential license counts are not simply proxies for faster-growing counties.

5. **Provide more detail on the event-study sample.** The event-study focuses on the first license allocation and bins endpoints, but it is unclear how many counties contribute to each post-treatment year. A panel with sample sizes and perhaps a figure showing the number of treated counties over time would help readers understand the precision loss. Additionally, an analogous event study for repeated treatment events would clarify whether the observed logarithmic growth is driven by a few large counties or the entire sample.

6. **Address potential spillovers and broader outcomes.** If license allocations lead to new drinking places, there may be spillovers on adjacent counties (through substitution) or on other NAICS sectors (competitive responses). A brief exploration of cross-border effects or placebo industries (e.g., personal care services) would strengthen the argument that the effect is specific to the targeted licenses. Similarly, examining whether the employment increase reflects more establishments or larger ones (e.g., using establishment counts if available) would improve the economic interpretation.

7. **Improve discussion of long-run null.** The current narrative suggests that the long-run market size is demand determined, but this relies on the cumulative estimate being unbiased. The paper would benefit from a theoretical or structural framing—perhaps comparing the short-run flow to a temporary rent model and explaining why long-run equilibrium adjustment erases the effect. Alternatively, estimating a simple dynamic panel (e.g., including lagged employment) could help gauge persistence beyond one year.

8. **Document data limitations transparently.** The paper notes that QWI suppresses small counties and that the treatment is constructed rather than observed. Include an appendix table listing which counties are excluded, and potentially show that these omissions do not bias the estimates (e.g., by replicating the flow regressions on the subset of counties with complete data). Also, discuss whether the ACS population estimates used are lagged or forecasted, since the treatment hinges on timely population data.

Overall, the paper tackles an interesting regulatory natural experiment with novel data. Strengthening the treatment construction, addressing the population-growth confounding more directly, and supplementing the aggregate employment evidence with finer-grained outcomes would substantially enhance credibility and policy relevance.
