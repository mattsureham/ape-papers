**Summary**

This paper proposes a novel identification strategy in the pollution–education literature by using Gaussian plume dispersion predictions as a source of exogenous variation in industrial pollution exposure at schools. Using school-level proficiency rates and year-to-year changes in wind direction, the author finds that a one-standard-deviation increase in plume-predicted exposure reduces standardized math proficiency by roughly 0.22 standard deviations, with effects concentrated beyond the plume “touchdown distance.” The paper claims this structural physics approach isolates pollution impacts from economic sorting and offers new policy-relevant insights about the geography of exposure.

**Essential Points**

1. **Causal Interpretation of the Plume Variable:** The key assumption is that plume-predicted exposure—constructed with uniform emission rates, a constant 75-meter stack, and yearly wind roses—is exogenous after school and year fixed effects. But the paper lacks direct evidence that the only source of variation is wind direction rather than time-varying local shocks (e.g., economic conditions, temperature/precipitation) that correlate with both wind and test scores. Without controls or tests that isolate the meteorological component from other contemporaneous shocks, it is hard to know whether the identifying variation is indeed as-if random. Please provide more systematic evidence (through balance tests, alternative weather controls, or placebo outcomes) showing that the within-school, across-year variation in the plume measure is uncorrelated with other determinants of achievement.

2. **Connection to Actual Pollution Exposure:** The reduced-form coefficient is interpreted as the effect of “predicted exposure,” not measured ambient pollution. While the author notes that uniform emissions create attenuation, the paper never connects the plume instrument to actual pollution levels (e.g., monitored SO₂/NOₓ) in the main sample; the sparse 2SLS exercise is underpowered. To assess external validity and policy relevance, the paper needs a clearer mapping from the physics-based measure to real pollution. At minimum, show the relationship between predicted exposures and monitored concentrations in the overlapping subsample (report first-stage strength with clear diagnostics) and, if possible, provide bounds or sensitivity analysis that translate the intent-to-treat effect into an effect per unit of actual pollution.

3. **Temporal Variation and Measurement of Exposure:** The paper relies on only three outcome years (2013, 2015, 2017) and constructs wind roses using data from 2013, 2015, and 2017 (with an extra 2010 snapshot). This sparse temporal variation raises concerns that the identifying variation (changes in wind direction probabilities) may be too coarse to credibly isolate shocks to pollution exposure. Additionally, matching wind data only every other year may blur the link between school-year and wind-year. Please clarify how wind data align with the school years, whether the wind variation is substantial relative to other sources of noise, and whether using interpolated or annualized meteorology would alter the results.

**Suggestions**

- **Strengthen the Exclusion Analysis:** To bolster the claim that the plume measure is exogenous, incorporate additional controls for contemporaneous weather (temperature, precipitation, humidity) and economic shocks (county unemployment, school spending changes) and show that the coefficient on predicted exposure is robust. Alternatively, include interactions of school fixed effects with linear time trends or local economic indicators to demonstrate that the effect is not driven by differential trends.

- **Report First-Stage Evidence Explicitly:** Even if the preferred specification is reduced-form, the paper should present the first stage of “plume exposure → monitored pollution” within the main text or appendix, including an F-statistic and sample size. This would clarify how much of the variation in actual ambient concentrations the plume model captures and help readers assess whether the proposed mechanism is quantitatively meaningful. If the first stage is weak, discuss how that shapes the interpretation of the reduced-form coefficient and whether it still bounds the causal effect.

- **Explore the Role of Emission Intensity:** Since $Q$ is set to 1 for every facility, the exposure measure is insensitive to differences in emission rates. Consider leveraging at least some facility-level emissions data (e.g., from the CAMPD or TRI data you already plan to use) to weight facilities by their true output, or, if these data are unavailable or very noisy, use facility fixed effects to test whether the coefficient changes when exploiting within-plant variation. Even if you cannot implement a full emissions-weighted model, a discussion in the text about how heterogeneity in emissions would affect the estimate would help clarify what the coefficient really captures.

- **Reconcile Time Alignment Issues:** The wind data appear to cover only 2013, 2015, 2017, but the school outcomes are also available for those years. Please spell out how the wind data are matched to the school years. For example, are winds measured in calendar year 2013 used for the 2012–2013 school year, and so on? If there are lags or averaging across years, state this explicitly. If feasible, augment the panel with additional years of wind data (2011, 2012, etc.) to increase variation and reduce concerns that identification rests on sparse differences.

- **Enhance Placebo and Robustness Checks:** Table 4 includes a low-exposure placebo with a very imprecise estimate; consider alternative falsifications with more power, such as using outcomes that should be unaffected by pollution but sensitive to weather (e.g., attendance rates) or using a “fake” dispersion model that rotates wind directions by 90 degrees. Another useful check would be to regress a non-education outcome that should not respond to local pollution (e.g., the share of students taking AP classes) on predicted exposure to ensure the instrument is not picking up other treatment trends.

- **Clarify the Interpretation of Distance Heterogeneity:** The large estimated effect beyond 25 km is intriguing. To lend further credibility to the physics story, try to link the heterogeneity to predicted touchdown distances computed for different facilities (varying by stack height and wind speed). For instance, produce figures showing how the effect varies with the plant-specific predicted touchdown distance rather than an arbitrary 25 km cutoff. This would turn the heterogeneity into a direct test of the Gaussian plume prediction and reinforce the methodological contribution.

- **Discuss Policy Implications with Caution:** The paper states that the Gaussian plume results suggest that regulatory focus should expand beyond fence-line communities. While this is plausible, emphasize that the estimated effect is for predicted exposure under uniform emissions, so exact magnitudes may differ when emissions vary. Additionally, mention that regulatory decisions also consider exposures from multiple pollutants and cumulative risk, which are not captured here.

- **Provide Additional Transparency on Data Construction:** The data appendix should include more detail on how wind roses are translated into plant–school exposure (e.g., how bearings are computed, how missing wind hours are handled). Readers would also benefit from a schematic or flowchart of the exposure-construction pipeline and a brief discussion of how measurement error (in geocodes, wind matching, etc.) might affect the estimates.

- **Consider Linking to Student-Level Data or Alternative Outcomes:** If possible, explore whether the effect shows up in other academic outcomes (e.g., reading proficiency, chronic absenteeism) or at more granular levels (district averages). Broadening the set of outcomes would help determine whether the effect is specific to math proficiency or reflects broader learning disruptions from pollution.

Overall, the paper proposes an innovative identification strategy. Strengthening the causal argument through richer tests of exogeneity, better alignment with actual pollution data, and clearer articulation of mechanisms will make the contribution much more convincing.
