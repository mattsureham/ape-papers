### 1. Idea Fidelity
The paper partially pursues the original idea manifest but deviates substantially in implementation, undermining key strengths. It retains the core Gaussian plume model as a physics-based instrument for pollution exposure at schools, using Iowa Mesonet ASOS meteorology, a 50km radius, school fixed effects, and wind-direction variation for within-school identification. However, it misses critical elements: (1) no EPA CAMPD CEMS hourly emissions or NEI SMOKE stack parameters—instead assumes uniform \(Q=1\) and default 75m stack height, making predicted exposure a pure geometry/meteorology index rather than emissions-weighted concentrations; (2) outcomes from coarse EdFacts proficiency rates (2013/2015/2017) instead of Stanford SEDA standardized scores (2009-2019 panel); (3) much smaller sample (6k schools, 17k obs vs. 15-25k); (4) no first-stage validation against AQS monitors or strong IV estimates—instead relies on reduced-form. These shortcuts yield a weaker, non-emissions-based instrument, shifting from causal IV for measured SO2/NOx to reduced-form on predicted geometry.

### 2. Summary
This paper constructs school-level predicted pollution exposure using a simplified Gaussian plume model (uniform emissions, default stack height, wind roses from ASOS) and estimates its reduced-form effect on EdFacts math proficiency rates in a school-year panel of facilities-nearby schools. The main result is a -0.218 SD effect (p<0.05, county-clustered SE) of a 1 SD increase in predicted exposure, with larger effects beyond 25km consistent with plume "touchdown" physics. It contributes a novel physics-motivated shifter but frames it as causal for industrial pollution amid data limitations.

### 3. Essential Points
1. **Implausibly large magnitudes without first-stage evidence**: The -0.218 SD effect on proficiency is enormous—comparable to the full Black-white school-level gap or 75% of STAR class-size effects—yet predicted exposure ignores emissions (\(Q=1\)) and uses a single stability class/default height, likely attenuating *upward* via noise rather than classical error. No first-stage plots/tables vs. AQS monitors (promised in manifest/Sullivan 2017) or F-stats; 2SLS is useless (790 obs, huge SE). Must show correlation with measured pollution (e.g., regress AQS SO2 on predictions by monitor-year) and bound LATE/WAT using heterogeneous first stages, or demote to reduced-form geometry effect.

2. **Coarse outcome and short panel erode economic meaning**: EdFacts proficiency % is binary/coarse (vs. SEDA continuous scores), compressing variance and biasing toward extremes; standardization within-sample doesn't fix ceiling/floor effects. Three uneven years (2013/15/17) yield thin within-school variation (median SD=0.32); state×year FE kill the result (-0.038, insignificant), confirming dependence on coarse state-level wind shifts. Switch to SEDA 2009+ for richer panel/variance, or reject if unfeasible—current result lacks clear "economically meaningful" cumulative exposure interpretation.

3. **Clustering invalidates inference**: County SEs (0.108, 495 clusters) shrink to 0.090 under state clustering (50 clusters), opposite typical pattern (coarser should widen SEs via less precise variance). Paper's "negative cross-correlations" rationale is post-hoc; wind shifts induce *positive* spatial serial correlation in exposures (downwind schools gain together). Use two-way clustering (school+county) or wild bootstrap; state-clustered p<0.05 hinges on few clusters (Angrist-Pischke risks). Fix or inference fails.

### 4. Suggestions
**Data and Measurement Improvements (Priority for Fidelity)**: Retrieve actual stack parameters/emissions from EPA NEI SMOKE (148MB flat files confirmed feasible) and CAMPD CEMS (hourly NOx/SO2 masses)—~50 lines R as manifest notes. Weight plumes by facility-year emissions \(Q_{jt}\), varying 2009-2019; this strengthens first stage (target F>30 per Sullivan 2017) and enables multi-pollutant (SO2/NOx separately). Geocode all ~100k NEI stacks (not just 35k ECHO majors) for cumulative exposure. Validate: (i) scatter predicted vs. AQS monitor readings (all facilities/pollutants/years); (ii) placebo >100km schools (null effect, as manifest); (iii) summer wind roses (weaker effects, kids absent). Match to full SEDA v5+ (math/ELA z-scores, 15k+ schools) via NCES IDs—boosts sample/power, aligns with manifest.

**Empirical Strategy Refinements**: Explicitly frame as reduced-form ITT on predicted exposure (fine, à la Olken 2009 ITM), but add 2SLS for measured county/zip pollution using school FE×pollutant (avoids sparse AQS subsample). Test exclusion via non-test outcomes: regress predicted exposure on teacher salaries/pupil spending/enrollment changes (manifest placebo; expect null). Control direct weather: add ASOS station×year FE or temps/precip (nonlinear wind specificity helps, but quantify). Heterogeneity table gold—expand: by stack height bins (if NEI data), stability classes (A-F via hourly Pasquill), or low/high-emission facilities. Distance: plot binned coeffs (0-10km, 10-25, 25-50km) with 95% CIs to visualize touchdown peak.

**Robustness Expansion**: Appendix balance table: Δpredicted exposure vs. ΔFRPL/Δenrollment/Δspending (reports nulls—tabulate). Event-study: leads/lags of exposure shocks around wind shifts. Spatial autocorrelation: Conley SEs (50-200km bands). Winsorize/alt radii good—add 25km-only sample (purer touchdown). Stack sensitivity strong—plot coeffs vs. H=30-200m grid. Longer panel: interpolate annual wind roses 2009-2019 (ASOS hourly available).

**Presentation and Interpretation**: Abstract/intro: tone down "causal effect of pollution" to "plume-predicted exposure" (avoid overclaim sans strong first stage). Magnitude context excellent (STAR/black-white)—add $/student cost: at $10k/pupil/yr, 0.218 SD ≈ $2k-5k loss (Reinhart 2023 conversions). Distance story compelling—figure plume C(x) curve overlaid with binned estimates. Policy: quantify "shadow zone" schools missed by fence-line rules (e.g., % US schools >25km exposed). Lit: cite APEP-adjacent (e.g., Deryugina wind IVs) and AERMOD validations. Trim LaTeX bloat (tabularray overkill; use booktabs). SDE table nice—extend to ELA/heterogeneities.
