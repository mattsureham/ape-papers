## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether a surprise windfall-style tax on renewable energy can chill the green transition. Using Norway’s unexpected 2022 announcement of a resource rent tax on onshore wind, it argues that wind output subsequently fell sharply relative to its prior growth path and to hydropower, suggesting that governments can deter clean-energy expansion by undermining regulatory credibility.

Why should a busy economist care? Because the broad question is important and timely: as governments look for revenue from the energy transition, can they tax “green rents” without discouraging green investment? That is a first-order policy question, and Norway is a potentially vivid test case.

Does the paper articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs? Not quite. The current opening jumps too quickly into “effectively halted” and “clean identification.” It leads with conclusion and design before making the broader economic question feel large. The first two paragraphs should frame this as a general problem of state capacity versus investment incentives in the green transition, with Norway as a sharp illustration rather than the whole point.

### The pitch the paper should have

Countries want renewables built fast, but they also increasingly want to tax the rents generated by high electricity prices. This creates a basic policy question: can governments extract revenue from renewable generators without damaging future clean-energy investment?

This paper studies that question using Norway’s surprise 2022 announcement of a resource rent tax on onshore wind. I show that after the announcement, Norwegian wind stopped growing relative to plausible benchmarks, consistent with a large chilling effect from policy instability in a sector that depends on long-lived, sunk investments. The broader lesson is that regulatory credibility may be a key input into the energy transition.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### Contribution in one sentence

The paper’s contribution is to use Norway’s surprise 2022 tax announcement to argue that unexpected taxation of renewable-energy rents can materially slow renewable-sector expansion by raising regulatory risk.

That is the contribution as I would state it. But the paper does not yet differentiate it cleanly enough from nearby work, and it is trying to make a bigger claim than its outcome really supports.

### Is the contribution clearly differentiated from the closest papers?

Only partially. The paper says it is the “first causal estimate” of chilling effects from a renewable windfall tax. That may be true in a narrow sense, but “first causal estimate” is not, by itself, an AER contribution. The reader immediately asks: first relative to what literatures?

There are at least two neighboring conversations:
1. policy uncertainty and irreversible investment,
2. energy/environmental policy and renewable deployment.

Right now the paper treats the resource-rent-tax angle as the novelty. That is too tax-instrument-specific. The larger contribution is about **how governments’ attempts to appropriate quasi-rents from clean infrastructure affect the pace of the transition**. That is the durable question.

### WORLD question or LITERATURE gap?

Mixed, but too often framed as a literature gap. The strongest version is a world question:

- When governments retrospectively or unexpectedly change the fiscal terms for clean energy, do they reduce subsequent renewable buildout?

That is much stronger than:

- There is little empirical work on resource rent taxes applied to wind.

The current paper often slips into the second framing.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the intro?

At present, they would probably say: “It’s a DiD paper showing Norway’s wind sector slowed after a surprise tax announcement.” That is not yet enough. They are less likely to say: “This paper changes how I think about the political economy of taxing infra-marginal rents in the green transition.”

That is the gap.

### What would make the contribution bigger?

Most importantly: **use outcomes that more directly measure investment and buildout, not just electricity production.**

Production is an awkward proxy for investment chilling. If the real claim is about investment, the paper needs to foreground one or more of:
- project starts,
- permitting applications,
- capacity additions,
- cancellations,
- financing terms,
- developer exits,
- turbine orders,
- project pipeline attrition.

Right now the central outcome is generation. That weakens the contribution strategically even if the estimates are large, because production is downstream of weather, installed base, maintenance, curtailment, and timing. For a paper about “investment chilling,” the reader wants the investment margin.

Other ways to make it bigger:
- Compare Norway not just to hydro but to a broader set of renewable technologies or neighboring countries’ project pipelines.
- Show heterogeneity by project exposure: pre-permit vs. under construction vs. operating plants.
- Lean into mechanism: was the response driven by uncertainty, retroactivity, precedent-setting, or expected future taxation?
- Reframe around **regulatory credibility as an input to decarbonization**, not around one unusual tax episode.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

### Closest neighbors

The paper is sitting between several literatures:

1. **Policy uncertainty and investment**
   - Bloom (2009)
   - Baker, Bloom, and Davis (2016)
   - Dixit and Pindyck (1994)

2. **Environmental/energy policy and directed technical change / transition policy**
   - Acemoglu et al. (2012)
   - Aghion et al. (2016)
   - Metcalf-type work on climate policy design

3. **Taxation of rents / natural resources**
   - Boadway and Keen / Boadway and Flatters-type resource rent taxation work
   - Daniel, Keen, and McPherson on resource taxation
   - Mintz and related work on rent taxes

4. **Political economy / credibility / hold-up in regulated infrastructure**
   - Helm (2003) is cited, but the paper should likely connect more broadly to regulatory commitment, expropriation risk, and hold-up in long-lived capital.

5. **Renewable deployment / energy investment under policy risk**
   - There is a large empirical literature on the effect of subsidies, contract design, renewable portfolio standards, feed-in tariffs, auction design, and policy reversals on renewable entry. The paper seems under-engaged with this.

### How should it position itself relative to those neighbors?

It should **build on and synthesize**, not attack.

The right positioning is:
- We know uncertainty depresses irreversible investment.
- We know climate policy design matters for clean-energy deployment.
- We know governments often seek to tax resource rents.
- What we do not know well is whether governments can apply rent-extraction logic to renewables without damaging future buildout.

That synthesis is stronger than claiming to inaugurate a tiny literature on “wind resource rent taxes.”

### Too narrow or too broad?

Currently: oddly both.
- Too narrow in instrument: “Norway’s wind resource rent tax.”
- Too broad in claim: “windfall taxes on renewable energy can generate substantial investment chilling effects during the green transition.”

The paper needs to occupy a middle ground:
**unexpected fiscal appropriation of renewable quasi-rents undermines clean-energy investment where projects rely on sunk capital and stable policy expectations.**

That is a real conversation.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?

It seems under-connected to:
- empirical work on renewable investment under subsidy reversals or policy instability,
- infrastructure hold-up / sovereign or regulatory risk,
- contract theory and incomplete contracts in regulated industries,
- political economy of ex post rent appropriation,
- energy-finance literature on cost of capital and policy credibility.

Those are all potentially richer than the narrow resource-tax literature.

### Is it having the right conversation?

Not yet. The most impactful conversation is not “is there one more quasi-experimental paper on an energy tax?” It is:
**What kinds of fiscal opportunism by governments can slow decarbonization, even when they target apparent rents rather than marginal returns?**

That is the conversation AER readers might care about.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

The world before this paper: governments need massive renewable investment to meet decarbonization goals, and at the same time high power prices create visible rents that governments may want to tax.

### Tension

The tension is conceptually strong: a tax instrument designed to capture rents efficiently in natural resources may not be innocuous when applied to renewables if it changes beliefs about future policy stability. Governments may think they are taxing rents; investors may think they are learning that policy promises are fragile.

### Resolution

The paper’s resolution is that Norway’s surprise wind tax announcement was followed by a large slowdown in wind relative to benchmarks, with effects appearing during the uncertainty period and persisting after implementation.

### Implications

The implication is that regulatory credibility is economically valuable and that taxing renewable rents may come at the cost of slower clean-energy buildout.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the ingredients of one, but not quite the arc. Right now it reads as:
- interesting event,
- empirical design,
- estimates,
- discussion.

What is missing is a sharper statement of the **underlying economic mechanism and why this case resolves a broader tension**. The story should not be “Norway did an unusual tax and here is the effect.” It should be:

1. Clean-energy investment requires stable policy over long horizons.
2. As renewables become profitable, governments will increasingly be tempted to appropriate quasi-rents.
3. This creates a fundamental tension in the transition.
4. Norway is a revealing case because the policy shock was sudden and salient.
5. The evidence suggests that the cost of such opportunism may be slower buildout.

That story is there in embryo, but currently the paper is still somewhat a collection of results looking for a larger claim.

Also, the paper overstates “investment chilling” while showing production. That creates narrative slippage. The story and the evidence are not perfectly aligned.

That is the clean dinner-party version.

### Would people lean in?

Yes, initially. The topic is timely, and Norway is a credible and surprising setting: a green-rich country seemingly taxing its own transition.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Immediately: **“But is this really investment, or just production?”**

That is the key vulnerability in the strategic positioning. The paper’s title, abstract, and rhetoric promise investment chilling; the main outcome is generation. A sophisticated economist will notice that immediately.

A second follow-up:
**“Is this about taxes per se, or about surprise and retroactivity?”**

That is actually a productive question, because the paper could become much more interesting if it is framed as evidence on surprise policy reversals and credibility rather than a generic anti-tax result.

### If findings are modest or null?

The findings are not modest. The issue is not insignificance; it is interpretability. The paper has a dramatic point estimate, but dramatic reduced-form effects can still feel strategically smaller if the outcome is one step removed from the stated mechanism.

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

### What would make it read better?

1. **Shorten the “identification” triumphalism in the introduction.**
   The intro currently announces “unusually clean identification” much too early. That is not the right selling point for an editorial audience. Lead with the economic question and the striking empirical fact.

2. **Move some specification detail later.**
   The third and fourth intro paragraphs get bogged down in why standard DiD in levels is wrong, why trends matter, and which specification is preferred. That belongs later. The introduction should say, more simply, that the paper compares wind to unaffected benchmarks and finds a large post-announcement break.

3. **Bring the mechanism to the front.**
   The most interesting result, strategically, is not the exact coefficient. It is the asymmetry:
   - effect begins during the uncertainty window,
   - persists or deepens after resolution,
   - final tax is lower than announced.
   
   That is the pattern that supports a credibility/uncertainty interpretation. It should appear earlier and more prominently.

4. **Demote some robustness discussion from the main text.**
   The paper spends a lot of space narrating specification variants. For a paper trying to claim broad relevance, that is too much econometric bookkeeping too soon.

5. **Elevate whatever direct evidence on investment exists.**
   The industry response is now anecdotal background. If there are data on permits, project pipeline, developer statements, capacity additions, those should be main-text facts, not side comments.

6. **Rewrite the conclusion to do more than summarize.**
   The current conclusion restates the findings competently, but it should end on the broader policy-design lesson: governments need credible ways to share upside from renewables without destabilizing investment expectations.

### Is the paper front-loaded with the good stuff?

Somewhat, but not optimally. The good stuff is:
- surprising policy event,
- economically important broader question,
- strong timing and persistence pattern.

The intro front-loads the estimate, but not the real reason the paper matters.

### Are important results buried?

Yes: the announcement-versus-enactment asymmetry is the most conceptually interesting result and should be more central. Right now it is present but not fully exploited as the core insight.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

This is not mainly a “fix the prose” problem. It is a **scope and framing problem**, with some novelty risk.

### What is the gap?

- **Framing problem:** The paper is better than its current “Norway wind tax DiD” packaging, but it has not yet elevated itself into a broader statement about regulatory credibility and clean-energy investment.
- **Scope problem:** The main outcome is too indirect for the central claim. AER-level papers need the outcome to match the concept more tightly.
- **Novelty problem:** Event-study evidence around a single policy change in one country and one sector can feel small unless it opens a broader conceptual door.
- **Ambition problem:** The paper is competent and sharp, but currently safe. It documents an episode rather than fully reframing how economists should think about taxing renewable rents.

### What would excite the top 10 people in this field?

One of two paths:

1. **Become the paper on policy credibility and renewable investment.**
   Show convincingly that surprise fiscal appropriation raises the cost of capital / kills the project pipeline / slows capacity growth, and connect this to a broader set of policy reversals across countries.

2. **Become the paper on taxing infra-marginal rents in renewables.**
   Show that the textbook logic of non-distortionary rent taxation breaks down in practice when policy changes are unanticipated, retroactive, or precedent-setting.

Either path requires stronger evidence on the investment margin and tighter mechanism.

### Single most impactful advice

**Replace “production” as the centerpiece with direct measures of renewable investment/buildout, and reframe the paper as evidence that surprise fiscal appropriation undermines regulatory credibility in the green transition.**

That one change would simultaneously solve the scope problem, sharpen the contribution, and make the paper much more legible to a top-journal audience.
