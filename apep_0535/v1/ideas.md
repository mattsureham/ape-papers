# Research Ideas

## Idea 1: Pump Prices and Perceptions: How State Gas Tax Hikes Shape Macroeconomic Beliefs
**Policy:** State gasoline tax increases, adopted by 34+ states between 2013-2024 with staggered timing. Magnitude ranges from 3 cents to 26+ cents per gallon. Mix of discrete legislative increases and variable-rate formula adoptions. States include California (+12c, 2017), Illinois (+19c, 2019), New Jersey (+22.6c, 2016), South Carolina (+12c phased, 2017), and many others.
**Outcome:** (1) Cooperative Election Study (CES) individual-level economic retrospection variable (`economy_retro`: whether the national economy has gotten better/same/worse), available 2006-2024 with 700K+ respondents and state identifiers. (2) Google Trends state-month search intensity for "inflation" and "recession" as high-frequency attention proxies. (3) EIA State Energy Data System annual state gasoline prices for first-stage validation.
**Identification:** Callaway-Sant'Anna staggered DiD exploiting the timing of permanent state gas tax increases. Treatment cohorts defined by the year each state first enacted a discrete increase (excluding automatic indexation adjustments). Never-treated states and not-yet-treated states serve as controls. Pre-trend validation using CES waves and Google Trends months before treatment. Within-state, within-year individual-level analysis controls for national sentiment shocks and state fixed characteristics.
**Why it's novel:** Jo & Klopack (JME 2025) study temporary gas tax holidays in only 5 states during the 2022 crisis. This paper exploits the full staggered panel of PERMANENT tax increases across 34+ states over a decade, providing far more statistical power, heterogeneous treatment timing, and the ability to study persistent belief effects (not just short-run responses to temporary holidays). Directly tests whether the most visible consumer price shapes broader macroeconomic beliefs — not just inflation expectations but overall economic sentiment — filling a gap between Coibion & Gorodnichenko (2015) correlational evidence and D'Acunto et al. (2021) grocery-price experimental evidence.
**Feasibility check:** Confirmed: (a) CES cumulative dataset freely downloadable from Harvard Dataverse (39.6 MB .rds, DOI 10.7910/DVN/II2DB6); (b) 34+ treated states exceeds 20-state threshold; (c) gas tax change dates available from Tax Foundation, NCSL, FHWA; (d) Google Trends accessible via gtrendsR R package; (e) EIA SEDS annual state gas prices for all 50 states via EIA API; (f) BLS/BEA state-level controls (unemployment, personal income) via FRED API. Not overstudied — no paper uses this full staggered variation for belief outcomes.

## Idea 2: Local Labor Market Shocks and National Economic Pessimism: A Belief Spillover
**Policy:** WARN Act mass layoff notifications — federally mandated 60-day advance notices of plant closures affecting 100+ workers. Thousands of WARN events occur annually across US states, providing high-frequency local labor market shocks with clear timing.
**Outcome:** CES individual-level economic retrospection (`economy_retro`) measuring perception of the national economy, aggregated to state-year level. Google Trends for "recession" searches as secondary outcome.
**Identification:** Event study around large WARN notification events at the state-year level. Compare CES respondents in states experiencing above-median WARN layoffs vs. below-median states, controlling for actual state unemployment rate changes. The key test: do local layoffs cause people to update beliefs about the NATIONAL economy disproportionately relative to the actual national impact?
**Why it's novel:** Tests the "local extrapolation bias" — whether personal/local economic exposure causes over-updating about aggregate conditions. Connects to Malmendier & Nagel (2016) experience-based learning and to the macro-behavioral literature on extrapolative expectations.
**Feasibility check:** WARN data is publicly available by state (state DOL websites), but compilation across 50 states is labor-intensive and format varies. CES confirmed available. Identification is weaker than Idea 1 (WARN events may be endogenous to state conditions). Would need strong controls for actual state-level economic conditions.

## Idea 3: When the News Goes Dark: Local Media Closures and Macroeconomic Belief Formation
**Policy:** Local newspaper closures — 2,500+ newspapers have closed since 2004, creating "news deserts" where residents lose access to local economic reporting. Data available from UNC Hussman School of Journalism.
**Outcome:** CES individual-level economic retrospection and Google Trends for economic search terms (state or county level). Could also examine whether news deserts increase partisan polarization of economic beliefs.
**Identification:** Event study / staggered DiD around newspaper closure dates. Compare counties that lost their newspaper vs. matched counties with surviving papers. Controls for pre-existing county characteristics, urbanization, demographic trends.
**Why it's novel:** Tests the information channel in macroeconomic belief formation — when people lose a source of economic news, do their macroeconomic perceptions become more biased, more partisan, or more volatile? Connects to Gentzkow, Shapiro & Sinkinson (2011) on media and political participation.
**Feasibility check:** UNC newspaper closure data is available. CES county_fips identifiers enable county-level matching. Main concern: newspaper closures are endogenous (papers close in economically declining areas), requiring careful identification strategy. Matching or synthetic control may be needed. Less clean than Idea 1's policy variation.
