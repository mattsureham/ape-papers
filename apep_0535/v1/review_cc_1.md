# Internal Review (Round 1)

## Reviewer 2 Assessment (Harsh)

**Overall:** The paper presents a well-identified null result using staggered state gas tax increases and the CES. The identification strategy is credible, the sample size is large, and the null is precisely estimated. The contribution is clear: this is the first comprehensive quasi-experimental test of whether the most salient consumer price causally shifts macroeconomic beliefs.

**Strengths:**
1. Clean identification with 29 treated states and flat pre-trends (p = 0.88)
2. Modern CS-DiD estimator with clear methodological contribution (TWFE bias demonstration)
3. Comprehensive heterogeneity analysis covering party, income, and age
4. Well-written with clear framing of the null as a contribution

**Weaknesses:**
1. CES measures retrospection, not forward-looking expectations — limits comparability with Binder & Makridis and Jo & Klopack
2. Annual survey frequency may miss short-run effects
3. Some treatment cohorts have late-year effective dates overlapping with CES survey window
4. Google Trends data unavailable, limiting attention mechanism analysis

**Verdict:** Minor Revision

## Editor Assessment (Constructive)

The paper makes a genuine contribution by providing the first large-scale quasi-experimental evidence on gas prices and macroeconomic beliefs. The null result is well-powered and the identification is credible. The methodological demonstration of TWFE bias adds value. The writing is strong.

**Recommendations:**
1. Strengthen the treatment timing discussion — address the late-year effective dates
2. Consider adding a power curve showing MDE as a function of effect size
3. The Related Literature section could be more concise

**Decision:** Minor Revision — proceed to external review.
