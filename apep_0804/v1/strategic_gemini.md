### 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
The paper examines whether state mandates requiring private insurers to cover expensive autism therapies (like ABA) allow mothers of children with autism to return to the workforce. Using a triple-difference design across 46 states, the author finds a "precise null": despite relieving a \$40,000–\$60,000 annual financial burden, these mandates have zero impact on maternal employment or hours worked.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this clearly. The first two paragraphs effectively bridge the gap between a specific health policy and the broader economic concept of the "caregiving tax."

### 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
The paper identifies that the massive labor supply gap for mothers of children with disabilities is a structural "time-use" problem rather than a "price" problem that can be solved via insurance liquidity.

*   **Differentiation:** It moves beyond the existing literature (which focuses on child outcomes or therapist wages) to address the *household* labor supply response.
*   **Question vs. Gap:** It frames the question about the **WORLD** (Why don't these mothers work?) rather than just a literature gap.
*   **Clarity:** A smart economist would immediately grasp the "precise null" takeaway. It is not "just another DiD" because the magnitude of the financial relief (the treatment "dose") is so large that a null result is theoretically provocative.
*   **Bigger Contribution:** To make it bigger, the author needs to prove the "Time Complementarity" mechanism. Currently, it’s a plausible explanation in the discussion. Showing that mandates actually *increase* time spent on therapy coordination (using ATUS data, perhaps) would turn this from a "policy evaluation" into a "fundamental paper on the nature of caregiving."

### 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
The paper sits at the intersection of Health (Mandates), Labor (Maternal Supply), and Gender (The "Child Penalty").

*   **Neighbors:** Chatterji et al. (2015) on mandates; Cidav et al. (2012) on the autism employment gap; Goldin (2014) on temporal flexibility.
*   **Strategy:** It should **synthesize and pivot**. It builds on the health literature but pivots to labor. It should more aggressively "attack" the implicit assumption in labor models that financial subsidies are substitutes for caregiving time.
*   **Missing Conversations:** The paper should speak more to the **"temporal flexibility"** literature. If therapy happens during 9-5 hours and requires parental transport, no amount of insurance money solves the coordination failure with a standard job.

### 4. NARRATIVE ARC
*   **Setup:** Mothers of autistic children face a massive employment gap and a \$60k/year caregiving bill.
*   **Tension:** Policy "solves" the financial bill. Standard theory predicts a labor supply increase. 
*   **Resolution:** Zero change.
*   **Implications:** The "tax" is time, not money. Policy should focus on "how" therapy is delivered (e.g., in schools) rather than "who" pays for it.

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong. It is a "result looking for a story," but the author has found a very good one.

### 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
*   **The Fact:** "We gave these families the equivalent of a \$50,000 annual voucher for child care, and not a single mother went back to work."
*   **Reaction:** Lean in. It’s counter-intuitive.
*   **Follow-up:** "Is it because the therapy is only 2 hours a week?" (Answer: No, it’s 25-40). "Is it because they can't find providers?" (This is the crucial GE question).

### 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
*   **Front-loading:** The "Dilution" and "ERISA" discussions on pages 7-8 are defensive. They are necessary but slightly dampen the excitement. I would move the "Mechanisms" discussion (Section 6) earlier to frame the null as a feature, not a bug.
*   **Appendix:** The standardized effect sizes (Table 6) are excellent for an AER audience; they should stay prominent.

### 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The gap between this and a "Top 5" is **Mechanism Evidence**. A precise null is valuable, but the AER usually requires you to show *why* the expected effect didn't happen.

**Single Most Impactful Advice:** Supplement the ACS data with **Time Use data (ATUS)** or **Provider Supply data**. If you can show that mandates increased therapy hours (using the same DDD) but those hours were "parent-intensive" (driving, meetings), you move from "we found nothing" to "we discovered a fundamental constraint on maternal labor."
