## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple, policy-relevant question: when states require private insurers to cover autism therapies, do mothers of affected children work more? Using staggered autism insurance mandates across states, the paper finds essentially no effect on maternal employment, hours, or labor-force participation, suggesting that the “caregiving tax” for mothers of children with autism is driven less by therapy costs than by the time-intensive nature of care itself.

A busy economist should care because this is not really a paper about autism insurance. It is a paper about whether relaxing a large financial constraint translates into labor-supply recovery when the underlying problem is caregiving time. That question speaks to labor supply, family economics, health insurance, and gender inequality.

### Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Reasonably, but not optimally. The current introduction starts with vivid facts and gets to the question quickly, which is good. But it still sounds somewhat like a niche policy evaluation of autism mandates, rather than a broader test of whether insurance can undo caregiving-induced labor-market penalties. The bigger paper is not “autism mandates affect maternal labor supply”; it is “large insurance expansions for child disability care do not restore maternal labor supply.”

### What the first two paragraphs should say instead

Something like:

> Mothers of children with severe developmental conditions work less, but it is unclear why. Is the maternal employment penalty mainly a financial constraint—families cannot afford professional care—or a time constraint that persists even when care is insured?  
>
> This paper studies that question using state autism insurance mandates, which shifted tens of thousands of dollars of therapy costs from families to insurers. If maternal labor supply responds strongly, that would suggest the caregiving tax is largely about money. If it does not, then the central constraint is the time-intensive coordination and management of care. I find precisely estimated null effects on employment, hours, and labor-force participation, implying that financial relief alone does not undo the maternal labor penalty associated with autism-related caregiving.

That framing immediately tells the reader the world question, the empirical lever, and why the null is substantively important.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### Contribution in one sentence

The paper shows that state autism insurance mandates—despite substantially reducing the price of autism therapy—do not increase maternal labor supply, implying that the maternal caregiving penalty in this setting is not primarily driven by treatment costs.

### Is this clearly differentiated from the closest papers?

Only partially. The paper does say prior autism-mandate work studied diagnosis, utilization, premiums, and therapists rather than maternal labor supply. That is a clean “new outcome” contribution. But “new outcome” is rarely enough for AER unless the outcome changes how we think about the underlying economic problem. The paper needs to lean harder into that: it is testing whether insurance coverage translates into parental labor-market responses, not merely adding one more reduced-form outcome to the autism-mandate literature.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a gap in a literature?

It mixes both, but still leans too much toward filling a literature gap. “No prior study has identified a causal link...” is fine, but not strong enough. The stronger version is: “We do not know whether the maternal employment penalty from child disability is fundamentally a price problem or a time problem.” That is a world question.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?

They could, but many would still summarize it as “a DiD/DDD paper on autism mandates and maternal employment.” That is the danger. The introduction presents the design clearly, but the conceptual novelty is not yet sharp enough.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Three possibilities:

1. **Reframe the estimand as a test of mechanism in family labor supply.**  
   The main value is distinguishing financial-relief channels from time-coordination channels. Right now that implication comes late and somewhat tentatively.

2. **Connect more directly to the broader question of whether insurance expansions affect family labor supply through caregiving substitution.**  
   A stronger comparison would be to settings where subsidized formal care *does* increase labor supply—childcare subsidies, eldercare supports, long-term care insurance, home health expansions—so the paper can say: autism therapy is different because it complements rather than substitutes for maternal time.

3. **Show a more policy-relevant margin if possible.**  
   If the paper can examine intensive-margin labor outcomes, occupational flexibility, part-time work, self-employment, or timing of work schedules, the null becomes more informative. A pure employment null can feel blunt; a broader pattern showing “mothers do not work more, but perhaps shift toward flexible work” would elevate the paper. Even without new identification, a different outcome menu could make the paper feel less narrow.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Mothers of children with autism work less. Intensive therapy is expensive, and autism insurance mandates dramatically reduce the out-of-pocket cost of accessing professional treatment.

### Tension

If the maternal employment penalty is largely due to families having to self-provide care because formal therapy is too expensive, then covering therapy should free mothers to work. But if care remains highly coordination-intensive even when insured, labor supply may not respond.

### Resolution

Maternal labor supply does not increase after mandate adoption. Employment, hours, and labor-force participation are all essentially flat.

### Implications

The caregiving tax in this context is not just a price problem. Insurance coverage may improve access to services without relaxing the time constraints that keep mothers out of work. Policies aimed at maternal employment may therefore need to target service delivery, respite, scheduling, school integration, or workplace flexibility rather than insurance coverage alone.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

Mostly yes, but it weakens itself by overloading the middle with design exposition and by not foregrounding the conceptual tension sharply enough. The paper has the ingredients of a strong story, but at moments it slips into “here is a policy, here is a DDD, here are nulls.” The story should be: **insurance relieves money but not time; this paper shows the difference matters.**

The discussion section actually contains the best narrative material—therapy may complement maternal time rather than substitute for it—but that should be seeded much earlier. Right now the story arrives after the results instead of organizing the results.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?

“States started covering autism therapy that can cost $40,000 to $60,000 a year, and mothers of affected children still didn’t work more.”

That is a good opening fact. It is intuitive, policy-relevant, and a bit surprising.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

Some would lean in, but only if you immediately add why this matters: “So the maternal employment penalty here seems to be about time-intensive caregiving, not just money.” Without that second line, many will hear “another null policy paper.”

### What follow-up question would they ask?

The first follow-up is obvious: **why not?**  
And then: **does this mean therapy complements maternal time rather than substituting for it?**

That is the right question. The paper should be designed narratively to answer it as far as the data permit. Right now it asks that question only in the discussion, and somewhat speculatively.

### Is the null itself interesting?

Yes, but only if sold correctly. A null in a setting with a large, salient price shock is informative because it discriminates among mechanisms. If therapy coverage worth tens of thousands of dollars does not move labor supply, then either policy exposure is very limited, the treated group is poorly measured, or the core constraint is not financial. That is useful knowledge.

The paper mostly makes this case, but it still sounds defensive in places—especially when it spends time reassuring the reader that dilution and ERISA “do not rescue” a positive effect. That is necessary, but editorially the stronger stance is: **the null is the result, not a failure to find one.**
