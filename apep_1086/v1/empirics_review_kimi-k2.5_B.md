 **Referee Report: "The Environmental Ratchet"**

**1. Idea Fidelity**

The paper deviates substantially from the original research manifest. The manifest proposed a study of "deregulation rebound and environmental inequity" that would examine whether "employment recovery differ[s] for minority workers while pollution rebounds more in minority neighborhoods." It explicitly committed to using TRI facility-level toxic releases with geocoding for "within-county spatial analysis," QWI manufacturing employment by race, and Census demographics to test for distributional externalities where "economic gains [are] broadly shared while environmental costs [are] spatially concentrated in disadvantaged communities."

The actual paper abandons this environmental justice focus entirely. It analyzes only aggregate county-level manufacturing employment and ambient air quality (PM$_{2.5}$ from monitoring stations), omitting the TRI facility-level data, the within-county spatial analysis, the racial stratification of employment outcomes, and the distributional incidence framework that constituted the manifest's core contribution. While the empirical strategy (staggered DiD of redesignation) remains intact, the research question has shifted from "who bears the environmental costs of deregulation?" to "do regulatory gains persist at the county level?" The current paper represents a study of regulatory hysteresis rather than environmental inequity, constituting a fundamentally different—and less distinctive—contribution than the one proposed.

**2. Summary**

This paper studies whether counties that transition from Clean Air Act nonattainment to attainment status experience a rebound in manufacturing employment and pollution. Using a Callaway-Sant'Anna staggered difference-in-differences design covering 362 redesignated counties and 2,433 never-designated controls from 2001–2019, the author finds no evidence of employment recovery (point estimate $-2.5\%$) and continued improvements in PM$_{2.5}$ concentrations ($-0.67$ $\mu$g/m$^3$). The paper interprets these results as an "environmental ratchet": once regulations induce abatement investment, the cleaner equilibrium persists even after regulatory pressure is formally relaxed.

**3. Essential Points**

*Failure to deliver promised distributional analysis.* The manifest's stated novelty was examining "within-county distributional incidence of deregulation" using geocoded TRI data and racial employment disparities. The actual paper contains no analysis of facility-level emissions, no spatial distribution of pollution within counties, and no demographic heterogeneity in employment effects. This omission removes the paper's promised contribution to environmental justice and significantly weakens its appeal for *AER: Insights*.

*Conceptual conflation of "redesignation" with "deregulation."* The paper frames redesignation as the removal of regulatory stringency, but acknowledges that redesignated counties enter a 20-year maintenance period with ongoing SIP obligations and PSD requirements. The maintenance plan requirements (Section 175A) ensure that "permanent and enforceable emission reductions" continue post-redesignation. The treatment is not deregulation but a change in regulatory regime from prescriptive command-and-control to maintenance-based compliance. The persistence of environmental gains likely reflects continued regulatory bindingness rather than irreversible technological lock-in.

*Identification threats from deterministic attainment.* Counties redesignate only after achieving three consecutive years of NAAQS compliance—a deterministic process likely correlated with underlying industrial decline or economic restructuring. The parallel trends assumption is tenuous when treatment timing is selected on the basis of air quality improvements that may simultaneously reflect manufacturing job losses (the very "cost" of regulation the paper seeks to reverse). Pre-trend tests are insufficient if redesignation timing is endogenous to county-specific deindustrialization trajectories.

**4. Suggestions**

**Reconcile with the manifest or reframe.** If the authors wish to fulfill the original research design, they should incorporate the promised TRI facility-level data with spatial coordinates to examine whether toxic releases increase in minority tracts post-redesignation while employment recovers elsewhere. The QWI data should be disaggregated by race to test for heterogeneous labor market effects. If these data cannot be obtained or the analysis proves infeasible, the paper should be reframed to abandon the environmental justice claims and positioned instead as a study of regulatory persistence or hysteresis. As currently written, the disconnect between the promised and delivered research programs undermines the paper's credibility.

**Clarify the institutional treatment.** The paper should explicitly model the maintenance plan requirements and distinguish between "redesignation" (formal status change) and "deregulation" (removal of constraints). Consider exploiting variation in maintenance plan stringency or the differential timing of NSR relaxation versus SIP maintenance obligations. If the "ratchet" operates through maintenance plans that remain binding for 20 years, the finding that environmental gains persist is unsurprising and reflects regulatory continuity rather than irreversibility. The paper would benefit from analyzing whether redesignated counties exhibit different emission trajectories *relative to never-designated counties* that also have manufacturing but no attainment history—comparing treated counties to ever-nonattainment counties that remain out of compliance could help isolate the effect of regulatory relief versus continued pressure.

**Strengthen mechanism evidence.** The paper posits three mechanisms (sunk abatement costs, permanent firm exit, and green infrastructure) but provides no direct evidence for any of them. To support the sunk cost interpretation, the authors could use establishment-level data (e.g., NETS or Census CMF) to show that incumbent firms do not shed abatement technology post-redesignation. For the exit mechanism, the paper could examine entry/exit flows rather than net employment changes—if the ratchet operates through churn, we should see fewer entrants in redesignated counties relative to controls. The infrastructure mechanism could be tested using data on local environmental agency budgets or enforcement actions, which the paper suggests persist but does not measure.

**Address alternative explanations for the employment null.** The finding that manufacturing employment does not rebound could reflect secular decline in manufacturing rather than hysteresis. The paper should interact the redesignation indicator with national manufacturing cycles or regional industry trends to test whether redesignated counties are simply those where manufacturing was already leaving. Additionally, the paper finds that *hires and separations both decline*, which suggests a general stagnation rather than the churn associated with adjustment costs. The authors should discuss why symmetric regulatory effects (Greenstone's 2–3% decline upon designation) should necessarily imply symmetric deregulation effects; if regulation accelerated exit of marginal firms, the counterfactual "rebound" requires new entry that may be impeded by unrelated barriers (labor market tightness, housing costs, etc.).

**Improve the air quality analysis.** Ambient PM$_{2.5}$ monitors capture background concentrations influenced by regional transport and secondary formation, not just local manufacturing emissions. The paper should use TRI facility-level toxic air emissions (as promised in the manifest) or EPA NEI data to examine whether *reported emissions* from manufacturing facilities rebound, rather than relying solely on ambient monitors that may continue improving due to mobile source regulations or upwind controls. If the ratchet truly reflects technological lock-in at the facility level, we should see this in the emissions data, not just ambient air quality.

**Consider heterogeneous effects by nonattainment history.** The paper pools redesignations across pollutants (ozone, PM, etc.) and designation waves. The 2005 cohort involved the transition from 1-hour to 8-hour ozone standards—a fundamentally different regulatory shock than PM nonattainment. The paper should interact cohort-by-pollutant fixed effects or present separate results by pollutant type to ensure that the aggregate null does not mask offsetting effects (e.g., ozone redesignations showing employment gains while PM redesignations show losses). This would also help address the concern that the 2005 cohort drives the results.

**Clarify the causal interpretation of continued pollution decline.** The finding that PM$_{2.5}$ continues falling post-redesignation is interpreted as evidence of technological persistence, but it may simply reflect that redesignation requires demonstrating permanent emission reductions that continue to bind under maintenance plans. The paper should test whether pollution *relative to trend* continues declining, or whether it merely stays at attainment levels. If the counterfactual is continued decline at a slower rate, the "ratchet" may be a regulatory floor rather than true hysteresis.
