**Idea Fidelity**

The paper builds on the core manifest idea by exploiting staggered Clean Air Act redesignations to study whether deregulation reverses the regulatory costs that accumulated during nonattainment. However, it diverges in two important respects. First, the empirical focus narrows to aggregate manufacturing employment and county‐level PM₂.₅/ozone concentrations, whereas the manifest envisioned studying toxic releases (TRI) and race-specific employment (QWI by race) to directly speak to distributional and environmental justice concerns. **Summary**

The paper documents that EPA redesignation from nonattainment to attainment does not spur a rebound in manufacturing employment—even finding a small transient decline—and that air quality continues to improve, suggesting a hysteresis (“environmental ratchet”) in which the gains from regulation persist after deregulation. Using Callaway–Sant’Anna staggered DiD on 362 redesignated counties over 2001–2019 with never-treated controls, the author finds no evidence that relaxing regulatory stringency brings manufacturing back but does find sustained reductions in PM₂.₅ (and modest ozone improvements). The results challenge the reversibility assumption embedded in much of the deregulation rhetoric and highlight the asymmetric persistence of environmental benefits.

**Essential Points**

1. **Control Group Comparability and Selection into Redesignation.** Even though the paper reports clean pre-trends, the treated (redesignated) counties are substantially larger and more industrialized than the never-designated controls. Observable differences create concern that the never-treated group does not provide a credible counterfactual: redesigned counties may be on different long-run manufacturing trajectories even absent treatment, and the timing of redesignation is plausibly endogenous if manufacturing decline contributes to improved air quality and thus triggers redesignation. The paper should rely more heavily on ever-nonattainment counties as controls or, better yet, exploit within–pollutant/persistent nonattainment comparison groups to reduce extrapolation. Without addressing this, the causal claim that redesignation (and hence deregulation) fails to revive manufacturing remains fragile.

2. **Treatment Definition and Heterogeneity across Pollutants/Standards.** The paper defines treatment as the first county-year transition from nonattainment to attainment for any pollutant, yet the institutional process differs by pollutant and over time (e.g., the 1-hour vs. 8-hour ozone waves). Lumping together disparate pollutant redesignations risks muddling the interpretation—especially because air quality improvements and regulatory relief differ in magnitude and scope. The analysis should explore whether the null employment result holds within more homogeneous subsamples (e.g., ozone-only redesignations, PM₂.₅-only, or post-8-hour standard cohorts) and clarify how the treatment aligns with permanent removal of specific regulatory constraints.

3. **Mismatch with the Distributive/Environmental Justice Research Question.** The original idea emphasized manufacturing recovery by race, TRI toxic releases, and within-county pollution distribution, yet the empirical strategy does not address these dimensions. Without examining racial employment shares, facility-level releases, or spatial redistribution of pollution burdens, the paper cannot substantiate the equity claim that deregulation’s environmental costs concentrate on disadvantaged communities while any economic gains (if present) fail to materialize for minorities. The paper should either reframe its contribution to focus purely on aggregate hysteresis or, preferably, incorporate at least some of the planned equity analyses to stay true to the stated motivation.

**Suggestions**

1. **Strengthen the Counterfactual by Leveraging Ever-Nonattainment Counties or Event-Study Weighting.** The manifest already noted the possibility of comparing to counties that remained nonattainment; the author briefly reports a robustness check but relies primarily on never-treated controls. To bolster credibility, present the CS estimates using ever-nonattainment controls as the main specification or, at minimum, report ATT estimates with alternative weighting (e.g., partially pooled CS that draws on more comparable units). Additionally, consider balancing on pre-treatment manufacturing trends (via entropy balancing or propensity-score trimming) to reduce the influence of large metropolitan nonattainment-cum-redesignated counties on the estimate.

2. **Disentangle Redesignations by Pollutant/Standard and Document Regulatory Changes.** Provide a version of Table 1 or the appendix that lists redesignation cohorts by pollutant/standard (e.g., ozone 1-hour vs. 8-hour, PM₂.₅, SO₂) and the regulatory burdens each cohort shed. This will help readers interpret the meaning of a “redesignation” in each context. Then re-estimate the main ATT separately for the largest homogeneous cohorts (e.g., PM₂.₅ counties, ozone 8-hour only, post-2007 redesignations) to test whether the ratchet is driven by particular regulatory relaxations or is a general phenomenon. If the effect varies, dive into why—for instance, perhaps ozone redesignations still face maintenance obligations that deter manufacturing entry.

3. **Address Potential Reverse Causality through Instrumental Variation or Lag Structure.** The worry that manufacturing decline causes pollution improvements (and hence redesignation) could be partly addressed by exploiting administrative delays or using instruments such as EPA procedural factors (e.g., timing of state petitions, phasedozone standard implementation) that affect redesignation timing but are arguably exogenous to local economic conditions. Alternatively, implement specifications that condition on lagged employment trends and other economic controls, or use a lead-lag structure that explicitly tests whether manufacturing declines precede or follow compliance improvements. Presenting a decomposition showing that improved pollution drives redesignation rather than vice versa would strengthen the causal story.

4. **Incorporate Partial Equity Analyses Already in the Data.** Since the manifest emphasized racial employment and TRI releases, the author could take feasible steps toward those analyses even within the current framework. For example, use available QWI disaggregated employment by broad demographic groups (if available) to test whether the null effect masks heterogeneity—do minority workers fare differently from white workers after redesignation? Similarly, TRI data can be used to examine whether total toxic releases or the share emitted near majority-minority census tracts respond differently than aggregate PM₂.₅ concentrations. Even if the spatial analysis is limited, showing that pollution improvements do not mask localized increases in facility-level toxic releases would guard against overlooking environmental injustice.

5. **Expand Mechanism Evidence Beyond Theory.** The discussion posits sunk costs, permanent exit, and infrastructure lock-in as the drivers of the ratchet, but empirical evidence is limited. To the extent possible, incorporate supporting analyses: for example, show that plant births remain depressed after redesignation (using CBP or NETS), or that investment indicators (e.g., machinery & equipment spending if available) do not rebound. Alternatively, use the hires/separations data already presented to infer that the labor market is not absorbing new entrants, consistent with persistent exit. Adding a few targeted tests would elevate the paper from descriptive to mechanism-rich.

6. **Clarify the Temporal Horizon of Treatment Effects and Anticipation Issues.** The event study shows a significant dip at year +1 and gradual return toward zero, suggesting temporary adjustment costs. Explicate whether this pattern is consistent with firms responding at the time of redesignation (e.g., administrative burden, uncertainty) rather than with deregulation per se. Also, discuss whether redesignation is anticipated—do firms adjust behavior in expectation of redesignation, and if so, does the estimator already capture those anticipatory effects? If data permit, examine leads further out (beyond five years) or use a placebo treatment date earlier to test for pre-announcement effects.

7. **Address the Missing Data and Measurement Issues in QWI and AQS.** While suppression is noted, provide more detail on how many county-years are missing, whether missingness correlates with treatment timing, and whether results change when using imputation or sample restrictions (e.g., drop counties with >20% missing). For air quality, explain whether monitor coverage differences systematically exclude certain counties (e.g., smaller or rural ones) and whether the pollution estimates might suffer from selective attrition. Robustness checks that reweight the sample to reflect the treated county distribution would reassure readers.

8. **Provide a More Nuanced Interpretation of Policy Implications.** The conclusion portrays deregulation as ineffective because the jobs do not return, but policymakers might still worry about future environmental deterioration. Discuss whether redesignated counties remain better able to defend standards (e.g., via maintenance plans) and whether ongoing federal or state oversight plays a role. Also, consider whether this ratchet logic generalizes to other regulations—are there contexts where relaxing rules would reinstigate pollution because compliance investments degrade? Acknowledging the limits of the ratchet metaphor would prevent overgeneralization.

By addressing these points, the paper would present a more convincing causal narrative, better align with its environmental justice motivation, and sharpen both the empirical and policy contributions.
