## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether losing a patent application causes inventors to leave their state. The broad claim is that patent examination does more than allocate intellectual property rights: it may also reshape the geography of innovation by pushing rejected inventors to relocate, which would connect federal patent policy to labor mobility and place-based growth.

Why should a busy economist care? Because if true, this would be a novel link between IP institutions and spatial equilibrium: patent policy would affect not just innovation incentives, but where high-skill people live and work.

### Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Reasonably, but not optimally. The opening is vivid, but it is also a bit over-written and too mechanism-forward too soon (“location-specific rents evaporate”) before the reader is persuaded that this is even a first-order phenomenon. The introduction gets to the question quickly, but the pitch is diluted by jumping into place-based policy before fully establishing the core world question.

The first two paragraphs should do less theorizing and more positioning around a sharp empirical fact and a big economic question:

> Patent rights are usually studied as incentives for invention and commercialization. But they may also affect where inventors choose to live and work. If losing a patent application makes inventors more likely to leave their current state, then patent examination policy has an unrecognized effect on the geography of innovation, not just on the production of ideas.  
>  
> This paper asks whether patent rejection causes inventors to relocate across state lines. Using quasi-random variation in USPTO examiner leniency, I study whether otherwise similar inventors become more mobile when their applications are rejected. The central finding is that rejection is associated with higher interstate mobility, especially among solo and experienced inventors, suggesting that patent decisions may influence the spatial allocation of innovative talent.

That is the pitch the paper should have. Clean, concrete, and world-facing.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### Contribution in one sentence

The paper claims to show that patent rejection increases inventors’ interstate mobility, implying that patent examination policy affects the geographic distribution of innovative human capital.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest papers?

Only partially. The paper says “no prior paper has examined mobility,” which is useful, but that is not enough for AER-level differentiation. “New outcome on an existing IV design” is not by itself a top-journal contribution unless the outcome is surprising, central, and belief-changing.

The closest neighboring contribution is the examiner-leniency literature on patent decisions and downstream outcomes. Relative to that literature, the current paper’s novelty is: not innovation, not firm growth, not financing, but geographic mobility. That is clear enough. What is less clear is why mobility is the margin that most changes how we think about patent policy.

### Is the contribution framed as a question about the WORLD, or as filling a gap in a LITERATURE?

It starts as a world question, which is good: does patent rejection make inventors move? But it repeatedly falls back to literature-gap framing: extending the outcome space, adding a margin no prior paper has examined, speaking to place-based innovation policy. For AER, the paper needs to lean harder into the world question and less into “here is another consequence of examiner leniency.”

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?

Right now they could say: “It uses the usual patent-examiner leniency design to ask whether rejected inventors are more likely to move across states.” That is understandable, but also perilously close to “another DiD/IV paper about X” in spirit. The introduction does not yet make the result feel inevitable-to-care-about.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Several possibilities:

1. **A bigger outcome than simple interstate mobility.**  
   “Moves state” is a fairly blunt measure. The contribution would be much bigger if the paper showed that rejection shifts inventors:
   - from low-innovation to high-innovation clusters,
   - from small firms to large firms,
   - from non-coastal to frontier ecosystems,
   - into or out of entrepreneurship,
   - toward co-location with prior collaborators or assignees.

   Any of these would turn “mobility” into “reallocation of innovative capacity.”

2. **A stronger geography framing.**  
   Instead of focusing on whether inventors move at all, ask whether patent rejection changes **where talent goes** and whether it amplifies regional divergence. Does rejection drain weaker states and thicken already-productive hubs? That is much more AER than a generic mobility effect.

3. **A cleaner mechanism tied to economic theory.**  
   “Location-specific IP rents” is plausible, but currently more asserted than made central. The paper would be bigger if it contrasted sharply different mechanisms:
   - loss of local bargaining power,
   - reduced value of staying with current employer,
   - increased search after commercialization failure,
   - selective continuation by inventors with mobile careers.

   Even descriptively, stronger mechanism evidence would elevate the paper beyond “rejection correlates with moving.”

4. **Aggregation to policy-relevant incidence.**  
   If the paper could show how much patent policy changes state-level inventor stocks or local innovation output, then it becomes a paper about spatial general equilibrium forces, not an individual-level reduced form.

- geography papers study why skilled workers sort across places;
- patent papers study how IP changes inventor and firm outcomes;

That bridge is the paper’s best strategic angle.

The framing promises a lot more than the outcome presently delivers. That mismatch is the strategic problem.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Patent decisions affect IP rights, firm value, and innovation incentives. Separately, economists care about why innovative workers cluster in some places and leave others.

### Tension

We do not know whether patent examination also affects inventor location choices. If rejection weakens the value of remaining embedded in a local ecosystem, then patent policy may alter the geography of innovation in a way the literature has missed.

### Resolution

The paper finds that rejected inventors are more likely to relocate across states, with larger effects for solo and experienced inventors.

### Implications

Patent policy may not only influence inventive output, but also where inventors reside, potentially interacting with place-based policies and regional inequality.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the skeleton of one, but the arc is not fully earned. The current paper often reads like:
1. here is a cool hypothesis,
2. here is a standard examiner-leniency design,
3. here are some mobility results,
4. maybe this matters for place-based policy.

That is not yet a fully integrated narrative. The “resolution” is too small relative to the scale of the implications being claimed.

### What story should it be telling?

The right story is not “patent rejection slightly raises the probability of crossing a state line.”  
The right story is:

> Patent rights help anchor inventive talent in place. When those rights are denied, some inventors detach from their local ecosystem and re-sort geographically. This makes the patent office an overlooked institution in the geography of innovation.

Everything in the paper should serve that story:
- main fact,
- who moves,
- where they move,
- why this changes our view of patent policy and regional development.

Without that, it risks being a collection of sensible results attached to an overextended phrase (“rejection drain”).

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?

“I’ve got evidence that losing a patent application makes inventors more likely to leave their state, so patent examination may affect where innovative talent ends up, not just whether ideas get protected.”

That is the right lead.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

Some would lean in initially, because the cross-field connection is interesting. But the next question would arrive immediately: **“Where do they go, and does it matter for aggregate innovation?”** If the answer remains just a modest increase in next-state moves, enthusiasm will fade.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Almost certainly one of these:
- Do rejected inventors move to more innovative places?
- Is this mostly solo inventors and fringe patentees, or core inventors?
- Does this reallocation affect startup formation, patenting, or local productivity?
- Is this really about mobility, or just who files again and where?

Strategically, the important point is that the natural follow-up questions are better than the paper’s current endpoint. That means the paper has found an intriguing doorway, but has not yet walked through it.

### If findings are modest: is the modest effect itself interesting?

Potentially yes, but only if the paper makes a case that small inventor-level effects can compound into meaningful spatial reallocation. Right now the back-of-envelope count of 2,750 additional relocations per year is not enough. It sounds small, and the paper admits it is modest. If the result is modest, the author must translate it into a bigger object: high-value inventors, thin-state inventor stocks, migration to hubs, or interaction with concentrated innovation ecosystems.

Otherwise it risks feeling like an interesting but second-order behavioral response.
