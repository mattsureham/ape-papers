## 1. **Idea Fidelity**

The paper pursues only the first half of the manifest, and that matters. The manifest proposed a two-step contribution: first, estimate the causal effect of patent rejection on inventor interstate mobility using examiner leniency; second, aggregate examiner-induced inventor flows to the state-year level and use them as an instrument for state knowledge-worker supply changes in QWI professional-services employment. The submitted paper does the first piece only. It does not use the Azure QWI data, does not implement the state-level second stage, and therefore does not deliver the broader economic claim about local labor-market consequences of rejection-driven inventor reallocation.

Even within the inventor-level design, the paper departs from the original ambition in an important way: the manifest treats the examiner-IV design as credible quasi-random variation, whereas the paper’s own balance and placebo tests show substantial violations of that premise. Once the current examiner predicts prior mobility, the identifying strategy is no longer cleanly the one advertised in the manifest. So the paper follows the topic, data source, and core micro question, but misses the macro consequence and ends up undermining its own main identification claim.

## 2. **Summary**

This paper studies whether patent rejection induces inventors to move across states, using variation in examiner grant propensity within art-unit-by-year cells as an instrument for rejection. The headline result is that rejection increases interstate mobility by about 1.1 percentage points, roughly 9 percent of a 12.2 percent baseline, with larger effects for solo and experienced inventors.

The question is interesting and potentially important. But in its current form, the paper does not establish a credible causal effect: the instrument fails key balance and placebo tests, and the interpretation of the estimates as “rejection drain” is stronger than the evidence supports.

## 3. **Essential Points**

1. **The identification strategy is not credible in its current implementation.**  
   The most serious issue is that examiner leniency significantly predicts pre-treatment outcomes and characteristics, especially prior interstate mobility. That is not a minor diagnostic blemish; it directly contradicts the as-good-as-random assignment claim conditional on art-unit × year. A placebo coefficient of -0.014 on prior moves is economically large relative to the main IV estimate of +0.011. With this result in the paper, the examiner-IV estimate cannot be interpreted as causal without much stronger repair work.

2. **The mobility outcome is potentially badly contaminated by inventor misidentification and sample selection.**  
   Matching inventors by first and last name is a very weak disambiguation strategy for this question. It can mechanically create both false interstate “moves” and false prior mobility, which may help explain the failed placebo. In addition, the outcome is defined only for inventors who patent again within ten years. Since rejection may affect the probability of patenting again, the paper conditions on a post-treatment variable, which can induce selection bias and alter the composition of observed movers. This issue is central, not peripheral.

3. **The paper overstates economic meaning relative to what is actually shown.**  
   The estimated magnitude at the inventor-application level is modest and could be interesting, but the paper repeatedly draws broader conclusions about place-based policy and geographic redistribution of knowledge workers without implementing the state-level analysis proposed in the manifest. As written, the paper shows at most a possible effect on the location observed in the next patent filing among continuing patentees. It does not yet show economically meaningful state-level labor-market consequences.

## 4. **Suggestions**

The paper is asking a good question, and there may be a publishable paper here, but it needs to become much tighter and more credible. My strongest recommendation is to treat the current draft as a promising first pass rather than a finished AER: Insights paper.

First, you need to rebuild the identification section around the failed diagnostics rather than around the canonical examiner-IV story. Right now the paper says examiner assignment is quasi-random, then shows evidence that it is not random enough in this sample. That contradiction is fatal unless resolved. At minimum, I would want to see balance and placebo tests at much finer conditioning sets than art-unit × filing-year: art-unit × year × inventor-experience bins, perhaps examiner-year workload controls, application type controls, and ideally art-unit × year × assignee-type or technology subclass cells if available. If assignment is really based on docket availability and internal routing, coarse art-unit-year fixed effects may be insufficient. You should also show whether the placebo survives when restricting to much narrower assignment cells.

Second, the paper should exploit the panel nature of inventors more carefully. A strong design would compare outcomes within inventor across applications, using inventor fixed effects or event-study style specifications around rejection versus grant events. Even if that does not replace the IV, it would tell the reader whether the observed relationship is driven by persistent inventor types. If the same inventor is more likely to move after a rejection than after a grant, that would be much more persuasive than the current cross-sectional design. The current balance failures suggest exactly this sort of unobserved inventor heterogeneity is important.

Third, I would strongly reconsider the inventor-linkage strategy. First-and-last-name matching is not adequate for a paper whose outcome is interstate movement. You need a more serious disambiguation procedure using full inventor names, middle initials, co-inventor networks, assignees, technology classes, and timing. If a validated external inventor disambiguation file exists for patents in your period, use it. At minimum, provide robustness in subsamples where disambiguation is cleaner: uncommon names, exact name strings with middle initials, or inventors linked consistently to the same assignee for multiple filings. If the placebo disappears in cleaner-ID subsamples, that would materially strengthen the paper.

Fourth, the outcome definition needs more work. “Moved” based on the next patent filing is a noisy and selected proxy for actual relocation. You should present extensive descriptives: the distribution of time to next filing, the share with no next filing, differences in continuation behavior, and how often state changes coincide with assignee changes. A key concern is that rejection changes prosecution strategy and future filing behavior, not necessarily residential location. For example, if rejected inventors later file under a different employer or assignee headquarters, the measured state change may partly reflect administrative address changes rather than true migration. You should test alternative outcomes: state change within two years, within five years, ever changes state, assignee-state change, and inventor-state change conditional on same assignee. That would help isolate actual mobility.

Fifth, address selection into the observed outcome head-on. Your mobility measure is only defined for inventors with a subsequent filing. If rejection causes exit from patenting, then the sample of observed “next filings” is endogenous. You should estimate the effect of rejection on the probability of any subsequent filing and report Lee bounds or other partial-identification exercises for mobility among continuing patentees. Without this, the 1.1 percentage point effect is difficult to interpret. It may be attenuated, exaggerated, or even sign-reversed by selection.

Sixth, the standard errors may not be your biggest problem, but they deserve more attention. Clustering at art-unit × year is mechanically linked to the level at which the instrument varies, so it is a reasonable starting point. But with generated examiner-level leniency measures and possible correlation within examiner and within inventor, I would want robustness to two-way clustering or alternative inference procedures: art-unit × year and examiner, or perhaps examiner and inventor if computationally feasible on collapsed data. Given 6,616 art-unit-year cells, asymptotics are not obviously poor, but the dependence structure here is richer than the paper acknowledges. Also, please report the first-stage coefficient, not just the F-statistic, and ideally the reduced-form regression with standard errors, so readers can assess whether the 2SLS magnitude is mechanically plausible.

On magnitudes: the main effect itself is plausible in the narrow sense. A 1.1 percentage point increase relative to a 12.2 percent baseline is not inherently absurd; it is modest. What is less plausible is the narrative load placed on it. The paper talks as if rejection substantially redistributes innovative talent across states, but among observed continuing patentees the implied effect is small in absolute terms. The heterogeneity estimates for solo and experienced inventors are more sizable, but given the identification concerns and the tiny solo-inventor share (5.3 percent), I would be cautious in emphasizing them. In particular, the experienced-inventor result may simply reflect cleaner measurement of mobility for prolific inventors or stronger selection into future patenting, not necessarily stronger treatment effects.

Seventh, the paper should be more disciplined in its mechanism claims. “Destruction of location-specific IP rents” is an interesting hypothesis, but the evidence offered is weak. Solo vs. team and experienced vs. novice are not clean mechanism tests; they are reduced-form heterogeneity patterns consistent with many stories, including career transitions, firm turnover, or differences in patenting persistence. If you want to push a rent-destruction mechanism, look for sharper tests: effects larger when assignees are local and small, when inventors are tied to startup firms, when the patent is in technologies with thicker out-of-state labor markets, or when the inventor previously had concentrated local co-inventor networks. Right now the mechanism section over-interprets suggestive heterogeneity.

Eighth, if you want the paper to have the economic punch appropriate for AER: Insights, I would seriously consider reinstating the second-stage state-level analysis from the manifest. As it stands, the paper’s broader claims about place-based policy are speculative. If you can aggregate examiner-induced inventor outflows to the state-year level and show effects on professional/scientific/technical employment, innovative employment, startup activity, or patenting in receiving versus sending states, then the paper becomes much more consequential. Even null second-stage results would be informative, because they would discipline the policy rhetoric. Without that macro piece, I would scale back the discussion substantially.

Ninth, improve the transparency of the sample construction. The paper shifts between applications, inventor-application pairs, unique inventors, and unique applications in a way that makes it hard to map treatment and outcome variation. A clean flow chart would help. I also want to understand why the sample has only 574,207 unique inventors out of 4.45 million inventor-application pairs; that implies many repeated observations per inventor and underscores the need to address within-inventor dependence and composition. It would also be useful to know how many observations are unique next-filing links rather than repeated uses of the same inventor in multiple application spells.

Finally, I would rewrite the framing to be more measured. The strongest version of the paper right now is: “Using patent records, I document that inventors assigned to stricter examiners are more likely to appear in another state on a subsequent filing, but validity tests raise concerns about random assignment and name-based mobility measurement.” That is not yet an AER: Insights result, but it is honest and potentially improvable. The current version instead states a clean causal result and then buries evidence that substantially undermines it. Readers will not find that persuasive.

In short: good topic, plausible baseline magnitude, but the current causal design is not convincing. Fix the instrument validity problem if you can; if you cannot, reframe the paper as descriptive or quasi-experimental with substantial caveats. And if possible, restore the state-level labor-market analysis promised in the original idea, because that is where the broader economic significance would lie.
