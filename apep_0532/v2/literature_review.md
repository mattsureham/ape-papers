# Literature Novelty Check — apep_0532 v2

## Core Literature
1. **Egan & Mullin (2012)** — Local weather affects Americans' climate perceptions. Our paper tests whether this generalizes to developing countries.
2. **Deryugina (2013)** — Weather shifts beliefs temporarily. We find similar transience in distributed lag models.
3. **Zaval et al. (2014)** — Attribute substitution: current temp → climate concern. We test whether economic structure mediates this.
4. **Herrnstadt & Muehlegger (2014)** — Weather salience affects congressional voting. US-focused.
5. **Hazlett & Mildenberger (2020)** — Wildfire exposure → pro-environment voting, but only in Democratic areas. Closest to our "heterogeneous effects" framing.

## Gap Identified
No paper tests whether agricultural economic dependence mediates the weather → climate attention channel. No paper documents attention substitution (climate → agricultural search) during heat shocks. The developing-country evidence on experiential learning about climate is thin.

## Novelty Assessment
The attention substitution analysis (where does search go during heat shocks?) is, to our knowledge, new. The India setting with agricultural variation provides a natural test of boundary conditions for experiential learning theory.
