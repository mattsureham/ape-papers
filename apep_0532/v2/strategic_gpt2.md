## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether experiencing unusual heat makes people pay more attention to climate change—and whether that mechanism depends on how people experience weather economically. Using cross-state variation in agricultural dependence in India, the paper argues that in places where weather is a livelihood shock rather than just a nuisance, heat does not translate into climate attention in the same way; instead, attention may shift toward immediate agricultural concerns.

A busy economist should care because the paper is trying to qualify a broad and increasingly influential claim in political economy and behavioral environmental economics: that lived exposure to climate events naturally generates climate awareness. If that mechanism breaks down precisely where climate vulnerability is greatest, that matters for how we think about belief formation, adaptation, and climate politics in the developing world.

**Does the paper articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Mostly yes, and better than many submissions. The opening does identify the big claim in the literature and the proposed boundary condition. But it is still somewhat too conceptual and not quite sharp enough about the one thing the paper wants the reader to remember. It also takes a little too long to get from “existing literature says X” to “we test X in a setting where the premise may fail.”

**What the first two paragraphs should say instead:**

> A growing literature argues that unusual weather makes climate change salient: when people experience abnormal heat, they become more concerned about global warming and seek more climate information. But nearly all of that evidence comes from rich settings where weather is primarily an amenity shock.  
>   
> In poorer, more agrarian settings, the same heat shock is also a livelihood shock. This paper asks a simple question: when weather is both a climate signal and an immediate economic threat, which interpretation wins? Using Indian states’ large differences in agricultural dependence, we show that hotter months do not uniformly increase climate attention; if anything, heat in more agricultural places appears to shift attention away from climate topics and toward livelihood-related search.

That is the pitch. The current intro is thoughtful, but it could be more ruthless.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:**  
The paper’s contribution is to argue that experiential learning about climate is conditional on economic structure: heat shocks generate less climate attention, and possibly more livelihood-oriented attention, in more agriculturally dependent places.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?
Partly, but not enough. The paper knows the immediate experiential-learning literature and says “this is not just the same question in India,” which is the right instinct. But the differentiation remains more asserted than demonstrated. A skeptical reader could still summarize it as: “another paper using weather variation and Google Trends to study climate attention, except in India with a heterogeneity interaction.”

The differentiating feature is **not** India per se. It is the claim that **economic exposure changes the cognitive meaning of the same weather event**. That has to be elevated much more aggressively.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a gap in a literature?
It is framed in a reasonably world-facing way, which is good. The strongest version is:  
- In the world, weather can be both information and an income shock.  
- Therefore the effect of experience on climate attention should depend on economic structure.

That is stronger than “the literature has not tested India” or “the literature has not studied agricultural states.” The paper mostly understands this, but sometimes slides back into literature-gap language.

### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what’s new?
Not quite cleanly. Right now they might say:  
> “It’s a weather-and-climate-attention paper in India with some interaction by agricultural dependence and Google Trends outcomes.”

That is not enough. You want them to say:  
> “It shows that weather only becomes climate information when people are not too busy treating it as an economic shock.”

That is memorable and belongs to a bigger conversation.

### What would make this contribution bigger?
Three specific possibilities:

1. **Move from attention to belief or action.**  
   Search data are inherently intermediate. If the paper could show that economic structure changes not just search behavior but climate beliefs, policy demand, adaptation choices, or political responses, the contribution becomes much bigger.

2. **Make substitution the centerpiece, not a side pattern.**  
   Right now the most interesting idea in the paper is that attention is reallocated, not absent. But the substitution evidence is presented as suggestive and secondary. Strategically, this should be the heart of the paper. If the authors had richer outcome data showing a clear shift from climate to livelihood attention, that would be far more distinctive than another attenuated interaction.

3. **Generalize the framing beyond India.**  
   India is the setting, but the claim is about how economic structure mediates experiential learning. The paper would be bigger if it framed itself as a model of climate cognition in vulnerable economies, with India as a particularly useful test case.

**Build on them, then qualify them.** Not “attack,” exactly. The right move is:

- Prior literature correctly identified a mechanism in settings where weather is mostly informational.
- This paper asks when that mechanism does and does not travel.
- The paper contributes a boundary condition, not a refutation.

That is a more credible and higher-status posture than implying the earlier literature got it wrong.

At present, oddly, both.

- **Too narrowly** in its empirical self-description: Google Trends, Indian states, agricultural share, monsoon splits.
- **Too broadly** in some of its theoretical claims: cognitive bandwidth, climate awareness formation in the developing world, policy communication, etc., without quite enough empirical weight to carry all that.

The paper needs a crisper middle level:
> “This paper is about the conditions under which weather experience translates into climate attention.”

That is broad enough to matter, narrow enough to be believable.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?
A few conversations it should probably engage more directly:

1. **Development and media/information literatures**  
   Not just internet penetration as measurement quality, but how information technologies mediate issue salience in low-income settings.

2. **Behavioral development / scarcity / bandwidth literatures**  
   The “cognitive bandwidth” idea is central to the paper’s story, but the cited attention papers are quite abstract. The paper would benefit from being in conversation with the economics of scarcity and bandwidth under economic stress.

3. **Climate adaptation and risk perception in developing countries**  
   There is likely relevant work on how farmers process climate risk, weather insurance, adaptation behavior, and local environmental knowledge. That is probably a better neighboring conversation than generic climate-beliefs papers alone.

4. **Agenda-setting / issue attention literatures**  
   Since the outcome is search attention, the paper should also speak to work on issue salience and attention allocation, not just climate beliefs.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?
Partly, but it could have a more impactful conversation if it shifted from:
- “Does heat increase climate search in India?”

to:
- “When does personal experience become political/environmental learning rather than immediate economic coping?”

That broader learning-under-stress framing could connect behavioral economics, development, and environmental economics in a more interesting way.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
The world before this paper: economists have accumulated evidence that unusual weather can make climate change more salient. The implicit takeaway is that lived experience may help generate climate awareness.

### Tension
That evidence largely comes from affluent settings where weather is mainly an inconvenience or cue. In poorer agrarian settings, weather is also an income shock. So the same event may not be cognitively processed as “climate.”

### Resolution
In India, hotter months do not robustly raise climate-related search on average. More agricultural states appear less likely to translate heat into climate attention, with suggestive evidence that attention shifts toward livelihood-related searches instead; under some conditions, especially monsoon periods, the pattern changes.

### Implications
Experiential learning is not universal. Climate communication, political responsiveness, and public belief formation may depend on whether people have the slack to interpret weather as information rather than immediate threat.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
Yes, more than most papers. There is a real story here. But the arc is weakened by the fact that the paper keeps interrupting its own narrative with caveats, fragility, and multiple semi-supported patterns. That may be honest, but strategically it diffuses the story.

At times the paper feels like:
- main interaction is weak,
- but here is a high-internet result,
- but bootstrap weakens it,
- but here is seasonal reversal,
- but bootstrap weakens it,
- but here is substitution pattern,
- but imprecise,
- but the triple interaction is p=0.049.

That reads less like resolution and more like a search for a result.

### What story should it be telling?
The paper should tell one disciplined story:

> Weather has two meanings: signal and shock. Whether people turn experience into climate attention depends on which meaning dominates. India lets us test that because some states experience weather mainly through services/urban life, others through agriculture. We show that the standard weather-to-climate-attention link does not generalize cleanly, and the most suggestive evidence points to attention being redirected toward livelihood concerns where weather is economically consequential.

Everything else should support that story. The paper should not present itself as having nailed four separate findings. It has one idea and several pieces of evidence around it.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?
I would lead with:

> In India, hotter-than-usual months do not reliably produce more climate attention—and in more agricultural places they may push attention toward crop and livelihood concerns instead.

That is the most interesting fact in the paper.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
Economists would lean in **if** the paper presents it as a challenge to a general mechanism they think they understand. They will not lean in for “Google Trends in India.” They might lean in for “experience does not automatically become learning when people are economically exposed.”

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Immediately:

> Is this really about agricultural exposure and attention reallocation, or just about internet selection / urban-rural composition / measurement?

That is the obvious follow-up. Since the paper cannot fully kill that question with its current design, the framing should acknowledge it while still stressing what is learned.

### If the findings are null or modest, is that itself interesting?
Potentially yes. A null average effect is interesting **if** it is framed as overturning an expectation imported from richer settings. But the paper should not present “we mostly get fragile interactions and sign patterns” as if that alone is enough. The null is interesting only because the paper has a strong prior claim to test.

Right now the paper does a decent job making the null informative, but it still overreaches in places. The strongest honest version is:
- the average U.S.-style experiential-learning result does not travel cleanly;
- the most suggestive explanation is economic mediation via attention allocation.

That is publishable territory in a good field journal. For AER, it needs either sharper evidence or a much more commanding framing.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

Be honest: the gap is sizable.

### What is the main problem?
Primarily a **scope-plus-novelty** problem, with some framing issues.

- **Framing problem:** The underlying idea is better than the execution’s self-presentation. The paper’s central concept—weather as signal vs shock—is AER-worthy in spirit.
- **Scope problem:** The evidence base is thin for the ambition of the claim. State-level Google Trends in one country, with fragile heterogeneity patterns, is not enough to carry a broad statement about climate learning in vulnerable economies.
- **Novelty problem:** In current form, readers can still see it as a modest extension of weather-and-attention papers.
- **Ambition problem:** The empirical ambition does not fully match the theoretical ambition. The paper wants to say something fundamental, but uses a relatively narrow and noisy proxy.

### What is the gap between this and something that would excite the top 10 people in the field?
A top-field version would probably need at least one of the following:
1. **Individual-level belief data** showing that farmers or economically exposed households process the same weather differently than others.
2. **A stronger behavioral margin** than search—e.g., policy support, adaptation demand, insurance take-up, voting, migration, or direct belief updating.
3. **Cross-country or multi-setting evidence** showing that the mechanism scales beyond India.
4. **A much cleaner demonstration of substitution**, not just sign patterns from broad search categories.

Without one of those, the paper is intellectually interesting but empirically too narrow and fragile for AER.

### Single most impactful advice
**If the authors can only change one thing, they should bring in individual-level data on climate beliefs or behavior linked to economic exposure, so the paper tests whether weather is processed differently by economically vulnerable people rather than inferring that mechanism from state-level Google searches.**

That would transform the paper from a suggestive ecological pattern into a real statement about how experience becomes—or fails to become—learning.
