## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a good world question: when people experience unusual heat, does that make them pay more attention to climate change, or does it instead push attention toward immediate economic survival? Using variation across Indian states in agricultural dependence, the paper argues that weather is not universally processed as a climate signal; in more agrarian settings, the same heat shock may crowd out climate attention and redirect attention toward livelihood concerns.

A busy economist should care because the paper is trying to qualify a now-common inference in the climate-beliefs literature: that lived experience of weather naturally generates climate awareness. If that mechanism depends on economic structure, then a lot of what we think we know from rich countries may not travel to the places most exposed to climate risk.

### Does the paper articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Mostly yes on the underlying idea, but not optimally. The paper does a better job than many at identifying the tension early: prior work treats weather as information, but in poor/agricultural settings weather is also a direct economic shock. That is the right setup.

The problem is that the introduction then drifts into a somewhat overconfident presentation of findings relative to what the results can support. The opening pages promise a “fundamental question” and an appealing conceptual answer, but the empirical results as presented are modest, fragile, and largely suggestive. So the paper’s first paragraphs are stronger than the paper’s evidentiary footing. For AER purposes, that mismatch is dangerous.

### The pitch the paper should have

Here is the version the first two paragraphs should deliver:

> A growing literature shows that unusual local weather increases climate concern in rich countries. But those settings share an important feature: for most people, weather is mainly an informational signal or amenity shock, not an immediate threat to income.  
>
> This paper asks whether experiential learning about climate survives when weather is also a livelihood shock. Using cross-state variation in agricultural dependence in India, we study whether heat anomalies increase climate-related attention, or instead redirect attention toward economically urgent topics. The broader question is whether the link from climate experience to climate awareness is a universal behavioral response, or one mediated by economic structure.

That is the paper’s strongest strategic framing. It is a world question, not a data exercise.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper’s contribution is to argue that the effect of weather experience on climate attention is mediated by economic structure: in more agriculturally dependent places, heat shocks are less likely to generate climate attention and may instead shift attention toward livelihood concerns.

### Is this clearly differentiated from the closest 3-4 papers?

Partially, but not sharply enough.

The paper clearly distinguishes itself from the standard experiential-learning papers by saying, in effect, “those studies are from post-industrial contexts where weather is mostly signal, not shock.” That is useful. But the differentiation is still somewhat conceptual rather than empirical. A smart economist could still summarize the paper as “another paper on weather and climate attention, now in India, with heterogeneity by agriculture.”

The paper tries to make the “attention substitution” angle the novel hook. That is the right instinct. But as currently written, that contribution is more asserted than established, because the substitution evidence is mainly sign-pattern-based and imprecise. So the intended differentiator is there, but the paper has not fully earned it.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a literature gap?

Mostly about the world, which is a strength. The best version of this paper is not “no one has done India” or “no one has interacted temperature with agricultural share.” It is “the same weather event means different things in different economies, and that changes how climate beliefs form.” That is the right level.

Still, the paper sometimes backslides into literature-gap language (“this is the first paper to test…”), which makes it sound smaller. The paper should lean harder into the substantive question: how does economic vulnerability shape the cognitive processing of climate shocks?

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?

Yes, but only in a somewhat fuzzy way. They would probably say:

- “It’s about whether weather-induced climate attention generalizes to India.”
- “The idea is that farmers treat weather as an income shock rather than a climate signal.”
- “They use Google Trends and state-level agricultural dependence.”

That is decent, but not yet memorable. Right now the paper is at risk of sounding like “a heterogeneity paper about weather and attention.” The introduction conveys the mechanism, but the contribution still feels narrower than it wants to be.

### What would make the contribution bigger?

Several concrete possibilities:

1. **Move from attention to beliefs or behavior.**  
   If the paper could connect weather shocks not just to search behavior but to climate beliefs, adaptation behavior, political demand, insurance take-up, migration intentions, or policy support, the contribution would become much larger. Search is a thin measure for an AER-level claim.

2. **Show true reallocation within a common attention budget.**  
   The substitution claim would be much more powerful if the paper could demonstrate not just opposite-signed coefficients on different search terms, but actual compositional reallocation across topics. Right now it is suggestive rather than decisive.

3. **Exploit within-state or within-person exposure rather than state averages.**  
   The state-level agriculture measure makes the contribution feel blunt. A more granular comparison—farmers vs. non-farmers, rural vs. urban, crop-dependent districts vs. less exposed districts—would better match the mechanism.

4. **Frame the paper as a test of external validity of a flagship behavioral result.**  
   The big version is: “One of the central behavioral facts in climate economics—experience raises concern—depends on whether people can afford to interpret weather abstractly.” That is more ambitious than “India differs from the U.S.”

5. **Bring in a sharper implication for climate policy or political economy.**  
   If economic insecurity suppresses climate attention, then the populations most exposed to climate risk may be least likely to generate endogenous political demand for climate policy. That is a bigger implication than the current “communication should be framed differently.”

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

### Closest neighbors

The closest neighbors seem to be:

1. **Egan and Mullin (2012)** on local weather and perceptions of global warming.
2. **Deryugina (2013)** on weather and short-run climate beliefs.
3. **Herrnstadt and Muehlegger (2014)** on weather and climate-related search behavior.
4. Possibly **Howe et al.** on geographic variation in climate concern / salience.
5. More distantly, the **experience-based learning / limited attention** literature:
   - **Malmendier and Nagel (2011)**
   - **Sims (2003)**
   - **Gabaix (2014)**
   - maybe **Bordalo et al.** on salience

There is also a neighboring development/climate literature on weather as an economic shock:
- **Dell, Jones, and Olken (2012)**
- **Jayachandran (2006)**
- **Burgess et al. (2017)**

### How should it position itself relative to those neighbors?

It should **build on** the rich-country experiential-learning papers while **qualifying their external validity**. Not attack them. The correct stance is: those papers identified a real mechanism, but in settings where weather is both information and livelihood threat, a different mechanism can dominate.

Relative to the attention literature, the paper should **borrow theory, not pretend to contribute to that literature in a first-order way**. Right now the paper gestures at rational inattention and sparsity, but the contribution there is loose. It is using that literature as a language for interpretation, which is fine.

Relative to the development/climate-shock literature, the paper should position itself as linking **economic exposure** to **information processing**. That crosswalk is actually the paper’s most distinctive move.

### Is it positioned too narrowly or too broadly?

A bit of both, oddly.

- **Too narrowly** in the empirical vehicle: state-level Google Trends in India can sound niche.
- **Too broadly** in the claims: phrases like “economic structure shapes the translation of climate experience into attention” sound grand relative to the evidence.

The right positioning is: **a boundary-condition paper with broader implications**, not a universal theory paper.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?

A few areas it could engage more effectively:

1. **Political economy of climate demand / salience.**  
   If the mechanism is that vulnerable populations do not automatically convert climate shocks into climate attention, that matters for political demand for adaptation and mitigation.

2. **Development literature on bandwidth/scarcity.**  
   The paper cites Sims/Gabaix, but the more intuitive bridge may be to the scarcity / cognitive bandwidth literature in development. That may resonate more naturally with the “livelihood threat crowds out abstraction” idea.

3. **Media and information acquisition under stress.**  
   There is room to speak to work on how crises shape what information people seek and process.

4. **Risk salience and adaptation.**  
   The paper hints at this via Gallagher, but could do more to connect to the literature on whether shocks induce insurance, adaptation, or political updating.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?

Not quite. Right now it is mostly having a conversation with the climate-beliefs literature plus a side conversation with rational inattention. The more interesting conversation is probably:

**When does exposure create political/economic demand for long-run risk management, and when does it merely intensify short-run coping?**

That is a much bigger and more AER-ish conversation. The paper is really about the failure of exposure to automatically generate long-run cognition under economic stress. That point travels beyond climate search.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Prior work suggests unusual weather increases climate concern and attention. This has created an implicit belief that climate exposure itself can generate awareness.

### Tension

That inference may rely on a hidden condition: in rich settings, weather is often an inconvenience or signal, but in agrarian settings it is also a livelihood shock. If the same event simultaneously demands economic coping, people may not process it as evidence about climate.

### Resolution

The paper finds that in India there is no robust average “hotter weather leads to more climate search” effect. Instead, the relationship appears to depend on agricultural structure, with suggestive evidence that in more agricultural states heat shifts attention away from climate and toward livelihood-related searches, though the evidence is not cleanly decisive.

### Implications

The main implication is that experiential learning about climate may not generalize to the most climate-vulnerable populations. More broadly, economic insecurity may block the translation of lived shocks into abstract, long-run attention or concern.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

Yes, conceptually. No, empirically.

This is the central strategic issue. The paper has a real story:
- world before: weather teaches climate
- tension: maybe not when weather threatens livelihoods
- answer: India suggests a boundary condition
- implication: economic structure mediates climate cognition

That is a coherent narrative. But the paper’s actual results section reads more like a search for enough suggestive patterns to sustain the story: null average effects, weak full-sample interaction, then internet split, triple interaction, substitution signs, monsoon reversal, placebo outcomes, etc. This creates the feeling of a collection of suggestive results orbiting a strong idea.

So: **strong concept, weaker narrative execution**.

### If it is a collection of results looking for a story, what story should it be telling?

The story should be disciplined around one central claim:

> Exposure does not mechanically produce climate attention; whether it does depends on whether people experience weather primarily as information or as an urgent economic shock.

Everything else should serve that claim:
- India as the setting where signal and shock coexist
- agricultural dependence as proxy for economic salience
- climate vs. livelihood search as the mechanism test
- internet heterogeneity as a measurement-quality refinement
- seasonality as a severity/intensity margin

Right now the paper gives each result almost equal rhetorical weight. It should instead create a hierarchy: one main claim, one main mechanism, one supporting heterogeneity margin.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?

I would lead with:

**In India, hotter-than-usual weather does not reliably increase climate attention overall, and in more agriculturally dependent places it may instead redirect attention toward immediate livelihood concerns.**

That is the arresting fact.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

They would lean in at first, because the question is genuinely interesting and cuts against a comforting narrative in the climate literature. The phrase “weather as signal vs. weather as shock” is strong. It contains a real conceptual distinction.

But the next question would come immediately:

**“How convincing is the evidence that this is really reallocation of attention rather than just noisy Google Trends heterogeneity?”**

And that is where the paper currently loses altitude. The idea is dinner-party good; the evidence, as currently packaged, invites skepticism fast.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Likely one of these:

- “Do you see this in beliefs or only in searches?”
- “Is this really agriculture, or just urban-rural/income/internet composition?”
- “Can you show farmers versus non-farmers?”
- “If the point is about scarce cognitive bandwidth, where is the sharpest mechanism evidence?”

Those are exactly the questions the paper needs to anticipate strategically.

### If findings are modest: is the modest result still interesting?

Yes, but only if framed correctly.

The paper should not sell this as “we prove crowd-out.” It should sell it as:

**a serious challenge to the universality of experiential learning, with suggestive evidence that economic urgency diverts attention away from climate interpretation.**

That is valuable even if the estimates are modest, because the underlying null is itself informative: the rich-country result does not just automatically reappear in a major climate-vulnerable democracy. That alone matters.

What would make the modestness feel like a feature rather than a failed experiment is a more explicit framing around **boundary conditions** and **external validity**. Right now the paper sometimes sounds like it wanted a clean positive effect and had to work around weak estimates. Better to say up front: “The important fact is the absence of a strong average experiential-learning effect in exactly the setting where many would expect it to matter most.”

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

Be honest: in current form, this is **not yet an AER paper**.

The core idea is AER-adjacent. The current package is not.

### What is the main gap?

Mostly a **scope/ambition problem**, with some **framing problem**.

- **Not primarily a framing problem**: the paper actually has a pretty good idea and title.
- **Partly a framing problem**: it should frame itself more explicitly as a boundary-condition/external-validity paper for a prominent behavioral result.
- **More fundamentally a scope problem**: the evidence is too narrow and too indirect for the size of the claim. State-level Google Trends in 21 Indian states is not enough to carry a broad claim about climate cognition and economic structure.
- **Also an ambition problem**: the paper is intellectually ambitious in prose, but empirically safe and thin. It wants to say something large about human attention under climate risk, but the design remains a modest state-panel heterogeneity exercise.

### What is the gap between current form and something that excites the top 10 people in this field?

Top people would want at least one of the following:

1. **Direct evidence on beliefs, preferences, or action**, not just searches.
2. **Micro-level heterogeneity that matches the mechanism**: farmers vs. non-farmers, district-level exposure, household-level survey evidence.
3. **A cleaner demonstration of substitution**, not just sign reversals across noisy indices.
4. **A sharper general takeaway** about how climate shocks translate—or fail to translate—into political or economic demand for adaptation/mitigation.

Right now the paper has an elegant conceptual distinction and a suggestive empirical pattern. That is enough for a decent field journal conversation. It is not enough for AER.

### Single most impactful advice

**If the authors change only one thing, they should add direct micro-level evidence that the same weather shock moves climate attention/beliefs differently for economically exposed versus non-exposed people within the same setting.**

That is the pivot. Everything else is secondary. If they can show, using survey or more granular behavioral data, that farmers/rural/agriculturally exposed individuals respond differently from urban/non-farm individuals to the same local weather shock, then the paper becomes much more than a noisy Google Trends heterogeneity exercise. It becomes a credible statement about mechanism and external validity.

If that is impossible, then the second-best single change is to **sharply narrow the claims** and present the paper as a suggestive boundary-condition paper rather than a broad theory of climate attention.
