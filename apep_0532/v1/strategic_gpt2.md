## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple and potentially important question: when people experience unusual weather, does that make them more attentive to climate change—or less? Using Indian state-month data, the paper argues that the answer depends on economic structure: in less agricultural states, heat anomalies raise climate-related Google searches, while in more agricultural states the same shocks may depress them, consistent with people processing weather as an income shock rather than a climate signal.

A busy economist should care because the paper is trying to qualify a widely cited idea from the U.S.-Europe literature—that personal weather experience increases climate concern—by showing that this mechanism may break down precisely where climate exposure is economically most consequential.

Does the paper articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs? Not quite. The current opening is competent, but it takes too long to get to the actual claim, and it starts from India's exposure rather than from the broader conceptual tension. The first two paragraphs should more quickly state the big fact, the puzzle, and the hypothesis.

### The pitch the paper should have

“Economists increasingly view personal experience of extreme weather as a force that raises concern about climate change. But that evidence comes mostly from rich, post-industrial societies where weather is experienced as inconvenience or signal, not as a direct threat to livelihood. This paper asks whether the same experiential-learning mechanism operates in a setting where weather is a first-order economic shock.

Using monthly data on Google searches and weather across Indian states from 2004–2023, I show that hotter-than-usual months are associated with more climate-related search in less agricultural states but less climate-related search in more agricultural states. The results suggest that economic exposure can mute or reverse the standard weather-to-climate-attention channel: when weather is a livelihood shock, people may think first about crops and income rather than about climate change.”

That is the paper’s real idea. It should lead with it.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:**  
The paper’s contribution is to argue that the effect of extreme weather on climate attention is mediated by economic structure, with agricultural dependence dampening or reversing the usual positive relationship between weather anomalies and climate concern.

### Is this clearly differentiated from the closest papers?
Partly, but not sharply enough. The paper knows the immediate neighbors—Egan and Mullin, Deryugina, Herrnstadt et al.—and it says “this is India, not the U.S.” But “same question in a developing-country setting” is not yet enough for AER. The real differentiator is not geography; it is the **economic-interpretation channel**. The paper needs to say more forcefully: prior work studies places where weather is mainly an informational signal, while I study a place where weather is also a production shock, and that distinction changes the mapping from experience to attention.

Right now the differentiation is still a little too close to “another weather and beliefs paper, but in India, using Google Trends.”

### Is the contribution framed as a question about the world, or a gap in the literature?
It is mostly framed as a question about the world, which is good. The strongest version is: **How do people interpret climate shocks when those shocks also threaten livelihoods?** That is a world question. The weaker version—“the literature hasn’t looked at developing countries”—is present too often.

The paper should lean harder into the world question.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?
Yes, but only with some effort. A good reader could say: “It argues that hot weather increases climate attention in urban/nonfarm settings but can reduce it in agricultural ones.” That is the novel idea.

But many readers would still summarize it as: “It’s a Google Trends panel paper on weather and climate searches in India with heterogeneity by agricultural share.” That is too method-description-heavy, and a warning sign.

### What would make this contribution bigger?
Several possibilities:

1. **Use a more direct measure of beliefs or salience than English-language Google searches alone.**  
   Right now the paper’s core outcome is narrow and vulnerable in positioning terms, even before any econometric concerns. Pairing search data with survey evidence on climate beliefs, adaptation beliefs, or policy support would make the claim much more consequential.

2. **Show the mechanism through what people search for instead.**  
   This is probably the highest-return expansion. If agricultural states search less for “climate change” during heat shocks, do they search more for “crop damage,” “rain forecast,” “irrigation,” “heatwave relief,” “MSP,” “insurance,” etc.? That would convert an interpretive story into a persuasive narrative. Right now the paper infers crowd-out; it does not really show where attention goes.

3. **Make the comparison more conceptually revealing.**  
   For example: compare agricultural versus non-agricultural populations within the same state, or rural versus urban search patterns if possible, or India versus a less agriculture-dependent middle-income country. That would elevate the paper from a single-country heterogeneity result to a broader claim about development and climate cognition.

4. **Connect attention to action.**  
   If the paper could show implications for adaptation, voting, policy demand, media consumption, or protective behavior, the “so what” would be much larger. As written, the endpoint is search behavior, which feels one step short of a top-field contribution.

But these are not really conversational neighbors in the same sense; they are context papers.

### How should the paper position itself?
Mostly **build on** the beliefs literature, but **reframe its scope conditions**. The paper should not “attack” the U.S. literature; the better move is: those papers identified a real mechanism, but implicitly in a setting where weather is mostly a signal. This paper asks when that mechanism generalizes and when it does not.

That is a strong and collegial positioning: not contradiction, but boundary conditions.

### Is it positioned too narrowly or too broadly?
At present it is both, oddly.

- **Too narrowly** in empirical presentation: lots of detail on Google Trends, Bartik, bootstrap p-values, etc.
- **Too broadly** in rhetorical ambition: claims about climate awareness, adaptation, communication strategy, and structural transformation, without enough empirical breadth to support all of that.

The paper needs a tighter center of gravity. The right level is: **this is a paper about the economic mediation of climate attention**. Everything should serve that.

### What literature does it seem unaware of?
It should engage more with:

1. **Attention and salience economics**  
   Sims, Gabaix-style salience, Bordalo et al., and the broader literature on limited attention. The “finite pool of worry” language is psychological; the paper would benefit from a cleaner economics framing around attention allocation under stress.

2. **Disaster experience, belief updating, and risk perception in development settings**  
   There is a wider literature on shocks, beliefs, and adaptation in poor countries that the paper should speak to more directly.

3. **Media/information environments and issue attention**  
   If the claim is about why weather does or does not translate into climate salience, literature on media mediation, agenda-setting, and information frictions is relevant.

4. **Structural transformation and political economy of environmental demand**  
   The paper hints at this in the conclusion, and that may actually be one of the most interesting framing avenues. Economic structure may shape not just output sensitivity to weather, but also the formation of environmental preferences.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?
Not quite. It is currently having the conversation “does weather affect climate searches in India?” The better conversation is “when does lived experience generate climate concern, and when does it not?” That is a much more important conversation and one with wider appeal across environmental, development, and political economy audiences.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Existing evidence, mostly from rich democracies, suggests that unusual weather increases climate attention or belief, at least temporarily.

### Tension
Those settings are ones where weather is not usually a direct livelihood shock. In much of the world, especially in agrarian economies, weather is not just information—it is income, risk, and survival. That raises the possibility that the same weather shock could crowd out climate attention rather than stimulate it.

### Resolution
Using Indian state-month data, the paper finds suggestive evidence that hotter months are associated with more climate search in less agricultural states but less climate search in more agricultural ones.

### Implications
Experiential learning about climate may be contingent on economic structure. Climate communication and adaptation policy cannot assume that exposure itself produces awareness, especially in agrarian settings.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
Yes, but it is diluted by over-exposition and by too much emphasis on empirical machinery relative to the story. The paper does have a story. It is not merely a pile of results. But it keeps interrupting its own narrative to litigate inference details and to introduce side plots—Bartik IV, power calculations, timing diagnostics—that do not strengthen the central arc.

The story it should be telling is:

1. We think experience teaches people about climate.
2. That may be wrong where weather is economically immediate.
3. India lets us test that because agricultural dependence varies sharply across states.
4. The same heat shock appears to raise climate attention in low-agriculture places and suppress it in high-agriculture places.
5. Therefore, the climate-attention consequences of weather depend on economic structure.

That is clean. The current paper is this story plus too much scaffolding.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?
“I’d lead with: in India, hotter-than-usual months appear to increase climate-related searches in less agricultural states but reduce them in more agricultural states.”

That is the memorable fact.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
Some would lean in. The idea is intuitively interesting and counter to a familiar narrative. But they would lean in only if it is presented as a conceptual claim about how economic structure mediates belief formation. If presented as “Google Trends estimates in 22 Indian states,” many will reach for their phones.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Almost immediately: **“Do people in agricultural areas pay attention to something else instead?”**  
That is the obvious and important follow-up. The paper does not answer it convincingly enough.

A second follow-up: **“Is this really climate awareness, or just a feature of who is online and what they search for?”**  
Again, that is more a positioning problem than a referee problem. A top-journal story needs a crisper conceptual defense or additional evidence.

### If findings are modest: is the modesty itself interesting?
The paper does a reasonably good job being candid that the main heterogeneity result is suggestive rather than definitive. That honesty is commendable. But from an editorial perspective, this also reveals the strategic issue: if the central result is modest and the outcome is narrow, then the story must be exceptionally sharp. At present it is not yet sharp enough to carry modest evidence all the way to AER-level excitement.

This does not feel like a failed experiment. It feels like an interesting paper whose best idea is larger than its current evidence base.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

At the moment, the gap is a mix of **framing problem**, **scope problem**, and **ambition problem**.

- **Framing problem:** The paper’s core idea is stronger than its current self-presentation. It should be a paper about the limits of experiential learning under economic vulnerability, not a paper about Indian Google searches.
- **Scope problem:** One outcome—English-language climate-related Google searches—is too thin to support the broader claims the paper wants to make.
- **Ambition problem:** The current design is careful but safe. It documents heterogeneity; it does not fully reveal mechanism or consequences.

I do **not** think the main issue is novelty in the narrow sense. The idea is novel enough. The issue is that the evidence package is not yet broad or deep enough for the claim the author wants to make at AER level.

### The single most impactful piece of advice
**Show what attention shifts toward in agricultural places during weather shocks, not just what it shifts away from.**

That is the key. If the author can demonstrate that heat shocks in agricultural states reduce climate searches while increasing searches related to crop losses, weather forecasts, irrigation, input prices, or relief programs, the paper becomes much more than a heterogeneity result. It becomes a convincing paper about the allocation of attention under climate risk. That would materially upgrade both mechanism and importance.
