## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple, important question: when people experience unusual weather, does that make them pay more attention to climate change—or less? Using Indian state-level Google searches matched to weather data, the paper argues that the answer depends on economic structure: hotter-than-usual months increase climate attention in less agricultural states, but not in heavily agricultural ones, where weather may be processed primarily as an income shock rather than a climate signal.

A busy economist should care because the paper is trying to overturn a tacit generalization from the rich-country literature: the “experience of weather raises climate concern” result may be a luxury-economy phenomenon. If true, that matters for how we think about belief formation, adaptation, and political support for climate policy in the places most exposed to climate risk.

**Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Not quite. The current introduction is intelligent and informed, but it takes too long to get to the real novelty. It opens with India’s exposure and then reviews the U.S./Europe literature before clearly stating the paper’s central claim. The first two paragraphs should do less scene-setting and more staking out the surprise: **the people most economically exposed to weather may be the least likely to translate that experience into climate attention.**

### The pitch the paper should have

“Do extreme weather events make people more attentive to climate change? Much of the evidence from rich democracies suggests yes: unusual heat temporarily raises climate concern. But that evidence comes from economies where weather is mostly an amenity shock, not a livelihood shock. In places where weather directly threatens income—especially in agricultural economies—the same event may crowd out climate attention rather than heighten it.

This paper studies that possibility in India, combining state-month weather anomalies with Google search measures of climate attention from 2004–2023. I show that hotter-than-usual months are associated with more climate-related searching in less agricultural states, but with less searching in more agricultural ones. The broader point is that the effect of lived weather experience on climate awareness depends on how economies mediate that experience.”

That is the paper’s AER-relevant idea. It should arrive immediately.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:**  
The paper argues that the effect of extreme weather on climate attention is not universal but depends on economic structure, with agricultural exposure weakening or reversing the usual positive weather-to-climate-attention relationship.

### Is this clearly differentiated from the closest papers?
Only partially. The paper cites the standard weather-and-beliefs papers and says, in essence, “I do this in India and show heterogeneity by agriculture.” That is not yet sharp enough. The differentiation should be:

1. **Not just another setting**: this is not “the same question in India.”  
2. **Not just heterogeneity for heterogeneity’s sake**: the key claim is that **economic exposure changes the sign and interpretation of experiential learning.**  
3. **Not just attention rather than beliefs**: the paper is really about the conditions under which weather is encoded as a climate signal versus an immediate livelihood shock.

Right now, a smart economist might summarize it as: “It’s a Google Trends/weather paper for India with an agriculture interaction.” That is not fatal, but it is too easy to say and too diminishing.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a gap in a literature?
Mixed, but too often as literature-filling. The stronger framing is about the world:

- **World question:** When do people interpret weather as evidence about climate, and when do they interpret it as just a shock to manage?
- **Literature-gap framing:** We don’t know this outside rich democracies.

The paper has both, but it leans too heavily on the latter. AER papers usually need the former.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the intro?
Yes, but not crisply enough. They would likely say:  
“Interesting—weather seems to increase climate attention in urban/non-agricultural places but not in agricultural India.”  
That’s decent. But too many readers would still come away with:  
“Another reduced-form paper linking weather to search behavior.”

### What would make this contribution bigger?
A few concrete ways:

1. **Make the conceptual object bigger than India.**  
   The real contribution is about **economic mediation of experiential learning**, not India per se. India should be the laboratory, not the headline.

2. **Connect more directly to behavior or policy demand.**  
   Climate searches are fine as a measure of salience, but the contribution becomes bigger if the paper more explicitly links this to:
   - support for climate policy,
   - adaptation investments,
   - media consumption,
   - demand for weather insurance / drought relief / agricultural coping.

   Even if those outcomes are not available here, the framing should say why climate attention is an economically meaningful margin.

3. **Sharpen mechanism comparison.**  
   The paper currently offers several mechanisms—cognitive framing, finite pool of worry, opportunity cost of attention, information environment. That weakens the contribution. It needs one central mechanism and the others as alternatives. Right now the paper sounds somewhat unsure what its own finding means.

4. **Broaden the comparison beyond “agriculture.”**  
   If the paper can frame agricultural dependence as a stand-in for a broader concept—whether weather arrives as a **signal** or an **income shock**—the contribution becomes much bigger and more generalizable.

That is the conversation AER readers are more likely to care about.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Prior work, mainly in rich democracies, finds that unusual weather temporarily raises attention to or belief in climate change. This has encouraged a broader intuition that lived exposure can generate climate awareness.

### Tension
But that intuition may fail precisely where climate risk is most economically consequential. In agricultural economies, extreme weather may be experienced less as “evidence about the planet” and more as “today’s income disaster.” So the core puzzle is: **why wouldn’t the most exposed populations become the most climate-aware?**

### Resolution
In India, temperature anomalies are associated with higher climate search interest in less agricultural states but lower search interest in more agricultural states. The paper interprets this as evidence that economic immediacy can crowd out abstract climate attention.

### Implications
Climate experience does not mechanically translate into climate awareness; that translation depends on economic structure. Therefore, communication strategies and theories of adaptation that rely on experiential learning may travel poorly to vulnerable agrarian settings.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
It has the ingredients, but the arc is not yet disciplined. The paper currently feels like it has:

- one promising central result,
- several auxiliary exercises,
- many words trying to elevate them into a bigger story.

At times it reads like a collection of plausible interpretations around a suggestive interaction result. The narrative should be simpler and more forceful:

1. Rich-country evidence says weather raises climate attention.  
2. That may fail where weather is an income shock.  
3. India lets us test that.  
4. The sign flips with agricultural dependence.  
5. Therefore, experiential climate learning is conditional on economic structure.

That’s the story. The paper should stop trying to do more than that.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The gap is mostly **framing plus ambition**, with a secondary issue of novelty.

### Is it a framing problem?
Yes, significantly. The science may be competent, but the current framing undersells and overcomplicates the contribution. The paper has a potentially strong idea but presents it too much as a setting-specific application and too little as a general proposition about belief formation under economic stress.

### Is it a scope problem?
Also yes. The paper is built around one main interaction in one proxy outcome. That can work if the idea is huge and the evidence is very clean. Here, because the outcome is somewhat indirect and the estimates are presented as suggestive, the paper needs either:
- a stronger conceptual framing, or
- broader outcome evidence showing that this is not just a quirk of Google searches.

### Is it a novelty problem?
Somewhat. Weather-and-beliefs papers, Google Trends papers, and agriculture-weather papers are all well-trodden genres. What is novel here is the **sign-reversing heterogeneity by economic structure**. The paper must keep the spotlight there. Any drift away from that makes it feel derivative.

### Is it an ambition problem?
Yes. The paper is smart but safe. It often sounds like it is trying to prove it belongs by being thorough rather than by making a bold claim cleanly. AER papers typically need one memorable claim pursued with conviction. This paper has one available, but it is still being handled cautiously and diffusely.

### Single most impactful advice
**Reframe the paper around a general economic proposition—weather changes climate attention only when it is experienced as a signal rather than as a livelihood shock—and make India the test case, not the contribution.**

That one change would do the most to move this toward AER territory.
