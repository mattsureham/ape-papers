## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper uses a unique series of tax hikes in Singapore—culminating in an unprecedented 60% stamp duty on foreign buyers—to estimate the causal effect of capital controls on housing. By comparing market segments with different levels of foreign exposure, it finds that while prices and volumes collapse, rents remain relatively stable. This suggests that foreign demand is driven by an "ownership premium" (wealth storage/residency) rather than a search for rental yields, meaning these taxes can cool housing markets without hurting renters.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this clearly. The first two paragraphs effectively bridge the "shock" of the 60% rate with the underlying economic mechanism (ownership vs. rental displacement).

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution:** The paper provides the first empirical "dose-response" curve for extreme capital controls in housing, demonstrating that foreign-buyer taxes primarily suppress an "ownership premium" rather than displacing demand into the rental market.

*   **Differentiation:** It moves significantly beyond Deng et al. (2018) by including the 60% "shock" and using modern staggered DiD methods. It differentiates itself from the broader stamp duty literature (Best & Kleven) by focusing on the *nationality wedge* rather than general transaction costs.
*   **Question vs. Literature:** It frames itself as a question about the world (Do these taxes work and who do they hurt?), which is strong.
*   **Clarity:** A smart economist would say: "It’s the Singapore paper that proves taxing foreign owners doesn't just push them into the rental market."
*   **Bigger Contribution:** To make this even bigger, the author should explore the *composition* of the "ownership premium." Is it money laundering (safe haven), or is it a "foot in the door" for immigration? Even a suggestive analysis of where this capital flows *next* would elevate the paper.

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

*   **Neighbors:** Favilukis & Van Nieuwerburgh (2021) on out-of-town buyers; Badarinza & Ramadorai (2019) on foreign demand in London; Best & Kleven (2018) on transaction taxes.
*   **Positioning:** It builds on the "Safe Haven" literature but acts as an empirical "stress test" for these theories using an extreme policy intervention.
*   **Narrow vs. Broad:** Currently slightly too focused on Singapore institutional details. It needs to speak more to the **International Macro/Finance** literature on capital controls.
*   **Missing Conversations:** The paper should engage more with the **Asset Pricing** literature regarding "convenience yields" or "safe asset" demand for real estate. This would move it from a "Regional/Urban" paper to a "General Interest" paper.

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

*   **Setup:** Global cities are facing housing crises; policymakers blame foreign "hot money" and implement taxes.
*   **Tension:** Critics argue these taxes are "leaky"—foreigners will just rent instead, keeping total demand high and pushing up rents for locals.
*   **Resolution:** Singapore's 60% tax acts as a "natural experiment" that proves the "leaky" theory wrong. The "ownership premium" is the primary driver.
*   **Implications:** High-intensity taxes are an effective tool for decoupling local rental markets from global capital flows.

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong and linear. It follows the "tension-resolution" model well.

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

*   **The Lead Fact:** "Singapore taxed foreigners at 60%, prices fell 20%, but rents barely moved."
*   **The Reaction:** People will lean in. The 60% figure is "eye-popping" for a developed economy.
*   **Follow-up:** "Where did the money go? Did it go to Dubai or London instead?" (The "leakage" to other cities is the next logical step).

---

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

*   **Front-loading:** The paper is well-structured. However, the HDB placebo (Section 5.4) is a "killer" result that proves no spillovers; it should be mentioned earlier or given more prominence in the results tables.
*   **Appendix:** The "Standardized Effect Sizes" (Table 6) are very helpful for cross-study comparison and should stay. 
*   **Section 2:** The institutional background is a bit dense. It could be streamlined to focus only on the nationality-based wedge to get to the identification faster.

---

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The gap is **Ambition/Generalizability**. In its current form, it is an excellent "Applied Micro" paper on a specific policy. To be AER, it needs to be a paper about **Global Capital Flows and the Nature of Real Estate as an Asset Class.**

**The single most impactful piece of advice:** Frame the "ownership premium" not just as a residual finding, but as a theoretical contribution—develop a simple model (or extend an existing one) that shows why an "ownership-only" tax reveals the underlying "safe-haven" value of the asset vs. its "service-flow" value.
