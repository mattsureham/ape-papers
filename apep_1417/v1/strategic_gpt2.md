## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple and policy-relevant question: when a city heavily taxes foreign homebuyers, does that actually cool housing markets, or does demand just spill over from buying into renting? Using Singapore’s repeated increases in its foreign-buyer stamp duty—culminating in an extraordinary 60% rate—the paper argues that high-foreign-exposure housing segments saw large declines in prices and transactions, while rents moved much less, suggesting the policy reduced ownership demand rather than merely reallocating it across tenure margins.

A busy economist should care because foreign-buyer taxes are now a prominent policy tool in global superstar cities, but we still do not have much convincing evidence on their equilibrium effects or on the basic mechanism: are these policies effective restraints on foreign capital in housing, or mostly symbolic?

### Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Partly, but not well enough. The current opening is vivid and readable, but it becomes methodological too quickly and slightly oversells the design before the reader has fully understood the economic question. The best version of this paper is not “I exploit five staggered hikes with differential exposure”; it is “Here is a rare policy laboratory for a question many cities now face.”

The first two paragraphs should do less scene-setting about the tax arithmetic and less immediate discussion of treatment/control groups, and more to establish:
1. why foreign-buyer taxes are economically important beyond Singapore,
2. what the unresolved question is,
3. why Singapore is an unusually informative setting, and
4. what the headline finding means for policy.

### The pitch the paper should have

“Cities from Vancouver to Hong Kong to Singapore have turned to foreign-buyer taxes to cool housing markets, but economists still know remarkably little about whether these taxes actually reduce housing demand or simply push would-be buyers into rental markets. Singapore provides an unusually sharp test because it repeatedly increased its foreign-buyer stamp duty over more than a decade, eventually reaching 60 percent—the highest rate in any developed economy.

This paper studies how those tax increases affected prices, rents, and transactions across housing segments with different exposure to foreign buyers. I find that the high-exposure segment experienced large declines in prices and transactions, while rents moved much less, implying that foreign-buyer taxes primarily compress demand for ownership rather than merely displacing it into rental demand. The broader implication is that these taxes can materially segment housing markets, but they do so by taxing an ownership-specific motive, not by broadly reducing housing consumption.”

That is the paper’s best AER-facing pitch.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper’s contribution is to provide evidence, from Singapore’s repeated increases in foreign-buyer taxes, that taxing foreign purchasers depresses housing prices and transactions in foreign-exposed segments much more than rents, implying that these policies target an ownership-specific demand margin rather than simply shifting demand into rental markets.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?

Not clearly enough. The paper gestures at differentiation, but the distinctions are still too much of the form “existing paper used earlier data; I extend the sample and use better methods.” That is almost never a strong enough top-journal contribution on its own.

The closest neighboring contributions appear to be:
- prior Singapore ABSD work such as Deng (as cited here),
- housing transaction tax papers like Kopczuk and Munroe, Best and Kleven, Hilber and Lyytikäinen,
- papers on foreign capital and local housing markets such as Badarinza and Ramadorai, Favilukis et al., and related work on global capital in superstar cities.

Relative to those papers, the paper needs to say much more crisply:
- transaction tax papers mostly study broad transaction wedges or domestic buyers,
- foreign-capital papers often study inflows and price pressure, not foreign-buyer surtaxes,
- this paper’s distinct value is not just “another tax effect,” but “evidence on the mechanism by which nationality-contingent housing taxes work.”

That mechanism claim is the real contribution. Right now it is present, but it is not yet framed as the center of gravity.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or as filling a gap in a LITERATURE?

Mixed, but too often as filling a literature gap. The stronger framing is plainly a world question: when governments restrict foreign capital in housing through buyer-specific taxes, what actually adjusts—ownership, rents, or both?

The introduction should lead with that question about the world. The literature gap can come later.

### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what’s new here?

At present, maybe, but not confidently. They might say: “It’s a DiD paper on Singapore’s foreign-buyer stamp duty showing prices fell more in the foreign-heavy segment.” That is not enough.

You want them to say: “It shows these taxes don’t just reduce transactions; they mostly hit the ownership margin, with much weaker rental spillovers, which is exactly the unresolved policy question in cities trying to tax foreign demand.”

That is a better, more memorable takeaway.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Most importantly: sharpen the mechanism and broaden the economic stakes.

Specific ways to make it bigger:
- **Different framing:** Shift from “effect of ABSD in Singapore” to “what foreign-buyer taxes do in segmented urban housing markets.”
- **Different outcome emphasis:** The most interesting comparison is not only prices and transactions, but ownership versus rental margins. That should be the main contribution, not a secondary asymmetry.
- **Different mechanism:** The paper currently advances the “ownership premium” idea, but this is underdeveloped. If the author can connect this to theories of safe-asset demand, residency value, wealth parking, or option value of ownership, the contribution becomes conceptually richer.
- **Different comparison:** Position Singapore as the extreme case in a policy class used globally. The 60% rate is valuable not just because it is large, but because it lets us learn about nonlinearities and the likely upper bound of policy effectiveness.
- **Different welfare/policy angle:** The paper stops at “it works.” Bigger papers ask, “works for what?” Affordability for locals? Reduced foreign speculation? Lower misallocation? Limited rental spillovers? Even without full welfare analysis, the introduction and conclusion should better define what policy success means.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

### Which 3–5 papers are the closest neighbors?

Based on the paper’s own citations and topic, the closest neighbors are likely:
- **Deng (2018)** on Singapore’s earlier ABSD rounds,
- **Kopczuk and Munroe (2006)** on transaction taxes and housing markets,
- **Best and Kleven (2018)** on housing transaction taxes and behavioral responses,
- **Hilber and Lyytikäinen (2017/2016 in spirit)** on stamp duties and housing market distortions,
- **Badarinza and Ramadorai (2018/related work)** on foreign buyers and local housing markets,
- possibly **Favilukis et al.** on foreign capital and real estate pricing.

If I were advising the author, I would reduce the abstract theory excursion into “price discrimination and segmentation” and more clearly anchor the paper in the intersection of:
1. housing taxation,
2. foreign capital in housing,
3. tenure choice / segmented housing demand.

### How should the paper position itself relative to those neighbors?

Mostly **build on** them, not attack them. The paper does not have the breadth to overturn a literature. Its comparative advantage is that Singapore offers a particularly clean and extreme policy setting.

The right positioning is:
- transaction-tax papers show that housing markets respond to tax wedges;
- foreign-capital papers show that international demand affects local real estate;
- this paper combines those insights by studying a buyer-specific wedge imposed on foreign capital, and, crucially, separates effects on ownership from rental markets.

That is a constructive bridge, not a frontal assault.

### Is the paper currently positioned too narrowly or too broadly?

It is oddly both:
- **Too narrow** in empirical framing: much of the paper reads like a case study of CCR versus OCR/RCR.
- **Too broad** in rhetorical claim: it sometimes sounds as if it has settled how foreign-buyer taxes work generally across world cities.

The fix is to be more disciplined: present Singapore as an informative extreme case with unusually strong policy variation, and make narrower but more credible claims about the mechanism.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of? What fields should it be speaking to?

It should speak more directly to:
- **urban economics** on segmented demand in superstar cities,
- **international macro/finance** on safe-asset demand and cross-border capital allocation,
- **public finance** on discriminatory or targeted taxation,
- **political economy/public policy** on “cooling measures” and the politics of housing affordability.

It may also be underplaying work on:
- nonresident demand in real estate,
- luxury housing as an investment asset,
- tenure substitution and housing services versus asset ownership.

The “ownership premium” language is potentially interesting, but currently a bit ad hoc. It would benefit from being tethered to broader literatures on asset demand, not just housing tax incidence.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?

Not quite. Right now it is having the conversation “Do foreign-buyer taxes reduce prices in Singapore?” That is a second-tier conversation.

The better conversation is: “What margin do foreign-buyer taxes actually operate on in high-end urban housing markets?” That is a more general, more intellectually ambitious question, and it is where the paper has something distinctive to say.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Cities facing housing affordability pressure increasingly worry that foreign capital is bidding up property prices, especially in prime urban neighborhoods. Many have adopted foreign-buyer taxes, but it remains unclear whether these taxes meaningfully restrain housing demand or merely reshuffle it.

### Tension

A foreign-buyer tax could work in at least two very different ways:
- it could reduce overall housing demand from foreigners, lowering prices and activity;
- or it could simply reallocate demand from owning to renting, in which case ownership prices might fall while rental pressure rises.

Without distinguishing these channels, we do not know whether such taxes actually cool housing markets or just move the pressure elsewhere.

### Resolution

Using Singapore’s unusually large and repeated foreign-buyer tax hikes, the paper finds large declines in prices and transactions in the foreign-exposed segment, but much smaller rental responses. The central interpretation is that the policy mainly taxes a motive tied specifically to ownership.

### Implications

If true, this matters for policymakers. It suggests foreign-buyer taxes are not purely cosmetic and need not simply worsen rental-market pressure. More broadly, it implies that at least part of foreign housing demand reflects an ownership-specific valuation—wealth storage, status, residency option value, or portfolio motives—rather than demand for housing services per se.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the ingredients, but the arc is not yet clean. Right now the paper often feels like:
1. an interesting policy setting,
2. some reduced-form effects,
3. then a mechanism claim attached afterward.

The mechanism should be the story from the beginning, not a post hoc interpretation.

### What story should it be telling?

Not “Singapore raised a tax five times and prices fell.”

Instead:

“Foreign-buyer taxes are politically popular but economically ambiguous: they may reduce ownership demand, or they may simply redirect it into nearby margins such as rental housing. Singapore’s repeated tax hikes provide a rare chance to distinguish those channels. The evidence indicates that these taxes primarily compress an ownership-specific demand component, with much weaker rental spillovers.”

That is a coherent narrative with setup, tension, resolution, and implications.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?

“Singapore raised its foreign-buyer tax all the way to 60%, and in the most foreign-exposed housing segment prices and transactions fell sharply, but rents barely moved by comparison.”

That is the right lead fact.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

They would lean in—initially. The 60% figure is arresting, and the ownership-versus-rental comparison is genuinely interesting.

But they will only stay engaged if the speaker can quickly answer: “What does this teach us beyond Singapore?” If the answer is just “the tax reduced prices there,” attention will fade.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Almost certainly: “So does this mean foreign buyers mainly value the asset aspect of housing rather than the housing-service flow?” Or alternatively: “Why didn’t those buyers show up in rents?”

That is exactly the question the paper should be designed around.

### If the findings are modest or null, is that interesting?

The findings are not null, so the paper does not need to sell a null. But the asymmetry result is doing the heavy lifting. The price effect alone is not enough for AER; the mechanism is what upgrades it from a local policy paper into a broader economics paper.

3. **Elevate the mechanism result earlier.**

4. **Trim the literature review in the introduction.**  
   The current three-literature contribution paragraph is competent but generic. It should be shorter and more pointed. The “price discrimination and segmentation literature” section feels especially nonessential in its current form.

5. **Be careful with quantitative claims in the abstract and intro.**  
   There are some inconsistencies across the abstract and main text on the magnitude of cumulative effects. Even apart from technical evaluation, that weakens narrative confidence and professionalism.

6. **Front-load the global stakes.**  
   Vancouver, Hong Kong, London, Auckland, and other cases should appear sooner as motivation, not just in the contribution or conclusion.

7. **Rethink the conclusion.**  
   The conclusion currently overgeneralizes from Singapore to a set of policy prescriptions. It should do less “rule of thumb” rhetoric and more disciplined interpretation of what this setting can and cannot tell us.

### Are there results buried in robustness that should be in the main results?

Yes. The **price-versus-rent comparison** is the main result, not a robustness check. The triple-difference framing, or at least the comparative margin result, should be promoted into the main results section and better integrated into the introduction.

The HDB placebo is useful but not central; it can remain later.

### Is the conclusion adding value?

Some, but less than it should. It mostly summarizes and then leaps to broad policy advice. A better conclusion would:
- restate the core economic lesson about ownership-specific demand,
- discuss external validity more carefully,
- explain what this implies for how economists should think about foreign demand in housing markets.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

At present, the gap is mostly a **framing and ambition problem**, with a secondary **novelty problem**.

The science may be serviceable, but the current version still reads too much like a polished policy evaluation of one country’s tax change. For AER, it needs to feel like it is answering a first-order economic question that happens to be studied in Singapore, not studying Singapore for its own sake.

### Is it a framing problem?

Yes, strongly. The paper’s best idea—the distinction between reducing ownership demand and displacing demand into rents—is not yet carrying the manuscript.

### Is it a scope problem?

Somewhat. The paper has prices, rents, and transactions, which is already decent scope. The issue is less missing outcomes than underexploited interpretation. It needs to do more with the outcomes it already has.

### Is it a novelty problem?

Moderately. “Tax goes up, prices go down” is not novel enough. “Foreign-buyer taxes primarily hit an ownership-specific margin rather than housing-service demand” is much more novel.

### Is it an ambition problem?

Yes. The paper is competent but safe. It presents a useful case study and then gestures toward larger significance. An AER paper would reverse that order: start with the big question, use Singapore as a powerful test case, and drive home the conceptual lesson.

### Single most impactful piece of advice

If the author changes only one thing, it should be this:

**Rewrite the paper around the mechanism question—whether foreign-buyer taxes reduce total housing demand or merely shift demand from ownership into renting—and make the price-versus-rent asymmetry the paper’s central contribution rather than a supporting result.**
