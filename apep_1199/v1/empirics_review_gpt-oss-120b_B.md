**1. Idea Fidelity**  
The manuscript follows the original manifest closely. It uses the three‑wave, staggered privatization of water‑ and sanitation‑service providers that resulted from Brazil’s 2020 Marco Legal do Saneamento, draws treatment timing from SN IS provider‑jurisdiction data, and links those municipalities to the DATASUS hospitalisation database (ICD‑10 A00‑A09). The empirical strategy is a Callaway‑Sant’Anna staggered DiD, exactly as prescribed. The paper also discusses the same mechanisms (investment obligations, service‑quality standards) and highlights heterogeneity across concession blocks. No major element of the proposed design (e.g., the use of under‑5 outcomes, the focus on 300+ treated municipalities, the inclusion of investment and coverage controls) is omitted.

**2. Summary**  
The paper evaluates Brazil’s large‑scale sanitation‑privatization reform (525 municipalities) by estimating its impact on municipal rates of water‑borne disease hospitalisations. Using a Callaway‑Sant’Anna staggered DiD on 2014‑2023 DATASUS data, the author finds essentially zero aggregate effect but uncovers sizable, opposite‑signed effects for the three concession waves: a large reduction in the poorest Alagoas block, an increase in the Rio de Janeiro block, and an ambiguous effect for the massive Rio Grande do Sul (Corsan) block.

**3. Essential Points**  

1. **Failure of the Parallel‑Trends Assumption** – The event‑study (Table 4) shows significant pre‑treatment differences at t‑5 and t‑3, and the formal test rejects parallel trends (p = 0.00). This undermines the core identifying assumption of the Callaway‑Sant’Anna estimator, casting doubt on the causal interpretation of *all* ATT estimates, including the heterogeneous wave‑specific effects. The paper acknowledges the problem but does not provide a credible strategy to address it.  

2. **Limited Post‑Treatment Observations and Heterogeneous Timing** – The Corsan wave (the bulk of treated units) provides only one post‑treatment year (2023), while the Alagoas and CEDAE waves have at most two. Short horizons make it impossible to distinguish temporary transition shocks from longer‑run health impacts, especially when the contracts commit to multi‑decade investment. The heterogeneity claims therefore rely on very sparse post‑treatment data.  

3. **Specification Sensitivity and Mixed Inference** – Results change sign and significance across estimators (CS vs. TWFE) and functional forms (winsorised rates, log rates). The log‑rate specification even yields a significant *positive* effect, suggesting that the finding of “no aggregate effect” is not robust. The paper needs to clarify which specification is the primary one and why it should be preferred.  

Given the gravity of the parallel‑trend violation, the manuscript cannot presently claim credible causal estimates. I therefore **recommend rejection** until the authors either (a) provide a convincing alternative identification strategy that restores plausibility (e.g., synthetic‑control methods, instrumental variables, or a design that restricts the sample to units with demonstrably parallel pre‑trends), or (b) conduct a rigorous sensitivity analysis (e.g., HonestDiD, bounding approaches) that quantifies how large a trend violation would have to be to overturn the main conclusions.
