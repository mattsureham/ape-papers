## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a big policy question: when governments privatize water and sanitation systems, do public health outcomes improve? Using Brazil’s massive post-2020 sanitation reform, the paper argues that privatization has no average effect on waterborne-disease hospitalizations, but that the average masks sharp heterogeneity: health improves in places with very poor baseline sanitation and may worsen, at least initially, in more complex or better-served systems.

A busy economist should care because this is not really a Brazil paper; it is a paper about whether ownership reform in essential infrastructure improves welfare, and under what conditions. That is a first-order question for development, public economics, political economy, and the economics of regulation.

### Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Not quite. The current opening is competent but too generic: it starts with the global burden of inadequate water and sanitation, then moves into a familiar public-vs-private setup, then reviews Argentina and some broad privatization debates. It takes too long to get to the actual hook, which is that Brazil just ran an unusually large, recent, policy-relevant privatization wave that offers a chance to revisit a classic question in a new institutional setting.

More importantly, the paper’s own strongest idea is not “another estimate of privatization effects,” but “ownership reform in infrastructure is context-dependent, and average effects are the wrong estimand for policy.” That idea appears, but too late.

### What the first two paragraphs should say instead

Brazil has launched one of the largest recent privatization waves in water and sanitation, transferring service in more than 500 municipalities to private operators under its 2020 sanitation reform. This creates a rare opportunity to study a classic economics question in a modern setting: does privatizing essential infrastructure improve health, or do the gains depend on where reform occurs?

This paper studies the reform’s effect on hospitalizations for waterborne disease. The central finding is not a simple pro- or anti-privatization verdict: average effects are near zero, but the effects vary sharply across concession environments—large improvements in places with severe baseline sanitation deficits, and no gains or short-run worsening in more complex urban settings. The paper’s broader claim is that the health consequences of privatization are not an intrinsic feature of private ownership itself, but of the baseline failures the reform is asked to solve.

That is the pitch the paper should lead with.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### Contribution in one sentence

The paper’s contribution is to use Brazil’s 2020 sanitation privatization wave to argue that the health effects of water-sector privatization are highly heterogeneous and depend on baseline service deficits, rather than being uniformly positive or negative.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3-4 papers?

Only partly. The paper differentiates itself most clearly from **Galiani, Gertler, and Schargrodsky (2005)** by saying: different country, larger scale, different institutional generation of privatization, and heterogeneity rather than a single average effect. That is a real distinction.

But relative to the broader infrastructure privatization literature, the differentiation is still fuzzy. Right now the reader can easily summarize it as: “a recent DiD paper on Brazil finds mixed effects.” That is not enough. The paper needs a crisper statement of what is new conceptually, not just empirically.

The strongest differentiator available is:

- prior work mostly asks whether privatization improved outcomes on average;
- this paper argues that **the policy-relevant object is how effects vary with baseline public-sector failure**.

That could be a meaningful contribution if framed as a general lesson rather than a descriptive afterthought.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or filling a gap in a LITERATURE?

It straddles both, but too much of the rhetoric is still “first evidence on Brazil’s reform” and “largest wave in history.” Those are useful facts, but they are literature-gap or setting-gap claims. The stronger framing is a world question:

**When does privatization of essential local infrastructure improve welfare?**

That is better than:

**There is no paper yet on Brazil’s 2020 sanitation reform.**

AER wants the first question.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?

At present, maybe, but not confidently. A smart economist would probably say: “It studies Brazil’s sanitation privatization and finds a null average effect with some heterogeneity across waves.” That is not yet a memorable contribution. The introduction needs to make the heterogeneity result feel like the result, not a decomposition after the null.

Right now it still reads too much like “another DiD paper about privatization and health,” albeit with an admirably honest discussion of limitations.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Several concrete possibilities:

1. **Reframe around allocative policy design, not ownership ideology.**  
   Instead of “does privatization work,” ask “where is privatization most likely to generate large welfare gains?” That makes the paper about targeting reform, not adjudicating a tired public-vs-private debate.

2. **Tie heterogeneity to observable baseline conditions more systematically.**  
   The current wave-by-wave story is suggestive, but it would be much bigger if the paper showed a continuous relationship between pre-reform sanitation deficits and estimated effects. Then the paper becomes a general proposition, not a three-wave anecdote.

3. **Bring in outcomes closer to the mechanism or broader welfare.**  
   If the paper could connect health effects to actual changes in water coverage, sewage treatment, continuity of service, water quality, or outages, the heterogeneity story would become much more convincing and more interesting. As written, the paper jumps from ownership change to hospitalizations with little intermediate evidence.

4. **Use the reform to speak to transition costs versus long-run gains.**  
   The Rio result is potentially interesting if framed as a transition-disruption story. But to matter, the paper would need to distinguish short-run handover effects from medium-run infrastructure effects.

If the author could only enlarge the paper one way, I would choose baseline-deficit heterogeneity tied to concrete infrastructure measures.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

### Closest neighbors

The obvious neighbors are:

1. **Galiani, Gertler, and Schargrodsky (2005, JPE/AER-adjacent classic depending citation memory)** on water privatization and child mortality in Argentina.  
2. **Estache, Foster, and Wodon (2002/2005-era work)** on infrastructure reform, privatization, and distributional/welfare consequences.  
3. **Shirley and Megginson / Megginson and Netter** survey-style privatization literature on whether ownership improves efficiency.  
4. **Davis, various work on water privatization failures and mixed evidence in Latin America**, especially Bolivia/Cochabamba-adjacent debates.  
5. Possibly **Andrés, Guasch, Foster, Straub** or related infrastructure concession literature in Latin America.

Also, methodologically adjacent though not substantively central, the paper situates near the recent treatment-effects literature. But that should not be the front-facing conversation.

### How should the paper position itself relative to those neighbors?

**Build on and reinterpret**, not attack.

It should say:

- Galiani et al. showed that privatization can improve health in a setting with severe deficits.
- Later debates emphasized that privatization’s record is mixed.
- Brazil’s reform lets us revisit that question in a contemporary setting and ask whether those mixed findings reflect real treatment-effect heterogeneity rather than mere empirical disagreement.

That is a useful synthesis. The current draft edges toward this, but not sharply enough.

### Is the paper currently positioned too narrowly or too broadly?

A bit of both, oddly.

- **Too broadly** in its opening rhetoric: “one of the most contested questions in development economics,” “fundamental tensions between market efficiency and equitable access.” That is generic and grand.
- **Too narrowly** in its operational framing: a paper about one law, three concession waves, and one health outcome.

The right positioning is narrower than “the great privatization debate” but broader than “Brazil sanitation reform.” It should be:

**A paper on when private provision of essential infrastructure delivers health gains, using Brazil as a modern test case.**

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of, or under-engaged with?

A few things:

1. **State capacity / public service delivery literature.**  
   The real conceptual competitor to “private ownership” is often “public capacity.” The paper should engage more with the idea that ownership may matter less than baseline state failure and contractual enforceability.

2. **Infrastructure and health production literature.**  
   The paper should speak more directly to work on water access, sanitation, child health, and environmental health—not just privatization papers. The current literature review is ownership-heavy.

3. **Political economy of privatization and regulation.**  
   The institutional success of private concessions depends on contract design, regulation, enforcement, and local politics. If the paper wants to explain heterogeneity, this literature matters.

4. **Urban economics / network infrastructure transition literature.**  
   The Rio result might be best understood through complexity, network coordination, and transition frictions in dense urban systems. That is potentially a more interesting conversation than simple “wealthier places benefit less.”

### Is the paper having the right conversation?

Not quite yet. Right now it is having the old “privatization: good or bad?” conversation. That conversation is tired, ideological, and hard to win.

The more promising conversation is:

**How should governments target and design ownership reform in network industries when baseline service quality and implementation capacity differ dramatically across places?**

That is more modern, more policy-relevant, and more AER-worthy.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Governments around the world struggle to provide water and sanitation, especially where public utilities are underinvested and poorly managed. Privatization has long been proposed as a solution, but the evidence is mixed and heavily shaped by older Latin American episodes.

### Tension

The core puzzle is that the literature offers conflicting answers: some privatizations seem to improve health substantially; others disappoint or backfire. Is that because the evidence is noisy and context-specific, or because average effects conceal systematic heterogeneity by baseline conditions?

### Resolution

Brazil’s 2020 sanitation reform provides a new test case, and the paper finds that the average health effect is essentially zero, but with sharply different patterns across concession environments—improvements in a high-deficit setting and no gains or deterioration in more urban/complex settings.

### Implications

Economists and policymakers should stop asking whether privatization “works” in the abstract. The more relevant question is where ownership reform is likely to generate high returns, where transition costs are likely to dominate, and how regulatory and baseline conditions shape outcomes.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the ingredients, but not the discipline. At times it feels like two papers awkwardly sharing the same manuscript:

1. a paper that wants to say “there is no aggregate effect”; and  
2. a paper that wants to say “average effects are misleading because effects are context-dependent.”

The second is the better paper. The first is not very exciting on its own.

The manuscript also spends a great deal of energy foregrounding its own limitations. Honesty is good, but the narrative starts to collapse under repeated caveats. The reader comes away thinking less “important new perspective on privatization” and more “mixed evidence from a design that may not support strong claims.” That may be true scientifically, but strategically it is not how to frame an AER ambition paper.

### What story should it be telling?

The story should be:

- Privatization of essential infrastructure is not a uniform treatment.
- Brazil’s reform reveals that effects differ systematically with the severity and nature of pre-reform failures.
- Therefore the right unit of analysis is not “public versus private” in the abstract, but “which institutional arrangement solves which service-delivery problem in which setting.”

That story is much more intellectually durable.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?

I would lead with:

**Brazil privatized water and sanitation in more than 500 municipalities, and the average health effect is basically zero—but that average hides a large improvement in one setting and a deterioration in another.**

That is the one sentence likely to get attention.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

They would lean in briefly, but only if the next sentence is strong. If the next sentence is “we use staggered DiD on hospitalization data,” they will reach for their phones. If the next sentence is “the lesson is that privatization pays where public systems have catastrophically failed, not where systems are already functional or transitions are complex,” then they may stay engaged.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Immediately:

**What explains the heterogeneity? Is it baseline sanitation deficits, urban complexity, regulatory quality, or transition disruption?**

That is the right follow-up question, and currently the paper does not answer it strongly enough. It offers plausible stories, but not enough evidence to elevate those stories from interpretation to contribution.

### If findings are null or modest, is the null itself interesting?

A plain null would not be very interesting here. “Privatization has no average effect” is not enough for AER; it feels like another mixed-infrastructure paper.

What rescues the paper is the heterogeneity claim. But that means the manuscript cannot present the null as the headline and the heterogeneity as a side note. The heterogeneity is the reason the paper is potentially publishable in a top field journal; otherwise it risks reading like a failed attempt to replicate the classic Argentina result in a new country.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

Be honest: in current form, this is not close to AER. The main issue is not just framing. There is a more fundamental mismatch between the paper’s ambition and the strength of the substantive payoff as currently delivered.

### What is the gap?

Mostly:

- **A scope problem:** it has one downstream outcome and too little evidence on mechanism or generalizable sources of heterogeneity.
- **An ambition problem:** it settles too quickly for “mixed effects in Brazil” rather than turning Brazil into a laboratory for a broader theory of where privatization works.
- To some degree also a **novelty problem:** the question “does water privatization improve health?” is not new. The only way past that is to make the heterogeneity lesson genuinely general and policy-design relevant.

### What would excite the top 10 people in this field?

Not another reduced-form estimate of a privatization episode. What would excite them is something like:

**A convincing demonstration that the welfare effects of privatizing local network infrastructure are systematically increasing in baseline service failure, with evidence on the mechanisms through which that happens.**

That would be a real paper. Right now, the manuscript gestures in that direction, but does not yet deliver it.

### Single most impactful piece of advice

**Rebuild the paper around a single claim: average effects are the wrong object, and the economically meaningful result is that privatization’s health impact depends on pre-reform service deficits—then bring in direct evidence that this, rather than wave-specific idiosyncrasy, is what the Brazil data are showing.**

That is the one change that would most improve its strategic position.
