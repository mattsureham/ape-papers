## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
This paper examines how a major Supreme Court decision (*Alice v. CLS Bank*) created massive, localized shocks to patent eligibility enforcement within the USPTO. It shows that even within the same administrative unit, identical legal changes can create "eligibility traps" for specific technologies (e.g., finance) while leaving others (e.g., databases) untouched. More importantly, it documents a "substitution effect" where examiners use high-threshold eligibility rejections to replace more labor-intensive prior-art analysis.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this pitch fairly well in the first two paragraphs, though it leans heavily on the legal name of the case. 
**The pitch the paper should have:** "When the Supreme Court changes a legal standard, we assume it applies broadly to an industry. This paper shows that the reality is a patchwork of 'eligibility traps' where specific sub-technologies see rejection rates jump 50% while neighbors remain unaffected. By looking inside the 'black box' of the patent office, I show that these legal shocks don't just change *who* gets a patent, but *how* examiners work, leading them to swap rigorous prior-art analysis for easier, doctrine-based rejections."

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**The Contribution:** The paper identifies extreme within-technology-center heterogeneity in legal shocks and uncovers a behavioral substitution effect between different types of patent rejections.

- **Differentiation:** It moves past Caskurlu et al. (2022) and Galasso & Schankerman (2018) by moving from the industry/firm level to the "art unit" (the actual group of examiners).
- **Framing:** It is currently framed as answering a question about the **WORLD** (how judicial doctrine creates pockets of prohibition), which is a strength.
- **Clarity:** A smart economist would say: "It's the paper that shows Alice didn't hit software evenly; it created specific dead zones and allowed examiners to take the 'easy' way out on rejections."
- **How to make it bigger:** The contribution would be much larger if the author linked these "traps" to **firm-level behavior**. Do firms stop innovating in "trap" units, or do they just change their filing language?

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
- **Closest Neighbors:** Jaffe & Lerner (2004) on patent office dysfunction; Sampat & Williams (2019) on examiner behavior; Galasso & Schankerman (2015/2018) on patent rights and innovation.
- **Positioning:** The paper builds on Sampat & Williams by using the examiner's decision as the unit of analysis. It should more aggressively position itself as a critique of "broad" innovation policy studies that ignore micro-level implementation.
- **Niche vs. Broad:** It risks looking like a "law and econ" niche paper. To reach the AER audience, it needs to speak more to **Labor/Organizational Economics** (how workers respond to changes in the difficulty of tasks).

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC
- **Setup:** The USPTO is a high-volume gatekeeper of innovation. 
- **Tension:** A "uniform" Supreme Court ruling creates non-uniform results. Why?
- **Resolution:** Because the doctrine is only "proximate" to certain sub-technologies, creating "traps" and changing examiner incentives.
- **Implications:** Policy evaluations of legal shifts that look at the aggregate level are missing the actual mechanism of the shock.

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong, but the "substitution effect" (§101 vs §103) is currently a side result. It should be the climax of the narrative—it's the most "economic" finding in the paper.

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
- **The Dinner Party Fact:** "After the Alice decision, if you filed a patent in financial data processing, you had a 93% chance of a summary rejection, but if you filed in database structures—the office next door—nothing changed."
- **Reaction:** People would lean in. It's a striking example of "postal code" justice in regulation.
- **Follow-up:** "Does this mean people just stopped trying to innovate in finance, or did they just start calling it something else?" (The paper doesn't answer this yet, which is its main weakness).

---

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
- **Front-loading:** Table 3 (the list of art units) is actually the most fascinating part of the paper. It should be visualized as a figure in the introduction to show the "cliff" of rejections.
- **Appendix:** Move the binary DiD robustness to the appendix and lead with the continuous treatment, which better captures the "trap" intensity.
- **Conclusion:** The conclusion is a bit repetitive. It should spend more time on the *normative* question: is this substitution good (faster processing) or bad (lower quality patents)?

---

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The paper is currently a very high-quality *Journal of Law and Economics* or *Research Policy* paper. To make it an **AER** paper, it needs to bridge the gap between **Administrative Outcomes** (rejections) and **Economic Outcomes** (innovation/investment).

**Single Most Impactful Advice:** Link the "Eligibility Trap" to a measure of firm-level response—do the firms that traditionally filed in the "trap" units experience a drop in market value or a shift in their R&D trajectories? If you can show that this examiner-level substitution actually distorted real-world investment, it’s an AER lead article.
