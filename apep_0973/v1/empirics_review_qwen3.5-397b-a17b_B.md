# 2. Summary

This paper investigates whether plastic bag charges, known to reduce retail sales, also reduce environmental pollution using staggered adoption across UK nations and OSPAR beach litter data. The authors employ a Callaway-Sant'Anna difference-in-differences design and find no significant reduction in bag litter, suggesting a "pollution gap" between consumption metrics and environmental outcomes. The study contributes to the literature on Pigouvian tax design by highlighting the limitations of retail-focused interventions for marine pollution.

# 3. Essential Points

1.  **Identification Threat from Failed Placebo:** The most critical issue is the placebo test using plastic bottles (Table 3), which shows a statistically significant positive effect (ATT = 0.997, p < 0.05). Since plastic bottles were not subject to the charge, this suggests the identification strategy is capturing broader compositional changes in beach litter or unobserved common shocks rather than the causal effect of the policy. If untreated items are "increasing" in the model, the null result for bags may be biased upward, or the control group (not-yet-treated nations) may be undergoing divergent trends unrelated to the policy. The authors must address why non-targeted litter responds to the treatment indicator before causal claims can be sustained.

2.  **Statistical Power and Treatment Variation:** The design relies on only four treatment cohorts (the four UK nations) for the staggered DiD estimation. While there are 47 beaches, the effective variation comes from the nation-level policy switches. This severely limits the power to detect moderate effects and makes the Callaway-Sant'Anna estimator sensitive to any single cohort's idiosyncrasies (e.g., Northern Ireland's thin pre-treatment data). The standard errors are large relative to the point estimates, raising the possibility of a Type II error (false null). The authors need to provide minimum detectable effect (MDE) calculations to clarify whether the study can rule out economically meaningful reductions in litter.

3.  **Data Aggregation and Confounding Cleaning Efforts:** Aggregating data to the beach-year level may obscure important variation and introduce noise related to beach cleaning efforts. If local authorities increased beach cleaning frequency concurrently with national bag charges (due to heightened environmental awareness), observed litter counts would fall regardless of the charge's effect on deposition. Conversely, if cleaning budgets were cut, litter would rise. Without controlling for local waste management intensity or cleaning frequency, the estimated effect conflates consumer behavior with municipal maintenance. The authors should discuss whether OSPAR data includes cleaning effort controls or if this omitted variable bias threatens the parallel trends assumption.

# 4. Suggestions

The following recommendations are intended to strengthen the paper's empirical rigor and policy relevance. Addressing these points will significantly improve the manuscript's contribution to the AER: Insights audience.

**Deepen the Investigation into the Placebo Failure**
The significant effect on plastic bottles is the most troubling result in the paper and requires a dedicated discussion or additional analysis. I recommend the authors investigate whether this reflects a general increase in plastic pollution across the North-East Atlantic during the sample period (2001–2020) that correlates with the treatment timing. For instance, if England (the largest cohort) adopted the charge later than others, and plastic pollution generally increased over time in English beaches due to unrelated factors, the DiD would attribute this to the policy. The authors could plot the raw time series of bottle litter vs. bag litter by nation to visually inspect whether the "bottle trend" mirrors the "bag trend." If both are rising in treated areas relative to controls, the identification strategy may be picking up a regional shock (e.g., changes in ocean currents, tourism patterns, or reporting protocols) rather than policy effects. If this cannot be resolved, the authors should temper the causal language, framing the results as "associations consistent with a pollution gap" rather than definitive causal estimates.

**Conduct and Report Power Analysis**
Given the small number of treatment cohorts, readers will rightly question whether the null result is meaningful. I suggest the authors conduct a simulation-based power analysis. Using the observed variance in the data, they should calculate the minimum detectable effect (MDE) at 80% power for their specific design (4 cohorts, 47 clusters). If the MDE is, for example, a 50% reduction in litter, then the study cannot rule out a policy-relevant 20% reduction. Reporting this explicitly would add valuable context: even if the result is null, knowing the precision of that null is crucial for policymakers. Additionally, the authors might consider pooling the data differently or using a synthetic control approach at the nation level (aggregating beaches to nation-year) to see if the results hold when focusing purely on the aggregate time-series variation, which might be cleaner given the policy is national.

**Exploit Survey-Level Variation and Cleaning Controls**
The current analysis aggregates multiple surveys per year into a beach-year mean. However, OSPAR surveys are typically conducted in specific seasons (spring/autumn). If policy adoption dates (e.g., October) fall between survey windows, the "post" period definition might be noisy. I recommend using survey-level data (rather than beach-year) to increase the number of observations and better align treatment timing with actual data collection dates. Furthermore, the authors should check if the OSPAR metadata includes information on beach cleaning frequency. If available, including this as a control variable would help isolate the consumer deposition channel from the municipal removal channel. If unavailable, this should be added to the limitations section as a potential confounder, as environmental campaigns often trigger both policy changes and increased volunteer cleaning.

**Clarify the Mechanism of the "Pollution Gap"**
The paper posits that the gap arises because bags come from non-retail sources (fishing, fly-tipping, wind-blown waste). While plausible, this is currently an assertion. To strengthen the argument, the authors could correlate the null result with external data on waste composition. For example, if UK waste strategy reports indicate that retail bags constitute only 5% of marine litter sources, this would align with the finding that taxing retail bags has little impact on total bag litter. Adding a simple back-of-the-envelope calculation linking retail sales reductions to expected litter reductions (based on source apportionment studies) would make the "pollution gap" argument more concrete. This would shift the paper from simply reporting a null result to explaining *why* the null occurs, which is more valuable for policy design.

**Refine Policy Implications and Writing**
The conclusion is strong but could be more nuanced. The statement "The bags were never coming from the cash register in the first place" is catchy but potentially misleading; bags *were* coming from the cash register, but those specific bags may not be the ones ending up on beaches (e.g., they are used for waste lining and end up in landfills). I suggest refining this to: "The bags reduced at the cash register are not the marginal bags contributing to marine litter." Additionally, the paper should acknowledge that bag charges may still be welfare-improving due to reduced production costs and landfill use, even if marine litter doesn't decline. This prevents the paper from being interpreted as an argument against the policy entirely, rather than an argument for better targeted environmental instruments. Finally, ensure all references to the "Autonomous Policy Evaluation Project" are clearly disclosed in the footnotes as per AER transparency standards regarding AI-assisted research.

**Data and Code Transparency**
Given the autonomous generation note, transparency is paramount. I strongly encourage the authors to ensure the replication package is fully functional and documented. Specifically, the code linking geographic coordinates to UK nations should be verified, as misclassification here would attenuate results. If the data was accessed via a GitHub mirror (`nicrie/seasonality_ospar`), the authors should confirm this mirror is up-to-date with the official OSPAR database, as discrepancies in litter coding over time (OSPAR updates their guidelines) could introduce measurement error. A brief appendix note confirming data versioning would reassure readers of the data's integrity.

**Visual Enhancements for AER: Insights**
Finally, consider adding a map of the 47 beaches colored by treatment cohort. AER: Insights readers appreciate visual intuition. Showing the geographic spread helps assess whether "contamination" across borders (e.g., bags washing from untreated to treated beaches) is a concern. Additionally, an event-study plot (currently in table format) would be more effective as a figure with confidence intervals, allowing readers to visually assess pre-trend parallelism. These visual aids would make the paper more accessible and align better with the Insights format's emphasis on clear communication of empirical results.
