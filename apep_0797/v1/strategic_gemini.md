## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
This paper examines how trade sanctions redistribute welfare within a sanctioned country by exploiting a natural experiment in Niger following the 2023 ECOWAS embargo. By comparing the price gap between imported rice and local millet in sanctioned Niger versus unsanctioned Burkina Faso, the author identifies a 14% "tradability tax" that disproportionately burdens urban consumers while leaving rural producers relatively insulated. It is a rare, micro-level causal look at the internal mechanics of economic coercion in a developing world context.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this well, though it is slightly too focused on the "how" (the triple-difference) rather than the "why" (the internal redistribution of sanctions) in the opening. 
**The Revised Pitch:** 
*Standard economic accounts of sanctions focus on aggregate GDP losses, but these totals mask how coercion actually works on the ground. This paper shows that sanctions act as a regressive consumption tax, specifically penalizing those dependent on imported staples while insulating the rural poor. By quantifying this "tradability tax" using the 2023 Niger embargo, I demonstrate that the effectiveness of sanctions as a political tool depends on whether the specific segments of the population bearing the cost actually have the leverage to influence their rulers.*

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**Contribution:** The paper identifies and quantifies the "tradability channel" of sanctions—showing that the burden of trade embargoes is fundamentally determined by a commodity’s import dependence rather than a general equilibrium price shock.

- **Differentiation:** Most sanctions literature (e.g., Felbermayr et al., 2020) is macro/gravity-based. This paper shifts the unit of analysis to the consumer basket in specific markets. It is closer to Aker (2010) in methodology but applies it to a high-stakes geopolitical shock.
- **Framing:** It is framed as a question about the world (how sanctions affect the poor), which is strong.
- **Clarity:** A smart economist would say: "It’s a paper about how sanctions create a wedge between tradable and non-tradable food prices, causing a regressive welfare shock."
- **Bigger Contribution:** To move this from "good" to "AER-great," the author needs to connect the "tradability tax" to political economy. Does this 14% price spike lead to protests? Does it correlate with areas of political support or opposition for the junta? Linking the price shock to *behavioral* or *political* outcomes would be the "home run."

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
- **Closest Neighbors:** Aker (2010) on Niger markets; Atkin & Donaldson (2015) on trade costs; and the macro-sanctions literature (Hufbauer et al.).
- **Strategy:** It builds on Aker by using the same geography but a more exogenous shock. It "attacks" the macro-literature by arguing that aggregate measures (GDP/Trade flows) are insufficient for understanding welfare or political efficacy.
- **Missing Literature:** The paper should speak more to the **Political Economy of Dictatorships**. If sanctions hit the "wrong" people (urbanites with no power), they fail. Literature on "selectorate theory" or "urban bias" in development would provide a more sophisticated theoretical backbone.

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC
- **Setup:** Global sanctions are a common tool of statecraft, but we usually only measure their aggregate "weight."
- **Tension:** Sanctions are intended to pressure regimes, but they must do so by inflicting pain. If the pain is felt only by those who cannot influence the regime, the tool is broken.
- **Resolution:** In Niger, the pain was concentrated in imported rice (urban/tradable), while the rural base (millet-growers) was largely spared.
- **Implications:** Sanctions in the Sahel may be structurally destined to fail if they don't hit the regime's core support or if the "tax" falls on the politically marginalized.

**Evaluation:** The arc is currently a bit "result-first." It reads like a very clean technical note. It needs more of the "Political Economy" tension to feel like a major AER statement.
