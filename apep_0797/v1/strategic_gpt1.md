## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple, policy-relevant question: when sanctions disrupt cross-border trade, who actually pays? Using the 2023 ECOWAS sanctions on Niger, the paper argues that the burden shows up not as a uniform rise in all food prices, but as a sharp increase in the price of imported staples relative to locally produced ones—what it calls a “tradability tax.”

A busy economist should care because this is a concrete micro-level incidence question about sanctions, market fragmentation, and food security. The broad claim is that sanctions operate partly through commodity-specific supply-chain exposure, with distributional consequences that aggregate trade or GDP measures miss.

**Does the paper articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?** Mostly yes, better than many papers. The opening is vivid and the core empirical contrast is understandable. But the introduction still leans too quickly into the design and coefficient before fully establishing why this is an important world question rather than a neat quasi-experiment. The paper currently sounds like “here is a clever DDD using rice and millet,” when it should sound like “sanctions reshape the cost of living through tradability, and that matters for incidence, welfare, and policy.”

**What the first two paragraphs should say instead:**

> Economic sanctions are usually evaluated in terms of trade volumes, GDP, or regime change. But for households in sanctioned countries, the first-order question is more basic: which goods become expensive, and who bears that burden? When ECOWAS sanctioned Niger after the 2023 coup, it abruptly cut a landlocked economy off from its main trade corridor. In principle, that should matter most for imported staples, not for foods produced domestically.
>
> This paper shows that it did. After sanctions, the price of imported rice in Niger rose sharply relative to locally produced staples, while the same rice-local gap did not widen comparably in neighboring Burkina Faso. I interpret this excess increase as a “tradability tax”: the consumer price penalty imposed on households that depend on imported food when sanctions fragment markets. The central contribution is to show that sanctions have a distinct incidence channel through tradability, not just an aggregate macroeconomic effect.

That is the pitch. World question first, identification second.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution in one sentence:**  
The paper provides micro evidence that trade sanctions raise the relative price of import-dependent staple foods, identifying a tradability-based incidence channel through which sanctions fragment consumer markets and impose regressive costs.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?
Only partially. The paper says the sanctions literature is macro and that this paper is about consumer prices, which is true, but the differentiation is still somewhat generic. “No one has looked at commodity-level consumer prices in this sanctions episode” is not enough for AER-level positioning. The sharper differentiation is:

1. most sanctions papers study aggregate outcomes;
2. market-integration papers study transmission of trade costs or information shocks, but not sanctions as an abrupt, political closure;
3. this paper identifies **incidence by tradability within the consumption basket**, not merely average inflation.

That third point is the real hook and should be foregrounded.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or filling a gap in a LITERATURE?
Right now it is mixed, but too often framed as a literature gap: “none of these studies trace sanctions through to specific consumer commodity prices.” That is weaker. The stronger frame is about the world:

- When borders close, do sanctions raise all prices, or only import-dependent ones?
- How do sanctions reallocate welfare across consumers depending on what they eat?
- Are sanctions effectively an implicit tax on tradability?

That framing is much more compelling.

### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what's new?
Yes, but only if they are charitable. They would probably say: “It’s a DDD paper showing sanctions in Niger raised rice prices relative to millet.” That is not enough. The goal is for them to say: “It shows sanctions have a tradability-incidence channel—import-dependent consumers, especially urban ones, get hit disproportionately—and you can see that directly in commodity-level prices.”

At present, the paper is in danger of being remembered as “another DiD paper about food prices after a shock.”

### What would make this contribution bigger?
Several possibilities:

1. **Move from price effects to incidence/welfare more directly.**  
   Right now “regressive” is asserted more than demonstrated. If the paper can show that poorer urban households consume more imported rice, or that rice dependence differs systematically by income/location, the contribution gets bigger immediately.

2. **Show heterogeneity by exposure to trade corridors.**  
   Border-adjacent versus interior markets, or markets more dependent on Nigerian corridors, would elevate the paper from a clean average effect to a map of how sanctions transmit spatially.

3. **Connect to substitution and nutrition.**  
   If households switch from rice to millet, what happens to caloric cost, diet quality, or volatility? Even a simple framing around substitution possibilities would strengthen the welfare interpretation.

4. **Broaden beyond one commodity pair.**  
   The robustness with sorghum and maize is useful, but the main story would be bigger if it were framed as a broader imported-vs-local basket contrast, not “rice versus millet.”

5. **Reframe as a general principle.**  
   The paper should claim that sanctions, conflict, and border closures generate a common “tradability tax” in consumer markets. That makes Niger an especially sharp case rather than an isolated one.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

### Closest neighbors

   - Atkin and Donaldson (2018) on internal trade costs
   - Bergquist (2020) on market integration in developing settings
   - Minot (2011) on world food price transmission in SSA
   - Badiane and Shively / Fackler and Goodwin on spatial price transmission

3. **Conflict / political shocks / food security**
   - Arezki and Brückner-type work on food prices and instability
   - Dillon and Barrett / Giordani et al. on food price shocks and welfare/insecurity
   - Possibly broader work on supply disruptions under war/blockade, though not cited here

### How should the paper position itself relative to those neighbors?
Mostly **build on and bridge**, not attack.

- Relative to sanctions papers: “We complement macro evidence by showing the incidence mechanism in consumer markets.”
- Relative to market-integration papers: “We study an extreme, politically induced reversal of integration rather than ordinary transport or information frictions.”
- Relative to food-security papers: “We show how geopolitical shocks map into staple-specific consumer burdens.”

The current draft is a bit too eager to claim firsts (“none of these studies,” “first causal estimates”) without enough confidence that the claim is truly defendable. Better to say the paper brings these literatures together in a new way.

### Is the paper currently positioned too narrowly or too broadly?
A bit of both, oddly.

- **Too narrow** because much of the paper reads like a case study of Niger’s 2023 crisis.
- **Too broad** because it gestures at sanctions, market integration, food security, political economy, and welfare without clearly prioritizing which conversation it most wants to lead.

My advice: anchor the paper in **sanctions incidence and market fragmentation**, then use food security as implication, not as an equal third literature.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?
A few likely omissions:

- The incidence/pass-through literature: who bears trade-cost shocks in consumer prices.
- Broader work on border closures, blockades, or conflict-induced market fragmentation.
- Welfare and cost-of-living measurement in developing countries, especially basket-specific inflation.
- Political economy of sanctions as targeting civilian welfare versus elites.

If the paper wants to use the word “regressive,” it needs to speak more directly to incidence and household consumption literature.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?
Almost, but not quite. The unexpected and most promising conversation is not just “sanctions are bad for food prices.” It is:

**Sanctions are a form of commodity-specific cost-of-living shock whose incidence depends on tradability and consumption baskets.**

That is a fresher conversation and one that could travel beyond this single setting.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Before this paper, we know sanctions reduce trade and hurt economies in aggregate, but we know less about how they reshape everyday consumer markets. In a landlocked low-income economy, some staples depend on trade corridors while others are locally produced.

### Tension
If sanctions sever cross-border supply chains, does the pain show up as broad inflation, or as a selective price spike in import-dependent goods? And who bears that burden?

### Resolution
In Niger, sanctions sharply increased the relative price of imported rice compared with local staples, especially during full border closure, suggesting that tradability is the key channel through which sanctions fragment food markets.

### Implications
Sanctions are not neutral macro pressure tools. They differentially burden households according to their consumption basket, likely hitting urban, import-dependent consumers hardest. That matters for welfare, political economy, and sanctions design.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
It has the ingredients, but not yet a fully disciplined arc. The paper currently has a strong setup and a clear result, but the tension and implication are underdeveloped. It jumps from event to design to estimates with relatively little intellectual drama.

More bluntly: this is still somewhat a **collection of sensible results around a clean design** rather than a fully realized AER story.

### What story should it be telling?
The story is:

1. Sanctions are usually analyzed as aggregate coercion.
2. But consumers experience them as selective supply shocks.
3. Tradability determines incidence.
4. Therefore, sanctions act like a commodity-specific tax on import dependence.
5. This changes how we should think about both welfare and the political economy of sanctions.

That is the paper’s real narrative. Everything in the draft should be subordinated to that.

That is a decent lead fact.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
Top field economists would lean in briefly, especially if they work on trade, development, or political economy. But many would quickly ask whether this is more than a nice case-study design. The result is intuitive. Intuitive findings can still be important, but they need a bigger conceptual wrapper.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Almost certainly one of these:

- “Is this just Niger, or a general feature of sanctions?”
- “Who actually consumes imported rice—can you show the incidence is regressive rather than just assert it?”
- “What happened to quantities, substitution, or welfare?”
- “Why is this more than a one-off price pass-through paper?”

Those follow-up questions are telling. The paper’s current weakness is that it does not fully answer the most interesting second question.

### If findings are modest, is that okay?
The findings are not null; 14–18 percent is economically meaningful. The issue is not that the effect is too small. The issue is that the paper has not yet made the reader feel that learning this fact changes how we think about sanctions.
