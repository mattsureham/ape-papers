## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether expanding local political representation improves a hard, welfare-relevant public outcome: infant survival. It uses constitutional population thresholds in Brazil that mechanically increase the minimum size of city councils and finds that marginal increases in council size do not reduce infant mortality, suggesting that simply adding legislators is not an effective way to improve local public-service delivery.

A busy economist should care because this is, in principle, a clean institutional-design question with broad reach: do more representatives improve government performance, or does governance quality depend more on incentives and information than on headcount? That is an AER-type question. The problem is that the paper currently presents it as a narrow Brazil-RD-null-result paper before fully selling the larger institutional question.

### Does the paper articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Not quite. The current introduction is competent, but it opens with infant mortality in Brazil rather than with the bigger question. That makes the paper sound like a health/governance application rather than a general political-economy paper about the returns to representation. The second paragraph moves quickly into the design, before the reader is fully convinced the question matters at a first-order level.

### What the first two paragraphs should say instead

The paper should lead with the institutional-design question, then explain why infant mortality is a demanding test of whether representation matters.

**The pitch the paper should have:**

> Does increasing political representation improve government performance? Democracies routinely debate whether larger legislatures provide better oversight and public goods, but there is little causal evidence on whether adding representatives changes consequential real-world outcomes. This paper studies that question in Brazilian municipalities, where constitutional population thresholds mechanically increase the minimum number of city council seats.
>
> I examine whether these increases in council size improve one of the most policy-relevant local outcomes: infant mortality. If additional legislators strengthen oversight of municipal health spending and service delivery, infant deaths should fall; if instead marginal seats mostly create duplication, coalition expansion, or symbolic representation without added accountability, health outcomes may not improve. Using the constitutional thresholds, I find no evidence that larger councils reduce infant mortality.

That framing makes the paper about **the return to representation**, not about “an RD on infant mortality in Brazil.”

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper’s contribution is to provide causal evidence that marginal increases in the size of local legislatures do not measurably improve a high-stakes public-service outcome—infant mortality—in decentralized Brazilian municipalities.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest papers?

Only partially. The paper gestures at several literatures—legislative size, oversight, Brazilian governance, infant mortality—but it does not sharply distinguish itself from nearby work. Right now the contribution risks sounding like: “another quasi-experimental paper using Brazilian population thresholds to study a municipal outcome.” That is not enough.

It needs to say more clearly:

1. **What prior work has established**
   - Larger legislatures may be associated with public goods in cross-country or observational settings.
   - Brazilian threshold papers have mostly focused on transfers, spending, electoral incentives, or accountability.
   - Health-outcome papers have examined fiscal resources or health interventions, not legislative body size as an institutional input.

2. **What this paper adds**
   - It isolates the **extensive margin of representation**: more seats, holding institutional environment broadly fixed.
   - It tests whether that margin affects a **hard outcome**, not just budgets, staffing, or politics.
   - It delivers evidence that institutional quantity alone may not improve state capacity or service delivery.

That is potentially interesting—but it needs to be stated more forcefully.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a gap in a literature?

Mostly as a literature gap, with some world framing. It should be much more firmly framed as a **world question**:

- Weak version: “This contributes to the literature on council size.”
- Strong version: “When governments expand representation without changing incentives or information, does public-service performance improve?”

The second is much stronger.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?

At present, maybe, but with hesitation. They might say:

> “It’s an RD paper on Brazilian council-size thresholds and infant mortality, and they find no effect.”

That is not yet an AER-level takeaway. You want them to say:

> “It asks whether more representatives improve state performance; in Brazil, adding council seats does not improve infant survival, which suggests the binding constraint is not the number of elected overseers.”

That is a bigger, cleaner idea.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Most importantly: **show that infant mortality is not just one arbitrary outcome but a crucial test case for whether representation improves local governance.** Right now the choice of infant mortality feels somewhat opportunistic: important, yes, but why this outcome rather than spending, primary care access, procurement quality, or other channels more directly linked to oversight?

Specific ways to make the contribution bigger:

- **Add more proximal outcomes/mechanisms**: municipal health spending composition, primary-care coverage, prenatal visits, vaccination, avoidable hospitalization, staffing, audit flags, procurement indicators, council committee activity if available.
- **Show whether the null is domain-specific or general**: if councils do not affect health but do affect some other municipal function, that is informative. If they affect nothing, that is a stronger institutional statement.
- **Exploit the distinction between oversight-intensive vs protocol-driven outcomes**: infant mortality may be too downstream and too mediated by SUS/federal protocols. A stronger paper would compare outcomes where councils plausibly matter more versus less.
- **Frame as the returns to marginal representation under weak local legislative capacity** rather than just one null health outcome.

If they could only enlarge one dimension, I would push **mechanisms/proximal outcomes**, because that would convert a null into a sharper statement about why more seats do not matter.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Local governments are responsible for major public services, and democratic systems often assume that more representatives can better oversee executive actions and improve service delivery.

### Tension

Theory cuts both ways: larger legislatures may improve oversight and specialization, but they may also fragment responsibility and dilute accountability. Despite the importance of this question, there is limited causal evidence on whether adding elected representatives actually improves meaningful public outcomes.

### Resolution

Using constitutional council-size thresholds in Brazil, the paper finds that crossing thresholds that add council seats does not reduce infant mortality.

### Implications

The findings suggest that simply expanding representation may not improve governance quality; what matters more may be incentives, information, and institutional capacity rather than the number of elected officials.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the ingredients, but not a fully convincing arc. Right now it reads more like:

1. Here is an interesting institutional rule.
2. Here is an outcome.
3. Here is a null.
4. Here are some robustness checks.
5. Here are some possible interpretations.

That is serviceable but not memorable.

### What story should it be telling?

The story should be:

1. **A foundational institutional question**: does adding representatives improve government performance?
2. **A demanding empirical test**: infant mortality is a high-stakes outcome that should respond if legislative oversight meaningfully improves local health governance.
3. **A surprising answer**: more representatives do not move this outcome.
4. **A broader lesson**: governance failures are not solved by increasing the number of elected overseers; the bottleneck lies elsewhere.

That is the story of a paper. At present, the paper has results looking for that bigger story.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?

> In Brazil, constitutional rules add city council seats at population thresholds, but municipalities that get more legislators do not experience lower infant mortality.

That is the lead fact.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

Initially, they would **lean in** because the question is broad and the design is intuitively appealing. But they will only stay engaged if the presenter immediately answers: **why infant mortality is the right place to look**, and **what this teaches us about political representation more generally**.

If not, they will drift to: “Interesting null in one setting.”

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Almost certainly:

> “Okay, but do more council seats affect anything closer to the mechanism—health spending, primary care, corruption, procurement, or oversight behavior?”

That is the paper’s biggest strategic vulnerability. The natural audience response is not “I don’t believe the design”; it is “Why should I infer anything broad from a null on this one downstream outcome?”

### Is the null result itself interesting?

Potentially yes, but only if the paper leans into why this null is informative. A null can be important if it rules out a widely presumed channel or common reform logic. Here the interesting claim would be:

- More representation is often presumed to improve oversight.
- In a decentralized system where local governments manage health resources, adding representatives does not improve one of the most consequential outcomes.
- Therefore, simple expansion of elected oversight bodies may be a weak reform lever.

That is valuable. But the paper has to make the null feel like a **theoretical update**, not a failed search for significance.

At present it is halfway there.
