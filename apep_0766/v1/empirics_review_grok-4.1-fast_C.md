### 1. Idea Fidelity
The paper faithfully pursues the original idea manifest. It implements a multi-cutoff RDD exploiting Brazilian constitutional population thresholds for city council size (\(vereadores\)) using IBGE annual population estimates as the running variable and DATASUS (SIM/SINASC) vital statistics microdata aggregated to municipality-year infant mortality rates (IMR) across 5,570 municipalities (85,979 observations, 2003--2020). The research question—whether larger legislatures causally reduce infant mortality—is directly addressed with pooled and cutoff-specific estimates. No key elements are missed, though execution details (e.g., annual treatment assignment) raise concerns addressed below.

### 2. Summary
This paper uses a multi-cutoff regression discontinuity design (RDD) at five population thresholds (15k--120k) that mechanically increase minimum city council seats by two to estimate the causal effect on infant mortality rates in Brazilian municipalities. The pooled estimate is a precise null (-0.32 deaths/1,000 live births, robust SE=0.41, baseline IMR=13.7), ruling out effects larger than ~9% of the mean; results hold across bandwidths, donuts, polynomials, and placebo cutoffs. The findings suggest that marginal legislative expansion does not improve health oversight, contributing to literatures on governance institutions and null political economy results.

### 3. Essential Points
**1. No evidence of discontinuity in actual council size (the treatment).** The paper assumes thresholds strictly add two seats, but the Constitution sets only *minimums*; actual vereadores elected may exceed minima based on proportionality rules or party lists (TSE guidelines). Without data on realized council size (e.g., from TSE election records), the "treatment" is unverified—RDD identifies variation in *assigned minima*, not implemented seats. Authors must merge TSE data (available via BigQuery or TSE API) and plot/show RDD estimates on actual vereadores per capita. Absent this, identification fails; results could reflect non-compliance.

**2. Treatment assignment ignores election cycles, biasing estimates toward zero.** Municipal elections occur every four years (2004, 2008, etc.), with council size fixed for the term based on IBGE population at election time (typically prior-year estimate). Annual population-based treatment coding implies frequent on/off switching, but actual treatment is persistent/lagged. This measurement error severely attenuates \(\tau\). Restrict to post-election municipality-years (e.g., years 1--4 of term), assign treatment using election-year population, and re-estimate. Sensitivity to full-term lags is essential.

**3. Insufficient validity checks undermine credibility.** Significant McCrary bunching at 15k (\(p=0.003\)) and 80k (\(p=0.018\)) suggests manipulation (consistent with Litschig 2012), but donuts crudely address it without quantifying bias. Only one covariate balance test (births/capita) is inadequate; test 5--10 pre-determined covariates (e.g., literacy, GDP/capita from IBGE RAIS/Censo, lagged IMR, ESF coverage from DATASUS). No RD plots (density, outcome, covariates) for visual inspection—mandatory for RDD. Provide these; if balance fails post-manipulation adjustment, reject.

These three issues are fixable with data/code tweaks but are critical for causal claims. Magnitudes are plausible (null/small effects align with governance nulls like Ferraz 2011), SEs appropriate (rdrobust clustering at municipality handles serial correlation), and pooled null economically meaningful (powers against policy-relevant 0.1--0.2 sd effects). However, fix or paper risks rejection.

### 4. Suggestions
**RDD Implementation and Power Enhancements (40% weight):** Leverage rdrobust's full suite: report bias-reduced linear/quadratic estimates alongside CCT-optimal (as in Table 3 robustness). For multi-cutoff, add global bandwidth selection (Cattaneo et al. 2019) and test cutoff heterogeneity formally (e.g., \(\hat{\tau}_k\) interactions). Power is strong pooled (\(N=22k\) effective, rules out 0.08 sd), but low at higher cutoffs (e.g., 120k: \(N=414\), SE=1.02)—drop sparse cutoffs (>80k) or weight observations by live births to stabilize noisy IMR in small municipalities (avg. 194 births below cutoff; Poisson RDD alternative via rdpowerr). Simulate minimum detectable effects (Andrews et al. 2024 style) targeting 0.1 sd (1.35 deaths/1k), confirming "hard null."

**Data and Sample Refinements:** Align years precisely: exclude pre-2003 (pre-EC 58/2009 stability) and 2021+ (COVID noise); focus 2004--2020 election terms. Weight IMR by births (or use deaths as outcome with births as exposure in Poisson) to downweight small-municipality noise—sd(IMR)=13.5 reflects Poisson variation. Merge complementary DATASUS outcomes (e.g., prenatal visits, low birthweight from SINASC Appendix) for multi-outcome RDD, testing oversight channels. Source TSE vereadores data (e.g., `basedosdados.br_tse_eleicoes` BigQuery) for first-stage plot: expect \(\Delta\) vereadores \(\approx +2\), or quantify compliance/complier-average effect.

**Robustness and Threats:** Expand donuts dynamically (e.g., \(\pm 1\%\) of cutoff pop) and report manipulation-adjusted RDD (Cattaneo et al. 2020 rddensity). Add two-way clustering (municipalities \(\times\) years) or wild bootstrap SEs for panel dependence. Placebo on leads/lags: estimate RDD on future IMR to rule out anticipation. Test ESF coverage discontinuity (DATASUS municipal aggregates)—null here strengthens governance interpretation.

**Economic Interpretation and Extensions:** Frame null crisply: standardized effect -0.024 sd (Table A1) vs. audit effects (Ferraz 2009: ~0.2 sd re-election). Add mechanisms: RDD on health budget (SIOPS data), committee assignments (if TSE), or corruption (CGU audits). Heterogeneity by mayor-council alignment (TSE data), ESF baseline, or region (North/Northeast IMR-sensitive). Cross-study: compare to Litschig (2012) fiscal thresholds. Figures are absent—add 3--4: (i) binned scatter pooled IMR; (ii) cutoff-specific; (iii) density; (iv) first-stage vereadores.
