## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether expanding local political representation improves a hard, first-order measure of public service delivery: infant survival. It uses constitutional population thresholds in Brazil that mechanically increase city council size and finds that adding council seats does not measurably reduce infant mortality, suggesting that simply increasing the number of elected overseers is not enough to improve public health.

A busy economist should care because this is, at least in ambition, a paper about whether institutional design at the local level affects real welfare outcomes. The intended contribution is broader than Brazil or infant mortality: when does “more representation” translate into better government?

**Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Not quite. The current opening is competent, but it starts too locally and too descriptively. It gets to the design quickly, but the broader stakes are underdeveloped. The introduction currently reads like a clean applied micro paper on a Brazilian institutional feature rather than a paper about a central political economy question with a sharp welfare test.

**What the first two paragraphs should say instead:**  
The paper should open with the big question: does increasing the number of elected representatives improve state capacity and citizens’ welfare, or does it merely add political bodies without adding accountability? It should then say that Brazil provides a rare quasi-experiment in legislature size, and that the paper studies one of the most consequential outcomes imaginable—infant mortality—and finds essentially no effect. That is the hook.

**The pitch the paper should have:**  
“Do larger legislatures produce better government? This paper studies that question in the context of Brazilian municipalities, where constitutional population thresholds mechanically expand city councils. Using those thresholds, I test whether more local legislators improve one of the most consequential public outcomes—infant mortality—and find that they do not. The result suggests that the quantity of representatives is a weak lever for improving service delivery absent changes in incentives, information, or executive constraints.”

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution in one sentence:**  
The paper provides quasi-experimental evidence that marginal increases in local legislature size in Brazil do not improve infant mortality, casting doubt on the idea that more political representation alone strengthens public-service oversight.

### Evaluation

**Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?**  
Only partially. The paper gestures toward several literatures—legislature size, political accountability in Brazil, and infant mortality—but it does not sharply establish what exact claim previous papers have or have not settled. Right now the reader sees a familiar structure: a nice institutional discontinuity, a public-good outcome, a null result. What is missing is a forceful statement of why this is not just “another quasi-experimental paper on Brazilian local institutions.”

The closest comparison is not really to infant mortality papers; it is to papers on representation, legislative size, public spending, and local political accountability. The paper needs to explain more crisply whether prior work shows effects on spending, corruption, transfers, or electoral outcomes—and whether this paper is the first to trace that chain all the way to health. If that is the novelty, say it cleanly.

**Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or filling a gap in a LITERATURE?**  
It is mixed, but too often it slips into literature-gap language. The stronger frame is world-facing: governments routinely expand councils, committees, and representative bodies in the hope of improving accountability. Does that improve lives? This paper says not necessarily.

**Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what’s new?**  
Right now they might say: “It’s an RD paper using Brazilian council-size thresholds to show no effect on infant mortality.” That is accurate but not memorable. They would be less likely to say: “It shows that expanding representation alone is not an effective governance reform for service delivery.”

**What would make this contribution bigger? Be specific.**  
The most obvious way to make it bigger is to move from a single distal outcome to a chain of governance outcomes:

- **Mechanism outcomes:** health spending composition, primary care coverage, prenatal care, immunization, avoidable hospitalizations, audit findings, committee activity, budget amendments, or mayoral oversight.
- **Intermediate service-delivery outcomes:** prenatal visits, skilled attendance, neonatal care access, vaccination, sanitation-related infant causes.
- **Broader framing:** not “council size and infant mortality” but “representation versus capability in local governance.”
- **Sharper comparison:** contrast legislature-size shocks with shocks to fiscal resources or information/accountability reforms in Brazil. That would let the paper say not merely “this reform doesn’t matter,” but “this margin matters less than resources/information/incentives.”

As it stands, the paper asks a big question but tests it with one outcome. That narrows the payoff. A null on infant mortality alone leaves the reader asking whether the wrong margin was studied.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Municipal governments in Brazil are central to health service delivery, and city councils are formally tasked with oversight. There is a broad belief, in both theory and policy, that more representation may mean better monitoring and better public goods.

### Tension
But it is unclear whether adding legislators actually improves governance. More seats could increase oversight, or they could simply create fragmentation and patronage. And most empirical work does not tell us whether representation changes final welfare outcomes rather than just budgets or politics.

### Resolution
Using constitutional thresholds that expand council size, the paper finds no detectable effect on infant mortality.

### Implications
The number of elected representatives may be a weak institutional margin for improving local service delivery; reforms that change incentives, information, or resources may matter more than reforms that simply increase the headcount of politicians.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
It has a **serviceable** arc, but not a strong one. The ingredients are there, yet the narrative is weakened by two issues:

1. The paper moves too quickly into design and specification rather than building the substantive puzzle.
2. The null finding is not elevated into a broader conceptual takeaway soon enough.

At present, it reads a bit like a collection of well-executed RD exercises organized around a sensible question. What it should be telling is a sharper story:

> Many institutional reform debates assume that more representation improves accountability. Brazil offers a clean test of that assumption. If more legislators improve oversight where municipalities control health delivery, infant mortality should respond. It does not. Therefore the binding constraint is not representative headcount but the effectiveness of oversight institutions.

That is a stronger story than “here is an RD on council size and IMR.”

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

**What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?**  
“Brazil has constitutional population thresholds that add city council seats, and those extra legislators don’t seem to improve infant survival at all.”

That is reasonably good. People will not leap out of their chairs, but they may lean in if the presenter immediately follows with: “So this is really a test of whether expanding representation improves actual state performance.”

**Would people lean in or reach for their phones?**  
Depends entirely on framing. If pitched as “a null RD on infant mortality,” phones. If pitched as “a quasi-experimental test of whether larger legislatures improve welfare,” some leaning in.

**What follow-up question would they ask?**  
Almost certainly: “Okay, but do larger councils affect anything else—spending, corruption, oversight, committee structure, primary care inputs?”  
That is the key strategic issue. The natural response to the paper’s main finding is not disbelief but **scope skepticism**: maybe infant mortality is too downstream.

**If the findings are null or modest: is the null result itself interesting?**  
Potentially yes, but only if the paper makes a stronger case that this is a meaningful null, not a failed search. Right now the paper tries to do that with language like “precisely estimated null,” which helps, but for AER-level interest the null must discipline an important prior belief. The paper needs to say more forcefully:

- Why a reasonable economist might have expected an effect.
- Why infant mortality is a hard but relevant test.
- Why learning that this margin does not matter changes how we think about institutional reform.

At present, the null is respectable, but it does not yet feel indispensable.
