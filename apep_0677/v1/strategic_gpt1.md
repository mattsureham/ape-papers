## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether the EU Deforestation Regulation, a major unilateral attempt to clean up global supply chains, actually reduces environmentally harmful production or instead reshuffles trade toward less regulated markets. Using bilateral trade data on tropical commodities, it argues that the most important early effect is not dramatic diversion to China, but sorting: large exporters with compliance capacity keep serving Europe while smaller exporters fall out of EU-facing supply chains.

A busy economist should care because this is a question about the equilibrium effects of unilateral green trade policy: when rich countries regulate production standards abroad, do they change the world or merely change who sells to whom?

**Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Only partly. The current opening is vivid, but it immediately leans into a China-diversion story that the paper does not really deliver. The paper’s strongest result is not “exports moved from Europe to China”; it is “the regulation appears to induce compliance-based reallocation across exporters.” The introduction currently sells one paper and delivers another.

**What the first two paragraphs should say instead:**

> The European Union’s Deforestation Regulation is one of the most ambitious attempts yet to use market access to discipline environmental damage abroad. By requiring importers to verify that coffee, cocoa, soy, palm oil, wood, and other commodities are not linked to recent deforestation, the regulation could reduce global forest loss. But it could also have a different equilibrium effect: reshuffling supply chains so that compliant, traceable producers serve Europe while less traceable producers sell elsewhere.
>
> This paper asks what kind of reorganization the EUDR is already inducing in global trade. Using bilateral trade flows for regulated and comparable unregulated tropical commodities before and after the regulation’s proposal, we study whether Europe’s imports fall, whether trade is diverted to other destinations, and which exporters remain in EU-facing supply chains. The central fact is not a clean shift to China, but a pattern of compliance sorting: larger exporters appear to retain EU market access while smaller exporters disproportionately exit.

That is the pitch the paper actually earns.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Before this paper, the world has seen growing use of rich-country market access rules to pursue environmental goals abroad. The EUDR is a landmark example: a major importer imposing traceability requirements on tropical commodities linked to deforestation.

### Tension
The key uncertainty is whether unilateral green trade policy changes environmental outcomes or merely changes trade patterns. More specifically: will Europe import less dirty production overall, or will the regulation screen suppliers by compliance capacity and push some trade elsewhere?

### Resolution
The paper finds suggestive evidence of lower EU-bound trade in regulated commodities, little clear sign of one-for-one China absorption, and stronger evidence that large exporters retain EU access while smaller exporters lose it.

### Implications
The implication is that unilateral due-diligence regulation may restructure supply chains more than it shrinks environmentally harmful activity. That matters for welfare, market structure, incidence, and the ultimate environmental payoff of these policies.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
Only partially. Right now it feels like **a collection of partially connected findings**:
- main negative EU coefficient,
- no China effect,
- heterogeneity by exporter type,
- discussion of deforestation and leakage.

The strongest story is not the one the paper foregrounds. The current arc is:

> “Does EUDR divert trade? Maybe somewhat. Does it go to China? Not really. But there is an interesting heterogeneity result.”

That is backwards. The heterogeneity result is the story.

**What story should it be telling instead?**

> EUDR is not just a demand shock; it is a compliance screen. The regulation appears to sort exporters into EU-facing and non-EU-facing supply chains by their ability to meet traceability requirements. Aggregate trade diversion is modest and noisy, but compositional reallocation across suppliers is the first-order response.

That is a coherent narrative. It also better fits the evidence.

The paper should not apologize for not finding a China effect; it should use that null to sharpen its argument.

At present, however, the paper overstates. The conclusion says “the same commodities reaching China” and “Europe’s import decline,” which is stronger than the body of results supports. That hurts credibility and positioning. The paper should not sell a dramatic leakage fact it does not establish.
