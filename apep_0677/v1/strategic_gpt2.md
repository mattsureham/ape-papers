## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether the EU Deforestation Regulation, a major unilateral import regulation aimed at cleaning up tropical commodity supply chains, actually changes global trade patterns before enforcement begins. Using bilateral trade data, it argues that the policy may be less about reducing trade in deforestation-linked goods overall than about reshuffling who can continue serving Europe: larger, compliance-capable exporters stay in, while smaller exporters are pushed out.

That is a question busy economists should care about because it sits at the intersection of trade, environmental policy, and regulatory incidence: when a rich market imposes green standards, does it reduce the bad activity, shift it elsewhere, or reallocate rents toward firms and countries that can document compliance?

**Does the paper articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?** Not quite. The current opening sets up a dramatic China-diversion story that the paper does not actually establish. By the end, the paper's most defensible and interesting result is not “Europe lost trade to China,” but “the regulation appears to induce compliance sorting across exporters.” The introduction oversells one story and then delivers another.

**What the first two paragraphs should say instead:**

> The EU Deforestation Regulation is one of the most ambitious attempts yet to use trade policy to influence land use abroad. By conditioning access to the EU market on proof that goods are not linked to recent deforestation, it raises a central question for economists: when environmental regulation is imposed unilaterally by a large buyer, does it reduce environmentally harmful production, divert trade to other markets, or sort suppliers by their ability to comply?
>
> This paper studies the EUDR's early trade effects using bilateral data on tropical commodities. We show that, even before enforcement, EU-bound trade in regulated commodities weakens relative to comparable goods, but the clearest pattern is not simple diversion to China. Instead, the regulation appears to reorganize supply chains by compliance capacity: large exporters maintain EU access, while smaller exporters disproportionately lose it. The implication is that unilateral green regulation may function as a screening device for supplier capability as much as an instrument for reducing environmental harm.

That is the pitch the paper should have.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution in one sentence:**  
The paper's core contribution is to argue that the EUDR's early effect is not clean trade diversion to alternative destinations, but compliance-based sorting that preserves EU access for large exporters and displaces smaller ones.

### Is this clearly differentiated from the closest 3-4 papers?
Only partially. The paper says “first causal evidence on the EUDR's trade effects,” which is a temporal novelty claim, not a substantive differentiation claim. That is not enough for AER-level positioning. “First paper on a new regulation” can matter, but only if the paper also teaches something broader. Right now the broader lesson is there, but underdeveloped.

The paper should distinguish itself from:
1. CGE/simulation papers on the EUDR and deforestation-free supply chains.
2. Empirical leakage papers in carbon/environmental regulation.
3. Work on standards, certification, and traceability in trade/ag supply chains.
4. Work on anticipatory effects of policy announcements.

At present, it gestures at (1), (2), and (4), but misses the stronger connection to the economics of standards/compliance/export selection.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or filling a gap in a LITERATURE?
Mostly framed as filling a literature gap (“first causal evidence,” “no published study uses observed bilateral trade data”). That is weaker than the world question, which is actually interesting: **what do green import regulations do when compliance is costly and enforcement is delayed?**

The paper should lead with the world question and treat the literature gap as supporting detail.

### Could a smart economist explain what's new after reading the intro?
Not cleanly. Right now they might say: “It's a DiD/DDD paper on whether the EUDR diverted trade.” That undersells it and is not distinctive enough. The paper needs them to say: “It shows that unilateral green trade regulation may sort exporters by compliance capacity rather than simply reduce trade or reroute it to China.”

### What would make this contribution bigger?
Several possibilities:

- **Different framing:** Move from “Did trade divert?” to “How do environmental import standards reorganize supply chains?” That is the most important change.
- **Different mechanism emphasis:** Bring compliance capacity, traceability, and exporter selection to the center. Right now that is presented as a heterogeneity result; it should be the headline.
- **Different comparison:** Compare larger versus smaller exporters, or standard-risk versus others, not just EU versus China. The paper is strongest there.
- **Different outcome variable:** If feasible, outcomes tied to market concentration, exporter entry/exit, unit values, or buyer composition would make the sorting story much bigger and more general than trade levels alone.
- **Different implication:** Position the paper as about the incidence of regulatory compliance costs in global supply chains. That makes it relevant beyond deforestation.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
A large importing bloc adopts a novel environmental market-access regulation covering major tropical commodities. Policymakers hope it will reduce the link between consumption and deforestation.

### Tension
Unilateral regulation may fail in two different ways: it may divert trade to unregulated markets, or it may simply reshuffle who can afford to comply. The paper begins with the first tension but the results point more strongly to the second.

### Resolution
The paper finds some negative movement in EU-bound regulated trade after the policy shock, but weak evidence of China-specific diversion. The sharpest pattern is heterogeneity across exporters: larger standard-risk exporters retain access while smaller exporters appear to lose it.

### Implications
The EUDR may act less like a blunt instrument reducing environmentally harmful trade and more like a costly screen that favors compliance-capable suppliers. That matters for policy design, for incidence, and for whether unilateral regulation changes outcomes in the world or just remaps certified and uncertified trade.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
Not yet. It currently feels like **two competing stories**:
1. dramatic trade diversion to China, and
2. compliance sorting by exporter capacity.

The first is vivid but not delivered. The second is more original and closer to the evidence, but it arrives too late and with too little conceptual scaffolding.

**The story it should tell:**  
“EUDR is an early test of unilateral green trade regulation. Its first observable effect is not obvious aggregate diversion to China, but a reorganization of market access around compliance capability. That changes how we should think about the distributional and environmental consequences of green trade policy.”

That is a coherent setup-tension-resolution-implications arc. Right now the paper is a collection of suggestive results organized around the wrong headline question.

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

### What fact would I lead with?
Not the China fact. I would lead with:

**“Before enforcement even begins, the EUDR appears to preserve EU access for large exporters while pushing smaller exporters out.”**

That is sharper, more novel, and more consistent with the evidence.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
If presented as “another paper on whether EU regulation reduced imports,” they reach for their phones. If presented as “green trade policy may screen suppliers by compliance capacity rather than reduce bad production,” they lean in.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
“Does that sorting change actual deforestation, or just who serves the EU?”  
That is exactly the right follow-up. The paper cannot fully answer it with the current data, but it can use that as the paper's honest frontier.

### If the findings are null or modest, is the null itself interesting?
The paper has several null-ish results: no clear China absorption, imprecise aggregate effects, randomization inference that doesn't reject. Those nulls can still be interesting **if** the paper explicitly argues that the early impact of headline green regulation is subtle and compositional, not a dramatic aggregate trade collapse.

Right now, however, the paper sometimes reads like a failed dramatic claim patched over with heterogeneity. It needs to flip that hierarchy: the aggregate diversion result is suggestive background; the compositional/sorting result is the finding.

There is also a damaging inconsistency: the abstract and conclusion still imply a stronger diversion story than the results support. A dinner-party economist will immediately notice that the paper says “China surged” but the table says “no China-specific absorption.” That needs to be cleaned up.
