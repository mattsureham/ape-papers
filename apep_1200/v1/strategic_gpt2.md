## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether anti-immigration sentiment, when publicly revealed through a local referendum vote, changes where immigrants choose to live. Using municipality-level vote margins from Switzerland’s 2014 Mass Immigration Initiative, it studies whether municipalities that narrowly voted “Yes” subsequently saw slower growth in their foreign-resident share than municipalities that narrowly voted “No,” despite facing the same national immigration policy.

A busy economist should care because the paper is trying to make a broader point: politics may matter not only through policy, but through signals. If local votes reveal hostility, they may reshape demographic sorting even when no local policy changes.

**Does the paper articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Not quite. The current opening is competent but not sharp enough. It spends too much time on the referendum as an event and too little time on the big economic idea: **can the public revelation of local preferences change equilibrium sorting behavior?** The introduction gets there by paragraph 3, but a top-journal intro should state that immediately.

### The pitch the paper should have

Here is the version the first two paragraphs should deliver:

> Immigration politics may shape economic outcomes even when they do not immediately change policy. When a community publicly reveals anti-immigration preferences, that vote may alter immigrants’ location choices, employers’ recruiting behavior, and landlords’ willingness to rent—thereby changing local demographics through sorting rather than law.
>
> This paper studies that mechanism in Switzerland’s 2014 Mass Immigration Initiative. Because federal policy was determined nationally, a municipality’s own vote did not change its local immigration rules; it mainly revealed local sentiment. I use close municipal vote margins around the 50 percent threshold to ask whether municipalities that barely expressed an anti-immigration majority subsequently experienced slower growth in their foreign-resident population. The broader question is whether the ballot box changes who lives where, not just what governments do.

That is a much stronger AER-style opening: world question first, setting second, design third.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:**  
The paper argues that publicly revealed local anti-immigration sentiment—independent of differential local policy—can affect subsequent immigrant sorting across places.

That is a real contribution in concept. The problem is that the paper’s execution does not yet make the contribution feel clean, distinct, or large.

### Is it clearly differentiated from the closest papers?
Only partially. The paper says “most prior work examines how immigration affects voting; I study the reverse channel.” That is directionally right, but still too generic. A reader could reasonably come away with: “okay, another paper linking anti-immigrant politics to some downstream outcome.” The author needs to distinguish more sharply between:

1. papers on how immigration affects voting,
2. papers on how anti-immigrant politics affects policy,
3. papers on whether local political sentiment itself changes migrants’ location choices.

Right now those are blurred together.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a literature gap?
Mostly about the world, which is good. The strongest line in the paper is essentially: **does democratic expression itself have demographic consequences?** That is much better than “there is little work on X.” The author should lean harder into that.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the intro?
Some could, but many would still summarize it as:  
“an RDD paper using a Swiss referendum to see if anti-immigrant municipalities got fewer immigrants afterward.”

That is too close to “another design-driven paper on a vote.” The novelty is not the RDD; the novelty is the claim that **political revelation can reallocate people spatially absent local policy variation**. The paper needs to make that unmistakable.

### What would make the contribution bigger?
Several possibilities:

- **A different outcome variable:** flows rather than stocks. If the paper could show effects on in-migration, out-migration, new permits, naturalizations, cross-border workers, school enrollments by nationality, or rental-market outcomes, the story becomes much more about sorting and much less about a modest shift in a stock share.
- **A stronger mechanism:** show that the effect is larger where immigrants have more locational flexibility, where local labor markets rely more on foreign workers, or where anti-immigration sentiment is more publicly salient. Right now the mechanism section reads like a list of plausible stories.
- **A different comparison:** compare effects for EU nationals versus non-EU nationals, or recent arrivals versus long-settled foreigners. The setting screams for heterogeneity tied to who was most exposed to the referendum’s signal.
- **A different framing:** elevate from “did a close Yes vote reduce foreign share growth?” to “when politics reveal local hostility, do people sort away from those places?” That framing travels beyond Switzerland.

At present, the paper’s empirical contribution feels modest because the estimated effect is modest/suggestive and the outcome is fairly blunt.

But the more interesting neighbors may actually be outside the immediate immigration-voting literature:

- papers on **preference revelation and equilibrium sorting**,
- papers on **identity, discrimination, and location choice**,
- papers on **how place reputation affects migration**.

### How should the paper position itself relative to those neighbors?
**Build on them, not attack them.**  
The right move is: “those papers show immigration changes politics; I ask whether politics changes immigration geography.” This is a natural reverse-channel paper. The paper should not overclaim that the literature has “largely ignored” this question unless it has really done a deeper search.

### Is the paper positioned too narrowly or too broadly?
Currently, oddly, both:

- **Too narrowly** in design and setting: it spends a lot of space on the Swiss institutional setup and the close-vote RD.
- **Too broadly** in claimed contribution: it invokes several literatures without clearly deciding which audience it most wants.

The paper should narrow the conversation around one central question:
**Do political signals about local hostility affect migrant sorting?**

Then the Swiss referendum becomes the clean setting, not the main event.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?
It seems underconnected to:

- the broader literature on **sorting on local amenities and disamenities**,
- work on **discrimination and residential choice**,
- work on **place reputation / beliefs about local social climate**,
- potentially political economy work on **expressive voting** and **information revelation**.

That omission matters because the paper’s mechanism is not fundamentally about immigration law; it is about **beliefs about place**.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?
Not yet fully. It is currently having a familiar “immigration and politics” conversation. The more distinctive and potentially impactful conversation is:
**how public political expression changes economic geography through social signaling.**

That is the unexpected literature bridge that could make the paper more interesting.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Before this paper, we know a lot about how immigration affects local politics and attitudes. We also know that immigrants sort across places for labor-market and network reasons. But we know less about whether a community’s publicly revealed hostility itself changes where immigrants go.

### Tension
A referendum vote reveals local sentiment but does not necessarily change local policy. So if demographics change afterward, that would suggest that politics works through a signaling channel, not just through law. But it is not obvious that migrants care about these signals, notice them, or can act on them.

### Resolution
The paper finds suggestive evidence that municipalities narrowly voting Yes saw slower growth in foreign population share, without overall population effects.

### Implications
If valid and generalizable, the implication is that electoral expression can shape local demographic composition even absent local policy changes. Politics can sort people across space.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
**Serviceable, but not yet strong.**  
The ingredients are all there, but the paper still feels somewhat like a collection of RD estimates with a thoughtful discussion attached. The strongest story is not fully driving the structure.

Two things dilute the narrative:

1. The paper’s main empirical result is described as “suggestive,” and the paper itself emphasizes reasons for caution. Intellectually this is admirable, but narratively it means the resolution is weak.
2. The mechanism section is generic. It does not deepen the story; it merely lists possible channels.

### What story should it be telling?
Not “did crossing 50% matter in Switzerland?”  
Instead:

- **Setup:** immigrants choose places partly based on wages, networks, and amenities; local social climate is hard to observe.
- **Tension:** a referendum suddenly makes local anti-immigrant sentiment visible.
- **Resolution:** places that publicly reveal hostility see less foreign-resident growth.
- **Implication:** voting changes migration geography via information.

That story is cleaner and broader than the current one.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?
I would say:

> “In Switzerland, municipalities that barely voted for an anti-immigration referendum later saw slower growth in their foreign-resident share than municipalities that barely voted against—even though immigration policy was set nationally, not locally.”

That is the right dinner-party fact.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
A subset would lean in—especially political economists and labor economists. But many would quickly ask:  
“Interesting, but is this really a clean discontinuity or just a smooth correlation between anti-immigrant places and immigrant growth?”

And the awkward truth is: the paper itself already tells them that is the issue.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Almost certainly:

- “Is this really about sorting, and can you show actual migration flows?”
- Or: “Why should 50% matter if no municipal policy changes at that threshold?”
- Or: “Is this just that anti-immigrant places were already on different demographic trends?”

Those are not econometric quibbles; they go to strategic positioning. The paper’s current design is stronger than a raw correlation but weaker than the story it wants to tell, because the institutional meaning of the 50% threshold is thin.

### If the findings are null or modest, is that still interesting?
The results are not null, but they are modest and presented with substantial caution. That can still be interesting if the paper makes the case that even a modest effect implies a new mechanism: **politics as a sorting signal**. Right now it partially makes that case, but not forcefully enough. The caution is appropriate; the problem is that caution has crowded out excitement.

The paper needs to say, in essence:
“Even a small shift in local foreign share generated purely by revealed sentiment is important because it identifies a mechanism outside formal policy.”

That is the “so what.”

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

### What would make it read better?

#### 1. Shorten the institutional background
Section 2 is too long relative to the paper’s core contribution. Readers do not need a mini-history of the initiative’s implementation unless it directly strengthens the signaling argument. Condense it.

#### 2. Move some validity material out of the introduction
The intro is overloaded with design diagnostics. The reader learns too early about McCrary tests, kernels, and p-values. For an editorially strong paper, the intro should prioritize:
- the question,
- the core design intuition,
- the main result,
- the implication.

Right now it reads a bit like an empirical workshop presentation.

#### 3. Front-load the conceptual novelty
The paper should tell the reader by page 1:
“this is about whether political expression changes migrant sorting independent of local policy.”
That line should appear before the literature review.

#### 4. Compress the robustness section
The paper currently gives a lot of main-text real estate to standard RD specification variation. That is not where the strategic value lies. Keep one compact table; move the rest to the appendix.

#### 5. Elevate the mechanism/interpretation section—or cut it back
As written, the mechanisms section is weak because it cannot discriminate among channels. Either:
- develop it into a sharper interpretive section with theory-like discipline and heterogeneity evidence, or
- shorten it and avoid the laundry-list feel.

#### 6. Rework the conclusion
The conclusion is well written, but it mostly restates the theme at a high level. A stronger conclusion would do two things:
- clarify what belief should change if one takes the results seriously,
- explain where else we should expect this mechanism to matter.

### Is the paper front-loaded with the good stuff?
Somewhat, but not enough. The most interesting conceptual point is buried behind institutional and methodological setup.

### Are important results buried?
Yes: the appendix hints at heterogeneity and mentions a deferred analysis of commuter flows. If there is anything there that more directly speaks to sorting behavior, it belongs in the main paper. An AER paper cannot rely on a single suggestive stock-share result alone.

### Is the conclusion adding value?
Moderately. It is elegant, but still more rhetorical than agenda-setting.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

At the moment, this is **not primarily a methods problem**. It is a combination of **framing, scope, and ambition**.

### What is the main gap?

#### Framing problem
The paper’s best idea is larger than its current self-presentation. It should be framed as a paper about **political revelation and spatial sorting**, not as a Swiss referendum RD with a mildly significant effect.

#### Scope problem
The outcome set is too narrow for the paper’s ambition. One stock outcome, however suggestive, is not enough to convincingly establish a broad new mechanism.

#### Ambition problem
The paper is careful, competent, and sensible—but safe. It does not yet do the extra work needed to turn an interesting empirical exercise into a field-defining statement.

#### Novelty problem
Moderate, not fatal. The reverse-channel idea is novel enough, but the current implementation does not make that novelty feel decisive.

### What is the gap between current form and something that excites the top 10 people in the field?
The top people would want one of two things:

1. **A much sharper empirical demonstration of sorting behavior**  
   e.g., migration flows, permit applications, cross-border commuting, nationality-specific responses, housing-market responses, employer outcomes.

2. **A much stronger conceptual repositioning**  
   so that even with modest estimates, the paper clearly opens a new mechanism: the public revelation of local preferences as an economic force.

Right now it has half of (2) and not enough of (1).

### Single most impactful advice
If the author can change only one thing:

**Rebuild the paper around the broader question—whether public political expression changes migrant sorting absent local policy variation—and add one outcome or heterogeneity result that directly speaks to sorting rather than relying mainly on a change in foreign population share.**

That is the single biggest upgrade. If they cannot add new data, they should at minimum fully reframe the paper around the signaling mechanism and stop presenting the RD itself as the central novelty.
