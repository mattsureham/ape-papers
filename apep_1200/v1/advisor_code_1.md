**Idea Fidelity**  
The paper largely tracks the manifest’s vision by exploiting municipal vote share margins in the 2014 Mass Immigration Initiative to study demographic responses, focusing on municipal foreign‐population outcomes. The identification strategy is clearly articulated as a close-vote RDD and the primary data sources (swissdd vote data and STATPOP municipal demographics) are the same as in the manifest. However, the paper omits the broader policy and panel context outlined in the manifest (the canton-level cross-border commuter outcomes and the supplemental DiD spanning 2010–2019). That is, while the manifest proposes a dual approach—municipal RDD for sentiment and canton panel DiD for labor market outcomes—the paper focuses exclusively on municipal demographic sorting and does not pursue the cross-border worker or wage outcomes. Consequently, an important dimension of the original idea (linking local sentiment shocks to labor-market behavior via canton-level outcomes) is missing, and the paper should explicitly acknowledge the narrowing of scope.

**Summary**  
The paper estimates the causal effect of a municipality narrowly voting in favor of the 2014 Swiss Mass Immigration Initiative on subsequent changes in its foreign resident share, using a sharp regression discontinuity design around the 50% yes threshold. The main result suggests a marginally significant decline (~0.8 percentage points) in foreign share growth for “Yes-majority” municipalities, while total population growth remains unaffected. The paper interprets this as suggestive evidence of sentiment-driven immigrant sorting and validates the design with standard RDD checks (density, covariate balance, placebo outcomes/cutoffs).

**Essential Points**

1. **Identification interpretation and validity**: The design is framed as a sharp RDD with the treatment being a revealed anti-immigration majority. Yet the manuscript treats the discontinuity as if it emulates a policy change, while the manifest emphasized that this is a *fuzzy* design since the federal policy did not change at the municipal level. The paper therefore needs to clarify the precise estimand: is the running variable merely capturing sentiment “treatment,” or is it serving as an instrument for municipal policy sentiment in a fuzzy RDD? In either case, the interpretation (local “emotional” signal leading to sorting) relies critically on the assumption that all other channels are smooth at 50%. Given the presence of pre-trend differences (placebo 2010–13) and gradient effects at other cutoffs, the identification is fragile. The authors should more systematically evaluate whether the 50% discontinuity is the only plausible source of variation and, if not, temper causal claims accordingly.

2. **Placebo and gradient evidence weakening the RDD**: The placebo cutoff tests show statistically meaningful discontinuities at 40% and 45%, and the pre-treatment change in foreign share exhibits a non-negligible jump. These findings suggest that the relationship between sentiment and demographics is not localized at 50%, undermining the sharp RDD assumption. The authors currently interpret these results as suggestive patterns, but to credibly claim causal identification they must either (a) demonstrate that the 50% cutoff is substantively distinct (e.g., via heterogeneity in post-vote responses, or by showing the pattern is flat elsewhere when controlling for observable shifts) or (b) reframe the analysis as estimating a broader dose–response and move away from a binary discontinuity framing. As written, the discontinuity assumption is not convincingly defended.

3. **Limited scope relative to manifest and policy relevance**: The manifest argues for linking local sentiment to canton-level cross-border worker flows and wages using a broader panel strategy, which would tie the findings more directly to the policy uncertainty window. The current paper focuses narrowly on demographic outcomes, leaving the manifest’s applied relevance (cross-border commuters and canton-level labor markets) unexplored. Without this, the connection to the Mass Immigration Initiative’s disruption of bilateral labor mobility is tenuous. The authors need to either incorporate the missing canton-level analysis or more clearly motivate why municipal demographics alone provide a sufficient test of the stated research question.

**Suggestions**

- **Reconsider the modeling of the running variable**: The current use of a sharp cutoff assumes the treatment is simply `YesShare >= 50%`. Given the public manifestation of anti-immigrant sentiment and the evidence of a smoother gradient, the authors might instead model the vote share continuously (e.g., local polynomial regression of outcomes on vote share) and interpret the derivative around 50% as the local “slope”. Alternatively, if they wish to keep the RDD framework, it would be helpful to complement it with a regression that includes interaction terms or controls for linear trends in the forcing variable to ensure that the observed jump is not part of a continuous relationship.

- **Address the pre-trend and placebo issues explicitly**: The pre-treatment placebo estimate of -0.35 pp is a signal that municipalities trending toward a yes majority already had distinctive demographic dynamics. The authors could attempt to control for municipality fixed effects (if some panel structure exists) or include additional covariates capturing pre-2014 growth rates to see whether the discontinuity persists once previous trends are accounted for. Additionally, the placebo cutoff results could be visualized to assess whether the pattern reflects curvature rather than discrete jumps—plotting the estimated treatment effect as a function of the cutoff (e.g., using local polynomial estimators across the distribution) could clarify whether 50% is special.

- **Include distributional analyses or heterogeneity**: Exploring whether the effect is concentrated in certain types of municipalities (rural vs. urban, border vs. inland, those with high vs. low initial foreign shares) could shed light on mechanisms and help substantiate the sentiment-based sorting story. For example, if the effect is stronger where information about the vote is more salient (e.g., in small municipalities with local newspapers), that supports the signaling mechanism; if it is uniform, it may reflect pre-existing selection processes.

- **Re-engage with the broader policy outcomes**: If data and space allow, the authors should revisit the manifest’s cross-border worker outcomes. Even a shorter analysis linking municipal sentiment to canton-level cross-border commuter flows or wages would significantly strengthen the paper’s policy relevance. At minimum, discuss why these outcomes were not pursued and whether the municipal demographic effects relate to anticipated canton-level labor-market adjustments.

- **Clarify the substantive significance**: The estimated −0.8 pp change is economically meaningful relative to the 2.0 pp average growth, but the narrative should contextualize both the magnitude and the uncertainty. A standardized effect size or a back-of-envelope calculation of the number of foreign residents “affected” could make the effect more tangible. At the same time, the paper should be more explicit about the interpretation of an effect that is only marginally significant (and perhaps underpowered) and avoid overstating the causal conclusion.

- **Enhance robustness reporting**: While the paper reports kernel and polynomial sensitivity, it would benefit from reporting visual RD plots (with binned averages) to show the discontinuity and from providing robustness checks that include the forcing variable’s higher-order polynomials, placebo outcomes beyond foreign shares (e.g., change in Swiss population), and alternative bandwidth selection methods (e.g., fixed bandwidth). Presenting the distribution of the forcing variable with respect to municipal characteristics may also help readers assess whether the 50% median truly isolates comparable municipalities.

- **Discussion of mechanism details**: The mechanisms section currently lists plausible channels but could be strengthened by suggesting empirical tests or referencing auxiliary data (e.g., municipal-level migrant registrations, firm hires, or rental activity). Even if such data are unavailable, explicitly acknowledging this limitation and proposing future work would add depth. The role of media coverage, social integration policies, or municipal fiscal capacity in mediating the effect could also be briefly discussed.

Overall, the paper takes a promising approach to an important question, but it needs to more carefully defend the localization of the causal effect, address the gradient evidence, and either extend or clearly justify the scope of the analysis relative to the manifest’s broader labor-market focus.
