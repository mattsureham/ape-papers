## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether OSHA’s 2022 Heat National Emphasis Program—the federal government’s main enforcement response to rising workplace heat risk—actually reduced worker injuries. The headline answer is no: once the author uses geographic variation in underlying heat exposure to isolate where the program should have mattered most, there is no detectable reduction in injuries, suggesting that conventional inspection-based enforcement may be poorly suited to climate-driven hazards.

This is a potentially AER-relevant question. Busy economists should care because it sits at the intersection of labor, regulation, and climate adaptation: can legacy regulatory institutions handle new environmental risks, or does climate change require different policy tools?

**Does the paper itself articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Almost, but not quite. The current opening is competent and serious, but it starts with occupational heat as a workplace-safety fact rather than with the broader economic question. The paper’s true hook is not “heat kills workers” but “climate change is stress-testing inherited regulatory institutions.” The introduction should lead with that institutional adaptation question immediately.

**The pitch the paper should have:**

> Climate change is creating hazards that vary across time and space in ways traditional regulation was not designed to manage. This paper studies whether OSHA’s 2022 Heat National Emphasis Program—the federal government’s most important enforcement response to workplace heat—actually reduced injuries in the industries and places where it should have bitten most. Using nationwide establishment-level injury data and cross-state variation in baseline heat exposure, I find no differential injury decline in hotter states, suggesting that inspection-based enforcement may be an ill-suited tool for climate-driven workplace risk.

Then second paragraph:

> The broader question is not just whether one OSHA initiative worked, but whether command-and-control enforcement can adapt to environmental hazards whose intensity is driven by weather rather than fixed workplace conditions. If the answer is no, that has implications far beyond heat policy: it bears on how regulators should respond as climate change increasingly affects labor markets, production, and worker safety.

That is the version that gives the paper a chance to matter beyond OSHA specialists.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution in one sentence:**  
The paper claims to show that OSHA’s principal federal heat-enforcement initiative did not reduce workplace injuries where it should have been most effective, implying a mismatch between standard enforcement tools and climate-driven occupational hazards.

That is a real contribution. But it is not yet fully differentiated from adjacent work.

### Is the contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?
Only partially. The paper names three literatures, but the differentiation is more asserted than demonstrated. Right now the introduction says, in effect, “OSHA enforcement has been studied; heat harms have been studied; I study heat enforcement.” That is fine as a first pass, but it is not enough for a top journal. The author needs to be sharper about what exactly has not been known until now.

The missing contrast is something like:

- prior OSHA papers study enforcement against conventional hazards;
- climate papers document the harm from heat and adaptation in markets/households;
- this paper studies **state capacity under climate stress**: whether an existing regulator can protect workers from a hazard whose incidence fluctuates with weather.

That is a better conceptual niche than “first evaluation of this program.”

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a gap in a literature?
Mixed, but leaning too much toward literature-gap framing. The stronger framing is world-facing:

- weaker: “the literature has not evaluated the regulatory response to heat”  
- stronger: “we do not know whether old regulatory institutions can govern new climate risks”

The paper should relentlessly choose the second.

### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what’s new?
Not quite. Right now they might say: “It’s a DiD/triple-DiD paper on OSHA’s heat enforcement program and finds a null.” That is not enough. You want them to say: “It’s about whether climate adaptation can be delivered through traditional regulatory enforcement, and the answer appears to be no in this important case.”

That is a big difference. One is a policy evaluation. The other is a statement about institutional adaptation to climate change.

### What would make this contribution bigger?
Specific ways to enlarge it:

1. **Make the object of study broader than a single OSHA program.**  
   Reframe the paper as evidence on the limits of enforcement-based adaptation, with the Heat NEP as the test case.

2. **Show outcomes more tightly linked to heat if possible.**

Even if data constraints prevent ideal measures, the paper’s ambition would rise if it got closer to heat-specific harms, summer-season outcomes, ambulance/ER use, workers’ comp claims, or citations/inspection responses. 3. **Open the black box of why enforcement failed.**

4. **Compare enforcement to alternative policy architectures.**

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Climate change is increasing workplace heat exposure. OSHA, a regulator built for conventional workplace hazards, responded with a national heat-enforcement initiative.

### Tension
It is unclear whether inspection-based enforcement—a tool designed for fixed, observable workplace hazards—can mitigate a hazard driven by ambient weather and fluctuating exposure conditions.

### Resolution
The program does not appear to reduce injuries differentially in the hotter places where it should have had the strongest bite.

### Implications
If this pattern is real, then climate adaptation may require different regulatory architecture—more rule-based standards, different monitoring technologies, or alternative policy tools—rather than simply more of the traditional inspection model.

This is a good narrative arc in principle. The problem is that the paper does not fully commit to it. It oscillates between two stories:

1. **A program-evaluation story:** did the Heat NEP work?
2. **A regulatory-design story:** can enforcement regulate climate hazards?

The second story is much better, but the paper keeps reverting to the first. That makes the manuscript feel a bit like a set of sensible empirical results looking for a bigger frame.

**What story should it be telling?**  
It should tell a clean institutional-design story:

- Old regulators were built for static hazards.
- Climate change creates dynamic hazards.
- OSHA’s Heat NEP is a natural test of whether the old tool kit can stretch.
- In the data, it does not.
- Therefore, climate adaptation policy cannot just be “more enforcement of the same kind.”

That is the story. The current draft has the ingredients, but not the discipline.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?
“OSHA’s flagship national heat-enforcement initiative didn’t reduce injuries more in hotter states, where the policy should have mattered most.”

That is the line.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
They would **lean in briefly**, but whether they stay engaged depends on the second sentence. If the follow-up is just “I run a triple-diff on OSHA injury data,” interest fades. If the follow-up is “it suggests traditional enforcement may not be a workable climate adaptation tool,” interest rises.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Almost certainly:  
**“Why didn’t it work?”**  
And then:
- Was enforcement intensity too small?
- Were firms already adapted in hot places?
- Are the outcomes too broad / too annual to detect effects?
- Did the program change inspections or citations at all?

Those are exactly the questions the current paper raises but cannot fully answer. That is the paper’s strategic weakness.

### If the findings are null or modest, is the null itself interesting?
Yes, potentially very much so. But only if the paper frames the null as informative about **institutional limits**, not merely as “we didn’t find an effect.”

Right now the paper partly succeeds. The null is not presented as a failed experiment; it is linked to deterrence and climate hazard structure. That is good. But to be publishable at the AER level, the paper must make the null feel decisive and conceptually revealing. At present it still leaves too much room for readers to conclude: “Interesting, but perhaps the outcome is noisy or the treatment margin is weak.”

A null can be top-journal if it closes off a plausible policy channel. This draft is not yet there rhetorically.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

Bluntly: in its current form, this feels more like a solid field-journal paper than an AER paper. The main reason is not that the question is unimportant. It is that the paper has not yet converted a narrow program evaluation into a broad economics argument.

### What is the gap?

**Primarily a framing problem, secondarily a scope problem.**

- **Framing problem:** The science/story currently reads as “an evaluation of OSHA’s Heat NEP with a null triple-diff.” That is not enough.
- **Scope problem:** The paper wants to conclude something large about regulatory design, but the evidence remains concentrated on one program and a somewhat indirect outcome set.
- **Some novelty problem:** The combination of OSHA + heat is novel enough, but “regulation of climate-related harms” is a crowded area unless the institutional insight is made sharper.
- **Ambition problem:** The paper is competent but safe. It evaluates the obvious treatment and outcome, reaches a sensible null, and offers plausible interpretations. AER papers usually either answer a larger question, use a more striking design/data combination, or produce a more belief-updating fact.

### What would excite the top 10 people in this field?
One of two things:

1. **A cleaner, broader conceptual payoff:** convincing evidence that inspection-based enforcement systematically fails for dynamic environmental hazards, not just this one OSHA initiative; or
2. **Richer evidence on mechanism/implementation:** showing that the program did or did not alter inspections, citations, compliance behavior, and ultimately injuries.

Right now the paper stops at the reduced-form outcome stage and then speculates. That limits its reach.

### Single most impactful piece of advice
**Reframe the paper around the general question “Can legacy enforcement institutions adapt to climate risk?” and reorganize every section to support that claim, with the Heat NEP as the empirical test case rather than the whole point of the paper.**

That one change would do the most to move it toward AER territory. If the author cannot broaden the evidence, then at minimum they must broaden and sharpen the question.
