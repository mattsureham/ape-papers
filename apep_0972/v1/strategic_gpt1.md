## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple, policy-relevant question: did state laws letting small breweries bypass wholesale distributors actually cause the craft brewing jobs boom? Using staggered state deregulation and employment data, the paper’s headline claim is no: self-distribution reform did not measurably increase beverage-manufacturing employment, suggesting the craft beer expansion was not primarily driven by this celebrated supply-side reform.

A busy economist should care because this is not really about beer. It is about a broader class of deregulatory policies that are sold as entrepreneurship and job-creation engines. If one of the canonical “cut out the middleman” reforms did not produce employment gains in one of the most visible boom sectors of the last two decades, that is potentially interesting well beyond alcohol regulation.

**Does the paper articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Mostly yes, better than many papers. The paper gets to the question quickly and states the answer in paragraph two. That said, the current opening is still a bit too sector-descriptive and a bit too confident about the interpretation. It foregrounds the craft boom and state policy activity, but it could do more to frame the paper as a general economic question about whether reducing vertical/distribution constraints generates net local employment.

### The pitch the paper should have in the first two paragraphs

Here is the version the paper should lead with:

> States frequently justify deregulatory reforms by claiming they will unleash entrepreneurship and create jobs. In the U.S. beer market, one of the most prominent examples is self-distribution reform, which allows small breweries to bypass mandatory wholesalers and sell directly to retailers. These laws are widely credited with helping fuel the craft brewing boom.
>
> This paper asks whether that claim is true for employment. Exploiting staggered adoption of self-distribution laws across states, I find little evidence that these reforms increased beverage-manufacturing employment or geographic expansion. The broader lesson is that removing an intermediary may alter how firms reach market without necessarily generating net job growth.

That version makes the beer setting a vehicle for a larger economic question rather than the whole point of the exercise.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:**  
The paper shows that a widely celebrated deregulatory reform in alcohol distribution—brewery self-distribution—does not appear to have generated detectable net employment growth in beverage manufacturing.

### Is this clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?
Only partially. The paper distinguishes itself from work on brewery counts, liquor-store entry, and vertical restraints, but the differentiation is not yet sharp enough. Right now the introduction says, in effect, “others study market structure and entry; I study employment.” That is a difference in outcome variable, but not yet a fully persuasive difference in scientific contribution.

The problem is that “we look at employment instead of establishment counts” can sound incremental unless the paper tells readers why employment is the economically decisive margin. Is the point that policy advocates explicitly sold these laws as job creators? Is the point that entry effects without employment effects imply reallocation rather than expansion? That needs to be much more explicit.

It is mixed, but leaning too much toward literature-gap framing. The strongest version is a world question:

- **World question:** Do entry-oriented deregulatory reforms actually create jobs, or mainly reshuffle activity across firms and channels?

That is stronger than:

- **Literature-gap framing:** prior papers study brewery counts, not labor-market outcomes.

The paper has the ingredients for the world-question framing, but it does not fully commit.

### Could a smart economist explain what is new after reading the introduction?
They could probably say: “It’s a staggered DiD on self-distribution laws and beverage employment, and it finds a null.” That is clear enough mechanically, but strategically weak. The risk is exactly that they summarize it as “another DiD paper about a state policy and a null result.”

### What would make this contribution bigger?
Specific ways to make it bigger:

1. **Reframe around net employment versus market structure.**  
   Don’t sell this as “beer policy had no effect on jobs.” Sell it as “deregulation can change market access and firm composition without increasing aggregate employment.” That is a much bigger claim.

2. **Put policy rhetoric at center stage.**

3. **Bring in the displacement margin.**

4. **Sharpen the outcome choice.**  
   If the paper can’t observe brewery-specific employment, it should argue more carefully that aggregate beverage-manufacturing employment is the right welfare-relevant margin. Otherwise readers will suspect it is missing the actual locus of impact.

5. **Connect to other direct-to-consumer / disintermediation policies.**

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
The world before the paper: the craft brewing sector boomed, and self-distribution laws are widely believed to have helped by removing a regulatory bottleneck.

### Tension
The puzzle is that these laws are politically celebrated as entrepreneurial/job-creation reforms, but there is little direct evidence that they generated net employment rather than simply changing routes to market or firm composition.

### Resolution
The paper finds no robust differential employment response in beverage manufacturing following self-distribution deregulation.

### Implications
The implication should be: deregulation of distribution constraints may affect market structure, bargaining, and product availability without producing aggregate job growth. Policymakers should not assume that removing intermediaries creates employment.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
It has a **serviceable** arc, but not a fully persuasive one. The main issue is that the narrative currently jumps too quickly from a sector-specific null to a broad causal story: “the craft brewing boom was demand-side.” That is a stronger interpretive claim than the rest of the paper’s narrative really earns. Strategically, this overreach weakens the story because readers will push back immediately: a null on NAICS 312 employment is not the same as proving the boom was demand-driven.

So yes, there is a story, but it is not yet the right story.

### What story should it be telling instead?
Not “self-distribution didn’t matter.”  
Better: **“Self-distribution may have mattered for who entered and how firms sold, but it did not translate into detectable net employment growth.”**

That story is more credible, more nuanced, and actually more interesting. It converts the paper from a blunt debunking exercise into a broader lesson about the limits of supply-side job-creation rhetoric.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?
I would lead with something like:

> Twenty states changed the law to let small breweries bypass wholesalers, and yet beverage-manufacturing employment grew no faster in those states than elsewhere.

That is crisp and memorable.

### Would people lean in?
Some would. The beer setting is vivid and the “middleman myth” framing is catchy. But they will only stay engaged if the paper quickly makes clear why this is not just a quirky industry null.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Almost certainly:

- “Okay, but did the laws increase brewery entry or small-firm activity even if net employment didn’t rise?”
- Or: “Is this just because your outcome is too aggregated to see craft breweries specifically?”

Those are exactly the strategic vulnerabilities. The paper needs to anticipate them and define its contribution around them.

### Is the null itself interesting?
Yes, conditionally. Nulls can be publishable when they puncture a strong policy narrative or discipline an important presumption. Here the null is potentially interesting because:
- the policy is salient,
- the sector visibly boomed,
- and the reform was sold as a growth engine.

But the paper must make the case that this is a **meaningful null on the economically relevant margin**, not just a failure to find significance in a noisy aggregate dataset.

Right now it is close, but not fully there. The “powered null” language helps, though the specific phrasing is a bit defensive. The stronger move is to say: **even if these laws helped some breweries, they were not a first-order driver of net job creation.** That is a more believable and more important takeaway.
