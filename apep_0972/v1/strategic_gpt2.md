## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple policy question: did allowing small breweries to bypass alcohol wholesalers create jobs during the U.S. craft beer boom? Using staggered state adoption of self-distribution laws, the paper argues the answer is no: the boom in craft brewing employment appears to have been driven by consumer demand, not by deregulation of distribution.

A busy economist should care because this is, in principle, a sharp test of a broader and important claim: when does supply-side deregulation translate into real economic activity rather than merely reallocating surplus or changing market structure?

**Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Not quite. The paper gets to the question quickly, which is good, but the opening is still more descriptive than strategic. It starts with county counts and sector growth, then moves to craft beer color, then to policy adoption. The core reason to care is not really “beer”; it is that this is a test of a widely used deregulatory argument in a salient, politically resonant market. The introduction should foreground that immediately.

**What the first two paragraphs should say instead:**

> Many regulatory reforms are justified with a familiar promise: remove an intermediary, lower entry barriers, and jobs will follow. This logic has shaped state alcohol policy, where advocates of self-distribution laws argue that allowing small breweries to bypass wholesalers should spur entry, expansion, and employment. Yet despite the centrality of this claim in policy debate, we have little evidence on whether such deregulation actually creates jobs rather than simply changing how products reach market.
>
> This paper studies whether self-distribution laws caused the craft brewing employment boom in the United States. Exploiting staggered adoption of these laws across states, I find little evidence that they increased beverage-manufacturing employment, hiring, or geographic expansion. The central implication is broader than beer: lowering vertical distribution constraints may alter market organization without producing the employment gains that often anchor the political case for deregulation.

That is the AER version of the pitch: broader question first, beer as the setting, null as economically meaningful.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution in one sentence:**  
The paper’s contribution is to show that a prominent form of vertical deregulation—brewery self-distribution—did not measurably increase employment during the craft beer boom, suggesting that the boom was driven more by demand than by removal of a distribution bottleneck.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?
Only partially. The paper cites a few adjacent literatures, but the differentiation is not yet sharp enough. Right now the contribution reads as: “others study regulation/entry/variety; I study employment.” That is a difference in outcome, but not yet a clearly bigger intellectual move.

To work at AER level, the paper must distinguish itself not just by using a different dependent variable, but by making a broader claim about what vertical deregulation does and does not do:
- it may affect entry, assortment, and margins,
- but not necessarily net employment,
- because equilibrium adjustment occurs through reallocation rather than expansion.

That broader economic claim is present, but only late and too timidly.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a gap in a literature?
Mostly the former, which is a strength. “Did self-distribution cause the employment boom?” is a world question. But the paper repeatedly slips into “no one has studied labor market outcomes” framing, which is weaker. AER wants the world question.

### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what’s new?
At the moment, maybe—but not cleanly. They might say: “It’s a DiD paper on self-distribution laws and brewery employment, and they mostly find no effect.” That is not enough. You want them to say: “It shows that one of the canonical political-economy stories about deregulation—remove the middleman, create jobs—fails in this setting, even though the sector visibly boomed.”

Right now it still risks sounding like “another staggered DiD paper on a niche regulation.”

### What would make this contribution bigger?
Several possibilities:

1. **Move from employment to industry structure.**  
   The current outcome is too blunt relative to the mechanism. If self-distribution matters, it may show up first in:
   - establishment counts,
   - small-firm employment share,
   - size distribution,
   - survival,
   - local product availability,
   - wholesaler employment versus brewery employment.
   
   If the paper showed “no net jobs, but meaningful reallocation toward small producers,” that would be a much bigger contribution.

2. **Measure the directly treated margin more precisely.**  
   NAICS 312 is too broad for a paper making strong claims about craft breweries. The paper knows this and admits it. Strategically, that is dangerous: it invites the reader to conclude the paper is asking a sharp question with a noisy outcome. A better outcome measure—brewery-specific employment, establishment counts, or linked administrative data—would raise the stakes.

3. **Lean into equilibrium logic.**  
   The best version of this paper is not “self-distribution doesn’t matter”; it is “self-distribution can matter for market structure without mattering for aggregate jobs.” That is a generalizable, economics-forward contribution.

4. **Compare labor effects to entry effects directly.**  
   If prior work finds more breweries, and this paper finds no more jobs, then the interesting question is not merely whether jobs rose. It is why increased entry did not translate into employment growth. That comparison could make the paper much more intellectually ambitious.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
There was a visible and dramatic craft brewing boom. Policymakers and industry advocates often credited self-distribution laws for enabling it by removing wholesalers as a bottleneck.

### Tension
The problem is that a booming sector and a plausible policy story are not the same thing. Did self-distribution actually create economic activity, or did it simply coincide with a demand-driven transformation already underway?

### Resolution
The paper finds little evidence that self-distribution laws increased beverage-manufacturing employment, hiring, or county-level geographic spread.

### Implications
The implication is that deregulation of vertical restraints may not create net jobs even when it seems tailor-made to foster entrepreneurship; it may instead affect market structure, margins, or who captures rents.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
It has the raw ingredients, but the arc is only **serviceable**, not compelling. The draft often reads like:
1. here is the policy,
2. here is the design,
3. here are the null results,
4. here is some interpretation.

That is more “results plus commentary” than a tightly told story.

### Is it a collection of results looking for a story?
A bit, yes. The paper has one main result and then several extensions, but they do not yet build toward a larger payoff. The education heterogeneity, for example, does not meaningfully advance the story. It feels included because the data allow it, not because the narrative requires it.

### What story should it be telling?
The story should be:

> Policymakers often mistake constraints on market access for constraints on aggregate activity. In craft beer, removing the wholesaler looked like an obvious way to create jobs. But the boom was demand-led, and this deregulation did not move net employment. The lesson is not that the policy is irrelevant; it is that reforms affecting market organization need not deliver the employment gains used to sell them politically.

That is a coherent AER-adjacent narrative. The current draft is a narrower “myth-busting” paper.

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?
“I have a paper showing that one of the most celebrated deregulations behind the craft beer boom—letting small breweries bypass wholesalers—did not measurably increase employment.”

That is a decent opening fact.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
They would **lean in briefly**, because the claim is surprising and intuitive. But the next question comes fast: “Interesting—but is employment the right margin, and are you measuring the treated sector precisely enough?” If the answer sounds like “well, I use NAICS 312,” attention will fade.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Almost certainly:
- “Did it affect brewery counts, firm size distribution, or survival?”
- “Could it have changed composition without changing net jobs?”
- “Are you just too aggregated to see the effect?”

Those are exactly the strategic vulnerabilities.

### If the findings are null or modest: is the null itself interesting?
Potentially yes, but only if the paper more forcefully establishes why the null is informative. The current draft makes a reasonable attempt by calling it a “powered null,” but that alone will not carry it. For an AER audience, the null matters if it overturns a widely believed mechanism and yields a broader lesson about deregulation and real effects.

Right now it still risks feeling like: “we didn’t find anything in a broad industry code.” The author must work harder to show that the null is conceptually revealing, not just statistically non-significant.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

Be honest: in current form, this is **not an AER paper**. The main gap is not mostly econometrics; it is strategic.

### What is the gap?
Primarily:

Somewhat. The core claim is interesting, but “state policy DiD with null effect on employment” is not by itself novel enough for AER. The novelty would have to come from a sharper conceptual contribution: deregulation can alter entry or structure without increasing jobs.

That would convert the paper from “null result in craft beer” to “important lesson about what deregulation does.”
