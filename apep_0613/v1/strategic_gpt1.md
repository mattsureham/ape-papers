## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple, policy-relevant question: when women win executive office, do they actually govern differently? Using close mayoral elections in Mexico, the paper finds that electing a woman does not meaningfully change municipal spending composition, suggesting that institutional budget constraints may swamp leader gender even in a setting where many observers would expect larger differences.

Why should a busy economist care? Because the paper speaks to a broad and important claim in political economy and public economics: whether descriptive representation translates into substantive policy change. Mexico is a useful test case precisely because female representation rose sharply and municipal institutions appear, at least superficially, to leave more room for discretion than the U.S. cases studied before.

Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs? **Reasonably, but not sharply enough.** The current opening is competent, but it starts from a politician anecdote and moves quickly into the existing U.S. paper. That makes the paper sound like “Ferreira and Gyourko in Mexico” before it sounds like an important world question. The introduction should lead with the bigger issue: parity reforms are being sold partly on the premise that who governs changes what government does; this paper tests that proposition in a high-stakes setting where the answer is not obvious.

### The pitch the paper should have

Countries around the world have expanded women’s political representation under the expectation that female leaders will govern differently, especially in allocating public resources. Yet we still know little about whether leader gender changes policy in executive office outside a handful of developed-country settings. Using close mixed-gender mayoral elections in Mexico—a country that recently saw a sharp rise in female mayors and where municipal governments control visible spending choices—this paper shows that electing a woman does not significantly change the composition of municipal spending. The broader implication is that increasing women’s representation need not produce immediate fiscal-policy change, either because executive constraints are strong or because female and male leaders selected into office are more similar than the rhetoric suggests.

That is the AER-facing pitch: not “another RDD,” but “a direct test of a central claim behind gender representation reforms.”

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:** The paper provides close-election evidence from Mexico showing that electing female mayors does not materially change municipal spending composition, extending prior null findings on leader gender and fiscal policy to a developing-country executive setting.

Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers? **Only partially.** The paper identifies Ferreira and Gyourko as the canonical comparator, which is right, but the differentiation is still too mechanical: new country, same design, same null. It needs to be much clearer on why Mexico is a hard test of the “institutions neutralize leader gender” hypothesis. Right now the contribution risks sounding like geographic replication.

Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the **world** or filling a gap in a **literature**? **It does both, but leans too much toward literature-gap framing.** The strongest version is a world question: when parity reforms increase female executive representation, should we expect budgets to change? That is stronger than “there is little evidence outside the U.S.”

Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what’s new? **Yes, but only at a middling level of precision.** They would probably say: “It’s an RDD on female mayors in Mexico and it finds no spending effects.” That is not enough. They should instead say: “It tests whether the substantive-representation argument behind parity mandates survives in a setting where female representation rose sharply and municipal executives plausibly have room to reallocate budgets; it doesn’t.”

What would make this contribution bigger?

1. **Shift from ‘do women spend differently?’ to ‘when does descriptive representation translate into substantive representation?’**  
   That framing enlarges the paper beyond municipal finance.

2. **Make the test more obviously demanding.**  
   The introduction should emphasize why Mexico should have been a place where effects, if they exist, ought to show up: rapid parity-driven increase in female officeholders, higher baseline gender inequality, decentralized local politics, visible distributive spending.

3. **Add a more revealing outcome family if available.**  
   Spending composition is clean, but perhaps not the margin where executive identity matters most. A bigger paper would examine procurement patterns, targeted social programs, bureaucratic appointments, corruption complaints, service delivery, public safety, or project selection. As written, the paper may be testing a margin that is too coarse.

4. **Leverage heterogeneity that maps to theory.**  
   The paper hints at fiscal rigidity. Then the most natural value-add is to show whether effects are larger where rigidity is lower: municipalities with higher own-source revenue, lower payroll commitments, less earmarked transfers, larger administrative capacity, post-parity cohorts, etc. That would move it from “null result” to “boundary conditions.”

At present, the contribution is intelligible but not yet big. It still reads a bit like “another close-election paper about female leaders,” albeit a useful one.

How should the paper position itself relative to those neighbors? **Build on and adjudicate between them.**  
The paper is sitting between two broad traditions:

- the **identity matters** literature, often from quota/reservation settings, showing women alter priorities, representation, or citizen beliefs; and
- the **executive constraints / median-voter / office-neutrality** literature, showing elected leaders often have less policy discretion than rhetoric implies.

That is the real conversation. The paper should not merely “extend Ferreira and Gyourko to Mexico.” It should say: prior work leaves open whether null effects reflect strong U.S. institutions or a deeper fact about executive office; Mexico helps distinguish those stories.

Is the paper currently positioned too narrowly or too broadly? **Too narrowly.**  
It is currently positioned as municipal finance + gender + Mexico. That is a good field-journal positioning, but not yet AER positioning. The broader audience is political economy, public economics, development, and gender economics. The paper should speak to all four.

What literature does the paper seem unaware of, or at least under-engaged with?

- **Political selection and convergence**: women who win close executive races may be selected to look like male winners.
- **State capacity / bureaucratic constraints / fiscal rigidities**: if the mechanism is constraints, this literature should be more central.
- **Substantive vs descriptive representation** from political science: the parity-policy link is an old debate, and the paper should use that vocabulary more explicitly.
- **Leader effects literature** more broadly: when do individual leaders matter for policy, and when are they constrained by institutions?

What fields should it be speaking to?  
At minimum: public finance, political economy, development, and gender economics. Potentially also comparative politics. Right now it speaks mostly to one empirical subliterature on female politicians.

Is the paper having the right conversation? **Not quite.**  
The highest-impact framing is not “female mayors in Mexico.” It is “does electing underrepresented leaders change policy when institutional constraints are binding?” That connects this paper to a larger literature on whether politician identity matters at all.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Many countries have expanded women’s representation, often with the expectation that female leaders will reorient public spending toward social priorities.

### Tension
Existing evidence is mixed and context-specific. In particular, the main U.S. evidence finds no spending differences, but it is unclear whether that is because U.S. local governments are especially constrained. Mexico should be a setting where differences might plausibly appear.

### Resolution
They do not: female mayors in Mexico do not significantly alter spending composition.

### Implications
Either executive fiscal discretion is more limited than many claims about representation presume, or the women and men who win competitive executive office are not very different in policy choices. Either way, one should be cautious about promising immediate policy transformation from parity reforms.

Does the paper have a clear narrative arc? **Serviceable, but not fully earned.**  
The ingredients are all there, but the paper still feels somewhat like a set of results organized around a familiar design rather than a high-level question resolved by a particularly revealing case.

The missing piece is stronger tension. The introduction says Mexico is a “powerful test case,” but it does not vividly establish why a reader should have expected the answer to differ from the U.S. If you want the null to matter, you need to sharpen the prior: why was this setting one where effects should have shown up? Without that, the story is just “null in another country.”

What story should it be telling?  
Not “do women spend differently in Mexico?” but:

- parity reforms have changed who holds office;
- many arguments for those reforms rely, explicitly or implicitly, on substantive policy change;
- whether that happens depends on the interaction of leader identity and institutional discretion;
- Mexico is an unusually informative place to test that interaction;
- the answer is no, at least for budget composition.

That is a cleaner and more important narrative.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?

> “Mexico sharply increased female mayoral representation after parity reforms, but in close races female mayors do not appear to spend municipal budgets differently from male mayors.”

Would people lean in or reach for their phones? **Mild lean-in, not full lean-in.**  
The topic has broad appeal, and the contrast between political rhetoric and policy reality is interesting. But the current version undersells the surprise and overstates the fiscal category analysis. If presented as a test of the substantive-representation claim behind parity reforms, it gets more attention.

What follow-up question would they ask?

Almost certainly: **“Maybe budgets are the wrong margin—do female mayors differ in corruption, procurement, services, staffing, or project choice?”**  
That is the key strategic vulnerability. The paper should anticipate it more aggressively. Right now the conclusion mentions this, but too late and too passively.

Are the null findings themselves interesting? **Yes, but only if the paper makes the null do conceptual work.**  
A null is interesting when:

1. theory or conventional wisdom predicts a non-null;
2. the setting is one where effects should be visible;
3. the estimate is informative enough to rule out important magnitudes; and
4. the null helps discriminate between competing views of the world.

This paper can satisfy those conditions, but currently only halfway. It does a decent job on (3) for transfers, less so on the broader conceptual payoff in (4). The null should be sold as evidence on the limits of officeholder identity under institutional constraints, not as a failed attempt to find gender effects.

   For AER purposes, long bandwidth and placebo walkthroughs are less valuable in the main narrative than one concise paragraph plus appendix material. The paper risks sounding defensive.

6. **Bring mechanisms/boundary conditions closer to the main results.**  
   The discussion section contains the intellectually interesting material—fiscal inertia, selection, implementation constraints—but too late. A brief preview of those interpretations should come right after the main result.

7. **The conclusion should do more than summarize.**  
   Right now it mostly restates the null and says maybe other outcomes matter. It should instead return to the broader claim: parity reforms alter representation, but policy consequences depend on the margins over which executives have real discretion.

One additional stylistic point: the title is serviceable but a bit small-bore. Something closer to **“Do Female Leaders Change Fiscal Policy? Evidence from Close Mayoral Elections in Mexico”** would travel better. It broadens the conversation while keeping the design transparent.
