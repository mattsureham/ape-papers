### 1. Idea Fidelity

The paper partially pursues the original idea but deviates substantially in key elements of the data and sample construction, undermining feasibility claims in the manifest. The manifest promised a close-election RDD on 3,946 mixed-gender competitive races (1989-2024, |margin| < 5%) with 768 female winners, merged with full INEGI EFIPEM data (1989-2023, ~2,386 municipalities/year), explicitly targeting social transfers, infrastructure, and security spending. Instead, the paper restricts to 468 "confirmed" mixed-gender races (2008-2022, post-2000 for data availability), yielding effective samples of 200-300 observations per outcome after bandwidth selection—over an order of magnitude smaller. Runner-up gender is imputed via name heuristics (not in manifest), drastically shrinking the sample from 842 potential mixed-gender races. The time period excludes pre-2008 variation (e.g., low female representation pre-parity), security spending is omitted, and placebo outcomes/revenue tests are under-implemented. These changes miss the manifest's core strength: abundant close elections for high power. The research question (female mayors and fiscal composition) and RDD strategy are retained, but scaled-down execution weakens novelty relative to promised "well-powered" design.

### 2. Summary

This paper uses a close-election RDD on 468 mixed-gender municipal races in Mexico (2008-2022) to estimate the effect of electing a female mayor on spending shares (payroll, social transfers, investment, etc.), merged with INEGI EFIPEM fiscal data. It finds precise null effects on social transfers (0.2 pp, p=0.81) and insignificant suggestive shifts toward payroll (+4.3 pp, p=0.15) and away from investment (-4.7 pp, p=0.16), robust to bandwidths and donuts. Results extend Ferreira and Gyourko (2014) null findings on U.S. mayors to a developing context, attributing outcomes to fiscal rigidities over gender preferences.

### 3. Essential Points

**1. Sample construction severely limits power and credibility of the RDD.** The manifest's 3,946 close elections (|margin| < 5%) with 768 female winners promised high power, but restricting to "confirmed" mixed-gender races via runner-up name heuristics yields only 468 elections (169 female wins), with effective N=200-300 after CCT bandwidths (e.g., 244 for investment). This underpowers null claims, especially for social transfers (SD=4.8%, min detectable effect ~3-4 pp at 80% power, α=0.05). Name-based gender imputation introduces measurement error in counterfactual gender, biasing the running variable/treatment toward non-mixed races and violating as-if randomness. Authors must validate heuristics (e.g., accuracy audit on 10-20% subsample via manual checks or supplementary data like Lucardi & Rosas 2025) and expand to all ~24k elections by proxying mixed-gender (e.g., via winner gender flips in close races) or relaxing post-2000 restriction—failure risks rejection.

**2. Pre-period covariate imbalance undermines key results.** Table 2 Panel A shows significant discontinuity in pre-election admin payroll share (10.5 pp, p<0.01, bandwidth=8.5%), the outcome with the largest suggestive effect. This violates continuity in covariates, suggesting selection on unobservables (e.g., municipalities favoring female candidates have higher payroll inertia). Social transfers/investment balance (p>0.10), but payroll result is uninterpretable without correction (e.g., include pre-payroll as control or bound bias via Oster-style decomposition). Transparent discussion is good but insufficient—authors must re-estimate with controls or exclude payroll, or reject if uncorrectable.

**3. Incomplete validity checks and missing security outcome erode identification credibility.** No RD plots (density, outcomes, covariates)—essential for AER:Insights visual inspection of continuity/smoothness. McCrary p=0.15 is marginal; placebo cutoffs are mentioned but not tabulated (one spurious p=0.02 noted). Manifest-highlighted security spending is absent despite relevance (Mexico violence context). Authors must add plots (rdplot), full placebo table (±1%, ±3%, ±5%), security share estimates, and CCT bandwidth table. Multiple testing adjustment (e.g., Romano-Wolf) needed for 6 outcomes. If unaddressed, paper lacks credible identification.

### 4. Suggestions

The paper is crisply written, with strong institutional detail, transparent robustness (bandwidth/donut tables), and policy-relevant framing—excellent for Insights format. Null results are convincingly argued for social transfers (tight CI rules out ±2.8 pp vs. 8.8% mean), paralleling U.S. evidence thoughtfully. To elevate to AER-quality:

**Expand and validate data/sample (~30% effort).** Beyond essentials, merge supplementary sources: Lucardi & Rosas (2025) precinct data for precise runner-up gender (manifest-mentioned, Zenodo-available); pre-2008 EFIPEM for baselines (balances most covariates already). Test sensitivity to gender imputation thresholds (e.g., high-confidence names only). Compute power curves (e.g., via rdpower) showing min detectable effects across bandwidths—reinforces null credibility. Include revenue shares (manifest placebo) and federal transfers (FAIS/FORTAMUN) as outcomes to probe mechanisms (e.g., do females shift within discretion?).

**Enhance visuals and empirics (~20% effort).** Add 4-6 RD plots: McCrary density, main outcomes, key covariates (pre-payroll), placebo cutoffs—use rdplot/rdrobust for binned scatter with CCT h. Figure bandwidth sensitivity (coefficients + 95% CIs vs. h). Tabulate quadratic/global polynomials (briefly mentioned) and cluster-robust SEs (staggered elections by state-year). Heterogeneity: Interact treatment with municipality size (log exp), parity reform timing (pre/post-2015), or violence (Pulido 2025 data)—does effect vary where inequality/discretion highest? Standardized effects (Appendix good) could benchmark vs. literature (e.g., Brollo & Nannicini 2016 Italian mayors).

**Strengthen mechanisms and external validity (~20% effort).** Discussion speculates fiscal inertia/selection well—test via leads/lags (1-3yr post vs. pre-term) or subsample small/large municipalities (inertia stronger in small?). Link to violence: Merge INEGI security spending or Pulido (2025) outcomes—null fiscal holds despite violence context? Compare explicitly to Ferreira & Gyourko: Table replicating their specs (e.g., crime if data available). Event study plot for dynamic effects over 3-year term.

**Polish framing and extensions (~20% effort).** Intro: Quantify discretion (e.g., % non-earmarked revenue: 10-30% per text). Abstract: Report CIs prominently. JEL/keywords spot-on. Add online appendix: Full balance table (all covariates: pop, GDP, party), raw data description (e.g., % races by state), code link (GitHub praised). Broader implications: Discuss vs. Chattopadhyay & Duflo (2004) India councils (positive social spending)—why null here? (shorter terms? executive vs. legislative?). For Insights (4-8k words), trim background slightly; expand SDE appendix to all outcomes.

**Minor tweaks.** Fix typos (e.g., "emagar2025" inconsistent citation; Table 1 "38.5%" vs. text 45.5%; p-values without stars in main table). Ensure reproducibility: Exact rdrobust syntax/options. With fixes, this could be a strong "publish minor revision"—novel context, clean null with mechanisms. Total length fits well; execution time note amusing but irrelevant.
