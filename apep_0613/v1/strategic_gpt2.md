## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple, policy-relevant question: when countries use gender parity rules to elect many more women, do women in executive office actually govern differently? Using close municipal elections in Mexico, the paper finds that electing a female mayor does not meaningfully change the composition of municipal spending, suggesting that institutional constraints may swamp leader gender even in a setting where one might have expected larger effects than in the United States.

Why should a busy economist care? Because a huge amount of rhetoric around women’s political representation assumes that descriptive representation translates into immediate policy change. If that is not true even in a developing-country context with weaker institutions and rising female representation, that is an important substantive claim about political selection, bureaucratic constraint, and the limits of identity-based representation.

Does the paper articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs? Reasonably, but not optimally. The current intro opens with a politician anecdote and then moves quickly to Ferreira and Gyourko. That makes the paper sound like a geographic replication exercise before it sounds like a first-order question about whether parity reforms change policy. The first two paragraphs should lead with the global policy question, then explain why Mexico is the critical test case.

### The pitch the paper should have

Around the world, gender parity reforms are transforming who holds political office. But do these reforms change policy, or do they mainly change who occupies constrained institutions? This paper studies that question in Mexican municipalities, where female representation rose sharply after parity mandates and where mayors plausibly have more fiscal discretion than in the U.S.; using close mixed-gender elections, I find that electing a woman does not significantly change municipal spending composition. The result suggests that the link between descriptive and substantive representation is weaker than commonly presumed, not only in rich countries with rigid institutions but also in a major developing democracy.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:**  
The paper shows that electing female mayors in Mexico does not materially alter municipal spending composition, implying that the null effects found for U.S. mayors may reflect a broader feature of local governance rather than a peculiarity of American institutions.

This contribution is clear enough, but only partly differentiated from the nearest literature. Right now the paper’s novelty is framed mostly as “Ferreira and Gyourko, but in Mexico.” That is a contribution, but not yet a large one. The paper needs to distinguish itself from at least three strands:

1. **Female leaders and policy outcomes in developing countries**  
2. **Quota/parity papers on descriptive vs. substantive representation**  
3. **Executive discretion under local fiscal constraints**

At present, the contribution is framed mostly as filling a gap in a literature: “thin outside the U.S.” That is weaker than framing it as a world question: **Do women in executive office change policy when institutions give them room to do so?** The latter is stronger and more AER-like.

Could a smart economist explain what is new after reading the introduction? Yes, but the risk is they would say: “It’s an RD on female mayors in Mexico and finds null effects on spending, extending Ferreira-Gyourko.” That is not nothing, but it is also not enough for AER on its own.

What would make the contribution bigger?

- **A different outcome margin:** Spending composition may be the wrong margin if budgets are rigid. The paper itself hints at more discretionary outcomes—procurement, corruption, staffing, project mix, service delivery, tax collection, crime, bureaucratic appointments. Any one of these could turn the paper from “no effect on aggregate shares” into “gender matters where discretion is real, not where budgets are sticky.”
- **A different comparison:** The interesting comparison is not merely Mexico vs. U.S., but **high-discretion vs. low-discretion municipalities**, or **pre-parity vs. post-parity**, or **municipalities with more own-source revenue vs. transfer dependence**. That would sharpen the mechanism.
- **A different framing:** Instead of “do women spend differently?”, frame it as “when does leader identity translate into policy?” Then a null on spending can be informative if paired with evidence that effects are absent precisely where discretion is limited.
- **A stronger mechanism:** If the claim is institutional constraints dominate preferences, the paper should organize itself around that proposition, not just offer it as an ex post interpretation.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

**Setup:** Gender parity reforms are increasing women’s representation worldwide, and many advocates expect women leaders to alter policy priorities.

**Tension:** Existing evidence is mixed, and the canonical U.S. mayor paper finds no effects—but maybe that is because U.S. cities are unusually constrained. Mexico looks like a setting where effects should be more likely: a developing democracy, higher gender inequality, dramatic growth in women’s representation, and potentially more executive discretion.

**Resolution:** Even there, electing a female mayor does not materially change municipal spending composition.

**Implications:** Either mayoral gender does not map strongly into fiscal priorities in competitive elections, or institutional and bureaucratic constraints are so strong that leader identity does not bite on this margin. This has implications for how we think about parity reforms, substantive representation, and where to look for policy effects.

Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?  
Yes, more than many papers do. But it is still somewhat thin. The paper has a clean setup-tension-resolution structure, but the implication section overreaches slightly relative to the evidence. The current story risks becoming: “We found no spending effects, therefore parity changes representation but not policy.” That is too broad for what is shown.

The better story is:

- There is a widely held claim that women leaders change policy.
- That claim should be most testable in a setting like Mexico.
- Yet on a central policy margin—municipal spending composition—the effect is absent.
- Therefore, we should update toward a more conditional view: identity matters only where discretion is meaningful, and spending shares may not be that margin.

That is a real story. It is stronger than a pile of null results, but it needs more discipline in the writing.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?

“Mexico massively increased the number of female mayors after parity reforms, but in close races female mayors don’t seem to spend municipal budgets differently from male mayors.”

Would people lean in?  
Some would. Not everyone. The topic is interesting because it cuts against a common presumption and bears on a salient policy debate. But the second question would arrive immediately:

**“Okay, but are municipal budget shares the right place to look?”**

That is the pivotal follow-up, and the paper needs a better answer than it currently has. Right now the answer is something like: “This is what Ferreira and Gyourko studied, and these are important outcomes.” Fair, but not enough. The reader will want to know whether the null is genuinely informative about substantive representation or just about rigid accounting categories.

Is the null result itself interesting?  
Yes, conditionally. It is interesting because:

- the prior from public discourse is that women leaders should change social spending;
- Mexico is a stronger test case than the U.S. for detecting effects;
- parity mandates are globally important.

But the paper must make the case that the null is informative, not just unsuccessful. The right argument is not “we found nothing, therefore nothing is there.” The right argument is: **if even in this favorable setting we see no movement on fiscal composition, then either preferences converge in competitive executive elections or fiscal institutions sharply limit officeholder influence on budgets.** That is publishable if well framed.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

At present, the gap is mostly a **framing plus ambition** problem, with some **scope** issues.

- **Framing problem:** The science may be competent, but the paper currently presents itself as an extension/replication of Ferreira and Gyourko in Mexico. That is too small for AER.
- **Scope problem:** One outcome family—budget shares—is probably not enough to settle the broader question the paper wants to answer about substantive representation.
- **Ambition problem:** The paper is careful and sensible, but safe. It does not yet try to reshape how economists think about leader identity and institutional constraint.
- **Novelty problem:** Moderate. The null effect of female leaders on broad fiscal aggregates is not a brand-new idea; the novelty comes from the setting. Setting alone rarely carries AER.

What would excite the top 10 people in this field?

Probably one of two versions:

1. **A conceptually sharper paper:**  
   “Leader identity affects policy only on discretionary margins.”  
   Then show nulls on rigid budget shares and effects on more discretionary outcomes.

2. **A broader substantive representation paper:**  
   “Parity reforms changed who governs but not municipal fiscal policy in Mexico.”  
   Then connect to multiple outcomes or heterogeneity by discretion, state capacity, or revenue flexibility.

If they could change only one thing, my advice is:

**Reframe the paper around the conditions under which descriptive representation translates into substantive policy change, and show that municipal spending composition is a hard test of that broader theory rather than merely a Mexico replication of Ferreira and Gyourko.**

That is the single most impactful move. If they can also add one genuinely discretionary outcome, the paper’s ceiling rises materially.
