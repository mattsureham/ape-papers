**1. Idea Fidelity**  
The paper follows the manifest idea closely. It uses the staggered AMLD‑5 transposition (2019‑2021) and the exogenous CJEU “undo” (late‑2022/early‑2023) as a two‑event natural experiment, treats the United Kingdom as a permanent‑treated control, and relies on Eurostat quarterly business‑registration indices (supplemented by FDI and sector‑level registers). The identification strategy, data sources, and research question (“does public beneficial‑ownership disclosure impose a cost on legitimate firm formation?”) all match the original proposal. No major element of the manifest is omitted.

**2. Summary**  
The paper estimates that making beneficial‑ownership registers publicly accessible has a statistically insignificant – and even slightly positive – effect on aggregate firm‑births in the EU, while the subsequent rollback of transparency produces a non‑significant decline that appears to be driven by macro‑economic shocks (as shown by a manufacturing‑sector placebo). The author concludes that the proportionality argument advanced by the CJEU lacks empirical support.

**3. Essential Points**  

| # | Issue (why it matters) | What needs to be fixed / clarified |
|---|------------------------|------------------------------------|
| 1 | **Pre‑trend violation & upward bias** – The event‑study (Appendix A) shows positive pre‑trends for early adopters, implying that the “adoption” coefficient captures part of an ongoing growth trajectory rather than a causal effect. | Re‑estimate the adoption effect using a more credible counterfactual: (i) exclude early adopters or (ii) apply a *stacked* DiD/event‑study that forces parallel trends, (iii) include country‑specific time trends *and* lead‑lag terms, and report the resulting coefficient with confidence intervals. If the pre‑trend remains, acknowledge that the estimate is an upper bound. |
| 2 | **Limited treatment clusters for the rollback** – Only seven countries “rolled back,” so clustering by country yields few‑cluster inference and potentially downward‑biased standard errors. | Adopt inference methods robust to few clusters: (i) wild‑cluster bootstrap (Cameron, Gelbach & Miller 2008), (ii) placebo permutations with the same number of treated units, (iii) report confidence intervals based on randomization inference. Show that the significance (or lack thereof) survives these more conservative approaches. |
| 3 | **Outcome aggregation masks heterogeneity** – The Eurostat registration index aggregates all firm types; the null may hide a sizable reduction in “opaque” entities (shells, holding companies) while legitimate start‑ups remain unchanged. | Exploit the Companies House PSC micro‑data (or Eurostat sector codes) to construct a proxy for opacity‑sensitive firms (e.g., shares‑holding companies, financial intermediaries). Run separate DiDs for “opaque‑prone” vs. “opaque‑immune” sectors. If micro‑data are unavailable, at least discuss the limitation more fully and consider robustness checks using alternative EU datasets (e.g., national business registers). |
| 4 | **Parallel‑trend assumption for the rollback** – The rollback coincided with the 2022‑2023 energy‑price shock, which hit precisely the countries that reversed transparency (Germany, Netherlands, Austria). The paper’s manufacturing placebo is a good start but does not fully rule out differential macro shocks across sectors. | Augment the placebo with (i) country‑specific energy‑price or electricity‑cost controls, (ii) interaction terms that capture exposure to the energy crisis, (iii) a synthetic‑control analysis for each rolled‑back country using a weighted combination of maintained‑access peers. Demonstrating that the rollback effect disappears after controlling for these shocks would strengthen the claim that the observed decline is not transparency‑driven. |
| 5 | **Standard‑error scaling & effect‑size interpretation** – The reported SEs (≈0.05‑0.09) are modest given only 21 × 36 observations, but the paper’s “standardized effect size” table inflates the magnitude (e.g., “large positive”) because it divides by the *pre‑treatment* SD of the **log** index (0.125). This can mislead readers about economic relevance. | Re‑present effect sizes in more intuitive units: (i) translate the log coefficient into percentage change in firm births, (ii) compare to the average quarterly growth rate (≈2‑3 %); (iii) report the 95 % CI in percentage terms. This will make it clear that even the upper bound (< 20 % effect) is economically modest. |
| 6 | **Treatment coding for the UK** – The UK is used as an “always‑treated” control, yet the paper’s TWFE specification includes the UK in the same sample as EU members. Because the UK never experiences a rollback, it may bias the reversal estimate if country‑specific trends differ. | Either (i) exclude the UK from the main regression and treat it as a separate reference group, or (ii) include a “UK‑always‑treated” dummy and interact it with the rollback indicator to verify that its presence does not drive the results. Report the robustness check. |

If the authors cannot satisfactorily address points 1‑3, the paper’s central claim—that transparency imposes no measurable cost on firm formation—remains unsubstantiated and should be rejected.  

**4. Suggestions**  

Below are recommendations that, while not essential for acceptance, would markedly improve the paper’s credibility, readability, and policy relevance.

| Aspect | Recommendation |
|--------|----------------|
| **Identification strategy** | • **Event‑study with leads/lags**: present full leads (e.g., up to 4 quarters before adoption) and lags (up to 4 quarters after) to visualise dynamics and test for pre‑trend violations. <br>• **Callaway‑Sant’Anna / Sun & Abraham estimators**: even though all units are eventually treated, you can still construct “not‑yet‑treated” groups for each cohort and report average treatment effects on the treated (ATT) for each adoption year. This helps assess heterogeneity and mitigates the TWFE bias highlighted by Goodman‑Bacon. |
| **Inference** | • Use **wild‑cluster bootstrap** (R or Stata code readily available) given only seven rollback units. <br>• Report **randomisation‑inference p‑values** for both adoption and rollback, alongside conventional clustered SEs. |
| **Outcome measurement** | • **Decompose registrations**: using Eurostat’s sector‑level breakdown, construct a “shell‑prone” index (e.g., finance, real estate, holding‑company sectors). <br>• If possible, merge the Companies House PSC data for the UK with national registers of the EU (many have open APIs) to compute the share of firms whose PSCs are foreign versus domestic. This would directly address whether transparency changes the *composition* of new firms. |
| **Control variables** | • Add **energy‑price exposure** (e.g., country‑level electricity wholesale prices) and **COVID‑19 intensity** (cumulative cases/deaths) as quarterly controls, to soak up the macro shocks that coincided with the rollback. <br>• Include **policy‑stringency indices** (e.g., Oxford COVID‑19 Government Response Tracker) to ensure that differential pandemic responses do not confound the adoption estimate. |
| **Robustness checks** | • **Leave‑one‑out for adoption**: similar to the rollback, drop each early‑adopter country to see if the positive point estimate is driven by any single case. <br>• **Alternative clustering**: cluster by both country and quarter (two‑way cluster) or use Driscoll‑Kraay SEs to accommodate serial correlation. <br>• **Synthetic control** for the rollback: build a synthetic “maintained‑access” counterpart for each rolled‑back country, compare post‑rollback trajectories. |
| **Presentation** | • **Tables**: include a clear “summary of treatment dates” table (adoption year, rollback month) for each country; this helps readers verify the timing. <br>• **Figures**: an event‑study plot for adoption and a parallel plot for rollback would replace several rows of text and make the (non‑)effects immediately visible. <br>• **Effect‑size interpretation**: after estimating β, compute “percentage change in registrations” and say, e.g., “the 95 % CI for the adoption effect spans –2.4 % to +19 % in quarterly registrations, i.e., at most a one‑in‑five change.” |
| **Policy discussion** | • Link findings to the **ongoing AMLD‑6 debate** more concretely: discuss how the null result may inform the design of “restricted‑access” versus “public‑access” registers (e.g., tiered access for authorities vs. public). <br>• Consider **cost‑benefit** framing: quantify the marginal administrative cost of publishing registers (e.g., per‑firm reporting burden) and juxtapose it with the estimated zero effect on firm formation. |
| **Literature positioning** | • Cite recent work on **‘opacity‑reducing policies’** (e.g., Pierrens & Van Wijnbergen 2022 on beneficial‑owner data, or the “ownership‐transparency” literature in tax evasion). <br>• Discuss whether the null is consistent with the broader “disclosure ≠ distortion” literature (e.g., Leuz & Wysocki 2008 on earnings disclosure). |
| **Technical details** | • Clarify the **construction of the log registration index**: is it log of the index (base 100) or log of the underlying count? Provide the exact transformation to avoid confusion. <br>• Explain how **quarterly fixed effects** are coded (e.g., year‑quarter dummies) and verify that they absorb the pandemic shock common to all countries. <br>• Provide the exact **code for the treatment variables** (especially the rollback indicator) in an appendix or a repository. |
| **Data availability** | • Deposit the **AML‑5 transposition panel** and **rollback dates** as a CSV in the public repo; this aids replication. <br>• Offer a short **data‑dictionary** for the Eurostat variables used (code, unit, adjustment). |
| **Writing style** | • Shorten the abstract to 150 words and focus on the key numeric results. <br>• Reduce redundancy (e.g., the “institutional background” is repeated in the intro and Section 2). <br>• Use the JEL code **K22** (corporate governance) and add **D23** (inter‑national business) to reflect the cross‑border nature of the study. |

Addressing the essential points above—especially re‑estimating with proper parallel‑trend checks and robust inference—will be decisive for acceptance. Once these issues are resolved, the contribution will be a valuable addition to the transparency‑policy literature and will provide concrete evidence for the ongoing EU AML‑6 discussion.
