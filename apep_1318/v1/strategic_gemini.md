### 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
This paper exploits a unique "on-off" natural experiment in Europe to test whether making corporate ownership public deters business activity. By analyzing the staggered adoption of public beneficial ownership registers and their subsequent court-ordered reversal, the author finds that transparency has no discernible impact on the volume of new business registrations. This provides the first empirical challenge to the "proportionality" legal argument—that transparency costs outweigh benefits—currently shaping global anti-money laundering policy.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this well, but the first paragraph is slightly too "policy-heavy." It leads with a $2 trillion figure that feels like a generic IMF report.
**The "AER" Pitch:** "Does corporate transparency chill entrepreneurship? While courts and lobbyists argue that public disclosure of 'beneficial owners' imposes a prohibitive secrecy tax on legitimate business, I exploit a unique sequence of EU policy adoption and court-mandated reversal to show that these costs are empirically invisible. My findings suggest that the legal 'proportionality' defense for corporate opacity rests on a flawed empirical premise."

### 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**Contribution:** This paper provides the first causal evidence that public ownership transparency does not reduce aggregate business formation, directly testing the empirical assumptions of European privacy jurisprudence.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It stands apart from Zucman or Johannesen by looking at *legitimate* registration volume rather than *illicit* flows.
*   **Question vs. Gap:** It frames itself as answering a question about the world (the cost of transparency), though it leans heavily on "filling a gap" in the literature on page 3.
*   **The "Smart Economist" Test:** They would say: "It’s a clever symmetric DiD that shows the EU's recent privacy rollback was based on a fake economic trade-off."
*   **Bigger Contribution:** To make this truly "Big AER," the author needs to bridge the gap between *aggregate* registrations and *entity-type* registrations. If transparency only kills shell companies (good) but aggregate registrations look flat because shells are a small %, the "No Cost" story is even stronger. The paper needs at least one proxy for "opacity-prone" sectors vs. others.

### 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
*   **Neighbors:** Djankov et al. (2002) on entry regulation; Alstadsæter, Johannesen, and Zucman (2019) on tax evasion; and the recent DiD methods literature (Bacon, Callaway & Sant’Anna).
*   **Strategy:** It should "attack" the legal and industry assumptions while "building on" the disclosure literature (Leuz et al.).
*   **Missing Conversations:** The paper needs to speak more to the **Law and Economics** community. The CJEU "proportionality" test is a specific legal standard that requires an empirical basis; this paper is essentially an amicus brief in journal form.
*   **Unexpected Connection:** It could connect to the literature on "the value of anonymity" in digital economics. Is corporate secrecy a "luxury good" or a "transaction cost"?

### 4. NARRATIVE ARC
*   **Setup:** The world is moving toward transparency to fight $2T in dirty money.
*   **Tension:** High courts are striking these laws down, claiming they hurt the "legitimate economy."
*   **Resolution:** When you turn transparency on, nothing happens to registrations. When you turn it off, the changes are driven by the energy crisis, not the law.
*   **Implications:** The "proportionality" argument is a myth. Regulators can have transparency without an economic haircut.

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong. The "reversal" (transparency off) is the hook that makes this better than a standard policy evaluation.

### 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
*   **Dinner Party Fact:** "The EU Supreme Court recently killed transparency laws because they supposedly hurt business, but when you actually look at the data, businesses didn't care at all."
*   **Reaction:** Lean in. It’s a "The Emperor Has No Clothes" finding regarding a major EU court ruling.
*   **Follow-up:** "But did it actually stop the money laundering?" (The paper admits it can't answer this, which is fine, but it must be addressed as the "other side" of the proportionality coin).

### 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
*   **Front-loading:** The paper is well-structured. The "Sector Placebo" (Table 3) is the "killer app" of the paper and should be mentioned even earlier in the intro.
*   **Appendix:** The event study with pre-trends (Appendix B) is actually quite concerning. Moving a version of the event study plot to the main text is essential for transparency. If the "null" is just a result of a massive positive pre-trend flattening out, the story changes.

### 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The gap is **granularity**. An AER reader will worry that "Total Business Registrations" is too blunt an instrument. 
*   **The Advice:** Use the sector-level data more aggressively. If the author can show that "Financial and Insurance Activities" (NACE K) or "Professional Services" (NACE M)—sectors where shells live—showed a different (or similarly null) response compared to "Retail," the paper moves from "interesting policy note" to "rigorous economic discovery."
