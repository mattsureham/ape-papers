**1. Idea Fidelity**

The paper follows the broad contours of the idea manifest—leveraging AMLD5 transposition and the CJEU rollback as a twin “on/off” experiment to assess whether public beneficial ownership registers impose costs on legitimate business formation. The institutional narrative, emphasis on reversal, and use of cross-country registration data align with the manifest’s core concept. However, several key elements of the proposed identification strategy and data plan are missing or underdeveloped. The manifest envisaged a staggered DiD (e.g., Callaway-Sant’Anna) exploiting pre-reversal timing variation across 27 countries, complemented by micro-level checks using Companies House PSC data, Eurostat enterprise births, FDI flows, and offshore escape routes. The paper instead relies on a fairly standard TWFE regression with 21 countries and stops short of implementing the more robust staggered estimators, the PSC ownership nationality analysis, or the FDI/offshore mechanism tests discussed in the manifest. The “reversal” heterogeneity test is present, but the linkage to mechanism outcomes and the richer data sources promised in the manifest are absent.

**2. Summary**

The paper asks whether public access to beneficial ownership registers deters legitimate business formation by exploiting staggered AMLD5 adoption (2019–2021) followed by the CJEU-ordered rollback in late 2022. Using Eurostat quarterly business registration indices for 21 EU countries, the author estimates TWFE models and finds a statistically insignificant (and economically small) positive adoption coefficient, while the post-reversal decline in registrations in the “rolled-back” countries is mirrored in a manufacturing placebo, suggesting macro shocks rather than transparency effects. The conclusion is that transparency imposes no measurable cost on enterprise births, undermining the proportionality argument invoked by the CJEU.

**3. Essential Points**

1. **Identification strategy is underspecified.** A key contribution of the original idea was the staggered nature of AMLD5 compliance and rollback, which calls for modern staggered-DiD tools (Callaway-Sant’Anna or similar) alongside robust event-study diagnostics. The paper defaults to a global TWFE spec, does not formally test parallel trends, and, per the appendix, the staged estimator failed due to convergence (without showing diagnostics or alternative implementations). This leaves the identifying assumption—timing orthogonal to unobservables—untested. The reported positive pre-trend (Appendix) further undermines the notion that the regression isolates the causal effect of transparency. Without more convincing identification or sensitivity analyses (e.g., variation in treatment timing, alternative estimators, interactive trends), it is hard to interpret the coefficients as causal.

2. **Reversal analysis lacks credible counterfactual.** The paper interprets the larger-than-average decline in rolled-back countries as macroeconomic, using a manufacturing placebo to bolster this claim. Yet identification of the rollback effect still depends on assuming that rollback status is exogenous conditional on time fixed effects. Countries that revoked access (Germany, Netherlands, Austria, Ireland) also differ systematically in industry composition, dependence on energy-intensive sectors, and exposure to policy shocks, which plausibly drive the post-2022 drop. Without more granular controls (e.g., energy price shocks, sectoral composition, differential pandemic recovery) or a more focal “within-country” comparison (e.g., before/after for rollback countries only, with pre-trends), the reversal coefficient may simply capture these other shocks. The manufacturing placebo is suggestive but insufficient; it would be more convincing to show that the rollout and rollback cohorts had parallel trends and similar exposure to confounders.

3. **Mismatch between research question and data scope.** The paper’s central policy question concerns illicit incorporations and “opacity seekers”; yet the chosen outcome—aggregate business registration indices—may be too coarse to detect such narrowly targeted effects, especially when combined with a null finding. The manifest promised richer data (PSC nationality patterns, offshore incorporations, FDI flows) that could speak more directly to opacity-sensitive activity. The current paper does not use those data, nor does it attempt to isolate the effect on shell-company-friendly sectors. The result is a null on a noisy aggregate outcome, which risks misinterpretation. If the paper’s claim is that transparency imposes no cost on “legitimate” businesses, it should either analyze explicitly legitimate (e.g., manufacturing) versus opaque (e.g., holding companies, financial services) sectors with appropriate data or justify why the aggregate index is informative for that question.

Given these issues, especially the incomplete identification and mismatch between the outcome and the policy inference, major revisions are required before publication.

**4. Suggestions**

- **Strengthen the identification strategy.** Given the staggered adoption (2019–2021) followed by a reversal, the paper should revisit the feasibility of staggered DiD estimators. The appendix notes convergence problems with Callaway-Sant’Anna because “all units eventually treated within a short window.” Alternative approaches—such as imputation of not-yet-treated effects using the “interaction-weighted” estimators (e.g., Sun and Abraham 2021), or simply restricting the sample to earlier adopters and using late adopters (2021) as counterfactual—might succeed. Presenting event-study plots with bootstrapped confidence intervals and showing that pre-trends are not driving the results is essential, especially since the appendix already indicates upward pre-trends. If the formal estimators remain infeasible, explain carefully why TWFE is still credible (e.g., showing that treatment timing is essentially binary within the adoption window, justifying that the weights converge to simple comparisons). Regardless, the paper should include tests for treatment effect heterogeneity over time and show that the results are not driven by a few outliers.

- **Expand the reversal analysis.** The reversal effect is the most policy-relevant dimension, but its interpretation is currently too tentative. One could reinforce it by (a) controlling for observable confounders that changed around late 2022 (energy price exposure, sectoral shares, COVID recovery speed), (b) estimating the reversal effect in a within-country difference (pre/post relative to the country’s own trend) rather than relying solely on cross-country comparisons, and (c) showing that the countries that rolled back transparency did not have divergent trends in the quarter before the rollback relative to maintained countries. The paper already implements permutation inference; consider complementing it with synthetic control or matched comparisons for the rolled-back group to highlight the “what would have happened” counterfactual. Exploiting the near-simultaneous nature of the rollback could also allow for an event-study-style plot focusing on the post-November 2022 quarters, provided sufficient data.

- **Leverage proposed data sources or justify their omission.** The manifesto emphasized PSC ownership data, FDI flows, and offshore incorporations. Integrating at least some of these sources would greatly strengthen the causal narrative. For instance, analyzing how beneficial owner nationalities in the UK PSC register changed after the EU rollback could reveal “escape” dynamics (e.g., surges in Luxembourg or Dutch nationals registering in UK companies). Similarly, the FDI data could test whether investment flows into EU rolling-back countries increased (suggesting transparency-deterring capital) or decreased (supporting the macro-shock story). If data limitations prevent these analyses, explain why clearly: is it data access, timing, or scope? Alternatively, focus the paper on what the available data can credibly say—e.g., frame it as “we test whether transparency imposes aggregate entry costs,” and treat the null as informative for that narrower question.

- **Clarify the scope of “legitimate” business formation.** The paper argues that transparency imposes no cost on legitimate businesses, but the aggregate registration index mixes all sectors. Consider decomposing registrations into sectors likely to include legitimate entrants (e.g., manufacturing, professional services) versus those commonly used for opacity (e.g., holding companies, financial intermediaries). Sector-level data already used for the placebo could be repurposed to test whether transparency affects specific sectors differently. If the data do not permit such granularity, explicitly acknowledge the limitation and temper the policy claims accordingly.

- **Address measurement and sampling issues.** The paper drops six EU countries because Eurostat lacked quarterly data. This may bias results if the missing countries systematically differ (e.g., newer EU members or smaller economies). Discuss whether their exclusion affects generalizability. Similarly, the appendix notes that UK is excluded from the main analysis despite being the “always-treated” case in the manifest. Consider including the UK as a robustness check—e.g., showing that the UK’s time series behaves smoothly, reinforcing the view that transparency has no effect. At minimum, justify why the UK is excluded (missing data? incompatible indexes?). 

- **Polish notation and tables.** Some tables currently leave empty cells or have inconsistent formatting (e.g., Table 2 has empty entries under columns 3 and 4). Ensure each coefficient is matched with standard errors and includes significance stars where appropriate. Also, specify in the notes how standard errors are clustered, the estimation sample, and any weighting used.

- **Discuss external validity carefully.** The null finding is policy-relevant, but caution against overgeneralizing. Transparency might still meaningfully deter illicit incorporations even if aggregate registrations remain unchanged—especially if illicit activity represents a tiny share of total incorporations. Acknowledge this explicitly and, if possible, estimate the implied upper bound on the number of opacity-seeking firms that could be affected (e.g., using shell-company prevalence estimates). This would help anchor the null result in the broader AML debate.

Implementing these suggestions would strengthen both the internal validity and the policy interpretation of the paper.
