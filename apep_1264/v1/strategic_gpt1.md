## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple, policy-relevant question: when governments impose firm-size-based regulation, do firms stay small to avoid crossing the line? Using the introduction of Switzerland’s 2020 pay-equity audit mandate for firms with 100+ employees, the paper argues that the answer here is no: firms do not appear to shrink or avoid growth, suggesting that not all regulatory thresholds distort firm behavior—especially when enforcement is soft.

Why should a busy economist care? Because the profession has gotten used to treating size-based regulation as inherently distortionary. A credible case that some thresholds are effectively non-binding would matter for how we think about regulation, misallocation, and the design of labor-market and transparency policies.

### Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Reasonably well, but not optimally. The current introduction starts from the broad literature on size-dependent regulation and quickly moves to the Swiss case. That is sensible. But the paper’s best idea is not really “a DiD on Swiss pay equity law.” Its best idea is: **a threshold is only as distortionary as its enforcement teeth**. The introduction gets there, but too late.

The current framing also overemphasizes the “textbook setting for threshold avoidance,” when the more interesting message is the contrast between the threshold’s formal salience and its weak economic bite. The title does this better than the first two paragraphs.

### The pitch the paper should have

Here is the version the first two paragraphs should say:

> Economists often view firm-size thresholds as a source of real distortions: firms cluster below regulatory cutoffs, stay inefficiently small, and reduce aggregate productivity. But that logic implicitly assumes that crossing the threshold meaningfully changes the firm’s payoff. This paper asks a more basic question: **when do size-based regulations actually bite?**
>
> I study Switzerland’s 2020 pay-equity mandate, which requires firms with 100 or more employees to conduct certified pay audits. The law creates a sharp, salient threshold, but enforcement is soft: firms face disclosure requirements rather than fines or direct sanctions. Using universe administrative data, I find no evidence that firms shrink or avoid growth around the policy. The broader lesson is that **size thresholds do not automatically create distortions; enforcement design determines whether they matter economically at all**.

That is the AER-relevant version of the story.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### Contribution in one sentence

The paper’s contribution is to show that a salient firm-size regulatory threshold can generate little or no detectable distortion when compliance is weakly enforced, implying that the distortions from size-dependent regulation depend critically on enforcement design rather than thresholds per se.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest papers?

Only partially. The paper does distinguish itself from the classic “thresholds cause bunching” literature by emphasizing a null result and soft enforcement. But it does not yet sharply separate itself from four nearby genres:

1. **Classic bunching/misallocation papers** showing strong responses to thresholds.
2. **Labor-regulation threshold papers** in Europe.
3. **Pay transparency/pay equity papers** studying wage-gap outcomes rather than firm-size margins.
4. **Methodological papers** that infer behavior from coarse bins or aggregated data.

Right now, a reader could still summarize it as “another threshold paper, but with a null.” That is not enough. The author needs to insist that the object of interest is not Swiss gender policy per se, but the **mapping from legal thresholds to economic distortions**.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a gap in a literature?

Mixed, leaning too much toward literature gap-filling. The strongest world question is:

- **Do firm-size thresholds distort behavior only when enforcement is costly enough to matter?**

That is a real economic question. The paper sometimes slips into the weaker formulation:

- “There are few null results in the bunching literature.”
- “Here is how to do an indirect test with coarse data.”

Those are publishable-supporting points, not the main event.

### Could a smart economist explain what is new after reading the introduction?

Some could, but many would still say: “It’s a DiD paper about whether Swiss firms avoided a pay-equity threshold, and they didn’t.” That is too small. The introduction should make them say instead: “It uses a pay-equity threshold to argue that regulation without enforcement may create compliance rituals without real distortions.”

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Most importantly: **make the paper about enforcement design, not about one null threshold episode**.

Concrete ways to do that:

- **Different framing:** Compare this law explicitly to thresholds with hard sanctions and argue that the paper identifies a boundary condition for threshold distortions.
- **Different mechanism emphasis:** Move “no fines, only disclosure” from interpretation to centerpiece. That is the hook.
- **Different comparison:** Add a more explicit within-country or cross-policy comparison to other Swiss thresholds with real teeth, or at least descriptively show why the 100+ GEA threshold sits in a different enforcement class.
- **Different outcome framing:** Rather than listing several null outcomes, focus on the single economically meaningful claim: no distortion in firm-size behavior despite a salient threshold.
- **Bigger ambition:** Recast as evidence against the profession’s tendency to infer distortion from legal discontinuity alone.

If the author could make one substantive extension beyond reframing, it would be to organize the paper around a **taxonomy of threshold regulations by enforcement intensity** and place the Swiss case within it. That would make the paper feel less like a single-country null and more like a general claim.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

The profession believes firm-size thresholds often distort behavior. Governments use such thresholds all the time. The literature has shown many cases where firms bunch below legal cutoffs, suggesting sizable efficiency losses.

### Tension

But are thresholds inherently distortionary, or only when they carry meaningful consequences? Switzerland’s pay-equity law creates a visible threshold at 100 employees—exactly the sort of policy where one might expect avoidance—yet it is weakly enforced. That sets up the puzzle: **does a salient threshold without sanctions actually matter?**

### Resolution

The paper finds no evidence of meaningful firm-size response. Firms do not appear to shrink, avoid the threshold, or otherwise behave as if the regulation had much bite.

### Implications

The implication is not merely “Swiss firms didn’t react.” It is: **regulatory thresholds are not sufficient for distortion; enforcement architecture matters.** Policymakers can potentially design mandates that alter reporting/compliance requirements without inducing the classic size-distortion margins economists worry about.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has one, but it is not yet disciplined enough. There is a good story in there, but the draft sometimes reads like:
- threshold paper,
- then data-constrained empirical design,
- then null results,
- then a conceptual label (“compliance mirage”).

The label and the central insight arrive too late. The story should be:

1. Economists fear threshold distortions.
2. But whether thresholds distort should depend on enforcement.
3. The Swiss GEA is an almost laboratory-like test of a salient threshold with soft teeth.
4. Firms do not respond.
5. Therefore, what matters is not the threshold alone but the enforcement regime behind it.

Right now the paper is still a bit of a collection of estimates looking for that stronger story.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?

“I looked at a new 100-employee regulatory threshold in Switzerland and found basically no evidence firms stayed small to avoid it—despite all our priors that size thresholds create bunching.”

Better still:

“Apparently a legal threshold without sanctions may not distort firm size at all.”

That is the line.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

Some would lean in, but only if the framing emphasizes the broader lesson. If presented as “a null DiD about Swiss pay audits,” phones come out. If presented as “evidence that regulatory thresholds only distort when they have teeth,” people listen.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Immediately:

- “Is this because the law had no real enforcement?”
- Then: “Can you compare it to thresholds that do have sanctions?”
- And then: “Does that mean soft disclosure mandates are less distortionary but maybe also less effective?”

Those are good questions. The paper should anticipate them and lean into them.

### Is the null itself interesting?

Yes—but only conditionally. A null is interesting here if it changes how we think about threshold regulation. The paper is close to making that case, but it needs to do so more forcefully. Right now the null sometimes feels like “we didn’t find bunching,” when it should feel like “this is evidence that the existence of a threshold is not enough; incentive design is the true object.”

That is what converts a null from a failed detection exercise into a real result.

- **Framing problem:** yes, strongly.

The gap is between:
- “Here is a null result on Swiss pay-equity threshold avoidance,” and
- “Here is evidence that the distortions from regulatory thresholds depend fundamentally on enforcement design, and that the literature has over-generalized from thresholds with teeth.”

The latter is an AER conversation. The former is not.

**Rewrite the paper around the proposition that enforcement design, not threshold existence, determines whether size-dependent regulation distorts firm behavior.**

Everything else is secondary. If the author can make readers leave with that sentence in mind, the paper becomes much more interesting. If not, it remains a well-executed niche null.
