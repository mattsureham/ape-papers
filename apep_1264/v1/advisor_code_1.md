**1. Idea Fidelity**

The submitted paper departs substantially from the original idea manifest. The manifest outlined an ambitious agenda of estimating a full structural cost schedule across Switzerland’s four key firm-size thresholds (20, 50, 100, 250 employees) via stacked bunching, exploiting the 2020 Gender Equality Act (GEA) shock at 100 employees as a difference-in-bunching lever. In contrast, the paper focuses narrowly on the 100-employee threshold, conducting a difference-in-differences on coarse size bins (10–49 vs. 50–249) to assess whether firms shrink after the GEA. The paper does not pursue the stacked bunching identification, does not model structural costs, and does not exploit the thresholds at 20, 50, or 250 employees. The promised multi-threshold data sources (KMU-HSG, STATENT finer bins, etc.) are not used for the intended structural estimation. As a result, the paper does not follow the original manifest’s core empirical and identification goals.

**2. Summary**

The paper sets out to test whether the 2020 revision to Switzerland’s Gender Equality Act, which requires certified pay audits for firms with 100+ employees, induced avoidance behavior by distorting the firm-size distribution. Using aggregate canton-size-bin data from STATENT (2011–2023) and a difference-in-differences comparing the 50–249 bin to the 10–49 bin, it finds no evidence of bunching below the threshold: average firm size in the medium bin increased slightly post-reform, firm counts did not decline, and female employment shares were stable. The null result is interpreted through enforcement design—the regulation lacks monetary penalties, so the threshold is a “compliance mirage” that does not bite firms economically.

**3. Essential Points**

1. **Identification insufficiency and power concerns.** The key research question—do firms shrink to avoid the 100-employee threshold?—is not credibly addressed by comparing averages across coarse bins. The treatment affects only firms within [100, 249], but the regression averages over the entire 50–249 bin, diluting any response. The paper acknowledges this attenuation but still relies on the DiD to make inferences. Since only a small subset of the bin is treated, a nonresponse could simply reflect insufficient statistical power rather than the absence of behavior. The minimum detectable effect discussion is not sufficient; what matters is the share of treated firms and the plausible scale of their response. Without bounding those quantities, the null is not interpretable. The authors should either obtain finer microdata that allow direct bunching estimation or, at the very least, calibrate the implied average effect under plausible firm-size distributions to show the specification could detect a meaningful behavioral response. 

2. **Differential pre-trends and validity of parallel trends.** The event-study coefficients for 2011–2018 are jointly significant, indicating systematic divergence between the medium and small bins well before the reform. The paper dismisses this by focusing on 2015–2019, but the baseline uses 2011–2023, so the omitted trend could bias the estimated treatment effect. Without convincingly showing that the pre-trend is not driving the post-2020 pattern (for instance, by re-estimating the main specification on a window with stable pre-trends or adding flexible time trends), the DiD estimate cannot be interpreted causally.

3. **Mismatch between research question and empirical strategy.** The literature the paper cites (e.g., bunching studies) measures threshold effects via discontinuities in the density of firms at the cutoff. Here, no such density is observed; instead, the paper compares aggregate means across bins. This strategy conflates (i) the treatment of interest (100-employee threshold) with (ii) other policies or shocks that affect the entire 50–249 bin. For example, COVID-19 support programs or sectoral shocks could disproportionately affect medium firms, biasing the estimate. Moreover, the paper’s interpretation of a null as indicative of “compliance mirage” enforcement rests on the assumption that any distortion should have been visible at the bin level, which is unsupported. The empirical design should align more closely with the stated goal—if threshold avoidance is the question, the data need to permit identification of firms just below vs. just above 100 employees. Otherwise the paper is left with a descriptive comparison that cannot isolate the GEA effect.

Given these issues, the paper should not proceed without a substantial rework.

**4. Suggestions**

- **Access finer-grained data or use alternative designs.** The most direct way to answer the research question is to observe the firm-size distribution around the 100-employee threshold. If the publicly available STATENT bins are too coarse, the authors should either apply for access to restricted microdata (which the manifest mentions as feasible) or seek alternative datasets (e.g., AHV register microdata, tax records, Social Security employment data) that allow for exact headcount. With counts at the integer level, the authors could implement the bunching approach of Kleven and Waseem (2013) or consistent density tests, recovering the cost of compliance. If restricted data are unattainable, consider other margin-wise proxies, such as analyzing job vacancies or wage bill growth in sectors close to thresholds, to triangulate the response.

- **Calibrate the implied treatment share and minimum detectable effect.** If the coarse-bin analysis must remain, calibrate how big the average effect would need to be to be detectable, given the share of firms within the bin that actually face the threshold. For example, estimate (or bound) the proportion of firms with 100–249 employees in each canton using supplementary sources (e.g., KMU-HSG caliber data or statutory filings) or assuming a distribution (e.g., Pareto). Then show what average shrinkage in treated firms would correspond to a 0.5 decrease in average bin employment, and compare this to realistic estimates of compliance costs. This would help readers assess whether the null is informative or simply due to aggregation bias.

- **Strengthen the parallel trends assumption.** If the pre-trend is a concern, re-estimate the DiD using only the 2015–2023 window (which already appears more stable) as the baseline specification. Alternatively, introduce bin-specific linear time trends or canton-by-time trends to absorb differential dynamics. Conduct placebo tests comparing 2011–2015 trends to 2015–2019 to verify stability. Presenting these alternative specifications upfront would bolster confidence in the identification.

- **Clarify interpretation of placebo heterogeneity.** The enterprise-level specification yields a statistically significant positive effect opposite to the hypothesized sign. This raises the possibility of other simultaneous shocks that differentially affect medium firms (e.g., sector-specific recoveries, productivity trends). The paper should explore whether the positive effect holds across sectors or is driven by a subset (e.g., services vs. manufacturing). Including sector controls or interacting the treatment with sector shares could rule out confounding.

- **Explicitly test for alternative mechanisms.** If the argument is that enforcement design (reputational penalties only) explains the null, try to operationalize this. For example, are firms with higher public visibility (stock-listed, large exporters) more responsive because reputational stakes are higher? Alternatively, do sectors with more female employees (where pay gaps might be more salient) show different patterns? These subgroup analyses would make the narrative around enforcement more concrete and less speculative.

- **Discuss the role of COVID with more nuance.** The timing of the GEA coincides with the pandemic, and COVID-19 relief programs were often size-dependent. Consider constructing an event-study relative to COVID policy implementation (e.g., short-time work eligibility) to check whether medium firms had differential responses regardless of the GEA. Including controls for canton-level COVID caseloads or policy stringency could also help isolate the GEA effect.

- **Reframe the contribution if null persists.** If the null result survives after improving identification, the main contribution could be a cautionary tale about soft enforcement and the limits of aggregate data. Frame the paper as providing evidence that thresholds without costly enforcement may not distort firm sizes, emphasizing the data challenges and the importance of measuring enforcement bite. This would align the empirical strategy (aggregate bins, DiD) with the claim (absence of response) rather than implying a structural cost schedule estimation that is not delivered.

By addressing these points, the paper would more convincingly connect its empirical approach to the research question and provide clearer lessons for policymakers regarding regulatory thresholds and enforcement.
