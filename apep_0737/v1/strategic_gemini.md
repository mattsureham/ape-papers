**RE:** Strategic Positioning of "Fear of Ten Billion: Bunching Evidence on the Regulatory Costs of Dodd-Frank"

---

### 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
This paper examines how discrete regulatory thresholds distort the size distribution of U.S. banks. Using the $10 billion asset threshold created by Dodd-Frank, the author applies bunching estimation to show that banks strategically stay small to avoid costly oversight and interchange fee caps, then uses a 2018 policy reversal (EGRRCPA) to "unbundle" these costs and identify which specific regulations drive the behavior. It matters because it quantifies the "shadow cost" of financial regulation and reveals how regulatory design can unintentionally stifle institutional growth and competition.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this reasonably well, but the first two paragraphs are a bit "technical-first." They jump into the *methodology* (Kleven-Waseem) before fully establishing the *economic stakes*. 

**The Pitch the Paper Should Have:**
"Regulatory 'cliffs'—where a single dollar of growth triggers millions in compliance costs—are ubiquitous in the modern economy, yet we rarely see the full extent of the distortions they create. Using the universe of U.S. bank filings, this paper documents a massive 'bunching' of institutions just below the $10 billion Dodd-Frank threshold, suggesting that for many banks, the cost of crossing the line exceeds the marginal benefit of growth. By exploiting a 2018 legislative rollback that removed some but not all of these costs, I am able to isolate which specific regulations—supervision vs. revenue caps—actually bite, providing a rare empirical look at the structural costs of the post-crisis financial regime."

---

### 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**Contribution:** The paper provides the first formal bunching-based quantification of Dodd-Frank’s regulatory costs and utilizes a policy reversal to decompose the relative burden of supervision versus revenue-based fee caps.

*   **Differentiation:** It differentiates itself from Bouwman et al. (2018) by moving from "noting a pattern" to formal "bunching estimation" and, crucially, by using the 2018 EGRRCPA as a reversal experiment.
*   **Framing:** Currently, it leans toward "filling a gap in a literature" (applying bunching to banking). It would be stronger if framed as a question about **optimal regulatory design**: "Do discrete thresholds create a 'growth trap' for mid-sized competitors?"
*   **Clarity:** A smart economist would get it. It’s "The Durbin Amendment/CFPB Cliff paper."
*   **Making it bigger:** To reach the AER’s "General Interest" bar, the paper needs to move beyond the banking sector's plumbing. It should link the findings to **market structure**. Does this bunching protect the "Too Big To Fail" banks by preventing mid-sized banks from scaling up? If $10B is a barrier to entry for the "next tier" of competition, the welfare costs are much higher than just compliance hours.

---

### 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
The paper sits at the intersection of **Public Finance** (Bunching/Tax) and **Banking/Corporate Finance**.
*   **Neighbors:** Kleven & Waseem (2013) for method; Bouwman et al. (2018) and Agarwal et al. (2014) for the $10B threshold; Ewens et al. (2024) for the "regulatory costs of being public."
*   **Strategy:** The paper builds on the method but should more aggressively "attack" the current lack of causal decomposition in banking. Most papers say "regulations are costly"; this paper needs to say "Regulation A costs X, but Regulation B costs 2X."
*   **Niche vs. Broad:** Currently a bit niche (Banking). It should speak to the **Industrial Organization** literature on how regulation affects firm-size distributions. 

---

### 4. NARRATIVE ARC
*   **Setup:** Post-2008, the U.S. moved to a tiered regulatory system based on asset size.
*   **Tension:** These tiers were meant to monitor systemic risk, but they created massive "notches" in the cost function.
*   **Resolution:** Banks are indeed "hiding" below the $10B mark. The Durbin Amendment (revenue loss) is a much scarier ghost than the CFPB (oversight).
*   **Implications:** When you "bundle" regulations at a single threshold, you create a massive barrier to growth. If you want mid-sized banks to compete with giants, you need to stagger the cliffs.

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong. The "reversal" in Section 5.1 is the most compelling part of the story.

### 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
*   **Lead Fact:** "There are 55% more banks sitting at $9.9 billion than there should be, and half of them stayed there just to keep their debit card fees high."
*   **Reaction:** Lean in. Everyone hates bank fees and everyone understands the "cliff" logic.
*   **Follow-up:** "Does this mean Dodd-Frank made the 'Too Big to Fail' problem worse by keeping the 'Middle' too small to compete?" (The author should answer this in the Discussion).

### 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
*   **Front-loading:** The year-by-year dynamics (Table 5) are actually more visually/narratively interesting than the pooled bunching estimates. A figure version of Table 5 should be the "money shot" in the Introduction.
*   **Appendix:** The robustness to polynomial order (Table 4) is standard; keep it brief. 
*   **The "De-bunching" story:** This is the AER-level contribution. Move the discussion of the 2018 reversal earlier. Don't treat it as just another result; treat it as the "Natural Experiment" that validates the whole paper.

---

### 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The paper is currently a very competent "Applied Micro" paper. To make it an **AER paper**, it needs to address **Welfare and Competition**. 

If the $10B threshold stops a bank from growing, it stops them from lending in new markets. The author should try to link the bunching to **real outcomes**: Do counties served by "bunched" banks see slower credit growth? Is there a "missing middle" in the US banking sector that harms consumers?

**Single Biggest Improvement:** Explicitly quantify the "Shadow Cost" of the regulation in dollar terms (or ROE terms) and discuss how this "tax on growth" impacts the competitive landscape of the US banking industry.
