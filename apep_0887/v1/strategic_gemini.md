### 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
The paper asks whether technical building mandates for new construction serve as "information interventions" that spur voluntary safety investments in the existing housing stock. Using the staggered adoption of Radon-Resistant New Construction (RRNC) codes across 20 U.S. states, the author tests for spillovers into the broader environmental remediation industry and finds a precisely estimated, unambiguous zero.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this pitch excellently. The first two paragraphs clearly set up the "Compliance Boundary" theory vs. the "Information Spillover" theory. It moves quickly from the specific setting (radon) to the broader economic question (the reach of regulatory instruments). No rewrite is necessary; the opening is professional and high-stakes.

### 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**Contribution:** The paper provides a precisely powered null result demonstrating that building codes do not generate information externalities for the non-regulated margin of the market.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It is well-differentiated. While Jacobsen (2016) and Bruegge et al. (2019) look at efficiency/seismic compliance, this is the first to specifically hunt for the *information* spillover channel using a high-frequency industry proxy.
*   **World vs. Literature:** It frames the question as one about the "World" (how far does regulation reach?), which is a strength.
*   **Clarity:** A smart economist would immediately understand the "Compliance Boundary" concept. It is not "just another DiD paper" because it uses the DiD to adjudicate between two competing theories of regulatory impact.
*   **Bigness:** To make the contribution bigger, the author could benefit from more "demand-side" data. Currently, it relies on NAICS 562910 (remediation services) as a supply-side proxy. While the null in Zone 1 is compelling, actual consumer-level data on radon test kit purchases (e.g., from a major retailer or lab) would make the "no-spillover" claim bulletproof.

### 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
*   **Closest Neighbors:** Jacobsen (2016) on energy codes; Bruegge et al. (2019) on seismic codes; Pinchbeck & Sheridan (2023) on radon information maps; and the Greenstone (2002/2011) "well-powered null" tradition.
*   **Strategy:** The paper correctly builds on the building-code literature while *contrasting* with the pure-information literature (Pinchbeck & Sheridan). This contrast is the most effective part of the positioning.
*   **Audience:** The audience is appropriately broad (Public/Environmental/Urban). It could arguably speak more to the **Behavioral** literature—specifically on why some information "sticks" and some stays in the "background" of a B2B transaction.

### 4. NARRATIVE ARC
*   **Setup:** Regulators often justify codes by assuming they raise general awareness.
*   **Tension:** If codes educate the market, we should see the existing housing stock get safer. If they are just "hidden" technical hurdles, they won't.
*   **Resolution:** A precisely estimated zero across all specifications and risk zones.
*   **Implications:** Building codes are not a substitute for direct-to-consumer information campaigns.

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong. It is a "clean" paper. The tension is resolved not by a "surprise" finding, but by the *precision* of the null, which is rhetorically effective.

### 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
At an economist dinner party: "We spend billions on building codes hoping they'll modernize the market, but it turns out they don't move the needle one inch for the 100 million homes already built—even when those homes are in high-risk zones."
*   **Reaction:** People will lean in because it suggests a massive missed opportunity or a fundamental misunderstanding of regulatory "nudges."
*   **Follow-up:** "Is it because homeowners don't care, or because the contractors don't bother to cross-sell to the neighbors?" (This highlights the need for a mechanism discussion).

### 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
*   **Front-loading:** The paper is well-structured.
*   **Appendices:** The "Standardized Effect Sizes" table (Table 5) is actually very important for a "null result" paper and should perhaps be moved into the main text or integrated into the Results section to emphasize the power of the study.
*   **Refinement:** The discussion of NAICS 562910 being a "noisy" proxy is honest, but the author should double down on the Zone 1 results to prove that noise isn't masking the signal.

### 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The distance is **Medium**. The "Science" is solid, and the "Story" is clear. The gap is **Ambition/Scope**. An AER-level paper on this topic usually requires seeing the same null across *multiple* domains (e.g., do energy codes also fail to spur retrofits? do fire codes fail to spur alarm upgrades?).

**Single Biggest Advice:** To clear the AER bar, provide a more granular "smoking gun" for the mechanism—ideally by showing that while the *industry* didn't grow, *search interest* (Google Trends) or *test-kit sales* also stayed flat, proving the failure is at the "Information" stage rather than a "Supply Constraint" stage.
