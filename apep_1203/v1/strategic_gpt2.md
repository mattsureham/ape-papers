## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper studies what happens when a government effectively shuts down a simplified legal form for starting a business. Using the 2020 de facto ban on SAS registrations in Buenos Aires City, the paper asks a clean and important question: when entry gets harder, do entrepreneurs simply switch legal forms, or do many of them not formalize at all? The headline answer is that about half switch, but half disappear from the main formal registration channels, implying a meaningful drop in total firm creation.

A busy economist should care because this is a first-order question about the real effects of business regulation. A huge literature says simpler entry rules are associated with more formal firms, but it is often unclear whether reforms create genuinely new firms or just reshuffle entrepreneurs across legal margins. This paper's value is that the reversal offers an unusually sharp test of that distinction.

### Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Reasonably clearly, but not as sharply as it should. The paper gets to the core question in paragraph 2, but paragraph 1 starts with institutional detail before telling the reader why the setting matters. And the introduction veers too quickly into literature taxonomy and identification language. For AER positioning, the opening should lead with the world question and the striking fact, not with “I exploit this natural experiment.”

### The pitch the paper should have

Here is what the first two paragraphs should basically say:

> Starting a business is easier in some legal forms than others, but economists still do not know how much these administrative barriers actually matter for entrepreneurship. When governments simplify business registration, do they create genuinely new firms, or merely induce entrepreneurs who would have registered anyway to choose a cheaper legal channel?
>
> Argentina offers a rare and unusually sharp answer. In 2020, Buenos Aires City effectively shut down registration in its simplified corporate form, causing that channel to collapse almost overnight. I show that only about half of would-be entrants rerouted into traditional corporate forms; the other half did not appear in the main formal registration channels at all, reducing total firm creation by 16 percent. The central implication is that entry barriers do not just relabel firms—they deter a substantial share of formal entrepreneurial activity.

That is the story. The current introduction contains it, but does not yet front-load it with enough confidence or economy.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### Contribution in one sentence

The paper provides unusually clean evidence that shutting down a simplified business-registration channel reduces total formal firm creation, because only about half of displaced entrants substitute into traditional legal forms while the rest exit the formal margin.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?

Partially, but not fully. The paper differentiates itself from the classic entry-regulation literature by emphasizing substitution versus real creation. That is the right axis. But the differentiation still reads a bit like: “existing papers study simplification; I study reversal.” That is helpful, but not enough on its own.

The paper needs to be crisper about what exactly prior papers could not distinguish and why this setting newly identifies that distinction. The novelty is not merely “Argentina” or “a ban instead of a reform.” The novelty is: **a sharp closure of one legal form, in a setting where alternative formal legal forms remain available, lets the paper measure rerouting versus genuine suppression within the formal sector.**

That is a stronger and more memorable contribution than “another paper on entry regulation.”

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a gap in a literature?

Mostly about the world, which is good. The best line in the paper is essentially: when the easiest formal entry route disappears, do entrepreneurs reroute or disappear? That is a world question.

But the introduction still has some “filling a gap” energy—especially around decomposition and method. The paper should lean harder into the substantive question: **How many entrepreneurs are marginal to entry frictions?**

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?

Yes, but only if they read carefully. Right now, a smart economist might say: “It’s a DiD paper on Argentina showing that a ban on a simplified corporate form reduced registrations, with some substitution to other forms.” That is accurate, but too procedural.

What you want them to say is: **“This paper quantifies the share of entrepreneurs who are truly marginal to legal entry barriers: when the easy incorporation vehicle disappeared, about half switched and half never showed up.”**

That is much better.

### What would make the contribution bigger?

Most importantly: make the object of interest bigger than “firm registrations by legal type.”

Specific ways:

1. **Connect to economic activity, not just legal filings.**  
   If possible, link to tax activation, employment, invoicing, social security registration, VAT enrollment, or bank-account opening. The paper currently shows fewer registrations; the AER version would show fewer economically active formal firms.

2. **Show who is marginal.**  
   Even coarse heterogeneity would help: industry, firm size proxy, capital requirement sensitivity, solo vs multi-founder, sectors where SAS was especially attractive. Right now the mechanism is asserted but not shown.

3. **Exploit the reactivation more ambitiously.**  
   The restoration under Milei is potentially a second act. If the same margin partially reopens and firms come back, that gives the paper a stronger “closure and reopening” narrative rather than a one-shot ban story.

4. **Reframe from legal substitution to entrepreneurial margin.**  
   The big question is not “what share moved from SAS to SRL?” but “what share of would-be entrepreneurs are deterred by administrative complexity?” If the paper can operationalize that more directly, it gets larger.

5. **Broaden the implications beyond development.**  
   This is not only about informality or developing countries. It is also about organizational form choice, administrative burdens, and the elasticity of business formation to regulatory design.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

### Closest neighbors

The closest papers are probably:

1. **Djankov, La Porta, Lopez-de-Silanes, and Shleifer (2002), “The Regulation of Entry”**  
   Big-picture motivation, but not a close empirical neighbor.

2. **Bruhn (2011), “License to Sell: The Effect of Business Registration Reform on Entrepreneurial Activity in Mexico”**  
   Very close in spirit: does simplification increase real entrepreneurial activity?

3. **Kaplan, Piedra, and Seira (2011), on business registration reform in Mexico**  
   Close because it pushes on whether reforms create new firms or mostly induce relabeling / limited real effects.

4. **Klapper, Laeven, and Rajan (2006), “Entry Regulation as a Barrier to Entrepreneurship”**  
   Again, broad framing and comparative evidence.

5. **Ulyssea (2018), “Firms, Informality, and Development”**  
   Not a direct neighbor on design, but important for interpretation if some displaced entrants go informal or remain unobserved.

Potentially also some work on **Colombia’s SAS** or on **legal form choice** should be brought in more directly if relevant.

### How should the paper position itself relative to those neighbors?

Mostly **build on them**, not attack them.

- Build on **Bruhn** and **Kaplan** by saying: those papers asked whether simplification matters, but the mechanism is hard to separate because easing entry can both attract new firms and relabel existing activity. This paper uses the closure of a simplified channel to isolate the substitution margin more cleanly.
- Build on **Djankov et al.** by offering micro evidence on one mechanism behind cross-country correlations.
- Connect to **Ulyssea** by acknowledging that “disappearing from formal registration” is not identical to disappearing from economic activity, but still highly informative about the formalization margin.

The tone should be: “This paper clarifies a core ambiguity in an existing conversation,” not “the prior literature got it wrong.”

### Is the paper positioned too narrowly or too broadly?

At present, a bit **too narrowly in evidence and too broadly in rhetoric**.

- Too narrow in evidence because the actual outcome is registrations in three legal forms in one city-country setting.
- Too broad in rhetoric because it sometimes sounds like it has settled the entire debate over whether entry barriers matter.

The right balance is: **this is a sharp, policy-relevant estimate of the marginal entrepreneur’s responsiveness to legal complexity in a setting with unusually clean institutional variation.** That is strong enough. It does not need grand claims about all entrepreneurship everywhere.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?

A few possibilities:

1. **Legal form choice / organizational economics.**  
   The switch from SAS to SA/SRL is not just a registration decision; it is also a legal-form choice under different fixed costs and governance rules.

2. **Administrative burden / state capacity literature.**  
   The paper is really about bureaucratic friction. It could speak to work on take-up costs, procedural burdens, and how state design shapes economic participation.

3. **Formalization literature beyond entry reform.**  
   The paper cites some development staples, but could engage more with work on tax registration, informality, and the costs of entering formal systems.

4. **Entrepreneurship and business dynamism literature.**  
   Particularly if the paper wants to speak to developed-country readers too, it should connect the setting to wider concerns about startup formation and regulatory barriers.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?

Mostly yes, but it may be having **too much of a development-policy conversation** and not enough of an **organizational-form / administrative-frictions / business dynamism conversation**.

The unexpected but fruitful literature bridge here is not just informality. It is: **how much entrepreneurial activity is sensitive to paperwork and legal design?** That speaks much more broadly across fields.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Governments simplify business entry in hopes of encouraging entrepreneurship, but observed increases in registrations may reflect either real new firm creation or simple relabeling across legal categories.

### Tension

The core empirical problem is that most deregulation episodes do not cleanly separate those two channels. If a simplified form becomes available, higher use of that form does not tell us whether entrepreneurs are newly created or merely re-sorted.

### Resolution

Argentina’s de facto shutdown of the SAS in Buenos Aires provides a sharp test. When the easy legal channel closed, only about half of the lost SAS registrations reappeared in other legal forms, and total firm creation fell materially.

### Implications

Administrative entry barriers have real bite. They do not merely change labels on existing entrepreneurs; they deter a substantial fraction of formal entry. That matters for policy design and for how economists interpret reduced-form changes in registration channels.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the ingredients of one, but it still reads somewhat like **a collection of sensible results arranged around a plausible story**, rather than a tightly controlled narrative.

The main issue is that the paper oscillates between three possible stories:

1. entry barriers suppress entrepreneurship;
2. substitution across legal forms matters;
3. methodological point: studying one channel alone overstates effects.

All three are true, but they are not equally important. The paper needs one main story and one secondary payoff.

The right hierarchy is:

- **Main story:** how many entrepreneurs are marginal to entry frictions?
- **Secondary payoff:** you can only answer that by tracking substitution across legal forms.

Right now, the “substitution decomposition” sometimes sounds like the main contribution in itself. It is better understood as the device that lets the paper answer the bigger substantive question.

### If it is a collection of results looking for a story, what story should it tell?

It should tell this story:

> Economists know that simpler entry rules increase use of simplified channels, but they do not know how many firms are truly created versus relabeled. Argentina’s reversal reveals the size of the marginal entrepreneur: when the easy channel disappeared, around half of would-be entrants paid the higher fixed cost of traditional incorporation, while the other half vanished from formal registration. That is direct evidence that administrative complexity meaningfully reduces formal entrepreneurial entry.

That story is cleaner than “The substitution illusion.”

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?

“When Buenos Aires shut down its simplified corporate form, total firm registrations fell by 16 percent—even though entrepreneurs could still use the traditional corporate forms.”

Or even better:

“Only about half of the entrepreneurs displaced by the ban switched legal forms; the other half disappeared from the main formal registration channels.”

That is the memorable fact.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

They would lean in, at least initially. The setting is sharp, the result is intuitive but not trivial, and the substitution share is a nice concrete object.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Immediately: **“Did those ‘missing’ firms actually disappear, or did they go informal / delay / show up elsewhere economically?”**

That is the key follow-up, and the paper should anticipate it more explicitly. Right now it acknowledges this in caveats, but for strategic positioning it should move that issue closer to center stage. The paper does not need to resolve it fully, but it must show that it understands that “not registering” is not the same as “not existing.”

A second likely question: **“Why is Buenos Aires a good laboratory, and how representative is it?”**  
Again, the paper partly addresses this, but the external-validity discussion could be more strategically framed.

### If the findings are modest, is the null itself interesting?

The findings are not null; the effect is meaningful. But the paper should avoid overselling the “half disappeared” result as if it were proof of destroyed real activity. It is best read as strong evidence on the formal entry margin and suggestive evidence on broader entrepreneurship.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

This is not primarily a technical-credibility problem. It is mostly a combination of **framing, scope, and ambition**.

### What is the gap?

#### 1. Framing problem
Yes. The science appears to support a better story than the paper currently tells. The paper should be framed around the elasticity of formal entrepreneurial entry to administrative complexity, not around a particular Argentine legal form per se.

#### 2. Scope problem
Also yes. The paper currently demonstrates an effect on registration counts. For AER, that may not be enough unless the framing is exceptional. The paper would be meaningfully stronger with evidence on:
- whether the missing firms later appear,
- whether economic activity falls, not just legal filings,
- who the marginal entrepreneurs are.

#### 3. Novelty problem
Moderate. The question itself is important and old, so novelty must come from the setting and the sharpness of the answer. The paper has that, but needs to make it unmistakable. Otherwise it risks reading as “another DiD on business registration reform.”

#### 4. Ambition problem
Yes, a bit. The current paper is careful and competent, but somewhat safe. The most ambitious version would try to say something larger about entrepreneurial selection, formalization, and the design of legal institutions—not only about one policy episode.

### The single most impactful piece of advice

**Rebuild the paper around the claim that it identifies the share of entrepreneurs who are truly marginal to legal entry barriers, and then add at least one piece of evidence showing what happens to the “missing half” beyond the registration margin.**

If the author can only change one thing, that is the change.
