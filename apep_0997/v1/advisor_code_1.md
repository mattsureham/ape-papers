**Idea Fidelity**

The paper closely follows the original manifest. It studies Romania’s 2019 construction sector tax holiday, uses sectoral employment and formalization data, and employs a difference-in-differences design comparing construction to other sectors with pre- and post-reform periods. **Summary**

The paper estimates the impact of Romania’s Law 18/2018 construction tax holiday on employment formalization using a TWFE difference-in-differences setup with ten one-digit NACE sectors (2010–2023). It finds that the reform reduced the self-employment share in construction by about 3–4 percentage points, with salaried employment rising substantially while total employment changes remain small, and documents a gradual post-treatment build-up of the effect. Robustness checks (placebo, leave-one-out, sector trends, manufacturing-only control) support the findings.

**Essential Points**

1. **Control group heterogeneity and inference with few clusters.** With only nine control sectors and a single treated sector, the identifying assumption relies heavily on the remaining sectors collectively tracking a valid counterfactual. Given the construction sector’s atypically high pre-reform self-employment share, Table 1 suggests large level differences that might mask divergent trends even if pre-trends appear flat in the aggregated event study. The paper should either (a) better justify why the pooled control group traces the counterfactual—perhaps by showing that combinations of controls mimic construction’s pre-trends on related outcomes (e.g., wage growth or labor costs)—or (b) adopt alternative estimators (e.g., synthetic control using weighted combination of sectors, or smoothed pre-trends via correlated covariates) that reduce reliance on the full-control average. Moreover, inference with ten clusters is unreliable. While leave-one-out and alternative specifications are reported, the paper should report wild cluster bootstrap p-values or a mixed effects approach with inference robust to few clusters.

2. **Mechanism ambiguity and bundling of policy components.** The reform combined income tax exemption, health contribution reduction, and a minimum wage hike. The paper attributes the formalization effect primarily to lower payroll taxes (exit mechanism), but the bundled minimum wage increase could also shape firms’ incentives or elicit demand responses. Without disaggregating these channels, it is unclear whether the observed formalization is driven by the tax cuts, the minimum wage, or simply stronger enforcement/compliance narratives. Adding any available data (e.g., variations in firm size, region, or employment levels relative to minimum wage thresholds) to test whether firms most affected by the tax cuts (versus those close to the minimum wage) respond differently would clarify mechanisms. If such variation is not available, the paper should explicitly discuss the limitations of attributing the effect to tax incidence and frame the contribution accordingly.

3. **Absence of demand-side or macro controls.** The construction sector is cyclical and sensitive to credit, public investment, and immigration shocks. The reform’s timing coincided with broader macroeconomic trends (e.g., pre-COVID boom, then pandemic). The paper includes year fixed effects but no time-varying controls that capture sector-specific shocks (e.g., construction output, housing permits, migration). Without controlling for these, the “gradual” post-treatment pattern could partly reflect demand-side recovery. The paper should incorporate sector-specific demand controls (construction output index, housing investment, government infrastructure spending) or, at minimum, show that the trends in such controls are not confounding the DiD estimates (e.g., event study on construction output with the same specification). Otherwise, the identification claim remains somewhat tenuous.

**Suggestions**

1. **Enhance pre-trend assessment with auxiliary outcomes and placebo sectors.** The event study is encouraging but limited to the self-employment share and salaried employment. Adding similar pre-trend plots for related outcomes (average wages, labor cost index, earnings per worker) would reinforce the parallel trends claim. Moreover, consider more placebo treatments (e.g., artificially treating other sectors or assigning treatment to pre-2019 years) to demonstrate that the observed dynamic pattern is unique to construction post-2019.

2. **Explore heterogeneous treatment effects.** The reform may have affected informal employment differently across firm size, region, or sub-sectors (building vs. civil engineering). If disaggregated data are available (e.g., NACE 2-digit outcomes, regional splits), the paper could estimate heterogeneous effects. For example, did regions with higher baseline informality see larger reductions? Did the effect differ between construction firms with foreign partners (more exposed to emigration/labor mobility)? Such heterogeneity would strengthen the story about the exit mechanism and could speak to the reform’s external validity.

3. **Formalize comparison with theoretical predictions.** The discussion references the “exit vs. exclusion” literature and suggests the effect is due to tax wedge reduction, but the empirical section stops short of directly testing this. As a suggestion, regress the change in the self-employment share on the reduction in the effective tax wedge (perhaps proxied by sector-level average wages relative to the new minimum wage) to see if sectors more exposed to the tax cut respond more. Alternatively, include interactions between the treatment indicator and proxies for enforcement intensity (regional inspections, e.g., if available). This would situate the findings within the broader literature and demonstrate where Romania fits within the set of experiments on informality.

4. **Clarify the treatment onset and compliance window.** The event study shows zero effect in 2019 and growing effects thereafter. It would be helpful to explain whether firms were able to switch to formal contracts immediately in January 2019 or whether legislative/regulatory guidance (e.g., enrollment processes) introduced delays. If the gradual pattern reflects implementation frictions rather than economic adjustment, that nuance should be highlighted. Additionally, was Law 18/2018 temporary or permanent? If there were expectations of repeal or policy uncertainty, mention how that might affect behavior.

5. **Provide more detail on data construction.** The text mentions Eurostat datasets but does not specify how sectoral employment is aligned across sources or how self-employment is defined (e.g., does it include unpaid family workers?). Also, clarify whether the employment counts are headcounts or full-time equivalent, and whether they include informal workers at all. If there are measurement issues (e.g., undercoverage of informal workers in the Labor Force Survey), acknowledge them and, if possible, argue why they don’t bias the DiD (e.g., if undercounting is stable across sectors). Including a short appendix on data cleaning/codebook would be valuable for replication.

6. **Address potential spillovers or general equilibrium effects.** The reform targeted a major sector; its impact may have spilled over into neighboring sectors (e.g., suppliers in manufacturing) or shifted labor across sectors. While the presented placebo (real estate) shows no direct effect, the paper could explore whether the control sectors experienced any offsetting changes (e.g., did manufacturing see relative declines as construction hired more formal workers?). Reporting coefficients for all control sectors (e.g., in an event study) would show whether the treated vs. control contrast is driven by any particular sector’s idiosyncratic shift.

7. **Re-check the standard errors and statistical inference.** Given the small number of sectors, conventional cluster-robust SEs can be misleading. The leave-one-out exercise is helpful, but supplement it with wild cluster bootstrap p-values, as suggested by \citet{cameron2008bootstrap}. Alternatively, randomization inference (permutation tests swapping sector labels) would provide additional assurance that the result isn’t due to idiosyncratic noise.

8. **Frame the conclusion with policy caveats.** The conclusion currently suggests that large tax incentives can formalize employment, but the empirical results stem from a unique sectoral package with multiple components and a favorable macro context. Emphasize that replicating the effect requires similar bundle size and institutional capacity (e.g., enforcement to ensure firms comply). This will keep the policy implications grounded and prevent overgeneralization.

Overall, the paper makes a compelling contribution, but addressing these concerns will solidify its identification claims and enrich its policy relevance.
