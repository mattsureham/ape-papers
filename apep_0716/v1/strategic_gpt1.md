## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether disclosure requirements act like a tax on nonprofits: do organizations manipulate or constrain reported revenues to stay below the IRS threshold that triggers the much more burdensome Form 990? Using the universe of registered nonprofits, the paper documents bunching just below the $200,000 receipts cutoff, suggesting that compliance and disclosure burdens meaningfully distort organizational behavior in a large sector that policymakers usually treat as mission-driven rather than regulation-sensitive.

Does a busy economist care? Potentially yes: the broad question is whether information regulation changes real behavior, not just reporting, and whether transparency mandates impose material fixed costs on smaller organizations.

### Does the paper articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Not quite. The current opening is competent, but it is still framed too much as “there is a threshold, I estimate bunching” rather than “here is a big economic question about disclosure regulation, and this setting gives a clean window into it.” The first paragraph is better than many, but the second quickly slides into sector scale and institutional detail before really sharpening the world-level question.

The first two paragraphs should more explicitly say:

1. **Disclosure rules are not free**; they can distort behavior just like taxes do.  
2. **We know this in some corporate settings, but not in the nonprofit sector**, where transparency is central to public accountability.  
3. **The paper shows that nonprofits alter behavior to avoid the full-disclosure regime**, with stronger responses among organizations likely to face high fixed compliance costs.

### The pitch the paper should have

“Do disclosure mandates distort behavior? This paper studies a sharp filing threshold in the nonprofit sector: organizations above $200,000 in gross receipts must file the much more detailed IRS Form 990, while those below can use the shorter Form 990-EZ. Using the universe of registered U.S. nonprofits, I show substantial bunching just below the threshold, implying that transparency requirements function like a fixed compliance tax, especially for smaller and less administratively sophisticated organizations.”

That is the AER-facing version. It is about **the economics of disclosure regulation**, not mainly about **Form 990 administration**.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper’s contribution is to show, using universe nonprofit data, that a federal disclosure threshold induces nonprofits to bunch below the cutoff, implying that compliance and transparency requirements create economically meaningful behavioral distortions.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?

Only partially. The paper names a few literatures, but the differentiation is still thin. Right now the contribution sounds like:

- bunching paper in a new setting,
- nonprofit regulation paper with a federal threshold,
- compliance-cost paper using revealed preference.

That is fine as a start, but not enough for AER unless one of those margins becomes clearly first-order.

What is missing is a crisp statement of **why this setting changes what we know**, rather than just extending bunching to another threshold. “First federal-level bunching analysis using universe nonprofit data” is a dataset distinction, not yet an intellectual distinction.

### World question or literature gap?

Mixed, but too often framed as a literature gap. The stronger framing is available and should be used: this is a paper about **how transparency regulation affects organizational scale and disclosure avoidance in the real world**. That is stronger than “the first bunching estimate for nonprofits.”

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the intro?

Right now they could probably say: “It’s a bunching paper on nonprofit filing thresholds showing some avoidance.” That is not enough. They would not yet say: “This changes how I think about disclosure regulation.”

The paper needs to help the reader say something like:  
**“Nonprofit disclosure requirements create a real fixed cost, and that cost is large enough to alter organizational scale or reporting choices among smaller charities.”**

That is much better than “another DiD/bunching paper about a threshold.”

### What would make the contribution bigger?

Several possibilities, in descending order of payoff:

1. **Reframe from nonprofit administration to disclosure regulation more broadly.**  
   The paper should claim relevance to the economics of transparency, regulatory design, and organizational behavior under non-tax mandates.

2. **Clarify whether the response is about reporting avoidance, real activity avoidance, or scrutiny avoidance.**  
   Even without new identification, the paper can organize the contribution around these competing economic interpretations. Right now it gestures at all three. A more explicit conceptual framing would enlarge the paper.

3. **Exploit the 2010 threshold reform as part of the story, even descriptively.**  
   The current “legacy bunching at $100k” point is suggestive but underdeveloped. If the paper could more clearly show migration of bunching from the old threshold to the new one, the contribution would feel much more like a paper about behavior responding to regulatory design rather than a static cross-sectional irregularity.

4. **Make outcomes more substantively legible.**  
   “941 organizations bunch” is not a naturally gripping fact. More compelling would be: what share of organizations near the cutoff distort, which types do so, and what that implies for the amount of activity or disclosure missing from the public record.

5. **Lean harder into heterogeneity as mechanism.**

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Nonprofits are large, economically important, and subject to public disclosure rules intended to promote accountability. At a fixed receipts threshold, they move from a short form to a much more detailed federal filing.

### Tension

Transparency is supposed to improve oversight, but if disclosure is costly or invites scrutiny, organizations may manipulate reporting or limit growth to avoid it. We do not know whether this burden is behaviorally meaningful in the nonprofit sector.

### Resolution

There is significant bunching just below the $200,000 threshold, no comparable bunching at a lower-burden placebo threshold, and stronger responses among smaller and certain types of nonprofits.

### Implications

Disclosure regulation is not neutral. It creates distortions and may disproportionately burden organizations with weak administrative capacity, potentially reducing both growth and the amount of information available exactly where oversight might matter most.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

A **serviceable but not fully convincing** one.

The ingredients are there, but the story is still somewhat “result inventory”-like:

- main bunching estimate,
- placebo,
- round-number tests,
- heterogeneity,
- state variation,
- discussion.

What is missing is a stronger through-line. The paper should tell a simpler story:

1. **Disclosure thresholds can act like fixed taxes.**
2. **This matters especially in nonprofits, where disclosure is central to public accountability.**
3. **The threshold induces avoidance.**
4. **The avoidance is concentrated where compliance costs or scrutiny costs should bite most.**
5. **Therefore, transparency policy has an equity-efficiency tradeoff that regulators should recognize.**

Right now, the paper has results looking for that story, rather than that story governing the presentation of results.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?

“I found clear bunching of nonprofits just below the revenue cutoff that triggers the full IRS Form 990, suggesting that disclosure requirements function like a fixed tax on smaller organizations.”

That is the cleanest hook.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

Some would lean in, but only if you immediately connect it to the bigger point: **disclosure mandates distort behavior even in the nonprofit sector**. If you leave it at “there’s bunching at a filing threshold,” many will classify it as a competent niche application.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Almost certainly:  
**“Is this real activity, reporting manipulation, or avoidance of public scrutiny?”**

That is the central economic question the paper raises and only partially answers. Importantly, that is not a fatal weakness for positioning; it is actually the question that makes the paper interesting. But the paper needs to foreground it as the key interpretation challenge, not bury it in discussion.

### If findings are modest, is that okay?

Yes, but the paper needs to sell the null/modest dimensions better. “Approximately 941 organizations” sounds small in levels, and “0.7% of those in the analysis window” sounds smaller still. That weakens the rhetoric if left uncontextualized.

The author should avoid overselling the magnitude and instead emphasize:

- the presence of a **sharp behavioral response** in a sector where we often assume compliance with mission-oriented regulation,
- the **concentration among vulnerable organizations**,
- the fact that the observed bunching is just the visible margin of a broader burden that also affects organizations that pay the cost rather than bunch.

The result is interesting if presented as evidence of **behavioral distortion under disclosure mandates**, not as a giant aggregate welfare effect.
