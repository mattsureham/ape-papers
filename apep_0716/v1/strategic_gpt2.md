## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether nonprofit organizations distort their behavior to avoid crossing the IRS threshold that triggers the much more demanding Form 990 filing requirement. Using the universe of registered nonprofits, it shows bunching just below the $200,000 receipts cutoff, suggesting that disclosure and compliance rules act like a real tax on organizational scale.

Why should a busy economist care? Because this is potentially about a broad question, not a nonprofit question: do disclosure mandates distort firm or organizational behavior, and how large are those distortions when the policy instrument is information rather than taxes?

Does the paper articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs? Not quite. The current introduction gets to the threshold and the bunching result quickly, but it frames the paper as “the first bunching-based estimate” in this setting. That is a methodology-and-gap pitch, not a world-and-question pitch. It also leans too early on institutional detail and “hidden tax” rhetoric without fully clarifying why the phenomenon matters beyond this filing form.

### The pitch the paper should have

Here is the first-two-paragraph pitch the paper should have:

> Governments increasingly rely on disclosure mandates to regulate organizations, but we know much less about the behavioral distortions created by disclosure thresholds than by tax thresholds. In the nonprofit sector, crossing the IRS’s $200,000 receipts threshold triggers a substantially more detailed public filing, requiring disclosure of compensation, governance, and lobbying information. If organizations manipulate reported activity or constrain growth to avoid that filing, then transparency rules carry real behavioral costs.
>
> This paper shows that they do. Using the universe of IRS-registered nonprofits, I document sharp bunching just below the $200,000 threshold and little comparable bunching at lower thresholds with much smaller disclosure changes. The evidence implies that even in a sector with no profit motive and no tax liability at stake, information requirements distort reported scale and likely organizational growth—highlighting a general tradeoff between accountability and compliance burden in threshold-based regulation.

That is the AER-relevant version of the paper. The current version is one rung below that: competent institutional paper, but not yet framed as a general economics question.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper shows that nonprofit organizations bunch below the IRS Form 990 filing threshold, implying that disclosure/compliance requirements alone can generate threshold avoidance behavior even absent a direct tax liability.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest papers?

Only partially. The paper distinguishes itself as:
1. a bunching paper in a nonprofit setting,
2. the first federal-level analysis of this threshold,
3. a “revealed preference” estimate of compliance burden.

But none of those, by themselves, is a big contribution statement for AER. “First federal-level bunching study in this niche setting” is a field-journal contribution unless tied to a larger economic claim. Right now the paper risks sounding like “another bunching paper, now in nonprofits.”

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a gap in a literature?

Mostly as filling a literature gap. The phrases “first bunching-based estimate,” “extends bunching to nonprofits,” and “contributes to three literatures” are all literature-gap moves. The stronger framing is the world question:

- Do disclosure mandates distort organizational scale?
- Are compliance costs large enough to alter behavior even in nonprofit sectors?
- Do transparency thresholds reduce information quality by inducing organizations to stay small or misreport?

That is the stronger version, and the paper only intermittently gets there.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the intro?

At present, they would probably say: “It’s a bunching paper showing nonprofits avoid the full 990 filing threshold.” That is clear enough, but not exciting enough. They would not naturally say: “This changes how I think about disclosure regulation.”

### What would make the contribution bigger?

Most importantly: show that the paper is about **the real effects of disclosure regulation**, not just bunching at a threshold.

Specific ways to make it bigger:
- **Exploit the 2010 reform more centrally.** Right now the old $100k threshold is treated as a placebo / legacy fact. That is a missed opportunity. The reform is the natural way to elevate the paper from static threshold response to dynamic migration of behavior when the rule changes.
- **Sharpen the mechanism as disclosure burden versus scrutiny aversion.** The paper currently mixes direct compliance costs and indirect scrutiny costs. Those are different economics. If it can distinguish them, the contribution becomes much more interesting.
- **Expand the implications beyond “941 organizations bunch.”** That number is too small and too local to carry an AER paper. The larger contribution has to be conceptual: threshold-based disclosure rules distort who grows, what gets reported, and how much transparency regulators actually obtain.
- **Show implications for information quality or organizational growth.** If the filing threshold leads to underreporting, timing distortions, or suppressed scale, that matters. If the paper can’t show that directly, it needs a stronger conceptual framing around why bunching here is socially costly.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

The world before this paper: governments use filing and disclosure thresholds to economize on regulatory burden, assuming that smaller organizations can be spared more onerous reporting while larger ones face more scrutiny.

### Tension

Those thresholds may distort behavior. But unlike tax notches, disclosure thresholds do not directly change tax payments, so it is not obvious whether organizations will meaningfully respond. If they do, that would imply that paperwork, scrutiny, and disclosure mandates themselves have economically meaningful bite.

### Resolution

The paper finds bunching just below the $200,000 threshold for the full Form 990, no similar bunching at a lower-stakes filing threshold, and stronger responses among smaller and religious organizations.

### Implications

Disclosure regulation is not neutral. It can induce avoidance, potentially reduce transparency, and disproportionately burden smaller or less professionalized organizations.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

Serviceable, but not fully coherent. It has the ingredients of a narrative, but the story is still too much “result inventory”:
- main bunching estimate,
- placebo threshold,
- old threshold,
- round-number placebos,
- heterogeneity,
- state variation.

That reads like a solid field paper. An AER paper would have a tighter spine: **disclosure thresholds distort behavior; here is the clean evidence; here is who responds and why; here is what that means for transparency policy.**

### If it’s a collection of results looking for a story, what story should it tell?

The story should be:

> Regulators use disclosure thresholds to target oversight efficiently, but those same thresholds cause avoidance. In nonprofits, where there is no tax payment at stake, the existence of bunching reveals that disclosure burden and scrutiny alone are enough to distort reported scale. The organizations most likely to respond are precisely those least equipped to bear fixed compliance costs. Thus, threshold-based transparency rules may perversely reduce transparency where accountability is most fragile.

That is the story. Everything in the paper should serve it.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?

“I found that nonprofits bunch right below the IRS cutoff that forces them to file the long public disclosure form, even though there’s no tax liability change at the threshold.”

That is a decent opener. Better than average.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

They would lean in briefly, because the absence of a direct tax change makes the setting conceptually interesting. But the next question comes fast: “Okay, but how important is that beyond this niche threshold?”

That is the vulnerability.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Probably one of these:
- “Is this about paperwork costs or about avoiding public scrutiny?”
- “Do organizations actually stay smaller, or just relabel revenue?”
- “Did bunching move when the threshold changed in 2010?”
- “Why should this matter for how we design disclosure rules elsewhere?”

The best version of the paper has strong answers to at least two of these. The current draft only partially does.

### If the findings are modest, is the modesty itself interesting?

Yes, but the paper does not yet handle the scale issue well. “941 organizations” sounds small. The author tries to rescue this by saying it is 0.7% in the window and likely a lower bound. That is fine, but not enough. The real case for interest is not that the quantity is huge; it is that **the behavior exists at all in a setting with no tax notch**. That’s the intellectually interesting null-to-positive contrast.

So the paper should stop leaning on the absolute number and lean harder on the conceptual point:
- even non-tax disclosure thresholds create distortion,
- in a sector presumed mission-driven,
- among smaller organizations with weak administrative capacity.

That is the “so what.”
