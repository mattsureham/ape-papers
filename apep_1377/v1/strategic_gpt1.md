## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a big development question: when governments privatize essential infrastructure, do households benefit—or do outcomes depend mostly on which private operator gets the franchise? Using Nigeria’s 2013 electricity privatization, the paper argues that households assigned to better-performing distribution companies received more reliable electricity and better welfare outcomes, while households in poorly performing territories were made worse off.

A busy economist should care because this is not just “does privatization work?” but “when does privatization improve household welfare, and when does it simply replace a public monopoly with uneven private monopoly?” That is a first-order policy question in development, regulation, and political economy.

### Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Not clearly enough. The opening is headed in the right direction, but it does not quite land the central question in a crisp, high-level way. The current intro starts with the reform and then moves quickly into cross-DisCo performance dispersion. That is useful context, but the paper’s real hook is broader: privatization is often sold as a remedy for state failure, yet the welfare consequences may hinge on operator quality within a monopoly franchise setting.

Right now, the introduction reads like a study of “variation in collection efficiency across DisCos,” which is a mechanism/measurement framing, not a compelling economic question. The first two paragraphs should say, much more directly: privatization of monopoly infrastructure is not a binary treatment; the welfare effects depend on the capability and incentives of the private firms that replace the state.

### The pitch the paper should have

> Governments across the developing world privatize electricity distribution in the hope that private ownership will deliver better service than failing state utilities. But in sectors organized as regional monopolies, privatization does not create competition; it replaces one monopolist with another, and household welfare may depend less on ownership itself than on the quality of the operator that wins the franchise.  
>  
> This paper studies Nigeria’s 2013 electricity privatization, one of the largest in Sub-Saharan Africa, which assigned households to 11 regional distribution companies with sharply different post-privatization performance. I ask whether households served by better-performing distributors experienced better electricity access and welfare—and, conversely, whether privatization by weak operators can leave households worse off than the state-run status quo.

That is the paper’s best version. It frames a world question, not a variable question.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper’s contribution is to argue that the household welfare consequences of electricity privatization in Nigeria depended strongly on the capability of the private distribution company, implying that privatization of monopoly infrastructure is only as good as the operator that inherits the franchise.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?

Not yet. The paper cites some broad privatization and electricity-access work, but it does not sharply mark out what is new relative to likely neighbors. The natural comparison set includes:
- cross-country privatization papers showing average efficiency gains,
- studies of electrification/access and household welfare,
- papers on regulated utilities and service quality,
- work on state capacity and implementation quality in infrastructure reform.

The current contribution is still fuzzy because the paper is caught between three different claims:
1. privatization did not deliver promised gains in Nigeria;
2. operator quality matters more than privatization per se;
3. collection efficiency predicts household welfare.

These are related but not identical. The strongest of them is #2. That is the one to lean into.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or filling a gap in a LITERATURE?

Mostly the former in aspiration, but too often the prose slips into “this paper estimates the effect of DisCo quality measured by collection efficiency.” That sounds like a design description, not a world question. The paper should more consistently ask: what determines whether privatization of essential monopoly services helps households?

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the intro?

At present, maybe not cleanly. They might say: “It’s a DiD paper using Nigeria’s electricity privatization to show that households did better in places with better distribution companies.” That is not bad, but it still sounds like “another DiD paper about a reform with heterogeneous treatment intensity.”

What you want them to say is: “It shows that in monopoly infrastructure, privatization is not the treatment—operator capability is. The same privatization can generate gains in some territories and losses in others.” That is more memorable and more general.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Several possibilities:

1. **Stronger framing around monopoly privatization rather than electricity alone.**  
   The biggest conceptual upgrade is to make this about franchised monopoly sectors: electricity distribution, water, rail concessions, bus franchises, telecom in early liberalization, etc. The paper should speak to how ownership change interacts with franchise quality when household choice is absent.

2. **Sharper comparison to the state-run baseline.**  
   The paper currently wants to say “worse than the status quo” in low-quality territories. That is a bigger and riskier claim than “better operators deliver better outcomes.” If the paper can genuinely sustain that framing, it becomes much more important. If not, it should stop hinting at it and instead focus on heterogeneity within privatization.

3. **Mechanisms more central to the story.**  
   Right now mechanisms are generic. The paper would feel larger if it could organize the contribution around one concrete mechanism: maintenance/investment, payment discipline, or generator substitution. “Households pay more and get less because weak distributors cannot translate billing into service reliability” is more powerful than a menu of possible channels.

4. **A more consequential welfare margin.**  
   Study hours are potentially important, but the magnitudes look almost too dramatic, and employment/enterprise results are weak. If there is a more directly interpretable welfare outcome—generator ownership, fuel spending, appliance damage, school attendance, business operating hours—that might strengthen the paper’s practical punch.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Developing countries often privatize failing state utilities to improve service delivery. Nigeria’s 2013 power-sector reform is a major example: one large state monopoly was broken into regional private distributors.

### Tension

Privatization is supposed to improve efficiency, but monopoly distribution franchises do not create consumer choice or market competition. So the core tension is: if households cannot choose providers, does privatization actually improve welfare—or does everything depend on which private monopolist they happen to be assigned to?

### Resolution

The paper’s resolution is that post-privatization outcomes diverged sharply across territories, and households in areas served by stronger distributors experienced better electricity service and better downstream welfare outcomes than those in areas served by weaker distributors.

### Implications

The implication is not merely that “privatization can fail.” It is that privatization in monopoly infrastructure is fundamentally an operator-selection and regulatory-capacity problem. Ownership change without capable operators and credible oversight may simply redistribute rents while leaving households exposed.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

A serviceable one, but it is not yet disciplined. Too much of the current draft feels like a collection of results attached to a familiar reform design.

The paper’s real story should be:

1. Privatization of monopoly infrastructure is often sold as a uniform reform.
2. In practice, households are locked into whatever operator wins their territory.
3. Nigeria provides a large, salient case where operator performance diverged dramatically after a common reform.
4. These differences translated into large household-level welfare differences.
5. Therefore, the key object is not privatization in the abstract, but the capacity and accountability of the private monopolist.

That is a coherent AER-style narrative. The current version gestures toward it but does not consistently organize the paper around it.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?

I would lead with something like:

> “Nigeria privatized electricity distribution nationwide, but because households were locked into regional private monopolies, the welfare effects depended enormously on which company got their territory. In some places privatization appears to have delivered much better service; in others, households were left paying more for backup energy and getting less reliable grid power.”

That is the interesting fact. Not “a 10-point increase in collection efficiency changes Y by Z.” That is too regression-coded for conversation.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

They would lean in if you frame it as a lesson about privatization under monopoly and weak regulation. They would reach for their phones if you frame it as “I have a continuous-treatment DiD using collection efficiency.”

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Probably one of these:
- “So is this really about privatization, or just about regional inequality between Lagos and the North?”
- “What exactly does collection efficiency proxy for—management quality, wealth, theft, politics?”
- “Are the bad operators actually worse than the old state utility, or just worse than the good operators?”
- “What should governments do differently—screen operators better, regulate harder, or keep distribution public?”

Those questions are good for the paper. The introduction and discussion should anticipate them and make clear what claim the paper is and is not making.

### If findings are null or modest

Not relevant here; the paper claims large effects. But the employment and enterprise results are modest/null, and that actually suggests a useful editorial point: the paper should not oversell broad economic transformation if the strongest evidence is on electricity access, study time, and energy costs. Better to claim a tight welfare channel than a sprawling development miracle.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

### What is the gap?

Primarily **a framing and ambition problem**, with some **novelty-positioning problem**.

The raw topic is absolutely AER-relevant: privatization, infrastructure, household welfare, state capacity, monopoly service delivery in a major developing-country setting. But the current paper reads smaller than it should because it is written as an applied reform evaluation rather than as a broader economic statement.

It also suffers from a serious “what exactly is the claim?” problem:
- Is this about privatization versus public ownership?
- Or operator quality within privatization?
- Or collection efficiency as a proxy for firm performance?
- Or regional divergence after reform?

Until the paper chooses one main claim and subordinates the rest, it will feel like a competent but not field-shifting study.

### Is it a framing problem?

Yes, strongly. The science may or may not hold up under review, but editorially the biggest issue is that the paper undersells and simultaneously confuses its own most important idea.

### Is it a scope problem?

Somewhat. To excite the top people in this field, the paper likely needs either:
- stronger mechanism evidence, or
- a more general institutional framing that travels beyond Nigeria.

At present it is a bit too tied to a single performance metric.

### Is it a novelty problem?

Moderately. “Privatization has heterogeneous effects” is not new. “In privatized monopoly infrastructure, consumer welfare depends on operator capability because households are locked into assigned providers” is more novel and much stronger. That distinction needs to be made explicit.

### Is it an ambition problem?

Yes. The paper is aiming at “here is an empirical estimate from Nigeria” when it should be aiming at “here is a general lesson about privatizing essential services under weak state capacity.”

### Single most impactful advice

**Reframe the paper around a broader claim: in monopoly infrastructure, privatization is not the treatment—operator capability and regulatory discipline are—and Nigeria’s reform provides a sharp household-level demonstration of that principle.**

That one change would do the most to move this toward AER territory.
