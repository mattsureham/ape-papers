## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a broad and important question: when governments privatize essential infrastructure, do households benefit, or do outcomes depend entirely on who gets the franchise? Using Nigeria’s 2013 electricity privatization, the paper argues that households assigned to better-performing distribution companies receive more electricity, spend less on expensive backup energy, and see downstream gains in children’s study time.

A busy economist should care because this is not really a paper about one utility reform in one country; it is potentially a paper about the conditions under which privatization improves welfare in weak-state environments. The stakes are large: privatization is a standard policy prescription, but the welfare consequences may hinge less on ownership per se than on operator capability and regulatory capacity.

### Does the paper articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Not yet. The current opening gives institutional background and then quickly turns into a within-Nigeria puzzle about why some DisCos do well and others do poorly. That is a plausible setup, but it is too mid-level and descriptive. The paper’s real hook should be: **privatization is often sold as a binary ownership reform, but in practice it is an allocation mechanism that places citizens under private monopolists of very uneven quality.** That is a much stronger AER-level pitch.

The introduction also gets distracted by “collection efficiency” too early, before the reader understands the larger question. “Collection efficiency” is not the headline; it is the proxy for operator quality. The intro should lead with the policy question and welfare stakes, not the measurement choice.

### The pitch the paper should have

“Privatization is usually evaluated as a change in ownership—from public to private. But for households, the more consequential question is which private operator they get. This paper studies Nigeria’s 2013 electricity privatization and shows that welfare effects varied sharply across the fixed monopoly territories assigned to different distribution companies: households served by higher-capacity operators received more reliable electricity, spent less on costly backup energy, and children studied more. The broader lesson is that privatization without strong operator selection and oversight can leave citizens worse off than the public monopoly it replaced.”

That is the paper’s best version. It is about **state capacity, private capability, and the welfare incidence of privatization**.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper’s contribution is to argue that the welfare effects of electricity privatization depend critically on the quality of the private monopoly operator, and that variation in operator performance after Nigeria’s reform translated into meaningful differences in household welfare.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest papers?

Only partially. Right now, the paper sounds like a generic reduced-form paper on electricity access and household outcomes, with a privatization setting attached. The distinct contribution ought to be one of the following:

1. **Privatization is not a single treatment**; it creates heterogeneous private monopolies.
2. **Operator quality is the key margin**, not ownership itself.
3. **Household welfare is the outcome of interest**, rather than firm efficiency or sector performance.

Those are potentially distinctive. But the introduction does not sharpen them against the existing literature. It says the evidence on privatization is mixed and Nigeria is “different,” but it does not crisply explain whether prior work has:
- focused on firm productivity rather than households,
- compared public vs. private ownership rather than quality variation across private operators,
- emphasized average effects rather than heterogeneous welfare consequences.

Until that distinction is explicit, a smart economist may summarize this as “another DiD paper about electricity reform in a developing country.”

### World question or literature gap?

The paper gestures toward a world question, which is good: **when does privatization improve household welfare?** But it keeps slipping into literature-gap language and case-study descriptives. It should commit much more strongly to the world question:

- Governments privatize infrastructure expecting efficiency gains.
- Citizens experience not “privatization” in the abstract but service from a monopoly provider.
- If provider quality varies, privatization may widen welfare inequality across regions and households.

That is a stronger frame than “the literature lacks evidence from Sub-Saharan Africa.”

### Could a smart economist explain what is new?

Not cleanly, based on the current introduction. They might say: “It studies Nigeria’s electricity privatization using variation across regional distributors and finds better distributors produce better outcomes.” That is intelligible, but not yet memorable. It lacks the conceptual upgrade.

The introduction should make the novelty something like:
- “Privatization reallocates citizens across private states of very different quality.”
- “In monopoly infrastructure, ownership reform is only as good as the operator assignment mechanism.”
- “Average treatment effects of privatization can conceal large welfare dispersion.”

Those are ideas a colleague could repeat.

### What would make the contribution bigger?

Several possibilities:

- **Different framing:** The single biggest upgrade is to frame this as a paper on the welfare consequences of **heterogeneous private state capacity** in essential services, not just electricity privatization.
- **Different comparison:** A stronger comparison would be between the welfare effects of “better private than public” versus “worse private than public,” rather than only high- vs. low-efficiency DisCos.
- **Different outcomes:** The paper would feel bigger if it foregrounded one or two deeper welfare margins—human capital, household coping costs, productive use of electricity—rather than a grab bag of standard outcomes.
- **Different mechanism emphasis:** “Operator quality” is still vague. The contribution would be more powerful if the story became: undercapitalized private monopolists underinvest, households self-insure with generators, and this creates a hidden welfare tax.
- **Different audience framing:** Connect it to broader political economy: privatization as contracting under weak regulation, not just service delivery.

In short: the paper gets bigger if it stops being about one reform and starts being about **why privatization fails under weak operator screening and weak regulation**.

Build on, not attack. The paper should say:

- Prior privatization work mostly studies **average firm or sector performance**.
- Prior electricity work often studies **access or reliability absent privatization design**.
- This paper links the two by showing how **within one privatization event**, fixed assignment to providers of different quality affects household welfare.

That is a nice bridge contribution. The paper need not claim to overturn the literature. It should claim to refine how economists think about privatization.

The right level is: one country, one reform, one sector—but speaking to a broad question about **operator selection and regulatory capacity in privatized monopolies**.

Right now it reads like a development-applied micro paper with a thin literature review. For AER, it needs to sound like part of a larger economics conversation.

Not quite. The current conversation is: “Nigeria privatized electricity; some DisCos did better than others; this affected households.” The better conversation is: **what determines whether privatization in essential services actually creates welfare gains?**

That framing would bring in development, public economics, IO/regulation, and political economy. That is the right conversation.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Governments privatize infrastructure because public utilities are inefficient and fiscally costly. Nigeria’s state electricity monopoly was dysfunctional, so privatization promised investment, better management, and improved service.

### Tension

Privatization is supposed to improve outcomes on average, but in practice monopoly territories were handed to private operators of uneven capability. That creates a central puzzle: if ownership changed everywhere at once, why did welfare gains appear in some places and losses in others? Is privatization really a reform, or just a reassignment of citizens to private monopolists of varying quality?

### Resolution

The paper’s resolution is that differences in operator quality matter enormously: households in better-performing DisCo territories receive more electricity, spend less on backup power, and children study more.

### Implications

The implication is not just “some companies are better than others.” It is that **privatization without effective operator selection, capitalization requirements, and oversight can worsen welfare relative to the status quo**. In other words, the welfare economics of privatization depend on the institutional capacity to choose and discipline private providers.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

Only in embryo. The ingredients are there, but the paper is currently closer to a collection of results than a polished story. The biggest weakness is that the narrative keeps oscillating between:
- “privatization failed,”
- “some private operators succeeded,” and
- “operator quality matters.”

These are related but not identical claims. The paper needs to choose one as the spine.

### What story should it be telling?

The strongest story is:

**Privatization of a monopoly service is not a yes/no ownership reform; it is a provider-assignment problem. Nigeria’s reform shows that when households are locked into private operators of unequal quality, privatization creates large welfare divergence.**

That is a clean setup-tension-resolution arc. Everything else should serve that story.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?

I would lead with this:

“Ten years after Nigeria privatized electricity distribution, households in territories served by higher-capacity distributors had substantially more grid electricity and lower energy coping costs than households assigned to weaker private monopolists.”

That gets attention. It is concrete, and it points to a large policy issue.

### Would people lean in?

Some would. But not yet all.

Why? Because “collection efficiency across DisCos” does not naturally make economists lean in. “Privatization can make citizens better or worse off depending on which private monopolist they get” does. The paper needs to translate institutional detail into a sharp conceptual fact.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Most likely:

- “Is this really about privatization, or just regional differences in income/state capacity?”
- Or: “So is the lesson that private ownership failed, or that weak regulation failed?”
- Or: “Would these households have been better off under the old public monopoly?”

Those are exactly the follow-up questions the paper should anticipate in framing, even if the referees will handle the technical side.

### If findings are modest or null?

The findings are not modest; if anything, they are presented as very large. So the issue is not whether the null is interesting. The issue is whether the paper convinces the reader that the findings speak to a larger question rather than just one reform’s implementation heterogeneity.

Right now, the paper occasionally feels like a failed average-privatization paper that has been reframed as a within-privatization heterogeneity paper. That is not fatal, but it must be turned into a feature, not a fallback. The paper should explicitly say: **the average effect of privatization is the wrong object when monopoly territories are assigned to providers of heterogeneous quality.**

That would make the “so what” much stronger.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

Be honest: in current form, this is not close. The gap is large, but it is not only a “methods” gap. It is primarily a **positioning and ambition gap**.

### What is the main problem?

Mostly:
- **Framing problem**
- **Ambition problem**
- Some **novelty problem**

The paper’s current form is competent in aspiration but not yet persuasive in intellectual positioning. It reads like a standard applied micro paper trying to infer a large message from a narrow institutional variation. For AER, the paper needs to sound like it is changing how economists think about privatization, monopoly provision, and household welfare.

### More specifically

#### Framing problem
The paper frames itself as “distribution company quality and household welfare in Nigeria.” That is accurate but too small. AER papers usually make a larger conceptual move. Here the move should be:

**Privatization of monopoly infrastructure is a provider-assignment problem under weak state capacity.**

That is much more powerful.

#### Scope problem
The scope is narrower than the title suggests. The paper is really about reliability, coping costs, and some household outcomes. That is fine, but it needs a sharper welfare architecture, not a longer menu of outcomes.

#### Novelty problem
“Heterogeneity across utilities matters” is not, by itself, novel enough. The novelty has to be that **this heterogeneity fundamentally alters the object of interest**: average effects of privatization are misleading when citizens are quasi-randomly locked into private monopolists of uneven quality.

#### Ambition problem
The paper is too safe. It reports results, gives a standard policy takeaway, and stops there. It should be bolder in saying:
- why existing privatization debates have focused on the wrong margin,
- why monopoly service provision in low-capacity states raises different welfare questions than textbook privatization,
- why household assignment to providers is the central institutional feature.

### Single most impactful advice

If the author could change only one thing, it would be this:

**Reframe the paper from a Nigeria electricity case study into a general argument that privatization of essential monopoly services creates welfare gains only when the state can select and discipline capable private operators; otherwise it simply reassigns households to private monopolists of unequal quality.**

That is the paper’s route to being memorable.
