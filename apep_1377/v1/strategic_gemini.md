### 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
The paper examines the 2013 privatization of Nigeria’s electricity sector to ask a fundamental question: does the success of privatization depend on the specific identity and capacity of the private firm? By exploiting the exogenous assignment of households to 11 different regional monopolies (DisCos) with radically different operational efficiencies, the paper finds that privatization under a low-capacity operator actually results in massive welfare losses—including lost study hours for children and high generator costs—relative to the previous state-run monopoly.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this reasonably well, but it spends too much time on the history of the reform and not enough on the **conceptual tension**. The pitch should emphasize that this isn't just about "privatization being hard," but about the **selection of firms** as the primary determinant of policy success.

*The pitch the paper should have:* "While economists often debate the merits of private vs. public ownership, we know little about how the heterogenous quality of private firms determines the success of a single national reform. Using the privatization of Nigeria’s electricity grid, I show that the same policy shift can be a boon or a disaster for household welfare depending on which firm wins the regional license. This suggests that in weak institutional environments, the 'who' of privatization is as important as the 'whether'."

### 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**One-sentence contribution:** The paper demonstrates that the benefits of infrastructure privatization are contingent on firm-level operational capacity, showing that "bad" private firms can deliver worse household outcomes than a "bad" state monopoly.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It differentiates itself from the "average effect of privatization" literature (e.g., Megginson & Netter) by focusing on intra-reform heterogeneity.
*   **Framing:** Currently, it leans toward a "gap in the literature" about Nigeria. It needs to be a "question about the world": Is "bad" private management worse than "bad" public management?
*   **Clarity:** A smart economist would see this as "the heterogeneous effects of firm capacity in a natural experiment."
*   **How to make it bigger:** The paper needs to better explore **why** some firms are better. Is it the origin of the capital (foreign vs. domestic)? Is it the initial level of state-level corruption? Identifying the *determinants* of the quality divergence would double the paper's impact.

### 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
*   **Neighbors:** Allcott et al. (2016) on electricity and development; Burgess et al. (2020) on political economy of grid expansion; and the broad firm-productivity literature (Bloom & Van Reenen).
*   **Strategy:** The paper should **attack** the notion of a "unitary" privatization effect. It should position itself as a bridge between Development (welfare effects) and IO/Contract Theory (firm selection).
*   **Niche vs. Broad:** It is currently a bit too "Nigeria-specific." To hit the AER, it needs to speak to the **Industrial Organization of Development**.
*   **Missing Conversations:** It should connect to the literature on **Selection/Procurement**. If the government picks the wrong winners, the efficiency gains of markets disappear.

### 4. NARRATIVE ARC
*   **Setup:** Developing nations privatize utilities to escape the "fiscal drain" of state failure.
*   **Tension:** In Nigeria, the same reform created "islands of success" and "seas of failure" across the same country at the same time.
*   **Resolution:** The difference is "collection efficiency"—a proxy for firm capacity. High-capacity firms improve lives; low-capacity firms lead to "privatized failure" where households pay more for less.
*   **Implications:** Regulatory oversight and firm selection are not "secondary" to privatization—they are the prerequisite for it to be welfare-improving.

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong. It is a "cautionary tale" narrative that resonates well with current skepticism about simple market-based prescriptions in weak-state contexts.

### 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
*   **The Fact:** "In some parts of Nigeria, privatization actually cost children 16 hours of study time per week compared to the old state monopoly."
*   **Reaction:** Lean in. That is a massive, tangible welfare loss that contradicts the "market-is-always-better" heuristic.
*   **Follow-up:** "Why did the government give licenses to the bad firms in the first place? Was it corruption or just a lack of bidders?" (The paper needs to answer this to be AER-level).

### 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
*   **Front-load the Mechanism:** Move the discussion of *why* DisCos diverge (Page 10) much earlier. The reader needs to know if these firms were different "at birth" or if they diverged due to local conditions.
*   **Appendix:** The 11-DisCo map and their respective ownership structures should be a centerpiece, not a footnote.
*   **Shorten:** The general "Privatization and Development" review is a bit generic. Make it more focused on the *heterogeneity* of firm performance.

### 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The gap is **Ambition**. Right now, it’s a very good empirical evaluation of a Nigerian reform. To be an AER paper, it must be a paper about the **Theory of the Firm in Developing Markets**. 

**The single most impactful change:** Adjudicate the "Why." You have 11 firms. Do a deep dive into the 11 owners. Was it the firms with previous utility experience that succeeded? Was it the firms with political connections that failed? If you can show that **ex-ante observable firm traits** predicted these welfare outcomes, you have a powerhouse paper on how to design privatization.
