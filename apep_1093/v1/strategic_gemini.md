## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper examines whether state-level mandates requiring salary ranges in job postings reduce the Black-White earnings gap among new hires. Exploiting the staggered rollout of these laws (led by Colorado), the author finds that transparency narrows the racial gap by approximately 0.9 to 1.4 percentage points, particularly in high-dispersion industries where employer discretion is highest. This suggests that public "price tags" on jobs constrain the ability of firms to offer lower starting wages to Black applicants.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this clearly by the second paragraph. However, the first paragraph is slightly too "policy-heavy." To truly grab an AER reader, it should lead with the theoretical tension: **Does transparency in the *offer* stage (before bargaining) matter more for racial equity than the *reporting* stage (after hiring) studied in the gender literature?**

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**The Contribution:** This paper identifies a significant "spillover" of gender-motivated pay transparency laws onto racial earnings gaps, specifically through the mechanism of constraining employer discretion at the point of hire.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It is well-differentiated from Bennedsen et al. (2022) and Baker et al. (2023) by shifting focus from *gender* to *race* and from *internal reporting* to *external posting*.
*   **Framing:** Currently framed as filling a gap in the literature ("first evidence on..."). It would be stronger if framed as a question about **Search and Bargaining**: Can public information anchors neutralize the "Black-White negotiation penalty"?
*   **Clarity:** A smart economist would understand the "what," but might dismiss it as "another DiD" unless the author leans harder into the **mechanism** (discretion vs. rules).
*   **Making it bigger:** To make this an AER heavyweight, the author needs to dig into the **compositional change** more deeply. Table 4 shows White hiring falls by 4.7%. Is this a "levelling down" where high-earning White workers exit these markets? If the gap narrows because the "best" White jobs disappear, the welfare implication changes entirely.

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

*   **Closest Neighbors:** Bennedsen et al. (2022, JF), Baker et al. (2023, AEJ: Applied), Cullen and Pakzad-Hurson (2024, Econometrica), and Kline, Rose, and Walters (2022, QJE).
*   **Positioning:** It builds on the gender transparency literature but should more aggressively "attack" or refine the search/matching literature. It needs to speak to **Information Asymmetry** (Bayer et al., 2018).
*   **Missing Literature:** It should connect to the **Monopsony** literature. If transparency reduces a firm's ability to price-discriminate across racial groups, it is a direct test of models of labor market power.
*   **Conversation:** It's having the right "policy" conversation but the wrong "theory" conversation. It needs to be a paper about **how prices are set**, not just whether a law worked.

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

*   **Setup:** Pay transparency is the new global regulatory trend, primarily sold as a fix for the gender gap.
*   **Tension:** Does forcing a "public anchor" actually stop racial discrimination in a world where employers still have final say over who they hire and what they pay within a range?
*   **Resolution:** Yes, the gap narrows by ~1%, but mostly in sectors where firms previously had "room to hide" (high dispersion).
*   **Implications:** Information policy can be a "race-blind" tool for "race-conscious" outcomes.

**Evaluation:** The arc is serviceable but lacks a "hook" regarding the White hiring decline. If the author frames the decline in White hiring as a cost of the policy (reduced flexibility leading to reduced hiring), the narrative becomes much more sophisticated and AER-worthy.

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

*   **Lead Fact:** "Posting a salary range on LinkedIn closes 1/15th of the racial wage gap overnight." 
*   **Reaction:** People lean in. That is a massive effect for a "low-cost" intervention.
*   **Follow-up:** "Wait, did firms just stop hiring expensive White guys to make the numbers look better?" (This is the critical question the paper must answer convincingly).

---

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

*   **Front-load the Mechanism:** The industry heterogeneity (Table 3) is actually more interesting than the headline DiD. Move the "Discretion" argument to the front.
*   **The Colorado Problem:** The paper is heavily reliant on one state (Colorado) for the "clean" result. This needs to be a "Case Study + Supporting Evidence" paper or it needs more years of data for the other states.
*   **Appendix:** Move the wild cluster bootstrap technicalities to the appendix; they distract from the story.

---

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The gap between this and an AER publication is **Ambition/Scope**. Currently, it feels like a very high-quality *AEJ: Policy* or *Journal of Labor Economics* paper. 

**The Single Most Impactful Advice:** Shift the focus from "Does this law work?" to "How does public price-anchoring change the bargaining game?" To do this, the author needs to explore the **White hiring decline** (compositional effects) with the same rigor as the earnings effect. If you can prove that transparency doesn't just "hide" the gap by changing who is hired, but actually changes the *pay* for the *same* types of people, you have an AER paper.
