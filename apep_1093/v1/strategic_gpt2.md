## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether laws requiring firms to post salary ranges reduce the Black-white earnings gap at the point of hire. That is a question economists should care about because these laws are spreading rapidly, they target employer wage-setting discretion directly, and they speak to a broader issue than pay transparency per se: whether changing the information structure of labor markets can compress racial inequality without race-specific enforcement.

The paper mostly does articulate this pitch in the first two paragraphs, and better than many papers do. The ingredients are there: a salient policy, an underexplored racial margin, and a mechanism tied to bargaining and wage posting. But the opening is still somewhat literature-driven and a bit too quick to move into “gender literature hasn’t done race yet,” which makes the paper sound like an extension rather than a first-order labor market question.

### The pitch the paper should have

“States are rapidly requiring employers to disclose pay ranges in job postings, on the theory that transparency limits arbitrary wage-setting. But the central economic question is broader than gender equity: when employers must reveal the price before bargaining begins, does that reduce racial disparities in wage offers to new hires? Using the recent staggered adoption of salary-posting mandates across U.S. states and administrative data on new-hire earnings by race, this paper shows that pay transparency modestly narrows the Black-white earnings gap at hiring, especially in industries where employers have the most discretion over pay.”

That version starts with the world, not the literature, and makes the reader understand immediately why the policy matters.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper’s contribution is to provide evidence that salary-range posting mandates modestly reduce the Black-white earnings gap among new hires, suggesting that pay transparency can compress racial disparities through the hiring channel.

### Evaluation

**Is the contribution clearly differentiated from the closest papers?**  
Only partially. The paper differentiates itself from the gender-focused pay transparency literature and from reporting mandates versus posting mandates. That is useful, but the differentiation is still too mechanical: race instead of gender, posting instead of reporting. The author needs to say more clearly what we learn about labor markets that those papers could not tell us. Right now the contribution risks sounding like “same question, new subgroup.”

**World question or literature gap?**  
The paper is halfway between the two, but it should lean much harder into the world question. The stronger version is not “the literature studies gender, I study race.” It is: “Do transparency rules limit employer discretion in ways that reduce racial inequality at the hiring stage?” That is a substantive question about price-setting, bargaining, and discrimination.

**Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?**  
Yes, but not crisply enough. Right now they might say: “It’s a DiD on pay transparency laws and racial hiring gaps.” That is not strong enough for AER positioning. You want them to say: “It shows that forcing firms to post wages changes the racial distribution of starting pay, which is direct evidence that opacity in wage offers is part of how racial inequality gets produced.”

**What would make the contribution bigger?**  
Several possibilities:

1. **Reframe the outcome as starting-pay inequality, not just ‘racial wage gap.’**  
   The distinctive thing here is the point of hire. That is sharper than the broad wage-gap framing.

2. **Lean harder into hiring-stage mechanisms.**  
   The current separation placebo is fine, but the larger story is about posted prices, bargaining, and wage-setting discretion. Anything that sharpens the “new-hire offer formation” angle would make the paper feel more fundamental.

3. **Connect more explicitly to monopsony / wage posting / bargaining models.**  
   The payoff would be bigger if the paper were framed as evidence that transparency changes firms’ wage-setting technology.

4. **Expand the comparison beyond race-vs-gender literatures.**  
   The more ambitious contribution is not “racial analog of the gender transparency literature”; it is “evidence on how public price revelation affects discriminatory wage-setting.”

5. **Show broader implications for labor market design.**  
   If the paper can persuade readers that salary posting affects who bargains, how firms screen, and how offers are distributed, then the contribution becomes a labor-market-organization paper, not just a policy evaluation.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

### Closest neighbors

The closest neighbors appear to be:

1. **Bennedsen et al. (2022)** on pay transparency and gender pay gaps in Denmark.  
2. **Baker et al. (2023)** on pay transparency / salary disclosure effects.  
3. **Cullen et al. (2024)** on equilibrium effects of pay transparency.  
4. **Duchini et al. (2024)** on UK gender pay transparency/reporting.  
5. On racial inequality and firm wage-setting: **Kline, Rose, and Walters (2022)** on systemic discrimination / firm contributions to racial inequality, and likely **Arnold et al. (2022)** or related work on measuring racial discrimination in labor markets.

Also nearby, though not foregrounded enough, are papers on:
- salary history bans,
- wage posting and bargaining,
- monopsony / recruiting frictions,
- information and search in labor markets.

### How should the paper position itself?

It should **build on** the pay-transparency literature, not attack it. Those papers established that transparency can affect gender gaps; this paper asks whether the same informational intervention changes racial inequality in starting pay and whether that tells us something about employer discretion at hiring.

Relative to racial inequality papers, it should **complement**, not claim to overturn. Those papers show that firms matter for racial wage inequality; this paper offers one concrete policy lever that may reduce that inequality.

### Too narrow or too broad?

Currently it is positioned a bit **too narrowly**, in the sense that it sounds like a niche policy extension within the pay-transparency literature. But it also sometimes gestures too broadly—toward anti-discrimination policy generally—without fully earning that breadth. The right positioning is narrower than “solution to racial wage inequality” but broader than “another state policy DiD.”

### What literature is it missing?

The paper seems under-engaged with at least four conversations:

1. **Wage posting / wage bargaining / salary history bans.**  
   Since the mechanism is anchoring and constrained discretion, those are natural neighbors.

2. **Monopsony and firm wage-setting.**  
   If transparency changes posted wages and narrows gaps, that fits squarely into firm wage-setting power.

3. **Search and information frictions.**  
   Salary ranges affect applicant information, outside options, and reservation behavior.

4. **Discrimination at hiring versus within-firm pay setting.**  
   The paper should more clearly say it speaks to the formation of starting pay, which is a distinct margin.

### Is it having the right conversation?

Not quite yet. The current conversation is: “pay transparency literature is about gender; I add race.” The more interesting conversation is: “what happens when labor markets become more price-transparent, and what does that reveal about the role of opacity in generating racial inequality?” That is the unexpected bridge that could give the paper more punch.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Pay transparency laws are spreading quickly. The dominant public and academic rationale emphasizes gender equity, while economists increasingly recognize that firms and employer discretion contribute importantly to wage inequality.

### Tension

If racial wage gaps partly arise from opaque wage-setting and bargaining at the hiring stage, then forcing firms to reveal salary ranges could reduce those gaps. But we do not know whether these mandates affect racial inequality at all, much less whether the effect operates specifically through new-hire wage setting.

### Resolution

The paper finds that salary-range posting mandates modestly reduce the Black-white earnings gap among new hires, with larger effects in Colorado and in industries where wage-setting discretion is plausibly greater. The lack of effect on separations is presented as consistent with the hiring channel.

### Implications

The implication is that a general transparency policy may reduce racial disparities without race-targeted enforcement, and more broadly that information structure matters for how firms set wages across groups.

### Evaluation

The paper **does have a narrative arc**, but it is only **serviceable**, not yet compelling. The problem is not lack of findings; it is that the narrative is still subordinate to the empirical design. The paper is organized like a competent applied micro paper, but the story is not yet sharp enough to feel like a major contribution.

At moments it reads like a collection of reasonable results:
- baseline estimate,
- Colorado-only,
- industry heterogeneity,
- separation placebo,
- hiring volume discussion.

What story should it be telling? This one:

**Opacity in wage offers gives employers room to produce unequal starting pay; salary-posting laws limit that room; the biggest effects show up exactly where discretionary wage-setting is most important; therefore transparency is not just a disclosure policy but a labor-market design intervention.**

That is the story that could unify the results.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at dinner?

“I’ve got evidence that forcing firms to post salary ranges modestly narrows the Black-white pay gap for new hires—and the effect is strongest in sectors where firms have the most discretion over pay.”

That is a decent opener. People would probably **lean in initially**, because the policy is current and the racial angle is underexplored.

### Would they keep leaning in?

Only if the speaker quickly made the broader point: this is evidence about **how wage opacity contributes to inequality at hiring**. If the pitch stays at “a 0.9 log-point effect in a state-policy DiD,” phones may come out.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Likely one of these:
- “Is this really about bargaining versus composition?”
- “Why race here if the literature has focused on gender?”
- “Is the effect economically meaningful or just statistically fragile?”
- “What does this imply for wage posting more broadly?”

The paper needs to prepare for that first and third question in the introduction, not only in the results.

### If the findings are modest, is that okay?

Yes, modest findings can still matter here, but only if the paper insists on why they matter. The author should not oversell the magnitude, but should argue that even a modest reduction in starting-pay inequality from a low-cost, general policy is substantively important—especially because starting pay has dynamic effects over careers and because the policy was not designed specifically around race.

Right now the null/modest-result framing is passable but not fully persuasive. The paper sometimes sounds defensive, translating the estimate into standardized effect sizes and “one-fifteenth of the mean gap,” which feels a little like trying to rescue a small coefficient. Better to own the scale: the effect is modest, but that is informative because this is a light-touch policy, early in implementation, and targeted at one margin of inequality.

   Since this is an editorial memo, not a referee report, my concern is narrative economy. The paper currently gives robustness more airtime than the substantive interpretation.

7. **Strengthen the conclusion.**  
   The conclusion currently summarizes. It should instead answer: What should economists now believe about transparency, wage-setting, and racial inequality that they did not believe before?

### Is the paper front-loaded with the good stuff?

Reasonably, yes. The introduction gives the main result quickly. That is good. But the “good stuff” is still presented as estimates rather than as a larger idea. The introduction needs a stronger conceptual spine.

### Are important results buried?

The industry heterogeneity is conceptually important and should be elevated. The hiring-volume discussion also matters because it touches the natural “what margin is moving?” question; that should be incorporated more explicitly into the main narrative rather than appearing as a sub-subsection after robustness.

### Is the conclusion adding value?

Not enough. It is competent but mostly reiterative. It should end on the bigger point: transparency alters the wage-setting environment ex ante, and that may be one reason a generic labor-market rule can have distributional effects across racial groups.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The main gap is **not primarily executional** in the editorial sense; it is **a framing-and-ambition problem**.

This paper is clearly written, topical, and respectable. But in its current form it reads like a strong field-journal paper: a timely policy evaluation with a new outcome margin. That is not yet an AER story.

### What is the gap?

**Mostly ambition/problem framing, with some novelty risk.**

- **Framing problem:** The paper is too often framed as “gender transparency literature, now race.” That makes it sound derivative.
- **Scope problem:** The current empirical object is narrow—new-hire earnings gap in selected industries over a short horizon. That may be enough for publication somewhere very good, but for AER the broader conceptual takeaway must carry more weight.
- **Novelty problem:** The policy is new and the race angle is fresh, but the underlying move—a staggered-adoption DiD on a hot labor policy—is now familiar. The paper needs to show why this case changes how we think about labor markets.
- **Ambition problem:** The paper is careful and safe. It needs a bolder claim about what salary posting reveals about employer discretion and starting-pay formation.

### The single most impactful piece of advice

**Stop selling the paper as “the race extension of pay-transparency studies” and instead sell it as evidence that wage opacity at the hiring stage is a mechanism generating racial inequality, with salary-posting laws as the test case.**

That one change would improve the title page, first two paragraphs, literature review, interpretation of heterogeneity, and conclusion. It would make the paper feel less incremental and more about a first-order economic question.
