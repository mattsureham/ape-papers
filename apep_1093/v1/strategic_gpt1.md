## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether laws requiring firms to post salary ranges reduce racial inequality in hiring outcomes. Using the staggered rollout of state pay-transparency mandates, it argues that forcing employers to reveal the wage range up front narrows the Black-White earnings gap among new hires, especially in sectors where employers have more wage-setting discretion.

A busy economist should care because this takes a policy currently framed almost entirely around gender equity and asks whether it also changes racial disparities at the point of hire. If true, that broadens the relevance of pay transparency from a niche labor-regulation issue to a more general question about how information disclosure reshapes bargaining power and discrimination in labor markets.

**Does the paper articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Mostly, but not optimally. The opening gets to the question fairly quickly, but it leads with Colorado controversy and then pivots to “the literature focuses on gender.” That is competent but still sounds like a literature-extension paper. The stronger version is to start from a world question: when wages are negotiated under asymmetric information, does forcing employers to disclose the price compress racial disparities in offers? That is a much better AER-style opening than “the gender literature has overlooked race.”

**What the first two paragraphs should say instead:**

> In many labor markets, wages for new hires are not posted prices but negotiated outcomes. That creates room for unequal information, unequal bargaining power, and potentially unequal treatment across groups. A new wave of U.S. state laws requiring employers to post salary ranges offers a natural test of whether making wages visible before bargaining changes racial inequality in hiring.
>
> This paper asks whether salary-posting mandates reduce the Black-White earnings gap among new hires. The question matters beyond pay transparency itself: it speaks to a broader issue in labor economics and discrimination research—how much of between-group wage inequality is sustained by employer discretion at the offer stage, and how much can be altered by a simple disclosure rule that constrains that discretion.

That would immediately elevate the paper from “another policy evaluation” to “a test of how posted prices affect discrimination and bargaining.”

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution in one sentence:**  
The paper provides early evidence that salary-range posting mandates modestly reduce the Black-White earnings gap among new hires, suggesting that transparency at the offer stage can compress racial disparities where employer wage discretion is high.

1. **Different policy margin:** public salary posting before bargaining, not ex post pay reporting or internal audits.
2. **Different outcome margin:** new-hire earnings, not incumbent wages or average firm pay gaps.
3. **Different conceptual target:** employer discretion in initial offers as a source of racial inequality.

That triad is stronger than “the literature studies gender; I study race.”

Right now it leans too much toward **filling a literature gap**. The phrase “the pay transparency literature's near-exclusive focus on gender” is fine as a sentence, but it should not be the engine of the paper. The engine should be a world question: **Do posted wages reduce inequality by limiting employer discretion in the market for new hires?**

That world framing is stronger and more durable.

They could probably say: “It’s a DiD paper showing pay transparency may reduce the Black-White new-hire wage gap.” That is understandable, but still sounds a bit like “another DiD paper about X.” The paper does not yet turn the result into a broader conceptual contribution.

### What would make this contribution bigger?
Several possibilities:

- **Broader inequality framing:** Not just race, but whether transparency compresses disparities more generally across groups or local labor markets. Even if race remains the headline, the framing should point to wage posting as a tool that changes the price-setting environment.

- **A stronger policy comparison:** If the paper could frame salary posting as an alternative to more compliance-heavy anti-discrimination tools, that would raise the stakes. Right now it hints at this but does not fully capitalize on it.

If I had to choose one: **make the paper about employer discretion at the point of hire, not about “race being missing from the transparency literature.”** That is the version with AER potential.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Before this paper, wage-setting at hiring is often opaque and negotiated, and a new class of laws requires firms to disclose salary ranges in job ads. Existing evidence has focused mainly on gender and on reporting regimes rather than on public posting mandates.

### Tension
We do not know whether transparency at the point of hire reduces racial inequality, or whether these laws are mostly symbolic, easy to evade, or relevant only for gender pay gaps. More fundamentally, we do not know whether employer discretion in initial offers is an important source of racial earnings disparities.

### Resolution
The paper finds that salary-range mandates modestly narrow the Black-White new-hire earnings gap, with larger effects in Colorado and in industries where wage-setting discretion appears greater.

### Implications
If that pattern is real, disclosure rules can reduce inequality not just by informing workers but by constraining employer discretion before bargaining begins. That would make salary-posting mandates a broader anti-inequality policy than currently appreciated.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
It has a **serviceable** arc, but not a fully compelling one. The pieces are present, but the story is somewhat diluted by:
- effect-size discussion that feels defensive,
- multiple framings competing with each other,
- an introduction that reads more like a competent applied micro paper than a must-read paper.

There is also a slight sense of “collection of results looking for a story”: pooled effect, Colorado-only effect, industry heterogeneity, separation placebo, hiring volume results. These are individually sensible, but the story connecting them should be sharper.

### What story should it be telling?
The story should be:

> Wage opacity gives employers room to tailor offers. Salary posting converts part of wage-setting from private bargaining to quasi-posted pricing. If racial disparities partly arise in that discretionary offer-setting margin, transparency should compress the racial gap specifically for new hires, and especially in sectors where discretion is greatest.

That story unifies the pooled effect, the industry heterogeneity, and the separation placebo. Right now the paper comes close to that, but it does not discipline the exposition tightly enough around it.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?
I would lead with:

**“When states require employers to post salary ranges, the Black-White earnings gap among new hires shrinks, with the strongest effects in high-discretion sectors and in Colorado, where the policy has had the longest time to bite.”**

That is the right dinner-party headline.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
Among labor economists and public economists, they would **lean in briefly**, but not yet for very long. The topic is timely and politically salient, and the hiring-stage angle is interesting. But the estimated effect is modest, and the current framing does not yet elevate it to a first-order conceptual result.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Probably one of these:
- “Is this really about bargaining and employer discretion, or just composition?”
- “Why race here when the transparency conversation is mostly about gender?”
- “Does this tell us something general about posted prices versus negotiated wages?”
- “Is Colorado doing all the work?”

The strongest follow-up is the third one. The paper should invite that question and answer it.

### If the findings are modest: is the modest result itself interesting?
Yes, but the paper needs to make the case better. A 0.9 log-point narrowing is not earth-shattering on its own. The reason it is interesting is not the magnitude per se; it is that:
1. the policy is simple and rapidly diffusing,
2. it acts at the hiring margin,
3. it may reveal something about the role of offer-stage discretion in racial inequality.

So the paper should not oversell the size. It should sell the **mechanism and margin**. Right now it spends too much energy translating the magnitude into standard deviations and fractions of the average gap. That reads as compensating for the fact that the effect is small. Better to say: **even a modest compression from a light-touch disclosure rule is informative about where inequality is generated.**

Be honest: in current form, this is **not yet an AER paper**. The main gap is not just style. It is that the paper currently reads like a solid, timely applied paper with one new angle, rather than a paper that changes how economists think about the relationship between wage transparency, bargaining, and inequality.

### What is the main problem?

Mostly a **framing and ambition problem**, with some **scope risk**.

- **Ambition problem:** It does not fully claim the bigger idea available in the data: salary posting is a test of whether reducing employer discretion at the offer stage reduces inequality.

I would not call it mainly a novelty problem. There is novelty here. But the novelty is not yet being translated into a big enough question.

### What is the gap between this and a paper that would excite the top 10 people in the field?
The top people would want to come away thinking:

**“This paper teaches us something general about how wage-setting institutions generate inequality.”**

Right now they are more likely to think:

**“Interesting extension of the pay transparency literature to racial gaps, with suggestive evidence.”**

That is a meaningful difference.

### Single most impactful piece of advice
**Reframe the paper around a first-order economic question—whether shifting new-hire wages from opaque negotiation toward posted-price disclosure constrains employer discretion and reduces racial inequality—rather than around being the first pay-transparency paper to study race.**

That one change would improve the introduction, the literature review, the narrative arc, and the perceived ambition all at once.
