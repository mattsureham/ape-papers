## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple and important question: when regulators create a way for already-regulated firms to escape costly environmental rules, do firms actually reorganize their behavior to take advantage of it? Using the 2018 withdrawal of the EPA’s “Once In, Always In” guidance under the Clean Air Act, the paper argues that firms did not respond at the single-pollutant threshold, but did bunch below the combined-pollutant threshold—suggesting that firms exploit escape hatches when compliance can be adjusted along a flexible, multi-margin dimension.

A busy economist should care because this is not just a paper about air pollution reporting. It is a paper about how the design of threshold-based regulation shapes strategic behavior: some thresholds are hard constraints, others are invitations to game the system.

### Does the paper articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Not quite. The current introduction is competent, but it opens with a generic conceptual definition (“a regulatory escape hatch is...”) rather than the sharp economic question. It takes too long to get to the reason anyone outside a narrow environmental/regulation audience should care. The big idea is broader than OIAI: **what happens when a durable regulatory status becomes reversible?** That is the pitch. The current first paragraphs read like a policy memo; they should read like a top-field-journal opening.

### The pitch the paper should have

Here is what the first two paragraphs should say instead:

> Many regulations hinge on thresholds, but one of the least understood margins is what happens when firms are suddenly allowed to move back across them. If a costly regulatory status becomes reversible, do firms actively manage their behavior to slip just below the line—or are such “escape hatches” largely irrelevant in practice?
>
> This paper studies that question using the EPA’s 2018 repeal of the Clean Air Act’s “Once In, Always In” policy, which for more than two decades had locked facilities permanently into major-source status once they crossed hazardous air pollutant thresholds. The repeal created, overnight, a valuable option to reclassify as a less-regulated source by reducing emissions below statutory cutoffs. I show that firms responded selectively: there is no detectable bunching at the 10-ton single-pollutant threshold, but significant bunching below the 25-ton combined-pollutant threshold. The central implication is that regulatory reversibility matters, but only when the threshold can be managed on flexible margins.

That would immediately tell the reader the question, the shock, the finding, and the general lesson.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### Contribution in one sentence

The paper claims to show that making major-source status under the Clean Air Act reversible induced strategic emission bunching only at the combined-HAP threshold, implying that firms exploit regulatory escape hatches when adjustment can be spread across multiple margins.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3-4 papers?

Only partially. The paper repeatedly says “first application” or “extends the literature,” but the differentiation is still mostly methodological or institutional rather than conceptual. The introduction risks sounding like: “Here is another bunching paper, now in environmental regulation.” That is not enough for the AER.

What needs to be clearer is how this differs from:
1. standard bunching-at-threshold papers,
2. environmental compliance papers about firms reducing emissions to avoid regulation,
3. papers about policy uncertainty or regulatory reversals.

Right now the distinction is: “this threshold used to be inescapable, and then became escapable.” That is actually interesting, but it needs to be elevated from a detail of the setting into the conceptual contribution.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or filling a gap in a LITERATURE?

Too much of the current framing is “filling a gap in the literature.” The stronger framing is about the world:

- When a regulator turns a permanent compliance regime into a reversible one, firms respond differently depending on the manipulability of the threshold.
- The structure of a threshold matters for behavior as much as the level of the threshold.
- Reversibility creates option value, and option value changes behavior.

That is a world question. The paper should lean much harder into it.

### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain what's new?

At present, maybe, but not cleanly. The colleague summary would probably be: “It’s a bunching paper on Clean Air Act thresholds around the OIAI reversal.” That is not fatal, but it is not what you want for an AER submission.

The better version is: “It studies whether firms exploit newly created regulatory exit options, and shows they do so only when the threshold is technologically flexible.” That sounds like a real idea, not just an application.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Specific ways to make it bigger:

1. **Tie the result to reversibility and option value much more directly.**  
   Right now the paper says “escape hatch” often, but it does not fully cash out the economics of reversibility. This could be the centerpiece.

2. **Make the threshold-comparative design the contribution.**  
   The most interesting feature is not just pre/post around one threshold, but the comparison across two different statutory thresholds with different adjustment technologies. That comparison is what gives the paper a general lesson.

3. **Show broader implications beyond emissions density.**  
   The paper wants to speak to regulatory design, but the outcomes are still confined to bunching in emissions. To feel bigger, it would help to connect more directly to reclassification, compliance burdens, plant operations, or downstream environmental exposure. Even one credible downstream outcome would materially enlarge the paper’s scope.

4. **Sharpen the mechanism.**  
   The main story is “distributed adjustment across multiple pollutants is easier than concentrated adjustment of one pollutant.” That is plausible, but currently mostly asserted. For strategic positioning, the paper needs to look like it is uncovering a general mechanism of threshold manipulation, not just reporting asymmetric estimates.

5. **Frame it as a design paper, not just a response paper.**  
   “Which kinds of regulatory thresholds are gameable?” is bigger than “What happened after Pruitt changed OIAI?”

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Regulation often classifies firms using thresholds. Under OIAI, crossing the hazardous-emissions threshold imposed a permanent major-source status, so there was little reason for already-classified firms to move back below the line.

### Tension

Then the EPA abruptly removed the permanence of that status. This created a valuable exit option. The puzzle is whether firms would actually exploit it—and whether the response depends on the type of threshold.

### Resolution

The paper finds no meaningful response at the single-HAP threshold, but significant bunching below the combined-HAP threshold after the policy change.

### Implications

Regulatory escape hatches matter, but not uniformly. Threshold design shapes manipulability: multi-dimensional aggregate thresholds invite strategic adjustment more than single-dimension thresholds. That matters for environmental policy and for threshold-based regulation generally.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the ingredients of a clear arc, but the story is still weaker than the results. The manuscript often reads like a collection of empirical exercises—bunching estimate, DiD corroboration, heterogeneity, placebo tests—rather than a tightly organized story driving toward one big insight.

The story it should be telling is:

1. **Permanent classification suppresses threshold management.**
2. **Reversibility creates option value.**
3. **Firms use the option only when the threshold is technologically pliable.**
4. **Therefore the design of thresholds determines whether reversibility induces gaming.**

That is a genuine narrative. Right now, the paper states versions of this, but it does not structure the introduction and results around it with enough discipline.

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?

“I’ve got a paper showing that when the EPA suddenly let plants escape major-source regulation by moving back below hazardous-emissions thresholds, firms only piled up below the threshold that let them cut a little bit of several pollutants—not the one that required cutting a lot of one pollutant.”

That is the fact.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

Some would lean in—especially economists interested in public finance, regulation, or environmental policy—because the cross-threshold asymmetry is genuinely interesting. But a lot depends on how it is presented.

If presented as “a bunching study around OIAI,” phones come out.  
If presented as “evidence on when reversible regulatory thresholds trigger gaming,” people lean in.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

The immediate follow-up is: **Is this real abatement or just reporting/manipulation?**  
The second is: **How general is this beyond this one EPA episode?**

The paper knows this and gestures at it, but those are precisely the questions it needs to own narratively.

### If the findings are modest/null: is the null interesting?

The 10-ton null is potentially interesting, but only because it is paired with the 25-ton positive result. On its own, a null at one threshold would feel like a failed bunching exercise. In comparison with the positive 25-ton result, however, it becomes informative: not all regulatory escape hatches are equally exploitable.

That comparative asymmetry is doing almost all of the heavy lifting. The paper should recognize that and put it front and center.
