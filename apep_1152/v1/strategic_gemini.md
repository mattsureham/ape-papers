## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
This paper asks whether the most ambitious state-level climate policies in the U.S.—100% Clean Energy Standards (CES)—actually accelerate the retirement of coal-fired power plants. Using generator-level data and modern staggered DiD estimators, the author finds that the answer is "no": the apparent effectiveness of these policies in standard models is an artifact of "composition bias," where states adopting these laws happened to own older, smaller, and already-dying coal fleets.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this pitch with exceptional clarity. The first two paragraphs of the introduction perfectly set the stage by contrasting the "stranded signal" hypothesis with the "market forces" hypothesis and then immediately delivering the "composition illusion" punchline. No rewrite is necessary; the author has a keen sense of the "hook."

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**One-sentence contribution:** The paper demonstrates that 100% Clean Energy Standards have had no detectable effect on coal retirements to date, proving that previous "successes" attributed to these policies are likely statistical illusions driven by pre-existing generator characteristics.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It is clearly differentiated from Greenstone and Nath (2020) and others by focusing specifically on the *100%* mandate (the "endgame" policy) rather than incremental RPS or MATS regulations.
*   **Framing:** It is framed as a question about the **WORLD** (Do these laws work?) while using a **LITERATURE** gap (the failure of TWFE in staggered settings) as the tool to answer it.
*   **Clarity:** A smart economist would immediately grasp the "illusion"—it is the "another DiD paper" trap, but with a high-stakes policy payoff.
*   **Bigger Contribution:** To make this even bigger, the paper could explore **leakage**. If CES doesn't retire coal, does it at least prevent *new* gas investment? Or does it simply shift the political capital away from carbon taxes?

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
The paper sits at the intersection of Environmental Economics and Applied Econometrics.

*   **Neighbors:** Greenstone & Nath (2020) on RPS; Callaway & Sant’Anna (2021) on DiD; Cullen & Mansur (2017) on gas vs. coal; de Chaisemartin & D’Haultfœuille (2020).
*   **Positioning:** It **attacks** the "naive" consensus that state mandates are the primary drivers of the energy transition, and **builds on** the "new econometrics" to prove why that consensus was wrong.
*   **Niche vs. Broad:** It is positioned broadly enough for any applied microeconomist to enjoy, but specific enough to be a "must-read" for energy policy.
*   **Unaware of?** It could speak more to the **Industrial Organization** literature on "exit" (e.g., Whinston). Is the lack of effect due to the long "tail" of regulatory recovery of capital?

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC
*   **Setup:** A wave of "ambitious" 100% clean energy laws sweeps the U.S.
*   **Tension:** Simple models say they work great; skeptics say market forces (gas/renewables) were doing the job anyway. Who is right?
*   **Resolution:** Neither—or rather, the "success" is a ghost in the machine. The laws were passed in states where coal was already on its deathbed.
*   **Implications:** Policymakers are taking credit for what the market was already doing. Future climate policy needs "teeth," not just targets.

**Evaluation:** The narrative arc is very strong. It follows a "detective story" structure (the "illusion" and its "unmasking") which is highly effective for the AER.

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
At a dinner party, you lead with: *"We found that those 100% Clean Energy mandates in California and New York didn't actually retire a single extra coal plant—they just targeted plants that were already 40 years old and tiny."*

*   **Reaction:** People lean in. It’s counter-intuitive and politically provocative.
*   **Follow-up:** *"So are the laws useless, or just symbolic?"* This leads directly into the paper's conclusion about political signaling.
*   **The Null:** This is a "Goldilocks" null result. It isn't just "we found nothing"; it's "we found why everyone else mistakenly thought they found something."

---

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
*   **Front-loading:** The paper is well front-loaded. Table 2 is the "money" table and appears early.
*   **Appendix:** Table 6 (Distributional Effects) feels like it's trying to do too much with too little power. Moving the subgroup analysis by MW to the appendix is a good move.
*   **Mechanism:** I would love to see a brief section (or more emphasis in the text) on the **Political Economy**—did states wait to pass these laws until their coal interest groups were sufficiently weak? This would strengthen the "composition" argument.

---

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The paper is currently a very high-quality *Applied Economics Journal* piece. To push it into the **AER**, it needs more **Ambition**.

**The Gap:** Currently, it's a "debunking" paper. To be a "defining" paper, it needs to answer: *If the mandates don't retire coal, what do they do?* Do they increase retail electricity prices (Greenstone's angle)? Do they spur innovation in storage? 

**Single Biggest Advice:** Expand the "So What?" section into a rigorous analysis of **Selection into Treatment**. Use the generator characteristics (age/size) to predict *which* states adopted CES. If you can show that state legislatures only "get brave" when the economic cost of the mandate reaches near-zero, you move from a narrow DiD paper to a major statement on the Political Economy of the Energy Transition.
