## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether aggressive state enforcement of local social-housing mandates fuels support for the far right. Using French municipalities sanctioned under the SRU law, it argues that when the central state overrides local housing autonomy, there is no ensuing electoral backlash toward the FN/RN—and perhaps even a slight decline in far-right support.

Why should a busy economist care? Because many economists and policymakers worry that redistribution, integration, and housing-supply mandates provoke populist backlash; if that fear is overstated, it matters for the political feasibility of state-led housing policy.

**Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Not quite. The current introduction is competent, but it opens with “wealthy French communes resisted social housing” and quickly drops into two literatures. It does not quite crystallize the broader stakes soon enough: the paper is not really about one French administrative device; it is about whether coercive integration policy generates populist punishment. That needs to be the headline immediately.

**What the first two paragraphs should say instead:**

> Governments often avoid housing integration and social-mixing policies because they fear political backlash: when the state compels affluent localities to accept low-income housing, voters may punish that intervention by turning to anti-establishment or far-right parties. This concern is central to debates over housing supply, segregation, and the political limits of redistribution, yet we have little causal evidence on whether coercive housing mandates actually produce such backlash.
>
> This paper studies that question in France, where the SRU law allows the central state to sanction municipalities that fail to meet social-housing quotas, escalating in the most severe cases to a *carence* declaration that raises penalties and strips local officials of some planning authority. Comparing sanctioned and unsanctioned noncompliant municipalities across national elections, I find no evidence that these sanctions increase FN/RN vote share. The core message is simple: one of Europe’s clearest cases of top-down housing enforcement does not appear to feed far-right mobilization.

That is the paper’s strongest pitch. It elevates the question from “a DiD on French communes” to “do coercive integration policies trigger populist backlash?”

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:**  
The paper provides evidence from French housing-mandate enforcement that coercive state intervention in local housing policy does not increase far-right voting, challenging the common presumption that integration-oriented housing policy politically backfires.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?
Only partially. The paper gestures at immigration-voting, housing-supply, and fiscal federalism literatures, but the differentiation is still fuzzy. A reader may come away with: “This is another paper on populist voting and local demographic anxiety, except with a housing-policy treatment.” That is not enough. The paper needs sharper contrast on what exactly prior papers studied and what this paper uniquely isolates.

The differentiator should be something like:

- Prior work studies **immigration flows**, **refugee placement**, or **economic shocks**.
- This paper studies **the political effect of the state threatening demographic change through housing policy before—or even without—large realized inflows**.
- So the paper is about **policy-induced anticipation and state coercion**, not simply exposure to migrants or change in composition.

That distinction is potentially interesting. Right now it is not pressed hard enough.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a gap in a literature?
It is mixed, but too often sounds like a gap-filling exercise: “no study has examined whether…” That is weaker. The stronger version is a world question:

- **World question:** When governments force localities to accept socially integrative housing, does that create a political opening for the far right?
- **Not:** The literature has not yet studied carence declarations in France.

The former is AER-style; the latter is field-journal style.

### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what's new?
Not cleanly enough. Right now the likely summary is: “It’s a DiD paper on French social housing sanctions and FN vote share, with a null.” That is not fatal, but it is not memorable.

The introduction needs to equip the reader to say instead:

> “It isolates the political effect of coercive state enforcement of local integration policy, and finds that even a salient sovereignty shock doesn’t increase far-right voting.”

That is a much better novelty statement.

### What would make this contribution bigger?
Specific possibilities:

1. **Better mechanism framing around anticipation vs. realization.**  
   The paper hints at this but does not own it. The big question is whether **the threat or imposition of social mixing** triggers backlash even before substantial population change occurs.

2. **More direct political outcomes beyond presidential vote share.**  
   If available, municipal elections, turnout, protest voting, abstention, or local referendum/protest behavior would make the contribution feel more politically complete. Right now one national electoral outcome is narrow.

3. **A stronger test of salience.**  
   The paper speculates that sanctions may not be salient. If it could show where declarations were publicly salient—media coverage, mayoral conflict, local campaigning—and still finds no FN/RN response, that would enlarge the claim.

4. **Actual housing construction / realized demographic change.**  
   The current paper is about sanctioning, not consequences. The contribution would be much bigger if it linked sanction -> construction/composition -> politics, or showed explicitly that coercion without yet-realized demographic change does not mobilize the far right.

5. **Comparative framing.**  
   A direct comparison to contexts where exposure shocks *do* raise far-right voting would help. The paper’s punchline could become: “Actual inflows matter politically; top-down housing enforcement by itself does not.”

The paper’s closest neighbors seem to be:

On the housing side, it is less obvious the current cited papers are truly the closest conceptual neighbors. Glaeser/Diamond/Combes are relevant background, but they are not really the conversation this paper is joining. This is not a housing-supply elasticity paper. It is a political economy paper about backlash to local integration policy.

### How should the paper position itself relative to those neighbors?
**Build on and distinguish**, not attack.

The right posture is:

- The immigration-backlash literature shows that **demographic exposure can raise far-right support** in some settings.
- This paper asks whether **state enforcement of socially integrative housing policy** produces a similar reaction.
- It finds that the politics of **mandated integration** differ from the politics of **migration inflows**.

That is a useful extension—not a frontal critique.

### Is the paper currently positioned too narrowly or too broadly?
Paradoxically, both.

- **Too narrowly** in institutional detail: a lot of ink on the SRU law and *carence* process.
- **Too broadly** in literature invocation: it gestures at multiple literatures without convincing the reader which conversation matters most.

The paper should narrow to one main conversation: **political economy of backlash to integration policy**. Housing-supply and fiscal-federalism literatures can be secondary.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?
It likely needs stronger engagement with:

- **Policy feedback / political reactions to redistribution and integration policy**
- **Place-based political economy of NIMBYism and exclusion**
- **State capacity / central-local conflict**
- Possibly **contact vs. threat** literatures in political behavior, since the paper’s interpretation leans that way

The current fiscal federalism citation feels too thin and generic. If the paper wants to claim something about central override of local sovereignty, it should speak more directly to political economy work on decentralization, local autonomy, and central mandates—not just cite one public-finance reference.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?
Not yet. The highest-impact framing is not “housing supply meets populism.” It is:

> **When does coercive state intervention to reduce local exclusion trigger populist backlash?**

That connects housing, redistribution, federalism, and right-wing politics in a way that could interest a broader AER audience.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
There is widespread concern that forcing affluent or resistant localities to accept more social housing will trigger political backlash, especially toward anti-immigrant or far-right parties.

### Tension
We know that actual demographic change and migration shocks can move voting. But it is unclear whether **government enforcement of integration policy itself**—especially when it overrides local autonomy—produces the same backlash.

### Resolution
In French municipalities sanctioned under the SRU regime, the paper finds no increase in far-right vote share following *carence* declarations.

### Implications
The political costs of coercive housing integration may be lower than policymakers fear. More broadly, not every sovereignty shock or integration-oriented intervention translates into populist mobilization.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
It has the bones of one, but not a fully disciplined arc. At times it feels like:

- institutional description,
- then a standard empirical setup,
- then a null result,
- then some speculative interpretation.

The paper is not exactly a “collection of results looking for a story,” but it is still too close to that. The story should be sharper and more singular:

> **The paper is about a widely feared political tradeoff—social integration policy versus populist backlash—and shows that in an important setting the tradeoff does not materialize.**

Everything should serve that.

At present, some details muddy the arc:
- too much emphasis on the administrative treatment itself,
- too much introduction real estate devoted to generic literatures,
- not enough emphasis on why a null here would revise beliefs.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?
“France’s central government can punish municipalities that refuse to build social housing and even partially take over permitting authority—and that doesn’t seem to boost far-right voting.”

That is a pretty good opening fact. It has institutional bite and broader resonance.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
Initially lean in. The setting is vivid. But the risk is that once they hear “and the result is basically null,” attention may fade unless the presenter immediately explains why the null is genuinely informative.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Probably one of these:
- “Is the sanction actually salient to voters?”
- “Does this affect municipal rather than presidential politics?”
- “Does actual housing construction later produce backlash even if the sanction itself does not?”
- “Is this really about coercive policy or just about affluent suburbs with stable political equilibria?”

Those are exactly the questions the paper should preempt in its framing.

### Is the null result itself interesting?
Potentially yes—but the paper does not yet fully earn that interest. A null is interesting when:
1. there is a strong prior that effects should exist,
2. the treatment is politically meaningful,
3. the null rules out an important fear or theory.

This paper has (1) and (2) available, but it needs to strengthen (3). The introduction should make clear that policymakers routinely invoke backlash fears to resist integration mandates. Then the null becomes informative rather than anticlimactic.

Right now the paper sometimes sounds like “we expected backlash, found none.” That can feel like a failed experiment unless the author more forcefully argues:

> “This setting gives us a hard test of the backlash hypothesis. If coercive local integration policy were going to feed the far right, this is where one would expect it. It does not.”

That would make the null worth publishing.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

Bluntly: in current form, this is **not yet an AER paper**. The main gap is not just polish; it is that the paper feels like a competent, narrow design attached to a modest null, rather than a field-defining result.

**Primarily a framing and ambition problem, with some scope issues.**

- **Ambition problem:** It settles too quickly for “null backlash in one French setting.”
- **Scope problem:** One electoral outcome, one intervention stage, and mostly speculative mechanisms make the contribution feel bounded.

It is **not mainly an identification-story problem** for purposes of this memo. The referees can sort that out. The strategic issue is that the paper does not yet persuade me that the answer changes how the top people in political economy, urban economics, or public economics think about the world.

### What would excite the top 10 people in this field?
A version of the paper that says something like:

> “A central fear in modern democracies is that policies forcing local integration—whether through housing, refugees, or redistribution—inevitably generate far-right backlash. That would be a bigger idea.

To get there, the paper likely needs one of two paths:

1. **Deepen the conceptual claim**

**Reframe the paper around the broader political economy question—whether coercive integration policy itself generates populist backlash—and make the null result informative by showing why this setting is a hard, consequential test of that hypothesis, not just a French housing case study.**
