## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple, important question: when a place-based subsidy program phases regions out after they cross an income threshold, do those regions keep converging, or does growth stall once the money tap is turned down? Using the EU’s 75% eligibility cutoff for major structural funds, the paper argues that regions just above the threshold subsequently underperform nearby regions just below it, suggesting that convergence may depend on continued transfers rather than becoming self-sustaining.

A busy economist should care because this is not just about EU regional policy. It is about the dynamic design of place-based policy everywhere: do subsidies create durable productivity gains, or merely support activity that fades when support is withdrawn?

**Does the paper articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Not quite. The current opening is decent, but it gets pulled too quickly into institutional detail and design. The first two paragraphs should do less “here is my RDD” and more “here is the big question about the world.” Right now the introduction reads like a well-executed evaluation paper; it does not yet announce itself as a paper about the sustainability of place-based development.

### The pitch the paper should have

“Place-based subsidies are often justified as temporary pushes that help lagging regions become self-sustaining. But do they actually create durable convergence, or do gains fade once transfers are withdrawn? This paper studies that question using the European Union’s most important regional policy rule: regions that cross 75% of average EU income lose access to the most generous tier of structural funding.

I compare regions just above and below that cutoff at the 2014–2020 reclassification. The evidence suggests that regions that ‘graduate’ out of full eligibility subsequently see slower convergence, with patterns consistent with a gradual unwinding of subsidy-supported manufacturing activity. The broader implication is that the success of place-based policy cannot be judged only by what happens while subsidies are in place; the key test is what happens after they start to disappear.”

That is the paper’s story. The current version has that story in it, but it is buried under methods and caveats too early.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:**  
The paper reframes the EU structural funds literature from the effects of receiving subsidies to the effects of losing them, asking whether place-based transfers generate durable regional convergence or convergence that depends on continued support.

This is the right contribution. It is potentially interesting. But the paper does not yet differentiate it sharply enough from the existing threshold-based cohesion-policy literature.

### Is it clearly differentiated from the closest papers?
Only partially. The paper repeatedly says prior work studies the positive effects of receiving funds, while this paper studies withdrawal. That is directionally right, but still sounds like a small variant unless the author makes crystal clear why the withdrawal margin is conceptually distinct.

The key distinction should be:

- **Receipt answers:** can transfers raise outcomes while they are flowing?
- **Withdrawal answers:** were those gains durable enough to survive partial termination?

That is a real question about the world, not just a new specification.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or filling a gap in a LITERATURE?
It is mixed. The paper often slides into “previous papers studied X, I study Y,” which is weaker. The stronger framing is the world question:

> Are place-based development gains self-sustaining once subsidies are scaled back?

That framing is broader, more important, and more memorable.

### Could a smart economist explain what is new after reading the introduction?
At present, maybe—but not confidently. They would probably say: “It’s an RDD on the EU 75% threshold, but about losing eligibility rather than gaining it.” That is not enough. You want them to say: “It asks whether place-based policy creates durable convergence or subsidy dependence, using graduation out of EU aid as the test.”

### What would make the contribution bigger?
Several possibilities:

1. **Center the paper on persistence rather than withdrawal per se.**  
   “Do place-based policies survive graduation?” is bigger than “What happens at the 75% threshold?”

2. **Strengthen the outcome concept.**  
   GDP per capita relative to the EU average is a natural policy metric, but still a bit narrow and potentially noisy. A bigger paper would show whether the post-graduation slowdown reflects:
   - weaker investment,
   - weaker productivity,
   - lower business formation,
   - lower tradable-sector performance,
   - outmigration or demographic adjustments,
   - weaker private capital crowd-in after public support falls.

3. **Make the mechanism less generic.**  
   Right now the manufacturing channel feels plausible but thin. A stronger version would distinguish among:
   - public-capital interruption,
   - subsidy-dependent firms/sectors,
   - institutional capacity built around EU-funded projects,
   - compositional reallocation rather than pure output loss.

4. **Clarify the treatment contrast.**  
   The paper itself admits this is not clean “subsidy stops,” but “reduced eligibility with a transition cushion.” That means the title and framing currently overclaim. Either sharpen the first stage or reframe the contribution around **graduation from the most generous tier**, not cessation.

My blunt view: the current paper has an interesting idea, but the contribution still reads too much like “another EU-threshold paper with a new sign.”

### Is it positioned too narrowly or too broadly?
Right now it is **too narrowly European-institutional in the body, but too broadly ambitious in the claims.**

The paper knows a lot about NUTS2 categories and programming periods, but the big takeaway is not carried strongly enough. At the same time, the title and some prose imply something like “the subsidy stops,” which is broader and cleaner than the actual treatment supports.

### What literature does it seem unaware of?
Two missing conversations:

1. **Program persistence / fade-out / dynamic treatment effects**  
   The paper should connect more explicitly to literatures asking whether policy effects persist after treatment ends or phases down—not just place-based policy narrowly. Economists care about fade-out in education, development, labor market policy, industrial policy, and regional policy.

2. **Aid dependence / transfer dependence / soft budget constraints**  
   The strongest conceptual analogy may be to settings where transfers sustain spending or activity but fail to induce self-sustaining adjustment. That could broaden the audience beyond regional economics.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?
Almost, but not quite. The paper thinks it is mainly in the cohesion-policy conversation. For AER purposes, it needs to be in the larger conversation about **the dynamic design of place-based policy and the meaning of policy success**.

Unexpected but useful connection: this paper could also speak to **optimal phase-out design**. Economists designing subsidies, means-tested transfers, or regional aid all care whether sharp graduation rules destroy the very progress they reward.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Governments spend enormous sums on place-based policy to help lagging regions catch up. Most evaluations ask whether subsidized regions grow while receiving support.

### Tension
But that is not the central policy question. If the goal is durable convergence, what matters is whether gains persist after aid is reduced. A program can look successful during treatment and still fail its deeper objective if graduation causes stagnation or reversal.

### Resolution
The paper presents evidence that regions crossing above the EU’s 75% threshold and losing the most generous tier of aid subsequently perform worse than nearby regions that remain below, with a gradual post-reclassification divergence and suggestive evidence of manufacturing contraction.

### Implications
Temporary place-based aid may not produce self-sustaining growth, or at least not within the time horizon and design used here. That matters for how we judge past program success and how we design phase-outs in future regional policies.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
It has the ingredients, but the arc is not fully controlled. Too often it feels like:

- threshold rule,
- data,
- RD validation,
- main result,
- event study,
- mechanism,
- many caveats.

That is competent, but not memorable.

The story it should be telling is:

> “The real test of place-based policy is not whether subsidized regions grow while money arrives, but whether they keep growing after graduation. I use the EU’s graduation rule to test that. The answer appears to be no—or at least not reliably—suggesting that convergence may have been policy-dependent.”

That is a story. The current paper too often sounds like a collection of reasonable exercises around a threshold.

A major narrative problem: the paper’s own appendices undercut the main drama. In particular, once the reader learns that:
- the main estimate is imprecise,
- the EU-only sample roughly halves the effect,
- the first stage is not significant,
- donut results are unstable,

the “subsidy withdrawal stalls convergence” message feels less like resolution and more like a tentative suggestion. That is fine if honestly framed, but then the paper must pivot from “we show” to “we provide suggestive evidence on a highly important margin.”

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at dinner?
“Its key claim is that when EU regions ‘graduate’ out of the most generous structural funds, their convergence appears to slow rather than continue.”

That is the right dinner-party fact.

### Would people lean in?
Some would. Regional, public, and development economists would lean in immediately. General-interest economists might lean in for 30 seconds and then ask: “Is that because the policy never created durable growth, or because the treatment wasn’t really sharp enough to tell?”

That is the real issue.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Almost certainly:

- “Is the funding drop actually discrete enough to interpret this as subsidy withdrawal?”
- Then: “Is this about growth dependence, or just mean reversion / changing composition / Eastern Europe maturing?”

You asked not to referee identification, so I won’t dwell on that. But strategically, the paper needs to anticipate the top-line intellectual follow-up: **what exactly does graduation reveal about the persistence of policy effects?**

### If findings are modest or null, is that okay?
Yes—if framed correctly. The paper’s evidence is suggestive, not definitive. That can still be publishable at a high level if the question is big enough and the paper is disciplined about what it shows.

But the current draft wants to have it both ways:
- It repeatedly emphasizes economic magnitude and policy stakes.
- Yet the actual evidence is noisy and some auxiliary pieces dilute the headline.

The author should embrace the paper’s actual comparative advantage: **it opens an important question and provides structured evidence that the answer may be more pessimistic than the “cohesion works” literature implies.**

That is more credible than trying to sell this as a clean, decisive finding.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

At present, this is **not yet an AER paper**. The main gap is a combination of **framing problem** and **ambition problem**, with some **novelty risk**.

### Framing problem
The paper’s true question is bigger than its current presentation. It should be a paper about **durability of place-based policy**, not a paper about “one more threshold in EU regional funds.”

### Ambition problem
The paper is too satisfied with one main outcome and one mechanism hint. For AER, the reader wants a broader answer to the persistence question. If the story is that graduation reveals subsidy dependence, show more of the anatomy of that dependence.

### Novelty problem
The design is adjacent to a well-trodden literature. “We use the same threshold but ask about losing treatment” is a good start, not a finish. The paper must make the reader feel that the withdrawal margin overturns how we interpret the whole cohesion-policy literature.

### Scope problem
The paper likely needs either:
- richer outcomes/mechanisms, or
- a much stronger conceptual reframing around graduation and persistence.

My view: the second route is more realistic than trying to brute-force scope with many more tables.

## Single most impactful advice

**Reframe the paper around one big question—whether place-based policy creates self-sustaining convergence after regions graduate from aid—and organize every section around that durability question rather than around the 75% RDD itself.**

That one change would improve the introduction, literature review, narrative, and conclusion all at once. It would also force the author to be more honest that the paper provides suggestive evidence on persistence, rather than a definitive estimate of “what happens when the subsidy stops.”
