---

## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks what forces are powerful enough to break "agricultural inertia"—the tendency of workers to remain in low-productivity farming despite massive industrial wage premiums. By exploiting a unique WWI draft exemption for non-citizens, the author shows that forced occupational disruption (conscription) acted as a "push" mechanism that permanently accelerated structural transformation, particularly for Black men and those in high-inertia agricultural counties.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this pitch very clearly. The first two paragraphs effectively bridge the gap between a specific historical event (the 1917 Selective Service Act) and a fundamental question in development and labor economics (why surplus labor doesn't flow smoothly to higher-productivity sectors). The author correctly identifies that the "why" of structural transformation is as important as the "how much."

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:** The paper identifies forced occupational disruption as a causal catalyst for structural transformation, demonstrating that the WWI draft broke labor market inertia for men trapped in low-productivity agricultural equilibria.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** The paper differentiates itself well by contrasting its findings with the Vietnam-era literature (Angrist 1990). While the latter found military service reduced earnings, this paper argues the effect is context-dependent: in a high-inertia, subsistence-to-industry transition, disruption is a "bridge" rather than a "detour."
*   **Framing:** It is framed as answering a question about the **WORLD** (what breaks inertia?). This is much stronger than merely "filling a gap" in WWI history.
*   **AER Quality:** A smart economist would recognize this as more than "another DiD." It’s a paper about the *mechanics of labor reallocation*.
*   **Bigger Contribution:** To make it even larger, the author could explore the **local general equilibrium effects**. If these men left the farms, did the wages of those who stayed behind rise? Did it trigger mechanization on those specific farms? Moving from individual-level effects to market-level consequences would cement its AER status.

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

*   **Closest Neighbors:** Angrist (1990) on military service; Caselli & Coleman (2001) on structural transformation; Collins & Wanamaker (2014) on the Great Migration; and Bryan et al. (2014) on migration frictions.
*   **Positioning:** The paper should continue to "synthesize" these. It acts as a bridge between the **Labor** (returns to service) and **Development/History** (structural change) literatures. 
*   **Conversation:** It is having the right conversation. By linking the WWI draft to modern development theory (Lagakos et al. 2018), it makes a 110-year-old event feel urgent.
*   **Unexpected Connection:** It could benefit from speaking more to the **Economic Geography** literature. The finding that transformation happened *without* inter-county migration is a "dinner party fact" that challenges the standard "migration = transformation" narrative.

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

*   **Setup:** 1910 America—huge wage gaps exist, yet 40% of men stay on the farm. 
*   **Tension:** Why doesn't the Lewis model work? Why is inertia so sticky? Is it preferences or barriers?
*   **Resolution:** The 1917 draft provided an exogenous "push" that bypassed these barriers.
*   **Implications:** Large-scale "shocks" can be welfare-enhancing if they overcome massive frictions; development policy might need more "pushes" than "pulls."

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong. It isn't just a collection of coefficients; it is a coherent story about the fragility of low-productivity equilibria.

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

*   **The Fact:** "The WWI draft didn't just win the war; it forcibly moved 1.6% of the rural workforce into the 20th-century industrial economy, and the effect was largest for Black men."
*   **Reaction:** People will lean in. The "inertia break" is a catchy, intuitive concept. 
*   **Follow-up:** "If the draft didn't cause people to move counties, did they just commute further? Or did factories move to the people because the labor was now 'unlocked'?"

---

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

*   **The RD Section:** The paper spends considerable time on the age-based RD (Section 5.3 and 5.4), only to dismiss it as confounded. While transparent, it risks boring the reader. Move the bulk of the RD robustness to an appendix and keep the "why DiD is better" discussion brief.
*   **Front-loading:** The heterogeneity by race and county (Table 3) is actually more interesting than the baseline DiD. Move some of that mechanism discussion earlier.
*   **The "In Place" Result:** The null result on migration (Table 2, Col 3) is a major finding. It needs its own subsection to discuss what "occupational transformation in place" looks like in 1920.

---

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The paper is highly competent and ambitious. The gap between "Good Revision" and "AER Accept" is the **Persistence and GE effects**.

**The single most impactful piece of advice:** 
Track these cohorts into the 1930 and 1940 censuses (if possible) to show that this wasn't just a temporary post-war blip, but a permanent shift in the trajectory of American labor, and investigate if the "pushed" men's children also benefited from this escape from the farm.
