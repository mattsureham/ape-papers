## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether a large, forced shock can accelerate structural transformation by pushing workers out of low-productivity agriculture and into better nonfarm jobs. Using the WWI draft and the exemption of many foreign-born non-citizens, it argues that conscription acted as an “inertia breaker”: draft-exposed young men were more likely to leave farming and move into higher-paying occupations by 1920.

A busy economist should care because the paper is trying to say something bigger than military history: why labor does not automatically flow out of agriculture even when wage gaps are large, and what kinds of shocks can overcome that persistence.

Does the paper itself articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs? Mostly, but not optimally. The current introduction is competent and reasonably clear, but it starts too much from “agricultural inertia exists” and only later gets to the sharper, more AER-worthy claim: this is a paper about the forces that impede structural transformation and about forced disruption as a mechanism for reallocating labor. The first two paragraphs should make the world question much more explicit and should foreground the surprising fact first.

### The pitch the paper should have

“A central question in development and economic history is why workers remain in low-productivity agriculture long after better-paying nonfarm opportunities exist. This paper shows that one answer is inertia: when World War I conscription forcibly disrupted young American men’s attachment to farming, those exposed to the draft were substantially more likely to leave agriculture and move into higher-paying occupations.

I use the fact that the 1917 Selective Service Act exposed native-born men to conscription while exempting many foreign-born non-citizens, and compare occupational transitions across eligible and ineligible cohorts in linked full-count census data. The results suggest that forced disruption can accelerate structural transformation not by moving workers across space, but by breaking occupational attachment that markets alone had failed to undo.”

That is the story. Right now the paper says this, but it takes too long to get there and spends too much early space on design mechanics.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper’s contribution is to argue that exogenous forced disruption—via WWI conscription—helped break persistent attachment to agriculture and accelerated the farm-to-nonfarm transition in early twentieth-century America.

### Evaluation

#### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?
Not clearly enough. The paper cites several literatures, but the novelty relative to each is still somewhat mushy.

- Relative to the military service literature, the contribution is not “military service affects later outcomes”—we already know that genre.
- Relative to structural transformation, the contribution is not “workers moved out of agriculture in the early 20th century”—also known.
- The novel claim is the intersection: **forced occupational disruption can be a causal mechanism behind structural transformation when wage gaps alone do not induce movement.**

That is a real contribution, but the paper needs to defend it more sharply against the obvious reader reaction: “So this is another military-service paper with historical linked-census data.”

#### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or as filling a gap in a LITERATURE?
Mostly as a world question, which is good. The phrase “what breaks agricultural inertia?” is stronger than “there is little evidence on...” The paper should lean even harder into the world question and spend less time sounding like it is slotting into three literatures for completeness.

#### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what’s new?
Maybe, but only if they are charitable. Right now they might say: “It’s a historical DiD using nativity differences around WWI draft exposure, showing positive occupational effects.” That is not enough.

What you want them to say is: “It shows that structural transformation was constrained by occupational attachment, and that a temporary forced disruption had lasting reallocation effects.”

That is a materially better takeaway.

#### What would make this contribution bigger?
Specific ways to enlarge it strategically:

1. **Make persistence the centerpiece.**  
   The most important underdeveloped line in the paper is “accelerated the structural transformation over the subsequent decade.” If the effect lasts beyond immediate demobilization, that is much bigger than a one-period postwar occupational shuffle. A temporary shock with persistent reallocation effects is top-journal material; a temporary wartime perturbation is less so.

2. **Show sectoral destination, not just “not farm.”**  
   “Farm exit” and occupational income score are useful, but broad. The title says farm-to-factory. The evidence in the main text does not yet fully earn that title. If the central claim is structural transformation, the reader wants to know where workers went: manufacturing, transport, clerical, skilled trades, urban services? The destination matters for the size and nature of the contribution.

3. **Clarify mechanism as attachment/information/network disruption rather than generic human capital.**  
   Right now the mechanism section lists several channels. That is fair, but diffuse. The paper’s comparative advantage is not proving military skill acquisition. It is showing that forced separation from agriculture changes occupational choices. Tighten around that mechanism.

4. **Connect more directly to contemporary structural transformation.**  
   The development relevance is there, but somewhat tacked on in the discussion. If the paper wants to be more than a very good economic history paper, it needs to tell readers why this historical case changes how we think about labor reallocation in poor countries today.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

### Closest neighbors

The closest conversations appear to be:

1. **Angrist (1990, 1994)** and related military service papers  
   On the causal effects of service on later civilian labor-market outcomes.

2. **Bryan, Chowdhury, and Mobarak (2014)**  
   On seasonal migration and barriers preventing movement out of low-productivity settings despite large gains.

3. **Lagakos and Waugh / Lagakos et al.**  
   On barriers to structural transformation and persistent agricultural labor shares.

4. **Hornbeck (2012)** and related American economic history reallocation papers  
   On shocks and labor reallocation in historical U.S. contexts.

5. **Collins and Wanamaker / Collins-related Great Migration work**  
   On constraints facing Black workers and movement out of Southern agriculture.

One might also add **Munshi and Rosenzweig**-type network/friction papers as conceptual neighbors.

### How should the paper position itself?
Mostly **build on and bridge**, not attack.

- Build on military service papers by saying context matters: service can harm careers in one setting and unlock transition in another.
- Build on structural transformation papers by offering a rare quasi-experimental shock to occupational reallocation.
- Bridge to migration/frictions work by arguing that the key margin here is not geographic migration but occupational detachment.

The paper should avoid overstating “I overturn the literature.” It does not. It adds a mechanism and a setting.

### Is it currently positioned too narrowly or too broadly?
A bit too broadly and a bit mechanically.

The intro currently has the standard “this paper contributes to three literatures” structure. That is fine for field journals; for AER it often weakens the sense of a single conversation. The paper should choose its primary battlefield. Right now the natural home conversation is:

**Why doesn’t labor leave agriculture faster, and what kind of shocks relax the frictions that prevent structural transformation?**

Military service should be the empirical setting, not the lead literature.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?
Not necessarily unaware, but under-engaged with:

- **Structural transformation in development**, especially work on misallocation, occupational choice, and barriers to reallocation.
- **Labor mobility and attachment** literatures that distinguish place attachment from occupation attachment.
- Possibly **forced displacement / disruption** literatures outside military service—e.g., natural disasters, plant closures, or institutional shocks that sever local ties. Even if not direct comparators, they could help frame “disruption as reallocation catalyst.”

### Is the paper having the right conversation?
Almost, but not quite. The right conversation is not primarily “what are the effects of WWI service?” It is “what prevents seemingly profitable labor reallocation, and can temporary shocks create permanent structural change?” That framing is much more interesting and much more AER-facing.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Workers often remain in agriculture despite apparent wage gains elsewhere. Standard economic models suggest labor should move, but in practice structural transformation is slow.

### Tension
Why doesn’t labor reallocate more quickly? Are workers truly choosing agriculture because it is optimal, or are there frictions—attachment, information, institutions, risk—that keep them there? It is hard to observe a plausibly exogenous shock that severs those ties.

### Resolution
WWI conscription provided such a shock. Men exposed to draft risk were more likely to leave farming and move into better occupations, especially where agricultural attachment was strongest and among more constrained groups.

### Implications
Structural transformation can be held back by inertia rather than pure preferences; temporary forced disruption may have persistent reallocation effects. This changes how we think about both historical industrialization and modern development policy.

### Evaluation
The paper does have a real narrative arc. That is a plus. It is not a random collection of tables. But the arc is weakened by two things:

1. **The title and framing promise farm-to-factory, but the evidence is more farm-to-better-occupation.**  
   That mismatch hurts the resolution.

2. **The paper occasionally slips from a clean narrative into a methods/results inventory.**  
   The supplementary RD discussion, for example, occupies more conceptual space than it should. It interrupts the story instead of advancing it.

The paper should be telling a simpler story:  
**A large temporary shock forced men off farms; many did not go back; that reveals the importance of attachment costs in structural transformation.**

Everything else should support that.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?
“I’ve got a paper showing that WWI conscription pulled young men off farms and permanently accelerated the U.S. farm-to-nonfarm transition—suggesting that structural transformation was held back by inertia, not just low returns.”

That is the most interesting version.

### Would people lean in?
Some would. Economic historians, development economists, and labor economists probably would. But many would ask, almost immediately, “Permanent how?” and “Did they actually go to factories?” Those are exactly the questions the paper needs to answer more forcefully.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Likely one of these:

- “So did the draft change occupational structure, or just temporarily scramble employment after the war?”
- “Where did these men go if not farms—manufacturing, urban services, skilled trades?”
- “Why should I interpret this as structural transformation rather than military service raising occupational status in some generic way?”
- “Is this really about occupational attachment rather than migration or wartime labor demand?”

Those follow-up questions are healthy. The current paper answers some of them partially, but not decisively enough in the framing.

### If findings are modest, is that okay?
The farm-exit estimate is modest in levels, though meaningful. The occupational-score effect sounds larger. The paper does a decent job making the effect nontrivial, but the “so what” hinges less on raw magnitude than on interpretation. If this is evidence of **persistent reallocation from a temporary shock**, it is interesting. If it is just a detectable average effect in a very large sample, it is less so.

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

### What would make it read better?

1. **Shorten the institutional and empirical-strategy exposition in the main text.**  
   The reader gets the basic design quickly. The paper currently spends too much valuable early real estate walking through mechanics. For editorial purposes, I would want the introduction to carry more of the conceptual burden and the methods section to be leaner.

2. **Move most of the supplementary RD material to the appendix or compress it heavily.**  
   Right now, a failed or unpersuasive supplementary design gets a lot of oxygen. That is not good storytelling. If the main design is the nativity-based comparison, then the paper should not seem emotionally attached to a second design that mostly demonstrates why the first is better.

3. **Front-load the destination and persistence results if available.**  
   If there is a result on manufacturing transitions, nonfarm sector entry, or longer-run persistence, it should appear in the introduction and main results—not as an aside or appendix table.

4. **Clarify what the heterogeneity is doing.**  
   The county-agriculture and race heterogeneity are useful because they support the “where inertia binds” mechanism. That should be made explicit. At present, they read a bit like standard heterogeneity add-ons.

5. **Trim generic discussion language.**  
   Phrases like “this carries implications beyond the specific historical context” are fine, but the discussion section could be sharper and shorter. The best conclusion would not merely summarize; it would tell the reader what belief about structural transformation should change.

### Is the paper front-loaded with the good stuff?
Reasonably, but not enough. The abstract is pretty good. The introduction reveals the main findings early. Still, the strongest conceptual claim—temporary disruption causing durable reallocation—needs to come even earlier and more forcefully.

### Are there results buried in robustness that should be in the main results?
Not from what is shown. If anything, too much low-value robustness is in the main text. The more important missing main-text result is a clean destination decomposition and any persistence evidence.

### Is the conclusion adding value?
A little, but mostly summarizing. It reiterates the story competently; it does not really elevate it. The final paragraph should leave the reader with the broader proposition: **structural transformation can stall because workers are stuck, and one learns that from what happened when a large shock forcibly unstuck them.**

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

This is not mainly a competence problem. It is mainly a **framing-plus-scope** problem.

### What is the gap?

#### Framing problem
Yes. The paper has a better idea than its current packaging fully conveys. It should be marketed as a paper on structural transformation and occupational attachment, not primarily as a WWI draft paper.

#### Scope problem
Also yes. For AER, the evidence feels one notch too narrow. The paper needs either:
- stronger proof of persistent structural change,
- more detailed evidence on destination sectors and mechanism,
- or a clearer conceptual payoff that generalizes beyond this episode.

#### Novelty problem
Somewhat. The empirical design and historical setting are interesting, but not enough by themselves. Without stronger positioning, some readers will view it as a well-executed historical DiD in a crowded causal-history style.

#### Ambition problem
Yes, a bit. The paper is careful and competent, but somewhat safe. It stops at showing that the draft “moved outcomes.” The more ambitious version would show that this episode changes how we think about why structural transformation is slow.

### Single most impactful piece of advice
**Reframe the paper around a bigger claim—temporary forced disruption revealed that occupational attachment was a major barrier to structural transformation—and then reorganize the evidence to prove persistence and destination, not just average postwar occupational improvement.**

If they can only change one thing, that is it.
