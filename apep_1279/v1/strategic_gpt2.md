## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether a large, forced disruption can accelerate structural transformation by pushing workers out of agriculture and into higher-paying nonfarm jobs. Using the WWI draft exemption for foreign-born non-citizens, it argues that conscription broke “agricultural inertia” in the United States: draft-exposed men were more likely to leave farming and move into better occupations, especially in heavily agricultural counties and among Black men.

Why should a busy economist care? Because the paper is trying to say something broader than a historical labor-market episode: that inefficient persistence in low-productivity sectors may reflect transition frictions rather than preferences, and that large shocks can permanently reallocate labor.

**Does the paper articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Partly. The opening gets to the broad theme quickly, but it oscillates between three possible papers: a military-service paper, a structural-transformation paper, and a paper about agricultural attachment costs. The first two paragraphs are competent, but not yet sharp enough about the core question in world terms: *why do workers remain stuck in low-productivity sectors even when higher-wage alternatives exist?*

**What the first two paragraphs should say instead:**  

> Large productivity gaps between agriculture and nonagriculture are a central fact of economic development, yet labor often moves out of farming much more slowly than simple models predict. The key question is whether this persistence reflects workers’ preferences for farm life or frictions—social ties, information gaps, risk, contractual constraints—that keep workers trapped in low-productivity occupations.
>
> This paper studies that question using a dramatic historical shock: World War I conscription in the United States. Because the 1917 draft exposed native-born men but exempted many foreign-born non-citizens, the paper compares otherwise similar cohorts with sharply different exposure to forced occupational disruption. I show that draft exposure increased exit from farming and movement into higher-status occupations, especially in the most agricultural places and among groups facing the strongest labor-market constraints. The broader implication is that structural transformation can be slowed not just by lack of opportunity, but by inertia that large shocks can break.

That is the pitch the paper should own.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution in one sentence:**  
The paper argues that WWI conscription accelerated the U.S. farm-to-nonfarm transition by forcibly disrupting workers’ attachment to agriculture, providing evidence that labor reallocation is impeded by inertia rather than driven solely by sectoral wage gaps.

### Evaluation

**Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?**  
Not clearly enough. The paper names several literatures, but the novelty is still blurry. Right now a reader could summarize it as: “another historical design showing military service affected later labor outcomes.” To land in AER territory, the differentiation needs to be much more explicit:

- relative to the military-service literature, the novelty is **not** merely “WWI had positive effects while Vietnam had negative effects”;
- relative to the structural-transformation literature, the novelty is **not** merely “here is another shock affecting occupational mobility”;
- relative to migration/frictions papers, the novelty is **that forced disruption can reveal latent nonmovement frictions even without geographic migration**.

The paper has a potentially interesting contribution, but it needs harder edges.

**Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a gap in a literature?**  
Mostly the former, which is good. “What breaks agricultural inertia?” is a world question. But the introduction then drifts into “this contributes to three literatures,” which makes the paper feel smaller and more conventional. The stronger version is: *This paper changes how we interpret slow structural transformation.*

**Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what’s new?**  
They could, but only vaguely. They would probably say: “It uses the WWI draft and census links to show conscription moved some men out of farming.” That is not enough. You want them to say: “It shows slow exit from agriculture partly reflects attachment frictions, because a shock that forcibly severs workers from farms causes lasting occupational upgrading even without moving them across counties.”

**What would make this contribution bigger? Be specific.**  
Several possibilities:

1. **Sharper outcome framing:**  
   Right now “farm exit” and “occscore” are serviceable, but the title promises “farm-to-factory transition.” Then the paper mostly shows farm exit and occupational score changes, not clearly a manufacturing reallocation story. If there is a strong farm-to-manufacturing margin, that needs to be front and center. If not, the title and framing should be toned down to “farm-to-nonfarm.”

2. **Mechanism more tightly matched to the claim:**  
   The paper claims “inertia break,” but current evidence is still inferential. The strongest version would directly distinguish among:
   - leaving agriculture,
   - entering manufacturing versus other nonfarm work,
   - staying in place versus moving,
   - effects concentrated where tenancy/sharecropping constraints were strongest.  
   The paper already gestures at some of this, but the main text should organize around it.

3. **Longer-run implications:**  
   If possible, showing persistence beyond 1920 would dramatically raise ambition. Right now “over the subsequent decade” is already the design, but the broader claim is about structural transformation, which begs the question of persistence. Without longer-run evidence, the paper risks sounding like a medium-run occupational detour paper rather than a structural transformation paper.

4. **Reframing around a general economic question:**  
   The biggest contribution is not the WWI episode; it is the claim that *large labor-productivity gaps can persist because workers are stuck, not because they choose to stay.* The paper should make that argument more directly and more boldly.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

The closest neighboring conversations appear to be:

2. **Angrist and Krueger / Bound and Turner / related military service returns papers** on the labor-market consequences of service.

4. **Lagakos and Waugh / Lagakos et al.** on productivity gaps, agriculture, and structural transformation.  
5. **Hornbeck (2012)** and related historical papers on shocks and labor reallocation in U.S. economic history.  
6. **Collins and Wanamaker / Hornbeck and Naidu / Great Migration papers** on Black mobility and escape from Southern agricultural institutions.

If forced to name the most important neighbors for positioning, I would pick: **Angrist (1990), Bryan et al. (2014), Lagakos et al., Hornbeck (2012), and Collins/Wanamaker.**

### How should the paper position itself?

**Build on and connect**, rather than attack.

- Build on **Angrist** by saying military service can either hinder or accelerate civilian advancement depending on where workers start.  
- Build on **structural transformation** by offering evidence that nonmovement out of agriculture reflects frictions.  
- Build on **migration/frictions** by suggesting that temporary displacement can relax occupational constraints even without permanent relocation.  
- Build on **racial economic history** by showing that forced disruption had larger effects where institutions were most binding.

The paper should **not** attack prior work as wrong. It should say: existing literatures emphasize pull factors, migration subsidies, or wartime service effects; this paper shows a distinct push mechanism.

### Is the paper currently positioned too narrowly or too broadly?

Ironically, both.

- **Too broadly** because it claims contributions to three literatures and gestures at development, labor, military service, Great Migration, and structural transformation all at once.
- **Too narrowly** because the empirical narrative is still centered on a very specific institutional fact about declarant versus non-declarant aliens in WWI.

For AER, it needs a narrower *core claim* and a broader *economic meaning*.  
Core claim: forced disruption breaks agricultural attachment.  
Broader meaning: sectoral labor misallocation may persist because transitions are costly.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?

It should speak more directly to:

- **Misallocation / structural change / occupational choice under frictions**, not just broad structural transformation surveys.
- **Specific historical work on WWI mobilization and labor markets**, if such exists, to avoid appearing naive about wartime labor demand, assimilation, and nativity differences.
- **Internal migration and network literature**, especially if the “no county migration effect” is central.
- **Occupational mobility and linked historical census literature**, beyond the existence of MLP itself.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?

Mostly, but the highest-value conversation is probably **not** “what are the effects of military service?” It is:  
**What do large shocks reveal about why workers remain in low-productivity sectors?**

That is the more interesting conversation and the one with wider readership.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Workers remained in agriculture even when manufacturing wages were higher; structural transformation was incomplete and slower than simple models suggest.

### Tension
Why didn’t these workers leave? Was agriculture sticky because of preferences, or because of frictions and constraints? This is hard to tell because occupational transitions are usually endogenous.

### Resolution
WWI conscription created forced disruption. Men exposed to the draft were more likely to leave farming and move into better occupations, with larger effects where agricultural attachment and social constraints were stronger.

### Implications
Slow labor reallocation may reflect inertia and barriers rather than efficient sorting. Large shocks—or perhaps policy interventions that mimic some aspects of them—can accelerate movement out of low-productivity sectors.

### Evaluation

The paper **does have** a narrative arc, but it is not yet clean. The main problem is that the paper alternates among three stories:

1. military service had positive labor-market effects;
2. the WWI draft accelerated structural transformation;
3. forced disruption overcomes attachment costs.

The third is the strongest and most distinctive. The first is too familiar; the second is potentially big but currently underpowered by the outcomes shown. The paper should subordinate the military-service angle to the structural-frictions angle.

At moments, the paper also feels like a collection of results looking for a story:
- farm exit,
- occupational score,
- no migration,
- heterogeneity by county agriculture,
- heterogeneity by race,
- an RD that the paper itself says is not informative.

These results can be unified, but only if the narrative is explicitly:  
**temporary forced displacement changed occupation more than location, implying that the main barrier was not absence of destinations but difficulty of severing attachment to the origin sector.**

That should be the story.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

**What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?**  
“Conscription during WWI appears to have pushed young American men out of farming and into higher-paying occupations, even though it didn’t noticeably increase county-to-county migration.”

That is the hook. The migration null is actually one of the most interesting facts in the paper because it sharpens the interpretation.

**Would people lean in or reach for their phones?**  
Some would lean in—but only if the framing is tightened. “WWI draft affected occupations” is a niche economic-history result. “A forced shock broke agricultural inertia without requiring geographic migration” is more interesting.

**What follow-up question would they ask?**  
Probably one of these:
- “So was the barrier really sectoral attachment rather than place-based immobility?”
- “Did these men go specifically into manufacturing, or just into anything nonfarm?”
- “How persistent were the effects?”
- “Is this a story about military service, or about labor-market frictions more generally?”

Those follow-up questions reveal the current weakness: the paper’s central claim invites exactly the evidence it does not yet foreground enough.

**If the findings are modest: is the modest result itself interesting?**  
The farm-exit magnitude is modest in levels, though maybe meaningful at scale. The occupational-score result is more striking. The paper should avoid overselling the farm-exit coefficient in isolation and instead package the findings as evidence of a directional mechanism with economically meaningful occupational upgrading. Right now the prose occasionally inflates. AER readers will sniff that out.
