**Idea Fidelity**

The paper aligns closely with the original manifest. It leverages the large ICFES Saber 11 microdata set and the Esri manzana estrato classifications to study the causal impact of estrato boundaries on student outcomes using a spatial RDD framework. The multi-cutoff design, the focus on the 5|6 placebo boundary, and the mechanism decomposition through observable household assets and school types are all present. One noticeable divergence is the paper’s reliance on the student-reported estrato variable rather than the geocoded manzana centroid distances described in the manifest’s spatial RDD proposal. While the manifest emphasizes geographic distance to boundaries, the paper implements an estrato indicator discontinuity with municipality fixed effects, which weakens proximity variation and raises concerns about the credibility of the running variable. This choice should be more explicitly justified or reconciled with the original spatial-RDD idea.

**Summary**

The paper studies whether Colombia’s block-level estrato classifications—linked to utility subsidies—induce discontinuous jumps in Saber 11 test scores by estimating multi-cutoff regression discontinuities at estrato boundaries in five major cities. It finds large, statistically significant score gaps at subsidized boundaries (1|2 through 3|4), which persist even after controlling for household assets and are absent at the surcharge-only 5|6 boundary, supporting a subsidy-driven sorting mechanism. Additional evidence suggests private schools drive the discontinuities and that the effects are stable over time, highlighting the potential unintended educational consequences of Colombia’s stratification system.

**Essential Points**

1. **Credibility of the “running variable” and spatial interpretation:** The manifest promised a spatial RDD based on geocoded distance to estrato boundaries, but the paper estimates a discontinuity based solely on the student’s self-reported estrato indicator within municipality fixed effects. Without exploiting geographic proximity, the design resembles a difference between adjacent estratos rather than a true boundary discontinuity. This raises concerns about whether students “just across the border” are comparable and whether the treatment variation is local enough for the RDD logic to hold. The authors need to (a) clarify the link between their estimator and a spatial RD (e.g., show that within-municipality distance to the boundary is balanced and drives the variation), (b) present evidence that residual spatial sorting within a single estrato does not drive the results, and (c) explore whether misclassification of estrato (self-report vs. administrative) biases the estimates. Without this, the causal interpretation is weakened.

2. **Interpretation of covariate discontinuities and control strategy:** Table 3 shows significant jumps in household diagnostics (internet, parental education, assets) across the boundaries. These are not “noise” but the very outcomes through which estrato is defined, so treating them as covariates to absorb bias risks conditioning on post-treatment variables and obscuring the mechanism. The authors must better motivate the use of these controls (are they pre-determined relative to the school boundary exposure?) and ensure the estimated residual effect is not just a reintroduction of the original stratification signal. Additionally, if these covariates differ sharply, the comparability assumption needs further defense—e.g., by showing smoothness in geographically proximate observations conditional on distance to the boundary.

3. **External validity and sample scope:** The paper samples only five large cities, but the estrato system extends far beyond them, and the degree of school choice, boundary sharpness, and subsidy penetration may differ in smaller municipalities. The policy implications (e.g., national reform debates) require evidence that the mechanism is not city-specific. The authors should either present evidence that the five cities are representative of the broader urban system (e.g., by comparing estrato distributions or boundary characteristics) or acknowledge the limitation and temper their conclusions.

**Suggestions**

1. **Reinforce the spatial RDD angle.** Since the manifest highlighted a spatial RDD, the paper would benefit from directly incorporating geographic proximity into the running variable. If geocoded manzana centroids and boundary polygons are available, construct a distance-based running variable (distance to nearest boundary segment) for each school or student and estimate a fuzzy/sharp RDD using that continuous measure. At minimum, present descriptive plots showing how outcomes change with proximity to the boundary within a narrow spatial band. Alternatively, explicitly justify why the discrete estrato indicator suffices and demonstrate that the estimator is consistent with a spatial design.

2. **Address measurement error and misreporting concerns.** The reliance on student-reported estrato could introduce error, especially near boundaries where respondents might misstate the classification. Consider validating the self-reported estrato against administrative manzana data for a subsample (if feasible) or, if not, discuss the likely direction of bias (classical error attenuates toward zero) and whether it may differ by boundary. Highlight any checks performed (e.g., comparing self-reported estrato to school-level modal estrato) to build confidence in the running variable.

3. **Clarify the timing of controls and the mechanism analysis.** Present a clearer causal path diagram or narrative: estrato classification → utility subsidies / residential sorting → household asset accumulation and school quality → Saber scores. Specify which variables are pre-determined (e.g., parental education vs. assets) and which are downstream. When presenting the “controls” specification, emphasize that these are considered proxies for household endowments rather than treatment effects, and be cautious about presenting the difference between unchecked and controlled estimates as the “school-sorting residual.” Complement the controls analysis with alternative mechanism proxies—such as explicit school-level quality indicators (teacher credentials, class size, accreditation)—to better isolate the school-sorting channel without conditioning on potentially endogenous household assets.

4. **Deepen the discussion of the 5|6 placebo.** The 5|6 boundary is a compelling placebo, but the small sample (N=31,543) and imprecise estimates (SE=5.6) limit conclusiveness. Provide density plots of covariates and outcomes around that boundary to show actual overlap, and consider whether there may be other differences between estrato 5 and 6 (e.g., private school penetration, housing prices) that could mask a label effect. If feasible, extend the placebo to other contexts (e.g., boundaries that share the same subsidy rate, such as between estrato 2 and 3 in municipalities that treat them equivalently) to strengthen the “label vs. subsidy” argument.

5. **Expand robustness checks on spatial sorting.** The manifold spatial sorting mechanisms—households, schools, teachers—warrant more granular evidence. For example, use distance to boundary as an instrument to predict the share of higher-estrato peers or private school attendance, then relate those first-stage changes to test scores. Alternatively, exploit within-school variation by focusing on schools that enroll students from both sides of a boundary (if such schools exist) and see whether the boundary discontinuity persists after controlling for the school fixed effect. This would help demonstrate that the effect operates through school assignment/composition rather than unobserved neighborhood characteristics.

6. **Discuss policy trade-offs more explicitly.** The conclusion hints at reform debates but could better quantify the educational cost of the estrato system relative to its utility subsidy benefits. Can the authors provide back-of-the-envelope calculations linking the estimated score gaps to future earnings or university access? Even a simple comparison (e.g., how many points would be recouped by moving across a boundary) would help policymakers weigh the trade-offs. Also address whether reforming the estrato system would create unintended consequences elsewhere (e.g., loss of universal subsidies) and how the educational sorting channel interacts with other policy levers (e.g., school choice regulation, housing vouchers).

7. **Improve presentation of standard errors and inference.** The paper clusters at the municipality level, but with only five cities, the effective number of clusters is low. Consider using wild cluster bootstrap or alternative inference methods suited to few clusters, and report whether the key findings survive these adjustments. Also, clarify how control variables are measured (binary indicators vs. indices) and whether they are lagged.

By addressing these points—especially the spatial identification, covariate interpretation, and robustness to sorting—the authors can substantially strengthen the paper’s credibility and its contribution to understanding how administrative classification systems shape educational inequality.
