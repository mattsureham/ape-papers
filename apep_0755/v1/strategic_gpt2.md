## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper studies whether Colombia’s block-level socioeconomic classification system, *estratos*, does more than allocate utility subsidies—specifically, whether it also sorts children into different educational trajectories. Using adjacent estrato comparisons in large urban exam data, the paper argues that score gaps are large at boundaries where subsidy schedules change and absent at the 5|6 boundary, suggesting that a place-based transfer system may unintentionally generate educational inequality through residential and school sorting.

A busy economist should care because this is, in principle, a paper about a broad question: when governments classify places for redistribution, do those classifications reshape the geography of opportunity? That is a big, policy-relevant question with implications far beyond Colombia.

**Does the paper articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?** Not quite. The current opening is vivid, but it overcommits to a quasi-causal boundary design before establishing the larger economic question. It also foregrounds “identification advantages” too early. For editorial positioning, the paper should lead with the institutional fact that governments often target transfers geographically, and that such targeting may itself become a sorting technology. Colombia’s estrato system is then the ideal setting to ask whether place-based redistribution changes educational inequality.

**The pitch the paper should have:**

> Governments often target subsidies by classifying neighborhoods, but these classifications may do more than redistribute resources: they may change where families live, which schools children attend, and ultimately how opportunity is allocated across space. This paper studies Colombia’s estrato system—a block-level classification that determines utility subsidies and shapes access to social programs—to ask whether place-based redistribution also creates educational sorting.
>
> Using national exam data for 1.19 million students in five Colombian cities, I show that test score gaps are sharply larger at estrato boundaries where subsidy schedules change, and disappear at the 5|6 boundary where both sides face the same surcharge regime. The central message is that a policy designed to equalize utility access may inadvertently stratify human capital by inducing residential and school sorting.

That is the AER pitch. Not “does your block determine your score?” but “do government place classifications create inequality by sorting families across space?”

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Governments use geographic or neighborhood-level classifications to deliver subsidies and social policy. Colombia’s estrato system is a particularly stark example: every urban block is assigned a category that affects utility prices and other aspects of social policy.

### Tension
A policy meant to redistribute toward poorer households may create incentives for residential sorting and alter school composition. So the core tension is that a system designed to reduce inequality in one domain may increase it in another.

### Resolution
The paper finds sizable score gaps at boundaries where subsidy schedules change, and no gap at the 5|6 boundary, which it interprets as evidence that the fiscal design of estrato—not just the label—drives educational sorting.

### Implications
Policy targeting rules can have long-run, unintended equilibrium effects on human capital. Reforming place-based transfer systems may matter not just for subsidy incidence but also for educational stratification.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
It has the ingredients, but not yet the discipline. Right now it reads somewhat like a collection of boundary estimates plus some mechanism rhetoric. The story is there, but the paper keeps slipping between three stories:

1. a causal boundary paper,
2. a neighborhood effects paper,
3. a policy-design paper about place-based targeting.

The third is the strongest. The paper should commit to it and demote the others.

### If it is a collection of results looking for a story, what story should it tell?
This one:

> Colombia’s estrato system is a vivid case of a general problem: when redistribution is tied to place labels, families sort on those labels, and schools become one channel through which redistribution policy reshapes opportunity. The paper documents that this unintended sorting is strongest exactly where the subsidy schedule changes and absent where only the label remains.

That story organizes the facts into something coherent and important.

That is the memorable fact.

### Would people lean in?
Some would, especially urban, development, public finance, and education economists. But many would only lean in if the framing were upgraded. “Exam score gaps across estrato boundaries” is specialized. “Place-based redistribution can unintentionally stratify opportunity” is much more compelling.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Immediately:
- “Is this about neighborhood labels, subsidy incentives, or school choice?”
- “Why should we think the educational margin is the key consequence?”
- “How general is this beyond Colombia?”
- “What does this imply for means-testing versus place-based targeting?”

Those are good questions. The current paper half-answers them, but not decisively enough.

Strategically, the null is valuable because it helps shift the story from “estrato as stigma” to “subsidy-linked sorting.” That is a cleaner and more policy-relevant message anyway.
