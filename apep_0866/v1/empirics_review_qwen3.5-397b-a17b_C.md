\section*{Review of "When the Roughnecks Arrive: Male-Biased Labor Demand and the Gendered Externalities of Resource Booms"}

\subsection*{1. Idea Fidelity}
The paper largely adheres to the core empirical strategy outlined in the Original Idea Manifest, specifically the use of the shale boom as a male-biased labor demand shock and the reliance on QWI sex-disaggregated data. The triple-difference design (Female $\times$ High-Mining $\times$ Boom) aligns with the proposed identification strategy. However, there is a notable deviation regarding data and mechanisms. The Manifest explicitly confirmed the use of NIBRS/UCR crime records and ACS sex ratios to test mechanisms (violence, marriage markets), yet the paper drops crime outcomes entirely and only briefly mentions ACS sex ratios in the limitations. Additionally, the Manifest proposed an IV strategy (instrumenting sex ratios with shale exposure), whereas the paper employs a reduced-form Triple-DiD with shale exposure as the direct treatment. While the reduced-form approach is standard, the omission of the crime/demographic channel weakens the connection to the "Resource Curse Asymmetry" theme originally proposed.

\subsection*{2. Summary}
This paper exploits the exogenous variation in shale endowments across US counties to estimate the causal effect of male-biased labor demand shocks on women's labor market outcomes. Using a triple-difference design on QWI data (2001--2022), the author finds that fracking booms reduced female non-mining employment by approximately 12 percent relative to male trajectories and widened the gender earnings gap. The results suggest that industrial policies favoring male-dominated sectors impose persistent negative externalities on women's economic standing, even after the boom subsides.

\subsection*{3. Essential Points}
The following three issues must be addressed to ensure the validity of the causal claims and the appropriateness of the inference.

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Inference and Clustering:} The paper clusters standard errors at the state level. With 24 states in the sample, there are only 24 clusters. Recent econometric literature (e.g., \cite{Conley2008}, \cite{Cameron2008}) warns that $t$-statistics with fewer than 30--40 clusters can be severely biased, leading to over-rejection of the null. Given that shale policy and oil prices vary significantly at the state level, residual correlation within states is likely high. You must either implement a wild cluster bootstrap percentile-$t$ procedure, use Conley spatial standard errors, or provide permutation-based inference to demonstrate that the results are not driven by a few influential states (e.g., North Dakota or Texas).
    
    \item \textbf{Parallel Trends and Event Study:} The identification relies on the assumption that the gender gap in employment would have evolved similarly in high- and low-mining counties absent the boom. Table 3 presents period dummies (Boom/Bust), but this masks the dynamics. You need to present an event-study figure (e.g., \cite{Sun2020} estimator) showing coefficients for leads and lags relative to the boom start (2006). Specifically, you must demonstrate that the female-male employment gap was not already diverging in high-mining counties prior to 2006. Without this visual evidence, the parallel trends assumption remains untested.
    
    \item \textbf{Mechanism and Data Consistency:} The Manifest confirmed access to NIBRS crime data and ACS sex ratios to test mechanisms (violence, marriage markets), but the paper excludes crime outcomes and relegates sex ratios to limitations. If the mechanism is "marriage market distortion" or "cost-of-living inflation," you need evidence that sex ratios actually shifted in treated counties (first stage for demographics) and ideally some test of the crime channel (even if null). Omitting these variables makes the "Roughneck Externality" label feel descriptive rather than mechanistic. At minimum, include a table showing the effect of the boom on county-level sex ratios (ACS) to validate the demographic shock.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection*{4. Suggestions}
The following recommendations are intended to strengthen the economic interpretation, clarify magnitudes, and improve the overall polish of the manuscript. These suggestions constitute the bulk of the review to help you elevate the paper to a publishable standard.

\textbf{Clarifying Magnitudes and Units.} The magnitudes in Table 3 require clearer interpretation. In Column 3, the coefficient on \texttt{female $\times$ treatment $\times$ boom} is $-0.306$. The text describes the treatment as "pre-boom mining employment share." If this share is measured as a fraction (0 to 1), a 10 percentage point difference in mining share implies a 3 percent drop in female employment. However, the Abstract claims a "6.7 percentage points [widening] per standard deviation of mining intensity." Please ensure the units in the text match the table coefficients exactly. If the treatment is standardized in some columns but not others, this must be explicit in the table notes. Additionally, a 12 percent relative decline in employment (Abstract) is economically large. Is this driven by women leaving the labor force or leaving the county? QWI data captures covered employment; if women migrate out of boom counties due to housing costs, QWI counts drop. Distinguishing between \textit{extensive margin} (migration/participation) and \textit{intensive margin} (wages/hours) would add significant value. If possible, link to ACS migration data to see if female out-migration exceeds male out-migration during the boom.

\textbf{Refining the Bust Period.} The paper defines the bust as 2015--2018. Oil prices collapsed in late 2014, but the employment response lagged. Some shale regions (e.g., Permian) recovered quickly by 2017, while others (e.g., Bakken) struggled longer. Consider testing the sensitivity of the bust results to alternative definitions (e.g., 2015--2016 vs. 2017--2020). The finding that effects persist through the bust is crucial for the "structural change" argument. If the effect disappears in a later recovery period, it suggests temporary equilibrium adjustment rather than permanent scarring. Adding a "Recovery" period indicator (2019--2022) in Table 3 would directly test the persistence claim made in the Abstract.

\textbf{Heterogeneity Analysis.} The Appendix includes a split by Urban vs. Rural, showing larger effects in rural counties. This is intuitive (housing supply is more inelastic in rural areas), but it deserves a place in the main text or a dedicated subsection. Why are rural women more affected? Is it because rural economies are less diversified, making the "Dutch Disease" crowding-out effect stronger? Elaborating on this heterogeneity would help policymakers understand where the externalities are most severe. You might also consider heterogeneity by initial sex ratio: did counties with already skewed sex ratios experience larger marginal effects?

\textbf{Placebo Tests.} The healthcare placebo (Table 5, Col 2) is well-chosen. However, consider adding a "male-dominated non-mining" placebo, such as Manufacturing or Transportation. If the shock is truly gendered, we should see no effect (or a positive effect) for men in non-mining sectors. Table 5 Col 4 uses Construction, which is good, but Construction is often correlated with Mining booms. A sector like Manufacturing (NAICS 31-33) might be a cleaner test of the Dutch Disease mechanism (tradable vs. non-tradable). If female-intensive tradable sectors also shrink, it supports the Dutch Disease story; if only non-tradables shrink, it supports the cost-of-living story.

\textbf{IV Strategy Revisited.} The Manifest proposed instrumenting sex ratios with shale exposure. While the reduced-form DiD is sufficient for the labor market outcome, reviving the IV strategy could isolate the \textit{demographic} channel. If you instrument the change in sex ratio with shale exposure, and then regress female outcomes on the predicted sex ratio change, you could directly estimate the elasticity of women's outcomes to male inflows. This would connect your paper more tightly to the Aizer (2010) literature mentioned in the Introduction. Even if you keep the DiD as the main spec, adding this as a complementary exercise would strengthen the mechanistic claim.

\textbf{Writing and Presentation.} 
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textit{Abstract:} The phrase "1,351 counties in 24 US states" is precise, but later the Appendix mentions "249 treated counties." Clarify the treatment definition early. 
    \item \textit{Introduction:} The paragraph on mechanisms (cost-of-living, Dutch Disease, marriage) is excellent. Consider ranking them by likelihood based on your results. 
    \item \textit{Tables:} In Table 3, the $R^2$ for earnings (0.71) is much lower than employment (0.96). This is expected, but ensure readers know earnings data is noisier. 
    \item \textit{Limitations:} You acknowledge the lack of individual-level data. Expand this to discuss the "composition bias." If low-skilled women leave and high-skilled women stay, average earnings could rise even if total welfare falls. QWI average earnings can be misleading if the composition of the workforce changes.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Final Thought.} This is a compelling paper with a clear policy hook. The "Roughneck Externality" is a memorable concept that resonates beyond fracking to infrastructure and defense spending. By tightening the inference (clustering), validating the pre-trends (event study), and reconnecting with the demographic mechanisms (sex ratios/crime), you can transform this from a descriptive labor paper into a definitive statement on the gendered costs of industrial policy. The core result is plausible and economically meaningful; the econometric scaffolding just needs to be fortified to support the weight of the conclusion.
