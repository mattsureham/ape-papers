## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple and important question: when a local boom creates jobs overwhelmingly for men, what happens to women in the same labor market? Using the US shale boom, the paper argues that male-biased labor demand widened local gender gaps in employment and earnings, and that these effects persisted even after the boom subsided.

A busy economist should care because this is potentially a broad point about how sectoral shocks reshape labor markets through gender, not just industry. If true, the paper says that “job creation” is not distributionally neutral even within place, and that local booms can generate gendered spillovers that aggregate employment statistics miss.

### Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Reasonably well, but not optimally. The paper opens with fracking and sex segregation in mining, which is fine, but it slips too quickly into a literature-gap formulation: “the resource boom literature has overlooked…” and “tests of the symmetric case remain absent.” That is not the strongest sales pitch. The real hook is a world question: **what are the local equilibrium consequences when a boom primarily hires men?**

The first two paragraphs should lead with that world question and make the shale boom a clean natural laboratory, not the topic per se. Right now the introduction sounds like “a fracking paper with a gender angle.” It should sound like “a paper about gendered local labor demand, with fracking as the sharpest empirical setting.”

### The pitch the paper should have

A better opening would be something like:

> Economic booms do not benefit all workers equally. When a local industry expands in a way that overwhelmingly hires one sex, the resulting labor demand shock may reshape wages, employment, migration, family formation, and local prices for everyone else in the market. Yet we know surprisingly little about whether male-biased booms improve women’s economic position through spillovers, or instead worsen it by reallocating local opportunity toward men.
>
> This paper studies that question using the US shale boom, one of the largest geographically concentrated labor demand shocks in recent decades and one that was extraordinarily male-biased. I show that counties more exposed to shale expansion experienced a widening of gender gaps in non-mining employment and earnings, and that these effects persisted after the boom reversed. The broader implication is that the incidence of place-based growth depends not just on where jobs are created, but on who those jobs are for.

That is a stronger AER-style pitch because it starts with a general economic problem, uses shale as the design, and ends with a broad implication.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### Contribution in one sentence

The paper’s contribution is to show that a large, geographically concentrated, male-biased labor demand shock can worsen women’s relative labor market outcomes within local economies, implying that sectoral booms generate gendered general-equilibrium spillovers.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?

Partly, but not sharply enough.

The paper distinguishes itself from:
- fracking papers that focus on aggregate employment/wages,
- Kearney and Wilson on marriage/fertility,
- Aizer on female labor demand and IPV.

That’s a start, but the paper still risks sounding like “another shale boom paper, now split by sex.” The differentiation is conceptually there, but it needs to be made much more forcefully.

The paper’s genuinely interesting differentiator is not merely that it uses sex-disaggregated QWI data. It is that it studies **the incidence of local labor demand shocks when the shock is strongly sex-skewed**, and asks whether local gains for one group come with losses for another group. That is broader and more memorable than “first paper to estimate male-biased demand shocks.”

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or as filling a gap in a LITERATURE?

Too much as a literature gap. Phrases like “the literature has overlooked” and “I fill this gap” weaken the ambition. The stronger framing is:

- In the world, many major policies and booms create jobs disproportionately for men.
- We do not know whether such shocks raise all boats or reallocate opportunity across gender.
- The shale boom lets us measure that reallocation.

That is a world question. The paper should live there.

### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what’s new?

Right now, maybe. But many would still summarize it as: “It’s a DiD/triple-diff paper on fracking showing women did relatively worse in boom counties.” That is accurate, but not distinctive enough.

What you want them to say is: “It shows that male-biased job creation can generate a persistent local gender externality. The boom helps men directly and hurts women relatively, even outside the booming sector.”

That requires stronger conceptual packaging.

Several possibilities:

1. **Make the object of interest the local gender incidence of growth**, not shale.
   - Reframe around who benefits from place-based booms and industrial policy.

4. **Add stronger comparative framing.**

If they could enlarge only via framing, the biggest move is to make this a paper about **the distributional incidence of local labor demand shocks by gender**.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Before this paper, we know resource booms raise local employment and wages on average, and we know mining is overwhelmingly male. We also know from labor and family economics that changes in relative labor demand can reshape bargaining power, labor supply, and social outcomes.

### Tension

The puzzle is that aggregate local gains may conceal within-place redistribution. A boom may look successful in headline employment terms while worsening women’s relative economic position if the new jobs are mostly for men and local equilibrium adjustments raise costs or shift sectoral composition.

### Resolution

The paper finds that in more mining-exposed counties, the shale boom widened gender gaps in non-mining employment and earnings: women’s relative outcomes deteriorated during the boom, and these relative losses did not reverse when the boom busted.

### Implications

The implication is that local job creation is not enough as a welfare metric. Policymakers should care about who the jobs are for and about indirect spillovers on non-target groups. More broadly, demographically skewed labor demand shocks can have persistent distributional effects within place.

### Does this paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the bones of one, but it is not fully disciplined. The paper currently reads as:

- intro,
- data/design,
- main results,
- one placebo,
- discussion of several possible mechanisms.

That is competent, but still somewhat like a collection of findings around an intuition. The paper has not fully decided what the central story is.

### What story should it be telling?

It should tell one clean story:

1. **A large local labor demand shock arrives.**
2. **The shock is not neutral; it overwhelmingly targets men.**
3. **That changes the local equilibrium in ways that widen gender gaps outside the booming sector.**
4. **These changes persist, so the effect is not just temporary sectoral churn.**
5. **Therefore, the incidence of growth depends on demographic alignment between the shock and the local workforce.**

That is the story. The “roughneck externality” label is memorable, but risks sounding a bit journalistic unless backed by a more rigorous conceptual frame. I would keep it sparingly, not as the main analytic object.

I would lead with:

That is the dinner-party fact. Even better if tightened to: “Boom counties got richer, but women fell behind locally.”

Lean in, if presented that way. The basic intuition is vivid and relevant. But if presented as “a triple-difference study of female outcomes in high-mining counties,” phones come out immediately.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Almost everyone will ask: **Why?**

More specifically:
- Is it sectoral crowd-out?
- Is it cost-of-living pressure?
- Is it migration/sex ratios?
- Is it family formation and reduced labor supply?
- Is it composition rather than wages?

And second:
- Is this about fracking specifically, or about male-biased growth more generally?

Those are exactly the questions the paper needs to anticipate in its framing.

### If the findings are modest or null

The findings are not null, but they are modest enough that the interpretation matters. The paper cannot coast on coefficient size alone. Its value depends on persuading the reader that the result reveals a general principle about labor demand incidence, rather than one more heterogeneous-effect result in a familiar setting.

The persistence result helps a lot here. That is what saves it from feeling like a routine “women benefited less from a male-heavy boom” paper.
