## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple, important question: when a local economic boom creates jobs overwhelmingly for men, what happens to women in the same local labor market? Using the US shale boom, the paper argues that male-biased labor demand widened local gender gaps in employment and earnings, and that these effects persisted even after the boom receded.

A busy economist should care because this turns a standard “resource boom raises local employment” story into a distributional question about who actually benefits from place-based growth. If true, it has implications well beyond fracking: industrial policy, infrastructure, defense spending, and any sectoral shock with a strongly gendered labor-demand profile.

### Does the paper articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Reasonably, but not sharply enough. The current introduction starts from shale, then gender segregation, then a literature gap. That is competent, but not optimal for AER positioning. The strongest version of the paper is not “resource booms have overlooked gender heterogeneity.” It is: **male-biased labor demand is an economically important and underappreciated force shaping gender inequality.** Shale is the setting, not the main event.

Right now the first two paragraphs are too literature-gap driven and too tied to one episode. The paper should open with the world-level question, then immediately explain why shale is an unusually clean setting to study it.

### The pitch the paper should have

> Many local growth shocks are not gender neutral. When a boom disproportionately raises demand for male labor—as in mining, construction, or defense—aggregate employment may rise while women’s relative labor-market position deteriorates.  
>   
> This paper studies that mechanism in the US shale boom, one of the largest recent local labor-demand shocks and one concentrated in an industry that is overwhelmingly male. I show that counties more exposed to shale expansion experienced larger increases in the gender employment and earnings gap, with effects that persisted beyond the bust, suggesting that male-biased growth can create durable gendered externalities within local labor markets.

That is the AER version: broad question first, canonical natural setting second.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper’s contribution is to show that a large local labor-demand shock concentrated in a male-dominated industry can worsen women’s relative employment and earnings outcomes in the broader local economy, even when aggregate economic activity rises.

### Is this clearly differentiated from the closest papers?

Only partially. The introduction names several literatures, but the differentiation is still a bit mushy. At present, a reader could summarize it as “another shale-boom reduced-form paper, but split by sex.” That is not enough.

The paper needs to distinguish itself more explicitly from at least three adjacent contributions:

1. **Shale/resource-boom local labor market papers**: these show aggregate employment, wage, and multiplier effects.
2. **Gendered labor-demand papers**: these show that female labor demand improves women’s outcomes or bargaining position.
3. **Family / marriage-market responses to male economic shocks**: these show changes in marriage, fertility, and household formation.

The paper’s novelty is not just “we add sex-disaggregated outcomes.” It is that it tries to estimate the **general-equilibrium spillover of male-biased labor demand onto women outside the booming sector**. That needs to be said more crisply.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or filling a gap in a LITERATURE?

Too much as a literature gap. Phrases like “the literature has largely overlooked” and “I fill this gap” are fine, but they are not what gets an AER editor excited. The stronger framing is a question about the world:

- Do male-biased booms widen gender inequality locally?
- Are aggregate employment gains masking distributional losses for women?
- Can place-based growth have persistent gendered costs?

Those are real economic questions. The literature should support that framing, not substitute for it.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the intro?

Yes, but not with maximal clarity. Right now they would probably say: “It’s a triple-diff paper showing the shale boom hurt women relative to men in exposed counties.” That is decent, but still methodology-plus-setting, not conceptually memorable.

What you want them to say is: **“It shows that male-biased labor-demand shocks can worsen women’s local labor-market outcomes even when the local economy is booming.”**

That is a finding about the world.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Several possibilities:

1. **Make the object of interest the local gender gap, not just female outcomes relative to men.**  
   The gender earnings-gap result is actually the most portable and memorable outcome. It should be much more central.

2. **Do more to separate “women lose” from “men gain more.”**  
   Strategically, this matters for interpretation and audience reach. If the headline is really about widening gender inequality, the paper should foreground gender-gap outcomes and decompositions.

3. **Show which non-mining sectors drive the result.**  
   “Non-mining” is too omnibus for a top-journal story. Retail, hospitality, education, local services, tradables, and public sector would give the paper a more economically legible anatomy.

4. **Lean harder into persistence.**  
   The most interesting result may not be the boom effect but the claim that the bust does not unwind the gendered consequences. If that is true, the paper becomes about hysteresis / structural local change, not just contemporaneous crowd-out.

5. **Connect beyond fracking.**  
   The paper gestures at military bases and infrastructure, but this feels tacked on. If the paper wants to be about “male-biased labor demand” as a general concept, it needs to sell that idea throughout, not only in the conclusion.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

### Closest neighbors

The closest literatures/papers seem to be:

1. **Feyrer, Mansur, and Sacerdote (2017, AER)** on geographic dispersion and local labor market effects of shale/resource extraction.
2. **Bartik et al. (likely the shale/local multipliers paper cited as 2019)** on local labor demand and spillovers from energy booms.
3. **Allcott and Keniston (2018, AEJ or similar local boom/bust paper)** on Dutch disease / local spillovers from oil and gas.
4. **Kearney and Wilson (2018, AER Papers & Proceedings / related work)** on fracking, fertility, and family outcomes.
5. **Aizer (2010, AER)** on relative labor market conditions for women and intimate partner violence / bargaining.
6. Potentially **Charles, Hurst, and Notowidigdo**-type work on male labor market shocks, marriageability, and family behavior.

### How should it position itself relative to those neighbors?

Mostly **build on** them, not attack them.

- Relative to the shale papers: “Those papers establish that shale affected local labor markets; this paper shows those effects were sharply gendered and that aggregate gains masked widening gender inequality.”
- Relative to Aizer: “This paper studies the mirror case—male-biased labor demand rather than female labor demand—and shifts attention from household bargaining to broader labor-market incidence.”
- Relative to Kearney/Wilson: “They show family responses to shale; this paper shows labor-market responses for women, which may help explain or complement those household-level effects.”

The paper should not overclaim “first causal estimate” unless it is absolutely sure; that sort of claim is vulnerable and unnecessary. Better to say “I provide evidence in a large-scale setting” or “I estimate the local labor-market consequences of a strongly male-biased demand shock.”

### Is the paper positioned too narrowly or too broadly?

At the moment, oddly both.

- **Too narrow** in empirical packaging: a shale paper, a county panel, QWI, triple-diff.
- **Too broad** in implications: fracking, industrial policy, military bases, infrastructure, marriage markets, domestic violence, Dutch disease, place-based policy.

The right move is to narrow the claim conceptually and broaden the relevance economically. The concept is **male-biased labor demand as a source of local gender inequality**. Shale is the clean empirical laboratory.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?

A few conversations feel underdeveloped or absent:

1. **Spatial equilibrium / local prices / boomtown adjustment**  
   If cost-of-living inflation is a central mechanism, this literature should be more visible in framing.

2. **Structural transformation and gender**  
   There is a wider literature on how sectoral composition shapes female labor force participation and gender gaps.

3. **Place-based policy and distributional incidence**  
   If the goal is to matter to policy economists, this connection should be stronger and earlier.

4. **Labor demand shocks by task/occupation and gender segregation**  
   The paper currently treats sex segregation as a stylized fact rather than connecting to the economics of occupational segregation.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?

Not quite yet. It is currently talking mainly to the shale/resource literature, with a side conversation with gender economics. The more impactful conversation would be:

> “What kinds of local growth reduce inequality, and what kinds widen it? Specifically, what happens when growth arrives in sectors that are inaccessible or unattractive to one group?”

That puts the paper in conversation with labor, urban/regional, gender, and policy economics simultaneously. That is a bigger and more AER-worthy conversation.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

The world before this paper: the shale boom is understood as a major positive local labor-demand shock; economists know mining is extremely male-dominated; the gender economics literature suggests relative labor demand can shape bargaining power and inequality.

### Tension

We know a lot about whether resource booms raise jobs and wages on average, but not who within the local labor market benefits. If the jobs go overwhelmingly to men, aggregate gains may conceal widening gender inequality—and it is unclear whether any such effects vanish when the boom ends.

### Resolution

The paper finds that more shale-exposed counties saw women’s non-mining employment and earnings fall relative to men’s trajectories, and that these relative losses persisted through the bust.

### Implications

Local growth is not distributionally neutral. Policymakers should think about the demographic incidence of industrial expansion, not just the number of jobs created. More broadly, the gender composition of labor demand may be a neglected determinant of local inequality.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the skeleton of one, but the current manuscript often reads like a collection of plausible mechanisms and reduced-form findings rather than one disciplined story.

The biggest narrative problem is that the paper cannot decide whether it is about:

- the shale boom,
- gendered labor-market incidence,
- sex ratios and marriage markets,
- Dutch disease,
- or persistent structural change after busts.

All are interesting; together they dilute the story.

### What story should it be telling?

The cleanest story is:

1. **Large local booms often create highly gender-skewed labor demand.**
2. **The shale boom is an extreme case that lets us study this cleanly.**
3. **When growth is this male-biased, women do not share proportionately in the local gains.**
4. **These effects persist, implying more than short-run crowd-out.**
5. **Therefore the composition of labor demand matters for the distributional consequences of local growth.**

That is a coherent arc. Marriage-market and violence references should be background color unless the paper actually tests them.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?

I would lead with something like:

> “In shale counties, one of the biggest labor-demand shocks in recent US history widened the local gender earnings gap rather than narrowing it.”

That is stronger than leading with Williams County sex ratios or with the 12 percent employment estimate. The gender-gap fact is more memorable and more conceptually central.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

Some would lean in—especially labor, urban, resource, and gender economists—but only if the framing is sharpened. In current form, many will hear “another fracking paper.” In improved form, they will hear “a paper about how the composition of labor demand shapes inequality.”

That difference matters enormously.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Likely:

1. **Through what mechanism?** Prices? migration? sectoral crowd-out? family formation?
2. **Is this really women losing, or men gaining more?**
3. **How general is this beyond shale?**

Those are exactly the questions the introduction should anticipate and structure the paper around.

### If the findings are modest, is the modesty still interesting?

Yes, but only if sold properly. The main estimates are not earth-shattering in absolute size, but the persistence result and the gender-gap framing give them bite. The paper should not oversell “roughneck externality” as a grand new law of economics. It should instead stress that even one of the clearest recent growth episodes produced uneven gains by gender—and that the unevenness endured.

That is interesting.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The current gap is mostly **framing plus ambition**, with some **scope** issues.

### Is it a framing problem?

Yes, primarily. The science may be competent, but the current story is not yet at the right level. The paper is written like a careful field-journal paper in resource/regional economics. For AER, it needs to sound like an answer to a broad economic question: **how the gender composition of labor demand shapes the incidence of local growth.**

### Is it a scope problem?

Also yes. “Non-mining outcomes” is broad in a way that is empirically convenient but intellectually blurry. To excite top people in labor/gender/regional, the paper needs a clearer anatomy of where the effects sit and why. The persistence angle helps, and sectoral decomposition or heterogeneity would help more.

### Is it a novelty problem?

Somewhat. The basic empirical design is familiar, and the setting is heavily studied. What rescues novelty is the conceptual lens: male-biased labor demand as a source of gendered spillovers. But that novelty must be asserted and demonstrated more clearly. Otherwise it will read as “shale, but by sex.”

### Is it an ambition problem?

Yes. The paper is competent but safe. It presents the main effect, a placebo, and policy implications, but does not quite reach for the broader conceptual payoff available in the design.

### Single most impactful advice

**Rewrite the paper around one big claim: local growth shocks are not gender neutral, and the shale boom shows that male-biased labor demand can durably widen gender inequality even in expanding economies.**

Everything else—setting, design, mechanisms, placebos—should serve that claim.
