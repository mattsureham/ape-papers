**Summary**

This paper provides the first causal estimate of Wales’ nationwide policy to make 20 mph the default urban speed limit by comparing quarterly STATS19 collision data across 22 Welsh and 329 English local authorities from 2019 to 2024. A simple TWFE DiD with local-authority and quarter fixed effects finds a sustained 17.9 log-point (≈16–22%) reduction in casualties on restricted roads in Wales relative to England, concentrated in slight injuries, and robust to various specifications. The results are positioned as evidence that default-setting alone can deliver population-level improvements in road safety.

**Essential Points**

1. **Parallel Trends and COVID-related Confounding.** The paper leans heavily on the parallel trends assumption, yet the event-study evidence is mentioned qualitatively but not shown, and pre-trends during 2019–2021 are arguably influenced by differential COVID lockdowns (which the paper itself notes). Because Wales implemented stricter lockdowns early on, aggregate casualty trends may have diverged before the treatment date, potentially biasing the TWFE estimate even after excluding the 2020–21 period. I recommend formally presenting the event-study coefficients (ideally with confidence bands) and, if possible, reweighting or modelling pre-treatment dynamics (e.g., via synthetic control or matching) to ensure that the DiD comparison rests on comparable trends rather than the absence of a visible break.

2. **Interpretation of the Log-linear Estimate and Reclassification.** While the manuscript rightly emphasizes the combined 20+30 mph outcome to avoid mechanical label effects, the substantial log-level changes reported in columns (1)–(2) of Table 3 suggest that post-treatment coding still plays a role; for example, the huge positive coefficient on 20 mph roads and large negative coefficient on 30 mph roads signal dramatic compositional shifts. It remains unclear how much of the estimated 17.9 log-point reduction stems from real casualty declines versus residual measurement artefacts, especially given imperfect compliance with the new default (some roads may remain at 30 mph by exception). Additional discussion (and, if possible, analysis using infrastructure-based measures of which roads were reclassified) is needed to convince readers that the outcome truly captures physical casualty reductions.

3. **Spillovers and the Choice of Control Group.** Border-area spillovers (Wales drivers encountering 30 mph England roads and vice versa) could contaminate the control group, which is effectively all non-Welsh LAs. The robustness check showing a larger estimate for border areas is interpreted as implying contamination, but this is counterintuitive: if border spillovers make England more similar to Wales, they should attenuate the estimates in the border sample relative to the full sample, not amplify them. The paper should clarify (i) whether the border sample consists of both English and Welsh LAs and how the estimate changes when only English border authorities are used, (ii) whether there is evidence of divergent trends within English control authorities (e.g., urban vs. rural) that could conflate with treatment timing, and (iii) whether alternative control groups (e.g., matched English LAs or synthetic control) yield similar magnitudes. Without this, confidence in the England counterfactual remains limited.

If these issues cannot be resolved satisfactorily, preliminary fears about identification connote rejection.

**Suggestions**

1. **Show the Event Study and Pre-trend Diagnostics.** Include the full event-study figure/table, covering both the 2020–21 COVID period and the 2022–2023 window used in robustness checks. Plot coefficients (with 95% wild-cluster bootstrap intervals) for pre- and post-treatment quarters to visually demonstrate the absence of pre-treatment jumps. If COVID introduces noise, consider using a synthetic pre-period (e.g., exclude 2020–2021 entirely or weight them less) and report how the pre-trend coefficients change. Complement this with a “leads-only” regression (Wales × quarter indicators for pre-period) to test for anticipatory effects.

2. **Strengthen the Reclassification Analysis.** The mechanical relabeling is well explained, but the main specification could be supplemented with an alternative strategy that uses an external map of reclassified roads. For instance, if Transport for Wales publishes GIS shapefiles of restricted roads before and after the reform, the author could calculate the share of each LA’s restricted road network that was reclassified and interact that with the treatment indicator. This would allow regression models that explicitly distinguish between reclassified and never-reclassified roads, reinforcing the interpretation that the casualty decline occurs on physically affected roadways, not just in the data labels.

3. **Address Spillovers More Directly.** To help convince readers that English controls remain valid, exploit spatial variation: restrict the sample to Welsh LAs versus English LAs that are geographically distant from Wales (e.g., the Midlands and North) and see whether the estimate persists. Alternatively, construct a synthetic control for Wales using a weighted combination of English LAs (perhaps leveraging the already aggregated Welsh result) and compare the results. These exercises would both serve as placebo/support on the counterfactual and make the case that the estimated effect is not driven by Wales-specific shocks that also affected some English LAs.

4. **Elaborate on the “Default” Mechanism.** While the paper argues that the default change caused the casualty reduction, it would help to link this narrative to data: Are there metrics indicating how many roads remained at 30 mph post-treatment (exceptions)? Do local authorities that filed more exemption orders show smaller reductions? Including a table or discussion of compliance (e.g., % of road miles still signed 30 mph by quarter) would show that the default had bite and that the observed effect is not just statutory. If compliance data is unavailable, consider surveying policy documents or contacting Transport for Wales for aggregate estimates.

5. **Expand on Severity and Reporting Concerns.** The severity breakdown is intriguing; consider extending it by exploring vehicle types (pedestrian vs. car occupant crashes) or times of day, which could shed light on whether the policy primarily affected residential streets. Additionally, provide more evidence that reporting practices did not change differentially in Wales—for example, show that the total number of reported collisions (not just casualties) behaves similarly to the casualty measure, or examine police staffing numbers to rule out administrative changes coinciding with the policy.

6. **Clarify the Wild Cluster Bootstrap Implementation.** The paper mentions WCB with Webb weights and 9,999 replications but only reports the main bootstrap p-value for the log specification. To aid reproducibility, include a note on whether the bootstrap is two-sided and whether p-values for other specifications are above or below standard thresholds. If space allows, provide a small appendix table with bootstrap confidence intervals for all key coefficients.

7. **Discuss External Validity and Long-Run Effects Cautiously.** The discussion rightly emphasizes the policy relevance but could more explicitly note that Wales is a relatively small, semi-urban country; the effect sizes may differ in denser cities or in countries with different enforcement capacity. Similarly, since only five post-treatment quarters are available, caution against over-interpretation about long-term persistence; consider plotting the quarterly treatment effect (maybe from an interaction with a “post” indicator split into early vs. later quarters) to assess whether the effect is stable or evolving.

By addressing these suggestions, the paper would strengthen its empirical credibility (via more transparent identification checks and controls) and its policy resonance (via mechanism evidence and nuanced interpretations), making it a valuable contribution to the road safety regulation literature.
