## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple, policy-relevant question: when a government lowers the default urban speed limit from 30 mph to 20 mph, does that actually reduce road casualties at population scale? Using Wales’s 2023 nationwide reform and England as a comparison, the paper argues that changing the default reduced reported casualties on affected roads by roughly 16–22 percent, mostly through fewer slight injuries.

A busy economist should care because this is not just a transport paper; it is about whether low-cost regulatory defaults can meaningfully change real-world risk. The broader claim is that changing the legal baseline—not just targeted enforcement or infrastructure—can alter behavior and social harm.

### Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Reasonably well, but not as sharply as it could. The current introduction opens with biomechanics, then moves to the policy. That is fine, but the real hook is not “pedestrians are safer at lower speeds”—everyone already believes that. The hook is: **we still do not know whether changing the default speed limit at scale actually changes casualties, because most prior evidence is from selected zones or before-after comparisons. Wales provides a rare population-wide test.**

The introduction should get to that immediately. Right now it slightly oversells “most sweeping in European history” and slightly undersells the general-interest question: do defaults matter in public safety regulation?

### The pitch the paper should have

Governments around the world are lowering urban speed limits, but we have little credible evidence on whether changing the **default** speed limit—rather than creating selected low-speed zones—actually reduces road injuries at scale. Wales’s 2023 switch from a 30 mph to 20 mph default on urban restricted roads, while England kept the old default, provides a rare natural experiment; the paper finds a sizable decline in casualties on affected roads, concentrated in slight injuries.

More broadly, the paper is about whether a regulatory default can produce large population-level safety gains without new infrastructure or intensive enforcement.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper provides evidence from Wales’s nationwide 20 mph default that changing the legal default speed on urban roads can substantially reduce reported road casualties at population scale.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?

Partly, but not yet strongly enough. The paper does distinguish itself from before-after studies of 20 mph zones and from descriptive monitoring reports. That helps. But the differentiation is still framed too much as “first causal estimate” and not enough as “different object of study.”

The distinctive object here is not just a better design. It is:
- **nationwide default change**, not selected traffic-calmed zones;
- **population-level effects**, not within-zone effects;
- **default rule versus case-by-case regulation**.

That needs to be the core differentiation. Otherwise the paper risks sounding like “yet another paper showing lower speeds may reduce injuries.”

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a gap in a literature?

Mostly the former, which is good, but it still slips into gap-filling language (“first causal estimate,” “credible counterfactual”). The stronger framing is a world question:

- When governments change the default speed on urban roads, do injuries fall?
- Are broad default changes effective where piecemeal local interventions have had limited reach?
- How much of road safety can be moved by default legal rules alone?

That is stronger than “the literature lacks a causal estimate.”

### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what’s new?

Yes, but with some risk. In its current form, a smart economist might say: “It’s a DiD on Wales’s 20 mph reform showing fewer casualties.” That is competent, but not memorable.

What you want them to say is: “It shows that **changing the regulatory default** on urban speed limits can generate sizable safety gains at national scale, which is different from the evidence on selected 20 mph zones.”

That requires tightening the intro and pruning some of the methodological throat-clearing.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Most importantly: **elevate the paper from ‘speed limits affect casualties’ to ‘defaults in regulation matter for public safety.’**

Substantively, the paper would feel bigger if it did one or more of the following:

1. **Measure exposure-adjusted effects**
   - Right now the outcome is casualties. The natural follow-up is: did casualties fall because speeds fell, because traffic volumes fell, or because route choice changed?
   - If the paper can speak to traffic volume or vehicle miles traveled, it becomes much more informative.

2. **Say more about welfare-relevant composition**
   - The result is concentrated in slight injuries. That is still important, but it narrows the welfare punch.

3. **Develop the “default versus local choice” comparison**
   - The most interesting claim is that changing the default accomplished what discretionary, zone-by-zone approaches did not.
   - If the paper can compare Welsh outcomes to places with prior local 20 mph adoption, or show why default rules expand coverage relative to opt-in regulation, the contribution becomes much bigger.

4. **Connect to a broader class of legal defaults**

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Cities and governments increasingly want slower urban traffic, but evidence is mostly based on selected low-speed zones or before-after comparisons. We know impact speed matters biomechanically, but we do not know whether changing the default legal speed limit across an entire jurisdiction actually changes casualties.

### Tension

A nationwide default change is politically salient and potentially cheap, but it might not do much in practice. Drivers may ignore it, local authorities may exempt major roads, traffic may divert, and measured safety gains may be smaller than proponents claim. So the key puzzle is whether a broad legal default meaningfully changes injury outcomes at scale.

### Resolution

In Wales, the switch from a 30 mph to a 20 mph default appears to reduce casualties on affected roads by roughly one-fifth relative to England, with the decline concentrated in slight injuries and little corresponding change on roads not directly affected by the reform.

### Implications

Default rules may be an effective policy lever in road safety. More broadly, regulatory defaults can matter even in domains where compliance is imperfect and enforcement is limited.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has one, but only in embryo. The ingredients are there; the current draft too often reads like a competent empirical paper assembled around tables rather than a paper with a strong storyline.

The biggest narrative problem is that the paper has **two possible stories** and has not fully chosen between them:

1. **Road safety story**: lower default speed limits reduce casualties.
2. **Regulatory defaults story**: opt-out legal design can produce large population-level behavioral change.

The first story is true but narrower and less novel. The second story is broader and more AER-relevant, but the paper only gestures at it in the discussion.

### What story should it be telling?

It should tell this story:

- Policymakers often rely on default rules because individualized regulation is administratively costly.
- In road safety, urban 20 mph zones have usually been adopted piecemeal, making evidence hard to interpret and scaling hard to achieve.
- Wales flipped the default.
- The flip appears to have reduced injuries at scale.
- Therefore, the architecture of legal defaults—not just the substantive rule—matters for policy effectiveness.

That is a coherent narrative. Right now the draft keeps reverting to “here are my estimates.”

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?

“Wales cut the default urban speed limit from 30 to 20 mph, and reported casualties on affected roads fell by about one-fifth relative to England within a year.”

That is a decent lead.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

Some would lean in, but mostly field economists and applied micro people with safety/public policy interests. The average economist will not automatically care unless the framing immediately broadens beyond road safety.

If instead you say:

“Changing a legal default—without new infrastructure—cut urban road casualties by around 20 percent,”

more people lean in.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Almost certainly one of these:
- “Is that fewer crashes, or less driving / route substitution?”
- “Are the welfare-relevant serious injuries also falling, or is this mostly minor incidents?”
- “Is this really about speed, or about compliance and salience?”
- “What does this tell us about defaults more generally?”

Those are strategic clues. The paper’s audience will want help interpreting mechanism and generality.

### If the findings are modest: is the modesty interesting?

The findings are not null, but they are more modest than the rhetoric of “the default kill?” might suggest. The concentration in slight injuries is politically relevant but somewhat limits the punch. AER readers will naturally ask: if the main gains are in slight injuries, is the first-order welfare effect big enough to matter?

The paper partly addresses this with a cost-benefit back-of-envelope, which is useful. But it should not oversell. The right message is not “20 mph defaults save many lives”; the paper does not show that clearly. The right message is “20 mph defaults reduce total casualty incidence, mostly by preventing less severe injuries, and likely do so cost-effectively.”

That is still worthwhile, but it must be framed honestly.
