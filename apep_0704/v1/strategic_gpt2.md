## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether paid sick leave mandates reduce worker turnover by making it less necessary for workers to quit when illness or caregiving conflicts arise. Using LEHD worker-flow data and staggered state adoption of paid sick leave laws, it argues that the main labor-market effect is not on employment levels overall but on retention, especially for young retail workers.

A busy economist should care because paid leave mandates are usually debated as labor-cost regulations with possible employment effects; this paper wants to reframe them as affecting labor-market frictions and worker flows. If true, that is a more interesting and policy-relevant margin than yet another employment-level estimate.

**Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Not quite. The introduction gets to the question fairly quickly, but it opens with generic turnover facts and then jumps into details of sectors and design before firmly establishing the big economic question. The reader should understand sooner that the paper is intervening in a broad debate about **what labor mandates do in equilibrium**: destroy jobs, do nothing, or improve matches by reducing inefficient separations.

### The pitch the paper should have

“Paid sick leave mandates are typically evaluated as labor-cost regulations: do they reduce employment or raise labor costs? But an equally important question is whether they reduce inefficient worker separations by allowing workers to absorb short health shocks without quitting or being fired. Using Census LEHD worker-flow data and staggered state paid sick leave mandates, this paper shows that the main effect is on retention rather than aggregate employment, with especially large reductions in separations among young workers in retail, where pre-mandate sick leave coverage was limited but formal employer policies can plausibly bind.”

That is the AER version of the pitch. It leads with the economic question, not the institutional detail.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:**  
The paper’s contribution is to show that paid sick leave mandates appear to reduce worker separations on the labor-flow margin, with effects concentrated among young workers in retail rather than broadly across low-coverage sectors.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest papers?
Only partially. The paper says prior work studies health outcomes and aggregate employment, while it studies flows. That is a start, but the differentiation is still too mechanical: “first administrative-data paper on worker flows” is not enough by itself. The introduction needs to say more sharply what existing papers would lead us to expect and why this paper could overturn or refine that expectation.

Right now, the contribution risks sounding like:
- another staggered-adoption policy paper,
- in another state-labor-mandate setting,
- using a slightly different outcome variable.

That is not enough for AER unless the outcome variable changes the interpretation of the policy in a first-order way.

### World question or literature gap?
The paper is trying to answer a **world question**, which is good: *Do paid sick leave mandates reduce turnover by improving retention?*  
But it keeps slipping into literature-gap framing: *first evidence on labor market flow effects using administrative data*. That weakens it. AER papers should be framed around a substantive question about behavior or markets, not a missing cell in a literature table.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the intro?
At present, they could probably say:  
“It’s a DiD/DDD paper on paid sick leave mandates using LEHD that finds little on average, but some reduction in retail separations and especially among young workers.”

That is better than nothing, but it is still perilously close to “another DiD paper about X.” The novelty is not yet distilled into a clean claim about how labor mandates operate.

### What would make the contribution bigger?
Several possibilities:

1. **Stronger framing around labor-market adjustment margins.**  
   The paper should explicitly pose the question as: when mandated benefits bite, do they operate through employment levels, wages, absenteeism, or worker flows? That is a bigger conversation.

2. **Make retail central from the start rather than a decomposition discovered later.**  
   Right now the headline average effect is null-ish and the interesting result is a subgroup. If retail is the economically coherent treated margin, build the paper around why retail is the natural place to look.

3. **Tie the age result to a broader mechanism.**  
   The age gradient is the most attention-grabbing fact. But the paper does not fully elevate it into a general point about attachment, outside options, and the incidence of mandated benefits. If it could show that the policy matters most where separations are most likely to be illness-triggered and least costly to replace, the paper becomes more general.

4. **Connect to firm-side economics, not just worker-side vulnerability.**  
   “Retention dividend” is an attractive phrase, but the paper should lean more into whether this changes the perceived cost of the mandate. That is a bigger implication than merely documenting one more positive effect.

Potentially also:
6. **Summers (1989)** / mandated benefits framing.
7. **Baicker, Chandra, Goodman-Bacon-style public/health labor policy papers**, depending on where they want to position the empirical contribution.

### How should the paper position itself relative to those neighbors?
Mostly **build on and reinterpret**, not attack.

- Relative to the paid sick leave literature: “You have studied health and employment levels; I show the missing margin is worker flows.”
- Relative to mandated-benefits incidence: “The relevant distortion/adjustment margin is not just wages or employment but separations.”
- Relative to employment protection literature: “Even a modest mandate can matter by stabilizing short-duration matches among low-attachment workers.”

What it should **not** do is overstate “first evidence” and pretend the literature has ignored labor-market implications altogether. The better claim is that the existing literature has focused on the wrong aggregate margin.

### Too narrow or too broad?
At present, the paper is oddly both.

- **Too narrow** in the empirical setup: it becomes a five-industry DDD with one standout sector.
- **Too broad** in the claims: it sometimes speaks as if it has learned a general lesson about employment mandates.

The right level is: a paper about **how a common labor standard affects labor-market churn**, illustrated in a setting where the policy plausibly binds heterogeneously by sector and age.

### What literature does it seem unaware of?
A few gaps in positioning:

1. **Search and matching / labor-market frictions literature.**  
   If the paper is about separations and retention, it should speak more explicitly to this literature. That would elevate the contribution above policy evaluation.

2. **Benefits and job quality literature.**  
   There is a broader conversation on nonwage compensation, schedule flexibility, and low-wage job quality. This paper belongs there.

3. **The quits literature post-COVID / worker reallocation.**  
   Even if not central, there is now a larger macro-labor conversation about churn and quits. This could help the “so what.”

4. **Human resources / personnel economics.**  
   The retail-vs-hospitality distinction is really about formalization of HR policies and contractibility. That is a deeper mechanism than the paper currently acknowledges.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?
Not quite. It is currently in the “state policy mandate effects” conversation. That is a crowded and usually second-tier framing. The more impactful conversation is:

**How do labor standards affect the composition of labor-market adjustment?**

That is a stronger conversation, and one that could make the paper matter beyond the PSL niche.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Before this paper, economists think of paid sick leave mandates mainly as either:
- a public health intervention,
- a mandated-benefit cost shock,
- or an employment policy whose effects might show up in levels, wages, or hours.

### Tension
The tension is that these mandates may have little visible effect on aggregate employment while still meaningfully changing labor-market dynamics by preventing avoidable quits/separations. Existing work has not really pinned down whether retention is the relevant margin.

### Resolution
The paper’s resolution is: on average, effects are modest, but paid sick leave mandates reduce separations in retail and especially among the youngest workers. So the policy’s labor-market effect is targeted, not broad-based.

### Implications
The implication is that labor mandates may improve match stability for specific workers and sectors, even when average employment effects are muted. That changes both welfare interpretation and predictions about incidence.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
It has the ingredients, but not a fully disciplined arc. Right now it feels like:

- broad setup on turnover,
- average effect is small,
- then a set of heterogeneous results,
- then a discussion trying to rationalize why only retail matters.

That is slightly backward. The reader is told the average is modest and imprecise before being given a compelling reason to care about where effects should be.

### What story should it be telling?
The story should be:

1. **Mandates can alter labor-market churn, not just levels.**
2. **They should matter where three conditions hold simultaneously: low initial coverage, formal enforcement, and high baseline mobility.**
3. **Retail and young workers sit exactly at that intersection.**
4. **The evidence lines up with that prediction.**

That is a real story. The current draft instead sounds like the author estimated a broad model, got a weak average, and then found an interesting subgroup. AER readers will smell that immediately unless the ex ante logic is made much more explicit.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party?
“Paid sick leave mandates don’t seem to move employment much, but they do reduce turnover among young retail workers quite a bit.”

That is the hook.

### Would people lean in?
Some would, but not all. The phrase “young retail workers” is more compelling than the average treatment effect. The average result alone would not hold attention. The heterogeneity is the only thing making this paper strategically interesting.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Almost certainly:
- “Why retail and not restaurants?”
- Then: “Is this really about quits?”
- And then: “Why should I care if the average effect is basically zero?”

Those are exactly the questions the paper needs to answer more powerfully in the introduction.

### If findings are modest or null, is the null interesting?
The paper tries to make the nulls informative, and that is the right instinct. The accommodation/food null is potentially interesting because it cuts against the simple “lowest coverage means biggest effect” prediction. But the paper has not fully earned that interpretation yet in narrative terms. Right now it reads a bit post hoc.

The null average effect can be interesting **if** the paper convincingly argues that aggregate estimates conceal binding effects only in the part of the labor market where formal rights actually change behavior. Without that argument, it feels like a failed average-effect paper rescued by subgroup analysis.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

Honest answer: the gap is mostly **framing plus ambition**, with some **novelty risk**.

### Framing problem?
Yes, definitely. The paper has a better idea than its current framing reveals. “Paid sick leave mandates and the retention dividend” is promising, but the paper does not yet fully own the bigger question: **what margin of adjustment matters for labor standards?**

### Scope problem?
Moderately. The paper leans heavily on one sector and one demographic pattern. That can still work, but then the paper must make the mechanism and external significance feel deeper. Otherwise it risks feeling too narrow.

### Novelty problem?
Somewhat. State labor-mandate papers are common. Paid sick leave is not a fresh policy topic. Using LEHD worker flows is useful, but not by itself a top-journal innovation. The paper needs to persuade the reader that it changes how we think about mandated benefits, not just what we know about PSL.

### Ambition problem?
Yes. The paper is competent but safe. It is content to say “first evidence on flows.” That is not ambitious enough for AER. The ambitious version says:

“We have been evaluating labor mandates on the wrong outcome. The first-order adjustment margin here is match stability, and aggregate employment estimates miss it.”

That is an AER-worthy claim if the paper can carry it.

### Single most impactful piece of advice
**Reframe the paper as evidence on the labor-market adjustment margin of mandated benefits—using paid sick leave as the setting—rather than as a policy evaluation of paid sick leave mandates per se.**

That one change would make the paper feel less niche, less incremental, and more like a contribution to core labor economics.
