**Summary**

The paper constructs a novel firm-year panel of publicly traded companies by linking Bloomberg’s FOIA-obtained H-1B lottery data to SEC financial filings via EINs, yielding 848 firms with non-missing R&D. Exploiting the pure randomization of the lottery, it regresses post-lottery firm outcomes on the firm-level win rate (conditional on registrations) and finds a precisely estimated null effect on R&D spending across specifications, industries, and horizons. The authors interpret the null as evidence that large publicly traded firms insulate innovation budgets from random high-skilled immigration shortages, shifting the welfare concern toward affected workers.

**Essential Points**

1. **Treatment Variation Precision and Weighted Estimation.** The win rate variable carries heteroskedastic measurement error because firms with few registrations face wild Binomial noise, while large registrants have win rates that concentrate near the mean. Although \cref{eq:main} includes log(Reg.) as a control, the regression still treats each firm-year equally. This equal-weighting gives undue influence to small firms whose measured win rates are mostly noise, potentially attenuating the treatment coefficient toward zero. The authors should report results that weight each observation by the precision of the win-rate estimate (e.g., inverse of Var(WinRate|N) = 1/(p(1−p)/N)) or, alternatively, instrument for the win rate with the count of selections (count data naturally reflect the Binomial hierarchy). Without this, it is difficult to interpret the null as the effect of a “true” win probability rather than the average over a noisy proxy.

2. **Control for Pre-Existing Trends Beyond R&D Placebo.** While the pre-lottery R&D placebo is reassuring, the significance of the win rate in the pre-lottery revenue regression suggests an imperfect balance between randomization and firm size—large firms submit many registrations, giving them more precise win rates, while smaller firms inherit higher variance. Because R&D intensity and R&D expenditure grow with firm size, differential pre-trends or measurement error correlated with size could still contaminate the main specification, especially when the sample mixes firms of widely different scales. The authors should present additional balance evidence (e.g., regressions of lagged R&D intensity, capital expenditure, or firm fixed effects on the win rate) and/or use a matching or stratification approach (e.g., bin firms by registration count and run within-bin regressions) to demonstrate that the null effect is not driven by residual size-related confounding.

3. **Interpretation of the Null and Power Calculations.** Claiming a policy-relevant null requires a precise sense of the minimum detectable effect and its economic magnitude. Although a 95% CI of [−0.41, 0.56] is reported for the log R&D coefficient, presenting this range without comparing it to, say, the standard deviation of log R&D or existing elasticity estimates leaves readers unsure whether the study is powered to detect plausible effects. The authors should report (a) the implied effect on dollars (e.g., a 10 percentage point increase in win rate changes R&D by $X$ millions) and (b) the detectable effect size at conventional power (e.g., compute MDE at 80% power), ideally benchmarked against effects found in prior H-1B / innovation studies. Without that, it is hard to distinguish a “true null” from a lack of statistical power (especially since the sample is only 587 firm-year observations for R&D).

**Suggestions**

1. **Explicitly Model the Stochastic Treatment.** Given the Binomial nature of the data, the authors could estimate a structural or “Bartlett-type” regression that acknowledges that each firm’s realized win rate is sampled from a Binomial process with known denominator $N$. One option is to run a generalized linear model where the dependent variable is the count of selections and the offset is log $N$, using a firm-specific random effect to capture firm demand, so that the reduced-form effect on outcomes can be interpreted as a derivative with respect to the latent selection probability. Alternatively, instrument the win rate with the raw number of selections (or the expected variance from the Binomial) to obtain consistent estimates of the causal effect of the true win probability. Presenting such an approach would bolster confidence that the reduced-form estimate reflects the exogenous lottery rather than sampling noise.

2. **Stratified or Binned Estimation.** To address concerns about size-related imbalances, the authors could stratify the sample by registration count (e.g., 5–9, 10–24, 25+) and estimate the regression within each stratum, weighting uniformly within strata or pooling with interactions. If the null holds across bins, it would alleviate worries that the result arises from the dominance of small firms with noisy win rates. They could also show that coefficients from a weighted regression (with weights proportional to $N$ or inversely proportional to the estimated variance of win rate) coincide with the baseline.

3. **Explore a Binary Treatment Variation.** As a complement to the continuous win-rate measure, the authors might define a binary treatment—e.g., “low win rate” vs. “high win rate” determined by whether a firm’s win rate is above the sample median conditional on $N$—and estimate the reduced form for this indicator. This discretization may align better with policy debates (firms either “won” or “lost” a meaningful share of their slots) and would reduce sensitivity to measurement noise. Reporting both continuous and binary specifications would reassure readers that the null is robust to the metric of H-1B access.

4. **Dynamic and Mechanism Evidence.** The discussion speculates about substitution through OPT, L-1, or domestic hires, but the paper lacks direct evidence. Even if these margins are outside the data, the authors could examine whether win rates affect the growth rate of total employees or R&D headcounts (to the extent they can be proxied by SEC filings) or whether they shift capital allocation from onshore R&D to offshore operations (through changes in PP&E or foreign asset shares). Additionally, plotting the trajectory of R&D before and after the lottery (e.g., event-study/fixed-effects models) would help readers visualize the absence of dynamic effects.

5. **Clarify Sample Composition and External Validity.** The analysis is confined to firms that are both large enough to file with the SEC and submit at least five H-1B registrations; 848 matched firms are an impressive achievement, but the paper should discuss how representative they are of the H-1B applicant universe. Are these mostly Big Tech giants, IT consulting firms, or a broader mix? A table reporting firm size distribution (e.g., deciles of revenue or market cap) alongside the share of total lottery registrations would help readers gauge the extent to which the results generalize beyond the public, high-R&D segment.

6. **Robustness to Alternative Outcome Definitions.** The authors already explore R&D/Revenue and PP&E, but given the policy framing around innovation, it would be helpful to examine patenting outcomes (if available) or proxies such as intangible assets or acquisition spending. Even if comprehensive patent data are unavailable within the SEC sample, publicly traded firms often disclose “R&D headcount” or “research scientists” in 10-K narratives—natural language processing or even simple keyword counts (if feasible) could offer complementary evidence that the composition of R&D labor changes without affecting aggregate expenditure.

7. **Discuss Implications for Smaller Firms.** The conclusion notes that startups may behave differently, but the analysis does not directly speak to them. The authors might note that the randomization strategy could, in principle, be applied to smaller employers if their financials (e.g., from private disclosures or trade registers) could be matched, or they could compare the matched sample to the universe of lottery participants (if Bloomberg data include firm size metrics) to show how much of the variation is captured. This would help contextualize the policy inference that “costs fall primarily on workers.”

8. **Enhance Transparency of Data Construction.** The paper states that 1,475 firms have matching EINs and 848 have financial data, but the construction of these subsets warrants more detail. For reproducibility, the authors should provide (perhaps in the appendix) the exact steps used to fetch EINs from the SEC API, any cleaning rules for EIN mismatches (e.g., leading zeros, reissued EINs), and a list of excluded firms due to missing data. Sharing code or pseudocode for the merging process would align with the manifest’s promise of full public replicability.

In sum, the paper presents a compelling data linkage and a clear identification strategy, but addressing the above issues—especially the treatment measurement precision, balance beyond R&D, and null-power clarification—will substantially strengthen the empirical claims and policy relevance.
