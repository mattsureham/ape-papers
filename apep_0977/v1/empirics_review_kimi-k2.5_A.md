 **Referee Report: "Selective Revenge: Product-Heterogeneous Trade Hysteresis from South Korea's 2019 Anti-Japan Boycott"**

**1. Idea Fidelity**

The paper departs significantly from the original research design outlined in the manifest. Most critically, the manifest proposed to test whether **recovery** diverged by substitutability (using Rauch 1999 classifications), predicting that homogeneous goods (beverages) would recover while differentiated goods (vehicles) would show persistent loss. The paper instead finds **uniform persistence** across all consumer goods and pivots to a signaling/visibility mechanism. This is a different research question than promised. 

Furthermore, the empirical results contradict the manifest’s own smoke-test data, which explicitly noted that beverages had fully recovered by 2023 (\$108M vs. \$110M in 2018). Table 1 in the paper instead shows no recovery (-0.506 log points in "Late Post" 2021–2023 vs. -0.561 in early post). The paper cannot simply ignore its own pre-registered data patterns without explanation. Finally, the analysis of the "cosmetics anomaly" is present but underdeveloped—the manifest suggested this revealed "strategic selectivity," but the paper treats it primarily as evidence for the visibility mechanism without addressing alternative explanations (e.g., duty-free tourism channels).

**2. Summary**

This paper exploits the 2019 Korea-Japan diplomatic dispute and subsequent grassroots boycott to estimate the causal effect of consumer nationalism on trade flows using a triple-difference design (consumer vs. industrial goods $\times$ Korea vs. China $\times$ pre/post July 2019). The key contribution is documenting persistent trade hysteresis—consumer goods exports remained 41% below trend four years post-boycott—and attributing this to social visibility rather than substitution elasticities, suggesting that boycotts function as signaling devices targeting publicly consumed goods.

**3. Essential Points**

*China as Control Group:* The validity of the triple-difference hinges on the assumption that consumer-industrial trade differential trends between Korea and China would have been parallel absent the boycott. This is questionable. China experienced concurrent trade war shocks with the U.S., different COVID trajectories, and divergent macroeconomic trends. Korea and China also have fundamentally different trade relationships with Japan (e.g., Korea is a hub for Japanese intermediate goods in semiconductor supply chains; China is a final demand market). The paper mentions the destination-month FE absorbs macro shocks, but this does not address differential trends specific to consumer goods. The event study (referenced but not shown) must demonstrate convincingly that pre-trends were flat, not merely that coefficients were insignificant.

*Data Anomalies and Aggregation:* Table 2 reports product-specific boycott effects with standard errors of exactly (0.000) for all eight HS2 categories. This indicates a critical coding error—likely perfect multicollinearity or separation in the product-specific DiDs where the treatment perfectly predicts zero trade in some cells. These estimates are not credible in their current form. Additionally, HS2 classification is extremely coarse for distinguishing "consumer" from "industrial" goods (e.g., HS 87 "Vehicles" includes both passenger cars and industrial trucks; HS 33 includes both cosmetics and industrial essential oils). This measurement error likely biases the DDD estimates toward zero and obscures heterogeneity.

*Persistence vs. Recovery:* The paper interprets the similarity between "Early Post" (-0.561) and "Late Post" (-0.506) as evidence of hysteresis, but this conflates level persistence with structural break persistence. The manifest promised to test recovery by substitutability—specifically that homogeneous goods would recover as consumers switched back to low-cost suppliers. The paper abandons this framework without explanation. If beverages truly recovered (as per the manifest’s smoke test) while vehicles did not, pooling them in Table 1 Column (2) masks crucial heterogeneity. The authors must reconcile their aggregate finding of uniform persistence with the manifest’s preliminary evidence of differential recovery.

**4. Suggestions**

*Empirical Implementation:* (1) **Show the event study figure.** AER: Insights requires visual evidence of parallel pre-trends and sharp post-treatment breaks. Referencing an equation without displaying the event study coefficients undermines credibility. (2) **Fix Table 2.** The zero standard errors suggest separation or collinearity. If using product-specific DiDs, some products likely had zero trade in certain months; consider Poisson pseudo-MLE or dropping zero-trade product-destination pairs rather than OLS on logs. (3) **Disaggregate to HS4 or HS6.** HS2 categories like "Electrical machinery" (HS 85) or "Vehicles" (HS 87) are hopelessly aggregated. Finer disaggregation would allow cleaner consumer/industrial classification and reveal within-HS2 heterogeneity (e.g., passenger cars vs. auto parts).

*Identification Strategy:* (4) **Justify China more rigorously.** Test for pre-trends using a linear interaction (Consumer $\times$ Korea $\times$ linear time trend) and report the coefficient prominently, not buried in robustness. Consider placebo tests using other Northeast Asian destinations (Taiwan, Hong Kong) if data permit. (5) **Address COVID and tourism.** The boycott coincided with a collapse in Korea-Japan tourism (Korean Air bookings -40%). Cosmetics are heavily sold in duty-free shops to tourists, not just consumed privately at home. The paper should test whether the cosmetics "anomaly" reflects the tourism channel (complementarity with travel) rather than pure visibility. Include tourist arrivals or airline passenger data as controls.

*Mechanism and Theory:* (6) **Reconcile with substitutability.** The manifest’s original contribution was testing Baldwin (1988) by linking hysteresis to sunk costs/switching barriers. The visibility mechanism is interesting but orthogonal to sunk costs. If the paper pursues signaling, add a simple model where utility depends on public image, generating predictions about which goods are boycotted. Then test substitutability (Rauch) *within* visibility categories—if signaling dominates, visibility effects should hold regardless of differentiation. (7) **Test for domestic substitution.** The hysteresis claim implies Korean imports of non-Japanese beverages/cars increased persistently. Use Korean import data from other partners (EU, US) to show diversion, not just Japanese export collapse. This would strengthen the interpretation that habits switched, not just that Japanese brands stagnated.

*Presentation:* (8) **Clarify the time horizon.** "Late Post" (2021–2023) includes the global supply chain crunch and ongoing geopolitical tensions. The abstract’s claim that "diplomatic tensions subsided" by 2023 is inaccurate—Japan maintained whitelist restrictions until 2023, and bilateral relations remained frosty. Avoid overstating the "permanent" nature of trade diversion based on 4.5 years of data when canonical hysteresis studies examine decades. (9) **Report product-specific recovery patterns.** Even if the main table pools early/late post, show in an appendix figure whether Beverages (HS 22) actually recovered while Vehicles (HS 87) did not, as promised in the manifest. This would demonstrate fidelity to the original research design and provide a more nuanced policy implication: hysteresis is conditional on product characteristics, not universal.

*Minor:* (10) **Check units in Table 2.** The coefficient for HS 94 (furniture) is positive (0.037) with three stars, suggesting furniture exports *increased* during the boycott, which contradicts the narrative. Verify coding. (11) **Standardize visibility classification.** The visibility coding appears post-hoc (splitting sample based on results). Pre-register or justify based on existing literature (e.g., conspicuous consumption indices).
