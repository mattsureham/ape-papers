## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper studies the 2019 South Korean consumer boycott of Japanese goods after Japan tightened export controls, asking whether consumer nationalism can materially and persistently reshape international trade. The headline claim is that the boycott was not only large and long-lasting, but highly selective: publicly visible goods were hit much harder than privately consumed goods, suggesting that boycotts operate partly as social signaling rather than pure substitution.

A busy economist should care because this is potentially a paper about when political identity enters consumer demand strongly enough to redirect trade for years, even without formal trade barriers. If true, that connects international trade, political economy, and social interactions in a way that is broader than “one boycott episode.”

### Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Reasonably well, but not optimally. The current opening is vivid and readable, but it still sounds a bit like “interesting case study of a boycott” rather than “general lesson about demand-side trade shocks and identity-driven consumption.” The first two paragraphs foreground the episode and a product anomaly, but they do not quite elevate the paper fast enough to the level of a general economics question.

### What the first two paragraphs should say instead

The introduction should open with the world question, not the event chronology:

> Can consumer nationalism create trade barriers that persist long after the political shock that triggered them? Standard trade policy focuses on tariffs, export controls, and sanctions imposed by governments, but many modern trade disruptions are mediated by consumers themselves. Whether such demand-side shocks are fleeting symbolic gestures or durable sources of trade reallocation is an important and largely unresolved question.
>
> This paper studies the 2019 South Korean boycott of Japanese goods, a sharp and highly salient episode of voluntary consumer retaliation. I show that the boycott caused a large and persistent decline in Japanese consumer exports to Korea, and that the effect was concentrated in publicly visible goods rather than privately consumed goods. The pattern suggests that politically motivated boycotts work not just through substitution toward alternatives, but through social signaling: consumers are most willing to incur costs when noncompliance is observable.

That is the pitch the paper wants. Right now it is close, but still too event-first and not enough question-first.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper argues that a voluntary consumer boycott can generate large, persistent trade diversion, with the strongest effects in socially visible product categories, implying that political consumption affects trade through identity signaling as well as substitution.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest papers?

Only partially. The paper distinguishes itself from descriptive boycott studies and from canonical hysteresis models, but the differentiation is still not sharp enough. The reader can tell there is an empirical niche here—product-level evidence on one boycott episode—but it is less clear why this is a distinctly new conceptual contribution rather than “another quasi-experimental paper on a trade shock with heterogeneous effects.”

The key problem is that the paper is making three claims at once:

1. consumer boycotts matter for trade,
2. their effects persist,
3. product heterogeneity reveals signaling.

Any one of these could be the paper’s central contribution, but the introduction currently treats all three as coequal. That blurs the novelty. My instinct is that the strongest contribution is **not** “first econometric analysis of this boycott.” That is too local. The strongest contribution is: **consumer-driven trade shocks can be persistent, and the cross-product pattern suggests observability is central to their force.**

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a gap in a literature?

Mixed, but too much gap-filling language. Phrases like “first product-level econometric analysis” and “extends the largely descriptive literature” are weaker than a world-facing framing. AER papers generally do better when they answer a substantive question about how the world works. Here, that question is something like: **When political conflict spills into markets, which consumer choices change, and do those changes last?**

### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what’s new?

They could probably say: “It’s about the Korea-Japan boycott; consumer imports fell a lot; visible goods fell more; maybe this is signaling.” That is decent. But there is still a risk they would summarize it as: “a DiD/DDD paper on a boycott episode.” That is not enough.

The novelty currently lands as empirical rather than conceptual. For AER, it needs to land as conceptual with the episode serving as a clean test.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Be more disciplined and pick the biggest claim. Specifically:

- **Make persistence central.** The paper should be about whether consumer-driven political shocks create durable reallocation in trade, not merely short-run drops.
- **Elevate the visibility mechanism.** Right now “visible vs private” is interesting, but still feels like a sample split. To make the contribution bigger, the paper needs to sell observability as a general theory of politically expressive consumption, not just an ex post classification.
- **Tie the mechanism to a broader class of outcomes.** If possible, show that visibility predicts persistence, not just initial impact. That would be a stronger and more original claim than simply visible goods fall more on impact.
- **Frame the comparison more ambitiously.** The interesting contrast is not just Korea vs China or consumer vs industrial; it is **state-imposed trade restrictions versus consumer-enforced trade sanctions.** That is a broader conversation.

The single most valuable enlargement would be: **turn the paper from a case study of a boycott into a general paper on when identity-driven demand shocks produce persistent trade hysteresis, with observability as the organizing mechanism.**

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Governments impose trade barriers, but consumers can also respond to political conflict by changing purchases. We know such responses occur, but it is less clear whether they meaningfully and persistently alter trade patterns.

### Tension

Consumer boycotts are often dismissed as symbolic, noisy, and short-lived. If they matter, why do they hit some goods much harder than others? Is the relevant margin substitution, social pressure, or something else?

### Resolution

In the Korea-Japan episode, Japanese consumer goods exports to Korea fell sharply and remained depressed for years. The decline was especially strong for publicly visible goods, consistent with boycott participation serving as a social signal.

### Implications

Political identity can function as a decentralized trade barrier. Informal, demand-side reactions to geopolitical conflict may be more selective and more persistent than formal policy barriers, especially in visible consumer categories.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has one, but it is not fully disciplined. At times it feels like three adjacent papers:

1. a paper on the aggregate trade effect of the boycott,
2. a paper on product heterogeneity,
3. a paper on long-run persistence.

They are all interesting, but the paper needs one backbone. Right now the backbone is implied rather than explicit.

### What story should it be telling?

The cleanest story is:

1. **Consumer boycotts are usually treated as symbolic and transient.**
2. **This case shows they can meaningfully redirect trade.**
3. **The cross-product pattern reveals why: boycott participation is partly a public act.**
4. **Because the shock changes habits and brand positions, the resulting trade diversion can persist.**

That is a coherent setup-tension-resolution-implications arc. The current paper sometimes gets bogged down in empirical details before clarifying that this is the story.

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

### What would make it read better?

A lot of the raw material is there. The main need is editorial tightening and reprioritization.

#### 1. Shorten the empirical setup in the introduction
The introduction currently moves fairly quickly into design details. For a general-interest audience, that comes too early. The first page should stay with the big question, the natural experiment, and the three headline findings.

#### 2. Front-load the most memorable facts
The best facts are:
- large overall decline,
- visible vs private gradient,
- persistence through 2023.

Those should appear in compressed form before any description of fixed effects or coding schemes.

#### 3. Move some case-specific background out of the main text
The historical chronology of the Japan-Korea dispute is useful but somewhat over-elaborated relative to the paper’s strategic objective. It can be shorter. The reader does not need a mini diplomatic history to understand the setting.

#### 4. Integrate the mechanism story earlier
The cosmetics example is excellent and should stay. It is one of the few pieces of the introduction that gives the reader an intuitive economic puzzle. But the framing should be sharper: “Why are some politically targeted goods spared? Because some acts of boycott are visible and some are not.”

#### 5. Be careful not to oversell sample splits as mechanism proof
This is not about referee concerns; it is about narrative credibility. The paper repeatedly says “reveals the mechanism” or “demonstrates signaling.” That is too categorical for what is, in presentation terms, a heterogeneity pattern. Better to say “consistent with” and then explain why this pattern is especially informative.

#### 6. Trim the robustness in the main text
The permutation test and pre-trend remarks are not what readers will remember. Keep robustness concise. Do not let the paper’s center of gravity drift toward specification defense.

#### 7. Rethink the conclusion
The conclusion is pithy, but a bit slogan-like. It should do more to return to the big-picture economics:
- when decentralized social pressure behaves like a trade barrier,
- why observability matters,
- why persistence changes how we think about geopolitical shocks.

### Are there results buried that should be promoted?

The persistence result is not buried, but it should be elevated even more. It is arguably the most AER-relevant result because it turns a short-run boycott paper into a paper about durable trade reallocation.

### Is the conclusion adding value?

Some, but not enough. It summarizes elegantly, but it does not fully cash out the broader implications for trade fragmentation, informal sanctions, and political consumption. It should end with a stronger statement of what economists should update.
