## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether a major consumer boycott can durably reshape international trade, and whether the effect depends on what kinds of goods consumers can be seen buying. Using Japan’s exports to South Korea around the 2019 anti-Japan boycott, it argues that visible consumer goods were hit much harder than private ones, and that the resulting trade diversion persisted for years. A busy economist should care because the paper tries to connect geopolitics, identity, consumer behavior, and trade persistence in one setting.

The paper more or less has a pitch in the first two paragraphs, but it is not yet the *best* pitch for AER. Right now it opens as a boycott paper with an interesting product pattern. The stronger pitch is not “here is a boycott episode,” but “temporary geopolitical shocks can create persistent trade reallocation through consumer identity, and the persistence is concentrated where consumption is socially visible.” That is a bigger claim about the world.

### The first two paragraphs should say something closer to:

Temporary political conflicts are often assumed to have temporary commercial effects. But when consumers turn geopolitics into a social identity test, even a short-lived diplomatic dispute may permanently redirect trade. The key question is not just whether boycotts reduce purchases, but which goods they affect and whether those effects survive after the politics cool.

This paper studies the 2019 South Korean boycott of Japanese goods following Japan’s export restrictions. I show that Japanese consumer exports to Korea fell sharply and remained depressed for years, with the largest declines in socially visible goods such as beverages, clothing, and cars, and much smaller declines in private goods such as cosmetics. The central implication is that consumer-driven trade shocks can generate hysteresis through social signaling, not only through firms’ sunk costs or formal policy barriers.

That is the paper’s real selling point. The current introduction gets there, but a bit too slowly and too descriptively.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:**  
The paper argues that a consumer boycott triggered by a geopolitical dispute caused persistent trade diversion, and that the effect was much stronger for publicly visible goods, consistent with social-signaling rather than pure substitution.

This is a plausible contribution, but it is only partly differentiated from nearby work.

### Is it clearly differentiated from the closest papers?
Not yet sharply enough. The contribution currently reads as a combination of:
1. a boycott case study,
2. product-level heterogeneity,
3. a persistence result,
4. a signaling interpretation.

Each of those ingredients has analogues in existing literatures. What needs sharpening is which one is the true marginal advance. My instinct is that the strongest distinctive angle is:

- **persistent trade hysteresis from a consumer boycott**, and
- **heterogeneity by social visibility as evidence on mechanism**.

If that is the contribution, then the paper should spend less time saying “first product-level econometric analysis” and more time saying “existing boycott studies show short-run trade effects; this paper shows durable reallocation and identifies where boycott pressure bites most.”

### Is it framed as a question about the world or a gap in the literature?
Mixed, but too often as a literature gap. “This paper contributes to three literatures” is standard but small-bore. The stronger version is a world question:  
**When do geopolitical disputes fade, and when do they permanently rewire trade?**  
Then the literature comes in as tools for understanding that question.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the intro?
Some could, but many would still summarize it as:  
“it’s a DiD/DDD paper on the Korea-Japan boycott showing consumer goods fell more, especially visible ones.”

That is not enough. You want them to say:  
“it shows that consumer nationalism can create long-run trade hysteresis, and that the mechanism appears to run through social observability of consumption.”

### What would make this contribution bigger?
Several concrete possibilities:

- **Better persistence framing.** Right now “four years later, trade had not recovered” is important, but the paper does not really exploit how unusual that is. Compare the persistence to other trade shocks, boycott episodes, or product categories that historically mean-revert. Make the long-run reallocation central, not a secondary result.
- **A sharper mechanism outcome.** If the signaling story is the big idea, then outcomes beyond trade values that map more directly into public visibility, brand salience, or social exposure would enlarge the paper. At present the visibility coding feels suggestive rather than transformative.
- **A more ambitious comparison.** The paper would feel bigger if it positioned the Korea-Japan episode as one instance of a broader class of consumer-driven trade shocks, rather than an isolated event.
- **A more general object than “boycott effect.”** If the paper can say something about when informal social sanctions substitute for formal trade barriers, that is much more AER than “this boycott was large.”

At the moment, the contribution is interesting but still somewhat vulnerable to the “another trade-policy natural experiment” reaction.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
A geopolitical dispute triggers widespread anti-foreign sentiment. We know such episodes can temporarily reduce demand for foreign goods, but it is less clear whether these are fleeting reactions or durable shocks.

### Tension
Two tensions, really:
1. **Persistence tension:** why would a voluntary, informal boycott outlast the diplomatic event that triggered it?
2. **Selectivity tension:** why do some targeted foreign goods collapse while others remain relatively resilient despite broadly shared boycott sentiment?

### Resolution
The paper finds large declines in Japanese consumer exports to Korea, especially in publicly visible goods, and little recovery several years later.

### Implications
Consumer identity can function like an informal trade barrier; temporary political conflict may permanently redirect trade; and the goods most vulnerable are those for which buying is socially observable.

This is a decent narrative arc. The problem is that the paper currently splits its energy across too many stories:
- size of boycott,
- heterogeneity across goods,
- signaling mechanism,
- persistence / hysteresis,
- implications for sanctions.

That makes it feel slightly like a collection of results rather than one inexorable story.

### What story should it be telling?
The cleanest story is:

1. **Temporary diplomatic shocks need not have temporary economic effects.**
2. **The reason is not just switching costs or supply disruption; consumer identity can produce persistent reallocation.**
3. **That force is strongest where consumption is publicly observable.**

Everything else should serve that story. Right now the product tables and robustness sections are doing too much independent work, while the core conceptual spine could be clearer.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?
I would lead with:  
“A grassroots boycott, with no legal ban on consumer imports, appears to have cut Japanese consumer exports to Korea by roughly 40 percent and the losses were still there four years later—especially for visible goods like beverages and cars.”

That is a strong opener.

### Would people lean in?
Yes, initially. The episode is vivid and the persistence claim is intrinsically interesting.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Probably one of these:
- “Why did it persist so long?”
- “Is this really about signaling rather than substitutability or brand-specific factors?”
- “Is Korea-Japan a special case because of the historical relationship?”
- “How general is this beyond one boycott episode?”

That last question is the strategic challenge. The findings are interesting enough to get attention, but the paper must work harder to persuade readers this is more than an idiosyncratic bilateral dispute with unusually loaded history.

### If the findings are modest or null?
They are not null. The issue is not weakness of effect; it is breadth of interpretation. The paper has a real fact. The question is whether that fact scales into a general insight.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

At present, the gap is mostly a **framing and ambition problem**, with some **scope risk**.

This is not primarily a competence problem. The paper has a real event, a clean intuitive design, and a plausible headline result. But in current form it still reads more like a strong field-journal paper in trade/political economy than an AER paper.

### What is the main gap?

#### 1. Framing problem
The paper is underselling its most important idea and overselling narrower novelty claims.  
“First product-level econometric analysis” is not an AER contribution.  
“Consumer identity can create long-run trade hysteresis, concentrated in socially visible goods” potentially is.

#### 2. Scope problem
One episode, one bilateral comparison, and somewhat hand-coded mechanisms make the paper feel narrower than the claims. To publish in AER, the paper either needs:
- a much sharper and more convincing general conceptual contribution, or
- broader evidence that this is not just a Korea-Japan historical artifact.

#### 3. Ambition problem
The paper is a bit too content to document a large effect and then map it to a plausible mechanism. Top-field readers will ask for a stronger conceptual payoff: what class of models or policy intuitions does this overturn?

### What is the single most impactful advice?
**Reframe the paper around demand-side trade hysteresis from consumer identity, with social visibility as the central mechanism, and make every section serve that one claim rather than presenting it as a boycott case study with several add-on findings.**

If the author changed only one thing, it should be this. Right now the paper’s science may be good enough for serious consideration somewhere strong, but its story is still too close to “an interesting DiD on a famous boycott.” To belong in AER, it needs to become “a paper about how informal social pressure can permanently rewire international trade.”
