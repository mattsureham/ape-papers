**SUBJECT:** Strategic Positioning of "Selective Revenge: Product-Heterogeneous Trade Hysteresis"

---

### 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
This paper exploits the 2019 South Korea-Japan trade dispute to show how grassroots consumer boycotts—absent any government mandate—can cause massive, permanent disruptions in trade. The author finds that these boycotts are driven by social signaling (visibility) rather than just economic substitution, as trade in "visible" goods (beer, cars) collapsed by 63% while "private" goods (face cream) were relatively spared. This matters because it challenges the assumption that consumer-level trade shocks are temporary, proving instead that demand-side nationalism can create long-run "hysteresis" or permanent trade diversion.

**Evaluation:** The paper does a **good** job of articulating this in the first two paragraphs. It immediately sets the scene with the "cosmetics anomaly" to hook the reader. However, the first two paragraphs could be punchier regarding the *persistence* (hysteresis). 
**The Pitch the Paper Should Have:** 
"Can a purely voluntary consumer movement permanently reshape international trade? This paper uses the 2019 South Korea-Japan boycott to show that consumer nationalism reduced exports by 41%, with a disproportionate 63% collapse in socially visible goods where boycotting serves as a public signal of identity. Crucially, four years after the diplomatic crisis subsided, trade has failed to recover, demonstrating that demand-shocks can induce permanent trade hysteresis that formal sanctions often fail to achieve."

---

### 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**The Contribution:** The paper identifies "social visibility" as the primary mechanism for boycott effectiveness and provides the first evidence of long-run trade hysteresis driven by demand-side social norms rather than supply-side sunk costs.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It is well-differentiated. While Pandya & Venkatesan (2012) or Heilmann (2016) look at boycotts, they are often descriptive or short-term. This paper pushes into the *mechanisms* (signaling vs. substitution) and the *duration* (hysteresis).
*   **World vs. Literature:** It starts with a question about the world (the Korea-Japan dispute), which is a strength.
*   **Clarity:** A smart economist would say: "It's the paper that shows you only boycott Japanese stuff if your neighbors can see you doing it, and that once you stop, you never go back."
*   **Bigger Contribution:** To make this truly "AER-big," the author needs to connect the "persistence" result to a welfare calculation or a formal model of habit formation. Why *exactly* is it permanent? Is it a shift in the "taste" parameter, or did the boycott trigger the entry of domestic firms that now enjoy economies of scale?

---

### 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
*   **Closest Neighbors:** 
    1.  **Heilmann (2016)** on the short-term impacts of boycotts.
    2.  **Baldwin (1988) / Dixit (1989)** on trade hysteresis.
    3.  **Bursztyn et al. (2020)** on social norms and visibility.
    4.  **DellaVigna and Pope (2018)** on signaling.
*   **Positioning:** The paper correctly builds on the boycott literature but attempts to "colonize" the hysteresis literature. This is the right move.
*   **Missing Conversations:** The paper should speak more to the **Industrial Organization** literature on brand loyalty and switching costs. If trade hasn't recovered, is it because of "stigma" or because Korean firms made sunk investments in advertising during the vacuum?

---

### 4. NARRATIVE ARC
*   **Setup:** A diplomatic spat leads to a massive, voluntary boycott.
*   **Tension:** Why did beer disappear while face cream stayed? And why hasn't trade recovered now that the fight is over?
*   **Resolution:** Consumption visibility drives the "bite," and the boycott induced a permanent shift in consumer habits.
*   **Implications:** Non-tariff, non-government barriers (social norms) are stickier and potentially more powerful than formal trade policy.

**Evaluation:** The narrative is excellent. It moves from a "puzzle" (the cosmetics anomaly) to a "mechanism" (visibility) to a "macro consequence" (hysteresis).

---

### 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
*   **Lead Fact:** "The boycott was three times more effective on goods you consume in public than goods you use in your bathroom."
*   **Reaction:** Lean in. It’s an intuitive, "Freakonomics-style" observation backed by trade data.
*   **Follow-up:** "Is it really social pressure, or just that there were no good Korean substitutes for Japanese face cream?" (The author addresses this in 5.3, which is crucial).

---

### 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
*   **Section 5.4 (Robustness):** The "Levels" vs "Logs" discussion (Column 4, Table 5) is a bit weak. It’s standard for trade papers to use logs, but the author should probably use a Poisson Pseudo-Maximum Likelihood (PPML) estimator to handle the "zeros" and the level/log debate more robustly, as is standard in modern trade (Silva & Tenreyro).
*   **Front-loading:** The paper is well-structured. The "Visibility" mechanism (Table 4) is the star of the show and should perhaps be even more central to the introduction.

---

### 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The gap between this and a "Safe IE" paper is the **theoretical depth of the hysteresis**. 
*   **The Science:** The DDD is competent, but standard.
*   **The Story:** The signaling story is compelling.
*   **The Ambition:** To be AER, the paper needs to prove *why* the effect is permanent. Is it a coordination failure (social stigma) or a market structure change? If the author can show that the boycott acted as a "Big Push" for domestic Korean brands to overcome entry barriers, it becomes a major paper on market dynamics.

**Single Most Impactful Advice:** Explicitly test—perhaps using brand-level data or advertising spend data if available—whether the "hysteresis" is driven by a permanent change in consumer *preferences* (stigma) or a change in *market structure* (domestic firms capturing the market and building their own brand equity).

---

### Strategic Assessment

*   **Current framing quality:** Compelling
*   **Contribution clarity:** Crystal clear
*   **Literature positioning:** Well-positioned
*   **Narrative arc:** Strong
*   **AER distance:** Medium (Needs more "meat" on the hysteresis mechanism)
*   **Single biggest improvement:** Use PPML for the main estimates and provide a more rigorous exploration (theoretical or empirical) of the specific mechanism behind the long-run trade diversion.
