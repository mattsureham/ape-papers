The paper largely pursues the core causal question outlined in the manifest: estimating the employment impact of the *Alice Corp. v. However, there are significant deviations in data implementation and scope that alter the empirical precision promised in the original idea. The manifest specified **Quarterly Workforce Index (QWI)** data from Azure (county × quarter × NAICS-3), covering 2012Q1–2017Q4. The paper instead utilizes **Census County Business Patterns (CBP)** (county × year × NAICS-3), covering 2012–2019. This shift from quarterly to annual data reduces the ability to detect immediate dynamic responses to the June 2014 shock and introduces potential selection bias due to CBP's data suppression rules for small establishments, which the manifest's QWI source would have avoided. **2. Summary**

This paper estimates the causal effect of the 2014 *Alice* Supreme Court decision on U.S. industry employment, finding that weakening software patent eligibility reduced employment in software-intensive industries by 9.3 percent relative to control sectors. The effect is heterogeneous, with patent-producing industries (software publishing, computer electronics) experiencing significant declines (up to 18.3 percent), while downstream users (data processing) show no significant response. The results challenge the conventional view that reducing software patent scope uniformly benefits the technology sector, suggesting that appropriability concerns dominate patent-thicket benefits for innovation-intensive producers.

**3. Essential Points**

1.  **Data Source and Suppression Bias:** The deviation from the manifest's proposed QWI data to CBP introduces a critical selection issue. CBP suppresses employment data for counties with few establishments to protect confidentiality. If *Alice* caused firms to shrink below suppression thresholds or relocate to avoid disclosure, the balanced panel requirement ("at least six of eight years of non-suppressed data") could systematically exclude treated units that were most affected by the policy. This selection bias could inflate or deflate the estimated treatment effect. The authors must demonstrate that suppression rates did not differentially change in treated counties post-*Alice*.

2.  **Inference with Few Clusters:** Standard errors are clustered at the state level (51 clusters). While common, inference with fewer than 50 clusters can suffer from downward bias in standard errors, leading to over-rejection of the null hypothesis (MacDonald & Reitz, 2011). Given the large estimated magnitudes (−18.3 percent for software publishing), the statistical significance relies heavily on these SEs. The authors should report wild cluster bootstrap confidence intervals or use bias-corrected accelerated variance estimators to ensure the *p*-values are robust to the few-cluster problem.

3.  **Structural Substitution vs. Policy Shock:** The large decline in NAICS 511 (Software Publishing, −18.3 percent) coincides with a known industry structural shift toward SaaS and cloud computing, which often recodes employment from NAICS 511 to NAICS 518 (Data Processing). The paper finds NAICS 518 employment rose insignificantly (2.8 percent). If the *Alice* decision accelerated this recoding rather than destroying jobs, the DiD might attribute structural substitution to patent policy. The authors need to disentangle whether jobs disappeared or simply shifted classification, perhaps by analyzing firm-level patenting intensity within NAICS codes to isolate the policy mechanism from industry evolution.

**4. Suggestions**

**Refining Inference and Standard Errors**
The current clustering strategy (state level) is standard but risky given the 51-cluster limit. I recommend implementing a **wild cluster bootstrap percentile-t procedure** (Brittain et al., 2018) to validate the significance of the −9.3 percent estimate. If the bootstrap confidence intervals widen significantly compared to the asymptotic SEs, the claim of "significance at the 1 percent level" may be overstated. Additionally, consider reporting **heterogeneity-adjusted standard errors** (HACSE) to account for cross-state variance in tech sector exposure. Since tech employment is heavily concentrated in California, Washington, and Massachusetts, state-level shocks may not be independent. A sensitivity check excluding these states (as mentioned in robustness) should be paired with a re-clustering at the **county level** (if feasible) or **industry-state level** to test if the significance holds under alternative variance structures.

**Addressing the CBP Suppression Mechanism**
Since the paper relies on CBP rather than the manifest's QWI, you must explicitly address the suppression bias. I suggest adding a table showing the **rate of data suppression** in treated vs. control counties pre- and post-*Alice*. If suppression rates increased in treated counties post-2014, your balanced panel condition selectively drops the most affected observations. To mitigate this, consider using **imputed CBP data** (e.g., from the Census Bureau's imputed series) or weighting observations by the inverse probability of suppression. Alternatively, if access to the QWI data (as per the manifest) is still possible, swapping to that dataset would eliminate this concern entirely and allow for quarterly event studies, which are crucial for a June 2014 shock.

**Clarifying the Timing of the Shock**
CBP employment data reflects the "mid-March pay period." The *Alice* decision occurred in **June 2014**. Consequently, the 2014 CBP observation largely reflects pre-treatment conditions. Your event study treats 2014 as $t=0$ with a significant effect (−3.6 percent), but the main DiD defines Post as 2015–2019. This is logically consistent, but the economic interpretation requires nuance. Patent policy affects R&D budgets and hiring plans with lags. A sharp drop in 2015–2016 is more plausible than 2014. I suggest shifting the **Post indicator to start in 2015** for all main tables to align with the data collection reality, ensuring the "immediate effect" in 2014 is not misinterpreted as a policy response when it was technically pre-decision data. Explicitly discuss the lag between patent eligibility changes and labor market adjustment in the mechanism section.
