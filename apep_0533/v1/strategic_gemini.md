### 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper evaluates the impact of salary history bans (SHBs) on the gender earnings gap using universe-scale administrative data (QWI). It asks whether "information subtraction"—preventing employers from anchoring offers on prior pay—actually compresses the wage gap at the point of hire, or if the policy is too porous to disrupt the equilibrium. The paper finds a precisely estimated zero effect, contradicting earlier studies and suggesting that simple information bans are insufficient to overcome structural labor market disparities.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this pitch excellently. The first two paragraphs clearly set the theoretical stakes (anchoring vs. permanent disadvantage) and the policy context. The "what should be said" is already there: it positions the paper as the definitive "rigorous null" against a backdrop of suggestive, less-powered results.

---

### 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution:** The paper provides the most statistically powerful and methodologically robust evidence to date that salary history bans have failed to move the needle on aggregate gender pay equity.

- **Differentiation:** It differentiates itself via data scale (QWI universe vs. CPS survey) and design (DDD using continuing workers as a within-state placebo). 
- **Framing:** It is framed as a question about the **WORLD** (does information regulation work?), which is strong. 
- **Clarity:** A smart economist would immediately understand the "New Hire vs. Continuing Worker" logic. It avoids being "just another DiD" by presenting a built-in mechanism test.
- **Making it bigger:** To make the contribution truly "top-tier," the paper needs to dive deeper into *why* the null exists. Is it because of "strategic disclosure" (the high-earners volunteer it)? If the author could identify states with different "loophole" levels (e.g., Oregon's voluntary disclosure allowance), the contribution shifts from "this doesn't work" to "this is the specific design flaw."

---

### 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

The paper sits at the intersection of **Gender Economics** (Goldin; Blau & Kahn) and **Information Regulation** (Agan & Starr).

- **Neighbors:** Agan & Starr (2018) on "Ban the Box"; Goldin (2014) on structural persistence; Hansen et al. (2020) and Sinha (2023) as the "incumbent" SHB studies.
- **Strategy:** It "attacks" the previous SHB literature on power and specification grounds while "building on" the Agan & Starr framework of information restriction.
- **Conversation:** It is currently having the "policy evaluation" conversation. It would be more impactful if it spoke more to the **Bargaining Literature**. If SHBs don't work, does it mean anchoring isn't the primary driver of the gap at hire? That is a bigger theoretical claim than "the law was weak."

---

### 4. NARRATIVE ARC

- **Setup:** Salary history anchoring is a primary suspect for why the gender gap persists across job switches.
- **Tension:** Twenty states passed laws to stop this, and early evidence suggested they worked. But were those results just noise?
- **Resolution:** Using the full universe of data and a sharper design, the effect vanishes. The "precisely estimated zero" is the hero of the story.
- **Implications:** Policy needs to move from "information subtraction" (bans) to "information addition" (transparency).

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong. It isn't a collection of results; it’s a systematic "debunking" that leads to a clear policy recommendation.

---

### 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

**The Dinner Party Fact:** "We looked at 150 million workers across every state that banned salary history questions, and the gender gap didn't move by even half a penny."

- **Reaction:** People lean in. The "death of a popular policy" is always more interesting than a modest success.
- **Follow-up:** "Does that mean women are volunteering their old salaries anyway?" (This points to the need for more discussion on the "strategic disclosure" mechanism).

---

### 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

- **Front-loading:** The paper is well-structured, but the "Why the Null?" section (6.1) is the most intellectually stimulating part. I would move some of those theoretical "failure mode" discussions into the introduction or literature review to heighten the tension earlier.
- **Appendix:** The industry-level heterogeneity (Figure 2) is great. It should stay in the main text because it proves the null isn't just "averaging out" (e.g., it’s null even in high-negotiation sectors like Finance).

---

### 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The gap is **Ambition**. A "precise null" is valuable, but for the AER, we usually want to know *which* of the proposed failure modes is the culprit. 

**The Single Most Impactful Advice:** Shift from being a "policy evaluation" paper to an "information environment" paper. Use the heterogeneity in law *stringency* (e.g., states that ban *use* of info vs. just the *ask*) to prove that the "loophole" or "strategic disclosure" is the reason for the null. If you can show that "stricter bans work slightly but porous ones fail," you have a much more profound paper about market signals.
