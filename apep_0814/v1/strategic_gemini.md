### 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper investigates one of the most dramatic homicide collapses in modern history: El Salvador’s 80% decline in violence between 2015 and 2019. By exploiting a novel municipality-level panel and measuring gang infrastructure via pre-2019 detention rates, the author tests whether the decline was a targeted dismantling of gang power or a diffuse secular trend. The paper finds that the decline was intensely concentrated in precisely those areas where gangs were strongest, suggesting that the state's intervention—whether through enforcement or negotiation—successfully struck the heart of the country's criminal organizational structure.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this well, but it is currently framed as a "where did they go?" mystery. The first two paragraphs should pivot from the mystery to the **institutional importance**. It should emphasize that El Salvador provides a rare "proof of concept" for whether entrenched, sovereign-like criminal organizations can be dismantled rapidly. 

**Revised Pitch:** *“Can states reclaim territorial control from entrenched criminal organizations that provide a 'parallel' sovereignty? Exploiting the historic 80% collapse of homicides in El Salvador, I show that the decline was almost entirely driven by the reversal of gang-controlled territory. This suggests that even the most 'locked-in' criminal equilibriums are susceptible to rapid state-led disruption, providing a critical data point for the political economy of state capacity.”*

---

### 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution:** The paper provides the first micro-geographic evidence that El Salvador’s homicide collapse was a targeted suppression of gang organizational presence rather than a broad-based improvement in social or economic conditions.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It is well-differentiated from **Dell (2015)** and **Melnikov et al. (2020)**. While those papers look at the *entry* and *costs* of gangs, this looks at the *exit*. It's the "mirror image" contribution.
*   **Framing:** Currently, it leans toward "filling a gap in the literature" about El Salvador. It needs to frame itself as answering a question about the **World**: *How do violent criminal orders collapse?*
*   **Bigger Contribution:** To make this an AER heavyweight, the author needs more than homicides. If gangs were "dismantled," we should see it in **economic activity**. Does a 10% larger decline in homicides lead to a jump in night lights, business registrations, or consumer spending in those same municipalities? Linking the *security* collapse to an *economic* rebirth is what moves this from a "crime paper" to a "top-tier general interest paper."

---

### 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

*   **Closest Neighbors:** Dell (2015, AER), Lessing (2017), Sviatschi (2022, Econometrica), and Blattman et al. (2021).
*   **Positioning Strategy:** The paper should **synthesize** the "Criminal Governance" literature (Lessing, Blattman) with the "State Capacity" literature (Acemoglu/Robinson). It should position itself as a study of how the state out-competes a violent rival for the monopoly on force.
*   **Unexpected Connection:** The paper would benefit from connecting to the **"Peace Economics"** literature. Is this a "peace dividend"? By framing the gang-state relationship as a civil war rather than just "crime," the stakes feel higher.

---

### 4. NARRATIVE ARC

*   **Setup:** El Salvador was the most dangerous non-war country on earth, ruled by a duopoly of gangs (MS-13/B18).
*   **Tension:** In 2019, homicides vanished. Critics and skeptics argue it was either a "statistical mirage," a demographic shift, or a fragile secret deal.
*   **Resolution:** Geographic data shows the decline "hit the bullseye" of gang territory. The "mirage" and "secular trend" theories are debunked by the dose-response to gang intensity.
*   **Implications:** Highly entrenched criminal orders are more fragile than we thought, but the "how" (negotiation vs. enforcement) determines the durability of the new peace.

---

### 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

**At a dinner party:** "Did you know that in the most violent places in El Salvador, the homicide rate didn't just fall—it converged almost perfectly to the level of peaceful villages in just three years?"
**Reaction:** People lean in. 
**Follow-up:** "But did they actually kill the gangs, or did the government just pay them off?"
**The Paper's Weakness:** The paper currently cannot answer the follow-up. It needs a "Mechanism" section that at least tries to proxy for "negotiation" (e.g., do we see a drop in detentions *during* the homicide drop, suggesting a deal?).

---

### 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

*   **Move to Appendix:** Table 4 (Placebo tests) is important but clutters the flow.
*   **Front-load:** The quintile "dose-response" result is the most visually and logically compelling piece of evidence. This should be Figure 1, not buried in text on page 10.
*   **New Section:** Add a "Spillovers" section. When homicides fall in Municipality A, do they rise in Municipality B (displacement)? If not, the "dismantling" story is much stronger.

---

### 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

**The Gap:** Currently, the paper is a very high-quality "Technical Note" on a historic event. To be an AER paper, it needs **ambition regarding welfare**.

**Single Biggest Advice:** Show me the "Economic Life after Death." Use satellite data (Night Lights) or any available high-frequency economic proxy to show that the geographic "targeting" of the homicide decline led to a geographic "targeting" of economic recovery. If you prove that removing gangs "unlocked" the local economy, this is a "Revise & Resubmit" tomorrow.
