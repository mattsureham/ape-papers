## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple and important question: when El Salvador’s homicide rate collapsed after 2019, did violence fall most in the places where gangs had been most entrenched? Using municipality-level data, the paper argues yes: the post-2019 decline was larger in municipalities with higher pre-existing gang presence, suggesting the country’s security turnaround was not geographically diffuse but concentrated in gang-heavy areas.

A busy economist should care because El Salvador’s violence collapse is one of the most striking recent facts in political economy and development. If the decline was concentrated where gang power had been strongest, that speaks directly to whether dramatic crime reductions can actually dismantle organized criminal violence rather than merely reflect background secular change.

### Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Mostly yes, but not sharply enough for AER. The title is excellent and the first paragraph has a strong fact. But the introduction drifts too quickly into “if policy then concentrated, if secular trends then diffuse,” which is a bit mechanical and undersells the bigger stakes. The paper’s true hook is not just geographic incidence; it is whether one of the most dramatic violence collapses in modern history was actually concentrated in former gang strongholds.

The first two paragraphs should do less scene-setting and more claim-making. Right now, the paper sounds like a competent municipal DiD about homicides. It should sound like a paper about how to interpret an extraordinary national event.

### The pitch the paper should have

“El Salvador experienced one of the fastest homicide declines ever recorded outside civil war. This paper asks whether that collapse actually occurred in the municipalities where gangs had been most entrenched, or whether it was a broad decline unrelated to the geography of organized crime. Using municipality-level data on homicides and pre-2019 gang presence, I show that the post-2019 decline was disproportionately concentrated in former gang strongholds, implying that the country’s violence collapse was tightly linked to the geography of gang control rather than a diffuse national trend.”

That is the story. Then, only after that, say why this matters: because it changes how we interpret the Salvadoran case, and because it informs whether extreme violence in organized-crime settings can be reversed in the places most affected.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper’s contribution is to show that El Salvador’s post-2019 homicide collapse was geographically concentrated in municipalities with greater pre-existing gang presence.

The paper needs to distinguish itself from at least two different literatures:

1. **Organized crime geography papers**: e.g. gang territories, cartel presence, mafia infiltration.
2. **Crime reduction / policing papers**: e.g. targeted enforcement, hotspots, deterrence.

What is new is not just “municipal heterogeneity.” It is that the paper uses the geography of pre-existing gang presence to interpret a historically large national violence collapse. That interpretive angle needs to be foregrounded.

Mostly about the world, which is good. But the introduction slides back into literature-filling by the end (“contributes to three literatures”). That is a common but weakening move. ### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what's new?

At present, maybe, but not confidently. They might say: “It’s a DiD paper on whether Bukele-era homicide declines were bigger in gang-heavy municipalities.” That is accurate but not memorable. The paper needs the colleague’s summary to be: “It shows that El Salvador’s extraordinary homicide collapse happened disproportionately in former gang strongholds, which changes how we interpret the whole episode.”

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Several possibilities:

- **Different framing:** Biggest payoff. Recast the paper as explaining the geography of a historically large national violence collapse, not as estimating the effect of a generic post-2019 “security intervention.”

- **Comparative framing:** Position El Salvador against other organized-crime declines or temporary truces. Was this pattern unique, or what would theory predict?

At the moment, the paper’s ambition is “where did murders go?” A bigger paper would ask “what does the geography of the decline reveal about how states reverse organized criminal violence?”

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

El Salvador had one of the highest homicide rates in the world, with violence tightly linked to gang territorial control. Then, after 2019, homicides collapsed at extraordinary speed.

### Tension

But aggregate decline alone does not tell us what happened. Was this truly a gang-centered transformation that reversed violence in gang strongholds, or just a broad national decline reflecting unrelated trends or reporting changes? The geography of the decline is a key diagnostic.

### Resolution

The paper finds that municipalities with greater pre-existing gang presence experienced larger homicide declines after 2019.

### Implications

The implication is that the violence collapse was tied to the geography of gang organization. More broadly, extreme organized-crime violence may be more reversible than often believed, though the mechanism—suppression, deterrence, or negotiation—matters enormously for external validity and welfare interpretation.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the ingredients, but the arc is still only **serviceable**. Too much of the paper reads like a standard empirical template rather than a paper built around a big interpretive puzzle.

The main problem is that the paper alternates between two stories:

1. “Was the homicide decline targeted where gangs were strongest?”
2. “What was the effect of Bukele’s 2019 security policy?”

Those are not the same story. The first is interesting and believable for this design. The second is larger but much harder to sustain, and it invites skepticism the paper does not need. The paper should commit to the first story. It is about the **geography of a national violence collapse**, not a clean estimate of one named policy.

If the paper is not careful, it becomes a collection of results—main interaction, event study, placebo dates, quintiles—looking for a story. The story it should tell is: **pre-existing gang geography provides a lens for interpreting a remarkable aggregate violence collapse.**

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?

“I’d lead with this: in one of the fastest homicide declines ever recorded, the biggest reductions happened in the municipalities where gangs had been most entrenched before 2019.”

That is the memorable fact.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

They would lean in initially because El Salvador is salient and the decline is astonishing. But they would only stay engaged if the paper quickly answers the obvious follow-up: what does this pattern tell us about the underlying transformation? If the paper remains at the level of “larger declines in high-gang municipalities,” attention will fade.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Immediately: **“Does that mean Bukele’s crackdown worked, or that the government cut a deal with gangs in those places?”**

And then: **“Why should homicide geography alone let us distinguish targeted suppression from other explanations?”**

Those are not referee-style identification quibbles; they are the natural “so what” questions. The paper currently acknowledges the mechanism ambiguity, but too passively. For a top journal, the author needs to turn that ambiguity into part of the contribution: even without fully identifying mechanism, the spatial pattern is informative about what kinds of stories remain plausible.

### If findings are modest

The findings are not null, but they are modest in magnitude relative to the enormity of the national decline. A 10 percent differential decline per standard deviation is meaningful, but it is not by itself a jaw-dropping estimate. The paper therefore cannot rely on coefficient size. It must rely on the importance of the fact being interpreted.

This is why framing matters so much. The interesting result is not “beta = -0.098.” It is “the country’s historic violence collapse was disproportionately concentrated in former gang strongholds.”

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

At present, the gap is mostly a mix of **framing problem**, **scope problem**, and **ambition problem**.

### Framing problem

This is the biggest one. The paper is better than its current self-presentation. It presents itself as a standard post-2019 continuous-intensity DiD around security policy. That is not an AER story. The AER story is about interpreting one of the most dramatic violence collapses in recent history using the pre-existing geography of gang control.

### Scope problem

Homicides alone may be too narrow for AER unless the conceptual contribution is much sharper. If the paper could show that gang-heavy municipalities also saw changes in extortion, market activity, schooling, mobility, migration, business entry, or some other welfare-relevant outcome, it would become much more consequential.

### Novelty problem

Moderate. The paper is not asking a stale question exactly, but “did violence fall more where exposure was higher?” is a familiar move. The novelty has to come from the setting, scale of the event, and interpretive leverage—not from the econometric design.

### Ambition problem

Yes. The paper feels competent but safe. It stops at the first interesting result. A top-field paper would ask what this implies for the nature of criminal order, state capacity, and the reversibility of violence.

### Single most impactful piece of advice

**Stop selling this as a DiD estimate of a 2019 security policy, and instead sell it as a paper that uses local gang geography to explain what El Salvador’s extraordinary homicide collapse actually was.**

That one change would sharpen the introduction, clean up the literature positioning, and make the contribution feel bigger. If the author can also broaden outcomes, even better—but the first-order issue is the story.
