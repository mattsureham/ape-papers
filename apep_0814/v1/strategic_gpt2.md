## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple and important question about one of the most dramatic violence declines in the world: when El Salvador’s homicide rate collapsed after 2019, did killings fall most in the places where gangs had been strongest? Using municipality-level data, the paper shows that areas with greater pre-2019 gang presence experienced larger subsequent homicide declines, suggesting that the national collapse was not spatially diffuse but concentrated in gang-heavy places.

A busy economist should care because El Salvador is an extreme and globally salient case. If one of the largest modern homicide declines happened disproportionately in gang-dominated municipalities, that speaks to broader questions about how organized crime, state capacity, and security interventions reshape local violence.

### Does the paper articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Mostly yes, but not optimally. The title is strong, and the opening fact pattern is attention-grabbing. The second paragraph gets to the right question quickly. Still, the introduction leans too fast into “if policy worked, the decline should be concentrated where gangs were strongest,” which narrows the paper prematurely into a quasi-evaluation of Bukele-era policy. The bigger, stronger pitch is not “did one policy work?” but “what explains the geography of one of the largest violence collapses in recent history?”

### The pitch the paper should have

“El Salvador experienced one of the fastest homicide declines ever recorded outside civil war. This paper asks whether that collapse occurred where gang power had been most entrenched. Using municipality-level data on homicides and pre-2019 gang presence, I show that the post-2019 decline was substantially larger in gang-heavy municipalities, implying that the country’s violence collapse tracked the geography of organized crime rather than reflecting a broad secular improvement. This matters because it helps distinguish between competing explanations for dramatic crime declines and clarifies what other high-violence countries should learn from El Salvador.”

That is the story. The current introduction is close, but it should foreground the world question more sharply and delay the design details.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper’s contribution is to show that El Salvador’s extraordinary post-2019 homicide decline was geographically concentrated in municipalities with greater pre-existing gang presence.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?

Not clearly enough. The paper cites a broad organized-crime canon, but the actual differentiation is still fuzzy. Right now the introduction risks sounding like: “existing papers study the costs of gangs; I study the reverse when gang activity declines.” That is serviceable, but not a memorable distinction. The more precise distinction is:

- existing work studies where gangs and criminal organizations emerge, how they govern territory, and what violence they produce;
- this paper studies the geography of a sharp *retreat* in lethal violence during a nationally salient collapse.

That is a cleaner contrast.

It also needs to distinguish itself from work on crime drops more generally. The paper is not just another paper on the determinants of homicide. Its niche is the spatial incidence of an exceptionally large national decline.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or filling a gap in a LITERATURE?

It starts with a world question, which is good. But as the introduction progresses, it slips back into literature-filling mode. The stronger framing is emphatically about the world: **when an organized-crime equilibrium breaks down, where does violence recede first and most?** That is a question people care about independent of this literature.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?

A reasonably smart economist could say, “It’s a DiD on whether homicide fell more in gang-heavy municipalities after Bukele took office.” That is not enough. They should instead be able to say: “It uses the most dramatic homicide collapse in the hemisphere to show that the decline tracked gang geography, which helps arbitrate between gang-centered and secular explanations of crime decline.”

At present, the paper is in danger of being summarized as “another reduced-form paper on violence in Latin America.”

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Three possibilities, in order of importance:

1. **Reframe the paper around the geography of violence collapse, not the effect of a policy episode.**  
   The current “Territorial Control Plan” framing is smaller than the raw fact. The bigger question is about the spatial anatomy of a historic crime collapse.

2. **Add outcomes that speak to whether this was a broader retreat of gang power rather than only fewer murders.**  
   Extortion, disappearances, business activity, mobility, school attendance, formal employment, or migration would all enlarge the paper materially. Right now “murders fell more where gangs were stronger” is interesting, but still narrow.

3. **Develop mechanism through heterogeneity rather than rhetoric.**  
   If the paper cannot separate enforcement from negotiation, it should stop pretending mechanism is secondary and instead use the limits to sharpen the contribution: e.g., the key fact is that whatever changed, it operated through preexisting gang geography. That is a valuable result if positioned correctly.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

### Closest neighbors

The nearest neighbors seem to be:

- **Melnikov (2020)** on gang territory / gang borders in El Salvador.
- **Dell (2015)** on drug trafficking organizations and violence in Mexico.
- **Pinotti (2015)** on organized crime and local economic effects in Italy.
- Likely also work by **Lessing** on criminal conflict and state policy in Latin America.
- On policing/crime concentration, papers such as **Blattman et al.** on hotspot policing are somewhat adjacent, though less directly comparable.

There is also likely relevant criminology and political-science work on El Salvador specifically that the paper should engage more seriously, especially on gang governance, pacts, and the spatial logic of violence.

### How should the paper position itself relative to those neighbors?

Mostly **build on** and **bridge** them.

- Relative to Melnikov: build directly. That paper helps establish the territorial logic of gangs in El Salvador; this paper should say it studies what happened to violence when that territorial order changed.
- Relative to Dell and Pinotti: build analogically. Those papers show how criminal organizations shape local outcomes; this paper studies the local geography of their apparent suppression or accommodation.
- Relative to the security-policy literature: do not overclaim. This is not a clean policy-evaluation paper in spirit, and trying to join too directly with police-intervention studies somewhat shrinks the paper.

### Is it positioned too narrowly or too broadly?

Oddly, both.

- **Too narrowly** in centering the 2019 policy shift and a single country episode as if the audience is mainly people who follow Salvadoran security policy.
- **Too broadly** in the literature review, which lists organized crime, policing, child exposure to criminal labor markets, and national-level accounts without a sharp sense of the conversation it most wants to enter.

The right audience is scholars of organized crime, violence, political economy of state control, and place-based crime dynamics. The paper should unapologetically target that conversation.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?

Two gaps stand out.

1. **Crime declines / spatial concentration of crime declines.**  
   The paper should connect to broader work on why crime falls and whether declines are concentrated in high-crime places. That makes the paper bigger than El Salvador.

2. **State-building / coercive governance / criminal governance.**  
   There is a political economy literature on territorial control, informal order, and bargains between states and violent non-state actors. The paper is plainly relevant there, especially given its own acknowledgment that negotiation may matter.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?

Not quite. The paper currently sounds like it wants to converse with “security policy evaluation in developing countries.” That is not its strongest comparative advantage. Its strongest conversation is:

**How do organized-crime equilibria unravel, and what does the local geography of that unraveling reveal?**

That is more original and more AER-relevant than “here is a municipal DiD about crime.”

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

El Salvador was one of the most violent countries in the world, with homicide deeply linked to gang territorial control.

### Tension

Then homicide collapsed at extraordinary speed. But it is unclear whether that collapse reflected a gang-centered transformation or a broad secular improvement unrelated to gang geography.

### Resolution

The decline was larger in municipalities with stronger pre-existing gang presence.

### Implications

The homicide collapse appears to have run through the geography of gangs. That changes how we interpret the episode and how policymakers in other high-violence settings should think about replicability: what mattered was not a uniform national trend but a transformation in places previously dominated by organized crime.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the bones of one, yes. But the paper weakens its own arc by repeatedly reverting to specification language and robustness cataloging. It is closer to a real story than to a bag of regressions, but it still reads too much like an econometric memo.

The story it should be telling is:

1. El Salvador’s homicide collapse is extraordinary.
2. Competing explanations imply different spatial patterns.
3. Gang geography provides a testable way to distinguish among those explanations.
4. The decline was concentrated where gangs had been strongest.
5. Therefore, the collapse is best understood as a transformation in the organized-crime landscape, not just a general national improvement.

That is a compelling narrative. The current draft has it, but does not discipline the paper around it tightly enough.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?

“I’d lead with: in one of the fastest homicide declines ever recorded, the murders disappeared disproportionately from the municipalities where gangs were previously most entrenched.”

That is the hook.

### Would people lean in?

Yes, initially. El Salvador is salient; the crime drop is astonishing; the geographic angle is intuitive. Economists would lean in for the first 60 seconds.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Immediately: **Does this tell us anything about mechanism—enforcement, incapacitation, deterrence, or negotiated peace?**  
And then: **Why is that not just mean reversion from the most violent places?**

You told me not to assess identification, so I won’t. But strategically, this tells you what the paper must handle narratively: if it cannot pin down mechanism, it must make crystal clear why the descriptive-geographic fact is still intrinsically important.

### If findings are modest, is the result interesting?

The point estimate itself is not gigantic in a way that sells itself. The interesting thing is not the coefficient magnitude per se; it is the mapping of an historic national decline onto pre-existing gang geography. So the paper should stop trying to sell “moderate negative standardized effect sizes” and instead sell the spatial logic of a country-level transformation.

The “standardized effect size” appendix material is actively unhelpful for top-journal positioning. No AER reader cares whether this is “moderate negative.” They care whether the paper changes interpretation of a major real-world event.

   Right now it mostly summarizes. It should instead tell readers what belief they should update: not “security policy can matter,” but “the geography of dramatic crime declines can reveal whether they are tied to organized crime dynamics.”

Partly, but not enough. The opening statistic is excellent. But the reader quickly gets bogged down in specification and p-values. The paper should reveal the memorable substantive fact before the econometrics.

### Is the conclusion adding value?

Only modestly. It needs to step back and explain why a spatially targeted homicide collapse changes how we should think about crime decline, gang power, and state intervention.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

This is not mainly a technical problem. It is a **framing and ambition problem**.

The paper has a good raw object: a historically huge homicide collapse in a country central to debates on gangs, state violence, and public security. But in its current form, it packages that object as a competent municipal DiD. That is too small for AER.

### What is the gap?

- **Framing problem:** Yes, strongly. The paper undersells the world question and oversells the regression design.
- **Scope problem:** Also yes. Homicides alone make the paper narrower than it could be. If it had credible additional outcomes showing broader retreat of gang control or improvements in economic life, the paper would be much bigger.
- **Novelty problem:** Somewhat. The baseline exercise is easy to summarize as a familiar design applied to a famous episode. The novelty comes from the episode and the geographic interpretation, not from method.
- **Ambition problem:** Definitely. The paper is careful but safe. It wants to show a targeted homicide decline, when it should aim to explain the spatial anatomy of an extraordinary national transformation.

### Single most impactful advice

**Stop selling this as a policy DiD and start selling it as evidence on the geography of one of the largest violence collapses in modern history.**

That one change would improve the title-adjacent framing, introduction, literature positioning, and contribution simultaneously. If the authors can then add one or two non-homicide outcomes that proxy for gang retreat, the paper’s ceiling rises further.
