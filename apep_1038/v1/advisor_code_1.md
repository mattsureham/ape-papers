The data sources (EPA Envirofacts TRI forms) and the central identification strategy (counting the contribution of new entrants relative to incumbents) are retained. Secondary treatments mentioned in the manifest (1999/2001 threshold reductions, 2014 electronic reporting, 2020 PFAS addition) are noted in background, but only the 1998 expansion is analyzed quantitatively; this is defensible for a concise AER: Insights format as long as it is framed as a representative event rather than a full-stack analysis. **Summary**

The paper documents that the 1998 TRI sector expansion mechanically increased reported form counts by about 17–18% and that this jump accounts for a large share of the apparent reversal in aggregate TRI trends at that time. Using an accounting decomposition and within-facility paired comparisons for incumbents, it shows that the reporting universe change—not actual emissions—drives the abrupt rise in TRI releases, highlighting a previously overlooked measurement trap in a foundational environmental dataset. The contribution is primarily methodological: it warns researchers relying on TRI trends that differential reporting policy can masquerade as environmental change.

**Essential Points**

1. **Causal Identification Beyond Accounting Needs Clarification:** The counterfactual relies on extrapolating pre-1998 trends for incumbent firms, but there is no explicit model (e.g., DiD) that exploits any cross-sectional variation to isolate the reporting-rule effect from broader economic or regulatory shifts. The paper treats the sector expansion as a “mechanical” increase, yet without a formal treatment of plausible confounders (e.g., macro shocks, other simultaneous regulation) the reader cannot distinguish between reporting artifacts and substantive changes unique to the new sectors. A more explicit causal framework—perhaps exploiting within-state variation in the density of expansion-sector firms or using facilities from the newly covered sectors that had voluntarily reported before 1998—would strengthen the argument. At minimum, the author should articulate and, if possible, test the assumption that nothing other than reporting expansion explains the jump in aggregate forms.

2. **Outcome Definition and Mechanism Should Be More Direct:** The paper infers the measurement artifact from counts of reported chemicals (forms) rather than the quantities of releases, which is how most empirical papers use TRI. There is no analysis showing that release quantities exhibit the same discontinuity or that new-sector entrants are quantitatively material to typical pollution outcomes. Without this, the claim that TRI declines “may be measuring administrative process” rests on an indirect margin. The paper should either (a) show that aggregate release quantities jump in tandem with form counts, or (b) explicitly argue why chemical-counts are the relevant metric for the claimed contamination of environmental econometrics.  

3. **External Validation and Scope of Implication Need Sharpening:** The manifest envisioned validation with ambient monitors and a stacked CS-DiD over multiple rule changes. The manuscript constrains itself to 1998 alone, without any external validation against independent pollution measures. This leaves open the possibility that the reporting increase coincided with actual pollution increases in the new sectors. The policy implications as stated (“every paper ... is measuring administrative process”) are too broad given the limited empirical scope. The paper should temper its claims or provide at least one auxiliary test (e.g., compare ambient air quality trends near expansion-sector hubs before/after 1998) to support the assertion that reported declines are spurious.

Given that the above issues touch the core identification and empirical scope, I would not reject outright but the authors must address them to justify the paper’s strong claims.

**Suggestions**

1. **Formalize the Empirical Strategy:** Recast the decomposition in a quasi-experimental language. For example, frame the design as a difference-in-differences comparing facilities from the newly covered sectors (treated) to continuously-covered manufacturing facilities (controls). Present regression estimates of the change in reporting intensity (or release quantities, if possible) around 1998 with facility and year fixed effects. This would allow you to estimate an explicit treatment effect rather than relying solely on aggregate extrapolations, and it would make the identifying assumptions and standard errors transparent.

2. **Leverage Release Quantities or Disaggregate Outcomes:** Expand the analysis beyond the number of chemicals reported. Compute total release quantities (e.g., on the air medium) for incumbent vs. new-sector firms before and after 1998 to demonstrate that the reporting jump translates into reported pollution increases. Alternatively, show that the average release per form remains stable, so that the increase in forms implies a proportional increase in reported quantities. This linkage would strengthen the claim that the artifact contaminates the specific outcomes used in prior literature.

3. **Validate With External Data:** Even a simple comparison using EPA ambient monitoring data would add credibility. For instance, compare trends in PM2.5 or TRI chemical concentrations near counties dominated by the newly added sectors to those near manufacturing-heavy counties. If ambient pollution does not jump in the same year despite reported releases increasing, that supports the measurement interpretation. At the very least, discuss why such data are not used, and provide suggestive evidence (e.g., no coincident economic shock in the non-manufacturing sectors) to reassure the reader that the reported increase is not reflecting real-world emissions growth.

4. **Refine the Policy Implications:** The conclusion currently extends the contamination claim to “every paper” that spans TRI rule changes, yet only one shock is analyzed. Either broaden the empirical scope (e.g., show similar decompositions for at least one of the other rule changes or discuss why those would likely be similar) or tone down the generalization. You could say the paper provides a blueprint for diagnosing reporting artifacts, with the 1998 expansion serving as a demonstrative case. If you keep the broader claim, clarify which types of studies (aggregate, national level) are most vulnerable.

5. **Benchmark the Magnitude of the Artifact:** To help researchers gauge the practical significance, compare the 17–27% artifact to typical treatment effects in the environmental economics literature. For instance, how does it compare to the estimated reduction in TRI releases attributed to the Clean Air Act? This would contextualize the importance of accounting for reporting changes. Adding a short table or figure summarizing the artifact alongside canonical treatment effects would enhance the reader’s grasp of “how big” the problem is.

6. **Document Data Construction Thoroughly:** The paper currently relies on EPA Envirofacts API counts but does not describe how new entrants are identified beyond a brief sentence. Include more detail on the matching process, treatment of facilities that temporarily drop out, and how states are defined (e.g., how territorial data are handled). If feasible, provide robustness checks where incumbents are defined using different pre-periods (e.g., 1994–1997) or where the sample excludes facilities with intermittent reporting.

7. **Clarify Standard Errors and Statistical Inference:** Some tables (e.g., Panel B of Table 4) report means without standard errors, which would help assess whether the within-facility declines differ significantly across subsets. Expand the statistical reporting, especially in the main within-facility verification, to include t-statistics, confidence intervals, and the source of the reported standard error (is it clustered?).

8. **Highlight Implications for Empirical Practice:** Close the discussion by offering concrete guidance: should researchers exclude post-1998 observations? Include facility fixed effects interacting with a post-expansion dummy? Apply the correction factors presented? A short “best-practice checklist” would translate the methodological warning into actionable advice for the field.

Overall, the paper raises an important question about measurement contamination in TRI. By tightening the empirical identification, expanding outcome coverage, and grounding the policy implications more carefully, the manuscript could make a valuable contribution to the literature on administrative data quality in environmental economics.
