## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
This paper argues that the "success story" of declining U.S. toxic emissions is partially a measurement artifact caused by changes in reporting rules. By analyzing the 1998 expansion of the Toxics Release Inventory (TRI) to new sectors, the author shows that adding new, high-intensity polluters created a "phantom baseline" that mechanically distorted aggregate trends and potentially biased a generation of environmental economics research.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this pitch excellently. The first two paragraphs of the Introduction are punchy, identify the "invisible distortion," and name-check the heavy hitters (Greenstone, Currie, Hamilton) whose work relies on this data. It successfully establishes high stakes immediately.

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
The paper’s contribution is the first systematic quantification of how administrative reporting-rule changes—specifically the 1998 expansion—mechanically contaminate the TRI trends used as the primary outcome variable in environmental economics.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It is well-differentiated. While others (Auffhammer & Mansur 2014) have noted measurement challenges generally, this paper provides a "causal estimate of the artifact" itself.
*   **World vs. Literature:** It frames the contribution as a question about the world: *Are we actually getting cleaner, or are we just changing how we count?* This is the strongest possible framing for a methodological/data paper.
*   **Clarity:** A smart economist would immediately understand this as "The TRI version of Levitt’s crime reporting paper."
*   **Making it bigger:** To make this truly "AER-sized," the author needs to do more than just *warn* about bias; they should take 1-2 seminal papers and show how their coefficients change when the artifact is removed.

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
The paper sits at the intersection of Environmental Economics and Applied Econometrics (Measurement Error).

*   **Closest Neighbors:** Hamilton (1995), Greenstone (2004), Levitt (1998) on crime reporting, and Currie et al. (2020) on diagnostic tech.
*   **Strategy:** It uses a "polite attack" strategy—building on the importance of the work by Greenstone and others while demonstrating that their primary yardstick is flawed.
*   **Missing Conversations:** The paper could benefit from connecting to the "Political Economy of Statistics" literature—how agencies choose what to measure to signal "progress."
*   **Framing:** The current framing is exactly right: it treats the TRI not just as a tool, but as an object of study.

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC
*   **Setup:** Decades of research and policy rely on the TRI to measure environmental progress.
*   **Tension:** In 1998, the EPA expanded the reporting universe. If we aggregate "old" and "new" facilities, the baseline shifts. Does this shift hide or create trends?
*   **Resolution:** The 20% jump in 1998 is entirely mechanical. By 2001, 27% of the database is an "artifact" of the rule change, not a change in behavior.
*   **Implications:** We may be overestimating the efficacy of information disclosure and regulatory policies.

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong. It feels like a detective story uncovering a "crime" in the data.

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
At a dinner party, the lead fact is: **"One-quarter of the TRI database is a measurement ghost."**
Economists would definitely lean in. The follow-up would be: "Does this overturn the finding that the Clean Air Act worked?" If the author can't answer that yet, they lose the room. The "So What?" is currently high for the *method*, but needs to be higher for the *results of previous studies*.

---

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
*   **Front-loading:** The paper is already well front-loaded. Table 2 is the "smoking gun" and appears early.
*   **Appendices:** The "Standardized Effect Sizes" (Table 5) could be moved to the main text if the author wants to emphasize the *magnitude* of the bias relative to typical treatment effects in the literature.
*   **Discussion:** Section 6 (Discussion) is the most important part of the paper. It should be expanded into a full "Re-evaluation" section where the author applies their "correction factor" to a stylized version of a major existing study.

---

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
**The Gap:** Currently, the paper is a very high-quality "Note" or "Journal of Economic Perspectives" piece. To make it a full AER article, it needs **Ambitious Re-estimation.**

**Single most impactful piece of advice:**
Pick one high-profile paper (e.g., Greenstone 2004) and show exactly how the estimated effect of the Clean Air Act changes (or disappears) once you account for the 1998 phantom baseline. Moving from "this data is biased" to "this famous result changes by X%" is the difference between a good paper and a landmark paper.
