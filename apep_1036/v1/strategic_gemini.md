## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
This paper examines whether the massive closure of local tax offices in France—a major withdrawal of state administrative presence—fueled the rise of the far-right Rassemblement National (RN). Using a staggered difference-in-differences design, it shows that while a "naive" analysis suggests a large backlash effect, this is actually a mirage: the offices were closed in precisely the declining peripheral areas where the RN was already trending upward. 

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this clearly. The second paragraph of the current introduction does an excellent job of setting up the "populism breeds from state withdrawal" narrative. However, it could be more forceful in the first paragraph about the **scale** of the shock (halving the network) to immediately signal to a general economist why this specific French case is the "gold standard" test for a global phenomenon.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
The paper’s contribution is a "cautionary tale" in political economy: it demonstrates that the correlation between state withdrawal and populism is largely driven by endogenous selection into service cuts rather than a causal reaction to the loss of the service itself.

**Evaluation:**
- **Differentiation:** It is well-differentiated from Fetzer (2019) or Autor et al. (2020). While those papers find causal effects of economic shocks, this paper provides a vital "null" counter-point regarding the *administrative* face of the state.
- **Framing:** It is framed as answering a question about the WORLD (Does the state's physical presence stop populism?), which is strong.
- **Clarity:** A smart economist would say: "It's a paper showing that the 'left behind' places were already leaving before the tax office left them."
- **Bigger Contribution:** To make this bigger, the author needs to explore **why** it’s a null. Is it because the service was replaced by digital tools? Or because the tax office is a "hated" symbol of the state, making its removal less painful than a hospital or school? 

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
The paper sits at the intersection of the "Geography of Discontent" (Rodríguez-Pose) and the "Credible DiD" (Callaway & Sant’Anna) literatures.

- **Neighbors:** Fetzer (2019, AER) on austerity; Algan et al. (2017) on the European trust crisis; Guilly (2014) on peripheral France.
- **Positioning:** It should position itself as a **skeptical check** on the "places that don't matter" literature. It’s not attacking the theory, but tightening the causal requirements for proving it.
- **Missing Conversations:** The paper should speak more to the **public economics of digitalization**. The reason these offices closed was the shift to online filing. Is the "withdrawal" less painful if the service is now on a smartphone?

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC
- **Setup:** The French state undergoes its largest administrative contraction in history.
- **Tension:** Populism is surging; politicians and scholars fear the "desertification" of the state is the cause.
- **Resolution:** It’s a selection effect. The "backlash" existed before the closures began.
- **Implications:** Policy-makers might not need to fear the electoral consequences of "rationalizing" the state as much as they think, but they should realize they are retreating from already-alienated areas.

**Evaluation:** The narrative is very strong. It follows a "mystery" structure (The 2.1pp effect that wasn't) which is highly effective for AER.

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
At an economist dinner party, I’d lead with: *"France closed half its tax offices to save money, and it didn't cost the government a single vote—because the people there had already given up on them."*

People would lean in. The follow-up would be: *"What about hospitals?"* or *"Did the online services actually work?"* This suggests the paper has "legs" but needs to address the "substitution" mechanism (digital vs. physical) more deeply to satisfy a general audience.

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
- **Front-loading:** The paper is well-structured. Table 2 and 3 are the "money" results and appear early.
- **Appendix:** Move the detailed breakdown of French "nuance codes" entirely to the appendix (A.2). 
- **Discussion:** Section 6 is currently a bit short. It needs to expand on the "Digitalization" aspect. If the tax office is gone but I can now do everything on my phone, is the "state" actually withdrawing, or just changing form?

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The gap between this and an AER acceptance is **ambition regarding mechanism**. Currently, it is a very high-quality "No" to a popular hypothesis. To get into the AER, it needs a "No, but..." or a "No, because...".

**Single biggest improvement:** Use the "France Services" (multi-administration counters) data to see if the *substitution* of a tax office with a general-purpose service point mitigated the backlash. If you can show that "Physical presence matters, but the *specific* agency doesn't," that is a much more profound AER-level discovery than just a null result.
