**Summary**

This paper documents that states with municipal broadband preemption laws experienced significantly lower Medicare telehealth utilization during and after the COVID-19 pandemic—a 2.4 percentage point average shortfall and a 6.4 point deficit at the peak in 2020Q2—using a difference-in-differences design with quarterly CMS telehealth data. The author interprets this as a “restriction trap,” where regulatory suppression of broadband capacity in normal times manifests as measurable welfare costs in crises. Robustness checks (event study, jackknife, wild bootstrap, phase decomposition) support the main finding, while the narrowing gap over time is linked to federal broadband investments post-2021.

**Essential Points**

1. **Credibility of the Parallel Trends Argument.** The empirical design assumes treated and control states would have trended similarly absent preemption, but only a single quarter of pre-treatment data is available, and telehealth adoption in 2020Q1 is already influenced by early pandemic dynamics. The paper argues that telehealth was “essentially zero,” yet there may still be meaningful differences in how quickly states scaled telehealth even before CMS’s full expansion. Without richer pre-trends (e.g., earlier telehealth proxies or related health-care utilization), the DiD identifying assumption is weakly supported. The authors should provide additional evidence that pre-pandemic trends in digital health readiness or alternative outcomes (e.g., broadband take-up growth, telehealth billing counts, or proxy measures like video visit share among private insurers) did not systematically differ between treated and control states.

2. **Treatment Definition and Timing.** Preemption laws span 1997–2019, yet the analysis treats all 22 states as contemporaneously treated post-2020, without exploiting variation in enactment timing. This collapses a potentially useful staggered treatment structure and raises concerns that unobserved heterogeneity among early adopters drives the results. The robustness check dropping “early adopters” helps, but the paper should more directly model treatment timing (e.g., event-study with state-level treatment dates, or inclusion of leads/lags of enactment) to ensure that the result is not confounded by omitted state characteristics correlated with early preemption. Given that some of the earliest states also tended to have different health-care systems or rural-urban mixes, this is particularly important for causal interpretation.

3. **Mechanism and Rural/Urban Heterogeneity.** The paper frames the mechanism as constrained rural broadband infrastructure, yet the triple-difference finds no differential rural effect, which contradicts the central narrative. The proposed explanation—that preemption affects statewide competition—is plausible but underdeveloped. The authors should more rigorously test this mechanism: for example, by using FCC Form 477 or ACS data to show that preempted states experienced larger declines or slower growth in broadband speeds/adoption, or by analyzing Medicare data to see if the effect is stronger among beneficiaries with known digital access barriers (e.g., low-income counties). Without such evidence, the “restriction trap” narrative risks being speculative.

**Suggestions**

1. **Strengthen the Parallel Trends Argument.** Even if telehealth was nascent pre-COVID, supplementary evidence of parallelism could come from related outcomes or pretreatment characteristics. For instance, use FCC Form 477 data or ACS broadband subscription trends to show that connectivity growth rates were similar before 2020 across treatment and control states. Alternatively, construct a placebo outcome—such as county-level in-person primary care utilization during early 2020—and demonstrate that preemption status did not predict those trends, reinforcing that telehealth divergence is due to broadband supply constraints rather than broader health system differences. Transparency about the limitations of the single pre-period and a clear discussion of how that affects inference would also improve the manuscript.

2. **Exploit Staggered Treatment Timing.** The dataset already contains the timing of each state’s preemption law. Consider estimating an event study where the “event” is the enactment date (or another relevant legislative milestone), and then interacting that with post-2020 periods to isolate whether the timing matters. Alternatively, reweight the sample (e.g., by splitting into cohorts based on preemption year) and present estimates for each cohort to show that the effect is not driven by a particular bunch of early or late adopters. This would alleviate concerns that the binary treatment mask is conflating states that adopted preemption for different reasons.

3. **Deepen the Mechanism Analysis.** Since the rural interaction is null, more evidence is needed to substantiate the “restriction trap.” Use the FCC Form 477 data (which the manifest indicates is available) to compare broadband provider counts, speeds, or deployment before and during COVID across treated and control states, highlighting whether preempted states lagged in the specific infrastructure that telehealth requires. If the data permit, interact preemption with county-level broadband coverage or with measures of provider competition to see if the telehealth gap is concentrated in areas with the weakest infrastructure. If no empirical heterogeneity is found, acknowledge that the mechanism may not be the rural digital divide but rather broader incumbent-friendly market structures; the narrative should align with the evidence.

4. **Address Potential Confounders Related to Health Policy.** While the paper controls for Medicaid expansion and runs a robustness check limiting the sample, preemption status might still correlate with other pandemic-response policies (e.g., provider outreach, telehealth reimbursement generosity, or health system digitization investments). Consider controlling for state-level measures of pandemic severity (cases/hospitalizations) or CMS telehealth reimbursement policies if available. Alternatively, include time-varying state controls (e.g., quarterly unemployment, COVID case rates) to ensure that the telehealth gap is not simply reflecting different pandemic burdens or responses.

5. **Clarify the Scope of Inference.** The paper states that it measures the effect on Medicare telehealth utilization but suggests broader welfare implications in the discussion. It would help to explicitly clarify that the analysis is limited to Medicare Part B and may not directly generalize to other populations. If possible, note whether the CMS dataset includes low-income beneficiaries or other groups, and consider exploring whether the telehealth deficit is larger in states with higher shares of vulnerable populations. Doing so would better ground the policy relevance without overextending the claim.

6. **Enhance Event Study Presentation.** The event-study table currently lacks coefficients; only a single row is shown. Including the full table or a figure showing the point estimates and confidence intervals for each quarter would make the dynamic story more persuasive. If the results already exist, present them graphically and highlight the convergence trend after 2021 that the text emphasizes.

By addressing these points—particularly the parallel trends justification, treatment timing, and mechanism—the authors can better establish the causal link between municipal broadband preemption and COVID-era telehealth outcomes.
