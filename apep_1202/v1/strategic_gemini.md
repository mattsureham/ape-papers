## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
The paper examines how state-level laws prohibiting municipalities from building their own broadband networks—passed years before the pandemic to protect incumbent telecoms—unintentionally crippled healthcare delivery during COVID-19. By comparing states with and without these "preemption laws," the author finds a 14% reduction in telehealth utilization among Medicare beneficiaries, illustrating a "restriction trap" where anti-competitive regulations create latent vulnerabilities that explode during crises.

**Evaluation:** The paper actually articulates this quite well in the first two paragraphs. It uses a concrete example (Massachusetts vs. Texas) to ground the reader and moves quickly to the causal estimate. However, it could be punchier regarding the *economic* mechanism.
*   **The pitch it should have:** "Anti-competitive regulations are often evaluated based on steady-state market outcomes. This paper shows that such regulations create a 'restriction trap': by suppressing redundant or marginal infrastructure investment to protect incumbents, they strip the economy of the 'option value' of capacity, leading to catastrophic welfare losses when demand shocks (like COVID-19) occur."

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
The paper identifies the "option value" of infrastructure capacity by showing how preemption laws—which had negligible effects on broadband adoption pre-2020—caused a massive divergence in telehealth access during a national emergency.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It differentiates itself from Béland et al. (2020) by moving from labor outcomes to health/infrastructure resilience.
*   **Question vs. Gap:** It frames itself as answering a question about the **WORLD** (Why did some regions fail to adapt to digital health?).
*   **Clarity:** A smart economist would say: "This paper shows that protecting telecoms from municipal competition isn't just a deadweight loss in price—it’s a disaster for resilience."
*   **Make it bigger:** The contribution would be massive if it could link these foregone visits to **health outcomes** (mortality, hospitalizations). Without that, it’s a paper about "utilization," which is a proxy for welfare but not welfare itself.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
*   **Closest Neighbors:** Béland et al. (2020) on preemption/employment; Kolko (2012) on broadband/growth; and the growing "Resilience" literature (e.g., supply chain robustness).
*   **Positioning:** It should move away from being a "COVID paper" and toward being a "Political Economy of Infrastructure" paper. It should build on the "Incomplete Contracts" or "Option Value" literatures.
*   **Unexpected Connection:** It should speak to the **Antitrust/Competition** literature. The "restriction trap" is essentially a story about the hidden costs of state-sanctioned monopolies.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC
*   **Setup:** Incumbent telecoms lobby for state protection; the world looks fine because private investment covers the "average" user.
*   **Tension:** A sudden shock makes "average" infrastructure insufficient.
*   **Resolution:** Preempted states can't spin up capacity; utilization craters relative to free-market/municipal-active states.
*   **Implications:** Regulatory cost-benefit analysis is fundamentally broken if it ignores tail-risk resilience.

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong. The "restriction trap" is a compelling conceptual hook that elevates this from a simple DiD exercise to a broader economic lesson.

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
*   **Lead Fact:** "States that banned cities from building fiber saw a 14% drop in doctor visits when the world went into lockdown."
*   **Reaction:** Lean in. It’s a clean story of "Rent-seeking kills."
*   **Follow-up:** "Did people actually die, or did they just wait three months for a checkup?" (This is the paper's Achilles' heel).

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
*   **Move to Appendix:** The "Standardized Effect Sizes" table (Table 5) is overkill for the main text.
*   **Front-load:** The "Mechanism" discussion on market structure (page 7) is actually the most "AER" part of the paper. It should be expanded and brought forward to explain *why* this isn't just about rural areas.
*   **Conclusion:** It’s a bit repetitive. It should spend more time on the "Option Value" of public investment.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The gap is **Scope and Magnitude.** Currently, it is a very clean, very competent "Impact of X on Y" paper. To be a "Top 3" paper, it needs to prove the welfare loss is more than just a lower number on a screen.

*   **Single most impactful advice:** Connect the "foregone telehealth" to a "hard" health outcome (e.g., Medicare claims for avoidable ER visits or mortality). If you can show that preemption laws literally increased mortality because people couldn't see a doctor digitally, the paper is a "slam dunk" for the AER.
