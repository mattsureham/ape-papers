## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether old state laws that blocked municipal broadband made states less able to shift medical care online when COVID hit. The core claim is that states with municipal broadband preemption saw meaningfully lower Medicare telehealth use during the pandemic, suggesting that seemingly sector-specific infrastructure regulation can have large downstream costs when a crisis suddenly raises the value of digital capacity.

A busy economist should care because the paper is trying to say something broader than “broadband matters”: it is about how pre-crisis regulatory choices shape crisis resilience. That is a potentially AER-worthy idea if the paper leans into it hard enough.

**Does the paper articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Not quite. The opening is vivid, but still reads like a sectoral policy paper about broadband and telehealth. The big idea — that dormant anti-competition regulation can become costly when the world changes — arrives later and should be the organizing frame from sentence one.

**What the first two paragraphs should say instead:**

> Many regulations look cheap in normal times because they suppress capacity whose value is only revealed in rare states of the world. This paper studies one such case: state laws that blocked municipalities from building broadband networks. When COVID abruptly moved medical care online, these laws may have reduced the digital infrastructure available for telehealth precisely when it became most valuable.
>
> Using state-quarter Medicare telehealth data from 2020–2025, I compare states that had municipal broadband preemption laws on the books before the pandemic to those that did not. I find that preempted states had lower telehealth uptake, with the largest gaps in the acute phase of COVID. The broader implication is that anti-competitive infrastructure regulation can create hidden vulnerabilities that only become visible during shocks.

That is the pitch. Right now the paper has the ingredients, but the paper’s own opening undersells the “why economists beyond this niche should care.”

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:**  
The paper argues that state municipal broadband preemption laws reduced Medicare telehealth uptake during COVID, revealing a broader crisis-resilience cost of anti-competitive infrastructure regulation.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?
Only partially.

The current contribution sits somewhere between:
1. broadband → economic outcomes,
2. broadband → telehealth/digital access,
3. municipal broadband/preemption political economy,
4. COVID telehealth adoption.

The paper names these literatures, but it does not yet sharply distinguish what is new relative to each one. A smart reader may still summarize it as: **“another reduced-form paper showing broadband affects a downstream outcome.”** That is the risk.

To differentiate, the paper needs to say more explicitly:
- prior broadband papers study steady-state outcomes like employment, prices, adoption, firm formation;
- prior telehealth papers emphasize reimbursement, provider behavior, and patient demographics;
- prior municipal broadband papers focus on telecom competition or local economic effects;
- **this paper’s novelty is the interaction between dormant regulation and an extreme demand shock.**

That last piece is the only angle with real top-journal bite.

### WORLD question or LITERATURE gap?
It is mixed, but it should be much more firmly framed as a **question about the world**:

- Weak framing: “I contribute to three literatures.”
- Strong framing: “Do regulations that suppress infrastructure create hidden social costs that materialize only in crises?”

The latter is stronger and more memorable. Right now the paper drifts too quickly into literature-accounting mode.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?
Not cleanly enough. They would probably say:

> “It’s a DiD on municipal broadband restrictions and telehealth during COVID.”

That is not enough for AER. You want them to say:

> “It shows that anti-competitive infrastructure regulation can lower crisis adaptation capacity, using telehealth during COVID as the setting.”

That is a much bigger statement.

### What would make the contribution bigger?
Specific ways to enlarge it:

1. **A broader crisis-resilience framing.**  
   The telehealth result should be presented as one instance of a more general phenomenon: regulation affects not just levels, but adaptability to shocks.

2. **Stronger downstream consequences.**  
   Telehealth use is an intermediate outcome. If the paper could connect to missed care, delayed follow-up, chronic disease management, preventable utilization, or health outcomes, the contribution gets much bigger. Right now the paper stops one step too early.

3. **A clearer mechanism beyond “broadband.”**  
   The paper hints that this is about market structure and competitive pressure, not just rural coverage. If that is true, then evidence on prices, speeds, provider availability, or video-capable access would make the contribution feel less generic.

4. **A sharper comparison.**  
   The best version compares two models of infrastructure governance under stress: places where public entry was allowed versus prohibited. That makes the policy stakes clearer than just “treated vs. control.”

5. **More than one salient outcome.**  
   If telehealth is the headline, a second outcome like total outpatient continuity, specialist access, or substitution away from in-person care would make the paper look less like a one-outcome application.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

### Closest neighbors
The paper appears closest to a few clusters:

1. **Municipal broadband / preemption / telecom competition**
   - Béland, Feng, and Wang-type work on municipal broadband and local economic outcomes
   - Gillett, Lehr, Osorio, Sirbu on municipal broadband policy and competition
   - Ford on municipal broadband restrictions / policy debates

2. **Broadband and downstream economic outcomes**
   - Kolko (broadband and local growth/employment)
   - Whitacre, Gallardo, Strover (broadband and rural economic outcomes)
   - Dettling (broadband and labor market participation/home production)

3. **Telehealth adoption and digital divide**
   - Mehrotra et al. on telehealth take-up
   - CMS/descriptive telehealth papers
   - Papers on broadband access and telemedicine utilization during COVID

4. **More general resilience / preparedness / infrastructure under shocks**
   - This is actually the conversation the paper should try harder to enter, though it currently doesn’t really.

### How should the paper position itself relative to those neighbors?
**Build on**, not attack. The right move is:

- Build on municipal broadband papers by showing costs outside the telecom market.
- Build on telehealth papers by showing supply-side regulation, not just demographics or reimbursement, shaped uptake.
- Build on broadband papers by showing that the value of broadband capacity is state-contingent and spikes in crises.

The paper should not present itself as overturning the neighboring literature. It should present itself as connecting them in a way that generates a bigger insight.

### Too narrow or too broad?
Paradoxically, both.

- **Too narrow** in that the outcome is one Medicare utilization measure, and the introduction spends a lot of time in the telehealth/broadband niche.
- **Too broad** in that “restriction trap” is offered as a sweeping conceptual claim with limited evidence beyond this single setting.

The solution is not to be less ambitious. It is to be **more disciplined**: one broad claim, one compelling setting, and clear limits.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?
It should probably engage more with:
- disaster resilience and adaptation,
- infrastructure option value / excess capacity / resilience economics,
- market structure and innovation/adoption under shocks,
- healthcare access and care substitution under COVID,
- public utility regulation / state capacity.

The “restriction trap” concept will otherwise feel coined rather than earned. If the authors want that phrase to stick, they need to locate it in a broader economics conversation about option value, resilience, and flexibility.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?
Not yet. It is currently having the conversation:
> “How does broadband policy affect telehealth?”

That is a respectable field-journal conversation.

The more impactful conversation is:
> “How do pre-shock regulations shape an economy’s ability to reallocate activity when a shock hits?”

That is the conversation top general-interest readers will care about.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Before this paper, we know:
- COVID caused a massive telehealth expansion,
- broadband matters for digital access,
- states differ in municipal broadband regulation,
- these laws were usually justified in narrow telecom-policy terms.

### Tension
The motivating puzzle should be:
- if broadband differences looked modest in normal times, did regulations that limited public broadband entry create hidden vulnerabilities that only became costly during COVID?
- more generally, do anti-competitive infrastructure rules reduce crisis adaptability?

That is the real tension. The current paper gets there, but only after some wandering.

### Resolution
The paper’s resolution is:
- states with preemption laws had lower Medicare telehealth utilization after COVID,
- the gap was largest when telehealth mattered most,
- it narrowed over time but persisted.

### Implications
Implications should be:
- infrastructure regulation has insurance value implications, not just static efficiency effects;
- policymakers should evaluate regulations partly by how they affect system adaptability to shocks;
- anti-competitive restrictions may impose costs outside the sector they directly regulate.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
**Serviceable, not strong.**  
It is better than a random collection of tables, but the paper still feels like it discovered an empirical result and then built the “restriction trap” story around it. That can be fine, but then the story has to be developed more carefully and earlier.

Right now the narrative is:
1. here is a policy,
2. here is an outcome,
3. here is a DiD,
4. here is a label (“restriction trap”).

The narrative it **should** be telling is:
1. some regulations suppress capacity whose value is latent;
2. COVID revealed that latent value in digital infrastructure;
3. municipal broadband preemption provides a clean setting;
4. telehealth is a high-stakes test case;
5. the findings imply broader lessons about regulation and resilience.

That is a stronger setup-tension-resolution arc.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?
I would lead with:

> States that blocked municipal broadband before the pandemic had substantially lower Medicare telehealth use when COVID forced care online, with the largest gap in 2020 Q2.

That is simple, intuitive, and potentially sticky.

### Would economists lean in?
Some would. Not all.

People interested in health, public economics, IO/regulation, infrastructure, and political economy would lean in. A generic macro or theory audience might not — unless the paper frames the result as evidence on **resilience and option value of infrastructure capacity**, not merely telehealth.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Almost immediately:

> “Interesting — but is this really about telehealth, or is telehealth just a proxy for something broader like care continuity or digital readiness?”

That is the key follow-up. And it reveals the paper’s current limitation: the outcome is interesting, but it still feels intermediate.

A second likely question:

> “Why should I believe municipal broadband preemption mattered much for actual broadband conditions by 2020?”

Again, that is partly an empirical question for referees, but editorially it means the paper needs a sharper mechanism story in the framing.

### If findings are modest, is the null/modest result interesting?
The average effect is moderate, not huge; the acute-period effect is the genuinely interesting one. The paper is smart to foreground the timing. The long-run narrowing is also useful, but the paper should not oversell persistence as the main event. The real action is: **the regulation mattered most when the stakes were highest.**

That is the “so what.”
