**Idea Fidelity**

The paper largely follows the original idea manifesto. It combines IPEDS closure data with QWI county-level employment flows, distinguishes treated counties (with closures) from control counties (for-profit presence but no closures), and exploits the staggered and chain-wide nature of the regulatory wave. The manifest’s emphasis on a continuous intensity DiD, robustness to enrollment weights, and an IV driven by chain exposure is reflected in the paper. However, the write-up omits some nuance from the manifest: the IV specification abstracts from closure timing (using a single post-2015 indicator) rather than aligning with each chain’s shutdown year, and the manifest’s discussion of alternative sectors (health care, accommodation) as potential spillovers is only cursorily addressed. Overall, the paper preserves the core research question and data sources but could engage more explicitly with the manifest’s detailed identification narrative.

**Summary**

This paper studies whether federal regulatory closures of for-profit colleges between 2013 and 2018 disrupted local labor markets. Using county-level IPEDS closure counts combined with QWI employment data, the author implements DiD and IV specifications (including chain exposure) across education, health care, accommodation, and total private-sector employment. The findings are consistently null: even the IV estimates yield economically negligible effects, suggesting that the closure wave had no measurable spillover on county employment.

**Essential Points**

1. **Timing of Treatment Intensity**: Equation (1) defines the treatment as county-level closure count multiplied by a post indicator that activates at the first closure year. Because most counties experienced multiple closure events spread over 2013–2018, this construction implicitly attributes the marginal effect of each closure to the same post period, potentially conflating early closures with later ones. This undermines the interpretation of “per-closure” effects and makes it difficult to assess whether closures closer in time to a given observation mattered more. The authors should adopt a dynamic treatment measure that varies over time (e.g., cumulative closures up to year _t_) or interact the closure count with a year-specific post indicator that honors the timing of each shutdown. Without this, the estimated β may conflate closures of very different vintages and leave open the possibility that the null result arises from attenuation rather than true absence of effects.

2. **Validity of Control Group / Parallel Trends**: Treated counties are substantially larger and more urban than controls (\autoref{tab:summary}) and the event-study coefficients for education employment suggest positive pre-trends (even if statistically insignificant). These differences raise concerns that the closure counties would have experienced different employment trajectories regardless of the closures, particularly if they were on sustained growth paths typical of metropolitan areas. While the paper reports county fixed effects and standard pre-trend tests, the absence of a more granular matching or weighting strategy means the identifying variation may come from comparing fundamentally different counties. The authors should either (a) implement coarsened exact matching or inverse probability weighting on pre-treatment employment levels/trends, (b) re-run the event study using demeaned residuals from a balanced set of pre-treatment periods to check for remaining differential trends, or (c) provide support for the assumption that treated and control counties would have trended similarly absent closures (e.g., by showing that placebo “pseudo-closure” years produce null effects).

3. **IV Timing and Exclusion Restriction**: The chain IV uses indicator HasChain × Post2015, yet the three major chains shut down in distinct years (Corinthian 2015, ITT 2016, ECA 2018). Collapsing to a single post-2015 period risks including time after Corinthian closures but before ITT/ECA closures, and more importantly, it assumes that the only channel through which pre-existing chain presence affects employment is the simultaneous closure. If, for example, an ITT campus located in a growing metro attracted investment or acted as a persistent employer before 2016, this would violate the exclusion restriction. At minimum the IV should be structured to align each chain’s post period with its actual shutdown year (e.g., HasITT × 2016+, HasCorinthian × 2015+, etc.), and the authors should discuss and test whether chain presence predicts employment trends prior to the regulatory shock to bolster the instrument’s credibility.

**Suggestions**

1. **Dynamic Event Study Specification**: To better capture both the staggered timing and intensity of closures, consider estimating event studies with cumulative closures (or closures-by-year) as the treatment. This would illuminate whether employment responses materialized shortly after mass shutdowns or whether the null result holds even when focusing on narrower windows around the largest events (Corinthian 2015, ITT 2016). Presenting figures for the Sun-Abraham estimates would also help readers assess the parallel-trends assumption visually.

2. **Exposure-Based Heterogeneity**: The manifest mentions that 83 counties experienced ≥3 closures, and 5 counties experienced a disproportionate number of closures (e.g., Los Angeles, Cook). Estimate heterogeneous effects by stratifying on closure intensity (e.g., none, low, high) or county size to test whether very large shocks produced detectable disruptions. This stratification could also reveal whether the null average masks positive effects in the most affected counties offset by zeros elsewhere.

3. **Sectoral Spillovers Beyond Education**: The paper briefly mentions health care and accommodation but could deepen the analysis by investigating other sectors with plausible linkages (e.g., administrative/support services, retail trade). Doing so would test whether the closures triggered broader multiplier effects (positive or negative). If data permit, explore whether nearby counties (within commuting zones) experienced spillover effects since displaced employment may have shifted across county lines.

4. **Placebo Outcomes and Falsification**: Extend the placebo analysis beyond total private employment to sectors unlikely to be affected (e.g., manufacturing in predominantly non-manufacturing counties). Demonstrating null effects in genuinely pristine sectors would strengthen confidence that the null findings for education and health care are not driven by noise or specification issues.

5. **Discussion of Power and Minimal Detectable Effects**: The paper concludes that closures were a “non-event,” but readers would benefit from a formal discussion of statistical power. What effect size could the design detect given the data? Reporting minimal detectable effects alongside standardized effect sizes (already computed) would contextualize the null findings. If the design is underpowered to detect, for instance, a 0.5% employment change, the policy implication would be weaker.

6. **Mechanism Exploration**: The mechanism table shows flat hiring/separation effects, but it would be informative to explore whether education-sector employment shifted towards nearby community colleges or public institutions. If enrollment data for local community colleges are available, a complementary analysis could test the hypothesis that displaced staff were reabsorbed within the education sector. Attachment to qualitative sources (e.g., news reports about community college expansions) could also bolster the reallocation story.

7. **Clarify Treatment Definition**: The treatment is currently defined as “Closures × Post.” Provide a precise definition in the text (e.g., how “Closures” are counted—only initial shutdown or all closures per county? Are closures weighted by size?). Additionally, explain how post-treatment periods are defined for counties with multiple closure years. Transparency here makes it easier to interpret β and to replicate the specification.

8. **Instrumental Variable Diagnostics**: Beyond reporting the first-stage F-statistic (which is already stated to exceed 6,800), provide the first-stage coefficient and the temporal evolution of instrumented closures. Show that HasChain × Post is strongly correlated with the actual timing of closures (e.g., a table showing average closure counts pre- and post-shock for exposed vs. unexposed counties). Also, test the IV’s exclusion restriction by estimating the effect of HasChain on employment in the pre-treatment period; any pre-trend would cast doubt on the instrument.

9. **Discussion of External Validity**: The conclusion that quality regulation “need not be tempered” rests on the assumption that for-profit college employment shares are small relative to county economies. Explicitly discuss to what extent these findings generalize to other kinds of institutional closures (e.g., hospitals) or to counties where for-profits were a larger share of employer base (e.g., small towns). Such discussion would help policymakers understand when null findings apply.

10. **Supplemental Data Visualization**: Include maps or histograms showing the geographic distribution of closure intensity and its overlap with employment trends. Visual aids would reinforce the narrative that these shocks were widespread but perhaps spatially diffuse, which underpins the claim that closures were too small to move county-level aggregates.

Implementing these suggestions would sharpen the identification narrative, deepen the empirical exploration, and make the null findings more compelling to readers concerned about labor market spillovers.
