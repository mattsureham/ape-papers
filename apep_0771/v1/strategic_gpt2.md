## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether the mass closure of for-profit colleges during the 2010s—closures that displaced students and eliminated over 1,200 campuses—also damaged local labor markets in the counties where those campuses operated. The headline finding is no: despite the scale and salience of the regulatory crackdown, county-level employment in education and nearby sectors barely moves, suggesting that this form of education-sector regulation imposed little detectable local employment cost.

That is a potentially interesting pitch, but the paper does not yet deliver it as cleanly or forcefully as it should in the first two paragraphs. Right now the introduction starts with examples of closures and then a literature review of student outcomes. That is serviceable, but not sharp. The paper’s best asset is the juxtaposition between a visibly large policy shock and an apparently absent local labor-market response. The opening should lead with that surprise.

### What the first two paragraphs should say instead

Something like:

> Between 2013 and 2018, federal enforcement actions shut down more than 1,200 for-profit college campuses across the United States, including chain-wide collapses such as ITT Tech and Corinthian. These closures displaced tens of thousands of students, but they also removed campuses as local employers and spending hubs. Did these highly visible institutional failures leave scars in local labor markets?
>
> This paper shows that they did not—at least not in county-level employment data. Despite the breadth of the closure wave, I find essentially zero effect on local education employment, adjacent service sectors, or total private employment. The result matters because it speaks to a broad question about local adjustment: when a service-sector institution disappears, does it create meaningful place-based economic damage, or are such shocks too small and diffuse to matter for local labor markets? In this case, the answer is the latter, with direct implications for how economists think about regulatory costs in higher education.

That is the pitch. Start with the fact, not the literature gap.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper argues that the large wave of for-profit college closures in the 2010s did not generate economically meaningful county-level labor-market disruption, implying that enforcement against low-quality colleges carried little detectable local employment cost.

### Is this clearly differentiated from the closest papers?

Only partially. The paper distinguishes itself from the student-outcomes literature by saying “everyone studies students; I study local labor markets.” That is a distinction, but not yet a compelling one. The reader’s immediate reaction is: “Yes, but why should county labor markets move much when some strip-mall colleges close?” The paper needs stronger differentiation from two kinds of neighbors:

1. **For-profit-college papers**: those study students, debt, earnings, returns, and regulation.
2. **Local labor-demand-shock papers**: those study plant closures, large employers, multipliers, and spatial incidence.

At present the paper sits awkwardly between these literatures without fully persuading the reader that the shock is important enough for the second literature or central enough for the first.

### World question or literature-gap question?

Too much “gap in the literature,” not enough “question about the world.” The strong question is:

- When regulators shut down low-quality educational institutions, do communities bear meaningful local employment costs?

That is a world question. The paper instead often frames itself as “no one has studied local labor-market effects of for-profit closures.” That is weaker and sounds derivative.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?

Right now they would probably say: “It’s a county-level DiD on for-profit college closures, and the effects are basically zero.”

That is not fatal, but it is not memorable. For AER, the introduction must make the reader say something more like:

- “Interesting—this is evidence that even a nationally salient wave of institutional closures had essentially no local employment footprint, which says something about local adjustment and the incidence of quality regulation.”

The paper is not there yet.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Several possibilities:

1. **A different outcome variable**  
   The current county-by-2-digit-sector outcomes are too aggregated relative to the likely size of the shock. The paper itself admits this. A bigger paper would show effects on:
   - establishment counts in education,
   - worker flows for employees of closing campuses,
   - commercial vacancies near campuses,
   - local wage bills,
   - unemployment insurance claims,
   - commuting patterns,
   - neighborhood-level spending or business activity around campus ZIP codes.

2. **A different mechanism**  
   Right now the mechanism section is essentially “maybe they reallocated quickly.” That is conjecture. A much stronger paper would document where the workers went:
   - community colleges,
   - other education providers,
   - outside education,
   - out of the county.

3. **A different comparison**  
   The paper could become more interesting if it explicitly compared these closures to plant closures, hospital closures, or military base contractions. Then the contribution is not just “null effect in this setting,” but “service-sector institutional closures differ fundamentally from manufacturing employer shocks.”

4. **A different framing**  
   The largest possible framing is not “for-profit colleges,” but:
   - the local incidence of regulation,
   - when institutional closures matter for places,
   - or the difference between employment shocks that are salient politically and those that are economically trivial locally.

That would elevate the paper beyond a sector-specific policy exercise.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

### Closest neighbors

The closest neighbors seem to be:

1. **Cellini, Darolia, and Turner / Cellini-related work on gainful employment and for-profit colleges**  
   Especially papers on returns, regulation, and institutional quality in the for-profit sector.

2. **Armona, Chakrabarti, Lovenheim-type work on for-profit attendance, debt, and student outcomes**  
   The paper cites some of this.

3. **Moretti (2010) on local multipliers**  
   For the claim that institutions can create local spillovers.

4. **Greenstone, Hornbeck, and Moretti (2010)** or related place-based local labor-demand shock papers  
   For large employer entry/exit and local effects.

5. **Notowidigdo and coauthors / Blanchard and Katz / broader regional adjustment literature**  
   For spatial incidence and labor-market adjustment to local shocks.

Potentially also:
- **Liu et al.** on community college responses to for-profit exit,
- hospital closure literature,
- worker displacement literature.

### How should it position itself?

It should **build on** the for-profit literature and **contrast with** the local-shocks literature.

Not “attack” either one. The right message is:

- The for-profit literature has established student harms and policy relevance.
- The local-shocks literature teaches us that some institutional closures can have meaningful place-based effects.
- This paper shows that for-profit college closures look much more like a shock with rapid reallocation and minimal local spillovers than like a classic major-employer shock.

That is the intellectual location.

### Too narrow or too broad?

Currently a bit **too narrow in substance** and **too broad in aspiration**.

- Too narrow because the actual exercise is county employment around one specific type of closure.
- Too broad because the paper gestures at “local multipliers,” “institutional closures,” and “regulation” without fully delivering evidence that speaks broadly to those literatures.

### What literature does it seem unaware of?

It should speak more directly to:

- **hospital closures / health care provider closures**, where the institutional closure has both service and labor-market effects;
- **worker reallocation/displacement** literature, if the implied story is fast absorption;
- **urban/service-sector agglomeration** or small-employer local spillover literature;
- possibly **regulation and incidence**: when the costs of quality regulation fall on firms, workers, students, or places.

### Is it having the right conversation?

Not quite. The current conversation is “student outcomes vs local labor markets in for-profit higher education.” That is too second-order.

The stronger conversation is:

- **What kinds of institution closures matter for local economies?**
- **How large are the collateral place-based costs of quality regulation?**
- **Why do some employer exits create local scars while others do not?**

That is a better room to be in.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

For-profit colleges expanded rapidly, became heavily dependent on federal aid, and then faced a wave of enforcement-driven closures. These institutions were not just schools; they were also local employers with physical campuses.

### Tension

The tension should be: these closures were politically salient and visibly large, but it is unclear whether they were economically large in the places where they happened. Existing work focuses on students, leaving open whether communities also paid a price.

### Resolution

The paper finds little to no detectable county-level labor-market effect in education, adjacent services, or total private employment.

### Implications

The paper wants the implication to be: policymakers can regulate low-quality colleges without fearing meaningful collateral local labor-market harm.

That is plausible, but the broader implication should be more disciplined: not all highly visible institutional closures are meaningful local labor-demand shocks.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has a **serviceable but not fully convincing** arc. The biggest weakness is that the tension is underdeveloped. The paper keeps asserting that campuses are “employers, training centers, and sources of local economic activity,” but it never makes the reader really believe that county employment should move. Then when the paper finds nulls, the result feels less like a puzzle resolved and more like what one might have expected all along.

That is dangerous for top-journal positioning.

### If it is a collection of results looking for a story, what story should it tell?

The story should be:

> Economists often worry that shutting down institutions has local collateral damage. But the local incidence of closure depends on the type of institution. For-profit colleges were important to students and politically salient, yet economically too small or too easily replaced to generate county-level disruption. This reveals a boundary condition for the local-shocks literature and for debates about the costs of regulation.

That is the story. Not “here are several null regressions around closures.”

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party?

“More than 1,200 for-profit college campuses closed in the 2010s, and county employment data suggest the surrounding labor markets barely noticed.”

That is the best fact in the paper.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

Some would lean in briefly because the contrast is surprising on its face. But many would immediately ask, “Were these institutions just too small to matter?” If the answer is yes, interest falls fast.

That is the core strategic problem. The paper’s main finding is a null, and the paper itself eventually concedes a likely explanation that undercuts the surprise: the institutions may have been too small relative to county labor markets, and the outcome measure may be too aggregated to detect their disappearance. Once the reader reaches that conclusion, the punchline weakens.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Almost certainly:

- “So where did the workers go?”
- Or: “Is the null because there was real reallocation, or because your outcome is too coarse?”
- Or: “Would this show up with establishment-level or worker-level data?”

Those are the questions the paper most needs to answer, and right now it does not.

### Is the null result itself interesting?

Potentially yes—but only if the paper can persuade the reader that a meaningful effect was plausible ex ante and that the null is substantively informative rather than mechanically unsurprising.

Right now it risks feeling like a failed detection exercise:
- county-level outcomes,
- broad NAICS aggregation,
- likely small underlying shock,
- then null effects.

The paper needs to make the null sharper and more informative. Either show that the shock was genuinely large in some places, or show the reallocation channel directly.

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

### What would make it read better?

1. **Rewrite the introduction around the surprising fact**
   Start with the closure wave and the null result, not the student-outcomes literature.

2. **Shorten the institutional background**
   The long descriptions of Gainful Employment, default sanctions, and chain histories are more detailed than needed for a paper whose contribution is not institutional history.

3. **Move methodological throat-clearing out of the introduction**
   The introduction currently gets bogged down in estimator names and p-values. For AER-level narrative positioning, the intro should give the design in one paragraph, not several sentences of technical detail.

4. **Bring the most interesting interpretation earlier**
   The paper should quickly tell the reader why the null matters and what it might imply about local adjustment.

5. **Cut the standardized-effect-size rhetoric**
   The repeated emphasis on “0.003 standard deviations” is not helping. It reads defensive. Better to speak plainly about economic magnitudes.

6. **Promote the boundary-condition argument**
   If the most interesting contribution is that some institutional closures do not operate like classic local labor-demand shocks, that argument should be in the main text, not emerge only late in the discussion and conclusion.

### Is the paper front-loaded with the good stuff?

Not really. The reader has to get through too much setup before the real point comes into focus. The headline result should appear earlier and more crisply.

### Are results buried that should be in the main text?

The paper’s own concluding caveat—that the sector is too aggregated and the shocks may be too small—is actually central, not incidental. It belongs in the framing, not just as a late limitation.

Also, if there are any subgroup results for places where closures were a large share of local education employment, those would matter enormously and should be front and center. As written, the paper has not found the one result that would make the null genuinely informative.

### Is the conclusion adding value?

Some. The best part of the conclusion is its candor about measurement and scale. But that candor also reveals why the current version falls short strategically. The conclusion is doing too much of the paper’s real thinking.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

Bluntly: in its current form, this is **not yet telling an AER-sized story**.

The gap is mainly a combination of:

### 1. Framing problem
The paper is framed as “the missing local labor-market angle on for-profit college closures.” That is too incremental.

### 2. Scope problem
The outcomes are too coarse to distinguish between:
- true frictionless reallocation,
- trivial underlying shock size,
- and simple non-detection.

Without pinning that down, the paper cannot make a strong claim about the incidence of regulation or local adjustment.

### 3. Ambition problem
The paper settles for the safe contribution: null county-level effects. An AER paper would either:
- reveal an unexpected mechanism,
- establish a new boundary condition of broad interest,
- or combine the reduced-form fact with richer evidence on reallocation.

### 4. Novelty problem
The question is adjacent to multiple literatures, but the answer as currently documented does not sufficiently change what economists think.

### Single most impactful advice

**Either show where the displaced workers and economic activity went using finer-grained data, or fully reframe the paper as a boundary-condition result about why some salient institutional closures do not generate place-based labor-market shocks.**

If the author can only do one thing, I would push the first option: get closer to the margin where the shock should actually register. Worker-level, establishment-level, or much finer geographic/sectoral outcomes could transform this from “null at the county level” into “rapid local reallocation with no net job loss,” which is a much stronger and more publishable claim.
