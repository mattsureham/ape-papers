## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether the wave of regulatory-induced for-profit college closures in the 2010s had meaningful spillover effects on local labor markets. Using the staggered shutdown of campuses across U.S. counties, it argues that even a large national contraction in the for-profit college sector produced essentially no detectable county-level employment effects, suggesting that this kind of education-sector regulation does not generate sizable local labor-market collateral damage.

A busy economist should care because this is, in principle, a broader question than for-profit colleges: when regulators shut down low-quality service providers that are locally visible employers, do communities suffer measurable labor-demand losses, or do workers and demand reallocate quickly?

### Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Not quite. The current opening is competent, but it starts too institutionally and too descriptively. It spends its first paragraphs naming ITT and Corinthian and summarizing the student-outcomes literature before cleanly stating the broader economic question. The paper’s real hook is not “nobody has studied local labor markets in this setting”; it is “here is a large, plausibly exogenous regulatory contraction in a controversial industry, and local labor markets barely notice.”

### The pitch the paper should have

A stronger opening would say something like:

> Regulators often hesitate to close low-quality firms because of fears of collateral damage to local jobs. The collapse of the U.S. for-profit college sector provides a sharp test of that concern: between 2013 and 2018, federal enforcement shut down more than 1,200 campuses across hundreds of counties. This paper asks whether these closures functioned as meaningful local labor-demand shocks, or whether workers and spending reallocated so quickly that county labor markets were essentially unaffected.
>
> I show that the closures had near-zero effects on county employment in education and related sectors. The finding suggests that shutting down low-quality educational institutions may impose much smaller local labor-market costs than the politics of closure often presume, and more broadly that not all visible institutional exits generate meaningful local multipliers.

That is the AER-relevant version: regulation, local adjustment, and the distinction between salient closures and economically consequential shocks.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper’s contribution is to show that a large, enforcement-driven contraction in the for-profit college sector did not produce meaningful county-level employment effects, implying limited local labor-market spillovers from these closures.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest papers?

Only partially. The paper distinguishes itself from the student-outcomes literature, but that is not enough. “Nobody has looked at local labor markets in this exact setting” is a valid novelty claim, but not yet a strong contribution claim. The paper needs sharper differentiation from two other literatures:

1. **For-profit college papers on students and institutional quality**  
   The paper says: they study students; I study places. Fine, but obvious.

2. **Local labor demand / local multipliers papers**  
   This is where the real contribution should sit, but the paper does not yet forcefully say how this case changes what we think. Is this a surprising null relative to plant closure logic? Is it evidence that service-sector educational providers have tiny local multipliers? Is it evidence that regulatory exits differ from manufacturing exits? That comparison is underdeveloped.

3. **Institutional closure papers more broadly**  
   Hospitals, military bases, manufacturing plants, and large anchor institutions all loom in the background. The paper should locate itself against those cases, not just against for-profit education studies.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or filling a gap in a LITERATURE?

Right now it is too often framed as filling a gap in a literature: “the consequences for local labor markets remain unstudied.” That is a weak frame.

The stronger frame is a world question:

- When the government shuts down low-quality service providers, how large are the local economic costs?
- Do campus closures behave like meaningful local labor demand shocks?
- How much should policymakers fear collateral job losses when regulating bad actors?

That is stronger and more AER-like.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?

At present, they would probably say: “It’s a DiD paper on for-profit college closures and county employment, and the effects are basically zero.”

That is not fatal, but it is not memorable. The introduction does not yet convert the design into a broader conceptual takeaway.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Specific ways to make it bigger:

- **Shift from closure counts to economically meaningful exposure.**  
  Not a methods point, a positioning point: the economic question is about local dependence. If the paper can show effects by counties where for-profits represented a larger share of local education employment or local enrollment, then the null becomes more informative.

- **Lean harder into comparison with other institutional closures.**  
  The paper would be more important if it explicitly argued: college closures are politically salient but economically unlike plant closures because employment footprints and local multipliers are small. That is a broader statement.

- **Use outcomes closer to the margin where effects should show up.**  
  The paper itself admits NAICS 61 may be too coarse. Strategically, that is a problem. A bigger contribution would show whether there is no effect even on the most exposed outcomes: education employment in college-heavy counties, commercial real estate, job-to-job transitions of campus workers, or nearby community-college employment. Right now the main result risks sounding like “county aggregates are too blunt to detect small shocks.”

- **Frame the paper as a test of a policy presumption.**  
  Many debates over regulation invoke local jobs as a reason not to act. If this paper became “evidence against a common political economy argument for regulatory forbearance,” it would feel larger.

  The paper should not imply “local multiplier effects don’t matter.” It should argue: “This type of institution does not appear to be large enough or locally embedded enough to generate detectable county-level multiplier effects.” That is a useful boundary condition.

- **Synthesize education regulation and local adjustment.**  
  This is probably the most promising positioning: the paper sits at the intersection of education economics, industrial organization of regulated service providers, and urban/local labor economics.

### Is the paper positioned too narrowly or too broadly?

It is oddly both.

- **Too narrowly** in that it leads with the niche claim that no one has studied the local labor-market effects of for-profit college closures.
- **Too broadly** in that it occasionally jumps to sweeping policy claims such as “quality regulation can proceed without risk of collateral labor market damage.”

That claim is broader than the evidence supports. The paper needs a middle ground: “In this setting, the local labor-market collateral damage appears negligible.”

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?

It could speak more directly to:

- **Anchor institution / institutional exit literatures**  
  Closures of hospitals, military bases, large colleges, or public-sector campuses.
- **Service-sector local multipliers**  
  The manufacturing anchor analogy may be the wrong benchmark; the right benchmark may be tradable vs. nontradable service providers and their spending linkages.
- **Political economy of regulation and community opposition**  
  The jobs argument is often a political constraint on enforcement. This could be a powerful framing even without new political economy analysis.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?

Almost, but not quite. Right now it is having the conversation: “there is an overlooked outcome in the for-profit college literature.” The higher-value conversation is: “how large are the local costs of shutting down low-quality firms?” That unexpected bridge—to regulation and local adjustment—is what gives the paper a chance to matter outside the immediate education field.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

For-profit colleges expanded dramatically, became heavily dependent on federal aid, and then faced enforcement actions that triggered widespread closures. These institutions were not just schools; they were also local employers and local demand generators.

### Tension

The closure of more than 1,200 campuses sounds like a substantial local shock. But it is unclear whether such closures truly damage county labor markets, or whether these institutions are too small and too substitutable for their disappearance to matter at that level.

### Resolution

The paper finds essentially no meaningful county-level employment effects in education or related sectors.

### Implications

The economic costs of regulating low-quality educational providers may be much smaller than commonly feared; more broadly, salient institutional exits need not produce meaningful local labor-market disruption.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has one, but it is only moderately strong. The paper is not a pile of unrelated results, but the arc is underpowered because the central “surprise” is not fully established. A reader may reasonably think: “Why would county-level total employment move much when some small campuses close?” If the null is unsurprising, the narrative loses tension.

### What story should it be telling?

The right story is not “no one has studied this before.” It is:

- Policymakers often fear that enforcement against bad actors kills local jobs.
- The for-profit college collapse offers a rare, large-scale test of that fear.
- Despite the salience of the closures, local labor markets do not meaningfully respond.
- Therefore, the economic geography of regulatory exits depends critically on the size and local embeddedness of the affected firms.

That story has setup, tension, resolution, and a conceptual lesson. The current draft reaches for that, but does not yet fully earn it.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?

“Federal enforcement shut down more than 1,200 for-profit college campuses across 383 counties, and county labor markets barely moved.”

That is the best headline.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

Initially, they would lean in, because 1,200 closures sounds large. But the very next question would be: “How big were these campuses as employers, really?” If the answer is “not very,” some of the excitement dissipates.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Almost certainly:

- “Were these institutions ever large enough relative to county labor markets for an effect to be detectable?”
- “Is this a true economic null or just an aggregation null?”
- “Do you see effects in more exposed margins—education employment shares, worker transitions, or community college expansion?”

Those are not econometric complaints; they are strategic questions about whether the result changes beliefs.

### Is the null interesting?

Potentially yes, but the paper has to work harder to make it interesting. A good null paper needs one of two things:

1. A prior that most economists would genuinely have expected a nontrivial effect, or
2. A setting so policy-relevant that ruling out meaningful effects is itself a major contribution.

This paper currently has some of (2) and limited amounts of (1). It does not yet make the reader feel that a meaningful employment effect was broadly expected. In fact, the conclusion itself undermines the contribution by conceding that the outcome aggregation may be too coarse and the institutions too small. That may be true, but strategically it weakens the paper.

The null result can be valuable if the paper frames it as ruling out a commonly invoked policy concern. If not, it risks reading like a failed search for effects in coarse data.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

Bluntly: in its current form, this is not yet an AER paper.

The main gap is a mix of **framing problem** and **ambition problem**, with some **scope problem** mixed in.

- **Framing problem:**  
  The paper has not yet made the reader feel that this is about a fundamental economic question rather than a narrow omitted-outcome exercise in the for-profit college literature.

- **Ambition problem:**  
  “We looked at county employment and found nulls” is too safe. AER papers usually either answer a first-order question, overturn a prior, or deliver a new conceptual insight. This paper is adjacent to a first-order question, but it does not yet hit it hard enough.

- **Scope problem:**  
  The paper studies broad county-sector outcomes in a setting where the affected units may be too small to move those aggregates. That leaves the contribution vulnerable to the response: “of course you found nothing.”

- **Novelty problem:**  
  The exact setting is novel enough, but the empirical object is not. Another reduced-form place-based DiD with null county outcomes will not clear the bar on novelty alone.

### What is the gap between current form and something that would excite the top 10 people in this field?

The paper would need to become a paper about **the local incidence of regulatory shutdowns** rather than a paper about **for-profit college closures per se**. And it would need one additional layer of evidence showing that the null is substantively informative, not merely mechanically unsurprising.

That could come from one of two directions:

1. **A sharper conceptual comparison:** show why these closures differ from canonical local shocks.
2. **A more exposed outcome/exposure margin:** show that even where one should most expect effects, they are absent or rapidly offset.

### Single most impactful advice

If the author could change only one thing, it should be this:

**Reframe the paper around the broader question of whether shutting down low-quality firms imposes meaningful local economic costs, and then show the null on the most economically exposed margin rather than relying primarily on broad county-sector aggregates.**

That one change would simultaneously improve framing, contribution clarity, and perceived ambition.
