**SUBJECT:** Strategic Positioning of "When the Campus Goes Dark: For-Profit College Closures and Local Labor Market Adjustment"

---

## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
This paper examines whether the regulatory "execution" of the for-profit college sector (shuttering over 1,200 campuses) caused collateral damage to local economies. Leveraging the exogenous timing of federal chain-wide shutdowns (e.g., ITT Tech), the author finds that these closures had a "precisely estimated zero" effect on county-level employment, suggesting that these institutions were either too small to matter or that local labor markets are remarkably frictionless.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this well in the abstract, but the first two paragraphs of the introduction are slightly too focused on the "for-profit college" literature.
**The Pitch the Paper Should Have:** 
"When a major local employer is shut down by federal decree, does the local economy bleed? We exploit the regulatory collapse of the for-profit college industry—a shock involving 1,200+ closures—to provide a high-powered test of local labor market resilience. We find that even the simultaneous disappearance of multiple campuses within a county leaves no detectable footprint on total employment or sectoral spillovers, suggesting that the 'collateral damage' of aggressive industry regulation may be vastly overstated."

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**Contribution:** The paper identifies a "hard null" result for local labor market multipliers in the context of institutional closures, providing a counterpoint to the literature on plant closures and "big-push" local development.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It is well-differentiated from the student-outcome papers (Cellini, Turner). It sits closer to Greenstone et al. (2010) or Moretti (2010) but finds the opposite of a "multiplier" effect.
*   **Framing:** Currently, it leans 60/40 toward "filling a gap in the for-profit literature." For the AER, it needs to be 90/10 toward "answering a question about the world"—specifically, the speed of labor market adjustment and the (lack of) spillovers from service-sector hubs.
*   **Clarity:** A smart economist would say: "It’s a high-powered null study showing that for-profit colleges don't actually matter to local economies."
*   **Bigness:** To make the contribution "bigger," the author needs to explore *why* the null exists. Is it because the employees were absorbed by community colleges (Liu & Belfield)? Is it because for-profits are "island" economies with no local supply chains?

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
*   **Closest Neighbors:** Moretti (2010) on multipliers; Greenstone, Hornbeck, and Moretti (2010) on "Million Dollar Plants"; Cellini et al. (2023) on GE rules; Notowidigdo (2020) on labor demand shocks.
*   **Positioning Strategy:** The paper should position itself as a **challenge** to the local multiplier literature. If manufacturing plants create 2.5 jobs for every 1 lost, why do colleges create 0?
*   **Fields:** It’s currently in "Economics of Education." It needs to be in "Urban/Labor."
*   **Unexpected Connection:** The paper could connect to the "Regulatory Taking" or "Industry-wide shock" literature. What happens when a regulator wipes out an industry?

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC
*   **Setup:** For-profit colleges were a massive presence in the 2000s; regulators shut them down rapidly in the 2010s.
*   **Tension:** Critics argued these closures would hurt local economies and jobs. Standard urban models suggest large multipliers.
*   **Resolution:** Nothing happened. The labor market yawned.
*   **Implications:** (Current) Regulators don't need to worry. (AER-level) Our models of local spillovers may not apply to service-sector/training institutions, or labor reallocation in the modern economy is faster than we thought.

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
*   **The Fact:** "When ITT Tech closed 130 campuses overnight, county-level employment didn't move by even 0.005 standard deviations."
*   **The Reaction:** People will lean in because it’s a "precisely estimated zero." Economists love a good null result if the power is high enough.
*   **The Follow-up:** "Wait, so where did the 35,000 employees go? Did they just walk across the street to a community college?" (The paper needs to answer this better).

---

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
*   **Front-loading:** The "Chain IV" is the strongest part of the paper. It should be elevated earlier to dismiss concerns about endogeneity.
*   **The "Why" Section:** Section 6 (Discussion) is currently a bit speculative. It needs to be a core empirical section. Use the QWI data to look at *flows* more deeply. If total employment didn't change, did *churn* increase?
*   **Appendix:** The standardized effect size table (Table 5) is actually one of the most compelling pieces of evidence and should be in the main text to prove the "power" of the null.

---

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The gap is **ambition and mechanisms.** Right now, it’s a very competent policy evaluation. To be an AER paper, it needs to be a paper about **the nature of the modern labor market.**

**The single most impactful piece of advice:** Move away from "Does this hurt the county?" and toward "Why is the multiplier zero?" The author should use the QWI's "worker flows" more aggressively to show the reallocation process. If you can show that for-profit instructors were absorbed by public colleges within two quarters, you move from "we found nothing" to "we found evidence of a highly efficient labor market for specialized human capital."
