## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether expanding high-speed internet changes how people vote, using France’s rollout of fiber-to-the-home across departments and elections. That is a question economists should care about because broadband policy is large-scale, expensive, and plausibly reshapes the information environment, political participation, and the appeal of anti-establishment politics.

Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs? Only partly. The opening is better than most working papers: it gives a concrete political trend, a concrete infrastructure shock, and a causal question. But it immediately drifts into a generic “theory is ambiguous / literature is mixed” frame. The stronger pitch is not “another paper on internet and politics,” but “one of the first papers on whether a second-wave digital infrastructure upgrade—fiber, not initial internet access—changes democratic behavior in a mature democracy.”

### The pitch the paper should have

“Governments are spending billions to replace legacy broadband with fiber, but we know very little about the political consequences of this new generation of digital infrastructure. Does faster, more reliable internet intensify anti-system politics by deepening exposure to algorithmic outrage, or does it reduce political alienation by improving access to information and civic engagement? Using the staggered rollout of fiber-to-the-home across French departments, this paper studies how a major public infrastructure upgrade changed anti-system voting, turnout, and protest voting in national elections.”

Then the second paragraph should say:

“The key finding is not that ‘the internet matters’ in some general sense, but that fiber expansion in France does not appear to fuel anti-system voting and may modestly reduce protest-oriented political behavior, especially in European elections. That makes the paper relevant not only to the media-and-politics literature, but also to the economics of infrastructure and the design of digital public investment.”

That is the AER-relevant story. Right now, the paper gets close, but then undercuts its own momentum by foregrounding estimator disagreement before the reader has fully internalized why the question matters.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper studies whether the rollout of fiber broadband in France changed anti-system voting and argues that faster internet access did not fuel anti-system politics and may instead have reduced protest-style electoral behavior.

### Is it clearly differentiated from the closest papers?

Not enough. The paper cites a broad set of internet-and-politics papers, but the exact novelty is still blurry. The closest distinction should be:

1. **This is about fiber deployment, not first-wave internet adoption or social media entry.**
2. **This is about anti-system/protest voting in a multiparty European democracy, not partisan polarization in a two-party system.**
3. **This is about a policy-driven infrastructure rollout, not individual internet use.**

Those distinctions are there, but scattered. A smart economist could probably say “it’s a paper on broadband and voting in France,” but might still summarize it as “another DiD paper about internet access and politics.” That is a positioning failure.

### World question or literature gap?

The paper mostly frames itself as a world question at first—good—but keeps slipping back into literature-gap language (“contributes to three literatures”). The stronger version is: **When governments upgrade digital infrastructure, does that improve or degrade democratic functioning?** That is a world question. “This contributes to the literature on information and voting” is not.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new?

Not crisply, yet. They would likely say: “It studies French fiber rollout and anti-system voting, and maybe finds a negative effect, though the results are mixed.” That is not a memorable contribution. The paper needs a simpler, more durable line:

- “Second-wave broadband upgrades do not look politically destabilizing.”
- or
- “Fiber appears to reduce low-stakes protest voting rather than amplify anti-system sentiment.”

That is something a colleague can remember.

### What would make the contribution bigger?

Most importantly: **reframe the core outcome.** “Anti-system voting” as currently defined bundles RN, LFI, and Reconquête, which makes for a noisy and contestable category. The most interesting empirical pattern in the paper is actually about **protest-style electoral behavior**—European elections, blank/null ballots, maybe turnout margins—not about extremism per se. That is a bigger and cleaner contribution because it ties directly to alienation, expressive voting, and political participation.

Specific ways to make it bigger:

- **Shift from ‘polarization/extremism’ to ‘political alienation/protest voting’.** The results fit that much better.
- **Separate anti-system right and anti-system left.** Combining them may obscure a more interesting asymmetry.
- **Make election-type heterogeneity the main result, not a robustness subsection.** Right now the most interesting finding is buried.
- **Connect to digital infrastructure as public capital.** AER readers may care more if the paper says something about the broader social returns to digital investment.
- **Show whether effects are strongest where fiber plausibly changes media consumption most.** Mechanism would make the contribution feel less like a reduced-form curiosity.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

### Closest neighbors

The closest papers/conversations seem to be:

1. **Campante, Durante, and Sobbrio (2018)** on broadband and political participation in Italy.
2. **Falck, Gold, and Heblich (2014)** on internet access and voting / electoral accountability.
3. **Lelkes, Sood, and Iyengar (2017)** on broadband and partisan hostility in the U.S.
4. **Guriev, Melnikov, and Zhuravskaya (2021)** on 3G/mobile internet and populism in Europe.
5. Possibly also **Boxell, Gentzkow, and Shapiro (2017/2018)** as the “internet is not the whole polarization story” benchmark.

### How should the paper position itself relative to them?

Mostly **build on and differentiate**, not attack.

- Relative to **Guriev et al.**, the paper should say: “That paper is about mobile internet diffusion and populist voting across countries; this paper is about fiber rollout within one country and asks whether upgrading connectivity in an already-connected democracy has similar effects.”
- Relative to **Campante et al.**, it should say: “Broadband can increase participation; here we ask whether higher-quality broadband changes the *type* of political expression.”
- Relative to **Lelkes et al.**, it should say: “Partisan hostility and anti-system voting are not the same object, and first-wave broadband in the U.S. is not the same treatment as FTTH in France.”
- Relative to **Boxell et al.**, it should say: “The political effects of digital technology may depend on which margin of connectivity changes—access, speed, or platform use.”

### Too narrow or too broad?

Currently both, oddly. It is too broad in claiming to speak to “political polarization,” “misinformation,” “internet and politics,” and “broadband subsidies” all at once. But it is too narrow in execution because the actual empirical design is one country, one infrastructure policy, one bundled political outcome. The fix is to **narrow the claim and broaden the conceptual relevance**.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?

Not unaware, exactly, but underconnected to:

- **Political economy of protest voting / expressive voting**
- **Turnout and political alienation**
- **Infrastructure as state capacity / public investment**
- **Media substitution and attention allocation**
- Possibly **regional economics / digital inclusion** if the argument is that fiber changes integration into national information markets.

The paper currently leans too heavily on “misinformation/polarization” references because that is the fashionable hook. But the findings are more naturally in conversation with alienation, protest, and democratic participation.

### Is it having the right conversation?

Not quite. The unexpected and potentially impactful conversation is not “does the internet cause polarization?” That conversation is crowded and often platform-specific. The better conversation is:

**What are the political spillovers of digital infrastructure investment in mature democracies?**

That frame pulls in public economics, political economy, media economics, and infrastructure economics. It is a stronger AER conversation.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

France rolled out fiber broadband rapidly while anti-system politics rose sharply. Governments worry that better digital connectivity may intensify misinformation, populism, and democratic disaffection.

### Tension

Theory cuts both ways: better internet could either broaden access to information and civic engagement or deepen exposure to outrage-driven content. Existing evidence is mixed and mostly concerns earlier technologies or different institutional settings.

### Resolution

The paper’s headline empirical pattern is that FTTH rollout is associated with less anti-system/protest behavior, especially in European elections and in blank/null voting, though the evidence is not uniform across estimators and election types.

### Implications

If true, that would imply that upgrading digital infrastructure is not politically corrosive by default and may modestly reduce forms of political alienation. More broadly, the political effects of digital technology may depend on whether the change is first-time access, mobile internet, platform adoption, or high-speed fixed upgrades.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the ingredients, but the arc is unstable because the paper wants to tell **two stories at once**:

1. “Broadband affects anti-system politics.”
2. “Actually the main interesting thing is protest voting/alienation, especially in European elections.”

Those are not the same story. The second is much better matched to the results. Right now the manuscript reads like a collection of estimates searching for the least awkward umbrella term. “Anti-system voting” is doing too much work.

### What story should it be telling?

The paper should tell this story:

“Digital infrastructure upgrades can alter how citizens express dissatisfaction. In France, fiber rollout did not increase anti-establishment voting overall; instead, it appears to have reduced low-stakes protest expression, especially blank/null voting and anti-system voting in European elections.”

That is coherent. It converts the sign reversals and election-type heterogeneity from an embarrassment into the central substantive point.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?

I would lead with: **“In France, places that got fiber earlier did not move toward anti-system politics; if anything, fiber reduced protest-style voting in European elections and lowered blank/null ballots.”**

That is the most dinner-party-ready version. Not “the TWFE coefficient is -0.017.”

### Would people lean in?

Moderately, yes—if framed correctly. Economists are interested in whether digital infrastructure has broader social returns and whether the internet-politics link survives outside U.S. social-media contexts. But they will lean in only if the paper is pitched as a surprising substantive fact about democratic behavior, not as a methodological horse race between TWFE and Callaway-Sant’Anna.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Immediately: **“Why would fiber reduce protest voting?”**  
Second: **“Is this really about internet quality, or just urban places evolving differently from rural ones?”**

The first is the intellectually productive follow-up. The second is the credibility question the paper cannot avoid. Since you asked for strategic rather than referee evaluation, the editorial point is: the paper should organize itself around being able to answer the first question, not around repeatedly confessing the second. Right now it spends too much of the introduction advertising its weaknesses.

### If findings are modest or null, is that interesting?

Potentially yes. “Broadband does not fuel extremism” is policy-relevant. But the paper has not fully earned the value of the null because it keeps using loaded language—polarization, misinformation, extremism—while the evidence is narrower and more mixed. A modest or null result becomes interesting when attached to a big ex ante concern: **do large public investments in digital infrastructure have hidden political costs?** That is the case to make.

As written, it sometimes feels like a paper whose exciting result is weak and whose careful result is modest. The strategic fix is to make the modest result the point.

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

   The three-literatures contribution section is too long and generic. Compress it to one paragraph and move some citations later.

2. **Move estimator caveats out of the first five paragraphs.**

   The current paper buries its most interesting substantive distinction. If European vs presidential elections tell fundamentally different stories, say so upfront.

4. **Re-center the paper around protest/alienation outcomes.**

5. **Cut repetitive institutional detail.**  
   The zoning and copper-decommissioning discussion is longer than needed for the current paper. The copper section especially feels like setup for a paper the manuscript does not actually deliver.

6. **Mechanisms section is currently weak and should be either strengthened or drastically shortened.**  
   Right now it mostly says what the paper cannot test. That is honest, but not useful. Better to give one tight paragraph acknowledging mechanism limitations than an entire section that underlines absence.

7. **Conclusion should do more than summarize coefficients.**  
   It should end with the substantive lesson: digital infrastructure’s political effects are not monotone, and second-wave connectivity upgrades may matter more for alienation than for extremism.

### Is the good stuff front-loaded?

Not enough. The reader does get the headline early, but the paper’s most interesting point—that the effect is concentrated in low-stakes protest elections and mirrored in blank/null ballots—arrives too diffusely. The introduction should front-load that distinction.

### Are results buried in robustness?

Yes. The by-election-type split is not a robustness check; it is the paper.

### Is the conclusion adding value?

Some, but not enough. It mostly recaps. It should sharpen the conceptual takeaway and acknowledge that the paper’s strongest contribution is redefining the political margin affected by broadband.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The gap is mainly **framing plus ambition**.

This is not primarily a “science is there, story isn’t” paper, because the evidence is still somewhat unsettled. But the bigger problem is that the paper is currently trying to make a fashionable claim—broadband and polarization—when its actual contribution is narrower and more original. It needs to become more ambitious conceptually while being more disciplined substantively.

### What is the main problem?

- **Framing problem:** yes, definitely. The current frame oversells “polarization/extremism” and undersells “political alienation/protest.”
- **Scope problem:** yes. The paper needs a more coherent set of outcomes that match the story.
- **Novelty problem:** somewhat. “Internet and politics” is crowded; “fiber rollout and protest voting in a mature democracy” is more novel.
- **Ambition problem:** yes. The paper is competent but safe. It reads like a careful applied paper, not yet like a paper that changes how the field thinks about digital infrastructure.

### What would excite the top 10 people in the field?

A version that says something like:

> “The political effects of digital technology depend on the technological margin. In contrast to first-wave mobile/social-media expansion, second-wave fixed broadband upgrades in France did not increase anti-system politics and instead reduced protest expression.”

That is a field-level claim. It places the paper in a broader taxonomy of digital shocks.

### Single most impactful advice

**Stop selling this as a paper about “polarization” and rewrite it as a paper about how fiber rollout affected political alienation and protest voting, with election-type heterogeneity as the central substantive result.**

That one change would improve the title, introduction, outcome logic, literature positioning, and narrative coherence all at once.
