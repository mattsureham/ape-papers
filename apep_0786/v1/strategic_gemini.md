## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
This paper examines how the removal of public disclosure requirements affects discriminatory behavior in the mortgage market. By exploiting a 2018 policy change that exempted small lenders from reporting detailed loan pricing data, the author finds that these lenders immediately exhibited a nearly 2 percentage point widening in the Black-White denial rate gap compared to non-exempt lenders in the same market. This suggests that the mere act of being "watched" via public data serves as a critical disciplinary mechanism against racial bias.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this well, though it spends a bit too much time on the institutional details of the EGRRCPA in the first paragraph. 
**The pitch it should have:** "Does transparency actually curb discrimination, or is it just a paperwork burden? I exploit a natural experiment where 2,000 lenders were suddenly freed from disclosing detailed mortgage pricing data. I find that when the 'lights are dimmed,' racial denial gaps widen significantly, suggesting that public disclosure is not just a data collection exercise but a vital deterrent against discriminatory lending."

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**Contribution:** The paper identifies the "deterrence effect" of disclosure by showing that removing reporting requirements leads to a measurable increase in racial lending disparities.

*   **Differentiation:** It is well-differentiated. While Bhutta (2015) and Ambrose et al. (2021) look at the *expansion* of disclosure, this is the first to look at the *removal* (deregulation). This is a cleaner way to test for behavioral "backsliding."
*   **Question vs. Literature:** It frames the question about the WORLD (Does oversight stop discrimination?), which is strong.
*   **"What's New?":** A smart economist would say: "It's a paper showing that when small banks stop having to report their pricing, they start denying Black applicants more relative to White applicants." It's more than "another DiD" because it isolates the *psychological/regulatory oversight* channel.
*   **Bigger Contribution:** To make this truly "top-of-the-AER," the author needs to bridge the gap between "denial rates" (which they still report) and "pricing" (which they stopped reporting). If the pricing data is gone, how does the *lack* of pricing data affect the *denial* rate? The current mechanism—that they "favor" whites more when the price-scrutiny is gone—is good, but needs more theoretical meat.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
The paper sits at the intersection of **Banking Regulation** (Agarwal et al., 2014) and **Lending Discrimination** (Bartlett et al., 2022).

*   **Closest Neighbors:** Ambrose et al. (2021) on HMDA disclosure; Bartlett et al. (2022) on FinTech and discrimination; and Jin & Leslie (2003) on the general theory of disclosure.
*   **Positioning:** It should **synthesize** these. It takes the "hygiene grade" logic of Jin & Leslie and applies it to the "civil rights" context of Bartlett. 
*   **Missing Conversations:** The paper should speak more to the **Political Economy of Deregulation**. Why was this specific exemption lobbied for? Is the "regulatory relief" worth the "distributional cost"? Connecting to the literature on "regulator capture" or "unintended consequences of deregulation" would broaden the appeal.

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC
*   **Setup:** HMDA is the gold standard for tracking fair lending.
*   **Tension:** Small banks claimed reporting was too expensive; Congress gave them a "pass." But does this "pass" create a "blind spot" for bias?
*   **Resolution:** Yes. Denial gaps widen by 25% at these exempt banks.
*   **Implications:** "Right-sizing" regulation for small firms has a hidden civil rights cost.

**Evaluate:** The narrative is strong, but the "Tension" section (Section 2) is a bit dry. It needs to emphasize the *irony*: the banks argued for relief from *paperwork*, but the result was a change in *equity*.

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
*   **Lead Fact:** "When we stopped making small banks report their mortgage prices, they immediately became 25% more likely to deny Black applicants than their neighbors."
*   **Reaction:** Lean in. This is a "gotcha" result that hits a hot-button policy issue.
*   **Follow-up:** "Is it because they are actually discriminating, or just because small banks have different types of customers?" (The paper tries to answer this with the Asian-White placebo and within-county FEs, but this is where the fight will be).

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
*   **Front-load the Mechanism:** The decomposition in Table 3 (showing it's driven by White denial rates falling faster) is actually the most interesting part. Move this up.
*   **Appendix:** The "Standardized Effect Sizes" (Table 6) should be in the main text to help the reader understand the "So What?".
*   **The "Limitation" (Section 4.2):** The author is too honest about the lack of pre-2018 data. Don't lead with the weakness. Frame it as "The 2018-2022 period provides a unique window into the *immediate* behavioral response to deregulation."

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
**The Gap:** Currently, it feels like a very high-quality "Policy Note" or a "Journal of Finance" paper. To make it AER, it needs **Generalizability**.

**Single Most Impactful Advice:** Shift the framing from a "Mortgage Paper" to a "General Theory of Mandatory Disclosure." Use the mortgage data to prove a broader point: *In the absence of direct enforcement, the mere requirement of public disclosure acts as a 'digital panopticon' that prevents firms from indulging in taste-based preferences.*
