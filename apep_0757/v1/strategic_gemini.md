## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
This paper argues that "food deserts" are not just a consumption story—they are a labor market shock with a stark racial bias. By linking SNAP retailer data with race-disaggregated Census employment records, the author shows that when supermarkets close, Black workers are displaced at much higher rates than their White coworkers within the same county and industry. Economists should care because this identifies a specific, policy-driven mechanism (retail restructuring) that exacerbates racial employment gaps, moving the "food desert" conversation from nutrition to structural inequality.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this very well. The first two paragraphs successfully pivot from the well-known Allcott et al. (2019) demand-side findings to a "supply-side" labor story. 

**The "Better" Pitch:** The paper’s current pitch is strong, but to reach the AER, it needs to lean harder into the *mechanism* earlier.
> "While 'food deserts' are synonymous with nutritional inequality, they also mark the disappearance of large-scale employers from marginalized neighborhoods. This paper demonstrates that supermarket exits act as a racially biased labor shock: Black workers experience a 22% decline in employment—five times the rate of White workers—following an exit. This suggests that the 'retail apocalypse' isn't just changing where people buy food, but is a primary driver of the widening racial gap in labor market precariousness."

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**Contribution:** The paper identifies the labor-market consequences of food desert formation, documenting that supermarket exits disproportionately displace Black workers through a combination of higher separations and lower re-hiring.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It is well-differentiated. It moves away from the "nutrition/prices" focus of *Allcott et al.* or *Handbury & Weinstein* and joins the racial inequality literature (*Derenoncourt & Montialoux*).
*   **World vs. Literature:** It frames itself as answering a question about the **world** (why is Black employment more precarious?), which is a major strength.
*   **The "Smart Economist" Test:** A reader would explain this as: "You know how we focus on how food deserts affect what people eat? This paper shows they are also destroying Black jobs at a much higher rate than White ones."
*   **Bigger Contribution:** To make this "AER big," the author needs to move beyond the *fact* of displacement to the *long-term scarring*. Do these workers leave the labor force? Do they move to lower-wage sectors? The "So What?" becomes much louder if you can show this is a permanent trap rather than a temporary friction.

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
*   **Closest Neighbors:** Allcott et al. (2019) [Food Deserts], Derenoncourt & Montialoux (2021) [Racial Gaps], and potentially the trade/automation displacement literature (e.g., Autor, Dorn, & Hanson).
*   **Strategy:** The paper should **synthesize**. It is currently "adding a dimension" to food deserts. It should instead "bridge" the urban/industrial organization literature with labor/racial inequality.
*   **Missing Conversations:** The paper is light on the **Urban Economics** side. It should speak to the "Spatial Mismatch" literature (Kain, 1968). Is this just a new version of spatial mismatch where the jobs aren't just far away, but are actively being replaced by "job-lite" convenience stores?

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC
*   **Setup:** Food deserts exist; we usually think about the "eaters."
*   **Tension:** However, supermarkets are also massive employers. If they are replaced by convenience stores (which employ 1/20th the staff), where do the workers go?
*   **Resolution:** Black workers are the first out and the last back in.
*   **Implications:** SNAP policy (stocking rules) is a de facto labor market policy that may be inadvertently harming the very communities it intends to help.

**Evaluation:** The arc is exceptionally clear. It is not a "results looking for a story"; it is a very disciplined inquiry.

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
*   **The Lead Fact:** "When a supermarket closes, Black employment in the food sector drops by 22%, while White employment barely budges."
*   **Reaction:** Lean in. It’s a clean, provocative number.
*   **The Follow-up:** "Is it because Black workers are lower seniority, or is the new 7-Eleven just not hiring them?"
*   **Null Result:** The log earnings "increase" for Black workers is a brilliant piece of narrative jujitsu—turning a positive coefficient into a grim story of negative selection (only the highest earners survived). This is high-level AER-style argumentation.

---

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
*   **Front-loading:** The paper is well-structured. The Triple-Diff logic is intuitive.
*   **Appendix:** The current Section 5.2 (Race-Specific Effects) is a bit of a distraction. The DDD is the "money" result. I would move the separate TWFE results to an appendix to keep the focus on the *differential* impact.
*   **The "Proposed Rule":** The discussion of the 2025 SNAP stocking rule in the conclusion is the "Policy Punch" the paper needs. It should be a standalone section or a more prominent part of the discussion.

---

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The paper is currently a "very good" empirical note. To be a "Lead Article" contender, it has a **Scope Problem**.

Currently, we see the *moment* of impact. The AER wants to see the **general equilibrium**.
1.  **Cross-Sector Spillovers:** When the supermarket closes, do these Black workers move to warehouses or fast food? Or do they disappear from the UI records entirely?
2.  **The "Why":** The paper hypothesizes "last-in-first-out" or "occupational segregation." If the author could use the LEHD to actually *test* the seniority/tenure of those displaced vs. those who stayed, that would move this from "suggestive" to "definitive."

**The Single Most Impactful Advice:** Trace the displaced workers' "next step" using the worker-level longitudinality of the LEHD to show if this displacement leads to long-term earnings scarring or industry exit.
