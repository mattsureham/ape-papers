## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether marginal patent grants in clean-energy technologies shape subsequent innovation. Using quasi-random assignment of green patent applications to more- versus less-granting USPTO examiners, it finds that patents handled by higher-output examiners receive more citations but do not generate more follow-on green patenting in the same technology class—suggesting that, on this margin, the patent system affects visibility more than the pace of cumulative clean innovation.

Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs? Not really. The opening is energetic, but it spends too much time on broad climate stakes and patent theory before telling the reader the paper’s concrete empirical question, margin, and headline result. A busy economist should know by paragraph two: what variation, what outcome, what main finding, and why that matters for the IP-and-climate debate.

### The pitch the paper should have

“Do patent grants accelerate or hinder cumulative innovation in clean-energy technologies? This paper studies that question using quasi-random assignment of green patent applications to USPTO examiners who differ in grant intensity, and finds that assignment to a higher-output examiner has essentially no effect on subsequent patenting in the same green technology class, even though it increases forward citations. The result suggests that, at the margin of examiner-driven patent grants, green patents may affect attention but not the trajectory of follow-on invention—an important fact for debates over whether IP is a bottleneck to climate innovation.”

That is the paper. Say it immediately.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper’s contribution is to show that examiner-driven variation in green patent grant intensity has no detectable effect on subsequent within-class clean-energy patenting, despite increasing patent citations.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?

Only partially. The paper knows the obvious comparators—Sampat and Williams especially—but it does not sharply tell the reader why green technology is a distinct and important test case rather than simply “another examiner design applied to another patent domain.” Right now the contribution reads as: “We do the gene-patent design for Y02 patents and also find a null.” That is competent, but not yet exciting.

The differentiation should be something like:

- Green technology is the policy domain in which IP restrictions are most actively debated on diffusion grounds.
- Green innovation is unusually cumulative, systems-based, and policy-dependent.
- The paper therefore tests whether a central argument in climate policy—that patents impede diffusion and follow-on progress—actually shows up in reduced-form innovation outcomes.

That would make it more than a domain transfer.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or filling a gap in a LITERATURE?

It is trying to do the former, but keeps slipping into the latter. The strongest framing is world-facing: **Are patents a meaningful bottleneck to the clean-energy innovation pipeline?** The weaker framing is: **No one has yet applied examiner IV methods to Y02 patents.** The paper currently contains too much of the second and not enough of the first.

### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what’s new?

Right now, probably: “It’s an examiner quasi-experiment on green patents, and they find a null.” That is not enough. The introduction needs to make the colleague say: “Interesting—on the policy margin people argue about, patent grants in clean tech seem not to slow or speed subsequent invention, though they do affect salience.”

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Most importantly: **a better outcome and sharper conceptual target**.

Specific ways to make it bigger:

1. **Move beyond subclass-level patent counts as the marquee outcome.**  
   The current outcome is so aggregated that it weakens the conceptual punch. More compelling outcomes would be:
   - third-party follow-on innovation rather than total subclass activity;
   - citations/follow-on by other assignees or entrants;
   - measures of technological recombination or cross-class spillovers;
   - deployment-linked outcomes if feasible.

2. **Make the citations-vs-innovation divergence the core contribution.**  
   That is the most interesting fact in the paper. Visibility rises, inventive activity does not. This could be framed as evidence that disclosure/attention effects exist without materially changing innovation direction or volume.

3. **Speak to diffusion, not just cumulative patenting.**  
   The climate audience cares about technology adoption and diffusion. If the paper cannot measure deployment, it should be much more honest that it studies one narrow innovation margin, not the broader diffusion question invoked in the introduction.

4. **Sharpen the comparison to genomics and startup patents.**  
   Why would we have expected a different answer in green tech? If there is no good reason, the paper risks looking incremental. If there is one—cumulativeness, policy salience, climate transfer politics—lean into it hard.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

### Closest neighbors

The closest neighbors are:

1. **Sampat and Williams (2019)** on gene patents and follow-on innovation using examiner-based variation.
2. **Farre-Mensa, Hegde, and Ljungqvist (2020)** on the effects of patents on startups using examiner assignment.
3. **Williams (2013)** on Celera and follow-on research.
4. **Galasso and Schankerman (2015)** on patent invalidation and cumulative innovation.
5. On the green innovation side: **Popp (2002)**, **Johnstone, Haščič, and Popp (2010)**, **Aghion et al. (2016)**, **Calel and Dechezleprêtre (2020)**.

### How should the paper position itself relative to those neighbors?

- **Build on Sampat and Williams, don’t just echo them.**  
  This is the obvious methodological predecessor. The paper should say: if gene patents were a natural test case for whether IP blocks science, green patents are the natural test case for whether IP blocks climate innovation.
  
- **Contrast with Williams and Galasso/Schankerman on margin, not findings.**  
  The right move is not “they found X, I find Y.” It is “they study broader or different policy margins—large IP estates or invalidation of existing rights—whereas I study the marginal grant environment.”
  
- **Connect to green innovation papers by reframing their omission.**  
  Those papers ask what drives green R&D. This paper asks whether IP itself is one of the constraints on cumulative progress. That’s a useful adjacent question.

### Is the paper currently positioned too narrowly or too broadly?

Oddly, both.

- **Too broadly** in its rhetoric: climate catastrophe, TRIPS, compulsory licensing, technology transfer.
- **Too narrowly** in what it actually measures: within-subclass follow-on granted patents among granted patents.

This mismatch is the paper’s central strategic problem. It invites a reader expecting a grand answer on green IP and climate diffusion, but delivers a narrower reduced-form fact about patenting patterns in a specific design.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?

Not entirely unaware, but under-engaged with:

- The **knowledge diffusion / patent disclosure** literature more broadly.
- The literature on **innovation networks and recombination**, which may be more natural if effects spill across subclasses.
- The literature on **entry and market structure in innovation-intensive sectors**, especially if the concern is whether patents deter entrants.
- Climate policy discussions about **technology diffusion and adoption**, where patents are part of a much larger ecosystem.

### What fields should it be speaking to?

- **Innovation and IP economics** is the home field.
- **Environmental economics / climate policy** is the second audience.
- Potentially **industrial organization of innovation** if the paper can say something about entrants/incumbents or licensing barriers.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?

Not quite. The current conversation is “Do patents hinder green innovation?” but the evidence is really about “Does examiner-induced variation in marginal green patent grant intensity affect subsequent patenting in the same class?” Those are not the same conversation.

The best repositioning is:  
**This paper provides disciplined evidence on one narrow but policy-relevant margin of the IP-climate debate: whether marginal patent grants in clean tech meaningfully alter subsequent inventive activity.**

That is honest and still important.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

There is a live policy and economic debate over whether patents in clean technologies stimulate innovation by rewarding inventors or impede it by blocking cumulative progress and diffusion.

### Tension

The literature offers mixed evidence across domains, and green technologies are exactly the sort of cumulative, high-stakes domain where blocking effects might matter—yet we have little causal evidence on whether marginal patent grants actually change subsequent innovation trajectories.

### Resolution

Using examiner assignment as a source of quasi-random variation, the paper finds essentially no effect of assignment to a higher-output examiner on subsequent green patenting in the same CPC subclass, though such assignment does increase forward citations.

### Implications

The marginal green patent grant appears not to be a major determinant of follow-on invention within technology classes; if patents matter in climate innovation, it may be via broader ex ante incentives, different margins of exclusion, or channels outside subclass-level patenting.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

Serviceable, but not fully clear. It does have a recognizable setup-tension-resolution structure. The problem is that the **resolution does not match the scale of the setup**. The setup promises an answer to the climate-IP debate writ large; the results answer a narrower question. This creates narrative slippage.

### Is it a collection of results looking for a story?

Somewhat. The citation result, the null follow-on result, the domain heterogeneity, the assignee heterogeneity, and the scope IV are all there, but the paper has not fully decided what story they tell.

### What story should it be telling?

The right story is:

1. Green patents are often alleged to slow diffusion and cumulative invention.
2. This paper tests whether marginal patent grants actually change subsequent inventive activity in green technologies.
3. The answer is no on within-class patenting, yes on attention.
4. Therefore the patent system may shape what inventors look at more than what they go on to invent—at least on this margin.

That is a coherent story. The paper should be organized around that contrast.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?

“I’d lead with: examiner-driven variation in green patent grant intensity increases citations, but has essentially zero effect on subsequent green patenting in the same technology class.”

That is the memorable fact.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

A mixed reaction. IP/innovation economists would lean in. Environmental economists might lean in initially because of the climate angle, then cool off when they realize the outcome is subclass-level patent counts rather than technology adoption or broader diffusion. General-interest economists would not immediately see AER-level significance unless the framing becomes sharper and the key fact becomes more surprising.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Almost certainly:  
**“Does this mean green patents don’t matter, or just that your outcome is too coarse / too narrow?”**

That is the question the paper must preempt and answer better. Right now it admits the limitation, but the limitation swallows too much of the claim.

### If the findings are null or modest: is the null itself interesting?

Yes—but only if the paper is disciplined about why. The null is interesting because:

- green IP restrictions are heavily debated in policy circles;
- the design is large-scale and precise;
- the paper can rule out economically meaningful effects on this margin;
- the citation result shows the design is not simply dead.

But the paper has to sell the null as **informative evidence against a widely presumed margin of blockage**, not as “we tried and didn’t find significance.” It mostly does this, but it still sounds defensive in places.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

### What is the gap between current form and an AER paper that would excite the top 10 people in this field?

At present, the gap is substantial. This is not mainly a competence problem; it is a **positioning and ambition problem**, with some **scope** issues.

- **Framing problem:** The paper overclaims broad climate-policy relevance relative to a narrow reduced-form estimand.
- **Scope problem:** The main outcome is too coarse and too indirect to fully cash out the big motivating debate.
- **Novelty problem:** The design and headline null are very close in spirit to existing examiner-based work in other domains.
- **Ambition problem:** The paper is careful and professional, but it feels like a well-executed extension rather than a field-defining statement.

### What would make it bigger?

If the authors can only work with this design, the path to a stronger paper is not more robustness. It is a **stronger conceptual object**:

- make the paper about the wedge between **attention** and **innovation**;
- show this wedge in a way that speaks to patent disclosure versus blocking;
- sharpen outcomes toward **third-party**, **entrant**, or **cross-boundary** follow-on invention;
- scale back climate-grandstanding unless they can connect to diffusion or adoption.

A stronger paper might ask:  
**Do marginal patents change who innovates, not just how much?**  
Or:  
**Do patents in green technologies increase knowledge visibility without affecting cumulative invention?**

Those are more novel and more intellectually portable.

### Single most impactful advice

**Reframe the paper around the citations-versus-follow-on divergence as the main fact, and stop pretending the current outcome alone resolves the broader climate-IP debate.**

That one move would make the paper more honest, more memorable, and more publishable.
