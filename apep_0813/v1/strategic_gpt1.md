## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether changing the *form* of intergovernmental transfers—from earmarked grants to unconditional block grants—changes where people choose to live. Using Switzerland’s 2008 fiscal equalization reform, it argues that resource-poor cantons attracted more in-migrants, but that much of the migration response began before implementation, suggesting that households responded to the anticipated reform process rather than to the formal policy switch itself.

A busy economist should care because this is potentially a nice intersection of fiscal federalism and spatial equilibrium: if “strings attached” alter local policy choices in ways households value, then grant design can affect migration, not just public budgets.

### Does the paper articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Not quite. The current introduction starts with a broad question about the flypaper effect and conditionality, then pivots to migration and Tiebout sorting. That creates a framing problem: the paper initially promises a contribution about **how governments spend transfers**, but the actual evidence is about **how households move in response to fiscal equalization**. The paper itself eventually admits this in paragraph 7, but by then the setup has oversold one question and delivered another.

### What the first two paragraphs should say instead

The introduction should lead with the world question the paper can actually answer:

> Intergovernmental transfers do more than reshape public budgets: they may also reshape where people live. If fiscal equalization improves the tax-service bundle in recipient jurisdictions, households may relocate toward those places, changing the spatial distribution of population and the incidence of redistribution itself.

> Switzerland’s 2008 fiscal equalization reform offers a rare opportunity to study this mechanism. The reform replaced a system of earmarked cost-sharing with formula-based equalization across cantons, creating large cross-canton differences in the size of fiscal gains and losses. I show that migration shifted toward recipient cantons, but the shift began around the 2004 referendum rather than at 2008 implementation, suggesting that population responded to anticipated fiscal reallocation and that common-shock reforms can generate substantial anticipation effects.

That is the paper’s best pitch. It is much cleaner than the current “conditionality/flypaper” lead.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### Contribution in one sentence

The paper shows that a major fiscal equalization reform in Switzerland was associated with increased in-migration into transfer-receiving cantons, with much of the response occurring before implementation, implying that anticipated intergovernmental redistribution can affect residential sorting.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?

Only partially. The paper name-checks Knight, Gordon-Knight, Schmidheiny, Brülhart-Parchet, and broad Tiebout references, but the differentiation is still fuzzy because it is trying to be two papers at once:

1. a paper on **grant conditionality**, and  
2. a paper on **migration responses to fiscal equalization**.

The actual evidence clearly supports the second. It does **not** clearly isolate the first. As written, the contribution is not sharply distinguished from existing work on fiscal competition, tax-induced mobility, and transfer incidence because the paper has not yet said crisply: *the novelty here is that households respond to anticipated equalization transfers across subnational governments, not just to taxes and housing prices.*

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a gap in a literature?

It starts as a literature-gap paper (“the conditionality question is understudied”) but becomes, later, a world-question paper (“do people move when fiscal equalization changes expected local fiscal conditions?”). The latter is much stronger and should dominate.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the intro?

At present, they might say: “It’s another reduced-form paper about a Swiss reform and migration, with pre-trends.” That is not good enough.

The introduction needs to equip the reader to say something more precise, like: “It’s a paper showing that fiscal equalization can move people, and that the movement may happen at announcement rather than implementation.”

That is a real idea.

### What would make the contribution bigger?

Most important: **choose one lane and deepen it**.

The biggest version of the paper is not “do earmarks matter?” unless the author can show what recipient cantons did with the flexibility—tax cuts, spending reallocation, service improvements. Without that, the conditionality claim is too indirect.

So the realistic path to a bigger contribution is:

- **Different framing:** make it about **spatial incidence of intergovernmental transfers** and **anticipation in spatial equilibrium**.
- **Different outcomes:** add outcomes that connect transfers to household-relevant local conditions—cantonal taxes, public spending composition, amenity proxies, housing prices, construction, employment. Even descriptive evidence would help.
- **Different mechanism:** show whether migration is concentrated among groups most likely to respond to fiscal bundles: high-income taxpayers, families with children, retirees, commuters, or homeowners/renters if data permit.
- **Different comparison:** compare anticipation at referendum vs implementation explicitly as the central empirical fact, not as a caveat.
- **Different framing:** connect to a broader question: when governments redistribute across places, who ultimately benefits—incumbent residents, landowners, or future migrants?

That last framing is much more AER-ish than “here is a Swiss DiD on migration.”

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Governments redistribute large sums across jurisdictions. In decentralized systems, these transfers may change local taxes and public services, which in turn may affect where households choose to live.

### Tension

We know a lot about how grants affect public spending, and a lot about how households sort across places in response to taxes and amenities. But we know much less about whether equalization transfers themselves alter population flows—and especially whether households respond at implementation or in anticipation.

### Resolution

In Switzerland, migration shifted toward transfer-receiving cantons, primarily through increased in-migration, but much of the shift began before the reform took effect, around the referendum period.

### Implications

Fiscal equalization may have spatial effects beyond budgets, and empirical strategies that code treatment at implementation may miss much of the action when reforms are anticipated. More broadly, the incidence of transfers may partly accrue to movers and property markets, not just incumbent residents.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the ingredients, but not the discipline. Right now it feels like a collection of estimates plus an honest caveat about pre-trends. The best thing in the paper is actually the awkward thing: **the response seems to start before implementation**. That should be the centerpiece, not a qualification.

The story should be:

1. Fiscal equalization should alter local fiscal attractiveness.  
2. Switzerland provides a clean, salient reform with known fiscal winners and losers.  
3. Population shifts toward winners.  
4. But the shift starts at referendum/announcement, not implementation.  
5. Therefore, the key lesson is about **forward-looking mobility in response to fiscal reform**.

That is a story. The current draft spends too much time trying to preserve a conventional post-2008 treatment design that its own evidence destabilizes.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?

“I’d lead with: when Switzerland voted in a major fiscal equalization reform, people started moving toward the cantons that stood to gain—and they seem to have done so before the reform actually took effect.”

That is the memorable fact.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

Some would lean in, because that is a nice, intuitive spatial-equilibrium fact with an anticipation twist. But many would ask almost immediately: “What changed in those cantons that made people move—taxes, spending, housing, jobs, expectations?” If the paper cannot answer that at least partially, attention may fade.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Almost certainly: **“What is the mechanism?”**

And second: **“Is this about the reform’s announcement, or is it just pre-existing convergence?”**

The paper is already aware of both questions. Strategically, it should build the paper around them.

### If the findings are modest, is that okay?

Yes—if the paper leans into the anticipation lesson. The issue is not that the effect is null; it is that the paper currently treats the messy timing as a threat to the main contribution. For positioning purposes, it should instead treat this timing pattern as the contribution.

A clean null on implementation combined with a meaningful response at referendum/announcement can still be interesting. But then the claim becomes: **policy timing matters, and implementation dates are often the wrong treatment date in common-shock reforms.**

That is publishable if framed well and tied to a bigger conceptual point.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

At present, this is not yet an AER paper. The gap is mainly **ambition and framing**, with some **scope** issues.

### What is the main gap?

#### 1. Framing problem
The science is not framed around the strongest question. “Do earmarks matter?” is not what the paper can answer credibly with the current outcomes. “Do anticipated equalization transfers change where people live?” is.

#### 2. Scope problem
For AER, one migration outcome in 26 cantons is probably too narrow unless the conceptual contribution is very sharp. The paper needs either:
- stronger mechanism evidence, or
- a bigger conceptual framing around spatial incidence / anticipation, with outcomes that make that framing persuasive.

#### 3. Ambition problem
The paper is careful, competent, and honest. But it is too safe. AER papers typically either answer a first-order question cleanly or reframe a known question in a way that changes how a field thinks. This paper could do the latter if it leans hard into anticipation and spatial incidence. Right now it does not.

#### 4. Novelty problem
There is some novelty here, but it is not yet unmistakable. Without sharper framing, it risks being seen as “a Swiss policy paper with pre-trends.”

### Single most impactful advice

**Reframe the paper around the anticipation-driven spatial response to fiscal equalization—not around grant conditionality—and then build everything around that fact.**

If the author changes only one thing, it should be this. The current draft’s most interesting result is being treated as a nuisance. It should be the paper.
