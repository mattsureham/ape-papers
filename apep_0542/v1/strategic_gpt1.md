This is a potentially interesting paper with a real-world event, a large administrative dataset, and a provocative bottom line. But in its current form, it is not yet telling an AER-sized story. The paper’s most distinctive fact is not “HS2 cancellation had no effect on house prices” per se; it is “even the cancellation of a nationally salient, formally approved mega-project appears not to move housing prices, suggesting anticipatory capitalization depends sharply on project credibility.” That could be a strong paper. Right now, however, the manuscript is caught between a case study of HS2 and a broader claim about infrastructure expectations, and it does not fully earn either framing.

## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

### What is this paper about?

This paper asks whether housing markets capitalize the expected benefits of major infrastructure projects before those projects are completed, using the abrupt cancellation of HS2 Phase 2 in the UK as a test case. The central finding is that despite the political drama and scale of the project, the cancellation produced no detectable break in nearby house prices, suggesting that distant and politically fragile infrastructure may generate little anticipatory capitalization.

A busy economist should care because this is a clean, intuitive way to study the market value of infrastructure *expectations*, not just infrastructure itself. If credible announcements move prices but non-credible ones do not, that matters for urban economics, public finance, political economy, and cost-benefit analysis.

### Does the paper articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Pretty well, but not optimally. The current introduction opens with a vivid anecdote and gets to the question quickly, which is good. But it spends too much of its opening energy on the event and not enough on the deeper issue: when do markets believe infrastructure promises? The current version still reads a bit like “here is a neat cancellation event in the UK” rather than “here is a broader test of capitalization under political uncertainty.”

The first two paragraphs should pivot faster from HS2 to the general question of whether announced-but-uncertain infrastructure is priced at all. Right now the contribution emerges only after several paragraphs; for AER, the conceptual hook should be upfront.

### The pitch the paper should have

“Do housing markets price infrastructure promises before the infrastructure exists? We study the abrupt cancellation of HS2 Phase 2—Britain’s planned high-speed rail extension—as a test of whether announced, approved, and partially implemented transport investments are capitalized into nearby property values. Despite the project’s scale and salience, we find no discrete housing-price response to the cancellation, suggesting that long-horizon infrastructure with weak political credibility may generate little anticipatory capitalization in the first place.”

Then the second paragraph should add why that matters:

“This question matters because much of infrastructure policy operates through announcements years before completion: routes are approved, land is safeguarded, and local development responds. If markets capitalize these promises, cancellation destroys household wealth and creates real distributional consequences. If they do not, then the incidence and welfare consequences of infrastructure politics look very different from what standard transit-capitalization evidence implies.”

That is the story. Everything else should support it.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### Contribution in one sentence

The paper’s contribution is to use the cancellation of HS2 Phase 2 to argue that politically uncertain, long-horizon infrastructure projects may not be meaningfully capitalized into nearby housing prices before completion.

### Is this clearly differentiated from the closest papers?

Only partly. The paper cites transit capitalization papers and says it studies a negative shock rather than a positive one. That is true, but as stated it still risks sounding like “another house-price event study around transit.” The differentiation needs to be sharper along two dimensions:

1. **Object of interest**: not transit infrastructure per se, but **the credibility of anticipated infrastructure**.
2. **Empirical shock**: not opening, announcement, or construction, but **cancellation of an already advanced project**.

That distinction is potentially important, but the manuscript doesn’t yet hammer it hard enough. It spends a lot of space proving the cancellation was surprising, then a lot of space explaining why there was no clean causal estimate, but less space situating the absence of a price break as evidence about market beliefs.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a literature gap?

Mixed, leaning too much toward literature-gap framing in places. The stronger world question is:

**Do markets actually believe governments’ infrastructure promises enough to price them?**

That is much better than:

**There is little evidence on negative infrastructure shocks / cancellations.**

The latter is publishable in a field journal; the former is potentially top-journal material.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?

Not quite. Right now they might say: “It’s a DiD/event study on HS2 cancellation, and the result is null because there are pre-trends.” That is not enough. What they should be able to say is: “It uses a high-profile cancellation to show that even legally advanced infrastructure may not be priced if completion is politically doubtful.”

At present, the introduction gives away too much of the econometric pathology and not enough of the conceptual punch.

### What would make the contribution bigger?

Several possibilities:

- **Shift from ‘did prices move?’ to ‘what does no movement reveal about credibility?’** This is the biggest one. The paper should more explicitly treat the null response as an estimate of the market-assigned completion probability, or at least as qualitative evidence on credibility.
- **Exploit cross-sectional variation in credibility.** If some station areas or project segments were more credible ex ante than others, the paper becomes much bigger. For example:
  - legislatively further advanced vs less advanced segments,
  - areas with more land acquisition,
  - places with more projected travel-time gains,
  - Eastern leg vs Western leg not just as a robustness check, but as a credibility gradient.
- **Tie to welfare incidence.** If little capitalization occurred, then the private wealth consequences of cancellation were limited. That is a strong policy implication, but it needs clearer articulation.
- **Broaden beyond property values conceptually.** Even if the paper remains house-price-focused empirically, it should frame the contribution as about the pricing of political commitments, not just housing.
- **Make the blight-vs-accessibility decomposition central only if it can be shown credibly.** Right now it is more of a speculative side story.

The paper becomes bigger if it is about **how markets process state capacity and political credibility**, not about one British rail line.

The closest literatures/papers appear to be:

### How should the paper position itself relative to those neighbors?

Mostly **build on and redirect**, not attack. The right positioning is:

- The existing literature convincingly shows that completed or near-certain transit investments can be capitalized.
- This paper studies a different object: capitalization of **uncertain future infrastructure**.
- The null result does not contradict the prior literature; it refines it by showing capitalization depends on **credibility and timing**.

That is a productive synthesis. The paper should avoid overclaiming that it “challenges” the transit capitalization literature in general. It only challenges a sloppy extrapolation from realized projects to uncertain promises.

### Is the paper currently positioned too narrowly or too broadly?

Oddly, both.

- **Too narrowly** as a case study of HS2 and British policy drama.
- **Too broadly** when it gestures vaguely at “spatial equilibrium” and “infrastructure investment” without really entering those literatures in a focused way.

It needs a tighter lane: **urban/public/infrastructure economics of anticipatory capitalization under political uncertainty**.

### What literature does it seem unaware of?

It seems under-connected to at least four relevant conversations:

1. **Expectation formation / credibility / political risk**:
   - how households and firms discount government commitments,
   - policy reversals,
   - time inconsistency and state credibility.
2. **Event-study interpretation with nulls and anticipation**:
   - not for identification details, but for conceptual framing around information arrival and partial anticipation.
3. **Real estate and local public goods under uncertainty**:
   - there is literature on school assignment uncertainty, environmental risk, zoning/planning uncertainty.
4. **Infrastructure planning and project credibility / state capacity**:
   - especially in transport economics and political economy.

The paper currently speaks mainly to transit capitalization and a bit to quantitative spatial equilibrium. It should also speak to **political economy of infrastructure promises**.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?

Not yet. The highest-value conversation is not “does transit raise nearby housing prices?” We know that. The more interesting conversation is:

**When do government announcements become economically real?**

That is an AER conversation.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Infrastructure announcements are often assumed to matter long before projects are completed. Housing markets, in particular, are supposed to price future accessibility gains, so planned rail should raise nearby property values even in advance.

### Tension

But many mega-projects are delayed, scaled back, or cancelled. If a project is politically contested and delivery is uncertain, should we expect markets to capitalize it at all? HS2 Phase 2 is a sharp test because it was high-profile, legally advanced, and then abruptly cancelled.

### Resolution

The paper finds no detectable break in relative house prices around the cancellation. The naive positive DiD is not the story; the story is that there is no visible discrete response at the event date.

### Implications

The implications are that anticipatory capitalization depends on project credibility, and that the distributional and welfare consequences of infrastructure cancellation may be smaller than commonly presumed when promised infrastructure is not believed. More broadly, policy announcements and formal approvals are not equivalent to economically credible commitments.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the pieces, but the arc is not fully disciplined. At present it feels like:

1. interesting event,
2. setup of DiD,
3. surprising positive coefficient,
4. then a long walk through why the coefficient is not causal,
5. then a cautious null interpretation.

That makes the paper feel defensive and method-driven. It reads like a collection of results plus caveats, rather than a confident story.

### What story should it be telling?

The story should be:

- **Setup**: economists often infer from transit-opening studies that anticipated infrastructure creates local wealth.
- **Tension**: but that only makes sense if people believe the project will happen; HS2 tests that premise.
- **Resolution**: cancellation of a headline mega-project does not move prices.
- **Implication**: markets discount politically fragile infrastructure promises, so credibility is a central determinant of anticipatory capitalization.

The pre-trends and failed DiD should appear as supporting diagnostics, not as the protagonist of the paper. Right now the protagonist is “parallel trends fails.” That is not an AER story.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?

“I would lead with: the cancellation of Britain’s most politically salient rail megaproject did not produce a discernible break in nearby house prices.”

That is a good dinner-party fact.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

Economists would lean in initially. The event is salient, the result is counterintuitive, and the stakes are obvious. But the second question would come immediately: **why not?** And then the paper needs a sharper answer than “maybe anticipation, maybe blight relief, maybe substitution.”

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Most likely:  
**“Does this mean people never believed HS2 would happen?”**

That should be the paper’s organizing follow-up question. Right now the manuscript poses it, but too tentatively.

A second likely question:  
**“Is this about infrastructure credibility generally, or just a UK one-off?”**

The paper should answer by framing HS2 as an extreme but informative case of broader relevance.

### Is the null result itself interesting?

Yes, but only if framed correctly. A null around a small local intervention is forgettable. A null around a huge, nationally debated, legally advanced infrastructure cancellation is interesting because it reveals something about expectations, not because nulls are inherently interesting.

At present the paper partially makes that case, but not forcefully enough. It still reads at points like a failed attempt to estimate a negative price effect. It needs to read as a successful paper about non-capitalization under uncertainty.

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

#### 1. Front-load the actual message
The good stuff is somewhat front-loaded already, but the introduction is still too long and too cautious. The key findings should be stated more simply:

- cancellation was salient,
- no break in prices,
- implication: limited anticipatory capitalization.

Do not lead with the naive positive DiD as a suspense device. That is clever once, but it risks misframing the paper around a result the authors themselves don’t believe.

#### 2. Shorten institutional background substantially
The institutional section is too long for the strategic payoff it provides. The detailed history of HS2 phases, route design, and cost revisions can be compressed. The reader mainly needs:
- what Phase 2 was,
- why cancellation was salient,
- why credibility was already in doubt.

The current background reads like a policy brief.

#### 3. Move most caveat-heavy identification prose out of the main text
For this memo’s purposes, I am not judging the identification strategy. But from a reading perspective, the paper spends too much main-text real estate cataloguing threats and limitations before the reader fully absorbs the substantive point. Some of that can be shortened and some moved later or to an appendix.

#### 4. Promote the event-study figure and raw-trends figure conceptually
The event study is the heart of the paper. The raw-trends figure, currently in the appendix, may deserve to be in the main text if it makes the central descriptive point transparently. If the paper’s core claim is “no structural break,” the visuals should do more narrative work.

#### 5. Downgrade or trim results that don’t advance the central claim
Some heterogeneity sections feel like the paper searching for action:

If those analyses do not sharpen the credibility story, they are expendable or appendicizable.

#### 6. Recast the “Phase 1 placebo”
This may actually be more interesting than the paper realizes, but as written it is muddy. Either:

- shrink it.  
Right now it distracts from the main contribution.

#### 7. The conclusion should do more than summarize
The conclusion is competent, but it could be sharper. It should end with one big takeaway: **economically meaningful infrastructure is infrastructure that markets believe will arrive.** That’s stronger than a recap of tables.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The gap is primarily a **framing and ambition problem**, with some **scope** issues.

This is not obviously a novelty problem: the event is novel enough, and the empirical fact is interesting enough. The problem is that the paper currently presents itself as a careful case study whose main econometric lesson is “naive DiD is misleading.” That is not enough for AER. The AER version is a paper about **the economic credibility of government infrastructure promises**.

### What is the biggest current gap?

The manuscript has not fully converted a good event into a big question.

The big question is not:
- “What happened to prices around HS2 cancellation?”

It is:
- “Under what conditions do announced public investments become capitalized into private asset values?”

HS2 is the test case, not the endpoint.

### Is it a framing problem, scope problem, novelty problem, or ambition problem?

Mostly:
- **Framing problem**: the science is there, but the story is too local and too defensive.
- **Ambition problem**: the paper is content to document a null around a famous event instead of using that null to say something larger about credibility and expectations.
- Secondarily a **scope problem**: it would benefit from stronger evidence or sharper heterogeneity around credibility/blight/anticipated gains, but the biggest gain is from narrative repositioning.

### Single most impactful advice

If the author can change only one thing, it should be this:

**Rewrite the paper around the claim that the economic incidence of infrastructure announcements depends on credibility, using HS2’s cancellation as evidence that politically fragile mega-projects may never be capitalized into local asset prices in the first place.**

That means:
- stop centering the naive DiD,
- stop centering pre-trend failure as the “result,”
- center non-capitalization under uncertainty.

If they can support that with a bit more cross-sectional evidence on where credibility was higher or lower, the paper becomes much more serious.
