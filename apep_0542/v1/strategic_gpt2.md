## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether housing markets had actually priced in the expected benefits of a major but politically fragile infrastructure project by studying what happened when the UK abruptly cancelled HS2 Phase 2. Using a very salient real-world reversal, it argues that there was no detectable break in local house prices at cancellation, suggesting that distant, uncertain infrastructure may generate much less anticipatory capitalization than economists often presume.

That is a question economists should care about: not “do rail stations raise land values when they open?” but “when do announced public investments become real enough to affect private asset prices?” That speaks directly to credibility, expectations, political economy, and how we should think about the incidence of infrastructure policy.

**Does the paper articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Almost, but not quite. The current opening is vivid and readable, but it drifts too quickly into “transit usually raises property values” and not quickly enough into the sharper, more AER-worthy question: **when does announced infrastructure become capitalized, and what does cancellation teach us about belief formation?** The paper’s true hook is not HS2 per se; it is the economics of credibility and anticipatory capitalization under political uncertainty.

**What the first two paragraphs should say instead:**

> Governments regularly announce large infrastructure projects years before completion, and households, firms, and planners act as if those projects will reshape local economies. But when do markets actually believe these announcements? We study this question using the sudden cancellation of HS2 Phase 2, the northern extension of the UK’s flagship high-speed rail project. If the market had capitalized the promised accessibility gains, cancellation should have reduced nearby property values. If prices did not move, that implies either that markets had already heavily discounted delivery or that expected benefits were offset by relief from construction blight.
>
> Using 2.1 million residential transactions around planned HS2 stations, we show that the cancellation did not generate a detectable break in local price trajectories. The broader contribution is to distinguish realized infrastructure effects from anticipated ones: for politically contested, long-horizon projects, official approval may be far from sufficient for full capitalization. That matters for how economists think about infrastructure appraisal, policy credibility, and the private incidence of public investment announcements.

That is the pitch. It elevates the paper from “a housing DiD on HS2” to “a paper about when markets believe the state.”

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution in one sentence:**  
The paper uses the cancellation of HS2 Phase 2 to argue that politically uncertain, long-horizon infrastructure projects may not be fully capitalized into nearby housing prices even after formal approval and partial implementation.

### Is this clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?
Only partially. The paper cites the right broad literatures, but the differentiation is still muddy. Right now, the contribution risks reading as:

- another transit capitalization paper,
- with a null effect,
- from a cancellation setting.

That is not enough. The paper needs to distinguish itself more sharply from:
1. papers on **actual rail openings** and realized accessibility,
2. papers on **announcement effects** of credible infrastructure,
3. papers on **housing capitalization of local public goods** more generally,
4. event studies using **negative shocks** to infer prior capitalization.

The key distinction should be: **this paper is about failed or noncredible state promises, not infrastructure per se.**

### World question or literature gap?
At present, it is mixed, but too often framed as “the literature studies openings; we study cancellation.” That is a literature gap. The stronger framing is a world question:

- **When do households treat government infrastructure promises as real enough to price into assets?**
- **How much private wealth is actually created or destroyed by infrastructure announcements before projects are built?**
- **What does cancellation reveal about state credibility?**

That is much stronger than “there are not enough cancellation papers.”

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new?
Not confidently, at least not from the current introduction. They might say:  
“It's a DiD/event study on house prices around cancelled HS2 stations, and they don’t find much because of pretrends.”

That is a problem. The memorable version should be:  
“They use the cancellation of HS2 to show that markets may not capitalize large infrastructure promises when delivery is politically shaky.”

That is a paper. The first is a design.

### What would make the contribution bigger?
Several possibilities:

1. **Shift from local house prices to belief formation / credibility.**  
   The paper should explicitly become about the credibility of government commitments. The current wording hints at this but does not fully own it.

2. **Exploit heterogeneity in credibility ex ante.**  
   The biggest upgrade would be to compare locations where completion was more credible versus less credible:
   - Phase 2a vs 2b,
   - stations with more advanced legal status,
   - places with more land acquisition,
   - places where route safeguarding/blight was more intense,
   - eastern leg vs western leg, but framed as differential prior credibility rather than just another split.
   
   If the paper could show that cancellation mattered only where the project looked genuinely likely, the contribution becomes much larger and much more economic.

3. **Separate accessibility-loss from blight-relief more concretely.**  
   Right now that mechanism is plausible but speculative. Strategically, the paper needs to show it understands why the net effect could be zero. If it cannot prove mechanisms, it should at least structure the paper around competing channels.

4. **Connect to welfare and appraisal more directly.**  
   The introduction gestures at benefit-cost analysis, but this could be much sharper: if uncertain projects are not capitalized, then the private incidence of announcements differs from standard intuition.

The most important “bigger” move is not more outcomes; it is a more ambitious conceptual frame.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Economists know that transport infrastructure often raises nearby land and house values. Governments frequently justify big projects partly through these expected local gains, and communities behave as if future infrastructure will reshape their fortunes long before completion.

### Tension
But most evidence comes from openings or highly credible projects. For long-horizon, politically contested megaprojects, it is unclear whether markets ever fully believe the promise. HS2 Phase 2 is ideal because it was formally advanced enough to seem real, yet politically fragile enough that sophisticated buyers may have discounted it heavily.

### Resolution
The cancellation does not produce a detectable break in local house prices. What appears at first to be a positive treatment effect is actually a pre-existing trend, leaving the main economic takeaway that there was no clear capitalization to unwind at the moment of cancellation.

### Implications
The incidence of infrastructure policy depends on credibility. Announcements alone may not create much private wealth if households discount the probability of completion; cancellations may therefore destroy less wealth than headline politics suggests. More broadly, economists should distinguish between announced, credible, and realized infrastructure.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
It has the ingredients, but the arc is not fully disciplined. The current paper often reads like:

1. Here is HS2,
2. here is the dataset,
3. here is the DiD,
4. the DiD fails,
5. here are several possible explanations.

That is somewhat more like a sequence of empirical diagnostics than a clean story.

### What story should it be telling?
The story should be:

1. **Megaprojects create expectations, but expectations depend on credibility.**
2. **HS2 Phase 2 looked advanced enough that cancellation should have mattered if the market believed it.**
3. **Yet cancellation did not move prices.**
4. **Therefore formal approval is not the same as credible commitment.**
5. **This changes how we think about anticipatory capitalization and infrastructure appraisal.**

That is a much clearer AER-style narrative than “the DiD has bad pretrends but the null is interesting.”

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

### What fact would I lead with?
“I would lead with: when the UK cancelled the northern extension of its flagship high-speed rail project, nearby house prices did not visibly break.”

That is a good lead fact. It is surprising enough to get attention.

### Would people lean in?
Yes, initially. HS2 is salient, cancellation is dramatic, and the result runs against a simple capitalization intuition.

But the second reaction will be immediate skepticism:  
“Wait — did the market already know it wasn’t happening?”  
That is exactly the right follow-up question, and in fact it is the paper’s real content.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Likely one of these:
- Was the cancellation truly a surprise?
- Were markets already pricing in failure?
- Can you separate lost accessibility from relief from blight?
- Are you finding “no capitalization” or just “offsetting effects”?

Those are not bad questions; they are the questions the paper should embrace.

### Is the null itself interesting?
Yes, but only if framed correctly. A null in a standard local housing DiD is not inherently interesting. A null in the face of a major, nationally salient policy reversal **is** interesting if it teaches us that markets discount government promises.

At present, the paper makes this case reasonably well, but it still sounds a bit defensive — as though the null is what remains after the intended design fell apart. The framing needs to be more assertive: **the absence of a price break is itself the finding.** The failed parallel-trends design is not the paper; the paper is that even such a dramatic cancellation did not visibly move prices.

If the authors do not take control of that message, readers will experience it as a failed experiment.

   The introduction does eventually say the right thing, but it takes too long to get there. The paper should declare earlier:

3. **Do not overinvest in standard empirical framing if the point is conceptual.**  
   There is a lot of space on treatment rings and control groups. That matters for a referee report; strategically, what matters is the economic object being learned about. The paper should spend more narrative energy on why cancellation is informative about prior beliefs.

4. **Mechanisms section needs to be tighter and more hierarchical.**  
   Right now there are several plausible stories:

   - broad regional convergence.
   
   These need ordering. My suggestion:
   - First-order interpretation: no discrete break implies little net capitalization remained by October 2023.
   - Two leading reasons: prior disbelief and offsetting blight relief.

5. **The Phase 1 result should be either elevated or downplayed.**  
   As written, it sits awkwardly. If the paper wants to use it, it should explicitly say: ongoing construction appears to depress nearby prices, which helps explain why cancellation could have small net effects. Reasonably, but not enough. The good stuff is:

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The paper’s biggest issue is **framing plus ambition**, not basic competence.

### What is the gap?
Primarily:

- **Framing problem:** The paper is more interesting than it sounds. It keeps presenting itself as a transit capitalization paper on HS2, when it should present itself as a paper on the credibility of government infrastructure commitments.
- **Ambition problem:** It stops at “we don’t detect an effect.” For AER, the paper needs to say something sharper about why and under what conditions markets capitalize announcements.
- **Scope problem:** It needs a clearer conceptual decomposition of the null. Right now the reader cannot tell whether the main lesson is disbelief, offsetting channels, or lack of surprise.
- **Novelty problem:** In current form, many readers will still bucket it as “another house-price quasi-experiment,” albeit with a cancellation shock.

### What would excite the top 10 people in the field?
A version of this paper that turns HS2 into a **test of state credibility** rather than a local real estate event study.

The top-field audience would want one of two things:
1. a cleaner demonstration that ex ante credibility determined capitalization, or
2. a more general framework for thinking about announced vs credible vs realized infrastructure.

Without that, it remains a smart, publishable paper, but likely not an AER paper.

### Single most impactful piece of advice
**Reframe the paper around the capitalization of credible government commitments, and organize every section around distinguishing “announced,” “credible,” and “realized” infrastructure rather than around a standard station-proximity DiD.**

If they only change one thing, it should be that. That single change would improve the title, introduction, literature review, interpretation of the null, and the overall ambition of the paper.
