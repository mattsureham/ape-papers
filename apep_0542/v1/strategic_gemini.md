## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
This paper exploits the abrupt 2023 cancellation of the UK’s High Speed 2 (HS2) Phase 2 to test a fundamental assumption in urban economics: do housing markets actually capitalize the promised benefits of long-horizon, politically uncertain infrastructure? By analyzing 2.1 million transactions, the authors find a surprising "null" result—no detectable price drop upon cancellation—suggesting that either the "accessibility premium" was never priced in due to low project credibility, or it was offset by the relief of removing "construction blight."

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this well in the second paragraph. However, the first paragraph is a bit "story-heavy" with the homeowner in Crewe. For the AER, the pitch needs to lean harder on the theoretical stakes. 
*The pitch the paper should have:* "Economists typically assume that infrastructure announcements are immediately capitalized into land values. We test this by examining the sudden cancellation of the UK’s largest rail project, HS2 Phase 2, finding that the market did not respond. This suggests that for mega-projects with decades-long horizons and high political risk, the 'anticipatory premium' assumed in cost-benefit analyses may be non-existent."

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**Contribution:** The paper identifies the limits of anticipatory capitalization for long-horizon, low-credibility public investments.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It differentiates itself from the "positive shock" literature (Gibbons & Machin) by looking at a "negative shock" (cancellation), similar to Greenstone & Gallagher (2008). 
*   **World vs. Literature:** It is framed as a question about the world (HS2), but it’s actually a paper about **Credibility**. This is its strength.
*   **Clarity:** A smart economist would say: "It’s a paper showing that if people don't believe a train is actually coming, they don't pay more for the house."
*   **Bigger Contribution:** To make this "AER big," the authors need to move beyond saying "we found a zero." They need to provide a **model or a metric for Project Credibility**. Can they use betting market data, Twitter sentiment, or political polling to show *why* the market discounted this? A "zero" is only interesting if you can explain the mechanism of the discount.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
The paper sits in the intersection of Urban Economics (Ahlfeldt et al., 2015) and Public Finance (credibility of government commitments).

*   **Neighbors:** Donaldson (2018) on railroads; Heblich et al. (2020) on London's historical transit; Greenstone & Gallagher (2008) on "valuation of non-events."
*   **Positioning:** It should stop "attacking" the positive DiD result and instead **synthesize** it. The story isn't "the DiD was wrong"; the story is "the DiD captures a 10-year regional convergence that the HS2 announcement failed to deviate from."
*   **Missing Lit:** It needs to speak to the **Political Economy of Infrastructure**. Look at Flyvbjerg (the "Iron Law of Megaprojects"). The housing market is essentially acting as a real-time auditor of government competence.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC
*   **Setup:** Government promises a £100B train; theory says prices should rise.
*   **Tension:** The project is a mess (costs triple, delays mount). Does the market believe the promise?
*   **Resolution:** The project is cancelled, and... nothing happens.
*   **Implications:** Benefit-cost models that assume 100% capitalization of *announced* projects are fundamentally flawed because they ignore a "political risk discount."

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong. It’s a "detective story" where the detective finds no footprints. However, the "Regional Convergence" (Section 6.1) currently feels like a "limitation." It should be moved up as a **finding**: The structural forces of the UK economy (post-COVID North-South convergence) are far more powerful than the marginal value of a theoretical train.

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
At an economist dinner party:
*   **Lead fact:** "The UK cancelled its biggest infrastructure project ever and the housing market literally didn't blink."
*   **Reaction:** People lean in.
*   **Follow-up:** "Was it because they knew it was never going to happen, or because living next to a construction site for 20 years is so miserable that the train wasn't worth the noise?"
*   **Conclusion:** This is a "high-signal null." It’s not a failed experiment; it’s a measurement of market skepticism.

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
*   **Front-loading:** Move the "Construction Disruption" result (Phase 1 negative effect) earlier. It’s the most "active" result in the paper and provides a beautiful counterpoint: "People didn't value the future benefit, but they *did* devalue the current noise."
*   **Appendix:** The station-by-station heterogeneity (Figure 3) is great but could be summarized in text. 
*   **Mechanism:** Section 6.2 is the most important part of the paper. It needs more data. Can the authors find "safeguarding" data (properties that were legally frozen)? That is the "smoking gun" for the blight-relief channel.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The gap is **Mechanism vs. Measurement.** Right now, it’s a very high-quality measurement of a zero. To be an AER paper, it needs to be a paper about **the Economics of Skepticism.**

**Single most impactful advice:**
Don't just report the null; **quantify the "Credibility Discount."** Use the cost-overrun history or political news cycles to show that as the project's "Probability of Completion" (P) fell, the "Capitalized Premium" (V) stayed at zero. Make this a paper about how markets price (or fail to price) government incompetence.
