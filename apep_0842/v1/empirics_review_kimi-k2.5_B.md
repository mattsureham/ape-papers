**3. Essential Points**

**Identification concerns regarding the null effect.** The triple-difference strategy relies on the assumption that designation timing is as-good-as-random conditional on the fixed effects. However, the paper acknowledges that "countries designate origins that already receive low recognition in their jurisdiction" (p. 6), suggesting endogenous timing. The event study shows positive pre-trend coefficients at *t*-4 and *t*-3 (albeit marginally significant), which raises concerns about parallel trends. The claim that the immediate pre-period (*t*-2) is "clean" is insufficient when earlier periods show differential trends. The authors must address why pre-trends exist—perhaps designation occurs after recognition rates peak and begin declining—and whether this invalidates the causal interpretation of the null. A more convincing approach would show that the *timing* of designation within a destination-origin pair is uncorrelated with prior recognition trends, or use an alternative instrument (e.g., political shifts in destination countries).

**Measurement error in treatment intensity.** The paper codes treatment as a binary indicator, but the policy mechanism (accelerated procedures, reversed burden of proof) likely varies in intensity across destinations. Germany's 2015 designation of Albania, for instance, may have been implemented more strictly than France's variable list or Belgium's designations. If treatment intensity varies and the binary indicator mismeasures the true "dose," classical measurement error biases coefficients toward zero, potentially explaining the null on recognition rates. The authors should validate the binary coding against actual procedural changes (e.g., processing times, legal appeal rates) or use an intensity-weighted measure (e.g., fraction of applicants from that nationality processed under accelerated procedures). At minimum, the paper should discuss measurement error as a potential explanation for the null.

**Confounding in the system-wide deterrence analysis.** The paper interprets the negative coefficient on "share of SCO neighbors" as evidence of system-wide deterrence (Column 2, Table 3). However, this variable varies at the origin-year level and may capture unobserved origin-specific shocks. For example, improved conditions in Albania could lead to (1) more EU countries designating it as safe and (2) fewer Albanians applying everywhere, generating a spurious correlation. The identification strategy for this result is weaker than the main triple-difference because it lacks the destination-specific variation that absorbs destination-time confounds. The authors should either strengthen this analysis (e.g., by including origin-specific linear trends or using variation in designation timing that is plausibly exogenous to origin-year shocks) or significantly qualify the interpretation, acknowledging that the result may reflect correlated origin-year effects rather than causal spillovers.

**4. Suggestions**

**Clarify the mechanism: label versus procedure.** The paper treats SCO designation as a "label," but the policy operates through specific procedural changes (accelerated examination, reversed burden of proof, reduced appeal rights). The null effect on recognition rates could mean that adjudicators ignore the label, or that the procedural changes do not bind because applicants still present valid individual circumstances. The paper should disaggregate the analysis by decision type (Geneva Convention vs. subsidiary/humanitarian protection) to test whether designation shifts the *type* of protection granted even if not the overall recognition rate. If designation reduces Convention status but increases subsidiary protection, the null on total recognition would mask meaningful substitution effects.

**Visual evidence and event study interpretation.** The paper references an event study but does not include the figure in the provided text. The description mentions positive coefficients at *t*-4 and *t*-3, which is concerning for pre-trends. The authors should include the full event study plot and discuss explicitly why the earlier positive coefficients do not invalidate the design. One possibility is that designation occurs after recognition rates peak for certain nationalities (e.g., during the 2015 crisis), and the pre-trend reflects the rising phase of the crisis. If so, the post-treatment period shows the continued decline, and the designation itself adds nothing—consistent with the null. This story should be made explicit.

**External validity and the 2025 common list.** The paper notes that the EU adopted a common list in December 2025 (a future date relative to the data), but the implications are not fully developed. The common list may operate differently than national lists because (1) it eliminates the diversion channel (no non-designating neighbors remain for listed countries), and (2) it may create coordination effects where the "safe" signal is stronger when broadcast by the EU rather than individual states. The paper should discuss how the findings map to the common list context: if designation works through deterrence, a common list may reduce total EU applications from designated origins, but if it works through diversion, the common list may be ineffective at reducing total claims (just redistributing them). The current draft conflates these mechanisms somewhat.

**Heterogeneous effects by volume and case strength.** The heterogeneity analysis splits by Balkan vs non-Balkan and large vs small destinations, but other cuts might be more informative. For example, does the null on recognition rates hold even in high-volume cells where adjudicators might be more reliant on the designation heuristic? Or does it vary by the "objective" safety of the origin (e.g., countries with high overall EU recognition rates vs. low ones)? The paper could also test whether designations affect processing times (as suggested by the "accelerated procedure" mechanism) using data on decision timing if available.

**Power and precision.** The 95% confidence interval for the main effect is [-0.055, 0.059], which excludes large positive effects but also large negative effects. However, the abstract emphasizes the "striking null." The authors should report the minimum detectable effect (MDE) given the sample size and variation to clarify what magnitude of effect could be ruled out. If the MDE is large (e.g., 10 percentage points), the null is less informative than claimed.

**Data validation.** The abstract cites a specific comparison: "An Albanian asylum seeker in Germany faces a 6\% recognition rate; the same applicant in Italy faces 33\%." The paper should verify this specific fact in the data and note whether these are first-instance rates from the same year, or if they reflect compositional differences in applicant characteristics (which the fixed effects should absorb, but the raw comparison might still be misleading).

**Ethical implications.** The paper raises important ethical questions about deterrence in the Discussion, but these could be sharpened. If designations deter without improving decision quality, they may violate the principle of non-refoulement (if valid claims are discouraged) or the right to seek asylum. The paper could connect the findings more explicitly to legal standards and the "externalization" debate in EU asylum policy.
