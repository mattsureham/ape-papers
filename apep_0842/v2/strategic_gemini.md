### 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
This paper asks whether formal "safe country" labels in the EU actually change how asylum cases are decided, or if they simply change who applies. By exploiting staggered policy adoptions across 22 countries, it finds that the massive 27-percentage-point gap in recognition rates for "safe" countries is a mirage caused by selection; once you account for fixed effects, the causal effect on decisions is zero, while the effect on deterring applications is a massive 35%.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this very clearly. The first two paragraphs successfully set up the "Asylum Lottery" puzzle, provide the punchline (the explanation is wrong), and state the dual finding (zero on the intensive margin, large on the extensive margin). It is a model of clarity.

---

### 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**The Contribution:** The paper demonstrates that formal legal classifications can be "adjudication-neutral" but "selection-powerful," resolving a long-standing debate over the cause of cross-country asylum recognition disparities.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It differentiates itself well from the "asylum lottery" literature (Neumayer, Toshkov) by moving from correlation to a triple-diff causal framework.
*   **Framing:** It is framed as answering a question about the **WORLD** (Why do asylum rates differ?), which is then abstracted into a broader point about bureaucratic discretion.
*   **Clarity:** A smart economist would immediately understand the "Selection vs. Adjudication" trade-off. It is definitely not "just another DiD."
*   **How to make it bigger:** The "system-wide" deterrence finding in Section 5.2 is actually the most "AER-sized" part of the paper. If the author could more convincingly tie this to a general equilibrium or "information as a public good" framework, the contribution moves from "asylum policy" to "theory of global policy signals."

---

### 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
The paper sits at the intersection of **Labor/Migration** (Hatton, Peri) and **Public/Political Economy** (Lipsky, bureaucratic discretion).

*   **Neighbors:** Neumayer (2004), Hatton (2009), Czaika & de Haas (2013).
*   **Positioning:** It acts as a "correction" to the first group and a "clean test case" for the second.
*   **Missing link:** It could speak more to the **Industrial Organization of Bureaucracy.** If adjudicators don't change their behavior, is it because of "fixed costs" in changing legal standards, or "reputational concerns" among judges?
*   **Conversation:** It is having the right conversation, but it could be broader. It should position itself not just as a migration paper, but as a paper on **Labeling Effects in Governance** (akin to how "Investment Grade" or "Organic" labels affect markets).

---

### 4. NARRATIVE ARC
*   **Setup:** EU countries have wildly different asylum grant rates; "Safe Country" lists are the prime suspect.
*   **Tension:** The raw data strongly supports this suspect, but the data is polluted by the timing of the Balkan crisis and country-specific selection.
*   **Resolution:** A rigorous triple-diff "acquits" the policy on adjudication but "convicts" it on deterrence.
*   **Implications:** Harmonizing EU lists won't fix the "lottery," but it will likely increase deterrence.

**Evaluation:** The narrative arc is very strong. It follows a "Detective Story" structure: investigating a lead (SCO labels), finding it's a red herring for one crime (grant rates) but the culprit for another (deterrence).

---

### 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
**The Party Fact:** "Labeling a country as 'safe' doesn't actually make it any harder for an individual to get asylum—it just scares them away from trying."

**Reaction:** People will lean in. The counter-intuitive nature of the "zero effect" on judges (despite the reversed burden of proof) is the hook. The follow-up question will be: "Wait, if the law says the burden of proof is reversed, why don't the judges change their minds?" That is a high-quality "So what?"

---

### 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
*   **Move to Appendix:** The "Standardized Effect Sizes" (Table 7) feels like overkill for the main text.
*   **Expand:** The "Protection-Type Substitution" (Section 5.3) is fascinating. It suggests judges *are* doing something, just not what we thought. This deserves more than one page. It’s the "smoke" that proves there’s a fire, even if the house didn't burn down.
*   **Front-loading:** The results are well-paced. No changes needed there.

---

### 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The paper is currently a very high-quality "Policy Evaluation" piece. To make it an **AER paper**, it needs more **Mechanistic Ambition.**

The gap is the "Why?" Why do adjudicators ignore a formal legislative mandate to apply a "presumption against protection"? Is it because they are professionalized civil servants who follow international law (Geneva Convention) over national lists?

**Single Biggest Advice:** Deepen the "Discussion" into a "Model of Bureaucratic Resistance." Use the substitution of Geneva status for humanitarian status to build a formal argument about how bureaucrats "budget" their discretion to offset political signals they find un-credible.
