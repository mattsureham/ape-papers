## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a clean and policy-relevant question: when governments label an origin country “safe,” do asylum officials become less likely to grant protection, or do potential applicants simply stop applying? Using variation in safe-country designations across EU destinations, origins, and time, the paper’s central claim is that these labels do not change adjudication outcomes but do substantially deter applications, including possibly across the wider European asylum system.

A busy economist should care because this is not just a migration-policy paper. At its best, it is about a broader question: when governments create formal categories, do those categories change decisions inside the bureaucracy, or do they mainly change selection into the system?

Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs? Mostly yes, but not optimally. The introduction currently opens with the “asylum lottery” and then quickly pivots to disproving one explanation. That is decent, but the sharper and more AER-worthy pitch is not “I revisit one asylum-policy explanation,” but “I distinguish between two fundamentally different channels of policy incidence: adjudication versus selection.” The first two paragraphs should frame the paper around that conceptual distinction, with asylum as the ideal empirical setting.

### The pitch the paper should have

Governments often attach formal labels to people, places, or firms—safe country lists, risk ratings, compliance categories—with the expectation that these labels will change how bureaucrats make decisions. But formal classifications can affect outcomes through two very different channels: they can change how officials treat cases already in the system, or they can change who enters the system in the first place. This paper asks which channel matters for one of the most consequential classification policies in Europe: the designation of asylum applicants’ origin countries as “safe.”

Using variation in safe-country designations across EU destinations, origin nationalities, and years, I show that these labels do not causally reduce asylum recognition rates once one accounts for who gets designated and when. Instead, they primarily reduce applications, suggesting that safe-country policy operates through deterrence and selection rather than through adjudication. The broader implication is that formal government classifications may shape access to administrative systems more than the decisions made within them.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:**  
The paper argues that safe-country designations in Europe operate mainly by deterring asylum applications rather than by changing the probability that filed claims are granted.

This is a real contribution, and the paper does a better-than-average job stating it. But the contribution is still not fully differentiated from adjacent migration-policy papers because it is presented as a result about one policy instrument rather than as a general decomposition of policy effects into entry and adjudication margins.

### Is it clearly differentiated from the closest papers?
Partially, but not enough. The paper names three literatures, but the introduction still reads a bit like “here is a DiD on an asylum policy.” What is distinctive is not just “causal evidence on SCO designations.” It is:

1. overturning a widely believed interpretation of the raw asylum-recognition gap,
2. separating decision-stage from application-stage effects for the same policy,
3. suggesting that harmonization may amplify deterrence without improving consistency.

That bundle needs to be more explicitly contrasted with the existing literature, which mostly documents cross-country recognition differences or estimates average deterrence effects of restrictive asylum policy, without isolating these two margins for the same instrument.

### World question or literature-gap question?
The paper is strongest when it is answering a **world question**: what do safe-country labels actually do?  
It is weaker when it says things like “this contributes to three literatures.” That is useful housekeeping, but not what will make people care.

The paper should lean harder into the world question: policymakers think these labels improve decision consistency and weed out weak claims. Do they? The answer, per the paper, is no: they mostly alter who shows up.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?
Almost, but not quite. A smart economist might currently say: “It’s a triple-diff paper showing safe-country designation doesn’t affect recognition rates much, but it lowers applications.” That is fine, but still sounds like “another DiD paper about asylum policy.”

What they should instead say is: “It shows a formal government label changes system entry, not within-system decisions, overturning a common explanation for asylum-outcome disparities.” That is a stronger novelty statement.

### What would make the contribution bigger?
Be specific:

- **Stronger mechanism on selection/composition.** The paper says the raw recognition gap is compositional and that applications fall, but it cannot show directly who stops applying. If it could show a shift in applicant composition—by age, sex, family status, repeat vs first-time applicants, or origin subgroups—that would dramatically enlarge the contribution.
- **Processing margins.** If safe-country designations do not alter grant rates but do alter processing speed, appeal rates, or withdrawal/abandonment, the paper could say more concretely what bureaucratic treatment does change. That would turn the result from “not adjudication” into “not adjudication, but administration.”
- **Welfare or normative bite.** The paper hints that valid claims may be deterred too, but does not show it. Even suggestive evidence on downstream consequences would raise stakes.
- **Broader framing with other classification policies.** The introduction gestures toward a general theory of formal labels. To make that bigger rather than decorative, the paper would need either a sharper conceptual framework or cleaner comparative language linking asylum labels to other administrative classifications.

That reframing would materially improve the paper’s strategic position.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Europe exhibits large cross-country differences in asylum recognition rates for the same nationalities. Safe-country designations are a natural and widely cited explanation because they explicitly alter asylum procedure for applicants from designated origins.

### Tension
The raw data strongly support that view—designated-origin applications have much lower recognition rates—but it is unclear whether the label actually changes adjudication or merely changes who applies and where. That is the central puzzle.

### Resolution
Once the paper accounts for origin-specific timing and destination-specific trends, the effect on recognition rates disappears. But applications fall substantially when a country is designated safe, and possibly even more broadly across destinations.

### Implications
Safe-country policy appears to function as a deterrence/selection device, not as a tool for harmonizing or improving adjudication outcomes. This changes how one should think about asylum policy design and, more generally, about the effects of formal government classification.

The good news is that the paper does have a real narrative arc. The bad news is that it occasionally loses confidence in its own story and reverts into a list of estimands, robustness notes, and literature contributions. It is not a collection of random results, but it is still written more like a competent empirical paper than like a paper with a memorable central narrative.

The story it should be telling, relentlessly, is:

1. There is a striking raw fact.
2. The usual interpretation of that fact is wrong.
3. The policy matters, but through a different margin.
4. That distinction changes both positive analysis and policy evaluation.

That is the paper.

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

### What fact would I lead with?
“I can explain 27 percentage points of apparent recognition-rate differences with composition, not adjudication; the safe-country label changes applications, not grant rates.”

That is a good dinner-party fact. Better still:

“Europe’s ‘safe country’ labels don’t seem to change whether asylum claims are granted once filed—they mostly change whether people apply at all.”

### Would people lean in?
Yes, moderately. This is more interesting than a standard reduced-form result because it overturns a plausible and widely held interpretation. The extensive-margin result gives it policy bite.

But they will only lean in if the paper leads with the conceptual distinction, not the estimator. If presented as “a triple-difference on safe-country designations,” phones come out. If presented as “a policy everyone thinks changes bureaucratic decisions actually changes selection instead,” people stay with you.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Probably one of these:

- “So what explains the asylum lottery, if not safe-country lists?”
- “Who exactly stops applying?”
- “Are governments using these labels to deter weak claims, or valid claims too?”
- “Does the policy affect speed, appeals, or procedural treatment even if not final recognition?”
- “How much can we trust the system-wide deterrence claim?”

Those are all productive follow-up questions. The best papers anticipate the first two in the framing itself. This one partly does, but could do more.

### If findings are null or modest
The main adjudication result is a null, but it is genuinely interesting because it overturns a prominent interpretation of a large descriptive gap. The paper does make this case, and that helps.

Still, the null will only travel if the author is disciplined. Top-journal readers are willing to care about a null when:
- the prior belief is strong,
- the raw correlation is large,
- the null sharply changes interpretation,
- there is another positive margin of adjustment.

This paper has all four. That is its path.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

This is not primarily a methods problem. It is mostly a **framing and ambition problem**, with some scope limitations.

### What is the gap?

- **Scope problem:** The selection story is compelling but somewhat underdeveloped. The paper can show fewer applications, but not much about who is deterred or what else changes procedurally.
- **Novelty problem:** Within migration/asylum, the paper is novel enough. For AER, the novelty has to be made legible outside that field.

The paper in current form feels more like a strong field-journal paper or a top-field paper than a clear AER paper. To get to AER territory, it needs to make readers outside migration care within the first page.

### Single most impactful advice
**Reframe the paper around a general economic question—whether formal government classifications affect outcomes through treatment or through selection—and make the asylum setting the clean test case of that broader mechanism.**

That is the one change that would most improve its odds. Everything else is second order.
