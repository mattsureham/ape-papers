**SUBJECT:** Strategic Positioning of "Legislating Peace? Anti-Open Grazing Laws and Farmer-Herder Violence in Nigeria"

---

### 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether formal legislation can mitigate lethal communal resource conflicts in "weak-state" settings. Specifically, it uses the staggered adoption of anti-open grazing laws in Nigeria to show that criminalizing nomadic pastoralism in favor of sedentary ranching significantly reduced non-state violence and fatalities in the targeted areas.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this clearly, but the second paragraph spends a bit too much time on the "mechanisms of the crisis" (climate change, population growth) rather than the "mechanisms of the paper." 

**The pitch the paper should have:** 
"Can a 'stroke of the pen' reduce centuries-old communal violence when state capacity is low? I exploit a unique wave of Nigerian state laws that effectively ended open-range pastoralism, finding a nearly 80% reduction in local conflict events. This suggests that even imperfectly enforced formal institutions can break retaliatory violence cycles by altering the expected costs of resource incursions."

---

### 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution:** The paper provides the first causal evidence that domestic legislative restrictions on land use can substantially reduce communal violence in a developing country context.

- **Differentiation:** It moves beyond the "climate-shocks-cause-conflict" literature (e.g., McGuirk & Nunn, 2025) to evaluate a policy *solution*.
- **Framing:** It is currently framed more as a gap-filler ("No study has applied modern causal inference..."). It needs to be framed as a question about the world: **Does the law matter in the absence of the Leviathan?** 
- **Bigger Contribution:** To reach the AER, the paper needs to move beyond the "it worked" finding and explain *how* it worked given the "implementation challenges" mentioned on page 6. If the state is too weak to provide ranches, why did the herders stop coming? Is it deterrence (fear of the "Livestock Guard") or simply a coordination signal to farmers?

---

### 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

- **Closest Neighbors:** McGuirk and Nunn (2025) on transhumance; Dube and Vargas (2013) on resource shocks; Dell and Querubin (2018) on state interventions.
- **Positioning:** The paper should **synthesize** the "conflict economics" and "political economy of institutions" literatures. It currently sits too squarely in "development/conflict." It should speak to the broader North (1991) / Acemoglu et al. (2001) conversation about whether formal rules can stick in the presence of strong informal norms.
- **Right Conversation?** It should explicitly engage with the "Coase Theorem" in the presence of high transaction costs/violence. The law is essentially reallocating property rights; why did this lead to less violence rather than just a different form of conflict?

---

### 4. NARRATIVE ARC

- **Setup:** A "tragedy of the commons" where population growth and climate change make open grazing a source of lethal conflict.
- **Tension:** Proponents say laws stop the trigger; critics say laws criminalize livelihoods and will backfire (displacement/escalation).
- **Resolution:** A massive, robust decline in violence with no evidence of spillover or displacement.
- **Implications:** Formal law acts as a "de-escalation" tool even when enforcement is incomplete.

**Evaluation:** The narrative arc is strong and linear. The "SGF wave" of 2021 provides a perfect "political shock" to address endogeneity, which serves as the "twist" that saves the paper from standard DiD critiques.

---

### 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

**The Dinner Party Fact:** "Nigeria passed a law banning open grazing, and even though they didn't have the police to fully enforce it or the money to build the ranches they promised, the death toll in those areas dropped by 80%."

**The Follow-up:** "If it was that successful, why hasn't every state done it, and did the herders just starve or move to the next state over?" (The paper needs to be bulletproof on the "Spillover" results in Table 3 to keep the audience leaning in.)

---

### 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

- **Front-loading:** The "Institutional Background" (Section 2) is a bit long. Move the "Federal Dimension" and "Historical Context" into a more concise "Institutional Setting" section.
- **Appendix:** The "Callaway-Sant’Anna" state-level results (Table 4) are weak/insignificant. These should be moved to an appendix or used only to justify why the LGA-level DDD is necessary. They currently muddy the lead.
- **Mechanism Section:** Section 6.1 needs to be promoted. The "Deterrence vs. Displacement" discussion is the most "AER-grade" part of the paper.

---

### 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The gap is **Theoretical Ambition**. Currently, it’s a very high-quality policy evaluation. To be an AER paper, it needs to tell us something fundamental about **Institutional Persistence and Change.** 

**Single most impactful piece of advice:** 
Develop a simple model or more rigorous empirical test of the *mechanism* of deterrence in a weak state. If the law reduced violence by 80% despite "uneven enforcement," then the *threat* of the law or the *legitimization* of farmer resistance is doing the work. Use the variation in "law detail" (Benue’s 1M naira fine vs. others) to test if the "severity of the threat" matters, or if it's just the "existence of the rule."
