## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
This paper evaluates whether formal legislation can mitigate communal resource conflict in a weak-state setting by examining Nigeria’s anti-open grazing laws. Using a triple-difference design that exploits staggered state-level adoption and sub-state variation in pastoral activity, the author finds that these laws reduced non-state violence in targeted areas by nearly 80%. This matters because it challenges the conventional wisdom that formal institutions are ineffective in "ungoverned spaces" where informal norms and resource competition dominate.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this well, but the first two paragraphs lean a bit heavily on the "policy puzzle" (Can laws work?) and the "descriptive horror" (73 people killed). 
**The pitch it should have:** 
"Does formal law matter in environments where state capacity is low and resource competition is existential? While conflict economics often emphasizes economic shocks or state capacity, this paper shows that a specific legislative shift—the criminalization of open-range grazing—can fundamentally re-equilibrate communal relations. By exploiting a wave of 'enclosure' laws in Nigeria, I demonstrate that even imperfectly enforced legislation can achieve a massive reduction in lethal violence by altering the costs of resource incursion."

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**Contribution:** The paper provides the first causal evidence that legislative "enclosure" policies can significantly reduce communal violence in a developing country context.

**Evaluation:**
- **Differentiation:** It differentiates well from the "economic shocks" literature (Miguel et al.; Dube & Vargas) by focusing on a policy lever rather than an exogenous shock. It also bridges the gap between qualitative political science and quantitative conflict economics.
- **Framing:** It is currently framed as "answering a question about the world," which is its strength. 
- **Explainability:** A smart economist would say: "It’s an enclosure paper. Like Hornbeck (2010), but for modern communal violence."
- **Bigger Contribution:** To make this truly "AER-big," the paper needs more on *why* it works despite weak enforcement. Is it the political signal? Is it localized enforcement by "Livestock Guards"? A mechanism section that moves beyond "it's not displacement" to "it is [X]" would be transformative.

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
- **Closest Neighbors:** Hornbeck (2010) on property rights/enclosure; McGuirk & Nunn (2025) on transhumant conflict; Dube & Vargas (2013) on conflict drivers.
- **Positioning:** It should position itself as the *institutional solution* to the problems raised by McGuirk & Nunn. If M&N show that climate-driven migration causes conflict, this paper shows that law can block that transmission mechanism.
- **Missing Conversations:** The paper needs to speak more to the "Legal Centralism vs. Legal Pluralism" literature. In Nigeria, traditional rulers (Emirs/Chiefs) usually handle these disputes. Why did a state law suddenly overrule or complement those centuries-old systems?

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC
- **Setup:** A world of escalating farmer-herder violence driven by climate change and traditional institution collapse.
- **Tension:** Critics say laws will just inflame tensions or be ignored; proponents say they provide the necessary "rules of the game."
- **Resolution:** The laws actually work. Violence falls by 79%. No significant displacement.
- **Implications:** Formal law *can* work in weak states if it targets a specific, regulable economic activity.

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong. However, the "Resolution" is a bit too clean. The tension between the "Benue Massacre" (which happened *after* the law) and the aggregate results needs to be handled more as a "feature, not a bug"—perhaps as a short-run escalation before long-run deterrence.

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
- **Lead Fact:** "Nigeria passed laws to stop cows from roaming, and it cut communal killings by 80%."
- **Reaction:** People will lean in. It's a massive coefficient.
- **Follow-up:** "Wait, if the state is too weak to stop Boko Haram, how can it stop a thousand-year-old tradition of nomadic herding?" That is the question the paper must answer to survive the referees.

---

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
- **Front-loading:** The "Effective Sample" analysis (Section 5.5/Table 4) is actually quite critical for transparency and should be mentioned earlier. It shows that the identification comes from 12 specific states.
- **Appendix:** Move some of the specific legislative dates to the appendix to keep the "Institutional Background" punchy.
- **Mechanisms:** Section 6 is mostly "tests for what it's NOT" (not displacement, not escalation). It needs a subsection on "What it IS." Is there data on cattle prices or agricultural yields? Even a proxy would help.

---

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The distance is **Medium**. The "Science" (the DDD) is competent, but the "Story" needs more ambition. Currently, it reads like a very good policy evaluation. To be an AER paper, it needs to be an *institutional theory* paper. 

**Single biggest improvement:** 
Explain the "Weak State Paradox": Why does a law work in a country where the state is ostensibly failing to provide security elsewhere? If the author can show that the law worked by *empowering local farmers to self-enforce* or by *providing a clear focal point for negotiation*, it becomes a landmark paper on institutional economics.
