## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether a highly salient but controversial policy response to one of Africa’s deadliest forms of communal conflict—Nigeria’s anti-open-grazing laws—actually reduces farmer-herder violence. Using variation in the timing of these laws across states and the fact that they should matter primarily in pastoral areas, the paper argues that the laws sharply reduce non-state conflict and fatalities in exposed locations.

A busy economist should care because this is, in principle, a paper about whether formal law can reduce violence in a weak-capacity setting by changing the rules governing resource use. That is a broad and important question; “do property-rights/enclosure-type legal reforms reduce violent conflict over land?” is much bigger than “what happened in Nigeria?”

**Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Not really. The current opening is vivid and informed, but it spends too much time on scene-setting and too little time crystallizing the general question. The introduction arrives at the broader stakes only later, after a lot of background and design description. The first paragraphs should move much faster from Nigerian facts to the general economics question.

**What the first two paragraphs should say instead:**

> Across much of the developing world, violent conflict emerges where economic actors compete over land under weakly enforced property rights. Nigeria’s farmer-herder conflict is an extreme example: clashes over grazing and cropland have killed thousands, yet governments increasingly respond with a blunt legal instrument—banning open grazing and forcing a transition from mobile pastoralism toward enclosed livestock management. The central question is whether such legal reforms reduce violence by clarifying rights and deterring encroachment, or instead inflame conflict by criminalizing livelihoods and displacing it elsewhere.
>
> This paper studies that question using the staggered adoption of anti-open-grazing laws across Nigerian states. Exploiting the fact that these laws should matter primarily in pastoral localities rather than elsewhere in the same state, I estimate whether the laws changed violence where exposure was greatest. The main result is that anti-grazing laws substantially reduce non-state violence and fatalities in pastoral areas, with little evidence of offsetting displacement to nearby non-pastoral zones.

That version makes the world question immediate and makes Nigeria the setting, not the contribution.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution in one sentence:**  
The paper’s contribution is to provide evidence that anti-open-grazing laws in Nigeria reduced farmer-herder violence in exposed pastoral areas, speaking to whether formal legal restrictions on land use can mitigate communal conflict.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?
Only partially. The paper clearly differentiates itself from the **qualitative Nigeria-specific literature** by saying “first causal estimate.” But that is not enough for AER positioning. “First causal estimate” is a weak differentiator unless the broader conceptual contribution is also clear.

Right now the paper distinguishes itself more by **method and setting** than by a sharp conceptual advance over adjacent economics papers. It says: no one has estimated this law causally; I do DDD. That is competent, but it risks sounding like “another policy evaluation in conflict.”

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or filling a gap in a LITERATURE?
Too much the latter. The introduction repeatedly says “no study has applied modern causal inference methods” and “this paper fills a gap.” That is not top-journal framing. The stronger frame is: **when resource conflict is tied to a specific economic practice, can legal prohibition of that practice reduce violence even where state capacity is weak?**

That is a world question. The literature-gap framing should be secondary.

### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what’s new?
At present, they might say:  
“It's a DiD/DDD paper on Nigeria anti-grazing laws and violence.”

That is the danger.

What they should instead say is:  
“It shows that a legal shift from open access/mobile grazing toward enclosure can reduce communal violence in a weak-state environment, not just in high-capacity historical settings.”

That would sound much more AER-worthy.

### What would make this contribution bigger?
Several possibilities, in descending order of strategic importance:

1. **Reframe around property rights / land-use regimes / enclosure and conflict.**  
   This is the single biggest gain. The paper already hints at Hornbeck and enclosure, but too timidly. If the paper can persuasively argue that anti-open-grazing laws are a modern property-rights reallocation, then it speaks to a much larger economics audience.

2. **Show reallocation in economic behavior, not just violence.**  
   If there were evidence on livestock movement, crop destruction, land use, cattle markets, agricultural output, or migration, the paper would become much bigger. Right now it is almost entirely a violence paper. AER will want more sense of how the law changes the underlying economy, not merely one bad outcome.

3. **Sharpen the mechanism around exposure and substitution.**  
   The deterrence/displacement framing is useful, but still somewhat descriptive. A stronger version would tie law to a more general model: when a policy narrows a contested-use margin, does violence decline or relocate? That could make the paper more than a Nigeria episode.

4. **Clarify the estimand and why it matters.**  
   The paper itself reveals that identification comes from a relatively limited set of mixed-composition states. That may be fine empirically, but strategically it shrinks the implied scope. The author needs to argue why these are exactly the settings where the theory bites, rather than letting the result feel narrow and local.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Open grazing and sedentary farming are increasingly incompatible in Nigeria; conflict is severe, salient, and politically central. States respond with anti-open-grazing laws that effectively try to legislate a shift in land-use regime.

### Tension
The laws could plausibly do opposite things. They might deter encroachment and reduce retaliatory violence, or they might criminalize pastoral livelihoods, provoke resistance, and displace conflict. The policy is high-profile, but its actual effect is unknown.

### Resolution
The paper finds that violence and deaths fall substantially in pastoral areas after adoption, with little detected increase in non-pastoral areas or adjacent untreated areas.

### Implications
Formal legal rules may matter even in weak-state settings when they target a specific economic margin that generates conflict. More broadly, changes in land-use/property-rights regimes can alter patterns of communal violence.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
It has the ingredients, but the execution is uneven. There is a story here, but the paper often reads like:
- institutional background,
- design exposition,
- result table,
- many validation exercises,
- mechanism section after the fact.

The reader can reconstruct the story, but the paper does not forcefully carry them through it.

### If it is a collection of results looking for a story, what story should it tell?
The story should be:

1. **A high-stakes conflict is rooted in a specific, regulable economic practice**: open grazing across contested farmland.
2. **The state intervenes by changing the legal regime governing that practice**.
3. **Whether law matters is ambiguous in weak-capacity settings**.
4. **The paper shows that the legal change reduced violence where exposure was greatest and did not obviously just move it elsewhere**.
5. **Therefore, formal legal reallocation of resource-use rights can reduce communal conflict, even without perfect enforcement.**

That is a much cleaner arc than “here is a DDD on Nigerian conflict.”

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Almost certainly:

- “Does it actually reduce conflict, or just move it somewhere else?”
- Then: “What is the mechanism—enforcement, signaling, or reduced contact?”
- Then: “Is this about Nigeria specifically, or about property-rights reforms more generally?”

That tells you what the paper needs to foreground.

### If the findings are modest or null, is the null interesting?
Not applicable here; the findings are not null. But a strategic issue is that the **headline effect is very large**, which raises natural skepticism. Again, referees can deal with whether it is credible. From an editorial-positioning standpoint, the danger is not “modest result”; it is “large result but narrow estimand.” The paper therefore needs to emphasize why the local margin it identifies is economically central.

6. **Shorten the conclusion and make it more synthetic.**  
   The conclusion currently summarizes a lot. It should instead do two things:
   - restate the broader lesson for economics,
   - be disciplined about what the paper can and cannot claim.
