## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether banning open grazing reduces farmer-herder violence in Nigeria. Using staggered state adoption of anti-open grazing laws and within-state differences between pastoral and non-pastoral areas, it argues that these laws sharply reduce communal violence and fatalities in the places most exposed to farmer-herder conflict.

Why should a busy economist care? Because this is, at least in aspiration, a paper about whether formal law can reduce resource conflict in a weak-state setting—an important world question that sits at the intersection of conflict, institutions, and property rights.

### Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Not really. The opening is vivid and topical, but the introduction initially reads like a Nigeria conflict paper rather than a broad economics paper. The first paragraphs emphasize the crisis and the policy chronology, but they do not immediately crystallize the bigger economic question: when conflict arises from competing land-use regimes, can a legal reallocation of use rights reduce violence, even where enforcement is imperfect?

The paper should lead with that broader question much earlier and much more cleanly. It should also avoid opening with Benue 2018 in a way that risks confusing the reader, since the anecdote is a post-law massacre and therefore immediately creates apparent tension with the main result before the reader understands the design.

### The pitch the paper should have

Farmer-herder conflict is one of the world’s most important forms of localized violence, driven by competition between mobile pastoralism and sedentary agriculture over scarce land. This paper asks whether formal law can reduce that violence: when Nigerian states ban open grazing and require livestock to be kept in ranches or designated reserves, do clashes fall in the areas where herders and farmers most directly compete?

Using staggered adoption of anti-open grazing laws across Nigerian states and within-state differences between pastoral and non-pastoral localities, the paper finds that these laws substantially reduce non-state violence and fatalities in pastoral zones, with little evidence of displacement to nearby areas. The broader implication is that even in a weak-capacity setting, legal changes to property-use regimes may alter behavior enough to reduce communal conflict.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### Contribution in one sentence

The paper’s contribution is to argue that anti-open grazing laws in Nigeria substantially reduce farmer-herder violence in pastoral areas, suggesting that formal legal restrictions on land use can mitigate communal conflict.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3-4 papers?

Only partially. The paper differentiates itself from qualitative Nigeria studies by saying it is the first causal estimate. That is fine as a narrow claim, but it is not enough for AER-level positioning. “First causal estimate of policy X in country Y” is rarely a top-journal contribution by itself.

The paper needs sharper differentiation from:
1. conflict papers on resource competition and climate shocks,
2. papers on state capacity and violence,
3. papers on property-rights regimes and enclosure,
4. spatial-policy evaluation papers using within-area heterogeneity.

Right now the paper’s novelty reads as “first DiD/DDD paper on anti-grazing laws in Nigeria.” That is too method-plus-setting driven.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or filling a gap in a LITERATURE?

It straddles both, but too often lapses into literature-gap framing: “no study has applied modern causal inference methods...” and “this paper provides the first causal estimate...” Those are weak formulations for AER. The stronger framing is the world question: can formal law reduce resource conflict when violence is generated by competing production systems and enforcement is imperfect?

### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what’s new?

At present, maybe, but not confidently. They would likely say: “It’s a DDD paper on anti-grazing laws in Nigeria showing violence falls in pastoral areas.” That is coherent, but it still sounds like another applied reduced-form conflict paper rather than a paper with a portable insight.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Most importantly: make the paper less about “anti-grazing laws in Nigeria” and more about “legal redefinition of property-use rights under weak enforcement.”

Specific ways to enlarge it:
- **Mechanism framing:** Treat the intervention as a change in de facto access rights to land, not just a security policy. That links the paper to property rights, regulation, and conflict.
- **Outcome scope:** Add outcomes that speak to reallocation, not just violence—agricultural production, crop prices, cattle market activity, land use, migration routes, or local economic activity. If violence falls but all one learns is violence falls, the paper remains narrow.
- **Mechanism evidence:** Even reduced-form evidence on cattle movement, crop destruction, market flows, or pastoral presence would make the story much bigger.
- **Comparison framing:** Explicitly compare this legal intervention to other conflict-mitigation tools—security deployments, mediation, or economic shocks—to show why law is a distinctive lever.
- **External relevance:** Connect to broader settings where states regulate mobile users of common resources: transhumance corridors, forest access, fisheries, informal mining. The underlying question is much larger than Nigeria.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Farmer-herder violence is a major form of communal conflict driven by competition between mobile livestock and sedentary farming. States sometimes respond by legally banning open grazing and trying to force a shift toward enclosed ranching.

### Tension

It is unclear whether such laws reduce violence or worsen it. They might deter incursions and reduce crop destruction; alternatively, they may criminalize livelihoods, provoke backlash, or simply displace conflict elsewhere. The core uncertainty is whether formal law has bite in a weak-state environment.

### Resolution

The paper finds that after adoption of anti-open grazing laws, violence falls in pastoral areas relative to non-pastoral areas, with null results on placebo outcomes and little evidence of displacement on the margins examined.

### Implications

The implication is that legal regulation of land-use access may reduce communal conflict even where enforcement is imperfect. More broadly, formal law may matter in weak states when it targets a specific, observable behavior closely tied to violent disputes.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the ingredients, but the narrative is too diffuse. The paper currently feels like:
1. Nigeria background,
2. empirical design,
3. many checks,
4. mechanism section,
5. broader discussion.

The reader does not always feel carried by a single central question. The paper especially suffers from a mismatch between the abstract/introduction’s certainty and the paper’s later admission that the estimate is local, mechanism evidence is indirect, and state-level aggregate effects are noisy or positive.

In other words: this is not a collection of random results, but it is still more “results around a design” than “a compelling narrative with one main economic insight.”

### What story should it be telling?

The story should be:

- Conflicts over scarce land often arise because two production systems overlap under weakly defined or weakly enforced access rights.
- Nigeria’s anti-open grazing laws are an attempt to redraw those rights by law.
- The paper studies whether such legal enclosure reduces violence where the overlap is most acute.
- It finds that it does, and that the reduction does not obviously come from simple displacement.
- Therefore, law can matter even in weak states when it changes the terms of interaction around a narrowly defined, conflict-generating activity.

That story is AER-relevant. The current version gestures at it, but does not own it.

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

### What would make it read better?

A lot, mostly by subtraction and reordering.

#### 1. Shorten the institutional background substantially
The background is informative but overlong. This is not a political science monograph. The history of reserves, federal tensions, and law-by-law detail can be compressed. Keep only what is needed to understand:
- why laws were adopted,
- how they plausibly affect violence,
- why adoption timing varies,
- why within-state heterogeneity matters.

#### 2. Move much of the empirical throat-clearing out of the introduction
The introduction currently contains too many design details, too many robustness previews, and too much inferential scaffolding. For AER, the introduction should answer:
- what is the question,
- why it matters,
- what you do,
- what you find,
- why it changes what we think.

The long list of six supporting checks in the introduction weakens momentum.

#### 3. Front-load the conceptual contribution
The property-rights/enclosure framing should appear on page 1, not page 5 and then again in discussion. Right now the broad idea is buried beneath institutional detail.

#### 4. Integrate the “mechanism” section earlier conceptually
The deterrence/displacement/escalation trichotomy is actually useful and should appear in the introduction as the conceptual framework. Then the later section can simply evaluate those channels.

#### 5. Be careful not to bury the most interesting qualification
One of the most important facts in the paper is that the DDD estimate is identified off a limited subset of mixed-composition states. That matters a lot for what the result means. It is good that the paper discloses it, but it is currently buried in robustness/effective sample discussion. This belongs earlier and more plainly.

#### 6. Trim repetitive robustness narration
The paper repeatedly explains why the preferred design is better than state-level DiD/CS. This can be condensed. One clean paragraph beats recurring defensive explanation.

#### 7. Rethink the conclusion
The conclusion mostly summarizes. It should instead do two things:
- state clearly what claim the paper can make,
- state clearly what claim it cannot make.

A strong conclusion would say: this is evidence that legal changes to access rights can reduce localized communal violence; it is not evidence that anti-grazing laws are universally welfare-improving or nationally peace-inducing.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

Be honest: the gap is meaningful.

### Is it a framing problem?

Yes, significantly. The paper has a potentially AER-worthy question but currently frames itself too much as a well-executed policy evaluation in one country.

### Is it a scope problem?

Yes. For AER, violence alone may be too narrow unless the conceptual contribution is much sharper. The paper would be much stronger if it showed what changed in the underlying allocation of economic activity or access rights.

### Is it a novelty problem?

Partly. “Staggered adoption + DDD + conflict data” is not novel on its own. The question needs to feel broader and less incremental.

### Is it an ambition problem?

Yes. The paper is competent but somewhat safe. It takes the most available outcome, estimates an effect, runs the standard battery, and stops. AER papers usually either answer a bigger question, show a more surprising mechanism, or connect the result to a more general theoretical or empirical debate.

### The single most impactful piece of advice

**Reframe the paper around legal reallocation of land-use rights under weak state capacity—and then add at least one outcome or mechanism that shows how the law changed behavior, not just violence.**

That is the one change that could shift this from a credible field paper to a paper with top-journal aspiration. If the authors cannot expand the evidence, then the paper still needs to narrow and discipline its claims: it is a strong Nigeria conflict paper, not yet an AER paper.
