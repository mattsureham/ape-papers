## 1. Idea Fidelity

Yes, the paper broadly pursues the original idea, but in a substantially narrowed and weakened form. It uses the FAA wildlife-strike data, the 2004 Part 139 expansion, a post period beginning in 2007, and emphasizes the reporting-versus-severity distinction that was central to the manifest. Those are all faithful choices.

That said, several key elements of the original design are not actually implemented. First, the manifest envisioned a larger treatment set—roughly 280 newly certificated airports—while the paper ultimately uses a historical roster of 37 and an estimation sample of only 20 treated airports. That is not a minor data-cleaning detail; it fundamentally changes the design’s power and external validity. Second, the manifest proposed richer treatment-timing methods and alternative control groups, including already-certified airports. The paper instead uses a simple common-post DiD against never-certified airports only. Third, the manifest framed the project as a test of whether mandatory certification reduced wildlife-strike damage at newly regulated airports; the paper ends up making a much narrower claim about rare severe strikes among a thin matched sample. That narrower claim may still be publishable in principle, but the paper should be more candid that it no longer delivers the broader test promised by the original idea.

## 2. Summary

This paper studies whether the 2004–2007 expansion of FAA Part 139 certification to Class III airports changed wildlife-strike outcomes. Using airport-year panels from the FAA wildlife-strike database, it finds no detectable effect on total reported strikes or on the damage share of reported strikes, but reports a marginally significant decline in severe strikes at newly certificated airports.

The paper’s core contribution is the sensible insight that management-based regulation may affect the upper tail of harm rather than event counts. But in its current form, the evidence is too fragile to support even that narrower conclusion with much confidence.

## 3. Essential Points

1. **The identification strategy is much weaker than the paper acknowledges because treatment assignment and comparison groups are poorly aligned.**  
   Comparing newly certificated commuter airports to never-certificated GA airports is not obviously credible. These airports likely differ in traffic volume, fleet mix, operating procedures, scheduled-service intensity, and reporting culture even absent the rule. Airport and year fixed effects do not solve differential trends in those dimensions. The paper needs a much stronger case for the control group, ideally including already-certified or otherwise more comparable commercial-service airports, and it needs to show pre-trends rather than simply asserting that event studies are “not informative.”

2. **The treatment sample collapse—from the policy universe to 37 airports to 20 treated airports—is a first-order problem for both inference and interpretation.**  
   With only 20 treated airports, and an outcome as rare as substantial-or-destroyed strikes, the headline estimate is driven by very few events. A 72 percent decline sounds dramatic, but the raw means reveal how tiny the base rates are: a decline from 0.046 to 0.006 severe strikes per airport-year implies only a handful of events in total. This makes the point estimate highly unstable and the external validity extremely limited. The paper needs to quantify the actual counts of severe events pre and post, by treatment status, and be much more explicit that the estimate is based on a very small number of treated-event realizations.

3. **Inference and functional-form choices are not yet convincing for such sparse outcomes.**  
   Airport-clustered standard errors with only 20 treated clusters and very rare outcomes are not obviously reliable. The paper should report inference using methods better suited to few treated clusters and sparse counts—e.g., wild cluster bootstrap, randomization inference, or permutation tests at the airport level. It should also justify the Poisson FE estimates more carefully and explain why the severe-strike sample loses so many observations. As written, the main result is “significant at 10 percent” under one specification and largely disappears once trends are added in linear models. That is not enough.

## 4. Suggestions

The paper has a real idea in it, and I would encourage the authors to keep pushing on it. But the current draft is caught between a broad policy story and a very narrow empirical signal. The best path forward is to tighten the design, sharpen the estimand, and make the magnitude interpretation much more concrete.

First, **rebuild the treatment definition and sample construction**. The biggest unresolved issue is why a policy that allegedly affected around 280 airports ends up being evaluated on 20. Perhaps the manifest overstated the likely count; perhaps the historical appendix truly lists only 37; perhaps code matching is dropping many valid airports. In any case, the paper must resolve this cleanly. I would want a table that starts with the regulatory universe, then shows each restriction: historical roster size, matched identifiers, airports present in strike data, airports with pre-period observations, airports surviving estimation. If many treated airports have zero reported strikes before or after, that is itself informative and should be discussed rather than silently discarded. Right now the sample attrition is too opaque.

Second, **use better control groups**. Never-certificated airports are a convenient control pool, but they are a weak counterfactual for airports serving scheduled 10–30 seat aircraft. The most useful improvement would be to compare treated Class III airports with airports that were already certified but similar in traffic scale or service type, or at least to implement a matched control design based on pre-policy strike rates, airport size, runway characteristics, scheduled service, and geography. Even if no perfect comparison exists, you should show results across several control groups. If the severe-strike finding only survives against one particular control pool, that is important to know.

Third, **bring exposure into the analysis**. Counts of strikes are not very meaningful without some denominator. If treated airports changed traffic volume differently after 2007, strike counts per airport-year could move for reasons unrelated to wildlife management. At minimum, the paper should use operations, enplanements, or departures as an offset in count models if those data can be obtained from FAA sources. If exposure data are unavailable for the full sample, then use a reduced sample where they are available and present those results prominently. A result on “severe strikes per 100,000 operations” would be far more interpretable than raw airport-year counts.

Fourth, **show the event-time evidence even if noisy**. I understand the authors’ concern that dynamic plots are unstable with 20 treated airports, but that is not a reason to omit them. In fact, when identification is this delicate, readers need to see whether treated airports were already trending differently before 2004. A coarse event study with binned leads/lags, or even a graph of treated-control mean differences over time, would help. If pre-trends are noisy, say so; if they are concerning, that is essential information. AER: Insights readers will expect to see some visual evidence on identifying assumptions.

Fifth, **be more disciplined about magnitude claims**. The “72 percent decline” is technically correct as an exponentiated log-point estimate, but in context it risks overstating the substantive importance of the result. Given the tiny baseline rate, readers need both relative and absolute magnitudes. For example: how many substantial-or-destroyed strikes are there among the 20 treated airports before and after 2007? How many events does the estimated effect correspond to per year, or over the entire post period? If the estimate amounts to preventing one severe strike every several years across all treated airports, that could still be policy-relevant, but it should be stated plainly.

Sixth, **clean up the severe-outcome specification**. The observation count for severe strikes is much smaller than for total strikes, apparently because of separation or dropped units. That is potentially consequential. I would like to see: (i) whether airports with zero severe strikes throughout are dropped; (ii) whether the treatment effect changes when using a linear probability/count model on the full panel; (iii) whether a negative binomial or hurdle/zero-inflated framework changes the conclusion; and (iv) whether collapsing to pre/post means at the airport level produces a similar signal. Sparse-outcome inference is not a place to rely on one estimator.

Seventh, **upgrade the inference**. With 20 treated airports, conventional clustered standard errors are fragile. A paper built around a marginally significant estimate must do more. Report wild-cluster-bootstrap p-values and, ideally, randomization-inference p-values based on placebo treatment assignment among comparable airports. For a design like this, such exercises are especially valuable because they make clear whether the observed severe-strike estimate is unusual relative to chance.

Eighth, **do more to address reporting changes directly**. The paper is right that certification may affect reporting. But the current evidence does not separate reporting from real incidence very sharply. A null effect on damage share is useful, but not decisive. Can you examine outcomes less susceptible to reporting inflation, such as engine-ingestion events, flight disruptions, or repair-cost thresholds? Can you look at species-specific strikes more likely to cause severe damage? Can you test whether certification increased reporting of no-damage strikes disproportionately? Those patterns would help support the mechanism.

Ninth, **tighten the writing around what the paper does and does not show**. The current draft is at its best when it is cautious, but it sometimes oscillates between caution and broad interpretation. I would rewrite the introduction and conclusion to make the contribution very explicit: “In a small matched sample of historically newly certificated commuter airports, I find suggestive evidence of fewer very severe wildlife strikes, but no detectable effect on total strikes or average damage rates.” That statement is honest and still interesting. Trying to sell a broader success story will backfire.

Finally, **drop material that distracts from the credibility of the design**. The “V1 paper” language, the autonomous-generation framing, and some of the standardized-effect-size discussion are not helping the paper. A short AER: Insights-style paper should use that space instead for sample construction, identifying assumptions, treatment/control comparability, and actual event counts. This project will stand or fall on empirical credibility, not on novelty of the data source alone.

In short: the topic is promising, the reporting-versus-severity distinction is sensible, and the severe-tail focus could yield an interesting paper. But the current evidence is too thin to support the headline result. If the authors can repair the sample construction, improve the counterfactual, incorporate exposure, and strengthen inference, the paper could become a useful short contribution. As written, it is not there yet.
