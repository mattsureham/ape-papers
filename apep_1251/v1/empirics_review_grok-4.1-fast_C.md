### 1. Idea Fidelity
The paper partially pursues the original idea manifest but deviates substantially on key elements. It retains the core research question (Part 139 expansion's effect on wildlife strikes using FAA NWSD data), policy variation (Class III airports), outcome decomposition (reporting/extensive vs. severity/intensive margins), and DiD framework. However, it misses several critical components: (i) treated units shrink from ~280 Class III airports to just 20 (using a narrow 37-airport regulatory appendix list rather than the full newly certificated roster); (ii) controls are only never-certificated GA airports (omitting already-certified Class I airports as specified); (iii) no staggered DiD or Callaway-Sant'Anna estimators (uses common-timing TWFE post-2007); and (iv) no explicit event study despite the manifest's emphasis. These changes narrow the scope and power dramatically, transforming a "full universe of ~660+ airports" into a low-power exercise.

### 2. Summary
This paper examines the 2004-2007 FAA Part 139 expansion to Class III airports using airport-year DiD on 1990-2024 FAA wildlife strike data, decomposing effects into total strikes, damaging strikes, severe strikes, and damage shares to distinguish reporting from severity channels. It finds null effects on total strikes and damage shares but a suggestive 72% decline in severe strikes (imprecise, marginally significant Poisson estimate of -1.275 log points, s.e. 0.756) among 20 matched treated airports relative to never-certificated controls. The result is framed cautiously as bounded evidence that management-based regulation may reduce tail risks more than incidence, without overclaiming causality.

### 3. Essential Points
1. **Critically small treated sample undermines credibility and power.** The manifest promised ~280 treated Class III airports from the full post-2007 rollout, but the paper uses a 37-airport regulatory appendix list, yielding only 20 matched units after restrictions. This attrition (93% loss) produces severe power issues: severe strikes have just ~200 treated airport-year observations pre/post, with means near zero (0.046 to 0.006). The 72% decline is plausibly large given rarity of events and management focus on tails, but imprecision (s.e. 0.756 on coefficient near -1.3) means CIs include implausibly large increases (+300% at upper bound). AER: Insights demands robust identification; reject unless treated N expands to >100 via full Class III roster.

2. **Control group comparability is unconvincing without Class I or parallel trends evidence.** Controls are only never-certificated GA airports, ignoring manifest's Class I (pre-treated large hubs) for a cleaner counterfactual of certification effects. GA airports differ systematically (lower traffic, different ecology/operations), biasing against parallel trends. No event study or pre-trend tests shown (appendix dismisses dynamics due to "small sample"); raw means show both groups trending down in severity, questioning DiD validity. Must add event study plot and formal pre-trend F-tests.

3. **Standard errors appropriate but understate threats in Poisson TWFE.** Airport-clustered SEs are correctly implemented for DiD, and Poisson handles zeros/sparsity well (no overdispersion issues apparent). However, with common timing post-2007, TWFE is valid (avoids Goodman-Bacon bias), but small treated N inflates variance. Recent literature (e.g., Sun/Abraham) flags heterogeneous trends; linear trends in robustness attenuate severe effect to -0.020 (s.e. 0.032, insignificant). No Callaway-Sant'Anna or randomization inference; essential for V2.

### 4. Suggestions
**Expand and validate treatment definition (priority for revision).** The 20-airport sample is the paper's Achilles' heel—replicate the manifest's ~280 Class III airports using FAA's monthly Part 139 certification lists (public at faa.gov/airports_airtraffic/airports/certification) cross-referenced with historical snapshots or FOIA if needed. Confirm compliance dates via FAA records to enable true staggered DiD (Callaway-Sant'Anna in R `did` package or Stata `reghdfe`). This boosts power: even 100+ treated units would halve SEs on severe strikes, making magnitudes testable (e.g., is 72% plausible vs. FAA benchmarks of 5-10% strike damage rates?). Appendix-match all 37 appendix airports explicitly, reporting attrition table by reason (closure, no strikes, code mismatch).

**Strengthen identification with dynamics and falsification.** Add a full event-study figure (e.g., 5-10 pre/post leads/lags) using Poisson PML or `eventstudyinteract` (Sun/Shumway), binning short windows if needed. Test parallel trends (joint F-test p>0.10 pre-coefficients=0). Falsify on placebo outcomes like "phase of flight" (e.g., no effect on ground vs. airborne strikes if management-focused). Compare to Class I controls in a triple-difference (treated x post x Class I dummy) to isolate certification from commuter-airport trends. Include airport covariates (traffic volume from FAA OPSNET, land use from NLCD) interacted with trends to proxy ecology/traffic confounds.

**Refine outcomes and estimators for economic meaning.** Magnitudes are plausible—severe strikes are <5% of total (per FAA reports), so management (e.g., training/plans under 139.337) plausibly targets tails without affecting incidence—but clarify costs. Compute welfare implications: multiply severe strikes by FAA repair costs (~$100k-$1M per event from database) for annualized savings (~$50k-200k per treated airport if 72% effect holds). Use NBREG if overdispersion (test alpha>0); for shares, beta regression or fracreg to bound [0,1]. Decompose further: species-fixed effects (birds vs. mammals), phase-of-flight (takeoff/landing most severe). Standardized effects (Table F1) are helpful; extend to cost-weighted severity.

**Robustness expansion without specification search.** Double robustness table: (i) entropy balancing on pre-means (traffic, strikes); (ii) synthetic controls (SCM with `synth`); (iii) wild cluster bootstrap (for small N); (iv) exclude post-2020 (COVID traffic drop). Subsample by region/ecology (e.g., migratory flyways). Address reporting: instrument total strikes with certification (if exogenous) for IV on severity. Heterogeneity: interact treated_post with pre-strike intensity or traffic growth.

**Writing and presentation tweaks.** Lean into novelty (first econ use of NWSD)—add lit review table contrasting Pfeiffer et al. (voluntary, military). Tone down "preferred Poisson" (all specs similar); star severity cautiously (* p<0.10). Figures: raw means plot by group/period (echo Table 3); strike trends 1990-2024.
