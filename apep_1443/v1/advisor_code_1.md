**Summary**

The paper estimates bunching at Taiwan’s housing capital gains tax notches using 344,274 repeat-sale pairs derived from the mandatory Actual Price Registration system. Under the tightened Tax 2.0 regime, the author finds no statistically or economically meaningful excess mass at the 2-year threshold despite a 10 percentage point rate drop, while the earlier Tax 1.0 regime shows modest bunching at its 2-year notch. A placebo test on grandfathered properties reveals large mechanical spikes in the holding-period distribution, raising concerns about address-level matching noise.

**Essential Points**

1. **Measurement error from address-level matching undermines density interpretation.** The huge “bunching” uncovered in the placebo sample points to systematic mismatching of distinct units at the same address, which also likely affects Tax 1.0 and Tax 2.0 samples despite the author’s claim that it cannot mechanically produce a null. Without a cleaner way to identify actual repeat owners or an adjustment for the noise-generating pattern, the counterfactual density may be misspecified and the bunching estimator biased (both upward and downward). The paper needs to either (a) restrict the sample to properties or transactions where the repeat-sale assumption is credible (e.g., landed houses, new developments with unique addresses, or first few years after a building sale where misidentification is less likely) or (b) model the measurement error explicitly and show that it cannot explain the Tax 2.0 null. At minimum, the existing placebo analysis should be expanded to demonstrate that the structural pattern is distinct in timing or magnitude so the null interpretation remains credible.

2. **Power and timing limitations under Tax 2.0 require clearer exposition.** The Tax 2.0 sample is comparatively young—its median holding period is 387 days—so many properties have not yet reached the 2-year threshold. The estimated counterfactual relies on extrapolating from fewer observations near 730 days, and the exclusion window/bandwidth choices (e.g., ±365 days around the notch) may overlap regions with limited data. The paper should provide (a) event-time plots showing how many observations exist in each bin around 730 days and how that compares to earlier regimes, (b) formal power calculations (akin to the appendix’s standardized effect sizes) that assess whether the null is informative about economically meaningful bunching (the conclusion states the confidence interval rules out excess mass above 0.5, but this needs to be derived transparently), and (c) a discussion of right-censoring: how many repeat-sale pairs purchased post-July 2021 could possibly reach 730 days within the sample period? Showing that sufficient mass is available before the notch is essential to validate the null.

3. **Counterfactual smoothness assumption needs more scrutiny in the presence of macro trends.** The bunching estimator assumes that the underlying holding-period density would be smooth absent the tax notch. However, housing markets experienced sizable price and turnover trends between 2021 and 2024, including increased demand in certain cities and possibly calendar effects (e.g., end-of-year sales). These secular patterns could introduce gradients or inflection points that confound the polynomial fit, especially over a ±365-day window. The paper should either control for time-varying fundamentals (e.g., include calendar-time fixed effects via local linear regression or allow the polynomial to flexibly interact with time) or demonstrate that such trends do not change the null. Similarly, the placebo result suggests that non-tax factors can generate spikes at round numbers; more evidence is needed to show these are not driven by some unobserved policy or macro factor that coincides with Tax 2.0.

**Suggestions**

1. **Improve repeat-sale matching.** The manuscript acknowledges the limitation of matching by district and street address but stops short of implementing corrections. Consider using auxiliary information—such as floor number, building name (if available), or transaction type—to refine matching. If the registry includes cadastral plot identifiers or building serial numbers, those would allow a subset of truly matched pairs. Alternatively, you could focus on transactions of detached houses or low-rise buildings where the risk of conflating distinct units is minimal. Even if this reduces sample size, demonstrating that the results hold in a cleaner subsample would strengthen causal claims and help convince readers that the Tax 2.0 null is not a data artifact.

2. **Model the structural holding-period peaks.** The placebo sample’s spike at 730 days raises a broader question: why do holding periods cluster at round years even without tax incentives? One hypothesis is that developers sell a batch of units after a standard construction period, creating a peak in the data when the template address is matched across transactions. You could attempt to model this by (a) estimating the density separately for properties with multiple sales within 6 months of each other (likely developer-induced), (b) removing addresses with clear multi-unit activity (e.g., those appearing more than five times in the sample), or (c) using the serial autocorrelation of holding periods at the address level to flag suspicious matches. Showing how the density changes when these “problematic” addresses are excluded would clarify whether the Tax 2.0 density is indeed smooth.

3. **Augment identification via variation in tax incentives.** The analysis currently compares across regimes and relies on a single notch under Tax 2.0. Consider exploiting heterogeneity in the effective tax incentive by property age, ownership type, or seller characteristics. For example, corporate sellers and individual sellers may face different liquidity constraints and therefore different responsiveness. If corporate sellers (who are subject to the same schedule) show a tendency to wait while individuals do not, that would support the behavioral interpretation even in the presence of noise. Similarly, you could use the 5-year notch (once enough time elapses) or the 10-year notch as placebo to see whether the pattern changes with horizon and incentive size. Even if those thresholds remain out of reach for now, documenting their expected sample size and schedule would help readers gauge future work.

4. **Discuss alternative interpretations of the null.** The conclusion touches on transaction frictions and extensive-margin effects, but the paper would benefit from more systematic treatment. For example, you could estimate reduced-form relationships between holding period and observable transaction costs (e.g., property price volatility, district-level inventory) to test whether certain environments are more responsive. Another idea is to explore whether the post-2021 cohort has systematically different incentive structures (e.g., more owner-occupiers vs. speculators) than the pre-2021 cohort; if so, the null might reflect a change in the marginal seller rather than the inefficacy of the tax. Providing plots or regressions that compare the characteristics of repeat sellers before and after July 2021 would anchor the interpretation.

5. **Validate the counterfactual with additional diagnostics.** The polynomial fit could be supplemented with local linear regression on either side of the notch, allowing for separate slopes that can detect pre-existing trends. Alternatively, you could implement the improvable “Donut” method (exclude a wider region around the notch and assess how the fitted polynomial behaves) and report the goodness-of-fit measures. Also, consider plotting the raw density along with the fitted counterfactual to visually reassure readers that the polynomial does not overfit or underfit. These diagnostics are standard in the bunching literature and would make the estimation strategy more transparent.

6. **Clarify the policy implications.** The paper concludes that holding-period taxes may deter entry rather than distorting timing, but this is speculative without supporting evidence. If possible, link the null results to broader market outcomes: did turnover fall after Tax 2.0 (consistent with extensive-margin deterrence), or did price appreciation accelerate (suggesting holding periods lengthened)? A simple difference-in-differences analysis of transaction counts or median holding periods for cohorts affected by the reform versus exempt cohorts could provide auxiliary support. Even if these exercise is preliminary, mentioning the direction of such evidence would strengthen the claims about policy relevance.

By addressing these points, the paper would offer a more solid empirical foundation for the intriguing null result and contribute more convincingly to the literatures on tax-induced bunching and housing lock-in effects.
