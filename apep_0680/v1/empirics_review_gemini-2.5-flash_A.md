---

This is a strong submission that addresses a timely and policy-relevant question with a robust empirical strategy. The paper successfully leverages granular administrative data to provide the first causal estimates of property value capitalization for Low-Emission Zones (ZFEs) in France, finding a significant negative impact. The identification strategy, particularly the difference-in-discontinuities design, is well-executed and appropriate for the research question. The findings, especially the negative capitalization and the insights into the equity debate, are novel and important.

---

**1. Idea Fidelity**

The paper largely adheres to the original idea manifest, maintaining high fidelity across most dimensions. The core research question ("Do Vehicle Bans Capitalize into Housing?"), the identification strategy (spatial RDD at the Lyon ZFE boundary), and the primary data source (DVF property transactions) are all faithfully implemented as envisioned. The paper successfully exploits the unique features of the Lyon ZFE, such as its boundary following the Boulevard Laurent Bonnevay, and the progressive tightening of restrictions for a phase decomposition. The stated novelty of being the first spatial RDD on French ZFE boundaries using such detailed microdata is also fulfilled.

The most significant deviation from the manifest is the omission of the "Secondary: Monthly NO2 concentrations from 13 EEA air quality monitoring stations in the Lyon area" outcome. The manifest explicitly listed air quality as a secondary outcome. Given the theoretical ambiguity discussed in the paper – that cleaner air might raise WTP while reduced accessibility lowers it – directly measuring the air quality impact would have been a crucial component of understanding *why* accessibility costs appear to dominate. While the paper cogently argues why this might be the case, empirical evidence on the air quality channel, even if modest, would substantially strengthen the analysis and help disentangle the mechanisms. Its absence is a notable missed opportunity from the original design.

**2. Summary**

This paper provides the first causal evidence on property value capitalization of Low-Emission Zones (ZFEs) in France. Exploiting the sharp geographic boundary of Lyon's ZFE as a spatial regression discontinuity, the author uses 121,207 parcel-level property transactions to show that the ZFE reduced property values inside the zone by approximately 10.5 percent relative to properties just outside. A difference-in-discontinuities design isolates the causal effect, demonstrating that accessibility costs predominantly impact housing capitalization, particularly for apartments, contradicting the standard environmental benefits narrative and providing critical insights for the ongoing debate on ZFE abolition.

---

**3. Essential Points**

1.  **Missing Air Quality Analysis & Mechanisms:** The paper identifies the core theoretical ambiguity as a trade-off between air quality benefits and accessibility costs. However, it only empirically investigates the property value outcome resulting from these two channels, completely omitting any analysis of air quality. The manifest explicitly listed "Monthly NO2 concentrations from 13 EEA air quality monitoring stations" as a secondary outcome. Without even a rudimentary analysis of whether air quality *actually changed* at the boundary following the ZFE implementation, the paper’s ability to conclusively argue that "accessibility costs dominate air quality benefits" is significantly weakened. This is not about estimating a separate air quality effect on prices, but showing the *first-stage* effect on air quality itself. Even a null finding would be informative. The authors must either incorporate an air quality analysis (as initially planned) or explicitly acknowledge this limitation and rephrase conclusions regarding the relative dominance of mechanisms.

2.  **Robustness of DiD vs. RDD & Covariate Adjustment:** The paper reports three main estimates in Table 1: a standard RDD, an RDD with covariates, and a DiD. The preferred estimate is the DiD (-0.1048), but the RDD with covariates is significantly larger in magnitude (-0.1632). It is unclear why the DiD coefficient is *smaller* than the RDD with covariates, when it's meant to additionally absorb pre-existing trends. Furthermore, the footnote states that for Column (2) and (3), the kernel is "---". This is confusing. Standard RDD methods (like `rdrobust`) automatically incorporate local polynomial regression with a kernel. If the DiD is estimated via OLS on a fixed bandwidth, this needs to be clarified, and the choice of bandwidth for the DiD should be justified (presumably 1km, which is stated as primary in the manifest but not optimal). The crucial question is: which standard errors are robust? The difference between a simple RDD (bandwidth-optimized, -0.0975) and and an RDD *with covariates* (fixed 1km bandwidth, -0.1632) is substantial. The authors need to provide a clearer methodological description for each column in Table 1, reconcile the differences, and ensure that standard errors fully account for any local regression and clustering aspects.

3.  **Nature of the Pre-Existing Discontinuity (Placebo Test, Table 2 & 4):** The paper uses a DiD to account for a pre-existing discontinuity at the Boulevard Laurent Bonnevay. Table 2 reports an RDD estimate for a "Placebo (Pre-ZFE)" period, showing -0.0697. It is unclear if this is actually a test *of* the pre-existing discontinuity or a placebo *effect* in the pre-period. Table 4, Panel B, states "Pre-ZFE period: p-value = 0.003, Bunching detected", which contradicts the idea of a continuous outcome variable (or at least, continuous *density*) prior to treatment. The paper needs to elaborate on the nature of this "bunching" in the pre-period. Is it merely a difference in densities of properties inside vs. outside the ring road, or does it imply behavioral sorting *before* the ZFE, which would challenge the exogeneity of unit assignment in the RDD framework? The spatial RDD requires randomness in assignment *conditional on the running variable*. If there's bunching, properties very close to the boundary (inside vs. outside) might not be comparable even conditional on distance if there are distinct "sides" to the original road, which would complicate the interpretation of the DiD. A discussion clarifying the McCrary results would strengthen the validity claims.

**4. Suggestions**

1.  **Air Quality Data (Crucial but non-essential for a reject):** While flagged as "Essential," I am moving this to "Suggestions" given the AER: Insights format often requires shorter papers. However, authors should strive to include even a brief analysis of the air quality data explicitly mentioned in the manifest. If it's truly impossible to retrieve or analyze within the scope of this paper, they must discuss *why* it's not and adjust wording on causality/mechanisms. For instance, directly measuring NO2 at the boundary with a spatial RDD *on air quality* would be ideal. Failing that, a discussion point on the potential *magnitude* of air quality effects from ZFE in Lyon (e.g., from other studies or official reports) and how this might compare to the observed accessibility costs would be valuable. The current absence makes the causal chain linking ZFE->property values->mechanisms incomplete.

2.  **Visual Evidence: RDD Plots:** The paper would greatly benefit from visual representations of the RDD. A standard plot showing the mean log price per m$^2$ against distance for both pre- and post-ZFE periods (separated) would clearly illustrate the discontinuities. This is particularly important for the DiD. Two separate plots—one for the pre-period and one for the post-period—with regression lines fitted on either side of the boundary would allow readers to visually inspect both the pre-existing discontinuity and the additional gap post-ZFE. This is standard practice in RDD papers and greatly enhances credibility.

3.  **Heterogeneity: Deeper Dive into Property Types:** The finding that the penalty is concentrated in apartments while houses show an insignificant positive effect is fascinating. The explanation (apartments being more sensitive to accessibility) is plausible but could be explored further.
    *   **Covariate Balance by Property Type:** Are the apartments inside and outside the ZFE more balanced on observables than houses?
    *   **Specific Accessibility Challenges:** Can you hypothesize more concrete mechanisms specific to apartments (e.g., lack of private parking, reliance on shared access roads, higher density of delivery/visitor traffic) versus houses (e.g., private driveways, less reliance on street parking for visitors)?
    *   **Robustness Check:** Could different bandwidths or polynomial orders change the significance or magnitude of the house effect?

4.  **Policy Reversal and Anticipation Effects:** The paper mentions the June 2025 ZFE suppression vote, adding to the policy relevance. This creates an interesting dynamic for future research, but also for *interpretation* of current results. The finding that the full penalty materialized in Phase 1 with no additional effect from Phase 2 (Crit'Air 3 restriction) is attributed to either "anticipation of future tightening" or a "salience shock." If anticipation is key, how might the market react to news of abolition or further modifications? This could be a rich area for future work, but even for current work, it raises a question: if market actors anticipate tightening, do they also anticipate *reversal*? A brief discussion on how future policy uncertainty (including potential abolition) might influence current (post-ZFE) capitalization would be insightful.

5.  **Covariate Adjustment and Interpretation:** Column (2) in Table 1 shows a substantially larger effect (-0.1632) when covariates are added within a 1km bandwidth, compared to the `rdrobust` default (-0.0975) or the DiD (-0.1048). The text states "the covariate adjustment corrects for inside properties being smaller and more apartment-dense." This implies that without covariates, the effect is *underestimated* in absolute terms. A detailed explanation for this difference would be helpful. For instance, if inside properties are smaller and more apartment-dense (which Table 4 confirms), and these characteristics are typically associated with lower prices, then if they are *more prevalent inside*, the raw RDD might be *downward biased* (or upwards biased depending on how prices are interpreted). Conversely, if the density difference means *apartments* are disproportionately moving into *treated areas*, this complicates interpretation. Clarifying the mechanics of why adding controls increases the magnitude of the negative effect is important for building confidence in the preferred DiD estimate.

6.  **"Bunching Detected" in Pre-ZFE McCrary Test:** As mentioned in Essential Points, clarify the interpretation of the pre-ZFE McCrary result ($p=0.003$). If it represents a real discontinuity in density of properties at the boundary *before* ZFE, it suggests properties are not randomly assigned at the boundary. While the DiD helps, understanding the nature of this pre-existing sorting/bunching is critical. Is it residential vs. commercial? Or properties built to different standards/ages? More details from the Data Appendix for this specific finding would be helpful.

7.  **Standardized Effect Sizes:** Section A.4 on Standardized Effect Sizes is a useful inclusion for interpretability. However, it seems to report the standard RDD estimate (from Column 1, Table 1), not the preferred DiD estimate. It would be more consistent to report the standardized effect size for the DiD, as that is the main causal estimate the paper emphasizes. Additionally, "Classification" (e.g., Large negative) feels a bit subjective; while common in some fields, in economics, it's often more persuasive to relate the magnitude to a meaningful economic benchmark (e.g., "equivalent to X years of property tax," or "X% of average annual income").

8.  **Mapping and Visualization:** A simple map showing the ZFE boundary and the distribution of property transactions (perhaps with different colors for inside/outside) would greatly enhance the paper's clarity and appeal. While the data sources and boundary are well-described, a visual aid would quickly orient the reader.

9.  **Clearer Terminology and Definitions:** While generally clear, ensure consistent use of `ZFE` versus `Crit'Air 5` or `Crit'Air 3` restrictions. Crit'Air refers to the classification, ZFE refers to the zone and the policy. Also, clearly define "effective N" in tables, especially for RDD, as it is a specific `rdrobust` output implying weighted observations.

These suggestions aim to sharpen the paper's arguments, enhance its methodological transparency, and broaden its impact within the literature.
