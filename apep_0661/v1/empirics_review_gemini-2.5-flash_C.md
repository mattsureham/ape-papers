### 2. Summary

This paper examines whether the exogenous placement of asylum seekers in UK communities, mandated by the no-choice National Asylum Support Service (NASS) dispersal policy, affects local crime rates. Using a panel of Community Safety Partnerships (CSPs) in England and Wales, the authors employ a shift-share instrumental variable (IV) approach, constructing an instrument from 2011 Census housing vacancy shares and national asylum inflows. The study finds that the IV is critically weak (first-stage F-statistic of 1.2), rendering causal inference via IV unreliable, and also notes concerning patterns in OLS estimates (sign reversals across subperiods, significant placebo effects from future dispersal), leading to the conclusion of a likely null causal effect of asylum dispersal on local crime.

***

### 3. Essential Points

1.  **Fundamental Identification Failure and Interpretation of "Null":** The paper explicitly states that the first-stage F-statistic is 1.2, far below conventional thresholds, and robustly concludes that the instrument is weak. This is a fatal flaw for any claimed IV estimates. The paper correctly highlights that IV estimates in this scenario are unreliable and subject to severe bias. However, it then proceeds to interpret its findings as "consistent with a null causal effect" and "provides no empirical support for the claim that dispersal increases crime." This is a significant overreach. A failed IV strategy, due to a weak instrument, means that the causal effect *cannot be identified* from this empirical design. It does *not* imply a null effect. Weak instruments prevent precise estimation, meaning one cannot reject *any* reasonable hypothesis, including a large positive or negative effect, within a statistically credible range. The conclusion of a "null causal effect" in the abstract, discussion, and conclusion section is an improper interpretation of a failed identification strategy and must be revised to reflect the inability to draw causal conclusions.

2.  **Lack of Deeper Exploration of First-Stage Failure:** The paper acknowledges the weak first stage and provides some qualitative reasons (private contractor decisions, hotel expansion, political resistance). However, given that the shift-share instrument (vacancy $\times$ national inflow) is the cornerstone of the identification strategy, a weak and even negatively signed first stage coefficient (Table 1, F-stat 1.2 for dispersal rate) demands rigorous, quantitative investigation. The paper should delve deeper into *why* the 2011 vacancy rate negatively predicts dispersal and why the assumed mechanism between vacancy and placement has broken down. For example, are there specific types of CSPs where the share component is stronger or weaker? Has the relationship changed systematically over time? How does the "dispersal accommodation" definition interact with the instrument when hotel accommodation expanded post-2019? This quantitative exploration would transform a mere acknowledgement of instrument weakness into a valuable methodological contribution, as hinted at in "The methodological contribution is to demonstrate the limits of shift-share identification."

3.  **Ambiguity and Inconsistency of OLS Interpretation:** The paper primarily relies on OLS estimates due to IV weakness, yet it immediately undermines their causal interpretation by noting sign reversals in subperiods and significant placebo effects. While acknowledging these limitations is crucial, the paper's narrative oscillates. The abstract implies the OLS association is "small and negative," but its "causal interpretation" is undermined by later diagnostics. This creates confusion about what, if anything, can be robustly claimed. If the OLS estimates are "consistent with compounding rather than causation" and show significant placebo effects, then they too cannot be used to argue for a "null causal effect." The paper needs to maintain a consistent message: if both IV and OLS fail to provide credible causal estimates, the *conclusion* should be about the **absence of identified effects**, not the **identification of a null effect**.

### 4. Suggestions

1.  **Refine the Narrative on Causal Inference and "Null" Findings:** This is paramount. The current interpretation of a weak instrument as evidence of a "null causal effect" is a misstatement that needs correction throughout the paper.
    *   **Language Adjustment:** Replace phrases like "The combined evidence... points toward a null causal effect" with "The combined evidence suggests that the current empirical strategy is unable to credibly identify a causal effect of asylum dispersal on local crime." Similarly, "consistent with a null effect" should become "consistent with the absence of a reliably estimated effect" or "we cannot reject a null effect due to lack of power."
    *   **Focus on Identification Impossibility:** Emphasize that the inability to identify an effect is the core finding, rather than a specific (null) effect. This makes the paper’s contribution about methodological limits more salient. The paper's strength lies in its diagnostic of why a seemingly plausible identification strategy failed, not in making a causal claim.

2.  **Deep Dive into the First Stage: Why Did it Fail?**
    *   **Quantitative Exploration of Shares:** Instead of just summarizing, provide scatter plots or regressions showing the relationship between (national inflow $\times$ 2011 vacancy) and observed dispersal rates, conditional on fixed effects. Are certain CSPs responsible for the negative coefficient? Is residual vacancy correlated with undesirable characteristics that private contractors avoid, thus *reducing* dispersal?
    *   **Temporal Heterogeneity in First Stage:** The paper notes the expansion of hotel accommodation post-2019. Does the first-stage relationship change significantly before and after this period? A two-period first-stage analysis would be very informative. If the share worked better pre-2019, that could be a significant insight.
    *   **Alternative Shares or Instruments:** Given the acknowledged failure of the 2011 vacancy share in identifying dispersal (the mechanism described in the manifest), the paper should explicitly state if alternative shares were considered or if this was the *only* plausible variable for the "housing surplus" component.
    *   **Connect to Bartik Literature:** The introduction mentions \citet{jaeger2020shift} and \citet{adao2019shift}. Make the connection more explicit by showing *how* the failure here exemplifies the concerns raised in that literature. What testable implication of those critiques does this paper's weak first stage confirm?

3.  **Scrutinize OLS Robustness and Placebo Tests More Critically:**
    *   **Magnitude and Plausibility of OLS:** For context, the mean crime rate is 20.767 per 1,000. A coefficient of -0.315 means that a 1-unit increase in dispersal rate (1 additional asylum seeker per 1,000 population per quarter) leads to a 0.315 decrease in crime. In the pre-COVID period, it was a *positive* 0.641. These are economically meaningful changes. The sign reversal across subperiods is a very strong indicator of unaddressed confounding. The paper reports these as findings but glosses over their profound implications for relying on *any* OLS estimate.
    *   **Placebo Test Nuance:** While the significant placebo for future dispersal is compelling evidence against OLS causality, the *negative* sign ("future dispersal indicates current falling crime") should be explored. Could this be driven by the observed selection of dispersal into areas where crime is already falling, or post-industrial decline? This "reverse placement selection" should be explicitly modeled or discussed as a key confounding channel.

4.  **Strengthen "Limitations" and "Future Work" Section:**
    *   **Alternative Identification Strategies:** The paper alludes to "staggered rollout of hotel and barracks placements" or "event-study designs around specific hotel openings" as future avenues. Expand on these potential strategies. How would they address the current instrument's weaknesses? What data would be required? This would solidify the paper's contribution as defining a challenging research frontier rather than just presenting a null.
    *   **Role of Contingency Accommodation:** The paper states it "focuses on dispersal accommodation." However, it later notes the expansion of contingency accommodation (hotels) post-2019, which "decoupled dispersal from the traditional housing stock." This is a crucial detail that likely impacts the first stage. This should be explicitly addressed. Did dispersal accommodation rates decline as hotel placements soared? How does the instrument's effectiveness vary if "dispersal rate" is broadly defined to include all supported asylum seekers (as in Column 5 of Table 1)?
    *   **Data Period Justification:** The paper uses 2016-2024 data, while the policy started in 2000. Is there a specific reason for this period? NASS began in 2000, so a longer panel might reveal different dynamics, though data availability is a known constraint for detailed asylum statistics.

5.  **Standardized Effect Sizes (Appendix):**
    *   The interpretation of SDEs should be carefully qualified given the paper's concerns about OLS and IV. Stating that the theft SDE is "small negative" or drug offences "moderate positive" might misleadingly suggest that these are credible estimates of actual impacts, when the core message is the opposite. Add a very strong caveat to the appendix explaining that these are mere associations based on an identified-as-biased OLS model.

6.  **Presentation and Aesthetics:**
    *   **Table Notes:** Ensure consistency. For example, Table 1's footnote mentions "Asylum rate (total) includes all accommodation types," but this isn't obvious from the variable names. It's also not used in the main OLS specification (Col 1-2).
    *   **Clarity on "Asylum rate (total)":** Column 5 of Table 1 uses "Asylum rate (total)." Is the share for this model the same 2011 Census vacancy? Does the national shift need to be total asylum seekers or just dispersal? This needs to be clarified, as the instrument description in section 4.1 refers to $g_t$ as "total national asylum seeker population in quarter $t$." If $g_t$ is already total national asylum seekers, then the instrument for "Asylum rate (total)" (col 5) should be stronger than for "Dispersal rate" (col 3-4), but their F-stats are 4.9 vs 1.2. Why the difference? This implies a different $g_t$ or $s_i$ was used or that "total national asylum seeker population" is distinct from "national quarterly asylum inflow." Please clarify this description.

By addressing these points, the paper can transition from simply reporting a failed IV to making a significant methodological contribution about the challenges of identifying causal effects in this policy area and offering clearer guidance for future research.
