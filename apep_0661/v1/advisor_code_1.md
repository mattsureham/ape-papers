**Summary**  
The paper exploits the UK asylum dispersal policy to estimate the causal effect of refugee placement on local crime across 291 Community Safety Partnerships between 2016 and 2024. Using a shift-share IV (2011 Census vacancy shares interacted with national asylum inflows), the authors find that the instrument is weak and potentially invalid, OLS estimates suggest a marginally significant negative association, and placebo tests argue against a stable causal effect. The key contribution is methodological: it highlights the perils of weak shift-share instruments in this setting and concludes there is no credible evidence that asylum dispersal raises crime.

**Essential Points**  
1. **Identification failure leaves policy question unanswered.** The shift-share IV is weak (first-stage \(F=1.2\)) and the negative first-stage coefficient contradicts the underlying mechanism. This undermines any causal claim and suggests that the instrument is not capturing the dispersal policy in practice. The paper rightly emphasizes this limitation, but it should go further in explaining whether any alternative sources of exogenous variation are feasible (see Suggestions 1–2). Without a credible instrument, the paper’s policy implications remain tentative and the reader is left with an OLS association likely confounded.

2. **Placebo evidence implies bad controls rather than causal effect.** Future dispersal predicting current crime strongly indicates omitted variable bias or reverse causality. The paper should dig deeper into the nature of these dynamics—what drives both crime trends and dispersal placement? Without controlling for those confounders or exploiting an alternative identification strategy, the null conclusion is not well-founded.

3. **Temporal heterogeneity needs more exploration.** The sharp sign reversal before and after COVID, although not surprising, is treated as evidence against causality without testing whether pandemic-driven dispersion mechanisms (such as use of contingency accommodation) altered the treatment variation. If the policy change itself shifted the correlation between dispersal counts and crime, that could explain both the subperiod results and the weak first stage. The authors must assess whether the underlying treatment variable changed structurally post-2019, which would also connect to instrument validity.

**Suggestions**  
1. **Investigate alternative quasi-experiments or stronger instruments.** Given the documented weakness of the vacancy-based shift-share, the paper’s primary identification strategy is unworkable. Examine whether the staggered opening of contingency accommodation (hotels, barracks) provides exogenous variation. For instance, if government procurement decisions to open new sites were driven by national-level contract timelines and property availability rather than local crime, this could offer plausible instrument or event-study variation. Similarly, consider exploiting administrative data on which private providers were contracted over time; differences in provider capacity or procurement cycles may induce quasi-random variation in local placement shares.

2. **Leverage discontinuities or rules in the dispersal allocation process.** The manifest emphasizes “no-choice" placement driven by housing availability. If there exist thresholds—such as capacity limits for providers, minimum occupancy requirements, or periodic reassessments tied to national asylum stock levels—these could be used in a regression discontinuity or conditional event-study design. Documenting and exploiting any such rules would allow causal estimation even if the shift-share approach fails.

3. **Decompose confounding mechanisms in more depth.** The lead regressions show that crime trends predict future dispersal, but it is unclear why. Analyze whether dispersal flows respond to lagged crime levels (e.g., government avoiding high-crime areas), to economic shocks (e.g., unemployment), or to political decisions (e.g., local opposition causing delays). Including lagged crime or local economic controls, or interacting the treatment with regional political variables, could clarify these dynamics and strengthen the interpretation of the null result.

4. **Probe the first-stage dynamics more thoroughly.** The negative coefficient on vacancy share is puzzling. Provide scatter plots of vacancy share against average dispersal rates, both raw and residualized on fixed effects, to visually assess the relationship. Explore whether the sign flips when restricting to earlier years or to CSPs where dispersal housing (rather than hotels) dominates. If the link varies over time, that may explain both the weak instrument and the subperiod heterogeneity and could guide a re-specified instrument (e.g., interacting vacancy with an indicator for pre-2019 periods).

5. **Strengthen descriptive evidence on the treatment.** Present time-series plots of national asylum inflows and local dispersal counts, ideally decomposed by CSP. This would help readers see whether places that receive more asylum seekers do so persistently or only during surges. Additionally, tabulate the share of placements by accommodation type over time: how much of the treatment is “dispersal housing” versus contingency accommodation? This contextual information can better justify whether the term “dispersal rate” accurately captures the policy exposure of interest.

6. **Clarify the causal claim and adjust conclusions accordingly.** As currently written, the conclusion is somewhat ambiguous—“the evidence is consistent with a null effect” while also reiterating policy-relevant messages. Given the identification issues, the paper should clearly frame itself as documenting the challenges in estimating the causal effect and only tentatively commenting on the absence of crime impacts. Emphasize that the failure to find a credible causal link is itself informative for future research design, as the abstract alludes to.

7. **Enhance robustness of OLS results for interpretability.** Even acknowledging that OLS is not causal, provide robustness checks that reduce concerns about confounding: include local trends, interact fixed effects with key covariates (e.g., regional economic shocks), and control for lagged outcomes to partially account for dynamics. Additionally, consider synthetic control comparisons for high-displacement CSPs vs. matched low-displacement ones to gauge the plausibility of the observed associations.

8. **Discuss measurement error and population denominators.** Dispersal counts may reflect administrative reporting quirks or lagged data updates, and crime counts may be influenced by policing intensity. Detail any adjustments made (e.g., smoothed counts, handling of zero population months), and if possible, test whether the results change when using alternative denominators (e.g., Labour Force Survey population estimates) or when scaling crime by police force areas.

9. **Expand the discussion of policy implications with nuance.** Since the current evidence does not support a causal claim, delicately frame policy takeaways as “current administrative data do not reveal any robust positive association, but better identification is needed before policy conclusions.” This prevents overstating the null result while still noting that strong evidence of a crime effect is absent.

10. **Consider including additional outcomes or heterogeneity analysis.** If credible instruments remain elusive for crime, the paper could pivot by exploring descriptive heterogeneity—do CSPs with higher dispersal receive more police funding, does public sentiment change, etc.? Alternatively, if other outcomes (e.g., housing prices) are more amenable to identification, integrating them could salvage the original broader research agenda outlined in the manifest.

By systematically addressing these suggestions, the paper can sharpen both its methodological critique and its contribution to the policy literature on asylum dispersal.
