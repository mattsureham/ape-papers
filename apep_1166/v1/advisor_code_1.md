**Idea Fidelity**

The paper diverges significantly from the original idea manifest. The manifest emphasized exploiting staggered treatment across local authorities (LAs) whose median prices crossed the £450k cap, comparing treated and never-treated LAs with triple-differences that isolate reactions of first-time buyers in high-FTB areas. In contrast, the submitted paper focuses almost entirely on a transaction-level bunching test at the £450k price point using pre/post comparisons and placebo thresholds, with the DiD exercise relegated to a supporting, clearly non-identifying correlation. The key intended tests—differences-in-differences with appropriate counterfactuals, triple-differencing to isolate LISA-eligible buyers, and leveraging the HMRC LISA withdrawal/SDLT data—are absent. Thus, the paper does not pursue the identification strategy or empirical approach laid out in the manifest.

**Summary**

The paper investigates whether the Lifetime ISA’s £450,000 property cap induces bunching in UK housing transactions. Using 7.2 million Land Registry transactions from 2010–2024, it computes the density ratio of sales just below versus just above the cap before and after LISA’s introduction and compares this change to seven placebo thresholds. The main result is a decline in the ratio after the policy launch that is statistically indistinguishable from the placebos, leading the author to conclude that the cap does not distort housing market prices or volumes.

**Essential Points**

1. **Identification of policy effects is weak.** The bunching test uses a naïve before/after comparison of two adjacent price bins without controlling for general changes in the price distribution, compositional shifts in transactions, or cyclical effects. While the placebo thresholds are informative, they do not substitute for a formal bunching estimation (e.g., polynomial counterfactual or local estimation) that accounts for smooth density trends and tests for excess mass at the kink. Without such an approach, the reported change in the ratio may simply reflect differential trends in the selected windows rather than the policy’s causal effect.

2. **Empirical approach deviates from the research question.** The manifest promised analysis of first-time buyer behavior, SDLT relief claims, and triple-differenced treatment effects across LAs; the paper instead centers on aggregate transactions within a narrow price window. This leaves the central question—does the LISA cap hurt the very buyers it targets—largely unaddressed. The supporting DiD is explicitly non-causal due to failed parallel trends, so there is no credible evidence about how LISA users respond to the cap. The paper should either realign with the original identification strategy or clearly justify why focusing solely on bunching is sufficient to answer the policy question.

3. **Policy relevance of the null result is unclear.** Even if the density ratio change is indistinguishable from placebos, the interpretation that the cap “does not distort housing markets” relies on assuming that bunching is the primary margin. Given the small size of the subsidy, limited take-up, and high transaction frictions discussed in the paper, one would expect the effect to be small anyway. The paper should therefore connect the empirical test more tightly to the mechanism (e.g., what share of transactions plausibly involve LISAs) and quantify whether the minimum detectable effect is large relative to the maximum possible distortion. Otherwise, the policy conclusion may overstate what the analysis can deliver.

**Suggestions**

- **Implement a formal bunching estimator.** Follow the standard procedure in the bunching literature (e.g., Kleven & Waseem 2013) by fitting a smooth counterfactual density on either side of £450k (using a polynomial or kernel), estimating excess mass, and calculating standard errors via bootstrapping. This will allow you to test whether the observed density differs from what would be expected absent the policy, rather than relying on simple ratios that may be sensitive to the chosen windows. Presenting the estimated counterfactual and the residuals would also make it easier to assess whether there is localized excess mass.

- **Explore heterogeneity with richer controls.** The cap only affects first-time buyers who actually hold LISAs. Consider restricting the sample to areas or months where LISA usage is higher (e.g., using HMRC withdrawal data by region) or interacting the bunching test with proxies for take-up (e.g., share of young buyers, average income). This would help determine whether the null result is due to limited exposure rather than an inherent lack of responsiveness. If possible, incorporate SDLT first-time buyer relief claims or FCA lending statistics to gauge whether affected buyers shift property types or locations when prices approach the cap.

- **Revisit the DiD strategy from the manifest.** Even though the current TWFE specification fails the parallel-trends test, the manifest’s idea of using staggered crossings could still provide useful evidence if better instruments or assumptions are employed. For example, focus on a narrower set of high-FTB areas where price growth is slower and pre-trends are more parallel, or use event-study graphs to show that treated LAs do not exhibit abnormal bunching patterns relative to controls post-treatment. Alternatively, construct a triple-difference comparing high-FTB areas above the threshold to those below, before and after LISA, which may be feasible using SDLT or LISA withdrawal data. At minimum, clarify why this avenue was abandoned and discuss whether any structural change could still be detected through LISA-relevant outcomes.

- **Clarify the policy interpretation of placebos.** Placebo thresholds are useful for benchmarking, but the paper should explain why the absence of a treatment-specific spike implies the cap is non-distortive. For instance, if all thresholds exhibit similar downward shifts in density due to rising prices, what would a policy-induced bunching look like? Would it have to exceed those general shifts? Consider plotting the distribution of placebo coefficients and showing the empirical distribution of density changes for other knots to illustrate the comparative magnitude of the £450k change.

- **Quantify the subset of transactions that could realistically bunch.** Use HMRC LISA statistics (available through 2025) to estimate the number of purchases funded by LISAs each year and compare that to the number of transactions near the cap. If only a small fraction of transactions are eligible, that would justify the null result’s plausibility. You could also calculate how many £1000 bonuses would need to be traded off to move 1% of transactions and compare it to typical negotiation costs (as already mentioned in the discussion). Making these calculations explicit would strengthen the policy discussion and help readers assess whether the null is meaningful.

- **Report robustness checks graphically.** Include kernel density plots (before vs after, with placebos) to visualize the transaction distribution around £450k. Displaying the data will help readers judge whether the patterns are indeed similar across thresholds. Additionally, provide event-study figures for the TWFE model, even if the trends diverge, to illustrate the timing of the divergence and whether any residual policy effect emerges post-2017.

- **Address potential sample selection issues.** The main ratio compares fixed windows of ±£10k but ignores potential shifts in transaction volume (e.g., more expensive areas might now dominate the sample). Consider normalizing the windows or estimating the ratio within narrower price buckets conditional on macro controls. Alternatively, implement the bunching test at the LA level (e.g., compute ratios within each LA and estimate panel regressions) to control for the evolution of local price structures and ensure that the observed decline is not driven by compositional changes in which local authorities dominate the £450k bin.

By aligning the empirical strategy more closely with the original identification plan, adopting a more rigorous bunching methodology, and connecting the null result to the scope of the policy, the paper could provide more credible evidence on whether the Lifetime ISA cap distorts first-time buyer markets.
