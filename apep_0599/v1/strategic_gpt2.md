## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple, policy-relevant question: when a government sharply restricts disability pension access for young adults, does it create more employment or merely reroute people into other welfare programs? Using Denmark’s 2013 under-40 disability pension ban, the paper argues that the main effect was bureaucratic reallocation into the newly created rehabilitation/resource scheme and some cash assistance, not clear labor market activation.

A busy economist should care because this is a first-order question about what disability reform actually does in modern welfare states: does stricter gatekeeping uncover latent work capacity, or does it just reshuffle claimants across programs? That is a broad question in public finance, labor, and political economy of the welfare state.

### Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Reasonably, but not sharply enough. The opening is competent and readable, but it takes too long to get to the central claim: this is not mainly a paper about Denmark, or about a rehabilitation program per se, or about a triple-difference design. It is about whether disability gatekeeping changes work or just changes labels.

Right now the introduction spends too much space on institutional detail and too little on the punchline economists will remember. The best version of the opening would foreground the general question, then immediately say why Denmark is unusually revealing: the reform both shut one door and built another, so substitution is directly observable.

### The pitch the paper should have

“Governments across the OECD are tightening disability insurance in the hope of moving people from permanent benefits into work. But when disability access is restricted, do people actually work more, or do they simply move to other programs? This paper studies Denmark’s 2013 under-40 disability pension ban, a reform that both sharply curtailed disability awards for young adults and created a new rehabilitation pathway. I show that the reform primarily redirected young claimants into the new resource scheme and other benefits, with little evidence of employment activation. The broader lesson is that disability gatekeeping often changes the administrative destination of vulnerable individuals more than their underlying labor market attachment.”

That is the paper’s best story. It is strong enough for AER consideration if executed well.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper’s contribution is to show that Denmark’s major disability reform primarily produced program substitution into a newly created rehabilitation bureaucracy, rather than a clear shift into employment.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3-4 papers?

Only partially. The paper cites relevant neighboring work, but the differentiation is still too generic: “I study Denmark,” “I use a different design,” “I observe the resource scheme directly.” That is not yet enough. For an AER-level introduction, the paper needs to say more crisply why this case changes what we know.

The most promising differentiator is not the estimator. It is the institutional design: Denmark simultaneously tightened disability access and created a dedicated substitute program. That means this paper can speak more directly to the mechanism of “bureaucratic absorption” than studies where substitution is inferred residually from movements across existing programs.

That should be the centerpiece of the contribution. Right now it is present, but not elevated enough.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a gap in a literature?

Mostly about the world, which is good. The paper asks: what happens to people when the disability door closes? That is the right framing. It occasionally lapses into “I contribute to three literatures” mode, which makes it feel smaller and more conventional.

The stronger framing is: many governments believe disability gatekeeping activates work; this paper shows that in a high-capacity welfare state, the likely margin is instead administrative reassignment. That is a claim about the world.

### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what’s new?

Almost, but not quite. Right now they might say: “It’s a Denmark DiD/DDD paper on a disability reform showing substitution into a rehab program.” That is decent but still sounds like “another reduced-form policy paper.”

What they should be able to say is: “It shows that when you close disability access, the state may create a holding category rather than workers; Denmark is especially informative because the substitute program was explicitly built for that purpose.”

That is more memorable.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Specific ways to make it bigger:

1. **Make the object of interest long-run labor market attachment, not just program stocks.**  
   If the paper could say more convincingly what happened to employment, earnings, or self-sufficiency, it becomes much more than a substitution paper.

2. **Lean harder into the newly created program as an institutional mechanism.**  
   The paper’s comparative advantage is that it can observe substitution into a purpose-built holding category. Make that the star.

3. **Reframe from “Danish disability reform” to “what governments build behind a closed door.”**  
   This is conceptually larger and more exportable.

4. **Clarify whether the interesting fact is substitution per se, or failed activation despite flexicurity.**  
   The latter is potentially bigger: if even Denmark cannot activate this margin, that is a striking external message.

5. **Show sharper accounting of the destination margin.**  
   Not more robustness, but clearer narrative accounting: among the plausible destinations, which one dominates and why should we care?

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Across rich countries, governments worry that disability insurance has become too expansive and hope tighter screening will move people with residual work capacity into jobs.

### Tension

But there is a competing view: many disability applicants face genuine, binding limitations, so closing disability access may not create workers; it may simply push people into other programs. Denmark is an especially revealing test because it paired a sharp restriction with an explicit new alternative.

### Resolution

The reform appears to have shifted young adults disproportionately into the new resource scheme and some cash benefits, with weak evidence of employment activation. The system absorbed people administratively rather than transforming them into workers.

### Implications

Gatekeeping reforms may change the composition and label of benefit receipt more than underlying dependency; policymakers should not equate fewer disability awards with successful labor market reintegration.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

Yes, in outline. But the arc is blurred by too much time spent on estimator exposition and by the paper’s tendency to narrate coefficient interpretation outcome-by-outcome. The reader can feel the paper’s real story trying to emerge, but it is often submerged beneath design caveats and stock-vs-flow discussion.

At times it feels like a collection of sensible empirical sections rather than a story told with escalating purpose.

### What story should it be telling?

The paper should tell this story:

1. Governments think disability tightening can activate work.
2. Denmark is an unusually informative test because it both closed the disability channel and created a visible substitute.
3. The substitute became the main destination.
4. Therefore, disability reform in a generous, high-capacity welfare state may function less as activation than as administrative relabeling.

That is a clean four-beat structure. Everything in the paper should serve that.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?

“When Denmark virtually banned disability pensions for people under 40, the main effect was not a visible employment shift; it was a surge into a new rehabilitation category that looks like a bureaucratic holding pattern.”

That is the dinner-party line.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

Lean in, if stated that way. The topic is broad and consequential. Economists care about whether tightening one program reduces welfare use overall or just moves people around. “A reform created a new holding pen rather than new workers” is a memorable fact.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

The immediate follow-up is: **“But did those people eventually end up working?”**  
That is the paper’s central vulnerability as an AER story. The current answer is effectively “I cannot really tell with these data.” Honest, but limiting.

Second follow-up: **“Is Denmark special, or does this generalize?”**  
The paper can handle that one better by emphasizing Denmark as a hard test for the activation view.

### If the findings are modest: is the modesty itself interesting?

Yes, but only if framed correctly. The paper does not have huge dramatic treatment effects on employment; the interesting result is not “nothing happened.” It is “the thing that happened was administrative substitution into a newly built category.” That is not a failed experiment. It is an important substantive finding.

The paper should be very explicit that the null on employment is informative because the reform happened in **Denmark**, where one might have expected activation to work if it works anywhere.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

### What is the gap between the current paper and an AER paper?

Primarily a **framing and ambition problem**, secondarily a **scope problem**.

This is not mainly a novelty problem. The underlying question is important and still open enough. Nor is it mainly a craftsmanship problem. The author knows what the paper is about. The issue is that the current manuscript presents itself as a careful country reform study when it needs to present itself as a paper about the fundamental consequences of disability gatekeeping.

The biggest gap is this: **the paper currently documents substitution; it does not yet fully convert that into a broad claim that changes how economists think about disability reform.**

To become an AER paper, it needs to convince readers of one of the following broader messages:

- stricter disability screening often reorganizes welfare dependence rather than reducing it;
- even under favorable institutional conditions, activation from disability reform is limited;
- reforms create administrative waiting rooms, not just incentives.

Any one of those could be big. Right now they are all gestured at, but none is driven home hard enough.

### Is it a framing problem?

Yes, first and foremost.

### Is it a scope problem?

Also yes. The paper needs a bit more on what this means for labor market attachment or the welfare state as a whole. Even if individual tracing is impossible, the paper must do more conceptually with the evidence it has.

### Is it a novelty problem?

Not fatal, but there is some risk. “Program substitution after disability reform” is known. The paper’s route around this is the visible, purpose-built substitute program in Denmark. That comparative advantage must be hammered.

### Is it an ambition problem?

Yes. The paper is a bit too careful, a bit too safe, and too willing to settle for “here is a well-identified case study.” AER papers need a claim with more reach.

### Single most impactful piece of advice

**Rewrite the paper around one big claim: Denmark shows that disability retrenchment can operate through bureaucratic absorption into newly created programs rather than through employment activation, even in a high-capacity flexicurity state where activation should have been most plausible.**

That is the one thing to change. Everything else follows from it.
