### 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

**The Pitch:** When governments restrict access to disability insurance (DI), the goal is usually to "activate" claimants into the labor market. This paper uses a 2013 Danish reform—which effectively banned DI for those under 40—to show that instead of finding jobs, blocked applicants were "absorbed" into a newly created, multi-year bureaucratic holding program (the "resource scheme"). It serves as a cautionary tale: supply-side reforms often fail to create workers if health limitations are the fundamental constraint.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this well in the second paragraph, framing the issue as "social support substitution." However, the first paragraph is a bit descriptive. It should more forcefully bridge the gap between the *specific* Danish context and the *general* economic theory of latent work capacity.

**The Pitch the paper should have:** "Economists often assume that disability rolls are inflated by individuals with latent work capacity who respond to program incentives. Using a near-total ban on disability pensions for young adults in Denmark, I find that even under 'flexicurity,' the margin of adjustment is not labor market reentry, but substitution into alternative welfare states. This suggests that for the marginal DI applicant, health constraints are binding and cannot be 'incentivized' away."

---

### 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**The Contribution:** The paper identifies the "bureaucratic absorption" of disability applicants into a dedicated, transparent substitute program, rather than the "hidden" substitution often inferred in other literatures.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It is well-differentiated from Haller et al. (2024), who find employment gains in Austria. The "Denmark is the best-case scenario for activation" argument makes the null result here more striking.
*   **World vs. Literature:** It frames itself as answering a question about the **WORLD** (where do applicants go?), which is a strength.
*   **Clarity:** A smart economist would call this "The Danish substitution paper." It avoids being "just another DiD" by highlighting the *intentional* creation of a substitute program.
*   **Bigger Contribution:** To make this "AER big," the author needs to move beyond *accounting* (who went where) to *welfare*. Does this 5-year delay in the "resource scheme" improve long-term health or just increase the "psychological cost of prolonged uncertainty"? Even a small amount of descriptive data on health outcomes or "mini flex job" earnings would elevate this.

---

### 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

*   **Closest Neighbors:** Borghans et al. (2014) on substitution; Maestas et al. (2013) on latent capacity; Autor & Duggan (2003) on DI rolls.
*   **Positioning:** It should **synthesize** the Borghans/Karlström "substitution" view with the Maestas "latent capacity" view, using Denmark as the tie-breaker.
*   **Missing Conversations:** The paper is light on the **Political Economy** of welfare. Why does a government *intentionally* create a transparent substitute program like the "resource scheme"? Is it to satisfy voters while technically cutting the DI rolls? Connecting to the "Orwellian" nature of welfare accounting would be a "power move."
*   **Right Conversation?** Yes, but it could speak more to the **Public Management** side—how municipalities manage "stocks" vs "flows" of people to meet national targets.

---

### 4. NARRATIVE ARC

*   **Setup:** The OECD-wide push to tighten DI eligibility under the assumption of latent work capacity.
*   **Tension:** If the "flexicurity" poster child (Denmark) can't turn DI applicants into workers, who can?
*   **Resolution:** Applicants didn't get jobs; they got a new label in a different folder (The Resource Scheme).
*   **Implications:** Closing the "front door" of welfare just leads to a crowded "side door" unless you fix the underlying health/human capital.

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong. It is not just results; it is a clear critique of "supply-side" welfare reform.

---

### 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

*   **The Lead Fact:** "Denmark banned disability for under-40s, and 5 years later, the number of people on benefits hadn't changed—they were just in a program called 'Resource Scheme' instead."
*   **Reaction:** People lean in because it confirms a cynical suspicion about government reform and "flexicurity."
*   **The Follow-up:** "Is it just a waiting room, or do they eventually get jobs after the 5 years are up?" (The paper needs to address this duration/long-term transition issue more directly, even if the data is limited).

---

### 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

*   **Front-loading:** The paper is well-structured. However, the "stock vs flow" discussion in Section 7 is vital and should be mentioned earlier in the Data section.
*   **Appendix:** The leave-one-out and RI are good but standard; keep them in the appendix.
*   **Mechanism:** The sex heterogeneity (Table 5) is fascinating (women respond to the "pull" of flex jobs, men don't). This should be moved earlier as a "Mechanism" section rather than a "Robustness" check. It's a "lead" result, not a "check."

---

### 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The gap is **Ambition**. Currently, it feels like a very high-quality "Policy Evaluation." To make it AER, it needs to be a "Theory of the Welfare State" paper.

**Single Most Impactful Advice:** Shift the framing from "evaluating a Danish reform" to "testing the limits of the incentive-based model of disability insurance." Use the "Resource Scheme" not just as an outcome, but as a lens to see how the welfare state hides its failures.
