## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper studies what happens when a country sharply restricts access to disability insurance for young adults while simultaneously creating alternative activation and rehabilitation programs. Using Denmark’s 2013 under-40 disability pension ban, it argues that blocked applicants were absorbed into a new “resource scheme” and related benefits rather than moved into employment.

Why should a busy economist care? Because many OECD reforms are built on the premise that tighter disability screening will activate latent work capacity. If the real effect is mostly relabeling people across welfare programs, then the policy lesson is much broader than Denmark.

**Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Reasonably well, but not sharply enough. The introduction currently gets bogged down in institutional detail before fully stating the big economic question. The first two paragraphs should more explicitly say: governments are tightening disability access all over the OECD; the central unresolved question is whether this creates employment or just administrative reshuffling; Denmark is a particularly useful test because it both shut one door and opened another.

**The pitch the paper should have:**

> Across the OECD, governments are tightening disability insurance in the hope that denied applicants will work rather than exit permanently from the labor force. But when disability access is restricted, do people actually become employed, or are they redirected into other welfare programs?  
>   
> This paper studies Denmark’s 2013 reform, which virtually barred disability pensions for people under 40 while channeling them into a new rehabilitation program and expanded subsidized jobs. I show that the reform primarily increased enrollment in the new resource scheme and related cash assistance, with little evidence of employment gains. The broader message is that disability gatekeeping can change the label of benefit receipt more easily than the underlying attachment to work.

That is the story. It is stronger than “here is a triple-difference on a Danish reform.”

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution in one sentence:**  
The paper’s contribution is to show that Denmark’s under-40 disability tightening primarily reallocated young adults into a newly created rehabilitation bureaucracy rather than into employment.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3-4 papers?
Only partially. The paper cites the right general literature, but the differentiation is still too method-centric and too local. Right now the novelty sounds like:

- Denmark instead of Sweden/Norway/Netherlands/Austria
- triple-difference instead of before-after
- direct observation of a newly created substitute program

The third point is the most interesting. The first two are not enough for AER positioning. “Another country, another reform, another quasi-experiment” is not a top-journal contribution unless the case changes what we think generally.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or as filling a gap in a literature?
Mostly about the world, which is good. The paper’s strongest framing is: what happens when you close the disability door and open an activation door behind it? But it drifts too often into “I contribute to three literatures” mode. That weakens the force of the paper.

### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what's new?
Yes, but probably in a slightly deflating way:  
“It’s a paper showing that Denmark’s disability reform pushed people into the new rehab program rather than employment.”

That is intelligible, but still sounds like “another substitution paper” unless the introduction presses harder on why Denmark is a crucial case. Denmark is supposed to be a hard test for the substitution view because it has stronger activation institutions than most countries. That should be foregrounded much more.

### What would make this contribution bigger?
Specific ways to enlarge the contribution:

1. **Make employment central, not auxiliary.**  
   The current story is strongest on substitution into the resource scheme, but that is only half the policy question. For this to feel AER-scale, the paper needs to more forcefully answer: did total labor market attachment rise, fall, or stay flat?

2. **Reframe the paper as a test of “activation optimism” in a best-case institutional setting.**  
   Denmark is not just another country; it is arguably the country where gatekeeping-plus-activation should work if it works anywhere.

3. **Show the full destination set.**  
   The current narrative mostly highlights the resource scheme. To make the contribution bigger, the authors should organize outcomes around a complete accounting: permanent disability, temporary rehabilitation, subsidized employment, ordinary employment, cash assistance, and non-employment/non-receipt if observable.

4. **Clarify whether the new program is a stepping stone or a waiting room.**  
   Right now “bureaucratic absorption” is a provocative label, but the paper cannot fully substantiate it from aggregate stocks alone. The story becomes larger if the authors either document progression patterns more clearly or scale back the rhetoric.

5. **Tie the Danish case to welfare-state design.**  
   The broad question is not merely substitution; it is whether adding active labor market infrastructure changes the incidence of substitution. That is a more general contribution.

That framing connects disability economics, labor economics, public finance, and state capacity. It is the right conversation for AER.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Governments worry that disability insurance has become a long-term labor force exit route for people with some residual work capacity. In response, many countries tighten screening and push activation.

### Tension
But tightening one program may not create workers; it may simply move people into neighboring programs. Denmark is especially interesting because it did not just tighten DI — it also created a visible alternative infrastructure intended to rehabilitate and activate claimants.

### Resolution
The paper finds that the main effect was growth in the new resource scheme and some cash assistance, with little persuasive evidence of employment gains.

### Implications
Disability gatekeeping may mostly reorganize welfare receipt unless the underlying constraints are genuinely remediable. More broadly, activation architecture may function as a bureaucratic buffer rather than a bridge to work.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
It has one, but it is not as disciplined as it should be. The manuscript sometimes reads like a collection of program-by-program results plus methodological caveats, rather than a story marching from policy ambition to empirical verdict.

The paper should be telling a simpler story:

1. Policymakers believed denied DI applicants could be activated.
2. Denmark built the ideal institutional machine to do that.
3. The machine mostly re-sorted people within the welfare state.
4. Therefore, the limits are not just about benefit generosity; they are about the underlying population and the functions bureaucracies actually serve.

That is a paper. Right now the narrative is somewhat diluted by too much discussion of stock mechanics, too many coefficient interpretations, and too much insistence on every empirical wrinkle in the main text.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?
“Denmark virtually banned disability pensions for under-40s, but the main result was not more employment — it was a surge into a new rehabilitation program.”

That is a good opening fact. Economists will listen.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
They would lean in initially. The question is first-order and very policy relevant. But if the next sentence is “using municipality-by-age-quarter triple differences,” they’ll reach for their phones. If the next sentence is “even in Denmark, where activation should work best, the reform mostly changed where people sit in the welfare system,” they’ll stay engaged.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Probably one of these:

- “Did employment actually change?”
- “Is the resource scheme a temporary bridge or just disguised disability?”
- “Why is Denmark different from Austria?”
- “How much of this is relabeling versus real rehabilitation?”

Those are exactly the questions the paper should anticipate more directly in the framing.

### If the findings are modest or partly null, is the null interesting?
Yes — if framed correctly. A null or weak employment response is interesting here because Denmark is a favorable test case for activation. Learning that even there the reform mostly induced program substitution is informative. But the paper needs to make this case more forcefully. Right now the employment discussion feels apologetic rather than central.

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

### What would make it read better?

1. **Shorten the institutional background.**  
   It is competent but overlong relative to the payoff. Readers need the reform’s logic, not a mini-monograph on Danish programs.

2. **Move most discussion of simple DiD limitations out of the results narrative.**  
   The paper repeatedly explains why some estimates are hard to interpret. That is honest, but it slows the paper and weakens confidence. Establish the preferred design once, then organize the main text around it.

3. **Front-load the core findings much earlier.**  
   By the end of page 2, the reader should know:
   - under-40 DI was effectively shut
   - a new resource scheme opened
   - the reform mainly increased enrollment in that scheme, not employment

4. **Collapse the “contributions to three literatures” section into a more pointed positioning paragraph.**  
   The current literature-tour format is conventional but not memorable.

5. **Reorganize results around economic questions, not estimators.**  
   Instead of:
   - descriptive evidence
   - DiD
   - DDD
   - event study
   - dose response  
   
   Better:
   - Where did denied applicants go?
   - Did employment rise?
   - Was the resource scheme a substitute or bridge?
   - Why is Denmark informative relative to prior reforms?

6. **Put more of the coefficient-by-coefficient stock interpretation in an appendix or footnotes.**  
   The paper spends too much time explaining why certain estimates do not mean what a reader thinks they mean. That is a symptom that those estimates should be less central.

7. **The conclusion should do more than summarize.**  
   It should leave the reader with one sharpened idea: tightening disability eligibility without creating truly viable work matches may transform social insurance into a different administrative queue, not into labor supply.

### Are results buried in robustness that should be in the main text?
The most important buried result is not exactly in robustness, but in framing: the weak employment response is central and should sit closer to the headline. If the paper’s claim is “bureaucratic absorption rather than activation,” employment cannot feel like an afterthought.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

At present, the gap is mostly a **framing + ambition + scope** problem, not just a prose problem.

### Is it a framing problem?
Yes. The science may be adequate, but the paper undersells the broader question. The right frame is not “a Danish reform and some substitution.” It is “can modern welfare states turn disability gatekeeping into employment when they build activation infrastructure to do so?”

### Is it a scope problem?
Yes. The paper’s main empirical success is documenting uptake of the resource scheme. But for AER, that is too intermediate unless tied more decisively to labor market outcomes, total welfare dependence, or a clear conceptual advance about what these programs do.

### Is it a novelty problem?
Partly. The general idea that tightening DI causes substitution is not new. The paper’s novel angle is that Denmark created an explicit destination program, making the substitute visible. That is interesting, but not yet enough. The authors need to show why that visibility changes the lesson, not just the descriptive label.

### Is it an ambition problem?
Yes. The paper is careful, competent, and somewhat safe. It does not yet claim enough. The concept “bureaucratic absorption” could be powerful, but the manuscript does not fully build the theoretical and empirical case around it. It remains a suggestive phrase rather than a paper-defining contribution.

### Single most impactful advice
**Reframe the paper as a decisive test of whether disability gatekeeping plus activation infrastructure increases work in a best-case welfare state — and organize every result around answering that question.**

That one change would force the authors to:
- center Denmark’s “hard test” status,
- elevate employment and destination accounting,
- use the resource scheme result as evidence for a bigger claim,
- and make the paper matter beyond Scandinavian disability policy.

As it stands, I can imagine this as a solid field-journal paper, maybe a strong general-interest second-tier outlet with the right tightening. For AER, it needs to feel less like “evidence from Denmark” and more like “a broad lesson about the limits of activation-based welfare reform.”
