## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether reducing access to a basic government service point—the rural post office—discourages entrepreneurship. Using the USPS’s 2012–2015 POStPlan, which sharply reduced operating hours at more than 13,000 rural post offices, the paper argues that counties exposed to larger cuts saw fewer new business applications, suggesting that physical state capacity still matters for market entry even in an ostensibly digital age.

A busy economist should care because the paper is not really about mail. It is about whether small frictions in access to the administrative state can suppress business formation in places already on the margin.

**Does the paper articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Not quite. The introduction is competent and readable, but it leads with “Rural America has a startup problem” and then quickly lists service uses of post offices. That is narrower and more descriptive than the strongest pitch. The real hook is bigger: a major federal retrenchment created a test of whether shrinking the everyday interface between citizens and the state reduces entrepreneurship.

### The pitch the paper should have

Here is what the first two paragraphs should say instead:

> Starting a business requires more than ideas, capital, and customers. It also requires access to the state: an address, identity verification, payments, filings, and legally recognized communications. In urban areas these administrative inputs are easy to obtain. In rural America, they are often bundled through a single local institution: the post office.
>
> This paper studies what happens when that access is reduced. Between 2012 and 2015, the U.S. Postal Service cut operating hours at more than 13,000 rural post offices—the largest retrenchment of federal retail presence in decades. I show that counties facing larger cuts experienced fewer new business applications, implying that seemingly mundane reductions in government service access can meaningfully slow entrepreneurship. The broader lesson is that physical state capacity remains an input into market formation, even in the digital era.

That framing is more AER-worthy because it elevates the question from “do post offices matter?” to “does access to the state shape entry?”

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution in one sentence:**  
The paper’s contribution is to show that reductions in the local physical accessibility of government service infrastructure can reduce rural business formation.

That is the right contribution. But the paper does not yet differentiate it sharply enough from adjacent work.

### Is the contribution clearly differentiated from the closest papers?
Only partially. Right now the paper places itself as “a previously unstudied determinant of business formation: postal infrastructure.” That is a literature-gap framing, and it invites the dismissive reaction: “interesting, but this is another paper showing local service access matters.” The introduction names broad literatures but does not clearly say what existing papers *cannot* tell us and what this setting uniquely reveals.

The differentiation should be something like:

- Existing entrepreneurship papers focus on finance, broadband, agglomeration, or regulation.
- Existing “service-point” papers mostly study private intermediaries like banks.
- This paper studies **government retail presence** as an entrepreneurial input, and one that is **especially salient in thin rural markets where substitution is limited**.
- The key conceptual move is not “post offices are important,” but “administrative access is an input into firm entry.”

### World question or literature-gap question?
Too much framed as filling a gap in a literature. The stronger framing is about the world:

- **Weaker:** “The literature has overlooked post offices.”
- **Stronger:** “Entrepreneurship depends on access to administrative infrastructure, and rural America may be especially vulnerable when that access contracts.”

The latter is much more publishable.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the intro?
At present, they could probably say: “It’s a DiD on rural post-office hours and business applications.” That is not enough. The paper needs the reader to say instead: “It shows that state access itself is a production input for entrepreneurship.”

### What would make the contribution bigger?
Several possibilities:

1. **Reframe the outcome as entrepreneurial state interaction, not just business applications.**  
   If there are outcomes closer to the mechanism—incorporations, employer-business applications, delays in filing, EIN applications likely to become employers—that would sharpen the conceptual contribution.

2. **Distinguish between ‘market access’ and ‘state access.’**  
   Right now the paper risks sounding like a generic local-access paper. It would be bigger if it showed that the affected margin is specifically the interaction with administrative/legal infrastructure.

3. **Exploit heterogeneity tied to substitutability.**  
   The paper already gestures at unbanked households, distance, and sparse alternatives. Making heterogeneity by bank-branch presence, broadband access, street-address quality, or remoteness central would turn the paper from a reduced-form finding into a broader proposition about when physical government infrastructure matters.

4. **Use a stronger framing around “state capacity and entry.”**  
   That would connect the paper to bigger literatures beyond rural studies.

The single biggest way to enlarge the contribution is **to make this a paper about administrative state capacity as a determinant of entrepreneurship, with the post office as the concrete setting**.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Entrepreneurship in rural America has declined, and most explanations emphasize capital, broadband, and population trends. Implicitly, the world before this paper treats post offices as peripheral or obsolete.

### Tension
Starting a business often requires interactions that are administrative, legal, and physical. In many rural places, those interactions are bundled through a local post office. So a large federal retrenchment creates a puzzle: can reducing access to this mundane state interface depress business formation?

### Resolution
The paper finds that counties exposed to bigger cuts in post office hours saw fewer business applications, with larger and growing effects where service reductions were more severe.

### Implications
The implication is that physical access to the administrative state remains economically consequential. Cutting government retail presence may save budget dollars but at the cost of reduced local dynamism.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
It has a **serviceable** arc, but not yet a compelling one. The ingredients are there, but the story is still too close to “policy happened, outcome moved.” It reads more like a competent applied paper than a paper with a memorable conceptual spine.

The story it **should** be telling is:

1. Entrepreneurship requires access not just to markets and finance, but to the state.
2. In rural America, that access is often concentrated in a single place.
3. POStPlan unexpectedly shrank that interface.
4. Business formation fell where the interface shrank more.
5. Therefore, everyday state presence is part of the infrastructure of capitalism.

That last sentence is the paper’s real narrative payoff. Right now it is buried.

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

### What fact would I lead with?
“I’d lead with: when the USPS cut hours at over 13,000 rural post offices, new business applications in affected counties fell meaningfully.”

That is a decent opening fact. Better still:
“Reducing the hours of the main local access point to the state caused fewer firms to start.”

### Would people lean in?
Some would, but many would initially think “post offices?” and mentally downgrade it as quaint. That is exactly why the framing matters. If presented as a post-office paper, some economists will reach for their phones. If presented as a paper on administrative state access and entrepreneurship, they will lean in.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Immediately: **What is the mechanism?**  
More specifically:
- Is this about legal/administrative filings?
- Is it just a proxy for broader local decline?
- Why didn’t digital substitution undo the effect?
- Which entrepreneurs are actually constrained?

Those are the right questions. The paper is aware of them, but the mechanism discussion remains somewhat assertive rather than revelatory.

### If the findings are modest, is the result still interesting?
Yes, but only if framed correctly. A 3–8 percent effect is not inherently headline-grabbing by AER standards unless it speaks to a big question. Here the big question is whether access to the state is a real input into business formation. If the paper insists on being about “postal hours,” the result will feel modest. If it is about “administrative infrastructure,” the same estimate becomes much more interesting.
