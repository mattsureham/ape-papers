## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether a seemingly mundane cut in government service access—reduced operating hours at rural post offices—depressed entrepreneurship. Using the USPS’s 2012–2015 POStPlan, the paper argues that shrinking access to postal services caused a meaningful decline in new business applications in rural counties, suggesting that physical public infrastructure still matters for economic activity even in a digitizing economy.

A busy economist should care because the paper is not really about stamps; it is about whether low-level state capacity and everyday administrative access shape market entry. If true, that is a broad claim with implications for entrepreneurship, rural decline, public infrastructure, and the design of cost-cutting government reforms.

### Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Not quite. The ingredients are there, but the introduction currently oversells the postal-specific mechanics before fully establishing the broader economic question. It starts from “rural America has a startup problem,” then moves quickly into a somewhat literal list of postal services. That makes the paper sound narrower than it is. The real hook is larger: when the state withdraws routine local administrative access, does private economic dynamism suffer?

### The pitch the paper should have

A better first two paragraphs would say something like:

> Economists increasingly recognize that entrepreneurship depends not only on finance, talent, and broadband, but also on access to the administrative infrastructure that allows firms to enter and operate. Yet we know little about whether small reductions in everyday government service access—shorter hours, fewer service points, less in-person capacity—meaningfully affect business formation, especially in rural areas where substitutes are scarce.
>
> This paper studies one of the largest modern contractions in U.S. retail government infrastructure: the USPS’s 2012–2015 POStPlan, which reduced hours at more than 13,000 rural post offices. I show that counties exposed to larger post-office hour reductions experienced fewer new business applications, implying that even in the digital era, local physical access to government-provided transactional infrastructure remains a meaningful input into entrepreneurship.

That framing makes the paper about the world: the relationship between state presence and market entry.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper’s contribution is to show that reductions in access to a ubiquitous but underappreciated piece of local government infrastructure—the rural post office—reduced new business formation, implying that administrative service access is a real constraint on entrepreneurship.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?

Only partially. The paper names some broad literatures, but the differentiation is still a bit generic: “previously unstudied determinant,” “speaks to rural decline,” “extends insight from bank branches to government service points.” That is serviceable but not sharp enough for AER-level positioning.

Right now, a smart economist could easily summarize this as: “It’s another paper showing that local service access matters for economic outcomes.” The paper needs to explain why postal hours are not just another instance of local amenities or branch closures, but a distinctive test of a broader hypothesis: that administrative frictions in firm entry are created by the physical design of the state itself.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or filling a gap in a LITERATURE?

Mixed, but too much of the current framing is gap-filling. Phrases like “overlooked,” “previously unstudied determinant,” and “contributes to three literatures” are conventional but weak. The stronger version is a world question: when government shrinks routine in-person access points, does that suppress economic activity?

That is a better AER question than “no one has studied post offices before.”

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?

Somewhat, but not crisply. They would probably say: “It’s a DiD paper on rural post office hours and business applications.” That is not enough. They should instead be able to say: “It shows that a low-salience contraction in state service capacity can materially reduce firm entry.” The paper is not yet forcing that summary.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Most importantly: reframe from “post office hours” to “administrative access as infrastructure for firm entry.” Beyond framing, several content expansions would make the contribution feel larger:

1. **A stronger mechanism outcome**
   - If possible, distinguish types of business applications most likely to depend on in-person postal/bureaucratic services.
   - Right now, “all business applications” is broad but somewhat blunt. A more mechanism-linked outcome—employer applications, likely-to-be-employer applications, incorporated businesses, or application categories requiring more formal processing—would make the claim feel much more economically meaningful.

2. **A more direct comparison to other service contractions**
   - The bank-branch comparison is gestured at late in the paper. It should be central earlier: how large is this effect relative to bank closures, DMV access, courthouse distance, or local public service access more broadly?

3. **A broader conceptual framing**
   - The paper would be bigger if it argued that the post office is a test case for a general proposition: in thin rural markets, “digitally substitutable” state services are not actually substitutable in practice.

4. **Heterogeneity that speaks to the world question**
   - Effects should be strongest where substitutes are scarcest: low broadband, high unbanked rates, greater distance to alternative service points, weaker street addressing. That would make the paper feel like it is explaining a mechanism in the world, not merely estimating a treatment effect.

- Connect to state capacity / administrative burden literatures by bringing them into the language of firm entry and local economic development.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

The world before this paper: rural entrepreneurship is weak, and economists usually explain that through broadband, finance, labor market decline, demographics, or market size. Government service infrastructure is usually not treated as a first-order input into business formation.

### Tension

A massive federal retrenchment reduced hours at rural post offices, but we do not know whether that kind of “small” administrative contraction matters economically. In a digital era, one might think shorter postal hours are an inconvenience, not a real barrier to starting a business. So the puzzle is whether these low-level frictions are actually consequential.

### Resolution

The paper finds that counties exposed to these hour reductions experienced fewer new business applications, with larger declines where hours were cut more sharply and effects that build over time.

### Implications

The implication is that routine, local, physical access to government-administered services can affect private economic dynamism. Cost-saving public-sector retrenchment may therefore impose hidden entry costs on firms, especially in rural places where substitutes are limited.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the raw materials for one, but the narrative is still somewhat underdeveloped. At times it reads like a collection of plausible results around a treatment rather than a fully realized story.

The main weakness is that the paper oscillates between two stories:

1. “The post office matters for entrepreneurship.”
2. “Government service access is a form of infrastructure.”

The second is much stronger. The first sounds niche; the second sounds important.

### What story should it be telling?

It should be telling this story:

> Economists often think of entrepreneurship as constrained by capital, ideas, or broadband. But in rural areas, routine administrative access can itself be an input. POStPlan provides a rare large-scale test of what happens when the state narrows the window through which firms access basic transactional infrastructure. The result is less firm entry. Therefore, the physical footprint of the administrative state has real economic consequences.

That is the paper’s actual AER-worthy story.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?

I would say: “When the USPS cut hours at 13,000 rural post offices, business formation fell in the affected counties.”

That is a good opener. It is concrete and mildly surprising.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

They would lean in initially, because the treatment is unusual and the claim is intuitively provocative. But then the key question is whether the paper can make them believe this is about more than post offices. If it stays too postal-specific, interest will fade. If it pivots to “the economic value of local state access points,” they will keep listening.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Almost certainly:
- “What’s the mechanism?”
And then:
- “Why weren’t digital substitutes enough?”
- “Is this really post offices, or just broader rural decline?”
- “Which businesses are being deterred?”

Those are not only referee questions; they are strategic-positioning questions. The current draft anticipates them only partially.

### Are the findings interesting enough if modest?

Yes, the findings are interesting, but the paper needs to make clearer why this is not just a small rural anecdote. The null-risk here is not that the results are small; it is that they could be interpreted as “another paper where a local amenity worsens and outcomes decline.” The paper must argue that this is revealing something deeper about how administrative frictions enter firm formation.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The gap is mostly **framing plus ambition**, with some **scope** concerns.

This does not primarily feel like a paper that fails because the design is uninteresting. The treatment is interesting. The setting is novel. The basic result is surprising enough. The problem is that the paper currently presents itself as a competent reduced-form paper about a quirky institutional margin, rather than as a paper about how the local administrative state shapes entrepreneurship.

### Is it a framing problem?

Yes, strongly. This is the biggest issue. The paper’s current title and introduction make it sound narrower than the underlying idea.

### Is it a scope problem?

Also yes. For AER, one outcome—total business applications—may feel too thin unless the paper can really persuade readers this is a first-order conceptual contribution. More mechanism-linked outcomes or sharper heterogeneity would help a lot.

### Is it a novelty problem?

Moderately. The exact setting is novel, but the general empirical move—service contraction reduces economic activity—is not novel enough on its own. The paper needs to claim a bigger conceptual space than “post office access matters.”

### Is it an ambition problem?

Yes. The paper feels a bit safe. It estimates the effect, shows event study and dose response, and stops. An AER paper would more aggressively ask: what does this teach us about the design of the state, digital substitution, and the geography of entrepreneurial frictions?

### Single most impactful advice

If the author changes only one thing, it should be this:

**Reframe the paper from a study of post office hours to a study of how local administrative state capacity affects firm entry, and reorganize the introduction, literature review, and headline results around that broader claim.**

That one change would immediately make the paper feel larger, more coherent, and more plausibly AER-relevant.
