## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
This paper asks whether major cybersecurity mandates actually force firms to improve their technical security or if they simply generate "compliance theater." Using the EU’s NIS2 Directive—the world’s most ambitious cyber mandate—the author exploits a sharp 50-employee regulatory threshold and staggered national implementation to show that firms only invest in substantive technical upgrades once the law becomes locally enforceable. In the absence of immediate enforcement, firms opt for "cheap signals" like staff training rather than costly infrastructure.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this pitch excellently. The first two paragraphs successfully bridge a high-stakes real-world problem (cyberattacks on mid-sized firms) with a deep economic tension (Stiglerian regulation vs. symbolic compliance). 

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**The Contribution:** The paper provides the first causal evidence that the behavioral response to cybersecurity regulation is bifurcated: "announcement" triggers low-cost symbolic actions, while only "enforcement" triggers substantive technical investment.

- **Differentiations:** It moves past the US-centric "breach notification" literature (Romanosky et al.) to look at direct investment mandates.
- **Framing:** It is framed as a question about the WORLD (Does regulation work?), but leans heavily on the "compliance theater" vs. "real investment" gap in the literature.
- **Clarity:** A smart economist could easily explain this: "It’s a DDD on the EU cyber law showing that firms wait for the actual local enforcement before buying the expensive tech, but they do the cheap training early to look like they’re trying."
- **Bigger Contribution:** To make this an AER "blockbuster," the author would need to link these investments to **outcomes** (actual breach rates or insurance premiums). Currently, it measures inputs (security measures), which leaves the "Does this actually make the world safer?" question unanswered.

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
The paper sits at the intersection of **Law and Economics (Regulation)** and the **Economics of Information Security**.

- **Neighbors:** Shimshack (2014) on environmental enforcement; Romanosky et al. (2011) on notification laws; Duflo et al. (2013) on inspections.
- **Positioning:** It builds on the "enforcement matters" literature but applies it to the digital frontier. It essentially "attacks" the idea that EU-wide announcements have a uniform effect.
- **Niche/Broad:** It is currently broad enough for a general interest journal, but it should lean harder into the **Environmental Economics** literature as a structural analog. The "deterrence" model of pollution is the perfect parallel for "cyber-pollution" (externalities from breaches).

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC
- **Setup:** Governments are passing massive cyber laws to stop the bleeding from hacks.
- **Tension:** Do these laws actually change the server room, or just the HR training manual? Skeptics say it's all theater.
- **Resolution:** It’s theater *until* the transposition deadline hits. In the 6 countries that met the deadline, technical measures (encryption, testing) actually rose. In the other 21, only training moved.
- **Implications:** Policy ambition is meaningless without administrative follow-through.

**Evaluation:** The narrative arc is very strong. It isn't just a collection of results; it's a "Wait for it" story.

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
- **Lead Fact:** "When the EU passed its biggest cyber law, 21 countries missed the deadline, and in those countries, firms didn't buy a single piece of new security tech—they just scheduled more staff meetings."
- **Reaction:** People lean in. It confirms a cynical suspicion with rigorous data.
- **Follow-up:** "Does the staff training actually help at all, or is it purely a waste of resources?"

---

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
- **Front-loading:** The paper is well-structured. The "Dosage" test (using large firms already under NIS1) is a brilliant addition and should be highlighted even more in the intro as a "sanity check" for the mechanism.
- **Robustness:** The "Leave-one-out" analysis for the 6 transposed countries is critical because $N=6$ is small for the treatment group. This belongs in the main text, not just the appendix.
- **Conclusion:** The conclusion is a bit brief. It should expand on the "cyber-insurance" angle—does regulation act as a substitute or complement to private insurance?

---

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The gap is **Ambition/Scope.**

Currently, the paper uses aggregate Eurostat data (shares of firms per cell). This is "clean" but feels a bit "light" for the AER. To truly excite the top 10 people in the field, the author needs to find a way to tie these results to **market valuations** or **cybersecurity job postings**.

**Single Most Impactful Advice:** Supplement the Eurostat adoption data with high-frequency data (e.g., scraping job boards for "Cybersecurity Manager" roles or "NIS2 Compliance" keywords in those specific countries) to prove that the "real investment" involves labor market shifts, not just survey-response shifts.
