**Idea Fidelity**

The paper largely adheres to the original manifest. It leverages the staggered adoption of state day-supply limits as specified, draws on the CDC NCHS drug-specific mortality data, and pursues the manifest research question: whether these quantity caps reduced prescription opioid deaths or instead redirected fatalities toward illicit opioids. I appreciate the emphasis on a drug-type decomposition and placebo categories (cocaine, psychostimulants) described in the idea. **Summary**

The paper studies the unintended mortality consequences of state opioid day-supply limits enacted between 2016 and 2019. Using Callaway–Sant’Anna staggered DiD on CDC drug-specific overdose counts, it finds a near-zero average effect on total overdose deaths but evidence that the strictest (3-day) caps were associated with declines in prescription opioid deaths paired with large increases in synthetic opioid (fentanyl) deaths, while the more common 7-day limits generated prescription reductions without substitution. The dose-response insight suggests that supply-side policy can be calibrated: moderate limits reduce prescribing without triggering lethal substitution, whereas overly tight caps backfire.

**Essential Points**

1. **Clarity and consistency of empirical findings:** The principal empirical narrative hinges on a dose-response: 3-day limits push users toward illicit fentanyl, but 7-day limits do not. Yet the key table reporting the Callaway–Sant’Anna ATT (Table 3) shows a negative coefficient for synthetic opioid deaths—even significant in the opposite direction—making the claimed substitution effect appear unsupported by the aggregated estimates. The paper must present the heterogeneous results (by limit stringency) explicitly in the main tables/figures (not only narratively) and reconcile them with the aggregate ATT. Without a transparent breakdown, readers cannot judge whether the substitution finding is driven by a small set of states, a few cohorts, or estimation noise, undermining the core claim.

2. **Credibility of parallel trends, especially for high-stringency states:** The identification relies on comparing treated cohorts to never-treated states, but the three states with 3-day limits (FL, TN, KY) share regional shocks and are among the worst-hit by fentanyl prior to treatment. The event study table shows sizeable (and, for Rx opioids, statistically significant) pre-treatment declines in outcomes ($k=-3$ is about $-0.7$ for Rx opioids), raising concerns that treated states were already on different trajectories. The paper should present cohort-specific (or stringency-specific) event studies and/or placebo treatments to demonstrate that the 3-day states satisfy parallel trends, as the substitution inference critically depends on that assumption.

3. **Heterogeneity, sample size, and robustness of the dose-response:** The inferred substitution mechanism rests on a handful of states that adopted 3-day/5-day limits. Yet neither the paper nor the manifest provides the exact number of states per bin (especially once TX, NV, and other “odd” caps are excluded), nor does it report how sensitive the 3-day effects are to excluding any one of those states. With so few observations, the risk of idiosyncratic shocks (e.g., Florida’s fentanyl crisis in 2017) generating the pattern is high. The authors need to demonstrate robustness (e.g., leave-one-out for 3-day states) and, ideally, exploit variation within states (e.g., rolling implementation during 2016) or alternative specifications (e.g., synthetic control on the 3-day states) to bolster the causal claim that stringency, not state-specific confounders, drives substitution.

If these issues cannot be resolved, the paper risks being rejected outright because the main contribution (the dose-response substitution story) is not convincingly identified.

**Suggestions (≈70% of review)**

1. **Make the heterogeneity explicit in tables/figures:** The current Table 3 aggregates all treated states and thus hides the dose-response. Consider adding a table that reports Callaway–Sant’Anna ATTs separately for 3-day, 5-day, and 7-day cohorts for each drug category, along with standard errors. Alternatively, present a figure with cohort-time patterns by stringency. These tables/figures would allow readers to see that the adverse synthetic opioid effect is driven by the tightest caps, aligning the empirical evidence with the paper’s narrative. If necessary, append supporting tables to the online appendix.

2. **Cohort-specific event studies and pre-treatment checks:** The event study table currently pools over cohorts and outcomes, and it shows non-zero pre-trends. The key identification assumption is that, absent the policy, 3-day states would have evolved similarly to never-treated states. Provide cohort-specific (or dosage-specific) event studies that plot the ATT for 3-day states relative to never-treated controls. If pre-trends remain problematic, consider augmenting the model with state-specific trends or placebo tests (e.g., assigning a fake treatment year before the actual law takes effect) to bolster the credible identification of the substitution effect.

3. **Robustness of the dose-response to omitted states:** Given the small number of 3-day/5-day states, run robustness checks such as dropping one 3-day state at a time (especially Florida) and re-estimating the dose-response. Report how much the synthetic opioid estimate varies. If the results hinge on a single state, carefully discuss that limitation. Conversely, if the effect persists, it strengthens the claim that stringency matters. Additionally, try estimating the dose-response using alternative control groups (e.g., propensity-score matched states or “not-yet-treated” states) to check whether the negative synthetic opioid effect is sensitive to the control selection.

4. **Address potential confounders related to fentanyl geography and demand:** Fentanyl penetration differed sharply across regions and time. Provide more discussion (and if possible, empirical checks) on whether states with tighter limits had faster pre-existing fentanyl growth or different socioeconomic shocks. One approach would be to interact treatment with time-varying measures of fentanyl supply (if available) or with baseline overdose rates. Another is to control for other contemporaneous policies (e.g., naloxone distribution, PDMP enhancements) that might correlate with both limit stringency and illicit deaths. At minimum, tabulate baseline characteristics of the 3-day, 5-day, and 7-day states to show they are comparable.

5. **Clarify the standard errors and aggregation in the dose-response TWFE:** The TWFE dose-response specification is useful, but its standard errors may be understated if there are few treated units per dose. Consider using wild cluster bootstrap (Cameron et al.) given the small number of clusters in some dose groups (especially 3-day). Also clarify how the TWFE coefficients relate to the CS estimator: are the TWFE results the ones driving the claim, or is it the CS heterogeneity? Consistency across methods would increase confidence.

6. **Expand the welfare interpretation with caveats:** The abstract and discussion suggest a welfare framing (net lives saved vs. lost). While compelling, this calculation assumes additive effects and neglects potential lags or spillovers. Consider qualifying the interpretation—e.g., total overdose mortality is the sum of drug-specific counts, but substitution may also affect non-fatal outcomes or involve cross-state spillovers. If feasible, estimate the implied mortality change conditional on the dose-response coefficients (with confidence intervals) to avoid overinterpreting noisy point estimates.

7. **Improve transparency on data and treatment coding:** The appendix notes that NV’s 14-day limit and TX’s 10-day limit are in an “other” category, but their treatment status appears omitted from the main discussion. Clarify whether these states are simply excluded, treated as “weak treatment,” or grouped with a particular dose. Also, the summary statistics mention 40 treated states, yet the text refers to 39 or “around 40.” Provide a table in the appendix listing each state, treatment year, and limit stringency to aid replication and to highlight that distinctions such as “initial prescription” vs. “opioid-naive” matter.

8. **Consider supplemental analyses with alternative outcomes:** Since ICD-10 categories overlap, multiple-substance deaths could affect the interpretation of drug-specific rates. If possible, examine deaths where synthetic opioids are the underlying cause (rather than contributing) or use combinations (e.g., synthetic opioid-with-heroin). Alternatively, explore hospitalization or emergency department data to see whether non-fatal substitution mirrors the fatal ones. These supplementary analyses would further validate that the stringency-synthetic link is not an artifact of coding.

By addressing these points, the paper would substantially strengthen its contribution. The topic is important, the data rich, and the methods appropriate, but the central heterogeneity claim needs clearer presentation and more rigorous validation before the policy implications can be confidently drawn.
