## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
This paper evaluates the impact of state laws capping initial opioid prescriptions (3–7 day limits) on overdose mortality. It finds that while the aggregate effect is a null, this hides a dangerous "Goldilocks" problem: moderate 7-day limits reduce prescription deaths without side effects, but strict 3-day limits trigger a massive substitution to illicit fentanyl, leading to a net increase in mortality. Busy economists should care because this identifies a specific "tipping point" where supply-side public health interventions become counterproductive.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this very well. The first paragraph uses a specific anecdote (Florida) to ground the policy, and the second paragraph clearly states the "substitution" vs. "reduction" tension.
**The Pitch the Paper Should Have:** The current pitch is excellent. It already moves from the specific policy to the broader economic phenomenon (the non-linear elasticity of substitution). No rewrite needed.

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
The paper’s contribution is identifying that the substitution from legal to illicit markets is not a binary response to supply shocks, but a "dose-response" threshold effect where only sufficiently aggressive restrictions trigger lethal substitution.

- **Differentiation:** It differentiates itself from Alpert et al. (2018) and Evans et al. (2019) by moving beyond "reformulation" (which was a one-time shock) to a graduated policy menu (3, 5, 7 days).
- **Framing:** It is framed as a question about the WORLD (how do users react to varying intensities of supply contraction?).
- **Explanation:** A smart economist would say: "It’s a paper showing that if you cut opioid prescriptions too sharply—specifically to 3 days—you kill more people via fentanyl than you save from pills, but 7-day limits are a safe middle ground."
- **Bigger Contribution:** To make this even bigger, the paper needs to bridge the gap between "mortality" and "behavior." It currently lacks the intermediate step: do 3-day limits actually create more "unfilled" demand or higher "search costs" than 7-day limits? 

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
- **Neighbors:** Alpert et al. (2018) on substitution; Buchmueller and Carey (2018) on PDMPs; Ruhm (2019) on the "three waves" of the crisis.
- **Positioning:** It builds on Alpert et al. by introducing a "stringency" dimension. It acts as a bridge between the PDMP literature (which often finds success) and the OxyContin reformulation literature (which finds disaster).
- **Narrow/Broad:** It is currently a Health/Labor paper. It could be broader by framing itself as a contribution to the "Economics of Prohibition" or "Regulating Sin Goods with Black Market Substitutes."
- **Unexpected Connection:** It should speak to the **Industrial Organization** of drug markets. Why is the 3-day mark the breaking point? Does it align with the typical "bridge" time a user can wait before withdrawal symptoms force an illicit purchase?

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC
- **Setup:** States tried to stop the opioid crisis by capping prescription lengths.
- **Tension:** Shorter is "safer" for new users but "deadlier" for existing users who might be pushed to the street.
- **Resolution:** 7 days is the "Goldilocks" zone; 3 days is a policy overshoot that increases fentanyl deaths by 8.6 per 100,000.
- **Implications:** Policy must be calibrated to the elasticity of substitution; "more" restriction is not always "better" health.

**Evaluate:** The arc is strong. However, the "tension" is slightly undermined by the small sample of 3-day states (FL, TN, KY). The narrative needs to defend why these three states aren't just outliers.

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
- **The Lead Fact:** "Capping prescriptions at 3 days instead of 7 killed 8 times more people via fentanyl than it saved via pills."
- **Response:** People would definitely lean in. It’s a classic "unintended consequences" story that economists love.
- **Follow-up:** "Are those 3-day states just different in other ways?" (This is the "referee" question that the author needs to pre-emptively solve in the narrative).

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
- **Front-loading:** The "dose-response" (Table 4) is the paper. The aggregate null (Table 2) is a "teaser" but takes up too much real estate. I would move Table 4's results into the Introduction or a "Main Result" Figure 1.
- **Robustness:** The cocaine "placebo" (which shows a negative effect) is a red flag. It’s currently in the main text; it needs to be addressed more aggressively or moved to an appendix if it's truly noise.
- **Conclusion:** It's a bit brief. It should expand on the *welfare* trade-off (Value of a Statistical Life) of 1 Rx death vs 8.6 Fentanyl deaths.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The paper is currently a very good "Health Economics" paper. To be an AER paper, it needs to be a **"Market Design/Incentives"** paper.

**Single Most Impactful Advice:** The author needs to move from *estimating* an effect to *explaining the mechanism* of the 3-day vs. 7-day threshold. Why 3? Is it because the average surgical recovery is 4 days? If the author can show that the 3-day limit specifically "breaks" the legal recovery path for a standard medical procedure, the paper moves from a "policy evaluation" to a fundamental study of "Market Frictions and Substitution."
