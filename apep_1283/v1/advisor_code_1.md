**Summary**

The paper studies the impact of recent state-level prevailing wage repeals on the Black-to-White earnings ratio within construction, exploiting staggered treatment timing across six states and comparing them to 28 untreated states using QWI data. The TWFE specification finds a 3.2 percentage point widening of the racial earnings gap, while a subsector mechanism test shows uniform effects across both publicly and privately funded construction subsectors, which the author interprets as evidence of sector-wide wage-floor spillovers. Robustness exercises include CS estimators, wild-cluster bootstrap, placebo manufacturing sectors, and leave-one-out analyses.

**Essential Points**

1. **Credibility of TWFE point estimate in light of heterogeneous treatment effects.** The TWFE specification yields a sizable, significant effect, while the Callaway-Sant’Anna estimator produces a much smaller, insignificant estimate. The paper briefly attributes this to CS sensitivity with few cohorts, but does not reconcile the discrepancy or present cohort-specific ATTs to show that the TWFE result is not driven by imprecisely weighted negative weights or overlapping cohorts. I recommend reporting cohort-level ATT estimates (e.g., through Callaway-Sant’Anna output) and discussing whether later cohorts drive the TWFE effect. If substantial heterogeneity exists, the TWFE estimate may not be a reliable average treatment effect.

2. **Parallel trends and event-study evidence are underdeveloped.** The paper mentions noise in long-horizon pre-period coefficients and relies on proximate quarters for credibility, but the provided appendix indicates a rejected joint pre-trend test. Without showing estimated event-study plots or further diagnostics, it remains unclear whether differential pre-trends or anticipation drive results. The authors should present the full event-study (with confidence intervals), report the joint F-test statistic and how much is due to distant lags, and explore alternative specifications (e.g., trimming early pre-periods, adding state-specific linear trends) to demonstrate robustness of parallel trends.

3. **Mechanism interpretation needs stronger support beyond non-significant subgroup differences.** The argument that effects are uniform across subsectors is based on point estimates that are similar in magnitude but imprecise; the triple-difference interaction is not only insignificant but also estimated with wide standard errors. This undermines the claim that the effect is “uniform” rather than simply not precisely estimated. To substantiate the spillover-floor claim, the authors should (a) increase precision with e.g., interacting treatment with a continuous measure of public funding intensity, (b) discuss why standard errors remain large despite pooling (maybe due to clustering by state), and (c) consider additional outcomes such as wage levels or employment shares by race within subsectors to triangulate the mechanism. Without this, the evidence for spillovers versus direct coverage remains suggestive rather than compelling.

If these concerns are not addressed, the paper’s identification and mechanism claims are not yet convincing, and I would recommend revision.

**Suggestions**

1. **Present comprehensive cohort-specific and dynamic estimates.** Include plots/tables showing Callaway-Sant’Anna group-time ATTs and Sun-Abraham event-study coefficients with confidence intervals for each cohort. This will help readers assess whether TWFE weights obscure heterogeneity or whether certain cohorts (e.g., Arkansas or Indiana) drive the aggregate result. Consider aggregating CS ATTs into an overall estimate using appropriate weighting (as in Callaway & Sant’Anna) and comparing that to TWFE, so readers understand the difference.

2. **Strengthen pre-trend diagnostics.** Display the event-study graph, ideally with both TWFE and interaction-weighted estimates, and overlay confidence intervals. Report the joint F-test statistic, p-value, and explain why distant lags (10+ quarters) are believed to reflect level differences rather than trend violations. A robustness check involving state-specific linear or quadratic time trends would bolster confidence that omitted trends do not confound the results. Alternatively, use synthetic control-type diagnostics or restrict the sample to states with similar pre-treatment dynamics.

3. **Explain the role of right-to-work (RTW) and other concurrent policies more clearly.** While dropping KY and WV lends support, the remaining four states still differ in RTW timing and other labor-market policies. Provide a table listing treatment timing alongside RTW status and any other relevant policy changes (e.g., state GDP shocks), and, if feasible, include controls that vary at the state-quarter level (e.g., construction employment growth, total construction spending, or unionization proxies) to show these do not drive results. Another option is to interact a post-repeal indicator with RTW status to test whether concurrent RTW legislation amplifies the effect.

4. **Clarify the outcome measurement and weighting.** The paper constructs the B/W earnings ratio from average monthly earnings, but QWI suppresses values with low employment. Discuss how missing cells are handled, whether the ratio is weighted by employment, and whether trimming or winsorizing occurs. Also, given the focus on the Black-to-White ratio, consider reassurance that changes in the ratio are not driven by extreme values in either numerator or denominator (e.g., due to outliers when employment is low). If appropriate, provide descriptive plots of average earnings by race over time for treated versus control states.

5. **Expand robustness suite with alternative control groups.** The manufacturing placebo is helpful, but it still relies on the same set of states. Consider using a permutation approach where you randomly assign repeal dates to control states to show that the observed effect is unlikely under a null of no reform. Another useful check is to restrict the control group to a subset of states more similar to the treated ones (e.g., in terms of construction employment share or racial composition) to verify that the result is not sensitive to the composition of the untreated sample.

6. **Deepen discussion of economic magnitude and policy implications.** The paper translates the 0.032 ratio change into a $130 monthly differential, but readers might benefit from standardization (e.g., effect relative to within-state SDs or to average wage gaps). Additionally, since TWFE and CS estimates differ, provide a confidence interval or range for the policy-relevant effect size. Discuss whether a 4 percentage point change in the ratio is meaningful for policy debates on prevailing wage, perhaps by comparing it to the effect of past prevailing wage repeals or other wage policies.

7. **Address potential general equilibrium concerns.** The manufacturing placebo shows an imprecise but non-zero point estimate. Discuss whether this could reflect broader economic shifts in treated states (e.g., statewide construction boom/bust) that affect manufacturing through supply chains or labor mobility. If feasible, control for state-level economic indicators (GDP growth, construction employment) to show the construction-specific effect remains.

8. **Transparency and reproducibility.** It would be helpful to include the code or pseudo-code used to process the QWI data, especially the construction of the B/W ratio and the creation of PostRepeal indicators. While the appendix details variable definitions, more specifics about aggregation (e.g., how industries are merged) and treatment timing (exact quarters) would assist replication. If space is limited, consider citing a replication repository or providing a supplementary appendix with code snippets.

By addressing these suggestions, the authors can better justify their identification, provide clearer evidence for the spillover mechanism, and bolster confidence that the results reflect causal effects of prevailing wage repeal on racial inequality in construction.
