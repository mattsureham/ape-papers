## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether repealing state prevailing wage laws widened racial wage inequality in construction. Using the 2015–2018 repeal wave, it argues that removing a public-project wage floor reduced Black workers’ relative earnings not only on directly covered work, but across the broader construction sector, suggesting that labor-market institutions can shape racial inequality through spillovers.

Why should a busy economist care? Because the paper is trying to connect two important conversations that are usually separate: the economics of labor standards in construction, and the broader question of how wage-setting institutions affect racial inequality.

**Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Not quite. The current opening is competent, but it reads like a field-paper introduction: policy background, then literature intersection, then design. It does not lead with the big-world question sharply enough, and it does not tell the reader early enough why prevailing wage laws are a useful setting for learning something general about labor-market institutions and racial inequality. It also introduces the design before the stakes are fully established.

**What the first two paragraphs should say instead:**  
Something like:

> Labor-market institutions may shape racial inequality not only by raising wages on covered jobs, but by setting pay norms that spill over through entire sectors. Construction is a particularly important test case: public contracts often impose prevailing wage requirements tied to union-scale pay, and Black workers have historically been concentrated in parts of the industry where those wage floors may matter most. When states repeal those laws, do racial earnings gaps widen?
>
> This paper studies six state prevailing wage repeals between 2015 and 2018. I show that repeals are associated with a widening of the Black–White earnings gap in construction, with similar patterns in heavily public and more private construction subsectors. The broader point is that targeted wage-setting institutions may affect racial inequality at the sector level, not just on the jobs they directly regulate.

That is the pitch. It is about the world, not about “I use staggered DiD to study X.”

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution in one sentence:**  
The paper’s core contribution is to argue that repealing prevailing wage laws widened the Black–White earnings gap in construction and that this effect appears to extend beyond directly covered public projects, implying sector-wide spillovers from a targeted wage floor.

### Evaluation

**Is this clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?**  
Only partially. The introduction names relevant literatures, but the differentiation is still fuzzy. Right now the contribution sounds like: “another paper showing wage institutions affect inequality, in a new setting.” That is not enough for AER unless the setting is uniquely revealing or the result is genuinely surprising.

The paper needs to separate itself more explicitly from at least four adjacent strands:

1. **Prevailing wage repeal papers** focused on construction costs, employment, or overall wages.
2. **Minimum wage and racial inequality papers** showing wage floors compress racial gaps.
3. **Union decline and inequality papers** showing collective bargaining matters for distribution.
4. **Older prevailing wage/race papers** especially those that found the opposite sign.

The most promising differentiation is not “first causal evidence” per se. That phrase is too easy to challenge and not very interesting editorially. The stronger differentiation is: **prevailing wage laws are a narrow, sector-specific institution, yet their repeal appears to affect racial inequality across the whole construction labor market, not only on covered projects.**

**Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a literature gap?**  
Mixed, but still too literature-gap oriented. The strongest version is a world question: *Do sectoral wage-setting institutions compress racial inequality beyond the jobs they directly regulate?* The current paper too often says “this connects two literatures” or “this contributes to modern staggered DiD applications,” which is much weaker.

**Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what’s new?**  
They could probably say: “It’s a paper on prevailing wage repeal and the Black–White earnings gap in construction.”  
That is decent, but they might also say: “It’s another staggered DiD paper on state labor policy.” That is the danger. The “spillover floor” idea is the only thing that elevates it above a standard state-policy DiD, but that idea is not yet developed with enough force or confidence to carry the whole paper.

**What would make this contribution bigger? Be specific.**  
Three possibilities:

1. **Bigger outcome concept:** Move beyond the Black/White earnings ratio alone to a broader decomposition of racial inequality in construction—earnings, employment, entry/exit, and perhaps occupational composition. Right now the paper risks being read as a ratio paper with one outcome.
2. **Sharper mechanism:** If the paper wants to own “spillover floor,” it needs stronger evidence that this is actually a sectoral spillover and not just a common state-level labor-market shock. The public/private subsector comparison is a start, but it is not yet decisive enough to support a big conceptual claim.
3. **Bigger framing:** Position prevailing wage repeal as a test of **sectoral labor standards** as a neglected determinant of racial inequality—somewhere between minimum wages and unions. That is larger than “prevailing wages matter in construction.”

If the author could expand the evidence to show that repeals altered racial wage dispersion within construction while leaving adjacent sectors or occupations less affected, the paper would feel much more like a general result about institutions rather than a niche policy study.

At present, oddly, both:

- **Too narrowly** in that it can read like a construction-policy paper for a small labor/public audience.
- **Too broadly** in that it occasionally gestures at huge claims about racial inequality and labor institutions without enough evidence to support that scale.

The right position is: **a labor paper about how sectoral wage-setting institutions shape racial inequality, using prevailing wage repeal as the test case.**

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?

A few missing or underdeveloped conversations:

- **Sectoral bargaining / wage boards / labor standards as meso-level institutions.** This could help elevate the framing beyond construction policy.

Not fully. The current conversation is: prevailing wage literature + wage institution literature. That is sensible, but a more impactful conversation is:

**What can targeted labor standards embedded in public procurement tell us about the role of institutions in shaping racial inequality?**

That is a more unexpected and more AER-worthy bridge.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Before this paper, we know that wage-setting institutions like minimum wages and unions can compress inequality, including racial inequality. We also know prevailing wage laws regulate pay on public construction projects, but the literature has mostly asked whether they raise costs or affect employment.

### Tension
What is unclear is whether a narrow, sector-specific wage floor does anything meaningful for racial inequality—and whether its effects are confined to covered public work or spill over to the broader construction labor market. There is also a direct tension with older work suggesting repeal may have narrowed racial gaps.

### Resolution
The paper finds that repeal is associated with a wider Black–White earnings gap in construction, and that similar patterns appear in public and more private subsectors, which the paper interprets as evidence of sector-wide spillovers.

### Implications
If true, the implication is that public procurement rules can function as broader labor-market institutions, affecting racial inequality beyond the directly regulated jobs. That matters for labor policy, racial inequality, and how economists think about sector-specific wage floors.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the ingredients, but the arc is not fully disciplined. Right now it is a bit of a tug-of-war between three stories:

1. prevailing wage repeal affects racial inequality;
2. the effect spills over beyond covered work;
3. the paper is methodologically cautious because one estimator is small and insignificant.

Those three can coexist, but the paper has not decided which is the headline. My sense is:

- **Headline:** sectoral labor standards shape racial inequality.
- **Setting:** prevailing wage repeal in construction.
- **Mechanism claim:** likely spillovers, but suggestive.
- **Methodological caution:** present, but not the story.

At the moment, the paper sometimes feels like a set of results searching for a hierarchy. The author needs to decide what the paper is *really* about. The best story is not “TWFE is significant but CS isn’t.” That is a referee issue, not a top-journal narrative. The story should be about institutions and racial inequality, with the estimator discrepancy treated as discipline and caution.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?

I would say:  
“States that repealed prevailing wage laws saw the Black–White earnings gap in construction widen—and not just in the public-project segment where the law directly applied.”

That is the only line likely to make people lean in.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

Some would lean in, especially labor, public, and inequality economists. But many would wait to hear whether the effect is really persuasive and whether the spillover evidence is strong. The current paper gets attention for the question more than for the conclusiveness of the answer.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Probably one of these:

- “Is that really prevailing wage, or just broader anti-union politics in those states?”
- “Why should the effect show up in private construction too?”
- “How do you reconcile this with the older paper finding the opposite?”
- “Does this reflect wage changes or composition/selection out of construction?”

Those are actually helpful, because they reveal what the paper should be organized around.

### If findings are modest: is the result itself interesting?

Yes, but only if sold correctly. The problem is not that the result is null-ish in one specification; the problem is that the paper currently wants to claim both an important substantive finding and a high level of caution. That is fine, but the author must be explicit: *the paper’s value lies in putting a new and important possibility on the table—namely, that sectoral labor standards can materially shape racial inequality through spillovers—even if magnitude remains uncertain.*

If framed that way, the paper does not feel like a failed experiment. If framed as “we causally estimate the effect and it is X,” the internal tension in the estimates weakens the story.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

Bluntly: in current form, this is **not yet an AER paper**. The gap is not primarily expositional. It is mainly a combination of **framing**, **scope**, and **ambition**.

### What is the core problem?

**Mostly an ambition/framing problem.**

The paper has a potentially important idea, but it is packaged as a careful policy-evaluation paper on one state-law domain. That makes it feel like a solid field journal submission rather than a general-interest breakthrough.

There is also a **scope problem**: one earnings-ratio outcome and one mechanism contrast are probably not enough to support the paper’s broadest claims. If the argument is that prevailing wage laws are a sector-wide institution shaping racial inequality, the evidence base should be broader than it currently is.

And there is some **novelty risk**: absent the spillover angle and the reversal relative to older findings, this can look like another paper showing labor standards matter for distribution.

### What is the gap between this paper and one that would excite the top 10 people in the field?

A version that excites them would do at least two of the following much better:

1. **Make the big conceptual point unavoidable:** prevailing wage laws are not just a construction-cost policy; they are a form of sectoral wage governance with racial distributional consequences.
2. **Show more than one margin:** earnings, employment/composition, maybe occupation or subsector sorting, ideally something demonstrating whether the ratio change is wage compression vs workforce reshuffling.
3. **Exploit the historical contrast:** why do modern repeals look different from older repeal episodes? That could be a paper-defining angle if developed.
4. **Sharpen the spillover claim:** give readers more reason to believe private-sector construction moved because of public wage floors, not because treated states trended differently.

### Single most impactful piece of advice

**Reframe the paper around a bigger question—whether sector-specific labor standards embedded in public procurement compress racial inequality beyond directly covered jobs—and organize every section around that claim rather than around the existence of a statistically significant DiD estimate.**
