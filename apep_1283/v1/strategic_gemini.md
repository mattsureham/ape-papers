## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
This paper examines how the recent wave of state-level prevailing wage repeals (2015–2018) affected the racial earnings gap in the construction industry. It finds that removing these union-scale wage floors on public projects widened the Black-to-White earnings gap by approximately 4 percentage points, with the effect spilling over into private-sector construction subsectors not directly covered by the law.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this clearly. However, the first two paragraphs are a bit "literary." To hit the AER bar, the pitch should more aggressively emphasize the **institutional reversal**: for decades, economists thought these laws *hurt* minority workers (the "Davis-Bacon as Jim Crow" narrative). This paper should lead with the fact that it documents a modern regime where these floors are actually critical for racial equity.

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**The Contribution:** This paper provides the first causal evidence that modern prevailing wage laws act as a "spillover floor" that compresses racial earnings inequality across both public and private construction markets.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It is well-differentiated. It moves beyond the "cost/employment" focus of the old prevailing wage literature (Kessler & Katz) and adds a sectoral dimension to the "wage floor" literature (Derenoncourt & Montialoux).
*   **Framing:** It is currently a mix. It frames the question around the "world" (racial gaps), but the mechanism section gets bogged down in "literature gaps."
*   **The "Smart Economist" Test:** A reader would say: "It's a staggered DiD showing that when you kill prevailing wage, Black workers lose more than White workers, even in the private sector."
*   **Bigger Contribution:** To make this "AER Big," the author needs to prove the *why* of the spillover. Is it a "lighthouse effect" (norms) or a "threat effect" (union bargaining)? The paper hints at this but doesn't isolate it.

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
*   **Neighbors:** Kessler & Katz (2001) on old repeals; Derenoncourt & Montialoux (2021) on minimum wage; Farber et al. (2021) on unions.
*   **Strategy:** The paper correctly identifies Kessler & Katz as the "foil." It should "attack" the outdatedness of the 1990s findings to justify why we need a new paper on this in 2024.
*   **Missing Conversations:** The paper needs to speak more to the **Political Economy** literature. Why did these six states repeal *now*? If repeal is endogenous to a decline in Black political power or union density, the DiD is in trouble.
*   **Unexpected Connection:** It should connect to the **Urban/Regional** literature on "Local Labor Markets." If the spillover happens because workers jump between public and private sites, this is a paper about labor mobility.

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC
*   **Setup:** Prevailing wage laws have governed public construction for nearly a century, long debated as either protective or discriminatory.
*   **Tension:** A sudden wave of repeals in the 2010s provides a natural experiment, but the "spillover" to the private sector creates a puzzle: why does a law about *public* roads change wages for *private* homebuilders?
*   **Resolution:** Repeal widens racial gaps everywhere because the public floor anchored the entire regional industry’s wage scale.
*   **Implications:** Labor market institutions that appear narrow in scope can have broad, equity-enhancing "shadow" effects.

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
*   **The Lead Fact:** "Repealing a law that only applies to public bridges actually lowered the relative pay of Black workers building private houses."
*   **Reaction:** People lean in. It's counter-intuitive.
*   **The Follow-up:** "Wait, if the effect is the same in the private sector, how do we know it's the law and not just a general anti-labor shift in those states?" (This is the paper's "weakest" point that needs a stronger defense).

---

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
*   **Front-loading:** The discrepancy between TWFE and Callaway-Sant’Anna (CS) is handled too defensively. Move the CS discussion into a dedicated "Econometric Robustness" section. Don't let the technical "noise" of the CS estimator muddy the waters in the Introduction.
*   **Mechanism:** Section 5.2 is the most interesting part but the shortest. It should be expanded with more descriptive data on worker flows.
*   **Placebo:** The manufacturing placebo is a "must-have," but it's currently a bit "tacked on." Move it earlier to build confidence before the subsector analysis.

---

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
**The Gap:** The paper is currently "competent and interesting," but the econometric tension (TWFE vs. CS) threatens to make it a "paper about DiD methods" rather than a "paper about the world." 

**Single Most Impactful Advice:** Shift the focus from "Look at my p-values" to **"Look at the composition of the workforce."** If the author can show *who* left the industry after repeal (e.g., did high-tenure Black union workers exit?), the narrative becomes an AER-level story about institutional erosion and path dependency.
