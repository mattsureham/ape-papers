**Idea Fidelity**

The paper largely adheres to the original idea. It exploits the QWI data to link firm dynamics and age-specific outcomes with the contiguous county-pair design popularized by Dube, Lester, and Reich (2010). The data description, outcomes (FrmJbGn, FrmJbLs, HirA, Sep, Emp by age), and the identification strategy follow the manifest. The paper could improve fidelity by clarifying the precise sample construction and variation that support the 1,310 county pairs—how many pairs have “high-contrast” separations, the role of within-state temporal variation, and whether the QWI panel includes all 8 age groups and the specified industries in the regressions presented. Also, the original idea emphasized testing the key assumption through placebo borders and pre-trends; the paper only briefly references robustness checks, so these should be elaborated (see “Essential Points” below).

**Summary**

This paper reexamines the canonical minimum-wage “employment null” within the border county-pair framework by exploiting firm-level job creation/destruction measures and age-specific hiring flows from the QWI dataset. While replicating the negligible effect on aggregate employment, the paper finds that minimum wage increases elevate firm job destruction without offsetting increases in creation, reduce net job creation, and depress hiring and separation rates—especially in restaurants—suggesting increased churning despite a static employment stock. Age decompositions hint at modest substitution away from the youngest workers, while manufacturing serves as a placebo sector with no discernible dynamics.

**Essential Points**

1. **Credibility of the identifying assumption and pre-trends.** The paper invokes the contiguous county-pair design but does not present any direct evidence on the parallel-path assumption for the new outcomes (firm dynamics, hires/separations by age). The manifest explicitly recommends placebo borders and pre-trend checks. Without them, it is hard to rule out other state-level shocks driving the firm-dynamics estimates. The authors need to (a) show event-study plots or tests indicating no pre-trend for job creation/destruction, hires, separations, and age-specific employment, and (b) report estimates using placebo borders (pairs with no minimum wage differential or with differential locked at zero) or using manufacturing as a falsification with pre-trend tests. These diagnostics are essential for trust in the new margins.

2. **Interpretation of net job creation decline in light of employment null.** The paper argues that net job creation falls while the employment level remains flat. However, net job creation is a flow rate and could decline while employment remains stable if the employment base is adjusting (e.g., if employment changes slowly with flows). The authors should clarify how the estimated decline in net job creation translates into long-run employment (or why it doesn’t), possibly by simulating steady-state employment from the estimated flows or showing that employment levels in high-wage counties indeed stagnate despite changing flows. They should also reconcile their findings with the canonical null by discussing whether their time frame is sufficient for flows to modify the stock.

3. **Treatment variation and functional form.** The regression uses log minimum wage and assumes within-pair log-linear effects. Given the presence of large absolute gaps (up to $9) and varying baseline wages, it would be helpful to show robustness to using levels or interacting the treatment with base wage levels. Additionally, the paper does not explain how the wide range of wage differentials is exploited—do they rely mostly on large differences, or on within-state increases? Presenting the distribution of treatment variation (e.g., histogram of differences or a figure showing how the high-side vs low-side wages evolve) would inform whether the estimates are driven by cross-sectional discontinuities or by time-series changes. This matters because the identification in \cref{eq:main} rests on within-pair temporal variation, while much of the variation cited in the manifest is cross-sectional. Without clarity, the interpretation of β remains ambiguous.

If further critical issues are needed, I would recommend rejecting, but the above three are manageable with revision.

**Suggestions**

1. **Pre-trend and placebo diagnostics for new outcomes.** Provide event-study graphs and/or regressions that plot the coefficients on leads and lags of the minimum wage for the firm dynamics, hires, separations, and the age-specific employment outcomes. Doing so will demonstrate whether the identifying assumption holds when using the new margins rather than just employment. Consider estimating specifications where the treatment is the interaction of the minimum wage change with time relative to the change, as in a stacked event-study. Complement this with a placebo exercise where you assign fictitious wage differentials to county pairs that never experience real differentials; the absence of effects will strengthen the causal claim.

2. **Clarify treatment variation and the leverage of the data.** Include a figure or table summarizing the distribution of minimum wage gaps across pairs and over time—perhaps binning by size of the gap (e.g., $0–2, $2–4, etc.) and showing how many quarters/pairs fall into each bin. If the identifying variation comes primarily from pairs with large gaps, report results stratified by gap size to show whether the firm dynamics estimates are sensitive to the sample. Also, explain how many pairs experience within-pair variation (i.e., both states changing minimum wages). If the majority of the variation is cross-sectional (always-high vs always-low), the fixed effects model relies largely on comparing neighboring counties with permanently different wages, which may violate parallel trends; acknowledging and, if necessary, addressing that concern would be helpful.

3. **Expand robustness checks and heterogeneity analysis.** The paper mentions high-differential pairs and exclusion of the COVID period; include corresponding tables in the main text or appendix (or at least the key coefficients), and discuss whether the point estimates and significance change materially. For the industry decomposition, add the net job creation (creation minus destruction) coefficient by industry to show whether restaurants’ large increases in both margins translate into net effects or are offsetting. For age-specific results, consider presenting firm-dynamics regressions by age group (e.g., job creation/destruction rates for young vs prime-age workers) if the data allow, to show that the churning occurs preferentially among the youngest workers.

4. **Address measurement error and noise infusion in QWI.** The QWI applies noise infusion for disclosure avoidance; this can induce classical measurement error that attenuates coefficients. The paper briefly notes this in the appendix, but quantifying the likely bias or showing that larger sample sizes mitigate it would reassure readers. One option is to compare results using aggregated (e.g., all-ages) data to those using disaggregated cells; if coefficients are similar, it suggests the noise is not driving patterns. Alternatively, cite existing work that validates the QWI for job flows in similar contexts.

5. **Interpretation and policy implications.** The discussion is compelling, but could be strengthened by more explicitly distinguishing between short-run and long-run effects. For instance, the decrease in hiring and separations suggests reduced turnover, yet the increased destruction implies displaced workers. Are these marginal workers likely to be re-employed across the border or in other firms? Consider discussing how the QWI data might be linked to LEHD longitudinal worker records (as hinted in the discussion) or how future research could examine the reemployment duration of displaced workers. This would ground the welfare implications more concretely.

6. **Presentation clarity.** Some tables (especially \cref{tab:firm_dynamics}) could benefit from scaling coefficients to interpretable units (e.g., effect per 10% minimum wage increase) in addition to reporting the log specification. Likewise, clarifying whether the reported rates are per 100 workers or percentages would help readers interpret magnitudes. Also, provide in the text the baseline mean of each outcome alongside the coefficient so readers can gauge economic significance without recreating it from the summary table.

7. **Address potential spillovers or sorting across borders.** The border design assumes limited cross-border commuting or migration induced by minimum wage differences. While county pairs are meant to capture shared labor markets, large wage gaps may incentivize south-side residents to seek work north of the border. Discuss whether such spillovers could bias results and, if possible, provide evidence on whether employment (or flows) on the low-wage side rise when the high-wage side increases wages, which could reflect worker relocation or cross-border employment. Alternatively, note that such spillovers would likely bias results toward finding no effect on the high-wage side, so the observed rise in job destruction is conservative.

By elaborating on these suggestions, the paper will provide a more complete and convincing account of how minimum wages reshape firm-level dynamics even when aggregate employment appears unaffected.
