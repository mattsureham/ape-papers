## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
The paper uses a border-county design to show that while minimum wages have a near-zero effect on total employment levels, they mask significant changes in firm-level dynamics: specifically, an increase in job destruction and a decline in net job creation. By decomposing the "employment null," the author argues that minimum wages act as a catalyst for "creative destruction," accelerating the exit of low-productivity firms and cooling labor market fluidity through reduced hiring and separation rates.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this pitch excellently. The opening paragraph uses the Washington-Idaho contrast to ground the reader, and the second paragraph immediately identifies the "black box" of the employment null as the central puzzle. It is a textbook example of a clear AER-style introduction.

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**Contribution:** The paper provides the first U.S. evidence that the minimum wage "employment null" is the result of offsetting firm-level dynamics (accelerated destruction and slowed creation) rather than an absence of economic impact.

- **Differentiation:** It differentiates itself well from Dube et al. (2010)—which it uses for the design—and Meer and West (2016)—which it improves upon by using the more granular border-pair identification rather than state-level variation.
- **Framing:** It is framed as a question about the **WORLD** (How do firms actually adjust?) rather than just a gap in literature.
- **Clarity:** A smart economist would easily explain this as "the paper that shows the null result is actually a lot of churn under the surface."
- **Bigger Contribution:** To make it even bigger, the paper needs to more explicitly link the "churn" to **productivity**. If low-productivity "vintage" firms are exiting (Aaronson et al. 2018), is aggregate productivity in high-minimum-wage counties actually rising?

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
The paper sits at the intersection of the Minimum Wage debate and the Job Reallocation literature.

- **Neighbors:** Card & Krueger (1994), Dube et al. (2010), Meer & West (2016), and Aaronson et al. (2018).
- **Positioning:** It **synthesizes** the empirical "null" findings of Dube/Card with the "dynamic" theoretical predictions of Aaronson and the German empirical evidence of Dustmann et al. (2022). It essentially reconciles two warring camps.
- **Missing Literature:** It could benefit from a deeper connection to the **Monopsony** literature (Manning 2021). If hiring falls but separations also fall, is that purely "creative destruction," or is it a reduction in the "vacancy-posting" intensity of monopsonistic firms?

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC
- **Setup:** A 30-year consensus that minimum wages don't kill jobs in the aggregate.
- **Tension:** If wages go up and employment doesn't fall, something *must* be changing within the firm's cost structure or the market's composition. Why haven't we seen it?
- **Resolution:** We haven't seen it because we were looking at stocks (total jobs) rather than flows (creation/destruction). Minimum wages accelerate firm exit and "cool" the market.
- **Implications:** The "null" isn't a free lunch; it involves a fundamental restructuring of the local economy toward more stable, higher-productivity, but less fluid employers.

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong. It feels like a detective story solving the mystery of the "Missing Disemployment Effect."

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
- **The Fact:** "When you raise the minimum wage, the number of jobs stays the same, but the rate at which jobs are destroyed by failing firms jumps by nearly 10%."
- **Reaction:** People lean in. It challenges the "nothing happens" narrative of the pro-minimum wage camp without necessarily supporting the "mass unemployment" narrative of the anti-camp.
- **Follow-up:** "If firms are exiting faster, who is winning? The big chains?" This leads directly to the paper's industry results (restaurants vs. manufacturing).

---

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
- **Front-loading:** The paper is well-structured. Table 3 (the "Anatomy of the Null") is the money shot and appears at exactly the right time (page 8).
- **Buried Treasure:** The finding that *both* hiring and separations fall (Table 3, cols 4-5) is a major result for labor market fluidity that deserves a bit more prominence in the abstract/intro.
- **Appendix:** The "Standardized Effect Sizes" (Table 6) are helpful but could stay in the appendix to keep the main narrative focused on the raw semi-elasticities.

---

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The paper is highly competent and strategically positioned. To move from "very good" to "AER Slam Dunk," it needs to push harder on **Mechanisms and Welfare**.

- **The Gap:** Currently, it tells us *what* happened (churning), but the "why" (is it productivity-led?) is mostly inferred from the Aaronson et al. reference.
- **Ambition:** Does this churn lead to a "better" economy (higher productivity) or just a "stagnant" one (lower fluidity)?
- **The Single Best Piece of Advice:** Use the industry results to build a more explicit test of the "Putty-Clay" hypothesis—show that the exit is indeed concentrated among the oldest/lowest-wage establishments within the restaurant sector to prove this is "Creative Destruction" and not just "Destruction."
