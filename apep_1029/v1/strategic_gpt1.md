## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple and important question: when firm-size regulations become more burdensome at bright-line thresholds, do firms distort their size to stay below them? Using UK firm data, the paper finds essentially no bunching at the main Companies Act thresholds and argues that this is because the UK’s “two-of-three for two years” classification rule creates enough slack that crossing an employee threshold alone does not trigger meaningful avoidance behavior.

A busy economist should care because the paper is trying to turn a familiar result on its head. Much of the literature says size-dependent regulation distorts firm growth; this paper’s claim is that regulatory design can neutralize those distortions, and that multidimensional thresholds may matter as much as the level of the thresholds themselves.

**Does the paper articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?** Not quite. The current opening spends too much time listing regulations and then immediately drops into the bunching literature. The paper’s real hook is not “there are thresholds” but “here is a setting where one would expect bunching, yet there is none, and the absence is informative because of a distinctive institutional design.” That should come much earlier and more sharply.

### The pitch the paper should have

“Economists have learned to expect bunching wherever regulation creates sharp firm-size thresholds. But the UK provides a revealing counterexample: despite substantial compliance costs at 10, 50, and 250 employees, firms do not cluster below these cutoffs. The reason, I argue, is that UK company law does not use a simple employee threshold. Reclassification depends on exceeding two of three criteria—employees, turnover, and assets—for two consecutive years, so firms can grow along one dimension without immediately triggering regulation. This paper shows that threshold design can largely eliminate the size distortions that the literature has documented elsewhere.”

That is the story. The current introduction eventually gets there, but too slowly and too defensively.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:**  
The paper claims that UK firms do not bunch at salient regulatory size thresholds, and that this absence of bunching is explained by the UK’s multidimensional “two-of-three” classification rule, which softens effective threshold incentives.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest papers?
Partially, but not yet cleanly enough.

The paper differentiates itself from:
- Garicano, Lelarge, and Van Reenen on France’s 50-worker threshold,
- Schivardi and Torrini / Gourio and Roys on size-dependent regulation and firm size distributions,
- Devereux et al. on UK tax-threshold bunching,
- Chetty/Kleven bunching methodology papers.

But the differentiation is still mostly mechanical: “they find bunching, I don’t.” The stronger differentiation would be conceptual: **prior papers mostly study unidimensional thresholds; this paper studies a multidimensional threshold system and argues that this changes the incentive geometry.** That is more interesting than “here is another bunching test in another country.”

### World question or literature gap?
Right now it is split between the two. The stronger version is clearly a **world question**:

- Weak framing: “There is little evidence on UK Companies Act thresholds.”
- Strong framing: “Do all size-dependent regulations distort firm growth, or can institutional design make thresholds effectively nonbinding?”

The latter is much more AER-facing.

### Could a smart economist explain what is new?
At present, a smart economist might still summarize it as: “It’s a bunching paper showing no bunching at UK firm-size thresholds.” That is not enough.

The introduction needs to make the novelty impossible to miss:
1. The UK thresholds are economically salient.
2. Standard models predict bunching.
3. Yet there is none.
4. The institutional feature that likely explains this is multidimensional, persistent classification.
5. Therefore, the paper is about **regulatory architecture**, not just bunching.

### What would make the contribution bigger?
Several possibilities, in descending order of importance:

1. **Directly show the mechanism, not just infer it.**  
   The paper’s big idea is the two-of-three rule, but the evidence for it is indirect. Strategically, the paper gets much bigger if it can show that firms near the employee thresholds are typically far from the financial thresholds, or that the relevant margin is joint crossing rather than employee count alone. Right now the mechanism is plausible but under-demonstrated.

2. **Reframe the outcome from “bunching/no bunching” to “effective threshold exposure.”**  
   If the authors can characterize how many firms near 50 employees are actually at risk of reclassification under the joint rule, that would elevate the paper from a density exercise to a substantive statement about the incidence of regulation.

3. **Use the 2025 threshold reform as more than a footnote.**  
   The reform is potentially the natural comparative lever that turns the paper from descriptive to conceptually sharper: when financial thresholds rise but employee thresholds do not, the slack widens. Even if post-period data are not yet available, the paper could build the framing around this and motivate the UK as a case study in how design changes alter distortion incentives.

4. **Compare more explicitly to France-style unidimensional thresholds.**  
   Not just rhetorically. The paper would benefit from a simple conceptual figure or calibration showing why a one-dimensional threshold should produce bunching but a two-of-three/two-year rule should not.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

### Closest neighbors
The obvious closest neighbors are:

1. **Garicano, Lelarge, and Van Reenen (2016)** on the French 50-worker threshold.
2. **Schivardi and Torrini (2008)** on employment protection and firm size in Italy.
3. **Gourio and Roys (2014)** on size-dependent policies and distortions in France.
4. **Devereux, Liu, and Loretz (2014)** or adjacent UK bunching/tax-threshold papers.
5. **Chetty et al. (2011)** and **Kleven and Waseem (2013)** for bunching methodology and interpretation.

Also relevant in spirit:
- **Guner, Ventura, and Xu (2008)** on size-dependent policies and misallocation.
- **Hsieh and Klenow (2009)** as the broader misallocation umbrella.

### How should it position itself?
It should **build on and qualify** the firm-size-distortion literature, not attack it.

The right message is:
- The literature has shown that sharp size-based rules can distort firm behavior.
- But these effects depend on the institutional mapping from observed size to regulatory status.
- The UK shows that when thresholds are multidimensional and persistent, bright employee cutoffs may be poor proxies for true regulatory exposure.

That is a useful refinement, not a takedown.

### Too narrow or too broad?
Currently it is oddly both:
- **Too narrow empirically** because much of the actual evidence is concentrated around the 10-employee threshold with limited leverage at 50 and 250.
- **Too broad rhetorically** because it wants to make big claims about regulatory design and misallocation writ large.

This creates a mismatch. The paper gestures toward a broad design lesson but has one strong result, one weak suggestive result, and one essentially unavailable threshold.

### What literature does it seem unaware of?
It should speak more to:
- **Regulatory design / multidimensional screening / nonconvex policy rules**, not just bunching.
- **Salience and inattention** in regulation, because one alternative explanation for non-bunching is that firms do not optimize around these cutoffs.
- **Public finance design of kinks/notches versus softened rules**, since the paper’s conceptual point is about how the policy schedule maps into behavior.
- Potentially **accounting/legal-institutional literature** on audit exemptions and firm reporting thresholds.

### Is it having the right conversation?
Not yet fully. Right now the conversation is “why no bunching in UK firms?” The more interesting conversation is:

**When do size-dependent regulations generate distortions, and how does threshold design determine that?**

That is the conversation that reaches beyond the niche audience of bunching-and-firm-size readers.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
The world before this paper: economists believe that size-dependent regulation often causes firms to distort size and bunch below thresholds. This has become almost a stylized fact in several settings.

### Tension
The UK has salient size thresholds with real compliance consequences, so one should expect similar avoidance. Yet the UK’s legal design is unusual: classification depends on multiple dimensions and persistence over time, which may blunt incentives on any single margin.

### Resolution
The paper finds no bunching at the main threshold it can study cleanly, and no obvious aggregate discontinuities at the higher thresholds. It interprets this as evidence that the UK’s multidimensional rule creates “compliance slack.”

### Implications
Thresholds need not be distortionary if they are designed in a way that weakens the payoff to manipulating one observable margin. The policy lesson is that institutional architecture may substitute for raising thresholds or reducing compliance burdens.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
It has the bones of one, but it is still too much a **collection of bunching exercises plus a post hoc mechanism story**.

The story it should be telling is not:
- Here are three thresholds,
- here are some densities,
- here is a null,
- maybe the two-of-three rule explains it.

It should instead be:
1. Standard intuition predicts bunching.
2. But that intuition assumes threshold status is tied tightly to one margin.
3. In the UK, threshold status is deliberately decoupled from employee count.
4. Therefore the absence of bunching is not surprising ex post; it is the key substantive finding.
5. This changes how we think about size-dependent regulation.

In other words, the paper should elevate the **design principle** above the empirical inventory.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?
“I expected UK firms to bunch below 50 employees to avoid audits and related rules. They don’t.”

That is the right opening fact. Better still:
“Despite sizable compliance costs, UK firms do not cluster below major regulatory employee thresholds.”

### Would people lean in?
Some would, but only if the follow-up comes immediately:
“Why not?”

If the answer is “because the rule is actually multidimensional and takes two years,” then yes, many economists would lean in. If the answer is just “we ran the bunching test and found zero,” interest will fade quickly.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Almost certainly:
- “Are firms really exposed at those thresholds, or is employee count the wrong running variable?”
- Or: “Can you show that the two-of-three rule is the reason, not inattention or data limitations?”

That is the paper’s central strategic vulnerability. The audience will grant that the null is interesting only if the paper can persuade them that the mechanism is not speculative.

### Are the null findings interesting?
Yes, but only conditionally. Null results can be very valuable when they overturn a strong prior and have enough power to rule out economically meaningful effects. The paper understands this and leans into power, which is good.

But a null result is not self-executing. The paper must make the case that:
1. We had reason to expect bunching.
2. The data are good enough, at least for the main threshold, to reject that expectation.
3. The institutional explanation is more than hand-waving.

Right now it makes (1) and (2) better than (3).

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

   The first paragraph should pose the broad question and the surprising answer. The list of reporting rules can come after the hook.

7. **Conclusion should end on the broad implication, not on bureaucratic detail.**  
   The current conclusion is decent, but it could be sharper: “the design of thresholds matters for whether regulation distorts growth.” That is the sentence readers should remember.

### Is the good stuff front-loaded?
Moderately, but not enough. The paper reveals the main null and proposed mechanism in the introduction, which is good, but the first two pages still read too much like a standard bunching paper. The design insight should dominate much earlier.

### Are results buried?

### Is the conclusion adding value?
Some value, yes. It articulates the architecture point. But the paper needs that voice earlier, not just in the conclusion.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

Frankly, the gap is substantial, but it is not hopeless.

### What is the main gap?
Mostly a **scope-plus-framing problem**, with some **novelty risk**.

- **Framing problem:** The paper is still framed as a bunching null result, when it wants to be a paper about the design of regulation.
- **Scope problem:** The mechanism is under-evidenced relative to the ambition of the claim.
- **Novelty problem:** “No bunching in the UK” by itself is not enough for AER; “multidimensional threshold design suppresses distortion” could be.
- **Ambition problem:** The paper is competent, but currently safe. It observes a null and offers a plausible explanation. An AER paper would need to go further in showing that the explanation is the substantive contribution.

### What would excite the top 10 people in this field?
A version of this paper that does one of the following:
1. **Directly measures firms’ distance from all three criteria and shows that most firms near the employee threshold are not truly at risk of reclassification.**
2. **Builds a clear conceptual model of bunching under multidimensional versus unidimensional thresholds and maps the UK/French contrast onto that model.**
3. **Exploits a reform or institutional variation that shifts compliance slack and traces the response.**

Without one of these, the paper risks being read as “interesting null, plausible story.”

### Single most impactful advice
**If the paper could change one thing, it should make the mechanism observable: show that the absence of bunching reflects low effective exposure created by the two-of-three rule, rather than simply inferring that from a null density result.**

That is the pivot from an intelligent null-result paper to a potentially field-shaping design paper.
