**MEMORANDUM**

**TO:** Editorial Board
**FROM:** Editor, American Economic Review
**RE:** Strategic Positioning of "The Compliance Slack: Why UK Firms Don’t Bunch"

---

### 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
The paper asks why UK firms do not distort their size to stay below regulatory thresholds (like mandatory audits or labor rules), whereas firms in France and Italy show massive "bunching" at similar rungs. It finds that the UK's "two-of-three" rule—requiring firms to exceed two out of three criteria (employees, revenue, assets) for two years—creates enough "compliance slack" to eliminate the allocative distortions usually caused by size-dependent policies.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this well, but it buries the "two-of-three" rule in the third paragraph. The first two paragraphs focus on the "what" (the null result). To be an AER paper, the "why" needs to be the lead.

**The Pitch the Paper Should Have:**
"Size-dependent regulations are notorious for creating 'notches' that stifle firm growth, yet the UK's rigid regulatory rungs show zero evidence of such distortion. We show that this is not due to low compliance costs, but to a specific multi-dimensional 'two-of-three' design that decouples marginal hiring from regulatory status. This suggests that the *architecture* of regulation is as important as its *stringency* in preventing misallocation."

---

### 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**The Contribution:** The paper identifies multi-dimensional eligibility criteria as a policy mechanism that eliminates the bunching and misallocation typically caused by size-dependent thresholds.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It differentiates itself effectively from Garicano et al. (2016) by offering a "solution" to the problem Garicano identifies.
*   **World vs. Literature:** It currently sits somewhere in between. It needs to lean harder into the "World" aspect—how should governments design growth-friendly regulations?
*   **Clarity:** A smart economist would get it. It’s not "another DiD"; it’s a "why-is-the-dog-not-barking" paper.
*   **Bigger Contribution:** To make this "AER big," the author needs to formalize the "compliance slack" concept. Can they show that firms specifically utilize the other two dimensions (turnover/assets) to grow their headcount? Without the joint distribution of all three variables, the mechanism remains a very plausible hypothesis rather than a proven fact.

---

### 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
*   **Neighbors:** Garicano et al. (2016), Gourio and Roys (2014), Hsieh and Klenow (2009), and Kleven (2016).
*   **Positioning:** It should position itself as the *antithesis* to the "French labor law" literature. It needs to move from "we found a null" to "we found a superior institutional design."
*   **Missing Literature:** It should speak to the **optimal tax** literature on multi-dimensional screening (e.g., Kopczuk). If the government wants to regulate "Big" firms, why is a 3-dimensional vector better than a 1-dimensional scalar?
*   **Unexpected Connection:** Connecting this to **robust mechanism design** would be powerful. Is the "two-of-three" rule a way to make regulation robust to idiosyncratic shocks in any single dimension?

---

### 4. NARRATIVE ARC
*   **Setup:** Size-dependent regulations (SDRs) hurt growth.
*   **Tension:** The UK has expensive SDRs, but the firm-size distribution is perfectly smooth. Why?
*   **Resolution:** The "two-of-three" rule provides a buffer zone (slack) that single-dimension thresholds lack.
*   **Implications:** Bureaucratic complexity (adding more dimensions) can actually *increase* economic efficiency by smoothing notches into gradients.

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong. It’s a detective story. The "resolution" (the two-of-three rule) is the most satisfying part.

---

### 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
*   **Lead Fact:** "The UK's 50-employee audit threshold—which costs firms £20k—causes exactly zero bunching."
*   **Reaction:** People lean in. The "bunching" literature has become a bit of a "me-too" field; finding a definitive *lack* of bunching in a high-stakes environment is counter-intuitive and exciting.
*   **Follow-up:** "Does this mean we should add *more* criteria to every regulation to stop people from gaming the system?"

---

### 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
*   **Front-loading:** The "two-of-three" rule needs to be in the first 500 words.
*   **Data:** The Companies House microdata (8,927 firms) is actually quite small compared to the NOMIS universe. The author needs to be careful not to over-claim based on the 10-employee threshold if the 50-employee threshold (which is more consequential) is too sparse for the micro-estimator.
*   **Appendix:** The standardized effect size table (Table 6) is a bit "formulaic." Move the "two-of-three" logic from the discussion to a formal (even if simple) conceptual model in the main text.

---

### 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The gap is **Mechanism Evidence**. Currently, the paper says: "There is no bunching. There is a two-of-three rule. Therefore, the rule stops the bunching."

To get into the AER, the author needs to show firms *using* the slack. Do we see firms with 60 employees (above the threshold) intentionally keeping their turnover at £10.1M (below the threshold)? The paper admits it cannot observe the joint distribution. 

**Single biggest piece of advice:** Find a way to proxy the other two dimensions (Turnover/Assets) in the microdata, even for a sub-sample, to prove that firms are "sitting" in the slack zone.

---

### STRATEGIC ASSESSMENT

*   **Current framing quality:** Adequate (needs to lead with the mechanism).
*   **Contribution clarity:** Crystal clear.
*   **Literature positioning:** Well-positioned but needs more theory on multi-dimensional notches.
*   **Narrative arc:** Strong.
*   **AER distance:** Medium.
*   **Single biggest improvement:** Provide empirical evidence of firms "occupying" the slack (the joint distribution of employees and financial rungs).
