## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple and potentially important question: when disasters strike, does recovery depend on whether the media is paying attention? Using floods in India, the paper tests whether competing major news events worsen post-flood recovery by crowding disasters out of the news, and finds no detectable effect—suggesting that a rights-based domestic safety net may be less attention-dependent than discretionary aid.

Why should a busy economist care? Because the bigger issue is not India per se; it is whether institutional design can make social protection robust to salience and politics. If true, that is a broad claim about state capacity, accountability, and the design of disaster response.

### Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?
Not quite. The current opening is lively, but it gets pulled quickly into Eisensee-Stromberg replication logic. That makes the paper sound like a narrow extension—“does the media-salience result travel to India?”—rather than a broader paper about whether entitlement-based safety nets can decouple recovery from attention cycles.

The first two paragraphs should state the world question more bluntly:

> Disasters do not only test infrastructure; they test institutions. A central concern in political economy is that public response depends on salience: when the media pays attention, governments act; when attention shifts elsewhere, victims are neglected. If that is true, then disaster recovery may be systematically weaker when competing events crowd disasters out of the news.  
>   
> But not all relief systems are equally vulnerable to attention shocks. Discretionary aid may depend on politicians noticing and choosing to respond, whereas rights-based social programs are designed to operate automatically when households need them. This paper asks whether India’s MGNREGA—one of the world’s largest employment guarantees—insulates flood recovery from media distraction. If it does, that would suggest a broader lesson: institutional design can make disaster response less dependent on what happens to be on the front page.

That is the pitch the paper should have.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution
The paper’s contribution is to test whether media distraction worsens disaster recovery in a setting with a demand-driven legal entitlement, and to show that, unlike in the foreign-aid context, competing news does not detectably amplify the economic damage from floods in India.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?
Only partially. The paper names Eisensee-Stromberg, Stromberg, and some MGNREGA papers, but the differentiation is still too implicit. Right now, the contribution reads as “apply a salience interaction design in a new setting.” That is not enough. It needs to say much more sharply:

- Eisensee-Stromberg: salience affects **discretionary foreign relief**
- This paper: tests whether salience matters when relief is mediated by a **domestic entitlement**
- Existing MGNREGA papers: study labor markets, poverty, smoothing, or resilience—not whether the program is **attention-proof**
- Thus the novelty is not India, floods, or DiD; it is the **interaction between media salience and institutional design**

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a gap in a literature?
It is split between the two, and too often falls back to literature-gap language. The stronger version is a world question: **Are some state capacities robust to salience shocks while others are not?** That is an AER-type question. “We extend Eisensee-Stromberg to India” is not.

### Could a smart economist explain to a colleague what’s new?
At present, many would say: “It’s another reduced-form paper on disasters and media salience, but with a null.” That is a problem.

What you want them to say is: “It asks whether entitlement-based safety nets are less dependent on media attention than discretionary relief, using floods in India, and the answer appears to be yes—or at least not obviously no.”

### What would make this contribution bigger?
Most importantly: connect the outcome to the institutional mechanism more directly.

Specific ways to make it bigger:
1. **Observe MGNREGA response directly**: person-days, expenditures, work activation, payment delays, flood-control works.  
   - This would transform the paper from “nightlights null under weak salience variation” into “the entitlement itself does/does not respond to salience.”
2. **Use actual media coverage data** rather than a coarse annual hand-coded index.  
   - Newspaper/article counts, TV archives, GDELT, LexisNexis, language press heterogeneity, or district/state-specific coverage.
3. **Frame the comparison more explicitly as institutional design**.  
   - Compare MGNREGA-type settings to more discretionary relief channels if possible.
4. **Consider outcomes closer to recovery policy** than annual nightlights alone.  
   - Relief timing, road restoration, migration, employment, wage payments, crop losses, or public works activation.

The biggest gain would come from direct evidence on whether the safety net itself is salience-proof.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
In many settings, disaster response is shaped by media attention. When the news is crowded, governments and donors may respond less.

### Tension
But India’s MGNREGA is not supposed to work that way. It is a legal entitlement, not a discretionary transfer. So does media salience still matter, or can institutional design insulate recovery from attention shocks?

### Resolution
The paper finds that floods reduce future nightlights, but competing major news events do not detectably worsen that effect.

### Implications
The implication, if one buys the interpretation, is that rights-based safety nets may be more robust to salience and political distraction than discretionary aid systems.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
It has the bones of one, yes. But it is still too close to being a collection of regression tables with a null centerpiece. The story is there, but the paper does not consistently commit to it.

The main problem is that the paper oscillates between:
- “Here is a broad institutional-design question,” and
- “Here is a limited replication-style salience test with low power”

The first is interesting; the second is modest. The reader keeps getting pulled back to the modest version.

### What story should it be telling?
Not “Do Olympics crowd out flood coverage?”  
Rather: **Can automatic, rights-based state capacity substitute for media-driven accountability in disaster response?**

That is the story.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?
“Its basic claim is that floods clearly hurt local economies in India, but those losses do not get worse when the media is distracted by major global events—suggesting that a legal entitlement may make recovery less dependent on attention.”

That is reasonably good dinner-party material. Not electrifying, but respectable.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
They would lean in initially because the premise is strong: media salience versus entitlement design is a nice contrast. But they would immediately ask whether the paper really measures salience and really observes the safety net response. If the answer is “annual hand-coded event index and nightlights,” some will start drifting.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Almost certainly: **“Do you actually see MGNREGA ramping up after floods, and does that response vary with media coverage?”**

That is the right question, and the paper currently cannot answer it. That is the clearest sign of the gap between current and top-journal versions.

### If the findings are null or modest, is the null itself interesting?
Potentially yes. A null can be very interesting here because it speaks to whether institutions can mute a politically important salience mechanism. But the paper must do more work to show this is an informative null rather than a low-powered one.

Right now it is commendably honest about power limitations, but that honesty also weakens the punch. The paper says, in effect, “we find no effect, but can’t rule out moderately sized effects, and our salience measure is coarse.” That sounds less like “important null” and more like “inconclusive first pass.”

To make the null valuable, the paper needs either:
- better salience measurement, or
- direct mechanism evidence showing the entitlement response itself is stable across attention shocks.
