**Idea Fidelity**

The paper departs significantly from the idea manifest. The original project proposed exploiting GDELT-derived variation in state-level flood coverage, instrumented by global competing-news volume, to trace a media → MGNREGA → nightlights recovery chain. The submitted draft instead estimates a reduced-form interaction between state-level monsoon rainfall anomalies and an annual competing-news index directly on district-level nightlights growth. There is no use of GDELT data, no first-stage on flood coverage, no MGNREGA expenditure or person-day outcomes, and the three-stage causal chain described in the manifest is absent. As a result, the submitted paper does not pursue the manifest’s key identification strategy or data sources, nor does it test whether media salience affects MGNREGA activity before looking at recovery.

**Summary**

This paper asks whether competing global media events—Olympics, World Cups, pandemics—attenuate the economic recovery from monsoon floods in Indian districts by crowding out media attention to domestic disasters. Using district-level VIIRS nightlights growth, state-level rainfall anomalies, and a hand-coded competing-news index for 2013–2020, the author finds that while unusual rainfall reduces next-year nightlights growth, its interaction with the competing-news index is statistically indistinguishable from zero (and in fact has the “wrong” sign). The paper interprets this null as evidence that MGNREGA’s demand-driven, rights-based design insulates it from media salience effects that matter for discretionary foreign aid.

**Essential Points**

1. **Identification of the Salience Channel Is Absent.** The paper’s stated research question is about whether media salience mediates disaster recovery by influencing MGNREGA responses. Yet the empirical specification never measures MGNREGA activity, nor does it link media coverage with bureaucratic behavior. Rainfall × competing-news is regressed directly on nightlights growth, so the paper cannot distinguish whether an interaction effect is missing because media do not affect MGNREGA or because the entire pathway is too noisy to detect. To make progress, the authors need to engage the mechanism: include MGNREGA outcomes (person-days, expenditure, flood works) and show that competing news predicts variation in those inputs conditional on rainfall. Without that step, the argument that “MGNREGA is salience-proof” remains untested.

2. **Internal Validity of the Competing-News Index Interaction.** The identification relies on the assumption that global media events are orthogonal to Indian rainfall shocks and that their interaction captures variation in reporting salience. However, the competing-news index varies only at the annual level (8 years) and is perfectly collinear with many omitted macro trends. Year fixed effects soak up any main effect, leaving only cross-district heterogeneity in rainfall to deliver identification. With only eight years and one “treatment” year (2016 for Olympics), the interaction is effectively identified off a handful of observations. The authors acknowledge low power but do not provide plausibly exogenous variation beyond the index (no event timing, no state-level heterogeneity). At the very least, the authors should (a) present the raw time series of the index and an event calendar to demonstrate variation, (b) consider alternative constructions (weekly/monthly, use GDELT counts to exploit within-year variation), and (c) run placebo tests showing that the index does not correlate with other district-level shocks unrelated to floods. Otherwise it is unclear whether the null reflects a true absence of salience effects or simply the lack of credible variation.

3. **Mismatch Between Question and Empirical Strategy.** The paper’s framing emphasizes the solicitation of work under MGNREGA and whether media pressure is necessary for districts to deliver assistance. Yet the empirical approach treats rainfall × competing-news as if it were a reduced-form “salience shock,” ignoring the key policy channel (MGNREGA operations) and the timing (MGNREGA response occurs within months of the floods, but the outcome is next-year nightlights growth). Without accounting for other sources of recovery (e.g., federal relief funds, informal insurance, private investment), the paper attributes all flood effects on nightlights to MGNREGA. This undermines the claim that MGNREGA alone shields districts from salience gaps. To credibly link the institutional mechanism to the outcome, the authors should either (a) narrow their inference to “does global media competition moderate the total flood effect on nightlights?” or (b) explicitly model the MGNREGA channel through intermediate outcomes or structural assumptions.

Given these issues, the paper as currently written cannot robustly answer the stated research question. Addressing them requires substantial revision.

**Suggestions**

1. **Measure the Mechanism Directly.** Incorporate MGNREGA microdata (MIS) on person-days, expenditures, or sanctioned works, ideally at the district-month level, and investigate whether these inputs respond to rainfall and competing-news interference. A three-stage approach (competing news → MGNREGA activity → nightlights) would align the empirical strategy with the manifest and allow the authors to test whether the right-hand side of the policy chain is “salience-proof.” If the data permit, exploit the timing of works approval, financial releases, or wage payments to examine when media salience might matter most.

2. **Improve the Salience Measure.** The current competing-news index is annual and hand-coded, which limits variation. Consider re-constructing it from GDELT or other high-frequency media data (page counts, topic tags) to capture monthly/daily fluctuations in flood coverage and competing events. Doing so could enable a more granular interaction with rainfall timing and provide stronger first-stage variation. If GDELT is not feasible, at least provide a transparent documentation of the index construction (events included, coding rules, scaling) and explore alternative codings (e.g., number of high-profile events per year, ratio of flood to non-flood articles). Additionally, consider a placebo test where you interact competing news with a non-disaster outcome to show the interaction is specific to flood-related damage.

3. **Address Power and Inference Concerns.** With only eight years and 29 state clusters, the interaction coefficient is imprecisely estimated. Consider (a) reporting wild-cluster bootstrap p-values, (b) estimating randomization inference confidence intervals under plausible nulls, or (c) aggregating to state-year units to reduce noise and explore whether the absence of effects persists. If power remains low, the paper should frame the null more carefully—as inconclusive rather than evidence for salience-proof entitlements—and explore the minimum detectable effect given the design.

4. **Clarify the Counterfactual and Mechanism Language.** The framing emphasizes that media salience crowds out attention to disasters, but it is not clear whether the paper is testing the effect of media on victims’ access to MGNREGA or simply on aggregate recovery. Explicitly define the salience gap in terms of observable counters: e.g., “Given flood severity, does higher competing-news intensity delay MGNREGA registration/implementation, thereby reducing economic recovery?” Distinguish this from a story in which global events independently affect economic outcomes (e.g., through remittances) and ensure the empirical tests can separate the two.

5. **Use Complementary Outcomes.** Nightlights, while useful, capture a noisy signal. Supplement the analysis with other outcomes—perhaps consumption proxies from CMIE, satellite-derived vegetation indices (NDVI), or infrastructure indicators—that may respond more quickly or directly to disaster relief. If those outcomes show similar patterns (or lack thereof), it strengthens the inference. Alternatively, show that the same interaction also does not predict unrelated outcomes, supporting the specificity of the null.

6. **Document the Competing-News Index and Provide Descriptive Evidence.** Include a table listing the years/events in the index, along with summary statistics for how often they occurred during the monsoon months. Plot monsoon rainfall, competing-news intensity, and nightlights growth over time to give the reader intuition about the data. Demonstrating that 2016 (Olympics) or 2014 (World Cup) actually differed in “news pressure” would make the interpretation of the interaction more credible.

7. **Explain the Positive Interaction Coefficients.** The point estimates on Rain × Competing are consistently positive, opposite to the hypothesized salience gap. Consider exploring whether there is a theoretical reason for this sign (e.g., global events divert attention not only from disasters but also from political opposition to MGNREGA implementation, leading to less political interference). Alternatively, use sign information to argue that the null is not merely due to lack of power but potentially due to compensatory mechanisms (e.g., bureaucrats may ramp up relief when global events draw attention away). Discussing this would demonstrate engagement with the unexpected result.

8. **Re-run with More Granular Time Fixed Effects.** Since the competing-news index varies only annual and the outcome is annual, consider using month or quarter fixed effects (if matching monthly rainfall variation) or estimating with detrended outcomes to ensure the identification does not rely on within-year timing absorbing all variation. Alternatively, exploit the fact that monsoon season occurs mid-year—interact the index with the deviation in rainfall for the relevant season only, to avoid contamination from other months.

9. **Engage with Alternative Mechanisms.** The paper claims that MGNREGA is automatic, but flood recovery could be driven by private insurance, remittances, or central disaster funds. Provide evidence that these other channels do not respond to competing news (e.g., by showing no correlation between the index and central disaster relief allocations). This strengthens the attribution to MGNREGA and justifies the policy conclusion.

10. **Refine the Narrative on Null Results.** Null findings can be publishable, but only if they are convincing. The discussion should acknowledge the design’s limitations, emphasize the bounds the study places on the salience effect (e.g., “we can reject effects larger than X”), and be careful not to overclaim (“MGNREGA is salience-proof” is too strong if power is limited). If the data allow, compute minimum detectable effect sizes relative to observed rainfall impacts to contextualize the null.

Addressing these points would align the empirical strategy with the research question, strengthen the identification of the salience channel, and allow the paper to make a more definitive claim about the role of media coverage in disaster relief within India’s policy regime.
