## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether media attention matters for disaster recovery even when relief is delivered through a domestic, rules-based safety net rather than discretionary foreign aid. Using floods in India and variation in competing global news events, it argues that while monsoon floods reduce subsequent economic activity, there is little evidence that news crowd-out worsens recovery—suggesting that entitlement-based public programs may be less vulnerable to salience distortions than foreign aid.

A busy economist should care because the paper is trying to answer a broader question than “do floods hurt?”: when does political attention shape public response, and can institutional design insulate welfare provision from the media cycle?

### Does the paper articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Reasonably, but not sharply enough. The introduction opens with a vivid anecdote and gets to Eisensee-Strömberg quickly, which is good. But it still reads a bit like “let me test their hypothesis in India” rather than “here is a larger institutional question about when salience matters.” The paper’s strongest angle is not India per se, nor nightlights per se, nor even floods per se. It is: **does a rights-based domestic safety net break the link between media attention and disaster response?**

The first two paragraphs should say that much more directly.

### The pitch the paper should have

“Do governments help disaster victims less when the media is distracted? Existing work shows that competing news reduces discretionary foreign aid, but it is not clear whether the same salience logic applies when relief is delivered through a domestic entitlement rather than political discretion. We study monsoon floods in India and find that although floods depress local economic activity, recovery is not detectably worse in years when global events crowd out attention—suggesting that rules-based safety nets may be less sensitive to media salience than discretionary aid.”

That is the pitch. Everything else should serve it.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### Contribution in one sentence

The paper’s contribution is to test whether media crowd-out affects disaster recovery in a setting where relief is mediated by a domestic entitlement program rather than discretionary foreign aid, and to report that it does not detectably do so.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest papers?

Only partially. The paper differentiates itself from Eisensee and Strömberg by institutional setting, but not yet in a way that feels fully decisive or important. Right now the contribution risks sounding like: “same salience idea, different country, null result.” That is not enough for AER unless the institutional contrast is made central and consequential.

The paper needs to be explicit that the comparison is not just geographic. It is between:

- **discretionary aid** versus **legal entitlement**
- **elite response to media pressure** versus **demand-driven delivery**
- **attention-sensitive policy** versus **attention-robust policy**

That is a real conceptual distinction. The paper should lean into it.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a gap in a literature?

It is mixed, but too often still sounds like a literature-extension paper. The stronger framing is a world question:

- **When does media salience distort public service delivery after shocks?**
- **Can institutional design attenuate politics-of-attention failures?**

That is much stronger than “we extend Eisensee-Strömberg to India.”

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the intro?

At present, maybe, but with some hesitation. The likely summary is: “It’s a paper on media salience and flood recovery in India using a DiD-style interaction, and they mostly find no effect.” That is not a great sign.

What you want them to say is: “It tests whether rights-based social insurance is insulated from media attention, unlike discretionary aid, and the answer appears to be yes—or at least much more so.”

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Most importantly: **observe the mechanism directly.** Right now the paper infers MGNREGA’s insulation from the absence of a nightlights interaction. That is one bridge too far. To make the contribution bigger, the paper should show not just recovery outcomes but whether MGNREGA itself responds to floods differently in high- vs low-attention periods.

Concretely, the most valuable expansion would be:

- district-level **MGNREGA person-days, expenditures, works sanctioned, payment delays, flood-control works**
- perhaps also **disaster relief fund flows**
- if possible, **actual media coverage measures** rather than a coarse annual event index

Short of that, a stronger comparison would help:
- compare districts or states with stronger versus weaker MGNREGA implementation capacity
- compare entitlement-based relief channels to discretionary relief channels within India
- compare flood response outcomes that are more tightly linked to public action than aggregate nightlights

As written, the paper’s outcome is too downstream and the mechanism too inferred.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

The world before this paper: we know that media attention can shape public responses to disasters, especially when aid is discretionary. We also know India has a massive demand-driven employment guarantee that often functions as de facto shock insurance.

### Tension

If media salience is generally important for disaster response, then even a domestic safety net may fail when attention is elsewhere. But if entitlement design truly constrains discretion, then recovery should be less sensitive to the news cycle. That is the key tension.

### Resolution

Floods reduce later district economic activity, but recovery is not detectably worse in years with more competing news. The paper interprets this as evidence that media crowd-out is not a first-order distortion in this institutional setting.

### Implications

Potentially, institutional design matters: rules-based, demand-driven social programs may be more robust to the politics of attention than discretionary aid. That has implications for social insurance design, disaster policy, and how economists think about media accountability.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the ingredients, but the arc is only partially realized. Too much of the paper reads like a collection of regressions around an interaction term, with the discussion retrofitting the bigger point. The story should be:

1. Attention matters when discretion matters.  
2. India’s employment guarantee is designed precisely to reduce discretion.  
3. Therefore India provides a test of whether institutional form moderates salience effects.  
4. We find flood damage, but no strong evidence of salience amplification.  
5. Hence the political economy of attention may depend on whether relief is rules-based or discretionary.

That is a coherent arc. The current draft is close, but it needs to be more disciplined and less apologetic.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?

“Ideally, disaster relief shouldn’t depend on whether the Olympics are on TV. This paper suggests that in India’s rural employment guarantee system, it may not.”

That is the best dinner-party hook. Not the coefficient. Not the nightlights construction. The institutional contrast.

### Would people lean in?

Some would. But only if presented that way. If you say, “We interact monsoon rainfall with a competing-news index and find an insignificant interaction in district nightlights,” they will absolutely reach for their phones.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Immediately: **“Do you show this through MGNREGA itself?”**  
And then: **“How do you know the null is institutional and not just measurement?”**

Those are exactly the questions the paper currently cannot answer fully. That is the core strategic vulnerability.

### Is the null result itself interesting?

Potentially yes, but only if the paper makes the case that the null is informative about institutional resilience, not merely absence of evidence. Right now it is halfway there. The paper commendably does not overclaim, but it also undersells the conditions under which a null can matter.

For a null to be AER-interesting, one of two things must be true:

1. it overturns a strong prior in a setting where the test is sharp, or  
2. it reveals a meaningful boundary condition for an important mechanism.

This paper should lean heavily on (2). The null is interesting only if framed as a boundary condition on salience politics. Otherwise it feels like a failed extension.
