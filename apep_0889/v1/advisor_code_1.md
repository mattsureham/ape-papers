**Summary**

This paper studies whether the loss of USPS postal-service establishments, driven by the RAOI closures between 2011 and 2017, affected county-level presidential election turnout. Using a staggered DiD framework (Callaway-Sant’Anna with never-treated controls), TWFE specifications, and several robustness and sensitivity analyses, the author finds that the statistically significant negative TWFE estimate is driven by pre-existing convergence in turnout between larger (treated) and smaller (control) counties, yielding a null result once pre-trends are accounted for. The paper concludes that closing these post offices had at most a small effect (minimum detectable effect ≈ 1.6%) on turnout, suggesting voters adapt through alternative channels.

**Essential Points**

1. **Identification concerns remain unresolved.** While the paper correctly identifies the parallel trends violation, the identification strategy cannot credibly isolate the causal effect of post office loss: the treated counties differ systematically in secular turnout trends (and possibly in unobserved determinants such as urbanization, population decline, or mail usage) from never-treated counties throughout the extensive pre-treatment period (2000–2012). The Rambachan-Roth sensitivity analysis indicates that even small trend deviations can drive the results to include zero, so the “null” finding is fundamentally limited by non-parallel trends rather than by statistical power. This undermines the paper’s central causal claim: unless the author can find a better control group, alternative normalization, or a credible instrument for closures, the paper should treat the estimates as descriptive of divergent turnout trends rather than causal impacts of postal infrastructure.

2. **Measurement of treatment lacks granularity and may be noisy.** The QCEW establishment count is an imperfect proxy for post office closures; it cannot distinguish between the loss of a central main office and the gain of a contract station, nor does it capture postal-processing delays or service standard changes. Because the treatment definition is simply “lost ≥1 establishment,” counties where a closure was offset by a new branch count as untreated, and counties that lost multiple offices are pooled with those losing one. This measurement error likely attenuates the treatment effect and may explain heterogeneity by urbanicity (larger counties may lose multiple establishments). A more refined treatment (e.g., tracking individual closure events, latitude/longitude distance to nearest office, or post office type) is necessary to support the infrastructure narrative.

3. **Null result lacks balance with alternative outcomes and mechanisms.** The paper focuses solely on total votes, log votes, and standardized effect sizes, but the research question includes voter registration and mail-ballot usage. Without examining registration rates, mail-in shares, or the availability of alternative postal channels (e.g., drop boxes, early voting), the conclusion that democratic participation is unaffected is incomplete. Furthermore, the paper does not rule out offsetting policy responses (election administration changes) that could mask underlying infrastructure effects. The paper should either expand its outcomes or temper the claims to reflect the limited scope.

Given these issues, the paper currently cannot make a convincing causal claim; a rejection would be appropriate unless the authors substantially strengthen the identification strategy.

**Suggestions**

1. **Strengthen identification with richer counterfactuals or alternative variation.** Since the manifest envisaged using DFA service standard downgrades, the authors could return to those data, even if current elections lack post-treatment observations, to illustrate the planned future analysis or to leverage pre-treatment variation in downgrade intensity (if any). Alternatively, within RAOI, consider a refined event-study where control counties are matched on pre-treatment trends (synthetic control or local projections) or where treatment is defined using distance to the nearest closing post office. For example, instrumenting for closure exposure with plausibly exogenous characteristics (e.g., post offices targeted for RAOI based on historical mail volume and not on contemporary turnout) could help isolate causal effects. Another possibility is to exploit cross-border variation: counties just inside vs. outside RAOI closure polygons but with similar turnout histories.

2. **Improve treatment measurement and heterogeneity analysis.** Investigate whether the QCEW establishment counts correlate with actual closure events, distances to the nearest remaining post office, or closures recorded in USPS/PRC documents. If possible, use geocoded closure data to construct a more precise treatment indicator (e.g., whether the county lost a main post office or experienced a closure that increased travel distance by x miles). Alternatively, incorporate measures of service degradation (e.g., delivery standard downgrades using ZIP pair data) into the treatment intensity. Including heterogeneity analyses by the number of closures, distance to the nearest surviving office, or reliance on mail registration (e.g., states where mail registrations are more common) could help support the mechanism.

3. **Address the parallel trend rejection directly rather than only via sensitivity bounds.** The detection of pre-trend differences calls for more than bounding exercises. Consider re-estimating using methods robust to pre-trends (e.g., including county-specific time trends, matching treated and control counties on pre-treatment outcome paths, or differencing in first differences that focus on short windows immediately before and after closures). Presenting results from these alternative estimators would bolster confidence that the null result is not merely due to identification failure. If pre-trends persist, explicitly interpret the estimates as reflecting the continuation of demographic convergence rather than a causal effect of postal infrastructure and focus the contribution on documenting this divergence.

4. **Expand outcome space and substantiate mechanisms.** Incorporate additional outcomes promised in the manifest, such as voter registration rates, mail ballot usage, and turnout decomposition by mode (mail vs. in-person). If such data are unavailable for the full panel, use available subsets (e.g., states reporting mode in certain years) to explore whether mail-intensive processes were affected. Also, survey whether the affected counties implemented compensatory policies (drop boxes, early voting) by collecting descriptive evidence or using proxies (state-level reforms). Demonstrating that postal infrastructure is not binding only after ruling out plausible mechanisms strengthens the policy relevance of the null result.

5. **Reframe “null result” narrative and discuss power explicitly.** Currently, the paper leans on the minimum detectable effect, but the confidence intervals and pre-trend issues imply that the study may not identify causal effects even if they exist. Reframe the narrative to emphasize what can be learned: namely, that treated counties were on distinct trajectories, and any effect of postal closures must lie within a relatively narrow band (±1.6–2.5%). Avoid concluding definitively that closures have “no detectable effect”; instead, couch the contribution in terms of bounds, policy implications conditional on assumptions, and the need for better data to capture the post-DFA impact. Use the SDE table to clarify how meaningful such effects would be in practical terms and to guide future studies.

6. **Clarify policy implications with balanced discussion.** The current conclusion suggests the USPS DFA plan is unlikely to affect turnout, but this is speculative given that DFA affects mail processing (delivery speed) rather than access points. Tonality could be improved by highlighting the limitations: the study focuses on closures that predominantly impacted rural areas, whereas DFA affects national mail flow, and the null result is conditional on the assumption that the pre-existing convergence fully explains the post-treatment patterns. A more cautious tone, acknowledging that the study cannot adjudicate all aspects of “postal infrastructure as civic infrastructure,” would better align with the evidence presented.

In sum, the paper addresses an interesting question and collects relevant data, but to make a convincing contribution it must tighten the causal inference, enrich the treatment and outcome measurement, and present a more nuanced interpretation of the results.
