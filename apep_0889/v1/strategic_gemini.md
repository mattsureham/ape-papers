### 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
This paper asks whether the closure of hundreds of U.S. post offices under the Retail Access Optimization Initiative (RAOI) suppressed voter turnout in presidential elections. Using a staggered difference-in-differences design, the author finds that while a naive analysis suggests a 2.5% decline in votes, this result is entirely explained by pre-existing demographic trends, ultimately ruling out any substantial negative impact on democratic participation.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this clearly. The first two paragraphs effectively bridge the gap between "costs of voting" and "infrastructure" literatures. 

**The pitch the paper should have:** (The current pitch is strong, but to maximize AER appeal, it should emphasize the *informativeness of the null*): 
"Does the degradation of a centuries-old federal infrastructure network undermine the bedrock of democracy? By exploiting the largest wave of post office closures in modern U.S. history, I provide a precisely estimated null result that suggests the 'physical' state is no longer a binding constraint on political participation in a digital age."

---

### 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**Contribution:** The paper provides the first empirical estimate of how physical postal infrastructure affects voter turnout, finding that modern closures have no detectable effect on participation.

*   **Differentiated?** Yes. It moves beyond "polling place distance" (Cantoni, 2020) and "historical postal routes" (Verdier & Zenou, 2024) to look at the *modern removal* of federal presence.
*   **Question about the WORLD?** Yes. It asks a fundamental question about the "bindingness" of state infrastructure.
*   **What's new?** A smart economist would say: "It uses the USPS closures to show that voters are resilient to the loss of local federal touchpoints."
*   **Make it bigger?** To reach the AER, the paper needs more on the **mechanism of adaptation**. *Why* is the effect zero? Is it because of a shift to online registration or because these specific closures were in counties with high car ownership? Without the "why," it’s just a "well-done zero."

---

### 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
The paper sits at the intersection of **Political Economy (Voting Costs)** and **Urban/Public Economics (Infrastructure)**.
*   **Neighbors:** Brady & McNulty (2011) on polling places; Thompson et al. (2020) on Vote-by-Mail; Donaldson (2018) on infrastructure.
*   **Strategy:** It should **synthesize** these. It currently "speaks" to them, but it should frame itself as the "21st-century update" to the voting cost literature—showing that while physical distance mattered in 1950, it may be obsolete in 2024.
*   **Missing conversations:** The paper should connect to the literature on **"The Hollow State"** or the psychological effects of losing the only federal building in town (Social Capital).

### 4. NARRATIVE ARC
*   **Setup:** The USPS is in a fiscal death spiral, leading to mass closures.
*   **Tension:** Alarmists argue this will disenfranchise rural voters; however, we live in a digital world where the "post office" may be a relic.
*   **Resolution:** The closures happened, but turnout didn't budge. The "negative" effects found in earlier studies were just artifacts of pre-existing rural-urban convergence.
*   **Implications:** The physical footprint of the federal government can be consolidated without a "democracy tax."

**Evaluation:** The arc is clean. However, the "tension" could be heightened by highlighting the massive political outcry that accompanied these closures.

### 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
*   **Lead fact:** "Closing the only federal building in a rural county has zero impact on how many people vote."
*   **Reaction:** Moderate leaning-in. The "spurious 2.5% result" is a great hook for an audience of economists who love a good "identification gotcha."
*   **Follow-up:** "Does it affect *who* votes (older vs. younger) even if the total doesn't change?" (The paper needs to answer this to be AER-worthy).

### 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
*   **Front-load:** The "HonestDiD" sensitivity analysis (Table 4) is actually one of the most compelling parts because it explains *why* the naive result is wrong. Move this closer to the main results.
*   **Appendix:** The Bacon decomposition is standard now; it can stay in the appendix. 
*   **Expansion:** Section 6 (Discussion) is currently too speculative. It needs a "Subsample" or "Mechanism" table testing if the null holds for populations most reliant on mail (e.g., the elderly).

### 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The gap is **Ambition**. A "precise null" is hard to sell to the AER unless it fundamentally changes how we think about a theory. 

**Single biggest piece of advice:** Move beyond "total votes" and look at **voter composition**. If total turnout stays the same but the *share* of elderly or low-income voters drops (compensated by high-income tech-savvy voters), then the "null" is actually a mask for a significant distributional shift. Finding *that* would make this an AER paper.
