## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper studies Denmark’s 2014 decision to cut welfare benefits sharply for adults under 30, creating a large age-based benefit cliff, and asks what happened to young people’s welfare use and labor market outcomes. A busy economist should care because the paper speaks to a central policy question in public economics: when governments reduce transfer generosity for young adults, do they actually increase work, or mostly just reduce caseloads?

The paper does **not** articulate this pitch as clearly as it should in the first two paragraphs. The opening anecdote is vivid, but the introduction quickly drifts into a generic literature tour and a “gap in English-language literature” justification, which is much weaker than the substantive question. The first two paragraphs should say, more directly:

> Governments increasingly use age-targeted welfare cuts to push young adults into work or education, but we know surprisingly little about whether these policies generate employment or simply shrink the welfare rolls. Denmark’s 2014 reform provides an unusually stark test: it cut cash assistance for those under 30 by roughly 43 percent while leaving benefits for those just above 30 unchanged.  
>   
> This paper asks a simple question with broad relevance for welfare policy: when a government makes welfare much less generous for young adults, where do they go? Using Danish administrative data, I show that the reform substantially reduced cash assistance receipt but produced only modest employment gains, implying that much of the decline in welfare use did not translate into work.

That is the pitch. It is about the **destination of people pushed off benefits**, not about “the first English-language evaluation of a Danish reform.”

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:**  
The paper shows that a large age-based cut in welfare generosity for young adults sharply reduced cash assistance receipt in Denmark but translated into only modest increases in employment, implying limited labor-market absorption.

This is a potentially interesting contribution, but it is **not yet clearly differentiated** from nearby work. Right now the reader could reasonably summarize it as “another paper showing that lower benefits reduce recipiency and somewhat increase work.” The introduction gestures at age-based benefit papers and Scandinavian labor supply papers, but it does not crisply explain what this paper adds beyond “new setting, big reform, Denmark.”

A stronger differentiation would be:

- most prior papers emphasize **duration/eligibility** margins, older workers, UI, disability, or sick leave;
- this paper focuses on a **large reduction in benefit levels** for **young adults**;
- the novel substantive point is not merely that incentives matter, but that **caseload effects can greatly exceed employment effects**.

That last point is the real hook. The paper half-sees this via the “absorption gap,” but it is not elevated enough.

On framing: the contribution is currently framed too much as filling a **literature gap** (“no English-language causal evaluation”), rather than answering a question about the **world** (“What happens when you slash welfare for young adults?”). The latter is much stronger and more AER-relevant.

Could a smart economist explain what is new after reading the introduction? Not cleanly. They would likely say: “It’s a DiD on a Danish youth welfare reform that reduced benefit receipt and modestly increased employment.” That is competent, but not memorable.

What would make the contribution bigger?

1. **Follow the missing margins directly.**  
   The biggest missed opportunity is that the paper’s most interesting claim is that recipients left welfare without commensurate employment gains. If so, where did they go? Education? disability? sickness? inactivity? parental support? emigration? The paper alludes to some of this in the discussion, but without putting it at the center of the contribution. A bigger paper would document the destination states explicitly.

2. **Make the paper about policy design, not just treatment effect estimation.**  
   The important question is whether age-targeted benefit cuts are effective tools for labor-market integration versus mere caseload reduction. That is broader and more consequential than “estimating the effect of Uddannelseshjælp.”

3. **Use education/activation outcomes as co-equal outcomes, not afterthoughts.**  
   Since the reform explicitly aimed to push youth into education, a paper that only foregrounds welfare and employment is oddly incomplete. If the policy moved people from welfare into schooling, that is not “leakage”; it may be the intended channel. Without making that a main outcome, the paper risks sounding smaller and more confused than it needs to.

4. **Sharpen the benchmark comparison.**  
   Compare the magnitude of the caseload reduction to the employment increase in a way that has policy bite: e.g., “for every 100 young adults leaving welfare, how many appear in employment versus elsewhere?” That would make the contribution concrete.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

Closest neighbors, based on the paper’s field and citations, likely include:

- **Lalive, van Ours, and Zweimüller (2006)** on extended unemployment benefits and job finding
- **Card, Chetty, and Weber (2007)** on UI benefit duration and search behavior / age-based discontinuities in Austria
- **Kolsrud et al. (2018)** on sick pay benefit paths and return to work in Sweden
- Classic welfare reform syntheses such as **Blank (2002)** and **Meyer and Sullivan / Meyer-related work**
- More broadly, the literature on transfer generosity and labor supply, including **Moffitt**
- Potentially the youth activation / NEET / education-assistance literature in Scandinavia and Europe, which the paper seems only partially aware of

How should the paper position itself relative to these neighbors? Mostly **build on them**, not attack them. The right claim is not “they missed Denmark,” but “existing work shows transfer generosity affects behavior; this paper shows that for age-targeted youth welfare cuts, the first-order effect may be on caseloads rather than employment.” That is a useful extension and nuance.

The current positioning is a bit **too narrow and too local**. “First causal estimate of Denmark’s Uddannelseshjælp reform” is a perfectly fine second-tier selling point, but it is not a top-journal framing. AER readers do not care enough about whether a reform has been evaluated in English. They care whether the paper changes how we think about welfare policy.

The paper seems underconnected to at least three conversations:

1. **Youth transitions / education vs. work literature**  
   Since the reform explicitly sought to redirect young adults into education, the paper should converse with work on youth activation, schooling incentives, school-to-work transitions, and NEET populations.

2. **Multidimensional responses to transfer cuts**  
   There is a wider literature on substitution across programs, margins of adjustment, and administrative relabeling. The paper is already gesturing toward this with “other benefits” and activation categories. It should engage more fully with that literature.

3. **Political economy / policy design of age-targeted welfare**  
   The age threshold is not just an identification device; it is a policy design choice. Papers on age-based rules, youth minimum wages, and age-contingent labor market institutions may be relevant framing neighbors.

The paper is having a reasonable conversation, but probably not the **best** one. The more impactful framing may come from connecting public finance to the economics of **youth human capital formation and welfare-state design**. The policy was not simply “cut benefits”; it was “make welfare for under-30s look like student support to induce education.” That is a more interesting conversation than standard labor supply alone.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

**Setup:**  
Governments worry that generous social assistance may discourage work, especially among young adults at the start of their labor market lives. Denmark introduced a sharp age-based reduction in benefits to push under-30s toward work or education.

**Tension:**  
Lower benefits should reduce welfare dependence, but it is unclear whether those leaving welfare enter jobs, education, other programs, or hardship. Caseload decline and labor-market integration are not the same thing.

**Resolution:**  
The reform substantially reduced cash assistance receipt among young adults, but employment rose only modestly.

**Implications:**  
Age-targeted benefit cuts are effective at shrinking the rolls, but may be blunt tools for increasing employment; policymakers need to understand the destination states of those pushed off welfare.

That is a good narrative. The problem is that the paper only **partially tells it**. It starts with a vivid setup, but then reverts to a standard empirical-paper template: literature review, institutional detail, estimates, robustness, generic conclusion. The “absorption gap” is the actual story, but it appears too late and feels bolted onto the estimates rather than serving as the organizing spine from the beginning.

So yes: in its current form, it feels somewhat like **a collection of results looking for a story**. The story it should be telling is:

> Denmark created an unusually sharp age-based welfare cliff to induce self-sufficiency among young adults. The reform succeeded mechanically in reducing benefit receipt. But the deeper policy question is whether youth left welfare for work, for school, or simply for other forms of support. The key finding is that caseload reduction far exceeded employment gains, forcing a reinterpretation of what “success” means in youth welfare reform.

That is coherent, intuitive, and policy-relevant.

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

At a dinner party of economists, the fact to lead with is:

**“Denmark cut welfare for under-30s by about 43 percent. Welfare receipt among young adults collapsed, but employment barely moved.”**

That would get people to lean in. It is a strong opening fact pattern because it immediately raises the right question: **where did the people go?**

And that is exactly the follow-up question they would ask:
- Into education?
- Into other transfer programs?
- Into family support?
- Into hidden nonemployment?
- Is this a policy success or just relabeling?

This is the paper’s biggest asset: the result naturally generates an interesting second question. But the paper currently does not answer that second question well enough. It identifies the right puzzle, but does not resolve it.

The findings are not null, and they are not boring. The caseload/employment disconnect is interesting. But the paper must make the case that this is not just “modest employment effects,” but a deeper lesson about the difference between **reducing recipiency** and **improving labor market attachment**.

The paper is close to making that case, but right now it still reads a bit like: “we found a modest employment effect, and maybe there were other margins.” To be memorable, it needs to say: “the core policy lesson is that caseload decline is a poor proxy for economic integration.”

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

The introduction currently tries to do too many things:

- broad public finance literature review,
- “gap in English-language literature,”

This is too much and in the wrong order. The first 2–3 pages should be brutally focused on:

### 2. Move some institutional detail later
The precise benefit amounts and background are useful, but the introduction is overburdened with details that belong in the institutional section. Give the reader the headline facts early; don’t crowd the pitch.

### 3. Front-load the “absorption gap”
This is the most interesting idea in the paper. It should appear in the abstract, first page, and contribution paragraph—not emerge only after the main estimates.

### 4. Make education/other destinations central if data permit
If the paper has any credible evidence on activation, education, or other benefit categories, those results should be in the main text, not vaguely referenced in discussion. Right now the discussion introduces claims that are more interesting than some of the earlier sections. That is backwards.

### 5. Compress the robustness in the introduction
The introduction spends too much time previewing placebo tests and pre-trends. That is not what should carry the paper strategically. Save this for later.

### 6. Rethink the conclusion
The conclusion mostly summarizes. It should instead do one of two things:
- interpret what the findings imply for welfare-state design, or
- sharpen the distinction between reducing dependence and increasing self-sufficiency.

Right now it is serviceable but not value-adding.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The gap is primarily a **framing and scope problem**, with some **ambition problem** layered on top.

The paper’s central empirical fact is potentially important. But in current form, it is too easy to view it as a small-country reform paper with a standard result. For AER, the paper needs to become a paper about a **big economic question**:

**What do age-targeted welfare cuts actually accomplish—employment, education, substitution across programs, or merely lower official caseloads?**

That is a broader, more important question than “what happened in Denmark in 2014?”

More specifically:

- **Framing problem:** Yes. The science is being sold too locally and too modestly.
- **Scope problem:** Yes. The paper needs more direct evidence on destination states, especially education and other programs.
- **Novelty problem:** Somewhat. “Benefit cuts reduce recipiency and modestly raise employment” is not novel by itself. The novelty lies in the disconnect between the two.
- **Ambition problem:** Yes. The paper currently settles for a safe contribution—documenting effects of a reform—when it should pursue the larger welfare-design question implied by its own results.

### Single most impactful advice
If the author could change only one thing, it should be this:

**Rebuild the paper around the destination question—where young adults went after leaving welfare—and make the caseload/employment disconnect, not the Danish reform itself, the core contribution.**

That is the difference between a competent policy evaluation and a paper with top-journal potential.
