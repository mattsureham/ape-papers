## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper studies a stark policy in Denmark: in 2014, the government sharply cut welfare benefits for people under 30, creating a large age-based benefit cliff at age 30. The paper asks a first-order public economics question—when you cut cash assistance for young adults, do they move into work, education, or simply off the welfare rolls?—and its core message is that caseloads fell dramatically while employment rose only modestly.

Why should a busy economist care? Because this is not just “another welfare reform paper”: the policy is unusually sharp, the age threshold is intuitive, and the substantive question is central to current debates about whether benefit cuts improve self-sufficiency or merely reduce measured recipiency.

### Does the paper itself articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Not quite. The current opening is lively, but then it immediately sinks into a literature survey and a “this paper fills a gap” frame. That weakens the pitch. The introduction should lead with the world-level question and the surprising fact: a very large benefit cut caused a very large fall in welfare receipt but only a small rise in employment. That is the hook.

### The pitch the paper should have

A stronger opening would say something like:

> In 2014 Denmark imposed one of the sharpest age-based welfare cuts in any rich country: unemployed adults under 30 suddenly faced benefits roughly 40 percent lower than otherwise similar adults just above 30. This paper asks a simple question with broad relevance for social policy: when governments make welfare much less generous for young adults, do recipients move into work—or mainly disappear from the welfare caseload?
>
> Using administrative data before and after the reform, I show that the under-30 benefit cut sharply reduced cash assistance receipt, but translated into only modest employment gains. The main lesson is that benefit cuts are highly effective at shrinking caseloads, but much less effective at generating labor-market absorption—a distinction that matters for how economists and policymakers interpret “successful” welfare reform.

That is the AER-relevant version of the story.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### Contribution in one sentence

The paper shows that a large age-based cut in welfare generosity for young adults substantially reduced welfare receipt in Denmark but generated only modest employment gains, implying that lower benefits shrink caseloads much more than they raise work.

### Is this clearly differentiated from the closest papers?

Only partially. Right now the contribution is differentiated mostly by institutional setting—Denmark, under-30s, age cutoff, Scandinavian context. That is not enough for AER. The paper needs to differentiate on the substantive margin:

- many papers ask whether less generous benefits increase labor supply;
- this paper’s distinctive contribution is that **caseload reduction and employment increase are not the same object**, and the wedge between them is economically large.

That wedge—the “absorption gap,” to use the paper’s term—is the real contribution. It should be the organizing idea, not a subsection title after the main table.

### Is the contribution framed as a question about the world or a gap in the literature?

Too much as a literature gap. “No English-language economics paper has studied this Danish reform” is not a top-journal contribution. The strong version is a world question: **What do benefit cuts actually accomplish?** Do they create employment, induce education, or merely reclassify hardship?

### Could a smart economist explain what is new after reading the introduction?

At present, they would probably say: “It’s a DiD on a Danish age-based welfare reform showing lower benefits reduce recipiency and modestly raise employment.” That is competent, but not memorable.

The introduction should make the newness much more explicit: “This paper uses an unusually sharp policy to separate two things economists often conflate—reductions in benefit receipt and increases in productive labor-market absorption.”

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Specific ways to make it bigger:

1. **Follow destinations, not just exits.**  
   The paper repeatedly says displaced recipients may go to education, other benefits, or family support. If the paper can document these flows even imperfectly, the contribution becomes much larger. Right now the central substantive question is raised but not answered.

2. **Reframe around policy success metrics.**  
   The paper should argue that welfare reform is often judged by caseload decline, but the economically meaningful outcome is reallocation across work, schooling, and other public programs. That framing gives the result broader bite.

3. **Exploit the “youth policy” angle more directly.**  
   Since the reform was explicitly designed to push people into education, education enrollment or training participation is not a side mechanism; it is one of the main intended outcomes. If those data can be elevated credibly, the paper becomes about the design of youth transitions, not just welfare use.

4. **Emphasize the intensive-vs-extensive policy distinction.**  
   The paper notes that prior age-threshold papers often study duration or eligibility. If this paper is instead about a large change in benefit levels with eligibility held fixed, that distinction should be sharpened.

5. **Compare “caseload elasticity” to “employment elasticity.”**  
   A simple conceptual comparison could become memorable: benefits are highly elastic in take-up terms but weakly elastic in employment terms.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Governments worry that generous welfare weakens work incentives, especially for young adults at the edge of labor-force attachment or schooling decisions. Denmark responded with a sharp under-30 benefit cut intended to move youth from welfare into education and work.

### Tension

A drop in welfare receipt is easy to observe, but it is not the same as successful labor-market integration. The real question is whether benefit cuts create employment or merely push people off one program and into other states.

### Resolution

The reform appears to have dramatically reduced welfare recipiency but only modestly increased employment. So the policy was powerful in shrinking caseloads, but limited in generating labor-market absorption.

### Implications

Economists should be cautious about equating lower caseloads with improved self-sufficiency. Policymakers designing youth welfare reforms should evaluate destination states—employment, education, other benefits—not just exits from the target program.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the ingredients, but they are not fully assembled. Right now it often reads like a competent policy evaluation with an interesting side observation. The “absorption gap” should be the spine of the paper. That is the story. Everything else should support it.

At present:
- setup: yes
- tension: somewhat
- resolution: yes
- implications: underdeveloped

The paper should be telling a cleaner story:

1. Governments use benefit cuts to induce self-sufficiency.  
2. But “leaving welfare” is not the same as “entering work.”  
3. Denmark’s age-based cliff provides a sharp test of that distinction.  
4. The results show a large wedge between caseload decline and employment increase.  
5. Therefore, standard metrics of welfare reform success are incomplete.

That is much stronger than the current sequence of literature review, design, and then results.

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?

“Denmark cut welfare for under-30s by about 40 percent. Welfare receipt among young adults collapsed, but employment barely moved.”

That is the fact.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

They would lean in—at least initially—because the policy is sharp and the result is intuitively important. The wedge is provocative.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Immediately: **Where did those people go?**

That is both the strength and weakness of the current paper. It tees up exactly the question people care about, but it only partially answers it. If the paper cannot answer destinations, it needs to be much more explicit that its contribution is about the distinction between program exit and employment entry, not about a full accounting. But if it can answer that destination question, the paper gets much more exciting.

### If findings are modest: is the modesty itself interesting?

Yes, here the modest employment effect is interesting because the treatment is large. A huge policy shock with only small employment gains is substantively meaningful. But the paper must make that case more forcefully. Right now “modest relative to benefit reduction” is there, but not as the central intellectual punchline.

The null/modest aspect works only if the paper presents it as:
- evidence against a simple “cut benefits, people work” model,
- or evidence that youth benefit reforms operate through education / relabeling / other-program substitution rather than immediate employment.

Otherwise it risks reading like a partially successful reform rather than an important lesson.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

This is not mainly a framing problem, though framing does need work. It is mostly a **scope/ambition problem**.

The current paper has a credible and interesting treatment with a strong headline result, but it stops one step too early. It documents that young adults leave welfare and only modestly enter employment, then speculates about education and other destinations. For AER, speculation needs to become evidence.

### The gap between current form and an AER paper

- **Not just “does cutting benefits reduce receipt?”** We know that is plausible.
- **Not just “is there some employment response?”** Also unsurprising.
- The AER version answers the deeper question: **what is the full reallocation induced by the policy?**

That means at least one of:
- education enrollment/completion,
- transitions to other transfer programs,
- nonemployment categories,
- medium-run labor-market attachment,
- heterogeneity by baseline attachment or education risk.

Right now the paper’s most interesting claim is that the reform reduced welfare rolls without fully integrating people into work. But the evidence on the second clause is incomplete.

### Is it a framing problem, scope problem, novelty problem, or ambition problem?

Mostly:
- **Scope problem:** too few outcomes relative to the question posed.
- **Ambition problem:** the paper accepts “first causal estimate of Denmark’s reform” as enough, which it is not.
- Secondarily, **framing problem:** the introduction underplays the broader message.

Less a pure novelty problem—the setting is novel enough if used to answer a bigger question.

### Single most impactful advice

If the authors can only change one thing, they should **turn the paper from a caseload-and-employment paper into a destination-accounting paper**: show where the young adults went after leaving cash assistance, especially into education and other benefits, and make that reallocation the central contribution.

That one change would transform the paper from “another DiD on a welfare reform” into a paper about what welfare reform actually does.
