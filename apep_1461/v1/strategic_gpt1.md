## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple, policy-relevant question: when a developing country raises the cost of formal employment through a non-wage mandate, do workers and firms escape into informality? Using Mexico’s 2023 reform that doubled statutory paid vacation for formal workers, the paper argues that even with a very large informal sector available as an outside option, formality did not decline—suggesting that at least some labor mandates do not meaningfully function as a “tax on formality.”

That is a potentially interesting AER-type pitch. The problem is that the paper does not quite deliver it cleanly in the first two paragraphs. The introduction starts with a broad developing-country dilemma, then quickly shifts into institutional detail and design. It tells me why the setting is convenient before it fully tells me why the question matters in the world. The paper should lead much more aggressively with the economic stakes: informality is the central adjustment margin in many labor markets, and this reform is a rare test of whether mandated benefits actually push employment across that margin.

### The pitch the paper should have in the first two paragraphs

“In economies with large informal sectors, labor regulation is often assumed to come with a first-order cost: each new formal-sector mandate should push workers and firms out of the regulated sector and into informality. Yet credible causal evidence on this mechanism is surprisingly limited, especially for non-wage mandates that affect only formal jobs and leave a clear informal escape valve.

This paper studies Mexico’s 2023 ‘Vacaciones Dignas’ reform, which doubled statutory paid vacation for formal workers while leaving informal jobs entirely exempt. If benefit mandates operate as a tax on formality, this reform should have reduced formal employment or widened behavioral differences between formal and informal workers. I find little evidence of that response. The main implication is that even in a labor market where informality is pervasive, increasing a valued non-wage benefit need not trigger flight from the formal sector.”

That is the story. Start there, not with “every developing country faces the same policy dilemma.”

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

In developing economies with large informal sectors, formal-sector mandates are widely thought to risk pushing employment out of the regulated sector. Mexico is a canonical case: informality is large, salient, and central to debates about labor policy.

### Tension

The puzzle is that theory cuts both ways. A new mandate raises formal labor costs, which should reduce formality. But if workers value the benefit, or if movement into informality is frictional or unattractive, the mandate may instead be absorbed through wages or internal adjustments. We lack clean evidence on which mechanism dominates for non-wage labor standards in a highly informal economy.

### Resolution

Mexico’s 2023 vacation reform offers a rare test, and the paper finds little evidence of movement toward informality or major shifts in formal-relative-to-informal hours. The putative “escape valve” did not open.

### Implications

The result suggests that not all labor mandates should be expected to shrink the formal sector, even where informality is pervasive. The broader implication is that economists and policymakers may need a more discriminating view of labor regulation—one that distinguishes between mandates workers value and those that operate more like pure taxes.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the ingredients, but not yet a disciplined arc. Right now it feels like a competent empirical paper with a strong title and a better abstract than introduction. The story gets diluted by:

- too much “this contributes to three literatures”
- a mismatch between the headline question (informality) and the headline result (hours)
- a late-arriving and underdeveloped interpretation of the null
- a few distracting side results, especially the wage result and gender heterogeneity, that feel appended rather than integrated

### What story should it be telling?

Not “we study a vacation reform.”  
Rather: **“We use a vacation reform to test whether informality is the key adjustment margin for labor standards in a dual labor market.”**

That is the story. Vacation is the instrument; the real subject is the adjustment margin.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?

“In Mexico, doubling legally mandated paid vacation for formal workers did not produce detectable movement out of formal employment, even though more than half the labor market is informal.”

That is a decent dinner-party fact. It is counterintuitive enough to earn attention.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

Some would lean in—especially labor and development economists—because the setting is attractive and the result runs against a common presumption. But many would quickly ask: “Did the reform actually bind?” If the answer is unclear, they may drift. The paper’s strategic problem is not that the result is null; it is that nulls need interpretation, and here the paper has not yet made the interpretation crisp enough.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Almost certainly: **“So where did the incidence go?”**  
Did wages adjust? Did firms not comply? Did workers value the benefit enough that there was no reason to exit? Were there compositional adjustments within formality? That is the natural follow-up, and the paper only partially answers it.

### Is the null itself interesting?

Yes, potentially very much so. But only if it is framed as a decisive test of a widely invoked mechanism. The current paper is close, but not fully there. A null can be publishable at the top if it rules out a mechanism people genuinely believe is important. The paper needs to be more explicit that this is a meaningful failure of a canonical prediction in a canonical setting—not just “we didn’t find significance.”

At present, parts of the results section still read like a failed treatment rather than a successful test of a hypothesis. The author needs to own the null much more confidently and conceptually.
