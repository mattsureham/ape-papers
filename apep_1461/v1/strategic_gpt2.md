## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple and important question: when a developing country raises the cost of formal employment through a mandated benefit, do workers and firms respond by shifting into the informal sector? Using Mexico’s 2023 reform that doubled statutory paid vacation for formal workers, the paper argues that even in a labor market where informality is large and readily available, a sizable non-wage mandate did not reduce formality or meaningfully alter labor market behavior.

A busy economist should care because this is a direct test of a foundational claim in development and labor economics: that labor regulation acts like a “tax on formality” in dual labor markets. If the result is credible and well-framed, it speaks not just to Mexico, but to how we should think about mandated benefits, incidence, and the political economy of labor protection in economies with large informal sectors.

### Does the paper articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Not quite. The current opening is competent, but it is still too much “here is an institutional setting with a neat DiD” and not enough “here is the big question in economics this episode can answer.” It also gets bogged down quickly in the formal/informal comparison as a design feature, before the reader has fully absorbed why this is an important test of theory.

The first two paragraphs should do less scene-setting and more claim-making. Right now, the paper’s best line is in the title and conclusion: **the escape valve was available, but workers did not use it.** That should be the introduction’s central framing device.

### The pitch the paper should have

For decades, economists have argued that labor mandates in developing countries discourage formal employment by raising the cost of complying with regulation. But that claim is rarely tested using a reform that cleanly increases the cost of formality while leaving the informal sector untouched. Mexico’s 2023 vacation reform provides exactly such a test: it doubled statutory paid leave for formal workers in a labor market where more than half of employment is informal and exempt.

This paper shows that the expected “escape valve” to informality did not open. Despite a large increase in mandated paid leave, I find no meaningful decline in formality and no detectable shift in key labor market margins. The result suggests that at least for valued non-wage benefits, formal-sector mandates do not mechanically function as a tax that pushes workers and firms into informality.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper provides evidence that a large, formal-sector-only mandated benefit increase in Mexico did not induce measurable movement toward informality, challenging the view that non-wage labor regulation necessarily acts as a tax on formality in developing countries.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?

Only partially. The paper names the right broad literatures, but the differentiation is still a bit generic.

What is distinct here is not just “I study Mexico” or “I study a benefit mandate rather than wages.” It is:

1. a **non-wage mandate** rather than a wage floor or enforcement shock,
2. in a **high-informality setting** where the standard escape margin is salient,
3. with a reform that **applies only to formal employment**, making it unusually sharp conceptually,
4. and with a result that is essentially **no reallocation to informality**.

That is the novelty. The introduction gestures at this, but it does not yet sharpen the contrast enough against minimum wage papers, enforcement papers, and US incidence papers.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a gap in a literature?

It is mixed, but too often it lapses into literature-gap framing. The stronger framing is world-facing:

- **When governments expand worker protections in economies with large informal sectors, do firms and workers exit formality?**
- **Are some labor mandates compatible with formalization rather than in tension with it?**

That is much stronger than “the literature has not yet studied vacation mandates.”

### Could a smart economist explain to a colleague what’s new after reading the introduction?

Not cleanly enough. Right now they might say: “It’s a DiD on Mexico’s vacation reform and they find little effect on informality.” That is accurate but not memorable.

You want them to say: **“It’s a sharp test of the ‘regulation pushes workers into informality’ hypothesis, and the answer is: not for a big paid-leave mandate, even when the informal sector is huge.”**

### What would make the contribution bigger?

Several specific things would materially enlarge the contribution:

1. **Make formality—not hours—the main outcome.**  
   The paper says the question is about the informality margin, but the lead empirical result is on the formal–informal hours gap. Strategically, that is the wrong flagship outcome for the paper’s stated question. The main fact should be transitions into informality, formality shares, or job reallocation. Hours can support the story, but should not carry it.

2. **Show whether the mandate actually changed leave-taking or effective working time.**  
   If the policy did not alter actual vacation usage, then the null on informality is much less informative. The paper needs, strategically, some demonstration that treatment intensity was real in practice.

3. **Tie the result more directly to incidence.**  
   The wage result is intriguing but currently underdeveloped and also awkwardly interpreted. If the paper can show costs were absorbed elsewhere rather than through informality, it becomes much more than a null result.

4. **Push harder on margins where theory predicts action.**  
   Job transitions, separations, new matches, sectoral composition, tenure composition, firm size, or payroll-based formal employment would all make the story feel more structural and less tied to one survey margin.

5. **Clarify whether the result is about all mandates or about valued mandates.**  
   The strongest version may be narrower: mandates that workers value highly need not reduce formality. That is a much more interesting and defensible contribution than “regulation does not matter.”

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

There is a canonical concern in development economics that labor regulation raises the cost of formal employment and therefore expands informality.

### Tension

That logic is plausible, but most existing evidence comes from cross-country comparisons, minimum wages, or enforcement shocks—not from a clean increase in a non-wage benefit that affects formal jobs only. Mexico offers a severe test because informality is large, legal coverage differs sharply by sector, and the reform was sizable.

### Resolution

The reform produced little to no movement along the formality margin. The supposed escape valve to informality appears not to have been used.

### Implications

The broad implication is that not all labor regulation in high-informality economies should be treated as a straightforward tax on formality. A narrower but more credible implication is that worker-valued non-wage mandates may be absorbed without pushing workers and firms out of the formal sector.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the ingredients, but the arc is not fully under control. At present it reads somewhat like:

- neat policy change,
- standard DiD setup,
- null on hours,
- some side results,
- broad discussion.

That is not yet a strong AER narrative. The story should be much more disciplined:

1. **Big hypothesis:** labor mandates push activity into informality.
2. **Clean test:** vacation reform in Mexico.
3. **Main fact:** no reallocation to informality.
4. **Interpretation:** either workers value the benefit, adjustment happened elsewhere, or compliance was incomplete—but in any case the canonical “escape valve” prediction fails in this setting.
5. **Boundary condition:** this is especially informative for valued non-wage mandates.

Right now there is also a mismatch between the story and the empirical emphasis. If the paper is about informality, the narrative should not revolve around hours.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?

“Mexico doubled statutory paid vacation for formal workers in 2023, in a labor market where most jobs are informal and exempt—and yet there was no detectable move out of formality.”

That is the dinner-party fact.

Some would lean in—especially labor and development economists—because the setting is unusually clean conceptually and the result cuts against a common intuition. But many would reach for their phones if the next sentence is about the formal–informal hours gap rather than actual movement into informality. The current lead empirical object is not the one that makes the finding vivid.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Immediately: **“Did workers actually take more vacation?”**  
Second: **“If not informality, where did the incidence go—wages, compliance, or within-job adjustment?”**

Those are exactly the questions the paper needs to anticipate more centrally.

### Is the null result itself interesting?

Yes, but only if the paper makes clear why this is a strong null rather than a failed design. The paper partly does this. The phrase “well-powered null” helps. But to really sell a null in a top journal, the paper must show:

- the policy was substantively large,
- the treated margin was real,
- the margin of escape existed,
- and meaningful effects can be ruled out on the core outcome.

The paper is closest on the first and fourth of these, weaker on the second, and strategically muddled on the actual “core outcome.”

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

At the moment, the gap is mostly a combination of **framing problem**, **scope problem**, and **ambition problem**.

### Framing problem
The science, as presented, is more interesting than the paper’s current rhetorical packaging. The paper’s best contribution is a test of a major idea in labor/development economics, but it is written too much like a tidy applied paper on one reform.

### Scope problem
The paper is trying to answer a big question with a relatively narrow set of outcomes, and the flagship outcome is not the one readers care most about. To reach AER level, it needs to show more clearly where incidence went and whether the treatment actually bit.

### Novelty problem
The core idea is novel enough if properly framed—clean non-wage mandate, huge informal sector, null on escape. But the design will still look to many readers like “another reform DiD” unless the paper is much sharper about the conceptual stakes.

### Ambition problem
The paper is competent but safe. It is content to document a null rather than fully convert that null into a statement about theory. AER papers usually do more than say “we found no effect.” They say “this null decisively shifts how we think about mechanism X.”

### Single most impactful advice

**Rebuild the paper around the claim that this is a direct test of whether a worker-valued formal-sector mandate functions as a tax on formality—and make actual movement into informality, not hours, the central empirical object.**

That one change would force the right framing, the right ordering of results, and probably the right expansion of outcome analysis.
