## 1. Idea Fidelity

The paper clearly pursues the broad question in the manifest: whether ANSES’s DNI-digit payment calendar generates within-month variation in liquidity that affects property crime in Buenos Aires. It uses the intended core data sources—ANSES payment timing and Buenos Aires daily crime records—and frames the exercise as a replication/non-replication of Foley-style payday-crime effects.

That said, the paper misses a key element of the original identification strategy. The manifest’s strongest idea was to exploit *cross-digit, and ideally cross-neighborhood, differential exposure* to payment timing, potentially using tract-level beneficiary shares or local composition to translate the digit schedule into neighborhood-level treatment intensity. Instead, the paper collapses the schedule into a city-wide average “days since payment” variable. This aggregation largely removes the quasi-random variation generated by DNI digits and leaves identification to come from deterministic within-month calendar movements. In that sense, the paper follows the spirit of the idea, but not its most credible empirical implementation.

## 2. Summary

This paper studies whether property crime in Buenos Aires rises as time passes since welfare payments are disbursed under Argentina’s ANSES DNI-digit calendar. Using daily city-level crime counts from 2019–2023 and measures of average days since payment, the paper finds no robust evidence of a depletion cycle and interprets this as a well-powered non-replication of prior findings from the United States and the Netherlands.

The question is interesting and policy-relevant, and a null result could be valuable. However, in its current form the empirical design does not convincingly isolate exogenous variation in liquidity exposure, so the paper does not yet provide a persuasive causal test of the underlying hypothesis.

## 3. Essential Points

1. **The treatment variable does not exploit the purported source of exogenous variation.**  
   The central identification claim is that the last DNI digit is quasi-random, but the estimating equation never uses digit-level or neighborhood-level exposure to different digits. Averaging days since payment across all ten digit groups at the city-day level produces a deterministic monthly time series common to the entire city. As a result, variation in the regressor is driven almost entirely by calendar structure, not by quasi-random assignment of individuals to payment dates. This is why the permutation test is not surprising: once treatment is reduced to a city-wide monthly cycle, it is hard to distinguish from generic day-of-month patterns. To make the design credible, the paper needs a panel where treatment intensity varies across units within the same day—e.g., commune-by-day or tract-by-day crime matched to local ANSES exposure based on beneficiary composition.

2. **The empirical approach does not align well with the research question.**  
   The theory concerns whether areas with more recently paid beneficiaries experience different crime patterns because local liquidity changes. But the paper estimates whether *aggregate city-wide crime* covaries with a city-wide payment-cycle index. With payments staggered across ten digit groups over many days, aggregate liquidity in a city of three million may barely move even if there are meaningful local effects. A city-level null therefore cannot distinguish between “no behavioral response” and “aggregation masks heterogeneous local responses.” This is especially important because the discussion itself argues that staggering may eliminate any city-level shock. If that is the mechanism, the correct empirical test is local exposure, not city totals.

3. **Inference and specification need to better account for serial correlation and highly structured time variation.**  
   The paper uses heteroskedasticity-robust standard errors in a daily time series with strong serial dependence, seasonality, and institutional timing. That is unlikely to be adequate. More fundamentally, with year-by-month and day-of-week fixed effects, the remaining identifying variation is narrow and mechanically tied to day-of-month position. The polynomial control helps, but this is not a substitute for a design that uses cross-sectional exposure. At minimum, the paper should report HAC/Newey-West or block-bootstrap inference, show the partial variation in treatment after fixed effects, and clarify exactly what variation identifies the coefficient.

## 4. Suggestions

The paper has the ingredients for a much stronger contribution, but it likely needs to be re-centered around the design implied by the institutional setting rather than around a city-level reduced form.

First, I strongly encourage the authors to reconstruct the analysis at a finer unit of observation. The obvious route is a **commune-day** panel using the geocoded crime records already in hand. The key then is to build a measure of each commune’s exposure to each DNI digit group among ANSES beneficiaries. Even imperfect proxies would be preferable to the current city-wide average. For example:
- If administrative counts by commune and program are available, combine them with assumptions about uniform digit shares.
- If not, use household survey data (EPH or census microdata if geographically linkable) to estimate the share of residents likely to be ANSES beneficiaries in each area.
- Interact local beneficiary share with the payment status of digit groups to create a commune-specific predicted liquidity measure.  
This would give treatment variation across space within the same date, allowing date fixed effects and sharply improving credibility.

Relatedly, the paper should think harder about **what margin of crime** is most likely to respond. The current outcomes are broad counts of robbery and theft city-wide. But if payment timing matters, effects may be concentrated in:
- crimes near banks/ATMs/payment points,
- street robbery rather than theft generally,
- daytime incidents on payment and immediate post-payment dates,
- neighborhoods with higher beneficiary concentration or lower formal employment.  
The geocoded microdata are a major asset here. A convincing paper could show either localized effects where theory predicts them, or convincingly rule them out.

A second recommendation is to be more careful in how the institutional argument is used. The paper repeatedly describes DNI digits as quasi-randomly assigned and therefore the instrument as “cleaner” than prior work. In the current implementation, this overstates the design. Once the digit schedule is aggregated to a single city-day series, the quasi-random assignment of digits across people is largely irrelevant. I would revise this language substantially unless the paper is redesigned to exploit actual cross-digit exposure. More generally, the paper should distinguish between:
- exogeneity of individual assignment to payment dates, and
- usable identifying variation in the estimation sample.  
These are not the same thing.

Third, the paper would benefit from a more transparent presentation of the **time-series structure of treatment**. I suggest adding a figure for a representative year showing:
- payment dates by digit,
- the city-wide average DSP variable,
- average daily property crime by day of month.  
This would make clear how much of the treatment is basically a smooth within-month ramp. A residualized plot—after removing year-month and day-of-week effects—would also be useful. If treatment has almost no residual variation beyond day-of-month, the reader should see that directly.

Fourth, the permutation exercise is potentially useful, but right now it partly diagnoses a design problem rather than strengthening causal interpretation. Two suggestions:
1. Explain more clearly what is being permuted and why that is the correct randomization benchmark given the institutional schedule.  
2. If you move to a commune-day design, permutation/randomization inference could become much more compelling—for example, by permuting digit weights or exposure profiles across communes while preserving time structure.  
As written, the result that random reassignment performs similarly to the “true” calendar mainly confirms that city-level treatment is too calendar-like.

Fifth, the paper should revisit **functional form and dynamic treatment coding**. The depletion hypothesis is unlikely to be linear in average days since payment. More natural specifications would include:
- event-time bins relative to payment date,
- indicators for 0–3, 4–7, 8–14, and 15+ days since payment,
- separate effects during the payment window versus the post-window,
- distributed-lag specifications using the share of locally exposed beneficiaries paid in the last \(k\) days.  
These would also align better with the theory and make comparison to Foley easier.

Sixth, I would strengthen the discussion of **power**. The claim that the paper is “well-powered” is not yet persuasive because power depends on the scale at which treatment variation exists. A city-day analysis can have many observations and still have little relevant identifying variation if treatment is common across all residents and heavily collinear with calendar time. Before making strong statements about ruling out meaningful effects, the paper should present a power calculation tied to the actual residual variation in treatment after fixed effects. It would also help to benchmark detectable effects against Foley’s implied magnitudes after accounting for the much more staggered Argentine schedule.

Seventh, the paper should improve the treatment of **standard errors and serial dependence**. Daily crime data are persistent and strongly patterned by holidays, month ends, and reporting practices. At minimum I would want to see:
- HAC/Newey-West standard errors with a reasonable lag length,
- sensitivity to clustering by month or week,
- controls for holidays and possibly month-end/start effects,
- exclusion or separate treatment of extraordinary pandemic periods beyond simply dropping 2020.  
These changes may not rescue the current design, but they are necessary for a careful time-series exercise.

Eighth, there is room to deepen the paper’s substantive interpretation. The null may reflect several different mechanisms: staggered payments, informal smoothing, digital payments, weak geographic concentration of beneficiaries, or offender composition. The paper currently leans heavily on these explanations, but without testing them. Some simple heterogeneity analyses could help:
- higher- vs lower-poverty communes,
- central vs peripheral areas,
- weekday/daytime patterns,
- pre- and post-inflation acceleration periods,
- neighborhoods with more retirees versus more families with children, if data permit.  
Even if the main result remains null, showing where the effect should be largest and is still absent would make the contribution more convincing.

Finally, I would moderate the paper’s strongest claims. “This paper fills that gap, and the answer is no” is too definitive relative to what is currently estimated. The present evidence supports a narrower statement: *at the city-day level, using a calendar-based aggregate treatment measure, the paper does not detect a robust payment-cycle pattern in recorded property crime.* That is already interesting. But a strong claim about the absence of a welfare-payment crime mechanism in developing countries requires a design that tracks local exposure much more directly.

In sum, I like the question, the setting, and the null-result ambition. But the paper’s current city-level implementation does not deliver on the identification promised by the institutional background. If the authors can redesign the analysis around spatially varying exposure to the DNI schedule, this could become a useful and publishable contribution. As it stands, the main result is not yet informative enough about the underlying causal question.
