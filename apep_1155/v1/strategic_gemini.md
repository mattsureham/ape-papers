## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
This paper re-examines the 2012 Salvadoran gang truce—a global landmark for negotiated peace with criminal organizations—to determine if it actually reduced violence through local gang restraint. Using a continuous difference-in-differences design, the authors show that the apparent "truce dividend" at the municipal level is a statistical artifact of broader geographic trends rather than a result of gang-specific behavior. This suggests that while national-level negotiations can coincide with violence drops, the mechanism is likely not the localized cessation of hostilities usually claimed by proponents.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this pitch quite well. The first two paragraphs clearly set the high stakes (a "canonical case study" for organized crime) and the tension (geographic uniformity vs. local mechanisms).

**The Pitch the Paper Should Have:** (The current pitch is strong, but to push for the AER, it needs to lean harder into the *theoretical* disillusionment): 
"Negotiated truces with criminal leviathans are often praised as a pragmatic alternative to failed 'iron fist' policing. By applying a more rigorous spatial identification to the landmark 2012 Salvadoran truce, this paper demonstrates that the localized 'dose-response' of peace—the expected reduction in violence where gangs are most active—is entirely absent. This result suggests we must fundamentally rethink the political economy of criminal peace: truces may function as national-level political signaling or media management rather than local behavioral shifts among combatants."

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**One-sentence contribution:** The paper identifies that the celebrated 2012 Salvadoran gang truce did not actually produce a gang-intensity-proportional reduction in homicides, revealing that the aggregate decline was likely driven by factors other than local gang restraint.

**Evaluation:**
- **Differentiation:** It differentiates itself well from Sviatschi (2022) by looking at "undoing" violence rather than its spread, and from the political science literature (Lessing, 2017) by subjecting a qualitative consensus to quantitative scrutiny.
- **Framing:** It is framed as answering a question about the WORLD (Does negotiating with gangs work?), which is excellent.
- **Clarity:** A smart economist would immediately understand the "within-department" null result. They would see it as a cautionary tale about spatial correlation in DiD.
- **What would make it bigger?** To be a "big" AER paper, it needs to move beyond the "Illusion" and provide more evidence on what the *real* mechanism was. If it wasn't local gang restraint, was it a change in police reporting? A shift in where bodies were dumped? A national-level signaling effect? Identifying the *actual* cause of the 40% drop would elevate this from a "debunking" paper to a "discovery" paper.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
- **Closest Neighbors:** Sviatschi (AER 2022) on gangs; Dell (AER 2015) on drug war spillovers; Blattman et al. (AER 2023) on criminal governance. 
- **Positioning:** It currently acts as a "corrective" to the optimistic literature on negotiated peace. It builds on the "Death of TWFE" literature (Goodman-Bacon) but applies it to a specific, high-stakes policy context.
- **Missing Conversations:** The paper could speak more to the **Political Economy of State-Group Negotiations**. When do states "fake" a peace for international aid or electoral gain? (e.g., Fearon, 2004).
- **Framing Suggestion:** Connect to the literature on **"Measurement of Conflict."** If homicides fall but the "truce" didn't cause it, are we measuring the right thing? Are gangs just hiding bodies?

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC
- **Setup:** A world-renowned truce in El Salvador halved homicides, creating a global model for conflict resolution.
- **Tension:** If the truce worked, violence should drop most where gangs are strongest. Standard models say it did, but those models might be confusing "gang presence" with "being in a city that was already trending down."
- **Resolution:** When you look *within* departments, the effect vanishes. The "success" was a geographic coincidence.
- **Implications:** Don't trust aggregate violence drops as evidence of gang-level behavioral change.

**Evaluation:** The arc is clean. It’s a "detective story" where the primary suspect (the truce) has a seemingly ironclad alibi (the 40% drop), which the authors then dismantle.

- **Lead Fact:** "The most famous gang truce in modern history didn't actually reduce violence in gang territory."
- **Reaction:** People lean in. The 2012 truce is famous among development and crime economists.
- **Follow-up:** "Then why did the national rate drop?" This is the paper's weak point. If I can't answer the follow-up at the dinner party, the contribution feels incomplete.

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
- **Front-loading:** The paper is already well front-loaded. Table 2 and Table 3 tell 90% of the story.
- **Appendix:** Move the "Leave-one-department-out" analysis to the main text; it’s a powerful way to show how specific "hot" departments (like San Salvador) are carrying the entire weight of the spurious result.
- **Mechanisms:** Section 6 (Discussion) needs to be more than a "maybe." The authors should try to test one of the alternative mechanisms (e.g., does the reporting source change coincide with the truce in a way that creates the illusion?).

---

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The paper is currently a very high-quality *Note* or a paper for a field journal (like *JDE* or *JCLE*). The "AER gap" is the lack of a positive discovery. It tells us what *didn't* happen, but it leaves us hanging on what *did*.

**The single most impactful piece of advice:**
Attempt to empirically distinguish between the "Truce Illusion" and the "Body Hiding" hypothesis. If the authors can show that the "drop" in homicides was matched by a "rise" in missing persons (disappeared) specifically in those departments, this moves from a methodological critique of DiD to a blockbuster paper on the dark side of criminal-state negotiations.
