 **Review of "The Truce Illusion: Gang Cease-Fires and the Geography of Violence in El Salvador"**

**1. Idea Fidelity**

There is a fundamental disconnect between the submitted manuscript and the research concept outlined in the manifest. The manifest proposed analyzing El Salvador's **2022 State of Exception** (*régimen de excepción*), which involved the mass incarceration of approximately 85,000 individuals under President Bukele's administration, using VIIRS nighttime lights and PLOS ONE microdata to estimate the "economic dividend" of incapacitation. The submitted paper instead studies the **2012 Gang Truce** (brokered under President Funes), examining homicide effects rather than economic outcomes.

This represents not merely a deviation in emphasis but a complete substitution of the research question, identification strategy, and policy context. The manifest's core economic question—"when organized crime is suddenly removed, does extortion reduction boost activity enough to offset labor supply removal?"—is replaced with a purely criminological question about the geographic distribution of homicide reductions during a temporary cease-fire. The 2022 policy (ongoing mass incarceration) and the 2012 policy (temporary negotiated cease-fire) operate through orthogonal mechanisms: one via incapacitation and state terror, the other via inter-gang contract enforcement. 

If this manuscript is intended to fulfill the manifest, it does not. If it is an independent contribution, the authors should remove reference to the 2022 policy entirely and reposition the paper as a study of negotiated cease-fires rather than mass incarceration. The current title and abstract do not signal this switch, creating confusion for readers expecting the analysis described in the manifest.

**2. Summary**

This paper examines whether El Salvador's 2012 gang truce differentially reduced homicide rates in municipalities with higher pre-truce gang presence (measured by 2011 detention rates). Using a continuous difference-in-differences design with municipality and year fixed effects, the authors find that a one-standard-deviation increase in gang intensity is associated with 0.52 fewer homicides per 10,000 during the truce. However, this effect vanishes with department-by-year fixed effects and is replicated by placebo tests assigning false truce dates to 2005 and 2008. The authors conclude that the apparent municipal-level truce dividend is an artifact of department-level trends confounded with urbanization and policing patterns, rather than a causal effect of the cease-fire.

**3. Essential Points**

There are three critical issues that undermine the paper's causal claims and economic interpretation. While the paper is valuable as a diagnostic exercise, these concerns must be addressed for publication in a top-tier journal.

**Critical Issue 1: Catastrophic Parallel Trends Violation.** The event-study coefficients in Table 2 reveal that high-gang municipalities were experiencing relative homicide declines *throughout the entire pre-period*, with statistically significant negative interactions as early as 2002–2008. The paper acknowledges this as a "violation of the parallel trends assumption" but then proceeds with the DiD interpretation anyway. This is methodologically untenable. When treatment effects appear 10 years before the treatment, the common trends assumption is not merely "violated"—it is inverted. The identifying assumption becomes "divergent trends happened to stabilize at the moment of the truce," which is unfalsifiable and implausible. The department-by-year specification is a insufficient remedy because it mechanically removes all cross-department variation, which is where the action is in this setting. The paper needs to either (a) reframe as a descriptive decomposition rather than causal estimation, or (b) adopt a strategy that actually addresses the pre-trending (e.g., synthetic DiD, matrix completion, or using the 2022 policy as a comparison).

**Critical Issue 2: Incoherent Post-Treatment Dynamics.** The post-collapse coefficient ($\beta_2$) is negative and often larger in magnitude than the truce coefficient (Table 1, Column 2: -1.921 vs. -1.376). This is economically nonsensical under the paper's mechanism. If the truce reduced homicides via gang cooperation, the effect should reverse when the truce collapses and gangs resume hostilities. Instead, high-gang municipalities remain "protected" after the truce ends—suggesting the treatment variable is capturing persistent urbanization trends, demographic changes, or spatial autocorrelation in policing rather than the truce itself. The paper notes this as a "puzzling pattern" but offers no resolution. Without a coherent story for why the effect persists (and strengthens) after the mechanism disappears, the empirical strategy lacks internal validity.

**Critical Issue 3: Measurement Validity of Gang Intensity.** Using 2011 gang-member detentions per capita as a proxy for gang *presence* conflates gang activity with police enforcement intensity. Municipalities with high detention rates may simply have aggressive policing (or historical targeting of specific gangs), not necessarily high gang penetration. This creates measurement error that is likely non-classical and correlated with the homicide trends being studied. More problematically, if police practices changed systematically around 2012 (e.g., reduced confrontations during the truce, followed by punitive crackdowns post-2014), the treatment variable is endogenous to the policy itself. The paper needs an alternative measure of gang presence—such as historical deportation inflows (following Sviatschi 2022), gang graffiti density, or extortion victimization rates—to validate that the results are not driven by police reporting artifacts.

**4. Suggestions**

**Reframing the Research Question.** Given the manifest deviation and the methodological challenges above, I suggest pivoting the paper from a "null effect of truces" story to a **methodological contribution** about spatial confounding in criminal governance research. The paper actually demonstrates something important: that two-way fixed effects (TWFE) designs generate spurious gang-specific effects when treatment is spatially clustered and confounded with geographic trends. This is a genuine contribution to the literature on DiD validity (à la de Chaisemartin-D'Haultfoeuille/Goodman-Bacon). The authors should lead with this insight and frame the 2012 truce as an illustrative case where naive DiD yields illusory results. This respects the empirical finding (the pattern is an artifact) without overstating the causal claim about truces themselves.

**Exploiting the 2022 Policy as a Falsification Test.** Since the manifest originally proposed studying the 2022 State of Exception, and the data presumably extends to 2021 (just before the policy), the authors should leverage the 2022 policy as a falsification test or comparative case. Does the *same* gang intensity variable predict differential homicide declines in 2022, even though the mechanism (mass incarceration vs. negotiated cease-fire) is entirely different? If yes, this confirms the variable is proxying for persistent spatial trends, not policy effects. If no, it suggests the 2012 period was unique. This comparison would rescue the paper's relevance to contemporary policy debates.

**Alternative Identification Strategies.** Given the pre-trends, standard DiD is inappropriate. Consider:
- **Synthetic Difference-in-Differences** (Arkhangelsky et al., 2021): This would downweight municipalities with poor pre-trend fit, addressing the 2002-2008 divergence.
- **Comparative interrupted time series** using matched pairs of municipalities across the Salvadoran border (if data permits) to difference out regional trends.
- **Spatial econometrics:** Report Moran's I tests for spatial autocorrelation in the residuals. Given that gangs operate in networks, standard errors are likely understated even with municipal clustering. Conley spatial HAC standard errors would be more credible.

**Mechanism Exploration.** If the paper retains the truce focus, it must test the mechanism explicitly. The truce theory requires that gangs actually reduced violence in their territories. The paper should test for:
- **Extortion:** The PLOS ONE data includes economic variables. Did reported extortion (not just detentions) decline in high-gang areas? Extortion is the gangs' primary revenue source; if the truce reduced violence but not extortion, the mechanism is political theater rather than gang restraint.
- **Displacement:** Did homicides increase in adjacent low-gang municipalities (spillovers)? This would be evidence of displacement rather than reduction.
- **Hidden bodies:** The paper cites Cruz & Durán (2016) on concealed homicides. Can you test for "disappeared persons" or mass grave discoveries in high-gang areas? If the truce merely shifted killings from public to private spaces, the homicide data are measured with error that correlates with gang presence.

**Standard Errors and Inference.** While you cluster at the municipality level (261 clusters), the effective number of independent units is closer to 14 (departments) given the spatial clustering of gangs. The robustness check using wild cluster bootstrap at the department level is welcome but should be moved to the main tables, not the appendix. Moreover, with only 14 departments, the *de facto* variation for the department-by-year specification is extremely limited—explain this power issue explicitly rather than treating the null result as pure evidence of no effect.

**Data Construction Details.** 
- Explain why municipalities with zero 2011 detentions (46% of sample) are coded as zero rather than missing or treated as a separate category. If zero detentions mean "no police enforcement" rather than "no gangs," this truncates the treatment distribution.
- Address the 2008 and 2014 data source switches (Police vs. Forensic) explicitly in the main text, not just the appendix. If institutional recording practices changed differentially across gang-intense areas (e.g., gangs influenced police reporting), this creates measurement bias that correlates with the treatment.

**Heterogeneity by Gang Type.** MS-13 and Barrio 18 have different territorial strategies. The truce required both to cooperate, but compliance may have varied. If the data permit disaggregation by gang type (or if certain municipalities are historically MS-13 vs. 18 dominated), testing for heterogeneous effects would provide sharper mechanism evidence. If the "illusion" is driven by one gang's territories but not the other, this suggests the DiD pattern captures gang-specific enforcement trends rather than the truce.

**Concluding Recommendation.** The paper should be repositioned as a study of **spatial confounding** in criminal policy evaluation, using the Salvadoran truce as a case study. The contribution lies in showing that the "truce dividend" is geographically diffuse, not gang-specific—a finding that actually complements Sviatschi (2022) by showing that gang presence variables pick up broad urban trends. The authors must abandon the language of causal effects ("the truce did not reduce violence locally") and adopt descriptive language ("gang intensity does not predict differential violence changes conditional on department trends"). With this reframing and appropriate humility about the identification limitations, the paper merits consideration as a cautionary tale for the growing literature on criminal governance.
