## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether cutting long work hours can reverse ultra-low fertility. Using South Korea’s 2018 52-hour workweek cap as a policy shock, it shows that the reform did reduce measured hours, but fertility continued to fall sharply relative to comparison countries—suggesting that time relief alone is not enough in a high-cost, marriage-centric setting.

A busy economist should care because this is a first-order policy question far beyond Korea: many governments implicitly assume that “work-life balance” policies will raise births, but this paper suggests that the constraint may be income, marriage formation, or broader family costs rather than hours per se.

**Does the paper articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Not quite. The current opening is vivid, but it starts with Korea’s pronatalist spending and broad dissatisfaction with conventional policy. That is interesting background, but it delays the real hook, which is much sharper: a country with the world’s longest hours tried one of the world’s boldest hour-reduction reforms, and fertility fell further. The opening should get to that immediately.

**What the first two paragraphs should say instead:**

> Many governments facing low fertility have embraced a simple idea: if work leaves too little time for family life, reducing working hours should help people marry and have children. South Korea offers an unusually powerful test of that idea. In 2018, it imposed a nationwide 52-hour workweek cap in one of the OECD’s longest-hours labor markets, explicitly aiming to improve work-life balance in the country with the world’s lowest fertility.
>
> This paper shows a stark pattern. The reform reduced working hours, especially in overtime-intensive industries, but Korea’s fertility declined more—not less—than in comparable OECD countries after the reform. The core lesson is not that hours never matter, but that in a high-cost environment, freeing time without adequately protecting income or relaxing other barriers to family formation may fail to raise fertility.

That is the paper’s story. It is stronger, cleaner, and world-facing.

- the first stage is real;

Several concrete possibilities:

1. **Shift the outcome from aggregate fertility alone to family formation margins.**

   - marriage rates,
   - first births versus higher-order births,

3. **Use the policy as a test of competing fertility theories.**

4. **Push the external validity angle more carefully.**

A bigger contribution is: **the effectiveness of work-time reforms as pronatal policy depends on environment.** Korea is a hard case where costs and marriage norms may dominate time scarcity.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
South Korea has extraordinarily low fertility and extraordinarily long work hours. Policymakers increasingly argue that overwork, not just financial cost, is choking off family formation.

### Tension
A major workweek cap offers a rare chance to test that idea. If time scarcity is truly binding, reducing hours should help fertility. But theory is ambiguous because shorter hours may also reduce earnings and may not solve deeper barriers like marriage delay, housing costs, and gender inequality.

### Resolution
The reform reduced hours, but fertility fell further relative to comparison countries. The policy appears to have succeeded on labor input while failing on demographic output.

### Implications
Work-time reductions are not automatically pronatal. In high-cost settings, the effect of more time may be offset or dominated by income losses or other structural barriers to family formation.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
Yes, more than many papers do. There is a real story here. But it is **overwritten and somewhat unstable in tone**. At its best, the paper has a sharp paradox: “shorter hours, fewer babies.” At its worst, it becomes a long accumulation of estimates, caveats, and mechanism speculation.

The paper is **not** just a collection of results looking for a story. It does have a story. The problem is that the story is told too many times, with too many qualifiers and too much scene-setting.

### What story should it be telling?
A cleaner version:

1. **A widely held policy belief:** reducing overwork should help fertility.
2. **A strong test case:** Korea’s 52-hour cap.
3. **A credible first-stage fact:** hours actually fell.
4. **A surprising reduced-form fact:** fertility did not improve and instead worsened relative to comparators.
5. **Interpretation:** time is not the only—or even main—binding fertility constraint in this setting.

Everything else should serve that arc.

### If the findings are modest or partly null, is the null interesting?
Yes. The result is not a boring null. It is informative because:
- the policy was large;
- the first stage appears meaningful;
- Korea is exactly the setting where the time-scarcity hypothesis should have had its best shot.

So learning that a major hours reform did **not** generate a fertility turnaround is valuable. But the paper should lean into that framing more explicitly: **this is a disciplined failure of a popular policy intuition.**

At present the paper sometimes sounds like it wants a stronger result (“the reform backfired”) than it can fully sustain. “Did not deliver the expected demographic response” is both more credible and, strategically, just as interesting.
