## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple, policy-relevant question: when a country with extreme overwork cuts working hours, does fertility rise? Using South Korea’s 2018 52-hour workweek cap, the paper argues that hours fell meaningfully, but fertility continued to fall—and perhaps fell faster—suggesting that freeing up time alone is not enough to raise births in a high-cost, high-pressure environment.

A busy economist should care because many governments implicitly assume that “work-life balance” policies will help solve low fertility. If this paper is right, that premise is badly incomplete: time constraints may be less important than the interaction of time, income, marriage markets, and childrearing costs.

**Does the paper articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Not quite. The current opening is vivid, but it takes too long to get to the paper’s actual question and overcommits rhetorically before the reader knows what the design can and cannot speak to. “South Korea is spending itself into demographic oblivion” is memorable, but it cues a broad critique of pronatalist policy rather than the more precise question here: whether shorter work hours can raise fertility.

The first two paragraphs should do three things much faster:
1. Establish the broad question: can reducing work hours raise fertility?
2. Explain why Korea is the right case: extreme hours, extreme low fertility, major reform.
3. State the core result up front: hours fell, fertility did not recover.

**The pitch the paper should have:**

> Many rich countries facing very low fertility are turning to work-life-balance policies on the theory that people have too little time, not just too little money, to form families. South Korea offers an unusually sharp test of that idea: in 2018 it imposed a 52-hour cap on weekly work in one of the longest-hours labor markets in the OECD.
>
> This paper studies whether that large reduction in working time translated into higher fertility. I find that the reform substantially reduced hours, especially in industries with high baseline overtime, but fertility did not improve; if anything, Korea’s fertility decline accelerated relative to comparable countries. The central lesson is not that hours never matter, but that shorter hours alone may fail to raise births when they also reduce earnings and leave broader barriers to family formation untouched.

That is the AER-relevant pitch. It is a world question, policy-relevant, and disciplined.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

The novelty is not “first DiD on Korean hours law.” The novelty is: **a high-profile work-life-balance reform in the world’s lowest-fertility country provides a direct test of a widely discussed mechanism, and the answer is not what policymakers hoped.**

It oscillates between the two. The stronger framing is about the world:
- What constrains fertility in advanced economies?
- Can time-saving or time-freeing labor policy raise births?
- When do hours reductions fail because they also reduce income?

Whenever the paper says “this contributes to three literatures,” it becomes smaller. The more powerful version is: **policymakers increasingly act as if overwork is a major fertility bottleneck; this paper tests that proposition in the hardest case.**

What you want them to say is:  
**“It’s a paper showing that one of the most prominent real-world attempts to use work-life-balance policy to raise fertility seems not to have worked, which suggests time is not the binding margin by itself.”**

3. **Reframe as a test of competing theories of low fertility.**  
   Not just “shorter hours didn’t help,” but “this reform distinguishes between time scarcity and structural cost/gender-inequality explanations.” That is a bigger intellectual frame.

4. **Put Korea in comparative perspective with Japan/Taiwan/France/Scandinavia.**  
   The strongest version is not “Korea is unusual.” It is “hours reductions only appear pronatalist when bundled with income protection and family-supporting institutions.” That would connect the paper to broader comparative political economy.

3. **Well-being / leisure / family policy bundle literature.**

That is a bigger, more AER-worthy conversation.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Advanced economies worry about very low fertility. A popular theory says long working hours leave people too little time for dating, marriage, and childrearing. South Korea, with both extremely long hours and extremely low fertility, enacts a major hours cap.

### Tension
If time scarcity is the binding constraint, this reform should help fertility. But theory is ambiguous because shorter hours may also reduce earnings, and in Korea family formation is expensive and marriage-centered.

### Resolution
The reform appears to reduce hours, but fertility does not rise; instead, Korea’s fertility falls further relative to comparison units.

### Implications
Work-life-balance policy by itself may be insufficient, or even counterproductive, in settings where reduced hours lower income and deeper structural barriers remain. Policymakers should stop assuming that “more free time” maps mechanically into “more babies.”

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
Yes, in skeleton form. But the paper is too long and repetitive, so the arc gets buried under layers of caveats, robustness narration, and mechanism discussion that often restates the same point.

It is **not** a collection of random results, but it is currently narrated like a very anxious seminar presentation: claim, caveat, restatement, second caveat, mechanism speculation, robustness summary, repeat. That weakens the drama.

### What story should it be telling?
A cleaner story:

1. **Policymakers think overwork suppresses fertility.**
2. **Korea’s 52-hour cap is a serious test of that hypothesis.**
3. **The policy reduced work hours.**
4. **Fertility nevertheless deteriorated relative to plausible counterfactuals.**
5. **Therefore time is not enough; the relevant constraint is the package of time, income, and family-formation institutions.**

That is the story. Everything else should serve it.

That is the memorable fact.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
They would lean in, at least initially. The topic is important, the policy is real, and the result cuts against a common intuition. That is promising.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Immediately:  
**“But is that the reform, or just Korea continuing to be Korea?”**

And second:  
**“Do you know whether the issue is lost income, marriage, or something else?”**

Those are exactly the questions the framing has to anticipate. The current draft does anticipate them, but not always strategically. It often overstates before retreating. Better to lead with a disciplined reduced-form claim and then explain why that reduced-form fact is informative.

### If the findings are null or modest, is the null interesting?
Yes—conditionally. This is not a boring null if the paper frames it correctly. The interesting null is not “no statistically significant effect.” The interesting null is:
**a large, salient effort to relax time constraints did not produce the predicted demographic improvement in the country where that channel should have mattered most.**

That is valuable knowledge. But the paper needs to avoid sounding like a failed fertility paper rescued by a null. It needs to sound like a successful test of a prominent hypothesis.

Yes. The science may be serviceable, but the story is not yet top-journal sharp. The paper needs to stop presenting itself as a generic Korea policy evaluation and start presenting itself as a test of a central policy hypothesis in modern fertility debates.

Moderate, not fatal. “Policy X in country Y using DiD/SCM” is not enough for AER by itself. The novelty has to come from the question and the case:

- major work-life-balance reform,

That one change would force better choices about introduction, literature, mechanism, and scope.
