# Internal Review — Claude Code (Round 1)

**Reviewer:** Claude Code (self-review)
**Date:** 2026-03-09

## Summary

This paper studies the relationship between South Korea's 2018 52-hour workweek cap and fertility outcomes. The first-stage evidence (hours fell) is credible; the fertility association is reduced-form and should not be over-interpreted as causal.

## Strengths

1. **Important policy question.** The intersection of working-hours regulation and fertility is understudied and highly policy-relevant.
2. **Multi-design triangulation.** Cross-country DiD, SCM, and within-Korea industry analysis provide complementary evidence.
3. **Honest limitations.** The paper acknowledges hours SCM fragility, single-treated-unit inference concerns, and treatment timing issues.
4. **Strong first stage.** The industry-level dose-response and event study provide credible evidence that the reform reduced hours.

## Weaknesses

1. **Single treated unit.** Korea is the only treated country. Country-clustered SEs overstate precision.
2. **Treatment timing.** July 2018 reform cannot plausibly affect 2018 annual TFR. The immediate SCM gap is suspicious.
3. **Concurrent confounders.** Min wage hikes, housing costs, education inflation all affect fertility and cannot be separated from the reform.
4. **Mechanism not directly estimated.** Income-loss channel is cited from external sources, not estimated in this data.
5. **SCM pre-fit is poor.** TFR RMSPE = 0.27 (148% of Korea TFR SD); hours RMSPE = 3.95.

## Verdict

The paper is publishable as a reduced-form descriptive analysis with a strong first stage. The causal claims have been appropriately softened in the current version. The main contribution is documenting that a major hours reform coincided with continued fertility decline, challenging the "more time = more babies" intuition.

**Decision:** ACCEPT WITH MINOR REVISIONS (causal language further softened, inference caveats added)
