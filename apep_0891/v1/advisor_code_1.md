**Idea Fidelity**  
The paper generally honors the original idea manifest. It focuses on the staggered SNAP Emergency Allotment (EA) terminations and their hypothesized effect on eviction filings, exploits the Princeton Eviction Lab data at the tract-week level, and layers in dose-response and placebo checks to probe identification. The only notable divergence is that, due to ETS coverage limitations, the empirical sample ends up with only eight treated states (instead of the 18 early opt-outs mentioned in the manifest), which should be highlighted more explicitly when discussing policy relevance and generalizability.

**Summary**  
This paper investigates whether the state-by-state early termination of SNAP Emergency Allotments during 2021–22 caused a rise in eviction filings, exploiting a staggered DiD design with tract-level ETS data. The main estimates point to a small positive level effect (0.16–0.25 filings per 1,000 renter units per month) that is driven by high-SNAP tracts and builds over 12–18 months, but the effect lacks statistical precision and is not robust to log/Poisson specifications. The paper frames the null finding as informative, suggesting that any “housing cliff” resulting from EA cuts may be modest relative to the detectable threshold in this design.

**Essential Points**

1. **Threats to Identification from Time-Varying State-Level Confounders.**  
   The treated states differ systematically from controls in baseline filing rates, demographics, and broader economic trajectories, and these differences coincide with other policies that could influence eviction dynamics (e.g., state eviction moratoria, rental assistance programs, differential recovery from the pandemic). The paper relies primarily on tract fixed effects plus month FE and a dose-response/placebo to argue for validity. With only eight treated states, the design is vulnerable to state-level shocks or trends that correlate with EA termination timing. Please augment the identification by (a) including state-specific linear trends or adding state×post controls for key policies (e.g., timing of moratoria ending, rental assistance disbursements) to show robustness, (b) doing falsification tests that shift the treatment date or use leading indicators of other relevant policies, and (c) exploring whether the event-study coefficients are zero within treated-state subsets to reduce reliance on cross-state comparisons.

2. **Functional Form Sensitivity Undermines the Causal Claim.**  
   The level specification produces a small positive effect, but both log-transformed and Poisson/count models yield negative estimates (with the Poisson effect statistically significant). This reversal suggests that the “effect” may be driven by outlier tracts with large filing counts rather than a systematic causal channel. Before interpreting any positive coefficient as evidence of a housing cliff, the authors should reconcile these functional form discrepancies. Potential remedies include winsorizing/robustifying the outcome, using median regression or other quantile approaches to reduce outlier influence, or estimating the level model on a trimmed sample. Additionally, the paper should clarify why the level scale is preferred theoretically and substantively, and explicitly test whether the results are being driven by a few large metros (beyond the leave-one-state-out showing). Without this, the causal interpretation is weak.

3. **Limited Statistical Power and Granularity of Treatment Timing.**  
   With only eight treated states, clustered standard errors are inevitably wide and the randomization inference yields a $p$-value of 0.415, indicating the point estimate is not distinguishable from noise. The paper argues that the null is policy-relevant, but it should more directly quantify the minimum detectable effect (MDE) relative to plausible policy-relevant magnitudes, and discuss how much policy space is left unexplored. Moreover, the staggered treatment dates span multiple months, but the main regressions collapse treatment into a binary “post” indicator; this risks masking heterogeneous dynamics if some states respond faster. Consider exploiting variation in actual termination dates via leads and lags (e.g., modeling the treatment as a series of discrete indicators relative to each state’s EA end date, with event windows unique to each treated cohort) and report cohort-specific ATTs if data permit. These additions would improve understanding of whether the noisiness stems from limited power or from the absence of an effect.

**Suggestions**

- **State-Level Controls and Parallel Trends Diagnostics.** Include time-varying state-level covariates such as state unemployment rates, rent price indices, timing/length of eviction moratoria, rental assistance disbursements, or COVID case rates to mitigate omitted-variable bias. Even if these covariates are noisy, their inclusion can shift the estimates in informative ways. At minimum, present a table showing that these covariates do not have differential pre-trends correlated with treatment timing.

- **Functional Form Transparency.** Provide additional evidence to justify the choice of the level outcome. Present a histogram of the filing rate distribution and demonstrate how many tracts are driving the tails. If outliers are responsible for the positive level effect, consider winsorizing the outcome or estimating the level effect excluding the top 1–2% of filing tracts to see whether the estimate remains positive. Also, juxtapose the log/Poisson results with a “marginal effect” interpretation to help the reader reconcile the conflicting signs.

- **Alternative Control Groups.** Given the sample is limited to cities/states with ETS data, consider constructing a synthetic control-style comparison for a subset of treated cities using carefully matched control cities (e.g., matching on pre-period filings, demographics). This would provide within-sample robustness that does not rely on the entire treated-vs.-untreated-state comparison. The synthetic control results could serve as a benchmark for the staggered DiD.

- **Disaggregated Heterogeneity.** The dose-response analysis currently interacts treatment with the continuous SNAP rate but finds no significance, while the quartile analysis shows suggestive heterogeneity. Consider exploring other dimensions (e.g., renter share, median rent burden, poverty rate) and report whether similar gradients exist. If the economic mechanism is “expense substitution,” neighborhoods with high rent burdens should be particularly sensitive. Alternatively, use a nonparametric spline on the SNAP rate to visualize the gradient rather than only quartiles.

- **Interpretive Clarity on Null Finding.** The abstract and conclusion frame the result as a “well-powered non-result,” but the power analysis suggests the detectable effect (0.37 filings per 1,000 renters) is meaningfully larger than the point estimate. Reframe this section to clarify whether the design is underpowered for policy-relevant magnitudes; explicitly state what magnitude of effect could have been detected, and whether the observed point estimates are substantively negligible or simply imprecise. This transparency will help readers gauge how strongly the evidence speaks to the absence of a housing cliff.

- **Additional Robustness on Cluster Inference.** With eight treated states, conventional state-clustered SEs may be unreliable. Complement the standard errors with inference methods suited for few clusters (e.g., wild cluster bootstrap) and report whether those methods alter conclusions. This is especially important given the randomization inference result looks so different from the TWFE $p$-value.

- **Data Coverage Caveats.** Explicitly address that ETS coverage is not nationwide and that treated tracts in the sample concentrate in certain cities/states; this affects the generalizability to the full population of SNAP recipients. Consider weighting or reweighting the sample to reflect national tract characteristics, or at least describe how the covered tracts compare to broader state averages (beyond Table 1) to reassure readers about external validity.

By addressing these points, the paper would strengthen its identification argument, clarify the causal interpretation of its estimates, and make its policy contribution sharper.
