**FROM:** Editor, American Economic Review
**RE:** Strategic Positioning of "The Housing Cliff: SNAP Emergency Allotment Expiration and Eviction Filing Rates"

---

### 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper investigates whether the withdrawal of pandemic-era SNAP "Emergency Allotments" (a monthly income shock of $95–$250) triggered a wave of housing instability. Using the staggered termination of these benefits across 26 states and tract-level eviction data, the author tests if food assistance acts as a critical "fungible" buffer that prevents households from falling into the eviction process.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this pitch quite well in the first two paragraphs, particularly with the framing of the "Hunger Cliff" and the question of whether it cascades into "housing instability." 

**The pitch the paper should have:**
"This paper provides the first causal evidence on how the largest simultaneous benefit reduction in U.S. history—the expiration of SNAP Emergency Allotments—affected housing stability. I exploit staggered state-level terminations to test whether food assistance is fungible with rent, finding that while the average effect is small and imprecise, the benefit loss significantly increased evictions in high-poverty neighborhoods with a long delay. These results identify the specific 'income-to-eviction' channel and provide a boundary on the spillover effects of the social safety net."

---

### 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution:** The paper provides a precise (though mostly null) estimate of the cross-program spillover from food assistance to housing stability during a period of massive safety net contraction.

**Evaluation:**
- **Differentiation:** It is well-differentiated from *Desmond (2016)* and *Collinson et al. (2024)* by focusing on a specific, exogenous income shock (SNAP) rather than the consequences of eviction.
- **Framing:** It is framed as a question about the WORLD (Does cutting food stamps make people homeless?), which is strong.
- **Clarity:** A smart economist would say: "It’s a well-executed study of a huge policy change that, surprisingly, didn’t seem to move the needle on evictions as much as advocates feared."
- **Bigger Contribution:** To make this "AER big," the author needs to move beyond the tract-level filing rate. The current "Poisson vs. Level" discrepancy suggests the story is about **heterogeneity**. The contribution would be much larger if the author could show *who* is protected (e.g., households in states with stronger tenant protections vs. those without).

---

### 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

**Closest Neighbors:**
1. *Hoynes & Schanzenbach (2009)* – SNAP and consumption.
2. *Ganong & Noel (2019)* – Unemployment insurance and spending.
3. *Collinson et al. (2024)* – Eviction and poverty.

**Positioning Strategy:** The paper should **synthesize** the "fungibility" literature (Hoynes) with the "eviction" literature (Desmond). It currently sits too narrowly in a "policy evaluation" niche. 

**Missing Conversation:** The paper is missing a connection to the **"Hand-to-Mouth" consumer** literature (Kaplan & Violante). Are these results small because SNAP recipients are sophisticated budgeters, or because the "buffer" is provided by food banks? Speaking to the **informal safety net** (food pantries) would broaden the audience significantly.

---

### 4. NARRATIVE ARC

- **Setup:** The pandemic created a massive, temporary expansion of the safety net.
- **Tension:** Policy makers "pulled the plug" at different times. If food and rent are fungible, we should see a "housing cliff."
- **Resolution:** We don't see a massive cliff in the aggregate, but we see a "slow-motion slide" in high-SNAP areas that is highly sensitive to how you measure outliers.
- **Implications:** SNAP is important for food, but perhaps less of a "linchpin" for rent than previously thought—or households have other ways to cope.

**Evaluation:** The narrative arc is currently "serviceable" but leans too heavily on the "it's a null result" angle. The story should be: **"The Delayed Cascade."** The most interesting finding is the 12-18 month lag. That is a narrative that changes how we think about "shocks."

---

### 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

**The Dinner Party Fact:** "When the government cut food stamps by $250 a month for millions of people, eviction filings didn't budge for a year, then started creeping up only in the poorest neighborhoods 18 months later."

**Reaction:** People will lean in. The follow-up: "Why did it take so long? Did they stop eating first, or did the landlords just wait?"

**Null Result:** The null is interesting because the policy change was so large. It disciplines the "catastrophe" narrative. However, the sign-reversal in the Poisson model is a red flag that needs to be resolved or turned into a "feature, not a bug."

---

### 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

- **Move to Appendix:** The "Wave 1 only" and "No COVID" samples (Table 4) are distractors. 
- **Front-load:** The **Dose-Response (Table 2, Col 4)** and the **Dynamic Effects (Table 3)** are the "good stuff." They should be the stars of the show.
- **Conclusion:** Needs to move beyond "it's not robust" to "what does this tell us about the American poor's hierarchy of needs?"

---

### 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The gap between this and a "Green-light" AER paper is **Mechanisms**. 

The "Poisson vs. Level" discrepancy (Table 4) suggests that the effect is driven by a few "hotspot" tracts. If the author can explain **why** those tracts are different (e.g., they are in "eviction mill" jurisdictions where landlords file at the first sign of trouble), then the paper becomes a study of **Institutional Interaction**. 

**Single Biggest Advice:** Solve the "sign reversal" puzzle. Don't just report that Poisson and Level give different signs; prove that this happens because the shock only triggers evictions in places with *already high* filing rates and explain the economics of that threshold.

---

### STRATEGIC ASSESSMENT

*   **Current framing quality:** Adequate
*   **Contribution clarity:** Somewhat fuzzy (is it a null or a delayed effect?)
*   **Literature positioning:** Could be stronger (needs more macro-labor/consumption theory)
*   **Narrative arc:** Serviceable
*   **AER distance:** Medium
*   **Single biggest improvement:** Pivot the paper away from the "average effect" (which is zero) and toward the **dynamic "delayed cascade"** in high-poverty tracts, explaining the outlier-driven results as an economic feature of how different rental markets react to income shocks.
