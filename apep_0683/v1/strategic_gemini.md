## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
The paper asks whether tripling property taxes on empty homes—a popular policy tool globally—actually reduces housing vacancy. Using a decade of escalating tax premiums in England and a staggered difference-in-differences design, it finds a precise zero effect, suggesting that the "vacancy problem" is driven by structural barriers (like probate and dereliction) rather than financial misallocation.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this well, but it frames the story slightly too much as a "policy evaluation" rather than an "economic puzzle." 
**The Revised Pitch:** "In theory, punitive vacancy taxes should activate idle housing stock. In practice, England tripled these taxes over a decade while vacancies rose 50%. This paper provides the first causal evidence that vacancy taxes face a near-zero elasticity of supply because the binding constraints on empty homes are legal and structural, not financial—rendering the most popular tool for housing affordability a purely fiscal, rather than behavioral, instrument."

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**One-sentence contribution:** The paper establishes a precise null effect of vacancy taxation on housing supply using a large-scale, decade-long natural experiment in England.

- **Differentiated?** Yes. Most literature (Vancouver/Paris) is descriptive or short-term. This uses a 22-year panel and modern DiD.
- **World vs. Literature?** Currently framed as a "first causal estimate" (literature). It is stronger when it frames it as the "failure of a global policy paradigm" (world).
- **Explanation:** A smart economist would say: "It’s a paper showing vacancy taxes don't work because the owners aren't 'speculating'—they're stuck in probate or have derelict shells."
- **Make it bigger:** To be a true AER "Big" paper, it needs to quantify the *revenue vs. behavior* trade-off. If it doesn't move vacancy, is it an efficient way to raise local revenue?

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
- **Closest Neighbors:** Diamond et al. (2019) on rent control; Glaeser & Gyourko (2005) on urban decline; Sun & Abraham (2021) for methods.
- **Positioning:** It should position itself as the "vacancy" counterpart to the literature on the unintended consequences of housing interventions (like rent control).
- **Unexpected connection:** It should speak to the **Public Finance** literature on tax salience and the "Inertia" of property owners. Is the tax too small, or is the owner's shadow price of action infinite?

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC
- **Setup:** A global housing crisis has led cities to tax "empty homes" as a moral and economic imperative.
- **Tension:** England provided the perfect test case, tripling the tax to 300%—yet the results didn't budge. Why?
- **Resolution:** The "Speculator" is a myth; the "Administrator of a deceased estate" is the reality.
- **Implications:** Policy is targeting the wrong margin. We are taxing the dead and the bankrupt for being slow, not the rich for being greedy.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The distance is **Medium**. The "precise null" is high-quality, but for the AER, a null result needs to fundamentally shift a theoretical prior. 

**Single most impactful advice:** Transition the paper from "We evaluated this UK policy and it failed" to "The Theory of Empty Home Taxation: Why Supply Elasticity is Zero." Add a simple 2-period model showing where the tax fails (e.g., when the cost of 'activating' the home is a random variable with a fat tail, like legal probate).
