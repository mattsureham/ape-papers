## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper studies whether a large currency devaluation compresses all imports equally, or whether economies protect production-related imports more than consumer imports when foreign goods suddenly become expensive. Using Egypt’s 2016 devaluation, it argues that imports of capital goods and intermediates proved more resilient than final consumption goods, suggesting that import adjustment is organized by where goods sit in the value chain.

A busy economist should care because this is, in principle, a broader question than “what happened in Egypt”: when countries adjust to exchange-rate shocks, do they shed consumer imports first and preserve productive capacity? That speaks to open-economy macro, trade, industrial policy, and the welfare incidence of devaluation.

**Does the paper itself articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Almost, but not quite. The current opening is reasonably vivid, but it takes too long to get to the big claim, and it frames the paper too much as a product-level devaluation study rather than as a paper about how economies ration scarce foreign exchange under stress. The first two paragraphs should more directly foreground the general question, the empirical fact, and why the answer matters for macro adjustment and development.

**The pitch the paper should have:**

> Large devaluations are usually treated as uniform price shocks to imports. But when imported goods play very different roles in production and consumption, the adjustment need not be uniform: economies may compress discretionary consumer imports while preserving inputs and capital needed to keep firms operating.  
>  
> This paper studies Egypt’s 2016 devaluation and shows that import compression was systematically tilted along the value chain: capital goods and, to a lesser extent, intermediate inputs were more resilient than final consumption goods. The broader implication is that devaluations do not just reduce imports—they reallocate foreign exchange toward production, with consequences for pass-through, welfare, and the real effects of exchange-rate adjustment.

That is the AER version of the pitch. Right now the paper has the ingredients, but not the sharpness.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Before this paper, the standard simplification is that a devaluation raises import prices across the board and compresses imports through expenditure switching. There is heterogeneity in pass-through and demand elasticities, but the benchmark mental model remains fairly uniform.

### Tension
But imported goods are not interchangeable in their economic function. Some are inputs needed to keep firms producing; others are discretionary final goods. So the puzzle is: **when a country is hit by a large devaluation, does it reduce imports proportionately, or does adjustment sort itself along the value chain?**

### Resolution
The paper finds that, in Egypt’s 2016 devaluation, production-related imports—especially capital goods—were more resilient than final consumption goods, and that the adjustment occurred more through import values/quantities than through product disappearance.

### Implications
The implication is that devaluations may preserve productive capacity more than uniform-shock models imply, while shifting the burden of adjustment toward consumers. That matters for macro modeling, welfare analysis, and industrial policy.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
It has the bones of one, but too often it reads like a collection of results looking for a story. The strongest story is the one above. The current draft weakens that story in three ways:

1. **It overexplains side results.**  
   The capital-goods finding gets wrapped in infrastructure-program discussion early, which makes the reader wonder whether the paper is really about state procurement in Egypt rather than a general mechanism.

2. **It introduces too many literatures too soon.**  
   The reader loses the plot.

3. **It is too eager to narrate every empirical branch.**  
   Intensive margin, unit values, monthly dynamics, trade diversion, fuels, placebo, RI, etc. This makes the main narrative diffuse.

### What story should it be telling?
It should be telling a simple four-step story:

1. **Large devaluations do not just reduce imports; they force economies to choose what to keep importing.**
2. **Because imported goods occupy different positions in production, that choice should not be uniform.**
3. **In Egypt, the import basket shifted toward production-related goods and away from final consumption.**
4. **Therefore, devaluation reallocates scarce foreign exchange in a way that partially shields production and disproportionately burdens consumption.**

That is a story. Much of the current draft is evidence in search of that story rather than organized around it.
