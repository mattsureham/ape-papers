## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper studies whether a large devaluation compresses all imports equally, or whether economies protect production-related imports by necessity. Using Egypt’s sharp 2016 devaluation, it argues that imports used in production—especially capital goods, and to a lesser extent intermediate inputs—fell less than final consumer imports, implying that exchange-rate shocks reshape trade composition along the value chain rather than simply shrinking imports uniformly.

A busy economist should care because this is a broad question about how macro shocks propagate through production networks and consumption, not just a country case study about Egypt. If true, the paper speaks to exchange-rate pass-through, crisis adjustment, imported-input dependence, and the real effects of devaluations on productive capacity.

### Does the paper articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Not quite. The current opening is competent, but it takes too long to get to the real claim, and it leans into a stylized anecdote plus textbook setup rather than immediately stating the big world question. The paper’s best idea is not “Egypt had a big devaluation and I run a DiD.” It is: **devaluations may induce a systematic reordering of imports by economic function, preserving inputs to production more than goods for consumption.** That is the hook.

The first two paragraphs should say that much more directly. They should also foreground why this matters for how economists think about devaluations: not only through expenditure switching, but through endogenous import prioritization along the value chain.

### The pitch the paper should have

“Large devaluations are usually analyzed as aggregate import contractions. But in economies dependent on imported inputs, the key question is not whether imports fall—it is which imports survive. This paper shows that Egypt’s 2016 devaluation compressed consumer imports much more than production-related imports, implying that exchange-rate shocks induce an endogenous hierarchy of import adjustment along the value chain.

That fact matters because it changes how we think about the real effects of devaluation. If imported intermediates and capital goods are relatively protected, then devaluations may preserve productive capacity more than standard uniform-shock views suggest, while shifting the burden of adjustment toward household consumption.”

That is the AER-facing version of the paper.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper’s contribution is to show that a large exchange-rate devaluation reshapes import composition systematically by end use, with production-related imports proving more resilient than final consumption imports.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?

Only partially. The introduction gestures at several literatures, but the differentiation is still muddy. Right now the paper reads as adjacent to three conversations without cleanly staking out where it is new:

1. exchange-rate pass-through papers,
2. imported-input/productivity papers,
3. distributional/welfare papers on exchange-rate movements.

The paper is closest to the first and third, but its actual empirical contribution is more specific: **heterogeneity in import adjustment by position in the value chain within one large devaluation episode.** That is distinct, but the paper does not yet make it feel sharply distinct.

A smart reader may still summarize it as: “It’s a product-level DiD on Egypt showing capital and intermediate imports are less affected than final goods.” That is too method-and-setting centered. The introduction should make them say instead: “It shows that devaluations reallocate scarcity toward production inputs—there is an import hierarchy.”

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world or filling a literature gap?

Mixed, but too often the latter. The stronger framing is clearly the world question: **When a country is hit by a large devaluation, what gets cut first—consumption or production?** That is a real economic question. The current introduction too quickly pivots into literature accounting and design details.

For AER, the world question should dominate; the literature gap should support it, not substitute for it.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the intro?

Some could, but many would not. The paper currently risks “another reduced-form paper about a devaluation episode” because the introduction is overloaded with:
- detailed sample counts,
- p-values,
- robustness summaries,
- caveats,
- and secondary mechanism claims.

That density actually obscures the core novelty.

### What would make the contribution bigger?

Specific ways to make it bigger:

1. **Tie import composition to real economic stakes more directly.**  
   Right now the paper stops at imports. For AER, the natural next question is: did preserving production-related imports help sustain domestic production, employment, exports, or sectoral output? Even suggestive evidence here would materially enlarge the contribution.

2. **Make the hierarchy itself more general.**  
   The current categories are BEC bins. Bigger would be a more continuous notion of “production essentiality” or input centrality, showing a gradient rather than just three buckets.

3. **Connect more directly to household-versus-firm incidence.**  
   The paper hints that consumers bear more of the adjustment burden. That is potentially very interesting. If framed properly, this becomes a paper about the distributional incidence of devaluation through trade composition.

4. **Clarify whether the central mechanism is necessity, supplier accommodation, or state prioritization.**  
   Right now several mechanisms coexist. The story gets larger if the paper can tell us which broad force explains the hierarchy in the world.

If the author can’t expand the evidence, then the framing should narrow to one claim and make it cleaner.

If forced to pick the closest economic neighbors conceptually, I would say:
1. Burstein et al. (large devaluations),
2. Campa-Goldberg / Gopinath et al. (pass-through heterogeneity),
3. Goldberg et al. / Halpern et al. (importance of imported inputs),
4. Cravino-Levchenko (distributional consequences).

### How should the paper position itself relative to them?

Mostly **build on and synthesize**, not attack.

The right move is:
- build on devaluation/pass-through work by saying those papers largely treat category heterogeneity as secondary;
- build on imported-input papers by saying they show imported inputs matter, but say less about how those inputs are rationed during macro adjustment;
- connect to distributional papers by arguing that trade composition is one channel through which devaluations shift burdens between consumers and producers.

The paper should not posture as overturning the standard open-economy model. That would feel overstated. It should say: the standard aggregate picture is incomplete because import compression is organized along the value chain.

### Is the paper positioned too narrowly or too broadly?

Paradoxically, both.

- **Too narrowly** because it is very Egypt/event specific in exposition.
- **Too broadly** because it claims contribution to three big literatures without fully earning each one.

For AER, it needs a sharper center. My recommendation: position it primarily as a paper on **the composition of import adjustment under large exchange-rate shocks**. Then secondarily connect to pass-through and imported-input dependence.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of? What fields should it be speaking to?

It should speak more to:
- **production networks / input-output / propagation of shocks**, not just trade and pass-through;
- **trade in tasks / global value chains**, even at a high level;
- **macro-development adjustment under foreign exchange scarcity**;
- possibly **state capacity / allocative distortions** if government prioritization is a meaningful part of the story.

The current paper mentions supplier accommodation and government procurement, but not enough of the broader literature on allocation under external crises or import compression in developing economies.

### Is it having the right conversation?

Not fully. The paper currently sounds like it wants to be in the pass-through literature. That is not wrong, but it may not be the most effective conversation. The more interesting conversation is:

**How do economies triage imports when foreign exchange becomes suddenly scarce?**

That framing can pull together trade, macro, and development. It is a more original and consequential conversation than “another source of pass-through heterogeneity.”

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Before this paper, the natural benchmark view is that a devaluation makes imports more expensive and pushes expenditure away from imports. In that benchmark, imports fall, with heterogeneity mostly discussed through elasticities, pricing, or firm characteristics.

### Tension

But that misses an economically central distinction: some imports are essential for keeping production running, while others are easier to defer or substitute away from. So the puzzle is whether devaluations simply reduce imports overall, or instead reorder them in a systematic way that protects productive capacity.

### Resolution

The paper finds that after Egypt’s 2016 devaluation, production-related imports—especially capital goods—were more resilient than final consumption goods, and that the adjustment occurred mostly through lower import values rather than disappearance of product lines.

### Implications

The implication is that devaluations may be less destructive to production than aggregate import declines suggest, because the burden of adjustment falls disproportionately on consumer imports. That matters for macro adjustment, incidence, and how policymakers should think about exchange-rate crises.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the ingredients, but the execution is uneven. There is a real story here, but the manuscript often reads like a collection of estimations and caveats wrapped around that story. The introduction especially tries to do too much:
- setup,
- conceptual framework,
- main findings,
- mechanism,
- robustness,
- limitations,
- and literature review.

That flattens the narrative.

### What story should it be telling?

The story should be:

1. **Devaluations create scarcity.**
2. **Economies do not absorb that scarcity uniformly across imports.**
3. **Imports tied to production survive relatively better than imports tied to consumption.**
4. **Therefore, the real effects of devaluation depend on import composition, not just aggregate import decline.**

That is a clean AER-style arc. The current paper has this arc, but it is obscured by too much local detail and too many subclaims.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?

“I’d lead with: after Egypt’s 2016 devaluation, the import collapse was not uniform—capital goods and intermediate inputs were more resilient than consumer imports, suggesting that devaluations triage imports in favor of production.”

That is the memorable fact.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

Lean in—if presented that way. Economists care about how devaluations affect production capacity, and many have an instinctive view that exchange-rate shocks work through broad expenditure switching. A result about systematic import triage is interesting.

But if presented as “a DiD on BEC categories in Egypt,” phones come out.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

The immediate follow-up is: **why?**  
Is it because firms cannot substitute away from imported inputs? Because foreign suppliers cut prices? Because the government prioritized foreign exchange for essential imports? Because public investment kept capital goods demand high?

That follow-up question is good news and bad news. Good because it means the result is interesting. Bad because the paper currently offers several explanations and does not fully decide among them. Strategically, that weakens the punch.

### If findings are modest or mixed, is that okay?

Yes, but the paper should stop overselling the intermediate result and stop making the capital result carry every conceptual burden. The publishable fact is not “every production input category robustly survives.” It is more modest and still interesting: **there is evidence that import compression is systematically tilted away from production-related goods.**

That is enough, if framed well.
