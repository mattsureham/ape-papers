## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a clean historical economics question with broader relevance: did the Great Depression undo the economic gains Black Americans achieved by leaving the South during the Great Migration, or were those gains durable even through a massive macroeconomic shock? A busy economist should care because the answer speaks to a first-order issue in labor and inequality economics: whether mobility into a structurally better market creates fragile cyclical gains or persistent upward repositioning.

The paper does articulate a pitch in the first two paragraphs, and better than many papers do. It opens with a familiar historical claim (“last hired, first fired”) and then poses the natural empirical question: what happened to the same migrants over the boom-bust cycle? That said, the introduction currently slips too quickly into “this paper fills a gap” and “here is my instrument,” before fully staking out the world-level claim. It reads like a competent job market paper intro, not yet like the opening of an AER paper.

### What the first two paragraphs should say instead

The first two paragraphs should more sharply foreground the big fact and the belief revision:

> One of the canonical claims about racial inequality in downturns is that Black economic progress is especially fragile: when recession hits, the gains go first. In the context of the Great Migration, that logic implies that Black Southerners who climbed into better Northern jobs during the 1920s boom should have seen those gains erased during the Great Depression.
>
> This paper shows that this widely held expectation is wrong at the individual level. Using linked Census records that follow Black Southern-born men from 1920 to 1940, I find that migrants’ occupational gains not only appeared during the boom but largely survived the Depression. The broader implication is that moving into a structurally different labor market can produce durable advancement even when discrimination worsens and aggregate labor-market conditions collapse.

That is the pitch the paper should have. The instrument, leave-one-out checks, and validation exercises belong after the reader understands the world-level claim.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:**  
The paper provides individual-level evidence that Black South-to-North migrants’ occupational gains from the 1920s Great Migration persisted through the Great Depression, challenging the view that downturns mechanically erased Black mobility gains via “last hired, first fired.”

This is a potentially interesting contribution, but it is not yet differentiated with enough force from nearby work. Right now the novelty is framed mainly as “first three-decade linked panel” and “first individual-level test.” That is a start, but “first” claims are fragile and usually not enough for AER positioning. The paper needs to differentiate itself conceptually, not just data-wise.

### Is it clearly differentiated from the closest papers?
Not fully. The paper names Collins and then aggregate Great Migration papers, but the differentiation still sounds like:
- prior work shows migration gains in the 1920s,
- this paper extends the panel to 1940.

That is incremental unless framed as overturning a central substantive belief about the durability of Black progress. The contribution is stronger if stated as: existing work established that migration improved outcomes in expansion; this paper asks whether those gains survived contraction, which is the relevant test of whether migration changed level versus merely timing.

### World question or literature gap?
It starts with a world question, which is good, but then quickly retreats into gap-filling language (“nobody has followed individual migrants through the full boom-bust cycle”). The stronger version is emphatically a question about the world: **Was Black economic progress through migration fragile or durable?** The data novelty serves that question; it should not be the question.

### Could a smart economist explain what is new?
At present, a smart economist could say something like: “It’s a Great Migration paper using linked Census data to show occupational gains lasted through 1940.” That is decent, but still perilously close to “another historical migration paper with IV.” The introduction should make it easier for a reader to say: “It overturns the standard expectation that the Depression wiped out migrants’ gains.”

### What would make the contribution bigger?
Several concrete possibilities:

1. **Reframe the estimand around durability, not just a 1930–1940 coefficient.**  
   The key object is not merely “effect during the Depression decade,” but “share of the 1920s gain retained by 1940.” That is a much more intuitive and important metric.

2. **Show whether migration changed occupational rank or just prevented collapse.**  
   The current language implies resilience. The bigger claim is about persistent repositioning on the occupational ladder.

3. **Connect more directly to racial inequality under macro shocks.**  
   If the takeaway is that structural relocation dominates cyclical vulnerability, then this should be framed as evidence on how place mediates the persistence of racial inequality.

4. **Use more intuitive outcomes if available in the existing data structure.**  
   Occupational score is standard but abstract. If the data permit, occupational categories, farm-to-nonfarm transitions, manufacturing/service entry, or white-collar access would make the substantive change more concrete and legible.

5. **Sharpen mechanism by comparing “different labor market ladders” versus “temporary wartime/boom rents.”**  
   The paper gestures at this. It should be central. If the gains persisted, migration was not just catching a boom; it was escaping a low-ceiling equilibrium.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

The paper sits at the intersection of three conversations:
1. the Great Migration and Black economic progress,
2. race and labor-market cyclicality during the Depression,
3. geographic mobility as a channel of long-run advancement.

### Closest neighbors
The nearest papers, based on the intro and field, appear to be:

- **Collins and Wanamaker (2014)** on individual-level gains from the Great Migration before 1930.
- **Boustan (2010)** on migration networks and receiving-area effects.
- **Derenoncourt (2022)** on the long-run consequences of the Great Migration / place-based racial inequality.
- **Sundstrom (1992)** and related work on Black unemployment and “last hired, first fired” during the Depression.
- Probably also **Margo (1993, 1995)** on Black labor-market outcomes during this era.

### How should it position itself relative to those neighbors?
Mostly **build on and reinterpret**, not attack.

- Relative to **Collins and Wanamaker**: “They show migrants gained during expansion; I show whether those gains lasted through collapse.”
- Relative to **Sundstrom/Margo**: “Aggregate evidence of disproportionate Black cyclical harm does not imply that prior migration gains were individually reversed.”
- Relative to **Boustan/Derenoncourt**: “The paper complements place-based and aggregate studies by following individuals through a full macro cycle.”

The paper should avoid overstating that it “overturns” the entire last-hired-first-fired literature. It can say that it overturns one **implication** often drawn from that literature: that downturns erased the gains of established migrants.

### Is the current positioning too narrow or too broad?
It is currently a bit **too narrow in evidence but too broad in rhetoric**.

- Narrow because much of the paper is written as a very specific Great Migration / Depression / Black men / occupational score exercise.
- Broad because the title and “Depression Shield” framing imply a general theorem.

The best positioning is: a focused historical setting that speaks to a broader question about the durability of mobility gains under adverse shocks.

### What literature does it seem unaware of?
It should speak more explicitly to:
- **economic history of labor-market adjustment and migration persistence**, not just Great Migration papers,
- **race and cyclicality** more broadly,
- **place-based mobility and spatial inequality**, including modern mobility literature,
- possibly **resilience/persistence after shocks** literatures.

It may also benefit from speaking to modern papers on whether moving to better labor markets creates gains that survive downturns, though this should be done carefully and not in a glib “Chetty citation” way.

### Is it having the right conversation?
Partly, but not fully. Right now it is having the “Great Migration paper” conversation. The more impactful conversation is:

**When disadvantaged groups gain access to structurally better markets, are those gains merely cyclical or genuinely durable?**

That is the unexpected bridge that makes the paper more than a historical extension.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Before this paper, the world looks like this: Black Southerners migrated north and improved occupational standing during the 1920s, but historians and labor economists often view Black progress in downturns as especially vulnerable because of discriminatory firing and occupational marginality.

### Tension
The puzzle is whether migration-induced progress was real repositioning or just boom-dependent improvement. If “last hired, first fired” dominated, the Depression should have erased migrants’ gains. If migration moved workers onto a different and better occupational ladder, those gains might persist despite severe recession.

### Resolution
The paper’s answer is that the gains persisted. Migrants’ occupational advancement was not undone during the 1930s; if anything, it remained substantial by 1940.

### Implications
The implication is that access to a different labor market may matter more than cyclical exposure in determining long-run progress. More broadly, structural relocation can create durable gains even for groups facing discrimination.

### Does the paper have a clear arc?
Yes, more than many submissions. It does have setup, tension, and a punchline. But the narrative is weakened by two choices:

1. **The paper coins “The Depression Shield,”** which feels slightly overmarketed relative to the evidence and creates skepticism rather than excitement.
2. **The introduction front-loads empirical machinery and caveat-heavy language before fully landing the conceptual point.**

It is not a collection of disconnected results, but it does occasionally feel like a competent empirical exercise reaching for a grand narrative after the fact. The story it should be telling is not “I found a positive IV coefficient in 1930–1940.” It is:

**Migration created durable occupational repositioning that survived the biggest negative shock of the era.**

Everything should serve that.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?
I would lead with this:

> Black migrants who moved north in the 1920s did not lose their occupational gains in the Great Depression; by 1940, those gains had largely persisted.

That is the memorable fact.

### Would people lean in?
Some would. Economic historians, labor economists, and scholars of race and inequality would lean in. A generic economist might not unless the broader stakes are made explicit. The topic is important, but the current framing does not yet make it feel universally relevant.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Probably one of these:
- “So what exactly persisted—occupational status, earnings, or just job titles?”
- “Does this mean moving changed the long-run level of opportunity rather than just timing?”
- “Is this about the Great Migration specifically, or about mobility into better labor markets more generally?”
- “How should I reconcile this with all the evidence on severe Black labor-market harm in the Depression?”

Those are good questions; the paper should anticipate them in the framing, not just in the discussion.

### Is the result interesting enough if modest?
Yes. Even if the effect were modest, showing **non-reversal** in the face of a massive downturn is intrinsically interesting because the prevailing expectation is reversal. The paper does make that case somewhat, but it would be stronger if it explicitly said:

- the null hypothesis in the historical narrative is not zero but **negative reversal**,
- therefore finding persistence is itself a substantive contribution.

That is important. The paper should not let the reader evaluate the result as “only +3 occscore points.” The relevant benchmark is “not wiped out by the Depression.”

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

At the moment, the gap is mainly a mix of **framing problem** and **ambition problem**, with some **scope problem**.

### Framing problem
The paper has a publishable historical result but not yet an AER-sized framing of why this result changes what economists think. The current title and “Depression Shield” branding reach for bigness, but the intro still sounds like “first linked panel extension.” The author needs to stop selling a dataset extension and start selling a revision to how we think about mobility gains under adverse shocks.

### Ambition problem
The paper is careful and competent, but somewhat safe. It could be more ambitious in defining the question as:
- Is geographic mobility a path to **durable** advancement?
- Are racial progress gains in better markets more robust than aggregate cyclical statistics suggest?

That is a bigger and more AER-relevant question than “did migrants’ occscores rise from 1930 to 1940?”

### Scope problem
The scope is currently a bit narrow for AER unless the framing does more work. One outcome, one historical setting, and one main contrast can be enough, but only if the result is framed as answering a foundational question. Right now, the paper still reads like a very good field-paper contribution in economic history/labor.

### Novelty problem
Not fatal, but real. Great Migration papers are a crowded space. “Three decades instead of two” is not enough on its own. The author has to convince readers that the unanswered question was the important one all along: whether gains were durable under extreme stress.

### Single most impactful advice
**Reframe the paper around durability of mobility gains under macroeconomic shock, and organize every section around the claim that migration changed workers’ long-run position on the occupational ladder rather than merely improving outcomes in a boom.**

That one shift would do the most to move this toward AER territory.
