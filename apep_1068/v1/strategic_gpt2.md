## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a sharp historical question: did the Great Depression wipe out the occupational gains Black Southerners achieved by migrating North in the 1920s, as the “last hired, first fired” narrative suggests? Using newly linkable individual records across 1920, 1930, and 1940, the paper argues that those gains largely persisted, implying that access to a different regional labor-market structure mattered more than cyclical vulnerability during the downturn.

A busy economist should care because this is not just a historical horse race. It speaks to a broader question: when disadvantaged workers gain access to a structurally better market, are those gains fragile in recessions or durable even under severe shocks?

### Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?
Partly, but not well enough. The current opening has a good hook—the famous phrase “last hired, first fired”—but it gets to the real stakes too slowly, and then slips into “this paper fills a gap” language. The introduction should foreground the world question, not the data novelty.

### The pitch the paper should have
Here is what the first two paragraphs should say instead:

> The Great Migration is one of the central episodes in American economic history because it gave Black Southerners access to fundamentally different labor markets. But a basic question remains unanswered: were those gains durable, or did the Great Depression erase them through the “last hired, first fired” dynamics that contemporaries described?  
>   
> This paper provides the first individual-level evidence on that question by following Black Southern men across 1920, 1930, and 1940. I show that the occupational gains of 1920s migrants were not undone in the 1930s. The broader implication is that moving workers from a low-opportunity to a high-opportunity market can generate resilience, not just temporary improvement.

That is the story. The data and instrument come after that.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution
The paper’s contribution is to show, using linked individual Census data across three decades, that Black migrants’ occupational gains from the Great Migration persisted through the Great Depression rather than being reversed.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?
Only somewhat. The paper says “first three-decade individual-level test,” which is clear as a data contribution, but less clear as an intellectual one. Right now the differentiation is mostly temporal and dataset-based: Collins stops in 1930; Boustan and Derenoncourt are more aggregate/community-level; this paper extends the window. That is real, but it risks sounding incremental unless the paper is much sharper about what belief it overturns.

The most promising differentiator is not “I have one more decade of panel data.” It is: **the paper revises a canonical interpretation of Black labor-market vulnerability during the Depression by contrasting aggregate narratives with individual trajectories.** That needs to be stated much more forcefully and cleanly.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a gap in a literature?
It oscillates, but too often falls into literature-gap framing. “Nobody has followed individual migrants through the full boom-bust cycle” is useful, but second-order. The stronger frame is a world question:

- Did migration create temporary gains that collapsed in a recession?
- Or did it permanently move workers onto a better occupational ladder?

That is the frame that belongs in AER.

### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain what’s new?
They could probably say: “It’s a linked-Census paper on Black migration showing gains persisted through the Depression.” That is decent. But they might also say: “It’s another migration/IV/history paper with occscore.” The introduction is not yet distinctive enough to prevent that reaction.

The phrase “Depression Shield” is memorable, but slightly too branded relative to the actual evidentiary burden. It risks sounding like a slogan in search of a result. Better to emphasize the substantive claim and let the label be secondary.

### What would make this contribution bigger?
Be specific:

1. **Different framing:** Make the paper about the durability of access to high-opportunity labor markets under macro stress, not just about testing a famous phrase from labor history.
2. **Different comparison:** Compare explicitly the persistence of migrant gains to the persistence of other forms of Black advancement in the same period. Was migration unique in surviving the Depression?
3. **Different outcomes:** If available, stronger occupational-transition evidence would help. “Stayed out of farm work,” “remained in urban nonfarm occupations,” or “occupation rank relative to Southern counterfactual” would feel more concrete than occscore alone.
4. **Different mechanism framing:** Right now “structural access” is plausible but thin. The paper would feel bigger if it showed that the persistence came from staying above the Southern occupational floor, not necessarily from continued upward mobility per se.
5. **Different audience bridge:** Connect the historical result to modern mobility/resilience questions—whether moving to thicker labor markets creates insurance against downturns.

The easiest way to make it bigger without changing the design is to shift from “the Depression did not reverse gains” to “migration changed the margin on which downturns mattered.”

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

### Closest neighbors
The closest neighbors appear to be:

1. **Collins and Wanamaker (2014)** on the economic effects of the Great Migration using linked individual data through 1930.
2. **Boustan (2010)** on migration networks and receiving-city effects.
3. **Derenoncourt (2022)** or adjacent work on the long-run effects of the Great Migration / place-based racial inequality.
4. **Sundstrom (1992)** and **Margo (1993, 1995)** on Black labor-market outcomes and Depression-era racial gaps.
5. More broadly, **Card (2001)** for the migration-network / shift-share logic, though that is methodological ancestry, not the conversation the paper should lead with.

### How should the paper position itself relative to those neighbors?
Mostly **build on and reinterpret**, not attack.

- Build on Collins and Wanamaker by extending the horizon into the 1930s and asking whether earlier gains proved durable.
- Build on Boustan/Derenoncourt by moving from place-level or aggregate consequences to individual resilience.
- Reinterpret Sundstrom/Margo by arguing that aggregate evidence of disproportionate Black distress does not necessarily imply reversal of prior migrant gains at the individual level.

That last point is potentially the most interesting. The paper should be careful not to imply older work was “wrong”; rather, it documented a different object. The paper’s value is in showing that aggregate cyclical disadvantage and individual persistence can coexist.

### Is the paper currently positioned too narrowly or too broadly?
Currently a bit **too narrowly historical in hook, yet too broadly in concluding claims**. It opens with a very specific Depression-era slogan, then closes by drawing fairly general lessons about mobility programs and structural access. The bridge between those is underdeveloped.

The right move is not to broaden further, but to **discipline the generalization**:
- Historical core: What happened to Black Great Migration gains during the Depression?
- General economic lesson: access to a different labor-market structure may be more durable than cyclical narratives imply.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?
It should speak more directly to:
- **Economic history of Black mobility and occupational change**, beyond the handful of cited papers.
- **Migration as insurance / spatial arbitrage / labor-market resilience**.
- **Opportunity and place** literature, including modern mobility and labor-market thickness, even if only lightly.
- Possibly **race and macroeconomic shocks** literature, if there is recent work on heterogeneous cyclical incidence.

The current “Chetty et al.” style nod at the end feels tacked on. If they want that bridge, they need to earn it.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?
Almost, but not quite. Right now it is having a somewhat narrow conversation with Great Migration historians and Depression labor historians. The more impactful conversation is with economists interested in **whether place-based opportunity changes are temporary or durable under stress**. That is the unexpected literature connection that could elevate the paper.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Before this paper, the common understanding is that the Great Migration improved Black workers’ opportunities in the 1920s, but the Depression exposed the fragility of those gains through discriminatory layoffs and extreme industrial contraction.

### Tension
The puzzle is that aggregate evidence suggests severe Black labor-market distress in the North during the Depression, yet we do not know whether this actually erased the gains of the individuals who had migrated. Aggregate unemployment patterns and individual occupational trajectories may tell different stories.

### Resolution
The paper’s core result is that Black migrants’ occupational gains persisted through 1940 rather than disappearing in the 1930s.

### Implications
The implication is that migration may have delivered access to a structurally superior occupational ladder that remained advantageous even in a deep downturn. More broadly, mobility may create durable gains, not merely cyclical upside.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
It has the ingredients of one, but the execution is uneven. The story is strongest in the opening pages and in the main result. After that, the paper becomes somewhat result-driven and slogan-driven: placebo, validation, robustness, heterogeneity, “Depression Shield,” then broad policy implications. The result exists; the story around it is not yet fully disciplined.

At moments it feels like a collection of empirics organized around a catchy phrase rather than a tightly unfolding argument.

### What story should it be telling?
This one:

1. **Migration gave Black Southerners access to a different occupational structure.**
2. **The Depression is the clean historical stress test of whether those gains were fragile or durable.**
3. **Aggregate evidence points one way, but individual trajectories show another.**
4. **Therefore, downturn vulnerability and long-run occupational improvement are distinct objects.**

That is a strong AER-style narrative. Everything should serve it. Several current details do not.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would you lead with at a dinner party of economists?
“I have linked Black Southern migrants across 1920, 1930, and 1940, and the surprising thing is that the Great Depression did not erase their occupational gains from moving North.”

That is a good opener. Better than leading with the IV or the first-stage F-stat.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
Economists would lean in—at least initially—because the claim cuts against a familiar historical narrative. But they will only stay engaged if the paper quickly clarifies what exactly persisted:
- occupational rank,
- urban nonfarm attachment,
- relative advantage over Southern stayers,
- and why that matters conceptually.

If it remains too buried in occscore changes and instrument description, attention will fade.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Probably: **“So if Black unemployment was so severe in the North, how can migrant gains have persisted?”**

That is the question the paper should proactively answer. Right now it gestures at the answer—Northern service work may still dominate Southern agriculture—but does not dramatize it enough. That tension is the paper’s asset.

A second follow-up would be: **“Is this persistence about staying in better occupations, or about continued upward mobility?”** The paper should be explicit. The summary statistics suggest little raw gain in 1930–1940 for migrants, so the “improvement” language can sound stronger than the intuitive pattern. The clearer claim may be persistence of relative advantage rather than absolute upward surge.

### If the findings are modest: is the result still interesting?
Yes. Even a “no reversal” finding is interesting here, because the historical prior is that downturn discrimination should have erased gains. A well-argued null against that prior is valuable. But the paper currently overstates with phrases like “failed to even dent them.” That rhetorical inflation is counterproductive. The result is more believable and more interesting if described as **durable advantage under severe contraction**, not triumphalist resilience.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

### What is the gap?
Mostly a **framing and ambition problem**, with some **novelty-risk**.

The science, as presented, is competent and the question is interesting. But in its current form, the paper reads like a solid economic history paper with one strong finding, not yet like a must-read AER paper. Why?

- The contribution is presented too much as “first individual-level test over three decades.”
- The outcome and interpretation are narrower than the rhetoric.
- The broader lesson is asserted rather than fully developed.
- The paper has not yet convinced me that it changes the conversation beyond this episode.

### Is it a framing problem?
Yes, primarily. The paper undersells the biggest intellectual move available to it: **reconciling aggregate evidence of racialized cyclical distress with durable individual gains from place-based mobility.** That is the part economists will remember.

### Is it a scope problem?
Somewhat. The paper could use a more concrete demonstration of what “persistence” means. If it had richer evidence on occupational transitions or escape from agriculture as the margin of resilience, the story would feel larger and more grounded.

### Is it a novelty problem?
Potentially. “Great Migration improved outcomes” is not new; “those gains persisted into 1940” is newish but still close to existing terrain. To feel AER-worthy, the paper must present this not as an extension but as a **revision of how we interpret a major historical episode**.

### Is it an ambition problem?
Yes. The paper is a bit too safe in its empirical packaging and a bit too loud in its rhetoric. It needs more conceptual ambition and less branding.

### Single most impactful advice
If the author changes only one thing, it should be this:

**Reframe the paper around the broader economic question of whether access to a better labor market creates durable gains under macroeconomic stress, using the Great Migration and the Depression as the sharp historical test case.**

That would immediately improve the title, introduction, literature positioning, and conclusion.
