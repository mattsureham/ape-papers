### 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
This paper uses a newly available three-decade individual-level panel to test whether the Great Depression erased the economic gains of the Great Migration for Black men. While the "last hired, first fired" narrative suggests these gains were volatile and temporary, the paper finds that the structural move from Southern agriculture to Northern industry provided a "shield" that allowed migrants to maintain their higher occupational status even during the 1930s. This is a "persistence of shocks" story that challenges a core tenet of 20th-century American economic history.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this well in the first paragraph, but the second paragraph gets bogged down in data mechanics.
**The "AER Pitch":** The first two paragraphs should focus less on the MLP linkage and more on the **structural vs. cyclical** debate. It should say: *"Economists have long viewed the economic progress of Black Americans as highly cyclical, epitomized by the 'last hired, first fired' hypothesis. By following the first longitudinal cohort through the full boom-bust cycle of 1920–1940, I show that the gains of the Great Migration were not a cyclical fluke, but a structural step-change that even the Great Depression could not reverse."*

### 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**Contribution:** The paper provides the first individual-level causal evidence that Great Migration occupational gains were durable across the Great Depression.

*   **Differentiation:** It moves beyond **Collins & Wanamaker (2014)**, who only look at the boom (1910–1930), and **Boustan (2010)**, who uses aggregate data. It is the first to follow the *same people* into the 1930s.
*   **World vs. Literature:** It answers a question about the **WORLD** (Did the Depression ruin the Great Migration?).
*   **Clarity:** A smart economist would say: "It's the longitudinal test of the 'last hired, first fired' myth."
*   **Bigger Contribution:** To truly elevate this, the author needs to dig deeper into **mechanisms**. *Why* did they survive? Was it union protection? Was it the specific types of service jobs? Was it that the "floor" in the North was simply higher than the "ceiling" in the South? Currently, it's a bit of a "black box" result.

### 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
The paper sits at the intersection of **Labor Economics (Racial Inequality)** and **Economic History**.
*   **Neighbors:** Boustan (2017), Collins & Wanamaker (2014), Derenoncourt (2022), and Sundstrom (1992).
*   **Strategy:** The paper should **"respectfully challenge"** the Sundstrom/Urban League narrative. It shouldn't say they were wrong about unemployment rates, but that they were wrong about the *permanent erosion* of human/social capital.
*   **Unexpected Connection:** The author should connect more to the **Place-Based Effects** literature (e.g., Chetty/Hendren). Is the "Shield" a property of the person or the city?

### 4. NARRATIVE ARC
*   **Setup:** The Great Migration was the path to the middle class for Black Americans.
*   **Tension:** The 1930s were a catastrophe. Did this progress vanish?
*   **Resolution:** No. The gains persisted.
*   **Implications:** Structural transitions (moving from farm to factory) are more powerful than cyclical headwinds.

**Evaluation:** The narrative is strong. However, the "Resolution" section needs more work on the **Stayer Placebo**. The author admits the instrument might be picking up regional spillovers. This weakens the "Migration" story and turns it into a "Northern Proximity" story. They need to resolve this tension better.

### 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
*   **The Lead Fact:** "Black migrants who moved North in the 20s didn't just keep their gains during the Depression—they actually continued to move up the ladder relative to where they started."
*   **Reaction:** People will lean in. It's counter-intuitive.
*   **Follow-up:** "But what about the 25% Black unemployment rate in Chicago?" The author's answer (aggregate vs. individual trajectories) is the core of the paper's value.

### 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
*   **Move to Appendix:** The "MLP linkage" technicalities in Section 3 can be trimmed.
*   **Front-load:** The sign reversal from OLS to IV (Section 5.1) is a major "hook" and should be mentioned earlier as evidence of how selection masks the "Shield" effect.
*   **Conclusion:** Needs to be less summary and more "Big Picture." What does this tell us about modern-day economic mobility?

### 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The distance is **Medium**. The science is solid, but the "Wow" factor is currently hampered by two things:
1.  **The Exclusion Violation:** The stayer placebo (Section 4.1) is a red flag for a top-5 journal. If the instrument helps people who *didn't* move, then the "Depression Shield" isn't just about migration; it's about being near the North.
2.  **The "Why":** We need to see *sectors*. Did those in manufacturing get crushed while those in government/service survived?

**Single Biggest Advice:** Resolve the instrument exclusion issue by either (a) finding a more restrictive instrument or (b) leaning into the "Structural Access" narrative—admit the effect is "Access to Northern Markets" (both via migration and proximity) rather than just "Relocation."
