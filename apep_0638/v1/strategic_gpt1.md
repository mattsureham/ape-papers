## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether compulsory military service can improve young men’s labor market outcomes when service is light-touch and functions mainly as a credential rather than as intensive training. Using Mexico’s long-running military lottery, the paper argues that eligibility for Saturday military service increases employment and formal-sector attachment, not by building skills, but by granting access to a credential required for some jobs and bureaucratic transactions.

A busy economist should care because, if true, this flips the usual conscription story. Instead of military service depressing civilian labor market outcomes through lost experience, this setting suggests that state-issued compliance credentials can move workers into the formal sector in a high-informality economy.

**Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Not quite. The opening is vivid, but the introduction leads with “large randomization hiding in plain sight” and “no one has studied it,” which is not the real reason this paper matters. The first two paragraphs should foreground the substantive question: **when does conscription act as a labor market tax, and when does it act as a gateway to formality?** Right now the paper sounds like “here is a neat natural experiment,” when the stronger version is “here is a case that changes how we think about military service and labor market formalization.”

**The pitch the paper should have:**

> Military conscription is usually thought to harm civilian careers by interrupting work and schooling. But in economies where state-issued compliance documents gate access to formal jobs, military service may do the opposite: it may operate as a credential that unlocks formal employment.  
>  
> This paper studies Mexico’s compulsory military lottery, which assigns young men to Saturday-only service that minimally disrupts weekday work but generates the *cartilla militar*, a credential used in public-sector hiring and other formal transactions. I show that exposure to this system is associated with substantially higher employment and formal-sector participation, with little evidence of earnings gains, suggesting that the main effect of conscription here is bureaucratic formalization rather than human capital accumulation.

That is the AER-relevant story. The current draft has pieces of it, but they arrive too late and are diluted by design-first exposition.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution in one sentence:**  
The paper’s core contribution is to argue that in Mexico, compulsory military service increases employment by acting as a formal-sector credential rather than by building productive skills, thereby reframing conscription as a mechanism of labor market formalization in a developing economy.

### Evaluation

**Is this clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?**  
Only partially. The paper cites the conscription literature and says Mexico is different because service is Saturday-only, but the differentiation is still too generic. “This is not like Vietnam because it is not full-time service” is true but not yet sharp enough. The more precise differentiation is:

1. Prior conscription papers mostly study **earnings penalties from labor market interruption**.
2. This paper studies **formalization effects from credential acquisition**.
3. Prior settings are mostly high-income or wartime/full-time service settings.
4. Mexico is a setting where the **formal/informal margin is first-order** and the service format barely withdraws labor supply.

That needs to be stated much more crisply.

**Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or filling a gap in the LITERATURE?**  
It is split between the two, but too often leans on “no one has studied this lottery.” That is a literature-gap framing and it is weak. The stronger world question is: **Can the state use compliance obligations to pull young men into the formal labor market, even absent skill gains?**

**Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the intro?**  
Not confidently. Right now many would summarize it as: “It’s a DiD paper on Mexico’s military lottery showing male employment rises after age 18.” That is not enough. The “new” must be stated as: **conscription can raise employment when its main output is a credential valued in formal labor markets.**

**What would make the contribution bigger?**  
Several possibilities:

- **Make the credential channel the centerpiece.** The paper currently asserts it, but the story would be much bigger if the outcomes were organized around access-constrained activities: public-sector jobs, occupations likely to require documentation, passport-holding, migration-related outcomes, or other bureaucratic margins.
- **Move beyond “employment/formal employment” to labor market transitions.** If the contribution is formalization, then transitions into wage work, social-security-covered jobs, or documented employment are more central than broad employment.
- **Exploit variation in where the credential matters more.** A convincing cross-sector or cross-occupation framing would make the world question larger: where do bureaucratic credentials matter most?
- **Tie the paper to a broader idea:** the economic consequences of administrative documentation requirements. That would enlarge the audience beyond military-economics readers.

The paper currently has an interesting idea, but not yet the full architecture of a big contribution.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
The standard view is that conscription harms civilian labor market outcomes because it displaces work experience and schooling. Meanwhile, in developing economies, formal-sector access is often shaped by paperwork, credentials, and state-facing compliance requirements.

### Tension
Mexico’s military service looks unlike canonical conscription settings: it is Saturday-only, so the opportunity cost is small, but it produces a credential that may matter for formal jobs and bureaucratic access. That creates a real conceptual tension: **is this military service, or is it labor market certification by another name?**

### Resolution
The paper finds large increases in employment and formal employment, with little effect on conditional earnings, and interprets this as evidence that the military system works through formalization rather than skill accumulation.

### Implications
If the interpretation is right, economists should update two beliefs:

1. Conscription’s labor market effects depend fundamentally on treatment design.
2. Administrative credentials can be a major, underappreciated determinant of formal labor market participation.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the ingredients, but the current manuscript still feels somewhat like **a collection of results looking for the strongest story**. The data tables and estimates come through, but the conceptual hook is underdeveloped. The paper keeps alternating between three narratives:

1. “This is a large randomization no one has studied.”
2. “This is a military-service paper.”
3. “This is a formalization-through-credentialing paper.”

Only the third is potentially AER-level. The first is trivial; the second is respectable but likely niche unless the effect is truly paradigm-shifting. The paper should tell one story: **the state can formalize labor through bureaucratic credentials generated by mandatory service.**

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?

“Mexico’s compulsory military service appears to raise formal employment a lot, not because it teaches useful skills, but because completing it gives men a credential needed for formal jobs.”

That is the line.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

They would lean in at first, because it is counterintuitive and reverses the usual conscription result. But the next question would come immediately.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Probably:  
**“Wait, is this really the effect of the lottery, or just the fact that men’s labor supply rises relative to women’s at age 18?”**

That is precisely why, strategically, the paper cannot afford to market itself too hard as “the labor market returns to Mexico’s compulsory military lottery” unless the design more directly maps to the lottery. Again, not a referee point here, but a positioning point: the title and framing overpromise a lottery-based causal estimate, while the empirical object as presented sounds closer to a gender-by-age discontinuity/DiD around lottery eligibility. That mismatch is dangerous editorially.

### If findings are modest or null
The findings are not modest. They are large. The issue is not significance; it is whether the result is interpreted in a way that feels genuinely informative rather than mechanically induced by broad lifecycle patterns. The paper needs to convince readers that the finding is a substantive fact about credentialing and formality, not just “men work more after 18.”

The reader needs to learn immediately:

That should come before the econometric setup.

**3. Compress the empirical strategy in the introduction.**  
The intro currently spends too much space walking through the design. For editorial positioning, the paper should lead with question, mechanism, and finding; the design should be summarized in a sentence.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

At present, the gap is mostly a **framing + scope + ambition** problem.

### Framing problem
This is the biggest issue. The paper is selling a design (“military lottery”) more than a question (“how administrative credentials shape access to formal work”). AER papers need a claim about the world that readers remember.

### Scope problem
The outcomes are still too generic for the mechanism being claimed. If the paper wants to be about formalization through credentialing, it needs to show more of the places where the credential bites. Employment and formal employment are a start, but not enough for a top-journal-level mechanism story.

### Novelty problem
There is some novelty, but as currently written it risks sounding like a local application of a familiar template. “Another DiD around an age threshold” is not enough. The novelty becomes real only if the paper clearly demonstrates a new conceptual channel: **compulsory state service as labor market certification**.

### Ambition problem
The paper is competent but safe. It reports standard labor outcomes and lightly interprets them. To excite the top people in labor/development/public economics, it needs to be more ambitious in claiming and demonstrating what this setting teaches us about informality, documentation, and state capacity.

### Single most impactful advice

**Stop framing this as an overlooked military lottery paper and recast it as a paper about how state-issued compliance credentials shape formal labor market access in a high-informality economy.**

Everything else follows from that. Once the paper adopts that frame, the title, introduction, literature review, outcome prioritization, and discussion all become clearer and more consequential.
