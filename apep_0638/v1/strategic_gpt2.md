This paper wants to be about the labor-market effects of Mexico’s compulsory military lottery, a huge and longstanding policy that has never been studied. The economically interesting claim is that military service in this setting does not build skills or reduce earnings, but instead acts as a bureaucratic credential that pushes young men into formal employment.

The problem is that the paper’s current pitch promises one paper and delivers another. It sells “returns to Mexico’s compulsory military lottery,” but the empirical object described in the introduction is really a gender-by-age discontinuity/DiD around age 18, not evidence tied directly to lottery assignment. That disconnect is a strategic framing problem, not just a technical one, and it matters enormously for whether this belongs in the AER conversation.

## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

### What is this paper about, and why should a busy economist care?

This paper studies whether a compulsory military-service institution can affect labor markets not through skill formation or labor-market interruption, but through credentialing. In Mexico, military registration and service generate a document—the *cartilla militar*—that appears to function as a gateway into formal employment, suggesting that state-imposed civic compliance can shape labor-market formalization in a developing economy.

That is an interesting question. Busy economists should care because it connects two large literatures that usually do not speak to each other: conscription and informality. If true, the paper’s central fact is not “military service has returns,” but “a civic/military credential can move workers into the formal sector without changing wages.”

### Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Not really. The current first two paragraphs lean too heavily on “one of the world’s largest randomizations” and “no economist has studied it,” which sets up the expectation that the paper uses lottery assignment directly. But the paper does not observe lottery outcomes, does not compare white-ball to black-ball men, and does not exploit variation in treatment intensity in a way that matches the title’s promise. So the opening currently overclaims on design and underdevelops the genuinely interesting economic question.

### What should the first two paragraphs say instead?

The pitch should be rewritten around the world question, not the data novelty:

> In many developing economies, access to formal jobs depends not just on skills but on bureaucratic credentials. This paper studies whether one such credential—Mexico’s military service booklet—changes young men’s labor-market trajectories by opening access to formal employment.
>
> Mexico provides a striking setting. Since 1942, men have been required to register for military service at age 18, and completion yields the *cartilla militar*, a document commonly required for government jobs, passports, and some formal-sector positions. I show that male employment and formal employment rise sharply relative to women at the age of military eligibility, with no corresponding increase in conditional earnings, a pattern consistent with credential-driven formalization rather than human-capital accumulation.

That is the honest, interesting paper. If the author wants to keep “lottery” in the title and abstract, the framing must be much more careful about what is actually identified.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper’s contribution is to argue that Mexico’s military-service institution increases young men’s formal employment by acting as a labor-market credential rather than by building human capital.

Only partially. The paper distinguishes itself from classic conscription papers by emphasizing Saturday-only service and the formal/informal margin. That’s promising. But it does not yet sharply separate itself from:

1. conscription papers showing labor-market effects of military service;
2. developing-country papers on informality and state regulation;
3. papers on identity documents, credentials, and bureaucratic barriers to labor-market access.

Right now the differentiation is mostly “new setting + different sign.” That is not enough for AER. It needs to be “new mechanism + new margin.”

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a literature gap?

It oscillates, but too often it is framed as “no one has studied this lottery.” That is a literature-gap argument, and a weak one. The stronger framing is about the world: in economies with large informal sectors, state credentials may matter as much as skills in shaping employment. The paper should lean hard into that.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?

Not confidently. Right now they might say: “It’s another quasi-experimental paper on military service, except in Mexico, and the outcome is formal employment.” That is too close to “another DiD paper about X.”

What they should be able to say is: “This paper shows that military service can matter because it creates a bureaucratic credential that opens formal-sector access, which flips the standard conscription narrative.” That is much better.

### What would make the contribution bigger?

Several concrete possibilities:

- **Make the key outcome more directly about formal-sector access.** Government employment, passport holding, sector/occupation composition, contracts, benefits, tax registration, firm size—anything that more directly links the *cartilla* to bureaucratic access rather than generic employment.

The paper would be much bigger if it more convincingly organized the contribution around one of these channels—especially government/formal jobs, where the credentialing story is sharpest.
- **Use the 2025 reform as framing, even if not estimable yet.** Not as a teaser paragraph, but as a motivating policy question: what happens when a state turns a rationed credential into a near-universal one?
- **Reframe away from “military service returns” and toward “bureaucratic credentialing and formalization.”** That is the bigger idea.

It should **build on** the conscription literature but **pivot away** from competing head-on with it. The point is not “the existing conscription literature got the sign wrong.” The point is “existing conscription studies mostly analyze settings where service removes men from civilian work; this setting reveals a different mechanism because the relevant margin is credentialing in a high-informality economy.”

Relative to informality papers, it should **synthesize** rather than attack: formalization can be induced not only by tax/enforcement margins or firm regulation, but also by worker-side credential requirements.

Oddly, both.

The right audience is not “people interested in obscure military lotteries.” It is economists interested in labor-market access, informality, and how the state allocates opportunity through documentation.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?

It seems under-attentive to:

These literatures could give the paper a more surprising and important conversation to join.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?

Not yet. It is currently trying hardest to have the conscription conversation. That is understandable because the institution is military service. But the more impactful conversation is: **how state-imposed credentials shape formal labor-market entry in developing countries.** That is the right conversation.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

In standard conscription settings, military service is thought to affect civilian labor markets through human capital loss, delayed labor-market entry, or veteran effects. In developing economies, however, formal labor-market access often depends on administrative credentials and compliance with state documentation requirements.

### Tension

Mexico’s military system is unusual: service is part-time and yields a credential. So does compulsory service in this context harm careers, do nothing, or instead expand access to formal jobs? More broadly, are we misreading military service if we ignore the role of bureaucratic gatekeeping?

### Resolution

The paper finds a sharp rise in employment and formal employment for men after the age of military-service eligibility, with little evidence of earnings gains conditional on employment. The author interprets this as evidence that the institution operates through formalization/credentialing rather than skill acquisition.

### Implications

If the interpretation is correct, then labor-market formalization can be shaped by state credentials in ways that standard labor or development models may underemphasize. Policy changes that relax, universalize, or eliminate such credentials could materially affect entry into formal work.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the ingredients of one, but currently it is more a collection of findings and claims than a fully coherent story. The story keeps shifting among:

- “huge randomization nobody studied,”
- “conscription but with a different sign,”
- “formalization paper,”
- “credentialing paper,”
- “institutional note with future potential.”

It needs to pick one. The best story is the credentialing/formalization story.

A useful test: if you removed the word “military” from the paper and replaced it with “state-issued compliance certificate,” would the economics become clearer? In some sense yes—which suggests the paper’s real contribution is not military service per se.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

Right now, the gap is mostly a **framing + ambition** problem, with some **scope** issues.

- **Framing problem:** The paper is pitched as lottery-based causal evidence on military service, but it reads as suggestive evidence on a male-at-18 institutional threshold. That mismatch weakens confidence and makes the contribution feel smaller.

- **Novelty problem:** Some novelty is real, but as currently framed it risks looking like a familiar design in a new setting.

### Single most impactful advice

**Rebuild the paper around the claim that state-issued compliance credentials can shape entry into formal employment, and make every section serve that claim rather than the broader “military lottery” branding.**

If the author can only change one thing, it should be the framing. The current title, abstract, and introduction promise direct evidence on lottery-assigned military service. Unless the paper can truly deliver that, it should instead sell the stronger and more credible contribution: this institution reveals a credentialing channel into formal work.
