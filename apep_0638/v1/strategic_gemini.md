## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
The paper uses Mexico’s massive, long-running military lottery—a "natural experiment hiding in plain sight"—to estimate the labor market returns of a low-intensity, non-disruptive form of conscription. It finds that instead of the "scarring" effect documented in US and European contexts, this service acts as a powerful formalization shock, increasing employment and formal sector access by over 11 percentage points because the military certificate is a required credential for "good" jobs. 

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this reasonably well, but it leads too much with the "scale" of the randomization rather than the **theoretical inversion**. 

**The pitch it should have:** 
"Standard theories of conscription predict human capital depreciation and delayed labor market entry. By studying Mexico’s unique 'Saturday-only' lottery, I show that when military service acts as a prerequisite for civic and economic participation, it functions as an involuntary formalization shock. I find that this 'credentialing' effect overcomes any human capital loss, increasing formal employment by 11.6 percentage points—a result that flips the canonical Angrist (1990) finding on its head for developing economies."

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**Contribution:** The paper identifies a "credentialing channel" of military service that facilitates entry into the formal labor market, contrasting with the "human capital/experience loss" channel dominant in the literature.

- **Differentiation:** It is clearly differentiated from Angrist (1990) and Card/Cardoso (2012) by the *nature* of the treatment (Saturday training) and the *outcome* (formalization vs. earnings).
- **Question:** It answers a question about the **WORLD** (specifically, how bureaucratic barriers and state-mandated credentials shape the formal/informal divide).
- **Explanation:** A smart economist could explain this easily: "In Mexico, military service is basically a work permit for the formal sector, so the lottery actually helps men get better jobs."
- **Bigger Contribution:** To make it bigger, the author needs to explore **long-run persistence**. Does this jump-start a career trajectory, or is it just a one-time entry effect that fades by age 35? The current window (ages 18-30) is a start, but 30-50 would prove it’s not just "shifting" the start date.

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
- **Neighbors:** Angrist (1990) [Conscription]; Ulyssea (2018) [Informality]; Levy (2008) [Mexico Labor]; Galiani et al. (2011) [Draft Lotteries].
- **Positioning:** It should **attack** the universality of the "conscription is bad for earnings" narrative. It needs to position itself as the "Development Economics" answer to the Labor Economics conscription literature.
- **Unexpected Connection:** It should speak to the literature on **"State Capacity and Legibility."** The *cartilla* is a way the state makes citizens "legible" to the formal sector. Connecting to James Scott (Seeing Like a State) or the Besley/Persson work on state capacity would elevate the framing.

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC
- **Setup:** Conscription is globally viewed as an economic tax/disruption.
- **Tension:** Mexico has a massive randomization that hasn't been studied, and it's "low-stakes" (Saturdays), which should theoretically minimize the tax—but the *credential* is high-stakes.
- **Resolution:** The lottery doesn't change skills (null earnings), but it unlocks the formal sector (huge employment jump).
- **Implications:** In countries with high informality, the state can "nudge" formalization through non-economic bureaucratic requirements.

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong. It’s a "clean" story.

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
- **The Fact:** "Winning the Mexican military lottery increases your chances of having a formal job by 30 percent, even though you only spend 44 Saturdays drilling."
- **Reaction:** People lean in. It’s counter-intuitive.
- **Follow-up:** "Wait, so is this just a tax on people who don't serve? Or is the military actually signaling something to employers?" This is the "So What" the author needs to nail.

---

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
- **Front-loading:** The description of the *cartilla militar* needs to move even earlier. The reader needs to know *why* a Saturday service would matter for a job before they see the DiD.
- **Appendix:** Table 6 (Standardized effects) is useful but can stay in the appendix. 
- **The 2025 Change:** Move this to the conclusion or a "Discussion" section. It's a "future work" point and distracts from the current empirical clarity.

---

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
**The Gap:** Currently, it feels like a very high-quality *Applied Economics* or *Journal of Development Economics* paper. To reach AER, it needs to move from "this happened in Mexico" to a **generalizable theory of bureaucratic credentials.**

**Ambition:** The paper is currently "competent but safe." It relies on a male-female DiD. An AER-level version would try to find a way to use the **within-male** variation (perhaps by state-level intensity of the lottery or historical changes in ball-draw ratios) to validate the gender DiD. 

**Single Biggest Advice:** Strengthen the **Mechanism** section. Prove it is the *certificate* and not the *social network* or the *discipline* gained on Saturdays. Can you find a group that gets the certificate without the Saturdays? Or a sector where the certificate isn't required? Without a tighter grip on the "Why," it remains a "what."
