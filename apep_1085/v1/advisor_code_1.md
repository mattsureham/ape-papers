**Idea Fidelity**

The submitted draft diverges appreciably from the original manifest. The manifest envisioned a county-level analysis exploiting the USWTDB‘s 701 treated counties, species-specific (raptor/grassland) composition outcomes from eBird, a staggered DiD with dosage, triple differences, placebo tests, and an explicit effort to show eBird as a credible monitoring source. The paper instead collapses to a state-year panel (38 treated states) and focuses solely on the raptor reporting rate (with a waterfowl placebo). Key elements from the manifest—county-level variation, species richness/community similarity indices, dose-response with turbine counts or MW, and mechanism testing (e.g., hub height, grassland share)—are absent. The manifest’s promise to establish detailed compositional shifts and to leverage the continuous 744 million-record eBird database for granular community analysis is not fulfilled in the paper.

**Summary**

The paper studies whether the staggered expansion of wind capacity across U.S. states (2000–2023) affected the proportion of raptor observations in eBird data. Using state and year fixed effects, the author estimates continuous and binary DiD specifications and reports a precisely estimated null effect: cumulative wind capacity has no statistically significant impact on raptor reporting rates. Event studies and robustness checks (drop Texas, alternative thresholds, placebos) are presented to support the finding. The paper concludes that wind-related raptor mortality, albeit nonzero, is too small relative to population stocks to register at the scale of the analysis.

**Essential Points**

1. **Identification and aggregation.** The paper’s identification strategy is fragile because it aggregates turbine treatment to the state level and thus mixes treated and untreated counties within the same unit. Wind development is strongly localized (Great Plains core vs. coastal states), and state-level aggregation risks conflating treatment with other concurrent state-specific ecological or economic policies. The parallel-trends claim should be substantiated with more granular evidence (e.g., county-level leads/lags or placebo regions) before inferring causality from the state-level event study. Without exploiting within-state variation, omitted time-varying confounders correlated with both wind investment and raptor reporting could drive the null.

2. **Outcome measurement and effort controls.** The primary outcome is the reporting rate (raptor sightings over total eBird counts), but the paper neither justifies why the denominator is immune to selection nor presents robustness to alternative normalizations (e.g., checklist-level effort weights, seasonal subsets, or effort-adjusted species modeling). Uncontrolled shifts in birding behavior—say, more checklists near wind farms or a changing mix of habitats surveyed—could alter reporting rates independently of actual raptor populations. The continuous treatment specification relies on assumption that log capacity is strictly exogenous once state and year fixed effects are included; however, wind farms tend to be built in areas with particular ecological characteristics that also influence eBird participation. This concern is compounded by the absence of key covariates (e.g., land-use change, habitat quality proxies, noise in observation effort) and diagnostic plots (e.g., treatment timing vs. eBird growth).

3. **Deviation from the original goal (compositional analysis).** The paper promises to detect compositional shifts within avian communities but only considers a single taxonomic group (raptors) and lacks any of the manifest’s proposed outcomes (grassland birds, species richness, community similarity, functional groups). The null finding is therefore limited to raptor share without establishing whether wind energy reshuffles the broader community. The paper also omits the manifest’s proposed dose-response tests (log turbine counts, cumulative MW) beyond the simple log capacity and lacks an explicit triple-difference comparison across sensitive vs. insensitive species. Without these additions, the paper fails to deliver on its stated goal and may underestimate compositional effects.

If the authors cannot convincingly address these issues, the paper is not ready for publication in this format.

**Suggestions**

1. **Reintroduce the spatial granularity and compositional breadth outlined in the manifest.**  Use the county-level (or finer) variation in the USGS database and link it to eBird observations with richer spatial resolution (e.g., county or hex grid). This will help isolate treatment effects from cross-state heterogeneity and permit tests of compositional shifts beyond raptors. Consider implementing stratified analyses (Great Plains vs. coastal states) and, where feasible, include multiple species groups (grassland specialists, generalists) and biodiversity measures (richness, Bray–Curtis, etc.). This would align the empirical work with the manifesto’s aspiration to show species-level restructuring, not just aggregate share changes.

2. **Strengthen the DiD identification and falsification approach.**  The current event study relies on only 37 treated states and 12 untreated states; reporting pre-trends that are flat at the state level may mask heterogeneity. Augment the DiD with variation in treatment intensity and timing at the county level, or use matching/subclassification to ensure treated and control units are comparable on land cover, human population density, and pre-treatment birding trends. Explicitly contrast counties within treated states that receive turbines with those that do not. The manifest mentioned triple differences (raptors vs. non-sensitive species)—implement that specification or a similar heterogeneity test to isolate the collision-risk channel, and add more placebos (e.g., species with declining trends unrelated to turbines) to reassure readers that the null is not due to aggregation.

3. **Improve the handling of effort and observer bias in eBird.**  Reporting rates can change if birders concentrate on certain habitats or seasons; the paper should control directly for checklist effort (duration, distance, number of observers) and explore weighting observations accordingly. Consider estimating hierarchical models at the checklist level (e.g., logistic regression for raptor detection controlling for effort and seasonality) before aggregating to the state-year level. Alternatively, construct panel data at the ecoregion-year level or restrict to checklist protocols with consistent detection probabilities. Include diagnostics showing that effort-adjusted outcomes mirror the baseline result.

4. **Clarify the scope and limitations of the null result.**  The conclusion currently reads as if the null extends to all raptor populations, yet the analysis only examines proportional representation in eBird at the state level over 16 years. Explicitly acknowledge that localized declines (e.g., near specific wind farms) or impacts on rare, long-lived species could be masked. It would help to contextualize the statistical power, perhaps via minimum detectable effect sizes or by comparing the size of hypothesized ecological impacts (from mortality estimates) to the variation in the outcome. This would temper policy conclusions and align the narrative with the manifest’s mention of scale and substitution effects.

5. **Document the data linkage and cleaning procedures.**  The manuscript currently summarizes the data sources but lacks details on how eBird observations were aggregated to the state-year panel (e.g., what qualifies as a “valid” state-year, how duplicate records were handled, how missing observation years were addressed). Provide a data appendix outlining these steps, including any filtering (e.g., minimum number of checklists) and how the reporting rate denominator is constructed. This transparency will reassure referees that the analysis is reproducible and not driven by inadvertent selection.

Addressing these suggestions would bring the paper closer to the original vision, bolster the identification, and justify the null result more convincingly.
