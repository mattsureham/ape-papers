## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple and policy-relevant question: when a rich country with near-universal school access raises the compulsory schooling age, does the legal mandate improve young people’s labor-market outcomes? Using Finland’s 2021 reform, the paper argues that the answer is no: extending legal obligation appears to shift some marginal students into continued education, but not into better employment, suggesting that in high-income settings the key constraint may be motivation or match rather than access.

Why should a busy economist care? Because the classic compulsory-schooling literature is built on settings where laws relaxed genuine access constraints; this paper wants to test whether that logic survives in a modern welfare state where access is already close to universal. If that framing holds, the paper speaks to a first-order policy question, not just to Finland.

### Does the paper articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Not quite. The ingredients are there, but the introduction spends too much time reciting old compulsory-schooling returns and too little time stating the sharp, modern question. The current opening sounds like “another compulsory schooling paper,” when the real hook is “do legal mandates still matter once access barriers are gone?”

The first two paragraphs should say something like:

> Governments around the world are raising the school-leaving age on the assumption that keeping teenagers in school longer will improve their long-run outcomes. But almost all of the evidence supporting this policy comes from settings where schooling laws relaxed real access constraints—school supply was limited, schooling was costly, or families relied on child labor. We know much less about whether the same policy works in rich countries where school is already free, completion is already high, and the remaining non-completers are disproportionately disengaged rather than excluded.
>
> Finland’s 2021 reform offers a rare test of that question. By extending compulsory education from age 16 to 18 in a high-income welfare state, the reform asks whether legal obligation alone can improve transitions to work when access is not the main problem. This paper shows that it does not: the reform does not improve post-graduation employment, though it does modestly increase continued schooling. The broader lesson is that compulsory schooling laws appear to work differently when they target motivation rather than access.

That is the pitch. It is much stronger than the current version because it is about the world.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### Contribution in one sentence

The paper claims to show that extending compulsory education in a high-access welfare state does not improve early labor-market outcomes, implying that the effectiveness of school-leaving laws depends on whether they relax access constraints or instead try to overcome student disengagement.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?

Only partially. The paper cites the standard compulsory-schooling canon, but the differentiation is still more asserted than demonstrated. Right now the contribution reads as:

1. first Finland paper,
2. first recent rich-country reform,
3. null effect in a welfare-state setting.

That is not yet enough. “First in Finland” is not an AER contribution. “First recent Nordic reform” is closer, but still sounds like institutional novelty standing in for intellectual novelty.

The paper needs to more directly contrast itself with at least three nearby literatures/questions:

- classic returns-to-compulsory-schooling papers in historical/low-access settings,
- modern papers on marginal students / disengagement / noncognitive constraints,
- recent evaluations of completion mandates or upper-secondary reforms in Europe.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or as filling a gap in a LITERATURE?

It aspires to the former, but repeatedly slips into the latter. The strongest version is: **When the main barrier is not access, legal mandates alone may not raise human capital or employment.** That is a world question. The weaker version is: **There is no causal evidence yet on Finland’s reform.** That is a literature gap.

The paper should purge most “first causal evaluation in X” language from the front end and lead with the world question.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?

A smart economist could probably say: “It’s a DiD/DDD paper on Finland’s extension of compulsory schooling, and they find no labor-market effect.” That is not enough. The current framing does not quite elevate the paper above “another policy evaluation with a null.”

The economist should instead be able to say: “This paper argues that compulsory-schooling laws work when they solve access problems, but not when they are aimed at disengaged students in already-high-access systems.” That is a memorable contribution.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Specific ways to enlarge it:

- **Longer-run outcomes.** The current one-year-after-graduation employment measure is too cramped for the scale of the claim. If the paper wants to say “mandates don’t improve labor-market outcomes,” it needs either a longer horizon or a much more careful claim that it is about short-run transition outcomes.
- **Completion and attainment outcomes.** The story is fundamentally about whether the mandate changed schooling behavior. Employment is downstream. If the reform didn’t increase completion, qualifications, credits, or meaningful participation, that becomes a stronger and more interpretable contribution.
- **Heterogeneity by baseline disadvantage.** The most interesting version is probably not “zero on average,” but “zero where disengagement dominates, possibly positive where material constraints still matter.” That would connect directly to the access-versus-motivation thesis.
- **Mechanism evidence on the nature of “continued education.”** Is this genuine human-capital accumulation, delayed completion, or administrative parking? Right now the mechanism is suggestive rhetoric, not an empirical payoff.
- **Cross-country or cross-reform framing.** Even without new data, the paper could more explicitly position Finland as an out-of-sample test of the canonical compulsory-schooling logic. The paper’s ambition is comparative; the evidence is single-country. It needs to bridge that gap intellectually.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

### Closest neighbors

The obvious neighbors are:

- **Angrist and Krueger (1991)** on returns to schooling using compulsory-schooling variation.
- **Acemoglu and Angrist (2000)** on human capital externalities and compulsory schooling.
- **Oreopoulos (2006)** on historical compulsory-schooling laws and earnings.
- **Harmon and Walker (1995)** on UK school-leaving-age reforms.
- Likely also **Pischke and von Wachter / Pischke (2007)**-type papers on weak returns in later European reforms.
- On broader interpretation, **Heckman, Stixrud, and Urzua (2006)** or adjacent noncognitive-skill work is a relevant conceptual neighbor, though not a direct design neighbor.

Potentially missing are more recent European papers on upper-secondary completion, dropout prevention, and vocational-track reforms. The paper may also need to engage the literature on **student engagement, school-to-work transitions, and vocational education** more directly, because that is where the mechanism lives.

### How should the paper position itself relative to those neighbors?

Mostly **build on** and **re-interpret**, not attack.

The paper should say:

- The classic literature established that compulsory-schooling laws often raised schooling and earnings.
- Those settings shared a common feature: the law was shifting students past a real access margin.
- Finland is a test of whether the same policy tool works when the remaining margin is disengagement rather than exclusion.

That is a constructive extension of the old literature, not a takedown.

### Is the paper positioned too narrowly or too broadly?

At present, both.

- **Too narrowly** in the empirical execution: lots of Finland-specific institutional detail, region-by-sector design talk, and internal validity language.
- **Too broadly** in the claims: “implications for dozens of countries” and big language about the limits of legal obligation.

The right level is: broad question, disciplined claim. “This case suggests compulsory education extensions may have limited short-run labor-market effects in high-access settings unless paired with interventions that change engagement or learning.” That is broad enough to matter and narrow enough to be credible.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?

It seems under-connected to:

- dropout prevention and re-engagement programs,
- vocational education and training literature,
- student engagement / behavioral barriers / noncognitive skill formation,
- school-to-work transition literature,
- policy design papers on mandates versus subsidies/services.

Right now the literature review is still dominated by the old Mincer/IV compulsory-schooling canon. That is necessary, but insufficient for the paper’s actual claim.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?

Not fully. It is currently having the conversation “what are the returns to compulsory schooling?” The more interesting conversation is “when do mandates work, and when do they fail because the binding constraint is not legal access?” That second conversation reaches beyond education and into public economics and policy design more generally.

That unexpected connection—to the broader economics of mandates when take-up constraints are behavioral rather than financial—could be the paper’s strongest positioning move.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Historically, compulsory-schooling reforms often raised education and later earnings because they forced students to stay in school when leaving early was easy or necessary.

### Tension

In modern rich countries, the students still at risk of dropping out may not be constrained by school fees, availability, or legal permission to stay; they may be constrained by disengagement, poor match, weak noncognitive skills, or low returns to the offered track. If so, simply extending legal obligation may not produce the old gains.

### Resolution

Finland’s reform does not generate detectable short-run employment gains, though it may increase continued schooling.

### Implications

Policymakers should not assume that extending the school-leaving age is a portable, one-size-fits-all tool. In high-access environments, complementary interventions aimed at engagement, match quality, or support may matter more than the mandate itself.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the bones of one, but the paper is not consistently organized around it. There is a good story in here, but the manuscript keeps interrupting that story with design exposition and a somewhat mechanical “three contributions” paragraph.

The main weakness is that the **resolution does not yet fully match the ambition of the setup**. The setup is about human capital and the limits of legal obligation. The resolution is about one-year employment after graduation. That mismatch makes the story feel somewhat overclaimed.

### What story should it be telling?

Not “Finland’s reform had no employment effect.”

Instead:

**Compulsory-schooling laws are not universally effective; their impact depends on the margin they move. Finland shows what happens when policy targets students whose main barrier is disengagement rather than access: legal obligation may extend enrollment without improving early labor-market outcomes.**

That is a paper. The current version sometimes lapses back into “null DDD estimate in Finland.”

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?

I would lead with:

> Finland raised the school-leaving age from 16 to 18 in a country where secondary education was already essentially free and widely accessible—and it did not improve early employment outcomes.

That is the dinner-party line.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

Some would lean in, because the policy is salient and the setting is unusual. But many would reach for their phones if the next sentence is just about a triple-difference coefficient and a confidence interval.

The way to keep attention is to immediately add the conceptual punchline:

> The result suggests that compulsory-schooling laws work differently when they solve access problems than when they try to fix disengagement.

That is what turns a null result into an idea.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Likely one of three:

1. **Did the reform increase completion or actual learning?**
2. **Are the students just staying enrolled longer, without gaining anything?**
3. **Is one year after graduation too early to see benefits?**

Those are exactly the questions the paper should anticipate and structure itself around.

### Is the null result itself interesting?

Yes, potentially very interesting. But the paper has not yet fully earned that interest.

Nulls are publishable at the top when they discipline an influential prior belief or policy consensus. This paper could do that if it convincingly shows that the belief “raising the school-leaving age helps” is context-dependent, not general. Right now it is still a bit too close to “we didn’t find much in Finland.”

To make the null feel valuable rather than failed, the paper should emphasize:

- the policy is widely used,
- prior evidence comes from very different margins,
- Finland is a theoretically important test case,
- the null meaningfully revises external validity, not just one estimate.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The main gap is **not primarily econometric** for present purposes. It is a mix of **framing, scope, and ambition**.

### Is it a framing problem?

Yes, significantly. The paper has a better idea than its current self-presentation suggests. It should be framed as a test of the external validity of the classic compulsory-schooling logic in a high-access setting.

### Is it a scope problem?

Yes. The evidence base is too narrow relative to the size of the claim. One-year employment after graduation is not enough to carry “the limits of legal obligation” unless paired with stronger schooling-behavior outcomes or longer-run trajectories.

### Is it a novelty problem?

Partly. The empirical design and setting are new enough for a field journal, maybe a strong field journal, but not automatically for AER. The intellectual novelty lies in the **context-dependence of mandates**; the manuscript has to make that explicit and convincing.

### Is it an ambition problem?

Yes. The paper is competent but safe. It wants to make a large conceptual point, but the evidence is presented in a relatively modest policy-evaluation template. To excite the top people in the field, it needs to be bolder conceptually while also tightening the exact claim.

### The single most impactful piece of advice

**Reframe the paper around a general proposition—compulsory-schooling mandates work when they relax access constraints, but not necessarily when they target disengagement—and then align the evidence to that proposition by showing more directly what the reform did to educational attainment/participation, not just one-year employment.**

That is the one thing. If the paper does that, it may become a meaningful contribution. If it does not, it risks reading like a well-executed but narrow null-result policy evaluation.
