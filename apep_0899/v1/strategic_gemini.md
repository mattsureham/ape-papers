## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
This paper evaluates the labor market impact of Finland’s 2021 extension of compulsory education from age 16 to 18—the first such reform in a high-income Nordic country in decades. Using a triple-difference design, it finds that the mandate failed to improve employment for the marginal students it targeted, suggesting that when financial and access barriers are already removed, legal compulsion alone is an ineffective tool for human capital development. This is a "powered null" that challenges a cornerstone of global education policy.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this pitch excellently. It identifies the "access versus motivation" tension immediately. 
**The pitch the paper should have (Optimization):** The current intro is strong, but to truly grab an AER reader, it should emphasize the *institutional* perfection of Finland as the "stress test" for human capital theory.
*Suggested Revision:* "Does legal compulsion create human capital when opportunity is already universal? Finland’s 2021 reform provides a unique laboratory to test this: in a state with no tuition, generous subsidies, and high baseline completion, we find that a mandate to stay in school until 18 has zero effect on employment. This suggests a 'motivation ceiling' for compulsory schooling laws that has been invisible in historical data from less-developed welfare states."

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**Contribution:** The paper establishes that the marginal return to compulsory schooling is zero in an environment where the binding constraint is student disengagement rather than institutional or financial access.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It differentiates itself well from the "classic" literature (Angrist, Duflo, Oreopoulos) by highlighting the different *margin* (age 16-18 in a modern welfare state vs. age 12-14 in industrializing ones).
*   **World vs. Literature:** It frames itself as answering a question about the **world** (Why do modern school-leaving laws seem to fail?).
*   **Clarity:** A smart economist would say: "It's the paper that shows compulsory schooling laws have hit diminishing returns in Scandinavia."
*   **Growth:** To make the contribution "bigger," the paper needs to move beyond the 1-year post-graduation employment window. The "So What?" is currently hampered by the short-run nature of the data (2021-2024).

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
The paper sits at the intersection of Labor and Education Economics.
*   **Neighbors:** Oreopoulos (2006) on LATE of schooling; Pischke & von Wachter (2008) on zero returns in Germany; Brunello et al. (2009) on the "Scandi" exception; and Heckman et al. (2006) on non-cognitive skills.
*   **Strategy:** It currently *synthesizes* these by using Finland as the "boundary case." 
*   **Missed Conversations:** It should speak more to the **"Job Guarantee" / "Workfare"** literature. If the government "parks" students in schools to satisfy a mandate (as the paper suggests), this is essentially a youth transition subsidy. Is it more efficient than direct labor market programs?

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC
*   **Setup:** Compulsory schooling is a global policy darling with legendary returns in historical data.
*   **Tension:** Modern Finland has removed every barrier except the law. If the law changes and nothing happens, the "Human Capital" model of schooling might be secondary to a "Signaling" or "Engagement" model at this specific margin.
*   **Resolution:** A precisely estimated zero for employment, but a "parking" effect in continued education.
*   **Implications:** Policy shouldn't just mandate presence; it must solve for engagement.

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong. It isn't just a collection of results; it's a targeted strike on a specific policy assumption.

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
At an econ dinner party, you lead with: **"Finland forced kids to stay in school until 18, and it didn't help a single one of them get a job."**
*   **Response:** People lean in. They ask: "Did they actually stay, or did they just ignore the law?" and "What were they doing instead?"
*   **The "Null" Problem:** Because the SEs are tight (0.132), the null is informative. It’s not "we can't find an effect," it's "we can rule out the effects that politicians promised."

---

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
*   **Front-loading:** The "Motivation vs. Access" framework in the Discussion (Section 6) is actually the paper's best intellectual contribution. It should be moved earlier, perhaps into a formal (but brief) conceptual framework section before the results.
*   **Appendix:** The leave-one-region-out and alternative intensity measures are standard; they don't need much space in the main text.
*   **Data limitation:** The 1-year-out employment outcome is a bit thin. If the author can find *any* proxy for "quality of schooling" during those extra two years (test scores, crime records), it would bolster the "parking" vs. "learning" argument.

---

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The paper is currently a very high-quality *Applied Economics Letters* or *Journal of Policy Analysis and Management* paper. To be **AER**, it needs to bridge the gap from "evaluation of a Finnish law" to "a fundamental statement on the limits of the State's ability to mandate human capital."

**The Single Most Impactful Advice:**
Expand the "Student Continuation" result (Table 2, Col 4). This is the "smoking gun" of the paper—it shows the mandate *did* something (moved kids into more school), but that "something" had zero market value. If the author can prove these were "low-quality/warehousing" programs, the paper moves from a "null result" to a "theory of policy failure" that is much more AER-worthy.
