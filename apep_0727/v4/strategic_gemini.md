## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper uses a decade of German solar data to show that when regulatory thresholds (exemptions) intersect with modular technology and professional intermediaries, they don't just cause "bunching"—they create a "threshold trap" that can wipe out an entire segment of a market. By exploiting a four-stage natural experiment (no policy, kink, notch, and abolition), the author finds behavioral distortions (bunching ratios of 50-90) that are an order of magnitude larger than those found in the standard tax and labor literature.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this very well. The first two paragraphs clearly define the "threshold trap" and provide a visceral empirical hook (the "missing middle"). 

**The pitch the paper should have:** (The current pitch is excellent, but to make it more "AER," I would lean harder into the "Intermediated Optimization" angle): 
> "Standard public finance assumes that optimization frictions like inattention and complexity attenuate the distortions caused by tax notches. This paper shows that in markets where decisions are delegated to repeat-optimizing professional intermediaries—such as solar installers or tax preparers—these frictions disappear, turning minor administrative thresholds into 'threshold traps' that cause extreme real investment losses."

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution:** The paper identifies "professional intermediation" and "technological modularity" as the specific catalysts that amplify behavioral responses to regulatory notches from marginal effects into market-shaping distortions.

*   **Differentiation:** It differentiates itself by its *magnitude*. While Kleven (2016) or Saez (2010) show people respond to notches, this paper shows a response so extreme that it creates a "missing middle" (the 10-13kWp range effectively ceased to exist).
*   **World vs. Literature:** It frames itself as answering a question about the **world** (Why did German solar capacity stagnate?) and uses the **literature** (Bunching) as the tool. This is a position of strength.
*   **What would make it bigger?** To reach the "super-AER" level, the author needs to prove the *Intermediary* mechanism more directly. Currently, it is an "interpretation supported by indirect evidence." If they could link specific installers to specific bunching patterns (showing that more "professional" firms bunch more precisely), the contribution moves from a case study to a fundamental law of intermediated markets.

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

*   **Closest Neighbors:** Saez (2010) on kinks; Kleven & Waseem (2013) on notches; Garicano et al. (2016) on firm-size thresholds; Chetty et al. (2011) on optimization frictions.
*   **Positioning:** The paper "attacks" the existing literature's consensus that bunching is usually modest. It builds a bridge between Public Finance and Environmental Economics.
*   **Conversation:** It is having the right conversation. By connecting the "behavioral" side of bunching to the "industrial" side of solar installers, it speaks to a broader audience than a pure "tax bunching" paper.

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

*   **Setup:** Germany wants to subsidize solar; they use a 10kWp threshold for administrative ease.
*   **Tension:** The threshold creates a "notch." Theory predicts bunching, but existing empirical evidence says it should be mild.
*   **Resolution:** The response is actually *catastrophic*. The 10-13kWp market segment collapses because the cost of crossing the threshold (surcharges) dwarfs the cost of adjusting (removing one modular panel).
*   **Implications:** "Administrative convenience" is a dangerous design principle in modular, intermediated markets. Graduated phase-ins are not just better; they are essential.

**Evaluation:** The narrative arc is exceptionally strong. It reads like a "forensic investigation" of a policy failure.

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

*   **The Lead Fact:** "In 2018, there were 8,287 solar systems at 9.9 kWp and exactly *one* at 10.1 kWp."
*   **The Reaction:** Economists will lean in. That ratio (8,000:1) is unheard of in tax data.
*   **The Follow-up:** "Why didn't the government fix this for seven years?" (The paper answers this: they eventually did, providing the "off" switch for the experiment).

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

*   **Front-loading:** The paper is well front-loaded. The "four-break" design is the star of the show and is highlighted early.
*   **Buried Treasure:** The "Module Count" evidence (Section 6.2) is the most "real" part of the paper—it proves this isn't just people lying on forms. I would move some of this visual evidence (Figure 5) earlier to kill the "reporting bias" objection before it even arises in the reader's mind.
*   **Appendix:** The 30kWp evidence in the appendix is actually quite strong; if the paper needs more "heft," that should be brought into the main text as a replication of the 10kWp finding.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The gap is **Mechanism Proof**. 

The "Threshold Trap" theory relies on three pillars: Modularity, Stakes, and Intermediaries. The paper proves Modularity and Stakes beyond a reasonable doubt. The **Intermediary** pillar—that professional installers are the ones doing the optimizing—is currently an "interpretation." 

**Single most impactful advice:** Find a way to "see" the installers. Even if the MaStR data is anonymized, can you use geographic variation in installer density? Can you show that in regions with more experienced installers, the bunching "turned on" faster in 2014? If you can show that *sophistication* drives the "trap," this becomes a landmark paper on the economics of intermediation.
