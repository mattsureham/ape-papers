This paper studies how Germany’s 10 kWp solar-policy threshold altered the size distribution of rooftop PV systems, arguing that the 2014 self-consumption surcharge exemption created an extreme notch-induced bunching response and materially reduced installed solar capacity. The paper is ambitious, policy-relevant, and based on an impressively large administrative dataset. The core descriptive fact—a dramatic spike just below 10 kWp that appears, intensifies, and attenuates with policy changes—is striking and likely important. The paper’s central substantive contribution is potentially publishable in a strong field journal and perhaps, with substantial strengthening, in a general-interest outlet.

At the same time, I do not think the paper is yet publication-ready for a top general-interest journal or AEJ: Economic Policy. The main reason is not that the central pattern is uninteresting, but that the causal and quantitative claims are stronger than the empirical design currently warrants. In particular, the identification for the headline causal claims is partly persuasive descriptively, but the inferential and measurement framework is not yet strong enough for the more ambitious claims about the magnitude of bunching, the welfare/capacity loss, and the mechanism. The paper needs a more rigorous treatment of bunching identification, mass reallocation, specification sensitivity, treatment timing, and alternative non-policy sources of heaping in capacity data.

Below I detail the main issues and how I think the paper could be substantially improved.

## 1. Identification and empirical design

### A. What is convincing

The strongest aspect of the paper is the repeated policy variation at the same threshold: no threshold, kink, notch, threshold increase, and eventual abolition. This within-threshold, over-time design is much stronger than a one-shot bunching exercise. The on/off pattern at 10 kWp is genuinely compelling, especially the large change after 2014 and attenuation after 2021/2022. The annual and monthly timing evidence in Sections 4–5 is helpful, and the fact that the response occurs at the exact policy threshold rather than diffusely across the distribution is important.

The paper is also right to emphasize that the same threshold and same broad market provide a cleaner comparison of kink versus notch incentives than many existing papers.

### B. What is not yet sufficient

#### 1. The paper leans too heavily on “smooth counterfactual density” without adequately confronting technological heaping

Section 4 argues that the counterfactual density is smooth through 10 kWp, supported by modest pre-2012 bunching. But the paper itself later documents very large non-policy heaping at 7 kWp due to common module configurations (Section 8.3 and Appendix Table A2). This is important: once one acknowledges that module-count discreteness and changing panel wattages can create substantial technological bunching at specific capacities, the smooth-density assumption near 10 kWp becomes less automatic than the paper suggests.

To be clear, I do not think the 2014–2020 10 kWp spike is fully explained by technology; the policy timing argues strongly against that. But the existence of strong technology-driven heaping means the paper cannot simply treat the pre-policy smoothness test as dispositive. The right interpretation is narrower: policy appears to amplify mass at 10 kWp dramatically above a non-smooth baseline generated by focal values and discrete module combinations. That is still an important result, but it is weaker than the current framing.

The paper needs a more systematic treatment of baseline heaping and module-configuration mass points:
- show the full fine-grid distribution around 10 kWp in each regime,
- document whether 9.9, 9.92, 9.96, 9.99, etc. correspond to common panel-wattage/module-count combinations,
- compare the 10 kWp pattern to nearby pseudo-thresholds with similar technological salience but no policy discontinuity.

#### 2. The “four-break design” is powerful descriptively, but not yet a fully formal identification strategy

Section 4 presents the four-break institutional structure as if it delivers identification “without relying on cross-sectional comparisons or parallel-trends assumptions.” That overstates the case. What the paper has is a highly suggestive event pattern, but not a formal design that separately identifies each regime effect from confounding changes in:
- module efficiency and panel size,
- reporting/registration behavior,
- FIT schedules and other contemporaneous reforms,
- changing economics of self-consumption and battery adoption,
- market composition during the 2020–2024 solar boom.

The text acknowledges some of these but does not fully integrate them into the empirical design. For example, the paper repeatedly states that “no alternative explanation predicts all four directional changes.” That is too categorical. A combination of policy changes and secular technology changes could jointly affect the distribution, and the empirical burden is to quantify how much of the effect is policy.

I would recommend reframing the identification more modestly: the paper provides unusually strong quasi-experimental evidence that policy thresholds materially affected sizing behavior, with repeated within-threshold timing variation strongly supporting a causal interpretation. That is credible. But the exact bunching magnitudes and welfare quantities remain model- and specification-dependent.

#### 3. Treatment timing is not fully coherent with the periodization

The paper repeatedly uses annual or multi-year pooled bins despite mid-year policy changes:
- EEG 2014 effective August 1, 2014,
- Osterpaket effective July 1, 2022.

The paper notes that 2014 and 2022 are mixed-regime years (Table 1 notes; Section 5.2), but then still uses pooled periods such as “2014–2020” and “2021–2022” as core treatment groups. For descriptive exposition this is acceptable, but for headline estimates it is problematic.

If the argument is that policy timing is extremely sharp—indeed within one month—then mixing treated and untreated months into annual or pooled periods weakens coherence. The concern is not bias direction only; it is that the paper sometimes interprets pooled estimates causally without fully respecting treatment timing.

At minimum, the main results should be re-estimated on a monthly basis or with exact effective-date windows:
- pre-August 2014,
- Aug 2014–Dec 2020,
- Jan 2021–June 2022,
- July 2022 onward.

The annual tables can remain secondary.

#### 4. The “threshold raised to 30 kWp” regime still leaves a 10 kWp FIT kink, so interpretation of attenuation is not clean

The paper acknowledges this, but the main text at times treats the 2021 change as though the 10 kWp notch simply disappeared and the remaining bunching is residual. Yet if a meaningful FIT kink remained, then the post-2021 distribution is not a pure placebo or post-treatment null. This matters for the interpretation of attenuation magnitudes and for the decomposition of kink versus notch effects.

The paper should more explicitly separate:
- the effect of the surcharge notch,
- the effect of the FIT kink,
- any residual round-number or configuration bunching.

The current “kink contribution” and “notch contribution” calculations in Section 6 are too informal for that task.

## 2. Inference and statistical validity

### A. Strengths

The paper reports standard errors and confidence intervals for main bunching estimates. It also reports sample sizes and uses bootstrap inference, which is much better than providing point estimates only.

### B. Major concerns

#### 1. The bootstrap does not solve the central statistical problem: model dependence of the counterfactual

The main uncertainty here is not sampling variation in a dataset with millions of observations; it is uncertainty from functional-form choice. The paper itself shows very large sensitivity:
- pre-specified family: 52–98,
- broader grid: 47–144.

That is enormous. Yet the reported SEs are tiny, e.g. 1.0 around a baseline estimate of 86.5 in Table 3. Those SEs reflect only resampling uncertainty conditional on a chosen polynomial/window specification. They do not reflect specification uncertainty, which is first-order here.

As a result, the paper currently gives a misleading impression of statistical precision. The practical uncertainty is much wider than the bootstrap CIs suggest.

The fix is not merely to mention sensitivity in Section 8. It should be integrated into the main inferential presentation. For example:
- report estimator-family ranges alongside point estimates in the main table,
- present model-averaged or sensitivity-robust intervals,
- move away from highlighting a single “baseline” as if precisely estimated.

#### 2. The bunching implementation appears not to enforce or verify credible mass balance

Table 5 reports a “mass balance” of 28.1 for the baseline specification. This is a red flag. In standard bunching applications, excess mass below the threshold should be reconciled with missing mass above the threshold over a plausible range, or else one must explain where the excess mass is coming from. A mass-balance ratio of 28 suggests the estimated excess mass is vastly larger than missing mass in the chosen exclusion region. That can happen if displaced mass comes from far above the threshold, but it can also indicate severe misspecification of the polynomial counterfactual or an exclusion window that is too narrow relative to the true affected region.

This matters a lot because the headline bunching ratio and the implied welfare/capacity calculations depend on where the counterfactual density is anchored.

The paper needs:
- a transparent mass-balance analysis across windows,
- evidence on where the “missing mass” is located,
- justification for why the chosen window captures the true manipulated region,
- ideally a notch-specific structural interpretation or at least a reallocation map.

Without that, the headline ratio of 86.5 is not yet fully credible as a measured economic object.

#### 3. Negative placebo “bunching ratios” are not a sufficient placebo design

Section 8.3 reports placebo thresholds with negative estimated bunching ratios. This is not especially informative in the current form, because applying a bunching estimator to arbitrary non-threshold points can mechanically produce noisy or negative estimates depending on local curvature and polynomial fit. What matters more is whether the 10 kWp change is unusual relative to nearby thresholds after accounting for baseline heaping and technological mass points.

The more persuasive placebo would be a formal permutation/randomization exercise:
- estimate period-by-threshold changes at many candidate thresholds,
- show where 10 kWp lies in that empirical null distribution.

Given the emphasis on “only the policy threshold exhibits a break aligned with policy timing,” the paper should formalize that statement statistically.

#### 4. Monthly event-study inference is underdeveloped

The monthly event-study graph is potentially very informative, but the text relies on:
- visual jumps,
- monthly bootstrap intervals,
- linear pre-trend tests.

This is not enough for a top journal. The monthly series should be estimated in a formal interrupted-time-series or event-study framework that allows for:
- anticipation windows,
- phase-in/project pipeline dynamics,
- seasonality,
- evolving sample size.

The current claims about “within one month” effects and “no anticipation” for 2021 are plausible, but not yet backed by a formal enough design.

## 3. Robustness and alternative explanations

### A. Robustness strengths

The paper does not hide sensitivity and includes several useful robustness exercises:
- alternative polynomial degrees,
- alternative exclusion windows,
- annual breakdown,
- placebo thresholds,
- geographic heterogeneity,
- some mechanism evidence from module counts.

This is all helpful.

### B. Where robustness remains insufficient

#### 1. Specification sensitivity remains too consequential

The estimate varies from about 47 to 144 across the larger grid. That is not a side issue; it is central. A general-interest paper cannot rest its headline quantitative claims on a specification-sensitive bunching estimator without a stronger argument for why the preferred specification is correct.

The paper’s response—pre-specifying a 3x3 family—is helpful but not enough, especially since the paper seems to have been written after looking at the data. “Pre-specified” within the paper is not the same as pre-registered or externally committed. A referee cannot evaluate that as strong protection against tuning.

The authors should either:
- develop a more principled estimator choice,
- use an alternative nonparametric density-counterfactual method,
- or recast the paper so the key contribution is the robust qualitative pattern rather than a precise quantitative bunching ratio.

#### 2. Alternative explanations tied to panel technology need deeper treatment

The paper asserts that technology improvements would shift the full distribution gradually, not create a threshold spike. That is directionally reasonable but too quick. Technology can interact with focal thresholds: as panel wattages increase, some module-count combinations may increasingly map just below 10 kWp, mechanically amplifying heaping there. That does not explain the entire policy pattern, but it could affect magnitudes substantially.

Given the paper’s own module-count evidence, this is testable. The authors should stratify by:
- module wattage bands,
- module-count cells,
- roof/installation characteristics if available,
- regions with differential diffusion of newer modules.

A key question is: conditional on module technology, does the threshold effect still jump sharply after 2014 and attenuate after 2021/2022? That would be much more convincing.

#### 3. Mechanism claims exceed the evidence

The paper’s central reduced-form claim is strong. The mechanism claim—professional installers as repeated optimizers—is plausible but not demonstrated. To the paper’s credit, the Discussion section partially acknowledges this. Still, the abstract and introduction lean too hard on installer intermediation as if identified.

The current evidence for the installer mechanism is indirect:
- institutional knowledge,
- module-count patterns,
- uniformity across states,
- absence of installer identifiers.

That is not enough to make intermediation a central explanatory result. It is best presented as an interpretation or organizing framework, not a demonstrated mechanism.

#### 4. The ground-mount placebo is too underpowered to carry much weight

The appendix notes \(N=325\) ground-mount observations in the relevant window during the surcharge period. That is too small for this placebo to be probative. Fine to include, but it should not be relied on.

## 4. Contribution and literature positioning

### A. Contribution

The paper’s main contribution is potentially substantial: an unusually clean example of threshold-induced real distortion in a climate-policy setting, with repeated policy variation at a single threshold. That is novel and interesting.

The paper also has a potentially valuable conceptual message: when thresholds apply to modular technologies and decisions are intermediated, distortions can be much larger than in classic tax bunching settings.

### B. Literature positioning needs strengthening

The paper cites the canonical bunching literature, but it should engage more deeply with work on:
1. modern bunching and density estimation methods,
2. manipulation tests and local density approaches,
3. renewable-energy adoption under nonlinear tariff structures,
4. salience/intermediation in household energy decisions.

Concrete papers to add or engage more directly with include:

- **Chetty, Friedman, Olsen, and Pistaferri (2011)** or related work on adjustment frictions and salience, beyond the brief citation to Chetty.
- **Cattaneo, Jansson, and Ma** on density discontinuity/manipulation testing and robust nonparametric density methods. Even if the design is not an RDD, these tools are relevant for local density behavior at a threshold.
- More recent **difference-in-differences / event-study cautions** are less central here since the design is not a standard staggered DiD, but the paper’s “event study” language would benefit from more formal empirical framing.
- Domain-specific work on **distributed solar adoption, net metering/nonlinear tariffs, and self-consumption incentives** should be expanded. The current policy literature discussion is thin relative to the importance of the application.

I would also encourage the authors to distinguish more clearly from standard tax-bunching papers: the novelty is not just “a bigger b,” but a real-investment setting with modular capital and policy reversals.

## 5. Results interpretation and claim calibration

### A. Over-claiming on exact magnitudes

The paper’s qualitative result is much stronger than its exact quantitative result. Yet the abstract and conclusion foreground highly precise numbers:
- bunching ratio 86.5,
- welfare bounds 100–135 MW,
- “order of magnitude above typical tax settings.”

Those claims should be toned down or broadened to reflect real uncertainty. The evidence supports:
- very large bunching relative to baseline,
- substantial missing mass above threshold,
- likely meaningful foregone capacity.

It does **not** yet support a precise welfare/capacity estimate with the level of confidence implied.

### B. Welfare interpretation is too strong for the underlying evidence

The “foregone capacity” calculations in Section 7 are interesting but not well enough identified to serve as a headline welfare result.

Problems:
1. The pre-policy and post-abolition periods are poor clean counterfactuals because technology, prices, tariffs, self-consumption economics, and market composition changed substantially.
2. The selected [10,13) range is plausible but somewhat ad hoc.
3. The calculation assumes displaced systems would otherwise have been at median capacities from other periods.
4. No structural welfare framework is provided; the paper is really estimating deployment loss, not welfare in the full Harberger sense.

I would recommend reframing this section as:
- “illustrative deployment implications” or
- “bounds on capacity displacement,”
not welfare.

### C. “Pure allocative inefficiency” is too strong

Section 7.4 calls the distortion “pure allocative inefficiency” and states that a smooth phase-in would have raised similar revenue without creating the dominated region. That is plausible, but not established. To say “similar revenue” requires a quantitative counterfactual revenue analysis; the paper does not provide one. Nor does it establish that all displaced capacity was inefficiently foregone in equilibrium once administrative complexity and compliance costs are considered.

This should be softened.

### D. Comparison to the bunching literature needs care

The paper repeatedly emphasizes that the solar estimate is far larger than typical tax bunching estimates. That is likely true, but cross-paper bunching-ratio comparisons are delicate because:
- definitions of the running variable and bins differ,
- counterfactual estimation methods differ,
- technological discreteness differs,
- baseline focal-point heaping differs.

This comparison is informative, but it should be framed as suggestive rather than exact.

## 6. Actionable revision requests

### 1. Must-fix issues before acceptance

#### Must-fix 1: Rebuild the main empirical design around exact policy timing
- **Issue:** Core pooled treatment periods mix pre- and post-reform months, especially 2014 and 2022.
- **Why it matters:** The paper’s causal interpretation relies heavily on sharp policy timing.
- **Concrete fix:** Re-estimate all headline results using monthly data and exact treatment windows: pre-Aug 2014, Aug 2014–Dec 2020, Jan 2021–June 2022, July 2022 onward. Keep annual tables as descriptive supplements.

#### Must-fix 2: Address specification uncertainty as a first-order inferential issue
- **Issue:** Bootstrap SEs are tiny relative to the much larger specification sensitivity.
- **Why it matters:** Current uncertainty reporting materially understates how imprecisely the bunching magnitude is known.
- **Concrete fix:** Report estimator-family ranges in the main table; provide model-averaged or sensitivity-robust intervals; explain why the preferred specification is credible; consider alternative nonparametric counterfactual estimators.

#### Must-fix 3: Provide a serious mass-balance and missing-mass analysis
- **Issue:** The reported mass-balance ratio of 28.1 is concerning and underexplained.
- **Why it matters:** Without credible reallocation accounting, the baseline bunching estimate may reflect counterfactual misspecification.
- **Concrete fix:** Show excess and missing mass by distance from threshold; vary windows systematically; explain where displaced mass comes from; consider a notch-oriented approach that explicitly models the affected region.

#### Must-fix 4: Reframe and strengthen the welfare/capacity-loss section
- **Issue:** The current 100–135 MW estimate is too aggressively interpreted given weak counterfactual support.
- **Why it matters:** This is one of the paper’s headline claims.
- **Concrete fix:** Recast as “capacity displacement bounds”; perform sensitivity to alternative counterfactual periods and ranges; avoid welfare language unless supported by a more explicit framework.

#### Must-fix 5: More rigorously rule out technology-driven heaping as an explanation for magnitudes
- **Issue:** The paper acknowledges technological bunching at 7 kWp but does not fully integrate that lesson for 10 kWp.
- **Why it matters:** Non-policy heaping affects the validity of the smooth-counterfactual assumption and the measured magnitude of policy effects.
- **Concrete fix:** Stratify by module wattage/module-count technology cells; show whether the policy-induced shift remains within common configuration groups; add richer placebo/permutation threshold analyses.

### 2. High-value improvements

#### High-value 1: Formalize the monthly event study
- **Issue:** Monthly figures are persuasive visually but under-modeled econometrically.
- **Why it matters:** The timing evidence is central to identification.
- **Concrete fix:** Estimate interrupted time-series/event-study models with anticipation windows, month fixed effects/seasonality, and dynamic treatment effects.

#### High-value 2: Calibrate mechanism claims more carefully
- **Issue:** Installer intermediation is plausible but not directly identified.
- **Why it matters:** The “threshold trap” framework relies heavily on this channel.
- **Concrete fix:** Recast installer intermediation as an interpretation unless stronger evidence can be brought in; if possible, merge installer identifiers or use richer proxies.

#### High-value 3: Improve counterfactual validation around 10 kWp
- **Issue:** The paper relies on one pre-period smoothness check.
- **Why it matters:** Local density assumptions are central.
- **Concrete fix:** Show fine-bin density plots by period; compare 10 kWp to nearby placebo thresholds and to permutation-based null distributions of break magnitudes.

#### High-value 4: Strengthen the kink-versus-notch decomposition
- **Issue:** The current decomposition is descriptive and confounds evolving market conditions.
- **Why it matters:** This is an important conceptual contribution.
- **Concrete fix:** Present a formal decomposition or clearly label it as suggestive descriptive evidence.

### 3. Optional polish

#### Optional 1: Tone down absolute statements
- **Issue:** Phrases like “no alternative explanation” and “pure allocative inefficiency” overstate the evidence.
- **Why it matters:** Better calibration will improve credibility.
- **Concrete fix:** Use more conditional wording.

#### Optional 2: Expand literature discussion on renewable-energy incentive nonlinearities
- **Issue:** Policy-domain literature coverage is thinner than method coverage.
- **Why it matters:** This is a policy paper as much as a methods application.
- **Concrete fix:** Add closer discussion of studies on distributed solar adoption under tariff thresholds and self-consumption regulation.

#### Optional 3: Clarify what object the bunching ratio measures here
- **Issue:** Readers outside public finance may over-interpret \( \hat b \).
- **Why it matters:** The paper is aimed at a broad audience.
- **Concrete fix:** Add a brief intuitive interpretation and caution on cross-setting comparability.

## 7. Overall assessment

### Key strengths
- Important and policy-relevant question.
- Exceptional administrative data coverage.
- Striking descriptive pattern with repeated policy changes at the same threshold.
- Potentially substantial contribution to both bunching and climate-policy design literatures.
- Good instinct to investigate mechanism with module-count data and timing variation.

### Critical weaknesses
- Identification is persuasive qualitatively but overstated quantitatively.
- Statistical uncertainty is understated because model/specification uncertainty dominates bootstrap sampling error.
- Mass-balance issues and counterfactual-density specification are not adequately resolved.
- Treatment timing is not fully coherent with headline pooled estimates.
- Welfare/capacity-loss claims are stronger than the design supports.
- Mechanism claims about installer intermediation are not directly identified.

### Publishability after revision
I think this paper is salvageable and could become an excellent paper. The core phenomenon is likely real and important. But to be publishable in AEJ: Economic Policy or a top field journal, the paper needs a substantial redesign of the empirical presentation and claim calibration. For a top-5 general-interest journal, the current version is too specification-dependent and over-assertive in its quantitative conclusions.
