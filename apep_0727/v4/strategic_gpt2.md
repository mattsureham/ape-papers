## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether sharp eligibility thresholds in climate policy can materially distort real investment choices, not just generate mild bunching in reported behavior. Using Germany’s rooftop solar market and repeated reforms at the same 10 kWp cutoff, it shows that a surcharge exemption induced installers to systematically shrink systems to stay just below the threshold, creating a “missing middle” of 10–13 kWp systems and reducing solar deployment.

Why should a busy economist care? Because this is a vivid case where administrative design, interacting with modular technology and professional intermediation, appears to destroy meaningful real output. The broader claim is that threshold design can matter as much as subsidy level in technology-adoption policy.

**Does the paper articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Mostly yes—better than many submissions. The introduction gets to the main fact quickly and gives a memorable empirical pattern. But it leans a bit too fast into the paper’s coined language (“threshold trap”) and bunching magnitudes before fully locking in the big-world question. The opening should lead less with “here is a novel concept/literature contribution” and more with “governments may be suppressing green investment by how they draw lines.”

**What the first two paragraphs should say instead:**

> Governments often use sharp size thresholds to determine who pays a tax, qualifies for a subsidy, or faces environmental regulation. In many settings these cutoffs generate modest bunching; in technology-adoption markets, however, they may do something more consequential: induce people to buy smaller technologies than they otherwise would. This paper studies that possibility in Germany’s rooftop solar market.
>
> In 2014, Germany exempted solar systems below 10 kWp from a self-consumption surcharge. Installers responded by designing systems just under the cutoff: installations between 10 and 13 kWp largely disappeared during the policy and reappeared after the exemption was changed and later abolished. The central message is simple: when a policy threshold meets modular technology and sophisticated intermediaries, administrative convenience can translate into a large, real reduction in clean-energy investment.

That is the AER pitch: a broad policy-design lesson illustrated by a striking real-market fact.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:**  
The paper shows that a sharp climate-policy threshold at 10 kWp caused installers to physically downsize rooftop solar systems, creating a large missing middle in system sizes and implying that threshold-based policy design can meaningfully reduce technology adoption when adjustment is cheap and optimization is delegated.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?
Partly, but not fully. The paper does distinguish itself from standard bunching papers by emphasizing:
1. real physical adjustment rather than reporting,
2. modular technology,
3. repeated reforms at the same threshold,
4. deployment consequences rather than elasticities per se.

That said, the current framing still risks being heard as: “another bunching paper, but in solar.” The paper needs to say more cleanly what is new relative to:
- standard bunching-at-kink/notch papers,
- renewable-energy policy papers on subsidy design,
- papers on regulation with real rather than reporting responses.

The most distinctive feature is **not** just that the bunching ratio is large. Large estimates alone do not make an AER paper. The distinctive feature is that the policy appears to have **shrunk the technology itself** and that this can be seen switching on and off with institutional changes at the same threshold.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or filling a gap in a LITERATURE?
It is mixed. The paper often drifts into literature-speak: “This paper contributes to the literature on threshold-based policy design by identifying conditions…” That is weaker than the world question. The stronger framing is:

- **World question:** When do regulatory thresholds reduce real adoption rather than simply bunch administrative choices?
- **Policy question:** Can threshold design in climate policy perversely suppress deployment?
- **Economic question:** Under what conditions do notches become first-order real distortions?

That is much stronger than “we add to bunching and renewable-policy literatures.”

### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain what’s new?
Yes, but only if they are attentive. Right now they might say:  
“Interesting paper on bunching around a solar policy notch in Germany; installers size systems below 10 kWp.”

That is not yet enough. You want them to say:  
“It shows that a threshold in climate policy caused actual technology downsizing and missing adoption, and the same threshold turns on and off across multiple reforms—so the paper is really about when administrative cutoffs create large real investment distortions.”

### What would make this contribution bigger?
Most importantly, **make the foregone adoption margin the centerpiece**, not a downstream welfare add-on.

Specific ways to make it bigger:
- Put the **missing middle** front and center as the main finding, not as a later consequence.
- Make the paper explicitly about **technology adoption under regulatory thresholds**, with bunching as the measurement tool rather than the object of interest.
- If possible, show more directly that the absent 10–13 kWp systems were economically salient in terms of self-consumption, generation, or household returns—not just MW. The current MW estimate is good but still feels like an extrapolated epilogue.
- Strengthen the mechanism framing away from “installers are sophisticated” toward “modularity plus threshold design creates one-panel downsizing.” That mechanism is more directly shown in the data.
- If there are ways to connect to emissions, grid economics, or household energy bills, that would enlarge the stakes. Right now “100–135 MW” is meaningful but still modest enough that some readers may think: nice local distortion, but how general is it?

The biggest upgrade would be to frame the paper as showing **policy-induced under-sizing of clean technology** rather than **extreme bunching in a novel setting**.

That synthesis is the paper’s comparative advantage.

Not quite. It is currently having a respectable conversation with the bunching literature and a secondary conversation with renewable-energy policy. The higher-impact conversation is:

**How should governments design eligibility thresholds when agents can cheaply re-optimize the characteristics of physical capital?**

That brings together public economics, environmental economics, and regulation design. That is the conversation that belongs in the AER.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Governments use sharp thresholds because they are simple to administer. Standard evidence suggests they create bunching, but often not enormous distortions. In rooftop solar, system size is flexible and policy rules are tied to capacity cutoffs.

### Tension
What happens when a sharp threshold applies to a modular physical technology and the relevant choice is effectively made by sophisticated installers rather than inattentive households? Does the threshold merely affect the distribution near the cutoff, or does it actually reduce clean-energy investment?

### Resolution
Germany’s 10 kWp surcharge exemption generated a dramatic disappearance of systems just above the threshold and concentration just below it. This pattern appears when the notch is introduced, attenuates when the threshold is moved, and largely fades when the surcharge is abolished. The evidence is consistent with real one-panel downsizing and substantial foregone capacity.

### Implications
Policy thresholds can create first-order real distortions in technology adoption; smoother phase-ins may preserve administrative objectives without suppressing investment. More broadly, the strength of behavioral response depends not just on the schedule but on market structure and technology.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
Yes, more than most empirical papers. There is a real story here. But the arc is diluted by two choices:

1. The paper spends too much energy insisting on the size of the bunching ratio, as if “86.5” is itself the story.
2. The coined concept “threshold trap” is pushed hard before it has earned enough authority.

The better story is:

- **Setup:** Climate policy often uses arbitrary thresholds.
- **Tension:** In modular capital markets these thresholds may shrink the technology itself.
- **Resolution:** Germany’s 10 kWp rule did exactly that.
- **Implication:** Threshold design is a first-order policy lever in adoption markets.

Right now the paper occasionally reads like a collection of strong stylized facts organized by bunching terminology. It should instead tell a cleaner policy-design story with the empirical pattern serving that story.

They would lean in initially. It is a very striking institutional fact. The visual is powerful and easy to understand. Economists like dramatic threshold responses, and this one has a clear real-economy angle.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Likely one of three:
1. “Is this just paperwork bunching or did people really install fewer panels?”  
2. “How much solar capacity did this actually cost?”  
3. “Is this a weird German solar anecdote, or a general lesson for policy design?”

The paper answers (1) reasonably well and (2) somewhat well. It needs to answer (3) more persuasively but more cautiously.

### If findings are modest or null?
Not applicable here. The findings are not modest. The issue is not whether the result is strong; it is whether the paper can convince readers that the result changes how we think about policy design beyond this one case.

For an AER-caliber reader, the bunching method can be described succinctly. The current exposition spends substantial space on estimator details and alternative explanations that are better handled tersely in the text and fully in the appendix.

The main text currently includes too much about specification families, placebo thresholds, and sensitivity ranges. Keep one concise paragraph and one table if needed, but do not let the paper’s center of gravity move away from the core factual pattern.

The module-count evidence is one of the most intuitive and convincing parts of the paper. It should appear earlier—possibly immediately after the main result—because it answers the reader’s first skeptical question: “Did they really shrink systems?”

#### Recast the welfare section as “deployment consequences”
“Welfare” is too ambitious a label for what is mostly a bounded deployment calculation. Call it:
- “Deployment Consequences”
- or “Foregone Solar Capacity”

That would be more accurate and strategically cleaner.

#### Tighten the discussion section
The discussion section repeats claims already made in the introduction and conclusion. It should do one thing: interpret why this setting produces such strong real responses and what that means for policy design.

#### Tone down coined language
“Threshold trap” is memorable, but used too often it sounds like branding. One mention is fine; building the paper around a label risks making it feel less serious than the underlying fact pattern deserves.

#### Reconsider the conclusion
The conclusion is well written but somewhat overdetermined. It repeats the generalization too forcefully. A shorter, sharper ending that emphasizes the main empirical lesson and the design implication would land better.

### Are results buried in robustness that should be in the main results?
Yes:
- The **module-count / physical downsizing evidence** should be more central.
- The **missing middle** should be elevated from the later “welfare” section into the main results framing.

This is not far-fetched as an AER aspiration because the core fact is genuinely striking. But in current form it still feels more like a strong field-journal paper with top-journal ambition than a paper that unmistakably belongs in the AER conversation.

### What is the gap?

#### Mostly a framing problem
The science may be there; the story is not yet optimally targeted. The paper keeps presenting itself as a bunching paper with an unusually large estimate. That undersells and mis-sells it. The right pitch is policy design in technology adoption.

#### Also a scope problem
The current paper shows a compelling distortion in one market. To feel AER-scale, it needs either:
- a stronger general framework for when thresholds create real under-adoption, or
- broader consequences/stakes from the German case, or
- a more disciplined and persuasive link from this case to a class of policies.

Right now the generalization is asserted more than demonstrated.

#### Some novelty risk
A skeptical reader could say: “Of course a notch causes bunching in a modular setting.” The paper’s answer cannot just be “yes, but the bunching ratio is huge.” The answer has to be: “yes, and here is a clean, policy-relevant case where the threshold visibly shrank adoption of green capital and we can watch that distortion turn on and off over time.”

#### Some ambition problem
The paper is good at documenting the pattern, but a little safe in what it ultimately claims. It could be more ambitious conceptually by offering a clearer framework for **when thresholds affect product characteristics and adoption**, not just location relative to a cutoff.

### The single most impactful piece of advice
**Rewrite the paper so that its main contribution is not “extreme bunching at a solar threshold,” but “a climate-policy threshold caused real under-sizing of clean technology and suppressed adoption; bunching is the tool, not the story.”**

That one change would improve the title logic, introduction, literature framing, section order, and takeaway all at once.
