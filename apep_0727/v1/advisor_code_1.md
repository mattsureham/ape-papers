**Idea Fidelity**

The paper mostly follows the manifest’s original vision: it leverages the MaStR/OPSD registry to study bunching around Germany’s EEG self-consumption surcharge exemption at 10 kWp and applies a Kleven–Waseem estimator to quantify the behavioral response. However, a key element of the manifest—the natural experiment provided by the 2021 threshold expansion from 10 to 30 kWp and the associated difference-in-discontinuities validation—is absent. The author acknowledges the data cutoff at 2018 in the discussion, but the paper therefore misses what the manifest billed as the “clean test that bunching is policy-induced not technological.” That validation should be included if the paper purports to deliver the full idea, or else the introduction should temper claims about exploiting the 2021 reform.

**Summary**

Using installation-level data on roughly one million German solar PV systems (3–20 kWp, 2008–2018), the paper documents extreme bunching at the 10 kWp threshold created by the EEG 2014 surcharge exemption, with a bunching ratio of 113 during the policy period versus 6.7 beforehand. Robustness checks across polynomial degrees, exclusion windows, placebo thresholds, and federal states support the finding, and the author translates the excess mass into foregone capacity to highlight the welfare implications.

**Essential Points**

1. **Confounding time trends and policy changes beyond the surcharge exemption.** The difference-in-bunching strategy compares the pre-2014 period (when the ETF tier changed) with 2014–2018. But the feed-in tariff schedule itself introduced a 10 kWp tier starting in 2012, and broader cost declines, installer learning, and market composition shifts occur over the decade. The paper needs to more fully demonstrate that these broader trends do not drive the dramatic jump in bunching. One possibility is to exploit more flexible counterfactuals (e.g., comparing 2013 vs. 2014 within narrower windows or using alternative thresholds unaffected by the surcharge) to show that the surge in bunching is unique to the policy change rather than a mechanical result of secular shifts.

2. **Absence of the planned difference-in-discontinuities validation.** The manifest emphasized the 2021 threshold expansion to 30 kWp as a test of whether bunching disappears at 10 and reappears at 30, bolstering the causal claim. The current paper lacks any post-2018 data, so this natural experiment is not exploited. Without it, the paper relies solely on pre/post comparisons around 2014, which are more vulnerable to omitted temporal variation. If the full MaStR data are not yet available, the manuscript should refrain from promising that validation and instead clearly frame the current exercise as a pre-2021 analysis; alternatively, the authors should obtain updated data (containing the 2021 reform) to deliver the manifest’s promise.

3. **Interpretation of the bunching ratio and elasticity.** The paper reports an elasticity of 42, which is mechanically derived from the bunching ratio and implies nearly complete avoidance. Given the discrete nature of solar modules and the nearly deterministic decision by installers, this elasticity may not have the same behavioral interpretation as in tax studies. More importantly, excess mass estimates can be sensitive to counterfactual specification (for instance, the polynomial degree). While robustness tables show variation, the paper should provide clearer guidance on how sensitive the welfare calculations are to assumptions about what installers would have chosen absent the threshold, especially since this is the basis for the 200 MW foregone capacity estimate.

**Suggestions**

1. **Operationalize the counterfactual more transparently.** Consider plotting the raw density (with the fitted polynomial) and presenting the estimated counterfactual densities across specifications in an appendix figure. That would allow readers to see how the polynomial behaves outside the exclusion window and to assess the plausibility of the “smoothness” assumption visually. Supplement the polynomial fit with alternative counterfactuals (e.g., local linear regressions with triangular kernels, or even semi-parametric density estimates) as sensitivity checks.

2. **Strengthen the identification narrative with additional placebo tests.** The placebo table is useful, but it would be helpful to show bunching at the 10 kWp threshold during 2014–2018 for subsamples of installers (e.g., by vintage, by region, by installer size if available) to demonstrate that the effect is pervasive. In addition, consider exploiting the 2012 FIT tier change as an internal placebo: the data already show a bump in bunching starting 2012, but explicitly quantifying how that bump compares with the post-2014 surge (e.g., by estimating changes in bunching around the feed-in tariff tier) would underscore that the 2014 surcharge created a qualitatively different response.

3. **Clarify the data limitations and future plans.** The paper notes in the discussion that MaStR data beyond 2018 would allow a fuller difference-in-discontinuities test. Given the manifest’s emphasis on that experiment, it would be informative to state more concretely whether such data are accessible and, if not, when they might be. At a minimum, the introduction should avoid asserting that the paper exploits the 2021 reform; instead, it can frame the current work as a first step, with a plan to incorporate later data once available. If data access is the only barrier, the authors should document any attempts to obtain post-2018 MaStR pulls.

4. **Expand the welfare calculation discussion.** The paper currently assumes that excess installations would have averaged 12 kWp absent the threshold. This assumption could be supported with empirical evidence: for instance, compare the average capacity above 10 kWp in pre-policy years or in regions where enforcement was weaker. Alternatively, perform a counterfactual exercise where the foregone capacity is expressed as a range (e.g., 1 kWp to 3 kWp) to highlight uncertainty. Moreover, comment on whether the foregone generation directly translates into welfare losses, or whether some of that capacity may have been forgone for reasons unrelated to the surcharge (e.g., rooftop limitations).

5. **Improve the discussion of the installation process.** The paper argues that professional installers optimize precisely, but it would strengthen the paper to include any descriptive evidence on installation annotations (e.g., module counts) that show how installers approached the threshold. If the dataset contains module counts or related technical fields, summarizing the typical increments (e.g., 26 vs. 27 modules) near the kink could illustrate how adding a single panel changes capacity and triggers the surcharge. If such fields aren’t available for the sample used, mention this explicitly.

6. **Mention potential policy heterogeneity.** The EEG reform may have had different local enforcement or administrative burdens by state. The heterogeneity table shows variation, but the paper could go further by discussing whether, for example, states with a higher density of installers or different building profiles exhibit systematically larger bunching, or whether region-year installation volumes correlate with bunching magnitudes. If state-level policy variations (e.g., differences in building codes) exist, mentioning them would contextualize the uniformity of the response.

7. **Consider alternative mechanisms.** While the surcharge is a compelling explanation, alternative channels—such as psychological preferences for round numbers (9.9 versus 10), standard system design packages, or installer marketing strategies—could generate some of the observed bunching. Although the placebo tests mitigate this, a short discussion acknowledging these factors and explaining why they likely cannot account for the dramatic 2014–2018 spike would fortify the causal story.

Implementing these suggestions would improve the paper’s transparency, strengthen the identification argument, and align more closely with the ambitious experiment described in the manifest.
