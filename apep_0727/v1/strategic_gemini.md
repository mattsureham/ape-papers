## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper identifies what may be the largest bunching response in the history of applied economics: a massive distortion in the size of German solar installations caused by a regulatory surcharge exemption at 10 kWp. It shows that when professional intermediaries (installers) rather than inattentive consumers make choices, behavioral responses to policy notches can be nearly "complete," leading to a significant loss of renewable energy capacity.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this very well. The first paragraph is a "hook" that leads with the 281-to-1 ratio, which is exactly how you sell an empirical paper to a general audience. The second paragraph provides the institutional "why." It is already written with the clarity expected of an AER submission.

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:** The paper demonstrates that regulatory notches, when mediated by professional agents, can produce near-total behavioral responses that dwarf standard tax bunching estimates, resulting in substantial allocative inefficiency in climate policy.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It differentiates itself from Saez (2010) and Kleven & Waseem (2013) by shifting the focus from individual labor supply/taxation to industrial/environmental policy and the "intermediary channel."
*   **World vs. Literature:** It frames the question as a problem with the *world* (how to design energy policy without accidentally shrinking solar panels). This is a strong framing.
*   **What would make it bigger?** To reach the "super-AER" level, the paper needs to more explicitly link the "Installer Channel" to the broader literature on **Delegated Choice**. Is this about information, or is it about the professionalization of optimization? If the author could show that markets with *fewer* professional installers show *less* bunching, the mechanism would be ironclad.

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

*   **Closest Neighbors:** Kleven & Waseem (2013) on notches; Garicano et al. (2016) on firm thresholds; Ito (2014) on energy pricing; and Chetty et al. (2011) on optimization frictions.
*   **Strategy:** It currently builds on these by saying "their effects are small; mine is huge." It should move from "my effect is bigger" to "my effect is different because of *who* is making the decision."
*   **Unexpected Connection:** The paper could connect to the **Industrial Organization** literature on "Principal-Agent" problems in construction/contracting. Why does the installer care so much? Is it because they compete on "payback period" metrics in their sales pitches? Connecting to the marketing of solar (the "turnkey proposal" mentioned on page 4) would bridge to IO/Applied Micro in a way that increases the audience.

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

*   **Setup:** Germany is a solar leader with a complex subsidy regime (EEG).
*   **Tension:** A policy meant to simplify administration (exempting small systems) creates a massive "dominated region" where it makes zero economic sense to install a 10.1 kWp system.
*   **Resolution:** Installers respond with surgical precision, creating a "trap" where capacity is artificially capped just below the threshold.
*   **Implications:** Policy design must account for professional optimizers; "sharp" thresholds are dangerous in the energy transition.

**Evaluation:** The arc is exceptionally clear. It is a "clean" paper. The tension is palpable because the magnitude of the result (281:1) is so visceral.

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

**The Dinner Party Fact:** "Germany's solar policy was so poorly designed that you were 281 times more likely to see a 9.9kW system than a 10.1kW system, literally capping the green energy transition by design."

**Response:** People will lean in. The follow-up question will be: "Did the government realize it?" The paper answers this (they eventually raised the threshold), which provides a satisfying "policy loop" to the story.

---

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

*   **Front-loading:** The paper is already well front-loaded. Table 1 and the Introduction tell 80% of the story.
*   **Welfare Section:** Section 5.5 (Foregone Capacity) is the "AER meat." It needs to be expanded. Don't just give a back-of-the-envelope range (100-200 MW). Model the *counterfactual distribution* more formally to show exactly what the "missing middle" looks like.
*   **Appendix:** The heterogeneity by state (Table 5) is interesting but secondary. It could move to an appendix unless the author can use state-level differences in "installer density" or "competition" to prove the "Installer Channel" mechanism.

---

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The gap is currently **Scope**. As it stands, it is a very high-quality "Natural Experiment" or "Applied Micro" paper that could easily land in *AEJ: Policy*. To make it *AER*, it needs to be more than a "cool fact" about Germany.

**Single Biggest Piece of Advice:** Prove the **"Professional Optimizer"** hypothesis. 
The paper argues that the bunching is huge because installers do it, not homeowners. Can the author find data on *who* installed the systems? Or compare residential (installer-led) to DIY (if it exists)? Or show that "experienced" installers (those with many registrations) bunch more tightly than "novice" installers? If you prove that *market sophistication* drives the magnitude of the distortion, this becomes a fundamental paper on how markets respond to regulation.
