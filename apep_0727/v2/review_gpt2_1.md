This paper studies bunching at Germany’s 10 kWp solar threshold using administrative registry data on essentially the universe of rooftop PV installations from 2008–2024. The descriptive pattern is striking, and the institutional setting is appealing: the same threshold changes from irrelevant, to a FIT kink, to a self-consumption surcharge notch, then becomes less relevant after reform. That said, in its current form I do not think the paper is publication-ready for a top general-interest journal or AEJ:EP. The main issue is not whether there is “something real” at 10 kWp—there plainly is—but whether the paper has yet established, with sufficient rigor, that the measured bunching is a credible estimate of real capacity distortion rather than some combination of policy response, reporting/manipulation, and functional-form artifacts. Relatedly, parts of the inference and placebo evidence currently undercut the paper’s stronger causal and welfare claims.

Below I focus on scientific substance, identification, inference, interpretation, and publication readiness.

## 1. Identification and empirical design

### Main strengths

The core empirical idea is good. The paper exploits repeated institutional changes at the same threshold (Sections 2 and 4), which is much more compelling than a one-shot bunching exercise. The sequence—little bunching pre-2012, more under the FIT kink, much more under the 2014 notch, attenuation after 2021/2022—does align with theory.

The use of universe-scale administrative data is also a major asset, especially given the very large number of observations near the threshold.

### Main identification concerns

#### 1. The paper has not yet ruled out reporting/manipulation as opposed to real downsizing

This is the most important substantive issue.

The paper interprets excess mass below 10 kWp as systems physically downsized to avoid the surcharge (Introduction; Sections 5 and 6). But the evidence shown is not yet sufficient to distinguish:

- real reduction in installed capacity,
- strategic reporting just below threshold,
- installer choice of nameplate rating/reporting convention,
- administrative classification behavior near the legal boundary.

This matters enormously because the welfare calculation in Section 6 assumes excess mass corresponds to physically foregone panels/capacity.

The current “module count evidence” (Section 5.2; Table 5 Panel B) helps, but it does not fully resolve the issue. A lower module count at 9.9 kWp than at 10.1–11.0 kWp is suggestive, but not dispositive, because module wattage varies substantially over time and across installers. In a market with changing module efficiency, a 32-module system and a 39-module system need not bracket a common physical roof constraint. More importantly, one can still manipulate reported capacity while reporting plausible module counts.

What is needed is direct validation that systems just below 10 were physically smaller than they otherwise would have been. For example:
- if module wattage is observed or can be inferred, construct implied DC capacity = module count × module wattage and compare it with the registered capacity;
- show the joint distribution of module count and module wattage around the cutoff;
- test whether systems just below 10 are disproportionately composed of “one panel less” designs relative to nearby bins in periods without the notch;
- if inverter capacity or panel model information exists, use it.

Right now, the paper repeatedly asserts “downsizing” as a fact, but the evidence shown establishes strong behavioral response at the registry threshold, not yet necessarily the magnitude of real foregone physical capacity.

#### 2. The smooth counterfactual density assumption is not yet convincingly defended given strong technological heaping elsewhere

Section 4 invokes the standard bunching smoothness assumption and uses pre-2012 low bunching as reassurance. That is helpful, but not enough given the paper’s own placebo evidence in Table 8.

Specifically, the placebo exercise reports:
- very large negative “bunching” estimates at 6, 8, and 14 kWp,
- and an extremely large positive estimate at 7 kWp (\(\hat b = 473.6\)).

This is not a minor footnote. It indicates that the local density of solar system sizes is shaped by substantial technological or market-driven heaping at non-policy points. Once the distribution has many local spikes and troughs generated by panel/inverter configurations, roof-size conventions, or installer templates, a global 7th-degree polynomial fitted outside [9,11) may be a poor approximation to the true local counterfactual near 10.

The paper tries to dismiss the 7 kWp placebo as “technological bunching” and the negative placebos as “expected null” (Section 7.4). That is not adequate. Under a valid placebo, one wants estimates centered near zero, not huge positive and negative masses produced by the same estimator. Large negative placebo masses suggest estimator instability or misspecification, not reassuring null results.

This is a serious challenge to the current empirical design. The paper must show that the 10 kWp result is robust to more local and more flexible counterfactual constructions, not only to changing polynomial degree and exclusion windows.

Concretely, I would want to see:
- local fits rather than only a global polynomial;
- donut or nearest-neighbor counterfactuals using symmetric windows;
- period-specific nonparametric smoothing;
- explicit demonstration that excess mass below the notch is matched by missing mass above it over a defensible dominated region;
- a comparison of estimated counterfactual density using alternative methods standard in bunching applications.

#### 3. The event-timing design is promising but underexploited

The paper repeatedly claims the “policy on/off” pattern provides causal identification “without relying on cross-sectional comparisons or parallel trends” (Introduction; Section 4). Broadly yes, but the timing evidence is currently coarser than it should be.

The key policy changes occur:
- Jan 2012,
- Aug 1, 2014,
- Jan 1, 2021,
- Jul 1, 2022.

Yet the main event study is annual (Table 4), even though the registry appears to permit much higher-frequency analysis and the sample is enormous. Since 2014 and 2022 straddle policy regimes, annual bins are unnecessarily blunt. For a top journal, the natural diagnostic is monthly or quarterly bunching around the exact implementation dates:
- Was there an immediate jump after August 2014?
- Was there anticipatory bunching in the months before implementation?
- Did the 10 kWp spike collapse sharply in January 2021?
- Did it fall further after July 2022?

This matters for identification because it would sharply distinguish policy response from gradual technology or market-composition changes. The paper itself notes this limitation (Section 7.5), but for publication readiness this should move from “future research” to main analysis.

#### 4. The paper overstates how much alternative explanations are ruled out

Section 4 says that “no alternative explanation” predicts all four directional changes. That is too strong as written.

A more calibrated statement is that policy timing lines up well with the observed changes, but some residual post-2021 bunching remains substantial (Table 3: pooled post-reform \(\hat b = 14.2\); 2021 = 27.9; 2024 = 7.3), and pre-period bunching is not literally zero (1.8 pooled; 4.0 in 2008). That means policy is clearly not the only force shaping the density around 10. FIT kinks, round-number preferences, technology templates, and reporting conventions remain relevant.

The causal story is still plausible, but the paper needs more discipline in separating:
- evidence that policy matters a lot,
from
- evidence that policy fully explains the observed distribution.

## 2. Inference and statistical validity

This is the second major area needing improvement.

### 1. Inference is incomplete for key estimates

Table 3 reports standard errors and confidence intervals for pooled period estimates, but Table 4’s annual event-study estimates are presented without standard errors or confidence intervals. Yet the paper draws strong conclusions from year-to-year changes such as 2013 to 2014, 2020 to 2021, and 2022 to 2023.

Given that these annual movements are central to the identification argument, uncertainty bands are necessary. With such a large sample, estimates may indeed be precise—but that needs to be shown, not assumed.

### 2. The bootstrap design is underdescribed and likely too thin

Section 4.3 says uncertainty is computed by nonparametric bootstrap with 200 replications. For percentile CIs on a highly non-linear estimator, 200 draws is sparse, especially for a paper leaning on very fine precision and huge t-statistics. At minimum, use 1,000+ replications.

More importantly, the bootstrap addresses sampling variation conditional on the estimator, but much of the real uncertainty here is specification uncertainty:
- polynomial order,
- exclusion window,
- binning choice,
- sample window,
- pooling across years with different underlying distributions.

The paper partly explores sensitivity (Table 8), but these specification changes produce enormous swings in \(\hat b\) (e.g., 54.7 to 144.3 across exclusion windows). That should substantially temper claims of ultra-precise inference such as \(t = 87.7\). The sampling SE of 1.0 is not an adequate summary of total uncertainty when the point estimate itself is so sensitive to functional-form choices.

### 3. The difference-in-bunching t-statistic is not transparently justified

Table 3 Panel B reports “Surcharge – Pre-FIT = 84.7 (1.0), \(t = 87.7\).” It is not clear how this standard error is computed. Is it the bootstrap SE of the difference, or is it simply inheriting the surcharge-period SE? If the two estimates are computed from different subsamples with separate fitted counterfactuals, the variance of the difference should be explicitly bootstrapped and reported.

This is a presentation detail but substantively important because the paper leans on this comparison heavily.

### 4. The placebo results undermine estimator validity and should be treated as such

As noted above, the placebo threshold exercise is not currently interpreted correctly. Large negative placebo estimates are not “the expected null result.” A null placebo test should show little systematic excess or missing mass. The fact that the estimator generates very large nonzero placebo masses suggests overfitting/misspecification or structural lumpiness in the size distribution. This is a statistical-validity issue, not just a robustness footnote.

### 5. Sample coherence is mostly good, but some quantities need reconciliation

The tables are generally coherent, but two points need attention:

- The pooled post-reform excess mass is very large (204,587) despite the paper’s claim that the distribution “largely returned to its pre-policy shape” (Abstract, Introduction). This is not a statistical error, but the magnitudes and interpretation need reconciliation.
- The module-count table and annual count table use slightly different sample definitions because of missing module data; that is noted. Good. But because mechanism claims rely on those comparisons, the paper should confirm that missingness is not differential around the threshold.

## 3. Robustness and alternative explanations

### What the paper does well

The paper already includes some useful robustness work:
- different polynomial orders,
- different exclusion windows,
- a rooftop vs ground-mount comparison,
- state-level heterogeneity,
- pre/post institutional variation.

These are all worth keeping.

### What is still missing

#### 1. The robustness section reveals instability rather than settling it

Table 8 shows the baseline estimate is not very stable to common bunching choices. A range from 55 to 144 across exclusion windows is not reassuringly “qualitative conclusion unchanged” for a paper making strong quantitative claims about “the largest bunching response in applied economics,” welfare magnitudes, and comparisons to other literatures.

For top-journal publication, the paper needs a stronger argument for the preferred specification and a more principled mapping from specification uncertainty to uncertainty in the substantive claims.

#### 2. No direct “missing mass” accounting

For a notch, one expects excess mass below the threshold to correspond to missing mass above it over the dominated region. The paper discusses a “missing middle” qualitatively (Sections 6 and 7) but does not actually report the missing mass estimate, the upper bound of the dominated region, or the balance between excess and missing mass under the preferred specification.

Without that accounting, the welfare exercise rests on a looser interpretation than is standard in the notch-bunching literature.

#### 3. The key mechanism claim—installer optimization—is plausible but not cleanly identified

The installer channel is a sensible hypothesis, but Section 5’s evidence is indirect:
- module counts,
- uniformity across states,
- contrast with ground-mounted systems.

None of these directly identifies repeat-optimizing intermediaries as the mechanism. Since the paper’s conceptual contribution hinges on “repeat-optimizing intermediaries + modular technology + high stakes,” it would be much stronger with direct evidence:
- installer identifiers if obtainable,
- concentration of bunching among frequent installers,
- faster adjustment among repeat installers after policy changes,
- heterogeneity by installer market structure or local installer density.

The paper is candid that installer identifiers are unavailable in the current data (Section 7.5). That is acceptable, but then the mechanism claims should be stated more cautiously.

#### 4. Welfare calculations are too assumption-driven in the current draft

Section 6 offers 135/269/404 MW scenarios. These are fine as back-of-the-envelope calculations, but the text often reads as though foregone capacity is firmly estimated. It is not.

At present:
- excess mass itself depends materially on counterfactual specification,
- the physical-vs-reporting distinction is unresolved,
- the implied counterfactual size of bunched systems is assumed rather than estimated.

A more credible welfare section would estimate the missing mass region and use observed post-reform or pre-notch distributions to construct empirical reallocation scenarios, rather than imposing 11/12/13 kWp assumptions.

## 4. Contribution and literature positioning

The paper’s potential contribution is real:
1. a compelling policy setting for bunching,
2. unusually large responses in an energy/climate context,
3. a broader argument about when thresholds create extreme distortions.

That said, the current draft somewhat overstates novelty and certainty.

### Contribution relative to prior work

The empirical pattern may indeed be unusually large. But because the estimate is sensitive to specification and because the baseline density is highly irregular, the claim that this is “what may be the largest bunching response in applied economics” should be softened unless the authors can demonstrate that the estimate is robust across defensible methods.

### Literature gaps / additions

The bunching citations are somewhat thin for a paper making a major methodological and comparative claim. At minimum, I would strengthen engagement with:
- the broader notch/kink bunching literature beyond Saez (2010), Kleven & Waseem (2013), and Kleven (2016),
- work on firm thresholds and administrative/reporting responses,
- energy-policy papers on non-linear distributed generation incentives and installer behavior.

Concrete additions to consider:
- Best, Brockmeyer, Kleven, Spinnewijn, and Waseem (2015), on production vs evasion/reporting margins under notches;
- papers on VAT/firm-size thresholds and reporting/manipulation margins;
- recent work on distributed solar adoption and installer intermediation.

The reason to add these is not cosmetic. This paper’s central challenge is exactly disentangling real adjustment from reporting/avoidance margins, and the literature on notches has treated that distinction seriously.

## 5. Results interpretation and claim calibration

This is an area where the paper needs significant tightening.

### 1. The causal claims are directionally credible, but some statements overreach

I believe the paper credibly shows that the 10 kWp threshold mattered a great deal. I do not think it has yet shown with equal credibility that:
- the excess mass fully reflects physical downsizing,
- the welfare loss is as large as claimed,
- alternative non-policy contributors are effectively ruled out,
- the post-reform distribution “largely returned to its pre-policy shape.”

The post-reform pooled bunching ratio of 14.2 is still far above the pre-FIT pooled 1.8 and above the FIT-kink pooled 12.7. That is not “back to baseline.” The text should say attenuation was substantial but incomplete, consistent with residual FIT-kink incentives and underlying focal-point/technology effects.

### 2. Policy implications are stronger than the evidence currently supports

The policy lesson—avoid sharp thresholds when technologies are modular and agents are sophisticated—is plausible and useful. But statements such as “The 135–270 MW of solar capacity left on German rooftops is a direct cost” (Conclusion) are too definitive given the current evidence.

A more defensible formulation would be: “If the excess mass reflects real downsizing rather than reporting, and if counterfactual systems would have been 1–2 kWp larger, then the implied foregone capacity is on the order of …”

### 3. The paper needs to distinguish better between reduced form and mechanism

The reduced-form result is strong: distributional mass shifts sharply with policy changes at 10 kWp.

The mechanism story—professional installers optimizing on behalf of consumers—is plausible but still not directly verified. It should be presented as the leading interpretation supported by suggestive evidence, not as definitively established.

## 6. Actionable revision requests

### 1. Must-fix issues before acceptance

#### (1) Resolve the physical downsizing vs reporting/manipulation issue
- **Why it matters:** This is central to the causal and welfare claims.
- **Concrete fix:** Use module-level information, panel wattage/model data if available, inverter data, or any engineering fields in MaStR to verify that systems just below 10 are physically smaller, not merely administratively reported below threshold. Show how implied physical capacity relates to registered capacity around the cutoff, by period.

#### (2) Rework the bunching estimation to address counterfactual instability
- **Why it matters:** Table 8 placebos and sensitivity results indicate the current polynomial approach may be unreliable in an irregular density.
- **Concrete fix:** Add alternative estimators: more local fits, nonparametric or semi-parametric counterfactuals, narrower symmetric windows, and explicit missing-mass accounting over a dominated region. Show that the main result survives across methods, not just across polynomial degrees.

#### (3) Provide valid and complete inference for annual/event-time estimates
- **Why it matters:** The timing evidence is central to identification.
- **Concrete fix:** Report standard errors/CIs for annual estimates and, ideally, move to monthly or quarterly event-time analysis around Aug 2014, Jan 2021, and Jul 2022. Increase bootstrap replications materially and bootstrap differences directly.

#### (4) Reinterpret or redesign the placebo analysis
- **Why it matters:** The current placebo section undermines the estimator rather than validating it.
- **Concrete fix:** Replace the current claim that large negative placebo masses are “expected null” with a serious diagnosis. Investigate why those placebos fail and show placebo performance under alternative estimators/windows.

#### (5) Substantially temper the welfare claims unless supported by stronger evidence
- **Why it matters:** The current capacity-loss calculations exceed what the design presently identifies.
- **Concrete fix:** Either (i) treat the welfare section as exploratory and explicitly conditional, or (ii) estimate counterfactual sizes empirically using missing-mass recovery and post-reform distributions.

### 2. High-value improvements

#### (6) Exploit exact policy timing with higher-frequency data
- **Why it matters:** This would substantially strengthen causal interpretation.
- **Concrete fix:** Monthly or quarterly densities and bunching measures around implementation dates; show immediate responses and any anticipation.

#### (7) Strengthen mechanism evidence on installer intermediation
- **Why it matters:** The conceptual contribution depends on this mechanism.
- **Concrete fix:** If installer identifiers cannot be obtained, use proxies more aggressively: local installer concentration, adoption of bunching by municipalities over time, differential response in areas with thicker installer markets, or repeated operator/developer patterns if present.

#### (8) Clarify legal treatment of exactly 10.0 kWp and near-threshold reporting
- **Why it matters:** The distribution at 10.0 and the concentration at 9.9/9.99 may contain important information about compliance behavior.
- **Concrete fix:** Explicitly document whether the exemption applied to “below 10” or “up to 10,” how MaStR records precision/rounding, and whether installers disproportionately choose 9.99/9.90 reporting conventions.

#### (9) Recalibrate comparative claims to the literature
- **Why it matters:** Quantitative comparisons are currently stronger than the robustness warrants.
- **Concrete fix:** Frame the response as among the largest documented in a policy threshold setting, subject to estimator choice and density irregularity.

### 3. Optional polish

#### (10) Add missingness checks for module-count data
- **Why it matters:** Mechanism inference depends on these data.
- **Concrete fix:** Show module-data coverage by year and threshold bin; verify no threshold-related missingness.

#### (11) Report excess and missing mass side by side
- **Why it matters:** Readers need to see the accounting behind the bunching interpretation.
- **Concrete fix:** Add a table reporting excess mass below, missing mass above, and implied dominated region under each main specification.

#### (12) Narrow and sharpen the main claim
- **Why it matters:** Better calibration will improve credibility.
- **Concrete fix:** Recast the headline as strong evidence that threshold-based climate policy induced major bunching and likely real downsizing, rather than asserting the welfare magnitude as established fact.

## 7. Overall assessment

### Key strengths
- Excellent institutional setting with repeated policy changes at the same threshold.
- Very large, policy-relevant administrative dataset.
- Clear descriptive evidence that the 10 kWp threshold mattered substantially.
- Potentially important contribution to both bunching and climate-policy design.

### Critical weaknesses
- The current design does not yet convincingly separate real physical downsizing from reporting/manipulation at the threshold.
- The bunching estimator appears fragile in the presence of strong technological heaping and irregular densities, as revealed by the placebo tests.
- Inference is incomplete for key event-time claims and understates specification uncertainty.
- Welfare and mechanism claims are currently stronger than the evidence supports.

### Publishability after revision
I think this paper is promising and salvageable, but not close to acceptance in its current form. If the authors can (i) convincingly validate physical downsizing, (ii) stabilize the bunching design with better counterfactual methods and higher-frequency timing evidence, and (iii) recalibrate the welfare/mechanism claims, the paper could become a strong field or policy paper and potentially contend for a broader outlet. At present, however, the empirical design is not yet sufficiently watertight for the target journals named in the prompt.
