## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether sharp policy thresholds can generate extremely large real distortions when decisions are made by sophisticated intermediaries using modular technologies. Using the universe of German rooftop solar installations, it shows that a 10 kWp exemption from a self-consumption surcharge led installers to bunch massively just below the threshold, shrinking subsidized systems and leaving substantial solar capacity “on the roof.”

Why should a busy economist care? Because this is not just another bunching paper: it speaks to a broader question about when administrative simplifications in regulation become first-order distortions, especially in sectors like climate policy where technology is modular and choices are delegated to expert agents.

**Does the paper itself articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Mostly, but not optimally. The current opening is vivid and data-rich, which is good, but it starts with the descriptive fact before fully stating the broader economic question. By paragraph 3, the paper gets to the real contribution—conditions under which thresholds generate extreme responses—but that should arrive sooner and more cleanly. Right now the reader could initially think: “interesting institutional oddity in German solar,” rather than “important general lesson about policy design.”

**What the first two paragraphs should say instead:**

> Many regulations use sharp thresholds to simplify administration. Economists know such thresholds can induce bunching, but most estimated responses are modest. This paper shows that threshold-based policy can generate extreme real distortions when three conditions hold: decisions are made by repeat-optimizing intermediaries, the regulated choice is modular, and the private stakes from avoiding the threshold far exceed the adjustment cost.
>
> I study Germany’s rooftop solar market, where a 2014 reform exempted systems below 10 kWp from a self-consumption surcharge. Using the universe of 3 million solar registrations, I show that installers responded by placing enormous mass just below 10 kWp and almost none just above it; when the threshold was raised and then removed, the distortion collapsed. The result is a clean demonstration that threshold design can materially reduce clean-energy investment, and more broadly that bunching can be enormous in intermediary-driven markets.

That is the pitch the paper should have.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Governments often use thresholds to simplify policy administration. Economists know thresholds can distort behavior, but in most studied settings responses are moderate, perhaps because people optimize imperfectly or adjustment is costly.

### Tension
So why do some thresholds matter little while others produce dramatic real distortions? We lack clear evidence on the conditions under which thresholds create extreme responses in actual investment decisions, especially in climate-policy settings.

### Resolution
Germany’s 10 kWp solar rule provides a striking case: installers massively downsized rooftop systems to remain below the threshold, and this distortion appeared, intensified, and then receded exactly as policy created and later removed the notch.

### Implications
Policy designers should not treat sharp thresholds as harmless administrative conveniences. In intermediary-driven markets with modular technologies, discontinuities can sharply distort investment, so smooth phase-ins or proportional rules may dominate threshold exemptions.

### Evaluation

The paper **does have a narrative arc**, and it is better than many empirical papers. The four-break structure is inherently narrative-friendly. But the arc is still weakened by an overemphasis on estimator mechanics and “largest bunching ratio” rhetoric.

At times it reads like:
- interesting institutional setup,
- bunching estimates,
- mechanism snippets,
- welfare back-of-envelope,
- broad discussion.

That is serviceable, but the story should be more deliberately organized around the central claim:

1. Thresholds usually produce modest effects.
2. Here they produced an extreme effect.
3. Why? Because of intermediaries + modularity + large stakes.
4. Therefore policy design matters more in these settings than we typically assume.

So: **not a collection of results**, but still not as narratively disciplined as it could be.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The paper’s main gap is **not primarily empirical competence**. The result is striking, the setting is clean, and the event pattern is compelling. The gap is mainly about **positioning and ambition**.

### What is the main problem?

**Mostly a framing problem, with some ambition problem.**

- **Framing problem:** The paper is stronger than its current self-description. It is not fundamentally about a German solar notch; it is about when policy thresholds become highly distortionary.
- **Ambition problem:** The paper is still somewhat content to be “a very nice bunching application.” AER needs it to say something larger about regulation, delegated choice, or climate-policy design.

There is also a mild **novelty risk**: bunching around thresholds is a known phenomenon. So the novelty has to come from the combination of:
1. extreme magnitude,
2. real capital-stock distortion,
3. intermediary-driven mechanism,
4. broad design lesson.

### What is the gap between this and a paper that would excite the top 10 people in the field?

A paper that excites the top people would make them revise a general belief, such as:

> “Thresholds are usually a second-order design issue.”  
> No—under common market conditions, they can be first-order.

or

> “Bunching evidence mainly tells us about local responses around tax schedules.”  
> No—it can reveal large distortions in real technology adoption when intermediaries optimize on behalf of households.

At present, the paper gives enough to interest them, but not quite enough to force that belief revision. It needs to be more explicit and more outward-facing.

### Single most impactful piece of advice

**Reframe the paper around a general economic claim: sharp thresholds become first-order distortions when expert intermediaries choose modular technologies—and use German solar as the cleanest demonstration, not the entire point.**

That one change would improve the introduction, literature review, discussion, and conclusion simultaneously.
