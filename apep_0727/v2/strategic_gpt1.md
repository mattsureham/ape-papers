## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper shows that a seemingly minor design feature of climate policy—a sharp exemption threshold at 10 kWp in Germany’s solar subsidy regime—caused installers to systematically shrink rooftop solar systems to stay just below the cutoff. Using the universe of German solar registrations, the paper argues that threshold policies can create extreme real distortions when decisions are made by sophisticated repeat players, the technology is modular, and the gains from gaming the threshold are large relative to adjustment costs.

Why should a busy economist care? Because the paper is not really about one quirky solar rule; it is about when threshold-based regulation creates first-order distortions in real investment, and why those distortions may be much larger in intermediated technology markets than in the canonical bunching settings economists usually study.

### Does the paper articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Mostly, but not optimally. The current opening is vivid and data-rich, and the raw 712:1 contrast is memorable. But the introduction leads with the spike rather than the broader economic question. It still reads too much like “here is a striking bunching fact in German solar” and not enough like “here is a general lesson about regulatory design in markets with expert intermediaries.”

The first two paragraphs should say, more explicitly:

> Many climate and regulatory policies use sharp thresholds for administrative simplicity. Economists know such thresholds can induce bunching, but we know much less about when they generate extreme real distortions rather than modest relabeling or timing responses.  
>  
> This paper studies Germany’s 10 kWp solar threshold, where an exemption from a self-consumption surcharge caused installers to systematically downsize rooftop systems to remain below the cutoff. Using the universe of German solar installations and multiple policy changes that turned the incentive on and off, I show that threshold-induced distortions become enormous when choices are made by sophisticated repeat intermediaries, adjustment is nearly costless because the technology is modular, and the private stakes from staying below the threshold are large. The result is not just bunching at a round number; it is less clean-energy capacity actually installed.

That is the pitch the paper should have.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### Contribution in one sentence

The paper’s contribution is to show that sharp regulatory thresholds can produce extremely large real investment distortions—far larger than standard bunching estimates—when optimization is delegated to expert intermediaries operating modular technologies.

### Is this clearly differentiated from the closest papers?

Only partially. The paper distinguishes itself from the bunching literature by magnitude (“largest bunching response”) and from renewable-energy papers by topic (“thresholds can shrink clean-energy deployment”). But the differentiation is still too descriptive. Right now the novelty is framed as:

1. a large bunching estimate,
2. in a new context,
3. plus a three-condition verbal framework.

That is not quite enough. The closest reaction from a smart reader may still be: “interesting, another bunching paper in a new market.” The author needs to sharpen that this is **not** just another bunching application because:
- the response is on a real investment margin, not taxable income/reporting;
- the optimizing unit is an intermediary, not the final consumer;
- the paper identifies conditions under which thresholds create near-complete avoidance regions;
- the policy consequence is lower deployment of a socially valuable technology.

### World question or literature gap?

It is framed partly as a world question, but it too often slips into “contributes to three literatures.” The stronger frame is clearly the world question:

**When do threshold-based regulations materially distort real production and investment choices?**

That is much stronger than “there is limited evidence on bunching in renewable energy.”

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new?

A smart economist could explain the fact pattern, but I am less sure they could cleanly explain the conceptual contribution. They would likely say: “It’s a very nice bunching paper on German rooftop solar, with an unusually large notch response.” That is good, but not AER-good.

For AER, they need to say something closer to: “It shows that thresholds can massively distort green investment when expert intermediaries can cheaply reoptimize modular technologies, which implies many threshold-based climate regulations may be badly designed.”

### What would make the contribution bigger?

Most specifically:

1. **Lean harder into real consequences, not bunching mechanics.**  
   The paper should make the headline result the loss of installed solar capacity caused by policy design, with bunching as the diagnostic tool.

2. **Turn the three conditions into more than a post hoc list.**  
   Right now “repeat optimization, modularity, disproportionate stakes” is plausible but somewhat essayistic. The paper would be bigger if it framed these as a predictive framework and showed, even descriptively, why each matters.

3. **Broaden from one threshold to a class of policies.**  
   If the paper can show that similar threshold logic appears across climate, energy, building-code, or subsidy design, the stakes rise substantially.

4. **Strengthen the intermediary angle.**  
   This is the most interesting conceptual move. If the paper can more directly persuade the reader that installers—not homeowners—are the operative optimizers, that materially elevates the paper.

5. **Make the welfare margin more concrete.**  
   “135–270 MW foregone” is decent, but still somewhat back-of-the-envelope. If the paper linked this more directly to policy goals, emissions, or deployment targets, the stakes would feel larger.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Governments often use thresholds because they are administratively simple. In most canonical settings, thresholds induce some bunching, but not enough to dominate the policy discussion. Climate policy also relies heavily on such thresholds.

### Tension

When policy is implemented in markets with expert intermediaries and modular technologies, those same thresholds may not just shift density—they may mechanically shrink real investment. Yet we lack clear evidence on when that happens and how severe it can be.

### Resolution

Germany’s 10 kWp solar threshold created exactly this distortion: installers bunched massively below the cutoff during the notch regime, much less before it, and much less again when the threshold moved or disappeared. The response is extraordinarily large and appears to reflect a setting with repeat optimizers, low adjustment costs, and high private gains from staying below the threshold.

### Implications

Threshold-based climate and regulatory policy can be badly misdesigned. If policymakers care about deployment, they should replace sharp cliffs with phase-ins or proportional schedules, especially in markets with intermediated modular technologies.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the bones of one, yes. But it still reads somewhat like a collection of well-executed bunching results plus a general lesson attached afterward.

The strongest story is not:
- “There was bunching at 10 kWp, then less later.”

The strongest story is:
- “Administrative thresholds are often assumed harmless; in this market structure they were not harmless at all—they caused economically meaningful underinvestment in green technology.”

That story should govern the whole paper. Right now, the paper too often reverts to reporting ratios and period-by-period estimates without continuously reminding the reader why those estimates matter.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?

I would lead with:

**Germany’s solar policy didn’t just create bunching at 10 kWp—it induced installers to systematically leave panels off rooftops, reducing deployed solar capacity because going just above the threshold was privately irrational.**

That is much stronger than “the bunching ratio is 86.5.”

### Would people lean in?

Yes, if presented that way. No, if presented as “this may be the largest bunching estimate in the literature.” Only bunching specialists care deeply about the superlative. A broader audience cares about climate policy shrinking clean energy by design.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Likely:
- “How general is this beyond one German threshold?”
- “How sure are we that installers, not households, are the real optimizer?”
- “How much capacity or welfare was actually lost?”
- “What policy design should replace the threshold?”

Those are exactly the questions the paper should more consciously answer in the framing.

### Are the findings modest or null?

Not modest. The findings are visually and economically striking. The challenge is not that the result is too small; it is that the paper sometimes sells the wrong result. The eye-catching number is not the bunching coefficient. The eye-catching fact is that a climate policy reduced the size of the solar systems it subsidized.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

At present, the gap is mainly a combination of **framing problem** and **ambition problem**, with a smaller **scope problem**.

- **Framing problem:** The paper undersells its true contribution by leading with bunching magnitude rather than policy design and real investment distortion.
- **Ambition problem:** It is careful and competent, but it is still written like a very good field-journal paper applying a known empirical tool to a novel setting.
- **Scope problem:** The paper makes a broad conceptual claim from one primary setting. That can still work, but only if the conceptual framing is extremely sharp and the generality is argued more convincingly.

I do **not** think the main issue is novelty in the narrow sense. The fact pattern is novel enough. The problem is that “largest bunching estimate in a solar setting” is not by itself AER-level. “Threshold-based climate policy can materially reduce technology adoption when intermediaries can cheaply optimize around policy cliffs” is much closer.

### Single most impactful advice

If the author could change only one thing, it should be this:

**Rewrite the paper so the main contribution is not “extreme bunching at a solar threshold,” but “a sharp climate-policy threshold caused real underinvestment in solar, revealing a general design failure of threshold regulation in intermediated modular markets.”**

That one shift would improve the introduction, literature review, narrative, title logic, and perceived contribution all at once.
