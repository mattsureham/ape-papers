## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple, important question: when do policy thresholds create very large real distortions rather than the modest bunching responses economists are used to seeing? Using the universe of German rooftop solar installations, it shows that a 10 kWp exemption from a self-consumption surcharge caused installers to systematically downsize systems just below the threshold, leaving substantial solar capacity “on the roof,” and that the distortion receded when the threshold was relaxed and then removed.

A busy economist should care because this is not just another bunching exercise. The paper’s broader claim is that threshold-based regulation can be especially distortionary when decisions are made by sophisticated intermediaries, the technology is modular, and the gain from gaming the threshold dwarfs adjustment costs—a combination likely relevant well beyond solar.

### Does the paper articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Not quite. The opening is vivid and the raw 9.9 vs. 10.1 ratio is memorable, but the first two paragraphs still read like an institutional curiosity before they become a general economics question. The paper gets to the big idea in paragraph three, but for AER the first two paragraphs should do more immediate conceptual work: start with the general problem, then introduce Germany as the near-ideal setting.

### The pitch the paper should have

“Economists know that thresholds distort behavior, but most estimates of bunching are modest. This paper shows that threshold-based policy can generate extreme real distortions when three conditions coincide: decisions are made by repeat-optimizing intermediaries, the underlying choice is modular, and the private stakes from staying below the threshold far exceed the adjustment cost. Germany’s rooftop solar market provides a striking test: a 10 kWp exemption from a self-consumption surcharge induced installers to design systems just below the cutoff at massive scale, and the distortion sharply attenuated when the threshold was raised and then abolished. The result is a first-order policy lesson for climate and regulatory design: small administrative thresholds can materially suppress adoption when markets are intermediated and technologies are easy to resize.”

That is the paper’s AER pitch. It puts the world question first and the German institutional detail second.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper shows that threshold-based climate policy can generate extremely large real distortions—not just statistical bunching—when sophisticated intermediaries can cheaply re-optimize modular technologies, and it demonstrates this using repeated policy changes at the same German solar threshold.

### Is this clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?

Partially, but not sharply enough.

The paper does distinguish itself from standard bunching papers by emphasizing magnitude and mechanism: this is not just “there is bunching at a notch,” but “here is why bunching is an order of magnitude larger in some environments.” That is the right differentiation. Still, the intro currently leans too much on being a very clean bunching application and too little on what general lesson is new relative to the large existing bunching literature.

Right now a smart economist could summarize it as: “It’s a bunching paper on German solar, with a big notch response.” That undersells the paper. The real novelty is the proposed framework for when thresholds become highly distortionary: repeat optimizers + modularity + large stakes relative to adjustment costs.

### World question or literature gap?

It is mostly framed as a world question, which is good: when do thresholds create extreme responses, and how did climate policy shrink solar systems? But it still slips back into literature-speak—“this contributes to the bunching literature / renewable policy literature / intermediated decision-making literature.” For AER, the world question should dominate much more clearly.

The stronger framing is not “there is a gap in the bunching literature about large responses.” It is “regulators routinely use thresholds under the assumption that behavioral distortions will be limited; in some settings that assumption is badly wrong.”

### Could a smart economist explain what is new?

Yes, but only if they are paying attention. The best version of the answer is:

“This paper shows that climate-policy thresholds can cause huge real distortions when installers, not households, choose modular systems, and it uses multiple policy regime shifts to show the distortion turning on and off.”

The weaker version—too likely in current form—is:

“It’s another bunching paper, but with solar panels and a very large effect.”

That is the risk.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Several concrete possibilities:

1. **Lean harder into real consequences, not bunching statistics.**  
   The bunching ratio of 86.5 is eye-catching to specialists, but “Germany left X MW/GWh of cheap rooftop solar unrealized because of a badly designed exemption” is more broadly important. Capacity foregone, generation foregone, and maybe carbon implications are what will travel outside the bunching literature.

2. **Make the intermediary point more central and more comparative.**  
   The paper claims installer intermediation is key, but the evidence is indirect. Strategically, the paper would feel bigger if it could show more directly that markets or subsamples with stronger intermediation exhibit stronger responses. Without that, “intermediaries matter” remains plausible but somewhat asserted.

3. **Connect to policy design generally, not only solar.**  
   The three-condition framework is potentially the big idea. Right now it is presented as a discussion insight. It should be elevated into the core contribution and linked to other domains where thresholds interact with modularity and delegated choice.

4. **Emphasize that this is a design failure in decarbonization policy.**  
   The strongest framing is not merely “there is bunching.” It is “a climate subsidy / exemption design intended as administrative simplification materially reduced clean-energy deployment.” That turns the paper from a tidy RD-adjacent institutional paper into a broader policy design paper.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Governments routinely use thresholds and exemptions because they simplify administration. Economists know such thresholds induce bunching, but the prevailing empirical picture is that responses are often moderate, presumably because agents face optimization frictions or costly adjustment.

### Tension

But some markets look very different: choices are made by professionals who repeatedly face the same rules, technologies are modular, and staying just below a threshold may be very cheap. In those settings, could a small administrative threshold produce a very large real distortion in technology adoption?

### Resolution

In German rooftop solar, the answer is yes. A 10 kWp surcharge exemption induced extreme bunching just below the cutoff, much larger than typical estimates in the bunching literature, and the pattern rose and fell exactly as policy changed from no threshold, to kink, to notch, to threshold relaxation, to abolition.

### Implications

Policy design should treat thresholds as potentially dangerous when markets are intermediated and technologies are easy to resize. For climate policy specifically, sharp exemptions can suppress deployment, and smoother phase-ins may preserve administrative goals without inducing “missing middle” adoption patterns.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

Mostly yes. This is one of the stronger features of the paper. The on/off policy sequence gives it a natural story.

But the arc is still somewhat split between two stories:

1. **A bunching-method paper:** look at this beautiful four-break natural experiment.
2. **A policy-design paper:** thresholds can suppress clean-energy deployment under intermediation.

The paper needs to choose the second as the main story and use the first as the engine. Right now, the engine sometimes becomes the story.

There is also some duplication: the intro, strategy, and discussion each restate the same logic in slightly different forms. That is fine for a workshop paper, but for AER the narrative should feel more distilled.

### If it is a collection of results looking for a story, what story should it be telling?

It should be telling this story:

“Administrative thresholds are often thought to be second-order implementation details. In intermediated markets with modular technologies, they are not implementation details; they are design choices that can materially alter real investment. Germany’s solar policy offers an unusually clean demonstration.”

That is the AER story.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?

“For seven years in Germany, installers put nearly 62,000 rooftop solar systems at 9.9 kWp and essentially none at 10.1 kWp, because crossing the threshold triggered a surcharge; when the threshold moved, the spike collapsed.”

That is the dinner-party fact. It is vivid, memorable, and immediately conveys a real distortion.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

They would lean in—at least initially. The raw pattern is astonishing and easy to understand.

The risk is what happens next. If the presentation pivots too fast into bunching ratios, seventh-degree polynomials, and counterfactual densities, non-specialists may drift away. If instead it pivots into “Germany’s climate policy literally caused smaller solar systems to be installed nationwide,” they will stay with it.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Probably one of these:

- “How much solar capacity did this actually destroy?”
- “Why didn’t homeowners just ignore the threshold if bigger systems were worthwhile?”
- “Is this just Germany, or does it generalize to lots of threshold policies?”
- “What exactly makes the response so much larger than in standard tax bunching settings?”

Those are good questions for the paper because they align with its potential big contribution.

### If the findings are modest or null...

Not applicable; the findings are not modest. The challenge is not whether the result is interesting—it is. The challenge is whether the paper fully cashes out why that result should change how economists think about policy design beyond this setting.

Some value, but mostly summarizing. It should be more declarative and less generic.
