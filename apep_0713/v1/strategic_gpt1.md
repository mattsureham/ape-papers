## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether state laws that block municipalities from offering broadband reduce entrepreneurship by protecting incumbent internet providers from local competition. Using variation in the timing of state preemption laws, it argues that restricting municipal broadband acts like a “startup tax,” lowering firm formation, especially in digitally intensive sectors.

A busy economist should care because the paper tries to connect a very specific regulatory choice in telecom to a first-order macro-regional outcome: business dynamism. If that link is real, then broadband policy is not just infrastructure policy or industrial organization—it is also entrepreneurship policy.

### Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Not quite. The opening anecdote about North Carolina is vivid, but the paper spends too much of its scarce introductory real estate on the mechanics of municipal broadband rather than immediately stating the broader economic question. The current intro gets to the main point, but it does not cleanly elevate the paper from “a paper about telecom regulation” to “a paper about how a specific anti-competition regulation affects entry and local dynamism.”

The first two paragraphs should do three things faster:
1. Establish the broad question: do regulations that shield incumbents in digital infrastructure markets reduce entrepreneurship?
2. Introduce municipal broadband preemption as a clean and important case.
3. Preview the core fact: these laws are associated with lower firm formation, especially in broadband-intensive sectors.

### The pitch the paper should have

“Can regulations that protect infrastructure incumbents suppress entrepreneurship? This paper studies state laws that prohibit or restrict municipal broadband networks—laws that remove a potential competitive threat to private internet providers—and asks whether they reduce new business formation. I show that states adopting these laws experience lower firm birth rates, with especially large declines in broadband-intensive sectors, suggesting that digital infrastructure competition matters for business dynamism.”

That is the AER-facing pitch. The current version is too close to “here is a policy debate about municipal broadband.” It needs to become “here is a general lesson about competition, infrastructure, and entry.”

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper’s contribution is to argue that laws restricting municipal broadband competition reduce new firm formation, thereby linking telecom market structure to entrepreneurship.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers in the literature?

Only partially. The paper says “first causal estimate” of the effect of municipal broadband preemption on firm formation, which is fine as a factual novelty claim, but that is not enough for AER unless the paper also makes clear why this is a different conceptual object from the existing broadband-access literature.

Right now the paper sits awkwardly between three literatures:
- broadband access and economic development,
- telecommunications regulation and market structure,
- regulatory barriers to entrepreneurship.

It names these literatures, but it does not sharply explain what is distinct. The novelty is not merely “no one has estimated this exact policy before.” The novelty should be: **most prior work studies the effect of broadband availability or rollout; this paper studies the effect of suppressing a particular source of competition in broadband provision.** That is a different question about the world.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or filling a gap in a LITERATURE?

Too much as filling a literature gap. The phrase “I fill this gap” is usually a warning sign in top-field positioning. The stronger framing is world-facing:

- How much does incumbent protection in digital infrastructure markets dampen entrepreneurship?
- Are anti-entry regulations in infrastructure sectors transmitted downstream into business formation?

That is much better than “the policy-lever question has not been answered empirically.”

### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what’s new here?

They could probably say: “It’s a DiD paper showing that municipal broadband preemption reduces firm births.” That is not enough. The risk is exactly that they summarize it as “another DiD paper about broadband policy.”

What they should be able to say is: “It shows that competition policy in a foundational input market shapes entrepreneurship. Blocking municipal entry into broadband seems to reduce downstream firm entry, especially in digital sectors.”

That sentence makes it sound like an economics paper, not a policy case study.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Most importantly: **reframe the contribution around downstream consequences of incumbent protection in essential-input markets.** That is the big idea already latent in the paper.

More specifically, the paper would feel bigger if it did one or more of the following:

- **Different framing:** position preemption as a form of incumbent protection in a general class of essential-input markets, not just a peculiar telecom statute.
- **Different outcomes:** beyond aggregate firm births, emphasize where entry should matter most—young high-growth sectors, digital services, rural entrepreneurship, or tradable digital businesses.
- **Different mechanism evidence:** connect more directly to prices, speed, quality, entry by ISPs, or measures of local broadband competitiveness. Right now the mechanism is asserted more than demonstrated.
- **Different comparison:** explicitly contrast municipal broadband preemption with other entry barriers. That would let the “startup tax” idea land more forcefully.
- **Different implication:** the paper could say more about how infrastructure-market competition affects regional inequality or the geography of opportunity.

As written, the paper’s ambition is narrower than the title and language suggest.

5. Potentially work on **municipal broadband itself**, even if from policy, law, or regional studies rather than top economics journals.

Also relevant, though not foregrounded enough:
- Industrial organization work on broadband competition, incumbent behavior, and regulation.
- Spatial/local public finance work on local public provision versus private provision.
- Political economy of preemption and state-local conflict.

### How should the paper position itself relative to those neighbors?

Mostly **build on** the broadband-access literature and **extend** it in a new direction. No need to attack those papers. The paper should say:

- Prior work shows broadband access matters.
- But access is an equilibrium outcome shaped by market structure and regulation.
- This paper studies a specific regulatory intervention that weakens a source of competition and asks whether that matters downstream for entrepreneurship.

Relative to entrepreneurship-barrier papers, it should not claim to overturn them, but rather to widen that literature’s domain: entry barriers do not only come from licensing or red tape; they can also arise indirectly via essential-input markets.

### Is the paper currently positioned too narrowly or too broadly?

Oddly, both.

- **Too narrowly** in the sense that it is stuck in the municipal-broadband policy niche.
- **Too broadly** in the sense that it gestures at entrepreneurship, telecom regulation, and digital infrastructure without choosing a single central conversation.

For AER, it needs one clear conversation with two supporting literatures. My instinct is that the best main conversation is:

**How regulation and competition in essential infrastructure markets affect downstream economic dynamism.**

Then the supporting literatures are broadband and entrepreneurship.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?

It seems underconnected to:
- IO work on competition in broadband markets.
- Political economy of state preemption and regulatory capture.
- Regional/spatial economics on place-based infrastructure and local growth.
- Public economics on local public provision as a competitive discipline on private suppliers.
- Broader entry-barrier literature beyond formal business regulation.

It also does not fully exploit the connection to the literature on **market power in upstream input markets** and downstream consequences. That may be the unexpected but valuable conversation here.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?

Not yet. It is having a policy-conversation when it should be having an economics-conversation.

The high-impact framing is not “Should states allow municipal broadband?” That is too legislative. The high-impact framing is:

**When governments suppress competition in a foundational infrastructure market, what happens to downstream firm creation?**

That is the right conversation.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Broadband has become a basic input into production, and many places—especially smaller cities and rural areas—have faced weak private provision. Municipal broadband emerged as a way to expand supply and discipline incumbents.

### Tension

States then passed laws restricting or prohibiting municipal broadband. These laws are often treated as a governance or regulatory issue, but they may have broader economic consequences if they raise the effective cost or lower the quality of a key production input. The unresolved question is whether protecting incumbents in broadband markets reduces entrepreneurship.

### Resolution

The paper finds that state preemption laws are associated with lower firm birth rates, with larger declines in digital or broadband-intensive sectors than in construction.

### Implications

If the result is correct, telecom preemption is not a narrow legal rule; it is an anti-entry policy with consequences for business dynamism and local economic opportunity. The broader implication is that competition policy in essential-input markets spills over into entrepreneurship.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

Serviceable, but not strong. The ingredients are there, but the story is not fully disciplined.

The paper currently reads somewhat like:
- interesting policy background,
- standard empirical design,
- main estimate,
- some heterogeneity,
- conclusion.

That is more “collection of results” than “tight narrative.”

### What story should it be telling?

The paper should tell one story:

1. **Entrepreneurs need digital infrastructure.**
2. **Municipal broadband can discipline incumbent providers where private competition is weak.**
3. **State preemption removes that competitive threat.**
4. **When that happens, downstream business formation falls—especially where connectivity matters most.**

Everything in the paper should serve that logic. Right now there are small distractions:
- too much attention to the existence of repeals, even though they do not yet seem central;
- too much methodological exposition in the intro;
- not enough emphasis on the conceptual leap from telecom regulation to entrepreneurship.

The “startup tax” phrase is good. It is the paper’s best narrative asset. But it needs to be earned by the framing, not just declared in the abstract and conclusion.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?

“I’d lead with: states that block municipalities from offering broadband appear to get fewer new firms, with especially large declines in information-sector entry.”

That is the cleanest, most memorable fact.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

Economists would lean in initially, because the combination is unusual: broadband regulation and entrepreneurship. But they will only stay engaged if the presenter quickly explains why this is not just a niche telecom paper.

If the pitch is “here is a new DiD on municipal broadband statutes,” phones come out.

If the pitch is “incumbent-protection policies in essential-input markets can reduce downstream entry,” people lean in.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Probably one of these:
- “Is this really about competition and prices, or just correlated politics?”
- “Why should municipal broadband, specifically, matter enough to move aggregate firm births?”
- “Is the effect concentrated in places where private provision was weakest?”
- “What is the mechanism—price, speed, coverage, reliability, or expectations?”

Those are not bad questions. They are exactly the questions of a paper with a real idea. The author should embrace them in the framing.

### If the findings are modest, is that okay?

Yes, but only if modesty is handled honestly. A 5 percent decline in firm births is not trivial, but it is not a headline-grabbing giant effect either. The paper should avoid overselling with language like “large negative” based on home-brew standardized effect labels. That kind of language cheapens the contribution.

The result is interesting not because it is huge, but because it links a targeted infrastructure regulation to downstream entrepreneurship. That is enough.

The null on broadband take-up is not, by itself, interesting. It currently feels like a weakly defended failed mechanism test. The paper does explain the data-window problem, which is fair, but strategically it should not foreground this secondary result. Better to say the direct mechanism evidence is limited, while sectoral heterogeneity is suggestive.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

At present, the gap is mostly a **framing and ambition problem**, with some **scope** issues.

This does not currently read like an AER paper because the paper’s central claim is narrower than it needs to be, and the introduction does not persuade the reader that this is a general economic question. The science may or may not ultimately hold up under refereeing, but even before that stage, the paper needs a much more forceful answer to: why is this not just a careful paper on a niche telecom policy?

### What is the main problem?

If I had to choose one, it is **framing**.

The paper has a potentially good idea:
- incumbent protection in essential infrastructure markets may suppress downstream entry.

But the paper currently presents itself as:
- a first estimate on municipal broadband preemption.

That is not enough for AER.

### Is there also a scope problem?

Yes. To feel top-journal, the paper likely needs either:
- richer evidence on mechanism and margins of adjustment, or
- a broader conceptual framing with clearer general implications.

As written, the evidence package feels a bit thin relative to the breadth of the claims. Again, not a referee point about validity—just a strategic point about whether the paper feels large enough.

### Is there a novelty problem?

Not fatal, but potentially. The broad claim that broadband matters for economic outcomes is well known. So the paper must lean hard into what is distinctive:
- not broadband rollout,
- not access per se,
- but regulation that suppresses a source of supply competition.

That distinction is the novelty. It needs to be made much sharper.

### Is there an ambition problem?

Yes. The paper is competent but safe. It does not yet fully claim the larger conceptual territory available to it.

### Single most impactful piece of advice

**Rewrite the introduction and framing so the paper is explicitly about how incumbent-protecting regulation in a foundational input market reduces downstream entrepreneurship, with municipal broadband preemption as the setting rather than the entire point.**

If the author changes only one thing, that is it.
