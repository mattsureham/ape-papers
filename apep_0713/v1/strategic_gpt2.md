## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether state laws that block municipalities from offering broadband reduce entrepreneurship. Using variation in when states adopted municipal broadband preemption laws, it argues that restricting local broadband competition lowers new business formation, especially in digitally intensive industries, implying that telecom regulation can act like an entry barrier for startups.

A busy economist should care because this is not really a paper about broadband per se; it is a paper about whether product-market regulation in a foundational input market shapes business dynamism. If true, that is a broadly important claim linking infrastructure policy, market power, and entrepreneurship.

### Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Not quite. The current opening is vivid, but it is a bit too policy-advocacy-flavored and too narrow. “A single state law silenced an experiment in local connectivity” is colorful, but the framing initially sounds like a case study in municipal telecom politics, not a general-interest economics question. The paper gets to the larger point eventually, but not fast enough and not at the right altitude.

### What the first two paragraphs should say instead

The paper should open with the broad economic question first, then introduce municipal broadband preemption as the setting:

> Many businesses now rely on broadband as a core input, yet broadband markets in much of the United States remain highly concentrated and heavily regulated. This raises a basic question: when policy protects incumbent internet providers from local competition, does it merely affect household connectivity, or does it also reduce business formation by raising the effective cost of going online?  
>  
> This paper studies state laws that prohibit or restrict municipalities from building broadband networks. I show that these preemption laws reduce firm entry, with larger effects in digitally intensive sectors. The broader implication is that regulation in input markets can operate like an entry tax on entrepreneurship: by limiting competition in broadband, states may depress local business dynamism.

That is the pitch. Start with the world question, not the Wilson anecdote.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### Contribution in one sentence

The paper’s contribution is to argue that laws restricting municipal broadband provision reduce new firm formation, thereby linking telecom market regulation to entrepreneurship and business dynamism.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest papers?

Only partially. The paper says “first causal estimate” of the effect of municipal broadband preemption on firm formation. That is plausible, but “first causal estimate” is not, by itself, a persuasive AER contribution unless the paper explains why this margin is substantively distinct from existing work on broadband rollout, internet adoption, and local economic development.

Right now the differentiation is too mechanical:

- prior work: broadband access affects outcomes
- this paper: preemption affects firm formation

That is a literature gap, but the paper has not yet made it feel like a conceptual advance. The authors need to say more clearly that they are studying **competition policy in an upstream infrastructure market**, not simply another shock to internet availability.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a gap in a literature?

Too much as a literature gap. “Prior work has studied X; I study Y” is adequate for a field journal but not strong enough for AER. The stronger version is:

- In the world, governments often regulate whether local public entities can challenge entrenched private providers.
- The economic stakes are not only prices and coverage, but downstream entry and innovation.
- Municipal broadband preemption is a clean and policy-relevant test of that broader proposition.

That framing makes it about the world.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?

At present, they could probably say: “It’s a staggered DiD paper showing anti-municipal-broadband laws reduce startup rates.” That is intelligible, but it still sounds like “another DiD paper about a regulatory shock.” The introduction does not yet equip the reader to say the more interesting thing: “This paper shows that protecting incumbents in digital infrastructure markets can suppress entrepreneurship downstream.”

### What would make the contribution bigger?

Several possibilities:

1. **Make the downstream-market-power angle central.**  
   The biggest upgrade would be to reframe this as a paper on how regulation that protects upstream incumbents affects downstream entry. That is larger than municipal broadband.

2. **Show more clearly that the economically meaningful margin is entry in broadband-dependent sectors and places.**  
   The sector heterogeneity is currently useful but thin. If the paper can sharpen “who is affected” in a way tightly tied to digital dependence, the contribution becomes much more than an average effect on firm births.

3. **Connect to competition, not just access.**  
   The current mechanism language emphasizes connectivity cost and access. But the setting is really about foreclosing competition. A stronger contribution would foreground competition, prices/quality, and entrepreneurship rather than “public broadband as a public good.”

4. **Shift from a narrow policy question to a general principle.**  
   The larger claim is: restrictions on public options in concentrated infrastructure markets can reduce business formation. That is a more ambitious and portable contribution.

It should **build on** the broadband/economic outcomes literature, **borrow framing from** the regulation-and-entry literature, and **speak much more directly to industrial organization and political economy**.

It should not “attack” the existing broadband literature. Rather, it should say:

- We know broadband matters.
- We know market structure matters for broadband outcomes.
- What we do not know is whether policy that blocks local competitive entry in broadband markets has downstream effects on entrepreneurship.

That is a natural bridge.

### Is the paper positioned too narrowly or too broadly?

Currently, too narrowly in setting and too broadly in claim.

- Too narrowly because it reads like a paper for people who already care about municipal broadband policy.
- Too broadly because the rhetoric occasionally implies sweeping claims about “economic opportunity” and “startup taxes” without enough architecture showing why this case matters generally.

The right balance is: narrow institutional setting, broad economic question.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?

A few literatures should be more explicitly in view:

1. **Industrial organization of broadband / platform and network industries**  
   The paper needs more serious engagement with how competition, quality, and entry work in telecom markets.

2. **Public option / government provision competing with private firms**  
   This is not exactly the same as broadband access. The setting is unusual because the potential entrant is municipal government. That links to broader debates on public provision, public options, and crowd-out/crowd-in.

3. **Political economy of regulation and incumbent protection**  
   The laws are presented as incumbent-driven. That naturally connects to capture, rent protection, and anti-competitive state policy.

4. **Business dynamism and local input costs**  
   The paper should connect more directly to work asking how local cost environments shape startup formation.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?

Not yet. It is currently having a “broadband policy evaluation” conversation. The more impactful conversation is:

**What happens to entrepreneurship when states insulate incumbent providers in essential input markets from local competition?**

That is a much better AER conversation.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Broadband has become an important production input, especially for newer and more digital firms. Meanwhile, many U.S. broadband markets are concentrated, and some states prohibit municipalities from entering as providers.

### Tension

If municipal networks are barred, two competing views are possible:

- maybe this is a niche governance issue with little economic consequence;
- or maybe restricting local broadband competition raises effective input costs and suppresses startup entry.

The tension should be about whether seemingly technical telecom regulation has real downstream effects on dynamism.

### Resolution

The paper finds that preemption is associated with lower firm birth rates, with larger declines in information-heavy sectors and little effect in a placebo sector.

### Implications

The implication is that infrastructure regulation can shape entrepreneurship, and policies that protect upstream incumbents may act like entry barriers downstream.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

Serviceable, but not fully coherent. Right now it is halfway between a policy brief and a conventional empirical paper. The “startup tax” phrase is good branding, but the paper has not fully earned it narratively. It needs a stronger arc that goes:

1. entrepreneurship depends on key inputs;
2. broadband is now one such input;
3. policy can affect broadband competition, not just deployment;
4. preemption laws are a test of whether protecting incumbents suppresses downstream entry;
5. evidence suggests yes.

At present, some sections feel like a collection of results attached to a policy topic. The sector heterogeneity helps, but the paper still reads somewhat like “here is a policy shock, here are the estimates, here is a plausible mechanism.”

### What story should it be telling?

It should be telling a story about **regulatory protection of incumbents in digital infrastructure markets and the consequences for entrepreneurial entry**. Municipal broadband is the institutional vehicle, not the main character.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?

“I find that state laws blocking municipalities from providing broadband reduce new business formation by about 5 percent, with larger effects in digital industries.”

That is the dinner-party line.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

Some would lean in, but many would hesitate. Why? Because “municipal broadband preemption laws” sounds niche and legalistic. The result becomes interesting only when translated into broader language:

“States that protect incumbent broadband providers from local competition appear to get fewer startups.”

That version gets attention.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Almost certainly: “Is this really about broadband competition, or is it proxying for a broader anti-business or anti-urban policy environment?”  

That is exactly why the paper’s framing must lean harder on conceptual contribution and mechanism, not only on average treatment effects.

### If the findings are modest, is that okay?

Yes, a 5 percent effect on startup formation is not trivial. The issue is not that the findings are too small; the issue is that the paper has not yet made clear why this specific policy margin is the economically important one.

The broadband-adoption result is weak, and that undercuts the mechanism story as currently presented. The paper acknowledges the data limitation, which is fine, but strategically it should stop overselling direct mechanism proof and instead say: the evidence is consistent with the idea that competitive restrictions in broadband markets matter most for sectors that rely heavily on digital connectivity. That is enough for positioning purposes.

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

### What would make it read better?

   This is the top priority. The current intro starts with an anecdote, then quickly goes into methods, then gives literature. It should instead start with the broad economic stakes and only then introduce the policy setting.

2. **Move identification details later.**  
   The introduction gets too quickly into estimator choice, number of treated states, and timing windows. That is not what should be occupying page 1 real estate. For strategic positioning, the front of the paper should sell the question and the finding, not the estimator.

3. **Cut the policy-background section by 25–40 percent.**  
   It currently reads like a topical briefing note. Keep only what is necessary to understand the laws and their variation.

4. **Promote the heterogeneity result.**  
   This is one of the more interesting substantive facts in the paper. It probably deserves to appear in the introduction as part of the headline contribution, not as a later add-on.

5. **Demote or eliminate weak ornamentation.**  
   The “standardized effect size” appendix and especially the “large negative under standard benchmarks” language are actively distracting. This is not how AER papers sell magnitude. Just report economically interpretable units.

6. **Tighten the conclusion.**  
   The conclusion now mostly summarizes and gestures at policy relevance. It should instead do two things:
   - restate the broad economic lesson;
   - explain what this means for debates on competition, infrastructure, and entrepreneurship.

### Is the paper front-loaded with the good stuff?

Partly, but not optimally. The main result appears early enough, but it is surrounded by too much methodological scaffolding and too much policy-specific narration. The best substantive hook—the entrepreneurship consequence of incumbent protection—is not foregrounded strongly enough.

### Are there results buried that should be in the main text?

The sector heterogeneity is main-text-worthy and should be treated as central, not ancillary. If the paper has any place-by-place heterogeneity by rurality, local ISP concentration, or baseline digital intensity, that would likely be more valuable than some current robustness material.

### Is the conclusion adding value?

Only modestly. It mostly restates findings. It should be used to elevate the paper from municipal broadband policy to a broader lesson about anti-competitive regulation in essential service markets.

Primarily a **framing and ambition problem**, with some **scope problem**.

- **Ambition problem:** It settles too quickly for “first estimate in this policy domain” instead of making a larger claim about how upstream competition policy affects downstream entrepreneurship.

Not fatal, but potentially. Broadband affects economic activity is not new. Regulation affects entry is not new. The paper’s novelty lies in connecting those through municipal broadband preemption. That can work if the paper makes the connection feel like a genuine conceptual contribution rather than a policy-specific mashup.

A paper that convincingly says:

> In essential digital infrastructure markets, state policies that shield incumbents from local entry reduce entrepreneurial dynamism downstream.

That is interesting to IO people, public finance people, urban/regional people, political economy people, and macro/biz-dynamism people. But the paper has to sound like that paper.

### Single most impactful piece of advice

**Reframe the paper from “the effect of municipal broadband preemption laws” to “how protecting incumbents in essential digital infrastructure markets affects downstream firm entry,” and make every section serve that broader claim.**

That one change would do more than any additional table.
