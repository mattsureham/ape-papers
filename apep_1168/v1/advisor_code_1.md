**Idea Fidelity**

The paper mostly tracks the manifest idea: it combines USGS turbines, NREL ordinances, and the SCI to ask whether anti-wind ordinances diffuse via social networks or geography. However, the econometric strategy diverges from the original manifest in a few respects. The manifest promised an explicit shift-share (Bartik) design with AKM standard errors and a large spatial sample of 695 turbine counties; the paper instead implements exposure regressions that resemble weighted sums without the classic shock-share instrumentation or CS-style inference (the paper clusters by state rather than using AKM). The manifest also emphasized “SCI-weighted exposure predicts ordinance adoption beyond geographic distance,” yet the paper’s preferred specification simply includes both exposures together—this still tests the comparative fit but lacks the clear causal framing of the original shift-share plan. Apart from these deviations, the paper stays faithful to the data sources and research question.

**Summary**

The paper investigates why counties that never hosted wind turbines nonetheless adopt restrictive wind ordinances, constructing two time-varying exposure measures: social proximity using the Facebook SCI and geographic proximity via inverse-distance-weighted turbine counts. Using a panel with county and year fixed effects, it shows that only nearby turbines (within ~100km) predict ordinance adoption, while SCI-weighted exposure adds no explanatory power once geography is controlled, suggesting a “proximity trap” driven by direct sensory exposure rather than social-network diffusion. Robustness exercises (distance bands, event study, state×year FE) support the spatial interpretation.

**Essential Points**

1. **Identification of Geographic Exposure:** The identifying assumption—that new turbines in other counties are exogenous to a given county’s ordinance decisions—is strong. Yet the paper provides little direct evidence that geographic exposure is plausibly exogenous beyond the event study, and even the event study is constructed using a median-split rather than continuous exposure, limiting its ability to rule out confounding trends. For example, counties close to new turbines might share unobserved economic or political shocks (e.g., state legislative campaigns or coordinated anti-wind lobbying) that both drive turbine siting and ordinance adoption. I would like to see either a more rigorous instrument (e.g., wind resource potential or terrain) for geographic exposure or a stronger argument that turbine siting is not correlated with county-level demand for ordinances beyond what the fixed effects absorb. Without this, the main causal claim remains suggestive.

2. **Mechanism Testing Between SCI and Geography:** The interpretation that the null SCI coefficient indicates the absence of network diffusion relies on the assumption that the SCI is a good proxy for the relevant social channels. But the SCI is a cross-sectional Facebook friendship matrix, and the paper acknowledges it is stale and may correlate with unobserved spatial factors (even the placebo shows SCI predicts own turbines). More importantly, the paper never constructs a counterfactual exposure that isolates purely social diffusion—you only get the residual association after conditioning on geographic exposure. This is a very blunt test. To strengthen the claim, the authors need to either (i) show heterogeneity where social exposure should be stronger (e.g., high-Facebook-penetration counties or migration links) or (ii) explore lag structures that would allow SCI shocks to precede ordinance adoption independently of geography. Without such nuance, the “networks don’t matter” conclusion risks overstating what the data support.

3. **Interpretation of the Effect Size and Policy Implications:** The paper translates coefficients into standardized effect sizes and makes broad policy statements about the 100km contagion zone affecting siting strategy. However, the LPM coefficients are very small (0.000035 per turbine) and the substantive significance seems driven by aggregating across large counts of turbines. The paper would benefit from clearer communication about what a “unit” change actually means (e.g., the effect of an additional large wind farm versus one turbine) and the plausible counterfactual—how much does getting turbines within 100km of a county increase its chance of passing an ordinance? As written, the policy implications feel overdrawn given the modest variation explained and low R². The authors should temper claims or provide simulations that connect coefficient magnitudes to realistic policy scenarios.

**Suggestions**

1. **Strengthen the Event Study / Parallel Trends Evidence.** The current event study interacts an above-median exposure indicator with year dummies. This coarse approach could mask differential pre-trends among counties with different exposure growth paths. Consider estimating an event study using continuous exposure changes (e.g., year-on-year increases in nearby turbines) or binning counties by exposure percentile and plotting coefficients relative to the year before exposure growth. This would allow you to visually and statistically confirm that ordinance adoption does not anticipate turbine spillovers, bolstering the identifying assumption.

2. **Explore Alternative Social Exposure Measures or Heterogeneity.** If the SCI is an imperfect proxy for the relevant social networks, try augmenting it with other metrics—such as shared media markets, direct migration flows, or membership in the same regional planning organizations—to see whether these add any explanatory power. Alternatively, interact SCI exposure with Facebook penetration or broadband access to test whether the null is driven by measurement error. If no alternative social link predicts adoption, that would provide more confidence that geography dominates.

3. **Consider Non-linearities / Thresholds in Geographic Exposure.** The distance-band analysis is useful, but you might also explore non-linearities in the inverse-distance weighting itself—e.g., use a flexible spline on distance, test different bandwidths, or allow for saturation effects (do additional turbines beyond a certain count have diminishing marginal effects?). This could clarify whether the 100km “zone” is truly sharp or if the effect decays smoothly. It would also help substantiate the “contagion zone” narrative.

4. **Clarify the Role of Own Turbines and Reverse Causality.** The positive coefficient on own turbines suggests that hosting turbines increases the likelihood of adopting ordinances, which contradicts the “counties with turbines adopt fewer restrictions” implication. Explain whether this reflects regulatory backlash after turbines arrive or mechanical correlation (e.g., counties that care a lot about wind also pass ordinances). Additionally, as SCI-weighted exposure predicts own turbines, discuss more explicitly how you interpret the SCI coefficient dropping when geography is added—is it multicollinearity, control picking up the same variation, or something else? Including variance inflation factors or conditional R² decompositions could help.

5. **Deepen the Policy Discussion with Counterfactual Simulations.** To ground the “proximity trap” narrative, simulate how ordinance adoption probabilities change under alternative siting strategies. For instance, what would happen if new turbines were allocated to counties already hosting capacity (and therefore “immune”) versus expanding outward? Even a simple back-of-the-envelope calculation showing the difference in expected ordinance counts between these strategies would make the policy takeaway more concrete.

6. **Address Measurement Error and Database Limitations More Fully.** The Appendix notes that the NREL database uses generative AI and that the SCI is cross-sectional. It would be helpful to assess how sensitive the results are to potential errors—for example, re-estimate using a restricted sample of ordinances with high-confidence dates only or conduct a placebo year test (use machines to randomly assign ordinance years) to see how robust the distance gradient is. A short robustness table showing results using alternative ordinance date assumptions would reassure readers that the main findings do not hinge on noisy data.

7. **Improve Communication of Effect Sizes.** The current standardized effect discussion is helpful but could be supplemented with a simple example: e.g., “A county at the 75th percentile of nearby turbine exposure (X turbines within 100km) is Y percentage points more likely to have adopted an ordinance than a county at the 25th percentile.” This anchors the reader’s intuition and highlights how the coefficient maps to real-world variation.

8. **Clarify the Treatment of State-Level Preemption.** The geographic exposure may be particularly powerful in states where counties retain full zoning authority; in states with preemptive statewide rules, ordinances may be forbidden regardless of exposure. Consider estimating heterogenous effects by state legal regime (home-rule vs. preemption) or restricting the sample to states where counties can adopt ordinances. This would also address concerns that state-level politics drive both turbine siting and ordinance adoption.

9. **Discuss Potential Spillovers from Ordinances to Turbine Siting.** If counties adopt ordinances, that could deter new turbines nearby, creating a feedback loop where the same exposure that triggers an ordinance later reduces turbine growth. Does the data show any post-ordinance slowdown in nearby turbine installations? Even descriptive evidence would enrich the narrative and highlight dynamic interactions between policy and infrastructure deployment.

Overall, the paper tackles an important question and leverages rich data, but it would benefit from deeper treatment of identification, more nuanced mechanism testing, and clearer translation of estimates into substantive policy implications.
