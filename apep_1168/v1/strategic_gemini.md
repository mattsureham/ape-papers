## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
The paper asks why local governments preemptively ban wind energy infrastructure they have never actually hosted. It tests whether this policy contagion spreads via social networks (information/ideology) or geographic proximity (physical exposure), finding that seeing and hearing turbines in a neighboring county—not reading about them on social media—is the primary driver of restrictive local ordinances. This is critical for economists because it identifies a specific, spatial "policy trap" that threatens the efficiency of the energy transition.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this remarkably well. The "puzzle" (190 counties banning what they’ve never seen) is a "hook" that immediately elevates the paper.
**The pitch it should have:** It's almost there, but the intro should lead more aggressively with the *economic* cost of this friction. It should explicitly state: "The U.S. energy transition relies on decentralized land-use decisions. We find that physical proximity to existing turbines creates a 100km 'contagion zone' of preemptive regulation, suggesting that current siting strategies inadvertently maximize political resistance."

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**Contribution:** The paper identifies physical proximity as the dominant mechanism for the spatial diffusion of restrictive infrastructure policy, providing a boundary condition for the literature on social network-mediated information diffusion.

**Evaluation:**
- **Differentiation:** High. It moves beyond "proximity affects attitudes" (Stokes) to "proximity creates policy." It uses the Social Connectedness Index (SCI) not to show it matters, but as a horse-race competitor to geography.
- **Question:** Framed as a question about the world (the "Proximity Trap"), which is strong.
- **What would make it bigger?** The paper needs an **economic welfare or "capacity-at-risk" calculation.** If the 100km rule holds, how many gigawatts of potential wind capacity are currently "threatened" by being in a contagion zone? Turning this from a "finding about ordinances" to a "finding about the future supply of renewable energy" is the AER-level move.

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
The paper sits at the intersection of Political Economy (NIMBYism), Urban/Regional Economics (spatial spillovers), and the nascent "Facebook SCI" literature.

- **Closest Neighbors:** Bailey et al. (2018) on SCI; Stokes (2016) on wind backlash; and the broader policy diffusion literature (e.g., Shipan and Volden).
- **Positioning:** It should position itself as a **skeptical check** on the "everything is a social network effect" trend. By showing that for visceral, physical nuisances, geography *still* beats Facebook, it provides a much-needed nuance to the SCI literature.
- **Unaware of?** It should speak more to the **Political Economy of Land Use** (e.g., Fischel’s "Homevoter Hypothesis"). Is this proximity effect driven by property value fears or aesthetic/noise nuisance?

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC
- **Setup:** The U.S. is rapidly expanding wind power to meet climate goals.
- **Tension:** A massive wave of local bans is rising, often in places with no turbines. Why?
- **Resolution:** It’s not "fake news" or social media bubbles; it’s the physical reality of turbines being visible/audible from across the county line.
- **Implications:** Siting wind farms in "virgin" territory creates a 100km radius of regulatory backlash.

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong and clean. It feels like a "discovery" rather than just a measurement exercise.

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
**The Fact:** "If your neighbor gets a wind farm, you are 11% more likely to ban them yourself within the next few years, even if you don't have a single turbine."
**Response:** People will lean in. It contradicts the idea that "familiarity breeds acceptance" and suggests instead that "proximity breeds preemptive defense."
**The follow-up:** "Is it because of property values?" The paper needs to do more to tease out *why* 100km is the magic number. Is it the range of a local TV news station? The distance people drive to the grocery store?

---

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
- **Front-loading:** The paper is well-structured. However, the "NREL database was assembled using generative AI" (p. 11) is a potential landmine. This needs to be moved to the Data section and defended vigorously with a validation exercise (e.g., "we hand-checked 10% of the AI-generated dates and found X% accuracy").
- **Appendix to Main:** The "capacity-at-risk" calculation mentioned in Section 2 should be a main-text figure.
- **Conclusion:** The discussion of "strategic siting" (clustering vs. spreading) is the most interesting part and should be expanded into a short counterfactual or policy-simulation section.

---

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The paper is currently a very good *Journal of Public Economics* or *AEJ: Policy* paper. To make it **AER**, it needs **Ambition and Scope.**

1.  **The Mechanism Deep-Dive:** Prove it's "sight and sound." Use topographic data to show that the effect is stronger for counties with a clear line-of-sight to their neighbor's turbines vs. those blocked by mountains. This turns a "distance result" into a "sensory result."
2.  **The Counterfactual:** Use the 100km estimate to simulate the "regulatory cost" of the current fragmented siting process versus a more centralized or clustered one.

**Single Biggest Advice:** Use GIS/Topography data to prove the "speed of sight" mechanism; if you can show that the effect disappears when a mountain is in the way, the paper becomes an instant classic.
