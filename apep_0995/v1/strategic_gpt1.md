## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple political-economy question: when the state forces local governments into larger, more distant administrative units, does that produce far-right backlash? Using France’s 2017 intercommunal consolidation under Loi NOTRe, the paper argues the answer is no: even a very large, compulsory reorganization of local governance did not measurably increase support for the RN.

A busy economist should care because the broader claim at stake is bigger than France: much contemporary work suggests that “distance” from decision-makers fuels populism. If a massive, imposed consolidation of local governance leaves far-right voting unchanged, that puts useful discipline on a popular narrative.

**Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Not quite. The opening is vivid, but the paper drifts too quickly into institutional detail and literature name-checking. The current introduction sounds like “here is a reform; here is a DiD; here is a null.” It does not cleanly establish the broader empirical claim being tested, nor why this particular reform is a sharp test of that claim.

**What the first two paragraphs should say instead:**  

> Across Europe and beyond, politicians and scholars often claim that populist backlash is fueled when governance moves farther away from citizens. But that claim is rarely tested in settings where institutions—not trade, migration, or macro shocks—directly increase the distance between voters and decision-makers. France’s 2017 intercommunal consolidation offers exactly such a test: roughly 17,000 communes were reassigned into larger supracommunal bodies, in one of the largest forced local-governance reorganizations in Europe.
>
> This paper asks whether that increase in administrative distance boosted support for the far right. It finds no meaningful effect on RN vote share. The result matters because it distinguishes a broad theory of populism from a narrower one: voters may react strongly to economic dislocation, immigration, or salient losses of local identity, but not necessarily to technocratic consolidation of an indirect layer of government that most citizens barely see.

That is the pitch the paper should have. It frames the paper as a test of a broad proposition about the world, not just an evaluation of one French law.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution in one sentence:**  
The paper provides evidence that a large forced consolidation of an intermediate local-governance layer in France did not measurably increase far-right voting, thereby limiting the scope of the “governance distance causes populism” hypothesis.

### Evaluation

**Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?**  
Only partly. The paper says prior municipal-merger work studies turnout rather than populist support, which is useful, but the differentiation is still a bit thin. Right now the reader can too easily file this under “another local-government-reform paper with voting outcomes.” The author needs to sharpen exactly what is new:

1. This is **not** a municipal merger paper in which municipalities disappear.  
2. It is instead a paper about **reorganization of an indirect governance layer**.  
3. The outcome is **far-right voting**, not turnout or participation.  
4. The core substantive payoff is a **boundary condition** for theories linking institutional distance to populism.

That combination is novel enough to be interesting, but the paper needs to say this more crisply.

**Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or filling a gap in a LITERATURE?**  
Mostly as filling a literature gap. That weakens it. “First causal estimate of X” is not an AER pitch by itself. The stronger framing is: *Do citizens punish technocratic consolidation by turning to the far right?* That is a world question with broad relevance.

**Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain what’s new?**  
At present, maybe only vaguely: “It’s a DiD on French local-government reform and RN votes, with a null.” That is not enough. The introduction needs to help the reader say: “It’s a sharp test of whether administrative distancing alone fuels populist backlash—and it doesn’t.”

**What would make this contribution bigger?**  
Several possibilities, in order of importance:

1. **Mechanism via salience/perception**, not just more outcomes.  
   The paper’s most promising interpretation is that the reform changed governance formally but not perceptibly. If the paper could show that effects remain null even where citizens were most likely to notice the reform—or conversely are only present where salience was high—that would elevate the paper from “null effect” to “theory refinement.”

2. **A comparison to reforms that do trigger backlash.**  
   The paper currently gestures to municipal mergers and refugee allocation, but the comparison remains rhetorical. A stronger framing would explicitly contrast intercommunal consolidation with reforms that eliminate visible local identity or redistribute resources visibly. The contribution then becomes: *not all forms of governance centralization are politically equivalent*.

3. **Broader political outcomes, not just RN vote share.**  
   Turnout is mentioned but buried. If the reform had no effect on RN share, did it affect turnout, incumbency patterns, anti-system voting more broadly, protest voting, or trust proxies? The paper need not become sprawling, but one or two strategically chosen complementary outcomes could help show whether the null is specific to the far right or general.

4. **Heterogeneity by exposure to local identity loss or service transfer intensity.**  
   The current intensity measure is administrative size change. That is plausible but not very evocative. A more meaningful “bite” measure—loss of local autonomy in salient services, distance to the new seat, or disappearance of familiar institutional anchors—would make the contribution more substantive.

- Existing work shows that visible, identity-relevant, or economically disruptive changes can shift political behavior.
- This paper tests a narrower mechanism: whether **administrative distance alone**, holding local identity largely intact, is enough to increase far-right support.

That is a valuable synthesis and boundary-setting exercise.

Oddly, both.

Right now the paper has the skeleton of these connections, but not enough flesh.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?

Not fully. It is currently having the conversation: “Here is a French reform and a null result.”  
The better conversation is: **“What kinds of centralization trigger populist backlash, and what kinds do not?”**

That is a much better AER conversation.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
There is a prominent idea in political economy that moving governance farther from citizens erodes democratic connection and fuels anti-system or populist voting.

### Tension
Much of the evidence usually bundled under that claim concerns shocks that also change economic conditions, immigration exposure, or local identity. So we do not know whether **administrative distancing itself** is enough to generate backlash.

### Resolution
France’s forced intercommunal consolidation offers a large-scale test, and the paper finds no detectable increase in RN support.

### Implications
The result suggests either that administrative distance is not by itself politically potent, or that it matters only when citizens perceive and experience it as a salient loss of voice, identity, or material control.

### Evaluation

There is a plausible narrative arc here, but the paper does not fully execute it. It currently feels somewhat like a collection of sensible empirical results arranged around a null, rather than a fully developed story. The main weakness is that the interpretation comes late and remains underpowered. The paper wants the null itself to do all the work.

**What story should it be telling?**  
Not “forced consolidation had no effect.”  
Rather:

> Theories of populist backlash often conflate several channels: economic shocks, identity threats, and institutional distancing. This reform isolates institutional distancing in a relatively technocratic form. The fact that nothing happens electorally suggests that distance without salience, identity loss, or visible redistribution may not be enough to move voters toward the far right.

That is a real story. It refines theory rather than merely reporting non-results.

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?

“France forcibly reassigned 17,000 communes into larger intercommunal governments, and it appears to have had essentially no effect on far-right voting.”

That is the lead.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

Initially, they would lean in—because the setup is good and timely. But they will lean back quickly if the next sentence is only “and here is a precise null.” A null on an obscure administrative layer is not self-sustaining. The follow-up has to be interpretive.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Almost certainly:  
**“Why not?”**  
Then:  
- Did voters not notice?  
- Is the reform too technocratic?  
- Would this differ for municipal mergers that eliminate identity?  
- Does this tell us anything general about centralization and populism, or only about French EPCIs?

Those are exactly the questions the paper needs to answer in the introduction and discussion.

### If the findings are null or modest: is the null itself interesting?

Potentially yes, but only if the paper makes a stronger case that this was a **hard test** of the theory. The paper already tries to do this by emphasizing scale and power. That is useful but not sufficient. What matters is not just that the reform was large administratively, but whether it was large **as experienced by voters**. If the reform was invisible to most citizens, then the null is less surprising and therefore less informative.

So the paper must argue more carefully:

- Either the reform was indeed salient enough that the null is theoretically surprising;
- Or the deeper contribution is that **formal institutional change without citizen salience does not produce populist backlash**.

At present it wants both, and the tension is unresolved.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

Bluntly, the current gap is substantial.

This is not mainly a craftsmanship problem. It is a **strategic ambition and framing problem** with some scope issues.

### The gap

**1. Framing problem**  
The paper is stronger than “French EPCI reform had a null effect,” but weaker than “governance distance does not cause populism.” It needs the middle position: *administrative centralization without salient identity loss or visible material effects may not generate populist backlash.* That is the real claim.

**2. Scope problem**  
The paper currently rests almost entirely on one outcome and one treatment interpretation. To rise to AER level, it needs either:
- sharper mechanism/salience evidence, or
- a richer comparison that clarifies why this centralization differs from politically consequential reforms.

**3. Novelty problem**  
The design is competent, but the novelty is mostly in setting plus null result. That is not enough by itself for AER. The paper needs to produce a conceptually reusable insight.

**4. Ambition problem**  
The paper is too safe. It asks a sensible question, answers it cleanly, and stops. Top-field papers usually do one more thing: they reshape how readers think about a larger class of phenomena.

### What would excite the top 10 people in this field?

A paper that says something like:

> “Populist backlash is not a generic reaction to larger jurisdictions. It arises when institutional change is salient, identity-threatening, or visibly redistributive. France’s intercommunal consolidation isolates administrative distance absent those features, and produces no electoral response.”

That would interest them. It turns the paper into theory refinement rather than case-study evaluation.

### Single most impactful piece of advice

**Reframe the paper around a boundary condition: not whether consolidation affects populism in France, but whether administrative distance by itself—without visible identity loss or salient material change—is enough to generate populist backlash.**

If the author changes only one thing, it should be that.
