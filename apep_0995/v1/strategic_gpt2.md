## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple, policy-relevant question: when the state forces local governments into larger, more distant administrative units, does that produce populist backlash? Using France’s 2015–2017 intercommunal consolidation reform, the paper finds that even a very large, compulsory governance restructuring had essentially no effect on far-right presidential voting.

A busy economist should care because there is a broad and influential story in political economy that “distance from local democratic control” fuels anti-system politics. If a massive, forced consolidation in a country central to the European populism debate does not move RN vote share, that meaningfully disciplines that narrative.

### Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Partially, but not well enough. The current opening is vivid and fact-rich, but it spends too much time on institutional detail before telling the reader the bigger question and why the answer matters. The paper does eventually get there, but the opening is still a bit “France/EPCI/Loi NOTRe” rather than “a key test of a major political-economy claim.”

The first two paragraphs should more explicitly do three things:
1. state the broad claim being tested,
2. explain why this reform is an unusually clean and important test,
3. preview the surprising substantive result: no backlash.

### The pitch the paper should have

A strong version of the opening would say something like:

> A central claim in modern political economy is that moving power away from local, familiar institutions and into larger, more distant governing bodies fuels populist backlash. Yet credible evidence on this mechanism is surprisingly scarce, because most institutional reorganizations are either voluntary, gradual, or bundled with fiscal and political changes.
>
> This paper studies one of the largest forced local-governance consolidations in Europe: France’s 2015 Loi NOTRe, which reassigned about 17,000 communes into larger intercommunal bodies. I show that this dramatic increase in administrative scale had essentially no effect on far-right voting. The result suggests an important boundary condition for the “democratic distance” hypothesis: administrative consolidation alone, when local identity and core local political offices remain intact, may not be enough to generate populist backlash.

That is the paper’s real hook. It should lead with that.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### Contribution in one sentence

The paper provides evidence that a large, forced increase in the scale of subnational governance in France did not increase support for the far right, thereby placing an important boundary on the idea that institutional consolidation mechanically fuels populist backlash.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?

Not yet clearly enough. The paper gestures at municipal-merger studies and broad populism papers, but the differentiation remains a bit schematic. Right now the contribution risks sounding like: “another paper connecting a local institutional reform to electoral outcomes.”

What it needs is sharper differentiation along the exact margin of novelty:
- not municipal mergers, but intercommunal consolidation;
- not voluntary cooperation, but forced reassignment;
- not turnout or civic participation, but far-right vote share;
- not a small regional setting, but a national, large-scale restructuring;
- not a positive treatment effect, but a disciplined null that speaks to a prominent mechanism.

That package is potentially distinctive. But the introduction needs to make the novelty feel conceptual, not just institutional.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or filling a gap in a LITERATURE?

Mostly literature-gap framing, with some world framing. It should be pushed much more toward the world.

The strong world question is:
**Does increasing the distance of governance from citizens actually trigger populist backlash?**

The weaker literature-gap version is:
**There is no causal paper on forced intercommunal mergers and populist voting.**

The first is an AER-style question; the second is not.

### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what’s new?

At present, maybe only vaguely: “It’s a DiD on French local-government reform and far-right voting, with a null result.” That is not enough.

The colleague should instead be able to say:
“Interesting paper: it tests a big idea in political economy—that larger, more distant governance breeds populism—using France’s forced intercommunal reform, and finds that administrative consolidation by itself did not move RN support.”

That is a memorable contribution. The introduction is close, but not there yet.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Several ways, strategically:

1. **Make the outcome broader than RN vote share alone.**  
   If the theory is democratic distance, the paper should ideally speak not just to far-right voting but to political engagement more broadly:
   - turnout,
   - protest voting beyond RN,
   - incumbent punishment,
   - local trust if available,
   - abstention/blank ballots.
   
   Right now the paper asks a big question but tests it on one narrow electoral outlet.

2. **Make mechanism/boundary conditions central.**  
   The most promising way to enlarge the paper is not more specification variants but sharper interpretation:
   - consolidation without identity loss,
   - administrative versus political centralization,
   - visible versus invisible institutional change.
   
   The paper already hints at this. It should become the point.

3. **Use stronger comparison framing.**  
   Contrast this reform explicitly with settings where backlash did occur:
   - refugee placement,
   - trade shocks,
   - municipal dissolutions,
   - EU governance.
   
   Then the paper becomes about *when* governance changes matter politically, not just whether one French reform did.

4. **If possible, exploit visibility/intensity in a substantively richer way.**  
   A bigger paper would show that backlash is absent on average but more likely where reforms were more salient:
   - larger jumps in population/governance scale,
   - more service competencies affected,
   - longer physical/administrative distance to the new center,
   - places with stronger preexisting local identity or anti-central-state sentiment.
   
   Even if these heterogeneities are modest, they help turn a null into a theory paper.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

### Closest neighbors

The paper’s nearest neighbors are probably from three literatures:

1. **Municipal mergers and political participation**
   - Lassen and Serritzlew (2006)
   - Koch and Rochat / related Scandinavian merger papers
   - Lapointe (2023) or adjacent recent municipal-consolidation work

2. **Political economy of populism and anti-system voting**
   - Rodrik (on globalization/governance and populism)
   - Autor et al. (trade shocks and political extremism)
   - Dustmann et al. (local shocks, trust, and political reaction)

3. **Jurisdiction size / democratic distance / local governance**
   - Dahl and Tufte-type foundational work
   - Alesina-Spolaore adjacent theory on size of jurisdictions
   - Modern local public finance / decentralization papers on administrative scale and representation

A fourth conversation, not yet developed enough in the paper, is:
4. **Null results with theoretical bite in political economy**
   - Cantoni et al. type examples where nulls meaningfully constrain influential narratives

### How should the paper position itself relative to those neighbors?

Mostly **build on and qualify**, not attack.

The right posture is:
- relative to municipal-merger papers: “we study a different margin of institutional scale—one that changes governance structure without abolishing municipalities”;
- relative to populism papers: “we isolate a governance-distance channel that is often theorized but rarely cleanly tested”;
- relative to democratic-distance theory: “we identify an important boundary condition rather than rejecting the theory wholesale.”

The paper should not overclaim “the democratic distance hypothesis is wrong.” Its evidence is better suited to: “administrative consolidation alone is insufficient.”

### Is the paper positioned too narrowly or too broadly?

Oddly, both.

- **Too narrowly** in institutional exposition: lots of France/EPCI detail early on.
- **Too broadly** in some of the big claims: it gestures to European governance reform in general, maybe more than the evidence can carry.

The sweet spot is:
**This is a test of one specific mechanism in the political economy of populism: whether increased administrative scale and indirect local representation, absent municipal dissolution, generate backlash.**

That is broad enough to matter and narrow enough to be credible.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?

It likely needs deeper engagement with:
- **fiscal federalism/decentralization** literature;
- **jurisdiction size and representation** literature;
- **state capacity versus local accountability** literature;
- **political salience / visibility of institutions** literature;
- possibly **French local politics** scholarship on intercommunalities and indirect democracy.

The paper currently cites a few broad populism papers and a few merger papers, but the conceptual bridge—administrative scale versus perceived political control—needs more intellectual scaffolding.

### What fields should it be speaking to?

At minimum:
- political economy,
- public economics / local public finance,
- comparative politics of decentralization,
- public administration / state reform.

The current draft is mainly speaking to applied political economy. It should also speak to economists who care about the design of subnational government.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?

Almost, but not quite. Right now the conversation is “does this French reform affect RN vote share?” That is too case-specific.

The more impactful conversation is:
**When do changes in the scale of governance alter mass politics?**

That is the right conversation.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

There is a widely held belief that moving governance farther from citizens undermines democratic connection and can fuel anti-establishment politics. Governments across Europe regularly consolidate jurisdictions for efficiency, but we know little about whether this political cost is real.

### Tension

The theory predicts backlash, but most evidence is indirect or bundled with many other shocks. France’s Loi NOTRe offers a rare setting where governance was forcibly consolidated on a massive scale without abolishing communes themselves. That makes it a sharp test of whether administrative “distance” alone is politically combustible.

### Resolution

It wasn’t: far-right vote share did not measurably rise in treated communes, despite the scale of the reform.

### Implications

The implication is not that institutions do not matter, but that a commonly invoked mechanism has narrower scope than often implied. Populist backlash may require visible loss of local identity, direct service disruption, fiscal pain, or a clear political antagonist—not merely reassignment to a larger intercommunal body.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the raw ingredients, but the arc is not fully disciplined. There is still some sense of “collection of estimands around a null.” The paper knows its result, but it has not fully earned the larger meaning of that result.

The main problem is that the story toggles between:
- a paper about a French reform,
- a paper about populism,
- a paper about null results.

These need to be unified.

### What story should it be telling?

The paper should tell this story:

> Many scholars and policymakers worry that enlarging the scale of governance fuels populist backlash. France’s forced intercommunal consolidation is an unusually clean test because it sharply increased administrative scale while preserving municipalities. The finding of no electoral backlash reveals a boundary condition: citizens do not punish all forms of institutional centralization equally. Administrative consolidation appears politically inert when it leaves local identity and visible local democratic offices intact.

That is a coherent narrative. It turns the null into a substantive claim about political salience and institutional design.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?

I would lead with:
**France forcibly reassigned roughly 17,000 communes into larger intercommunal governments, eliminating 39% of those bodies overnight—and it did not increase far-right voting.**

That is a strong dinner-party fact.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

Initially, they would lean in. The reform scale is striking, and the result cuts against a fashionable intuition. But they would only keep leaning in if the speaker quickly explains why this is not just an obscure French administrative episode.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Most likely:
- “Why not?”
- Or: “Does that mean people don’t notice these reforms?”
- Or: “Is this because communes kept their mayors and identity?”

That tells you where the paper’s real value lies: interpretation and boundary conditions, not the coefficient itself.

### If the paper’s findings are null or modest: is the null itself interesting?

Yes, conditionally. This null is potentially interesting because:
- the treatment is large-scale and compulsory,
- the theory being tested is influential,
- the estimate is substantively tight,
- the null helps distinguish between “governance distance” and “salient identity loss.”

But the paper needs to make that case more sharply. Right now it sometimes sounds defensive—“this null is informative, not empty.” That line is understandable, but the stronger move is not to defend the null; it is to explain what the null teaches.

It should not read like a failed attempt to find an effect. It should read like a successful test that rejected an overgeneralized mechanism.
