This paper asks a simple, historically important question: when U.S. states first introduced minimum wage laws in the 1910s—and applied them only to women—did those laws actually change women’s labor market trajectories? ### What the first two paragraphs should say instead

America’s first minimum wage laws were not general labor standards; they were gender-specific wage floors aimed at women in manufacturing, retail, and service work. They sat at the center of Progressive Era battles over labor regulation, contract freedom, and women’s place in the labor market, and critics warned that they would either protect vulnerable women or price them out of employment.

Yet we still do not know whether these laws actually changed women’s economic lives. Using newly linked census records that follow 1.6 million women from 1910 to 1920, this paper shows that the first wage floors left little detectable imprint on women’s labor force attachment, sectoral attachment, or occupational mobility. The broader lesson is not simply about minimum wages; it is about state capacity: early labor regulation can be politically transformative and legally consequential while remaining economically inert on the ground.

That is the pitch. It is sharper, more world-facing, and more AER-relevant.
