## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
The paper asks whether the first minimum wage laws in US history (1912–1920)—which applied exclusively to women—impacted their long-run labor market participation and mobility. Using a massive new 1.6-million-person linked census panel and a triple-difference design, it finds a "precise zero," suggesting these landmark policies were functionally invisible in the lives of the women they targeted.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this very clearly. The title and the "shield vs. sword" metaphor in the first paragraph set the stakes perfectly. The author successfully transitions from a niche historical policy to a fundamental question about the efficacy of regulation.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**The Contribution:** The paper provides the first individual-level panel evidence on the impact of early US minimum wages, overturning the assumption that these laws had significant (positive or negative) effects on labor market trajectories.

*   **Differentiation:** It is well-differentiated. While Seltzer and Fishback (2020) cover the institutional history and Goldin (1990) looks at aggregates, this paper’s "edge" is the 1.6M-person panel.
*   **World vs. Literature:** It frames itself as answering a question about the world (the impact of first-gen regulation), though it leans heavily on "filling a gap" in the historical record.
*   **The "So What":** A smart economist would say: "It’s a high-powered null result that proves early 20th-century minimum wages were toothless."
*   **To make it bigger:** The paper needs to bridge the gap between "the laws were weak" and "why does this matter for modern theory?" Is this a story about state capacity, or about the elasticity of labor supply in the 1910s?

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
The paper sits at the intersection of **Labor Economics** (Card & Krueger consensus) and **Economic History** (Abramitzky/Boustan census linking).

*   **Closest Neighbors:** Seltzer & Fishback (2020) on Progressive Era MWs; Goldin (1990) on the gender gap; Cengiz et al. (2019) on modern disemployment effects.
*   **Strategy:** It currently builds on the history and synthesizes it with modern labor methods.
*   **Missing Conversations:** The paper could engage more with the **State Capacity** literature. Why were these laws invisible? Was it lack of enforcement (Acemoglu/Johnson style) or employer evasion? Connecting to the literature on "Law and Development" in low-enforcement environments would broaden the audience.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC
*   **Setup:** Progressive reformers pass the first wage floors to protect women.
*   **Tension:** Economists have debated for a century if this helped (raised wages) or hurt (pushed women out).
*   **Resolution:** Neither. The laws were so weakly enforced they didn't exist in the data.
*   **Implications:** A law on the books is not a law in the market.

**Evaluation:** The arc is clean but a bit "flat" because the resolution is a null. To satisfy an AER reader, the "Implications" section needs to be much meatier. It shouldn't just be "it didn't work"; it should be "here is what we learn about the transition from informal to regulated labor markets."

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
At a dinner party, I’d lead with: *"We linked 1.6 million women in the 1910 census and found that the first 'landmark' minimum wage laws in America basically didn't exist in the real world."*

Economists would lean in, then immediately ask: *"Was it because the wages were too low or because they just didn't enforce it?"* The paper’s current answer ("it was invisible") is a bit of a letdown. To pass the test, the paper needs to prove *why*—was it the "Advisory" nature of the commissions, or did employers simply ignore them?

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
*   **Front-load the "Bite":** Page 4 mentions that Goldin (1990) found 25% of women earned below the minimum. This is crucial. If the law was "binding" on paper but "null" in result, that’s the story.
*   **Appendix:** Some of the sample-split robustness (white vs. non-white) is vital and could be moved into the main narrative to discuss who the law failed most.
*   **Conclusion:** Needs to move away from summarizing and toward the "Modern Minimum Wage" debate mentioned on page 10.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The gap between this and a "Top 5" is **Ambition.** Right now, it’s a very competent evaluation of an old policy. To be AER-level, it needs to be a paper about **the nature of enforcement and the limits of the state.**

**Single most impactful advice:** Don't just report the null; explain the **mechanism of the failure.** Use the variation in "Enforcement Strength" (Advisory vs. Mandatory) more aggressively. If even the "Mandatory" states (Table 3, Panel D) show a null (+0.0111), that is a profound statement about the inability of the early American state to regulate the "Sweated" industries. Focus on the *failure of the state* rather than just the *absence of a result*.
