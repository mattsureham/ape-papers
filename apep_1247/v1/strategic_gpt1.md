## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether a uniform increase in federal student aid can change the racial composition of colleges, even when the policy is formally race-neutral. Using the 2009 ARRA expansion of the Pell Grant, it argues that colleges with more Pell-eligible students—disproportionately colleges serving Black students—experienced a relative increase, or at least a stabilization, in Black enrollment share.

Why should a busy economist care? Because the broader claim is not really about Pell per se; it is about how universal transfer programs can have systematically unequal effects across places and institutions when eligible populations are unevenly distributed. That is potentially important and general-interest.

**Does the paper articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Not quite. The introduction starts with descriptive arithmetic and quickly moves into the policy details and the Bartik design. The idea is there, but the paper does not immediately tell the reader the big question in world terms: *Can race-neutral federal aid reshape racial composition through institutional exposure?* It also overstates the resolution early relative to what the results can bear; by the time we learn the main effect is modest and largely about arresting a decline, the paper’s initially bold framing has outrun the evidence.

**What the first two paragraphs should say instead:**

> Federal policies are often described as race-neutral because eligibility does not explicitly depend on race. But when eligible populations are unevenly distributed across institutions, a uniform national policy can still have racially differentiated effects on where people enroll, work, or receive services. Higher education is a natural place to study this mechanism because low-income students are concentrated in particular colleges, and Pell Grant eligibility is strongly correlated with race.
>
> This paper studies whether the 2009 ARRA increase in the maximum Pell Grant changed racial enrollment patterns at U.S. community colleges. I show that colleges with higher pre-ARRA Pell exposure experienced a relative increase—or more precisely, a stabilization—in Black enrollment share after the policy change. The broader implication is that universal aid programs can operate as a “composition lever”: they reshape institutional racial composition not through targeting, but through the uneven geography of eligibility.

That is the pitch the paper should have.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:**  
The paper argues that the 2009 ARRA Pell expansion modestly stabilized Black enrollment share at community colleges with high pre-policy Pell exposure, illustrating how a uniform federal transfer can generate racially differentiated institutional composition effects.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Before this paper, we know Pell affects college affordability, and we know disadvantaged students are concentrated in certain sectors and institutions, especially community colleges. We also know racial composition in higher education reflects both access and sorting.

### Tension
A uniform federal aid increase is formally race-neutral, but because Pell eligibility is unevenly distributed, it may have racially differentiated effects across institutions. The tension is whether such a policy meaningfully changes institutional racial composition—or whether all the action is just aggregate enrollment or recession noise.

### Resolution
The paper finds that community colleges with higher Pell exposure saw a modest increase, or more carefully a stabilization, in Black enrollment share after ARRA. But the resolution is muddied by the fact that the dynamic pattern looks less like a discrete break and more like an arrest of an existing decline.

### Implications
The implication is that universal aid policies may not be neutral in incidence: they can reshape the racial composition of institutions through exposure, even without explicit targeting. That matters for how economists think about redistribution, access, and institutional inequality.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
**Serviceable, but not clean.** At present it feels somewhat like a collection of regressions wrapped in an ambitious phrase (“composition lever”). The core story is there, but the paper has not fully decided whether its main message is:

1. Pell raised Black enrollment share at high-exposure colleges; or
2. Pell prevented a decline in Black enrollment share; or
3. national aid shocks can generate compositional racial effects in institutions.

These are related but not identical stories. The paper tries to tell all three, and as a result the narrative wobbles.

### What story should it be telling?
The strongest story is:

**Uniform aid changes institutional composition because institutions differ in exposure to eligible students. In the ARRA Pell episode, that mechanism appears in community colleges as a modest stabilization of Black enrollment share at high-exposure institutions.**

That story is narrower than the current rhetoric, but cleaner and more believable. The current draft reaches too quickly for “general framework” and “de facto racial composition tool.” Those phrases are stronger than the actual narrative support.

I would say:

That is the most honest and potentially interesting statement.

### Would people lean in?
Some would, but this is not yet a lean-in-for-the-whole-table result. The current version does not produce a startling stylized fact. The reaction from many economists would be: “Interesting, but is that just recession-era compositional change?” And because the paper itself foregrounds that concern, the result lands as suggestive rather than decisive.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Likely one of these:
- “Did Black enrollment actually rise, or just fall less than expected?”
- “Is this institutional sorting or aggregate attendance?”
- “Why community colleges specifically—did four-years lose students?”
- “Is the main takeaway about race, poverty, or recession exposure?”

Those are revealing. They show the paper’s current headline fact is not fully self-sufficient; it requires immediate clarification.

### If the findings are modest, is the modesty itself interesting?
Potentially yes, but the paper needs to make that case better. There is real value in learning that a very large national grant increase had only modest compositional effects, and mostly by stabilization rather than expansion. That tempers strong claims about what financial aid can accomplish on the extensive margin. But the current draft presents modesty almost apologetically, after initially signaling a much larger and cleaner finding. Better to embrace the nuanced result upfront.

Right now it sometimes reads like: “Here is a big mechanism, though the main causal evidence is limited.” That is not a satisfying top-journal equilibrium. Either make the evidence support the big mechanism, or scale the claim to the evidence.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

Honest answer: **this is not primarily a polish problem. It is a framing-and-ambition problem, with some novelty risk.**

### What is the main gap?

#### 1. Framing problem
The paper has a better idea than introduction. “Composition lever” is the right instinct, but the paper has not yet earned the breadth of that concept. It needs a tighter, world-facing question and less self-conscious emphasis on design novelty.

#### 2. Scope problem
The evidence base is too narrow relative to the claim. One outcome—Black enrollment share at community colleges in one policy episode—cannot comfortably carry a general theory of universal transfers reshaping racial composition.

#### 3. Novelty problem
If stripped of the rhetoric, the paper may sound like “another shift-share / DiD application to a federal aid shock.” The author needs to make much clearer why institutional composition is a first-order object, not a side margin.

#### 4. Ambition problem
The paper is competent and self-aware, but safe in the wrong places and over-ambitious in others. It is safe empirically in sticking to a narrow institutional outcome; it is over-ambitious conceptually in generalizing to all transfer programs.

### What would excite the top 10 people in this field?
A version that convincingly answers a bigger question, such as:

- **How do large federal aid expansions change the distribution of students across institutions, by race and income?**
- **Do universal aid policies reduce or reinforce stratification in higher education?**
- **What is the incidence of student aid across institutions, not just students?**

That likely requires broader outcomes, more direct sorting margins, or a stronger link between composition changes and institutional consequences.

### Single most impactful piece of advice
**Rebuild the paper around institutional sorting and composition as the core economic question, and make the “stabilization of Black enrollment share at high-exposure colleges” the honest headline rather than a secondary nuance.**

That one change would force the introduction, literature review, results ordering, and conclusion into a more coherent and credible shape.
