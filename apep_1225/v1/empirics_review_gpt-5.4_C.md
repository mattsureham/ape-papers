1. **Idea Fidelity**

The paper only partially executes the original idea in the manifest, and in one important sense it abandons it. The manifest proposed a study of the 2019 S60 relaxation using police.uk data, a two-cohort staggered DiD, and—crucially—an IV using GDELT “competing news” to identify the effect of S60 usage intensity on knife crime, with spatial displacement as the main contribution. The paper keeps the two-cohort staggered DiD and the displacement test, but it drops the IV entirely. That is a major deviation, because the manifest explicitly framed the IV as the way to address endogenous policing intensity. Instead, the paper ends up estimating the reduced-form effect of eligibility for relaxed S60 rules, and then argues that the weak first stage makes the IV moot. That is a coherent empirical pivot, but it should be acknowledged as such.

There are also several fidelity problems in the implementation details. The manifest proposed 43 forces and a pre-period beginning in January 2017; the paper uses 42 forces and starts in January 2018, losing a substantial amount of pre-treatment information that is especially valuable given the obvious concern that the seven pilot forces were selected because they were atypical. The outcome in the manifest was knife crime; the paper uses “possession of weapons” and “violence and sexual offences,” which is understandable given police.uk categories, but the connection to knife crime needs to be defended much more carefully. Finally, the manifest highlighted displacement as the key substantive question, whereas the paper ultimately becomes a “null first stage / null reduced form” paper. That can still be publishable, but it is no longer the paper originally advertised.

2. **Summary**

This paper studies the April 2019 pilot relaxation of Section 60 stop-and-search powers in seven police forces, later extended nationwide in August 2019, using a staggered difference-in-differences design on force-month data. The main finding is that the reform did not measurably increase S60 search activity at the force-month level, and correspondingly the paper finds no detectable effects on weapons possession, violent crime, or neighboring-force displacement.

3. **Essential Points**

1. **The first-stage result is not yet credible as presented because the measurement and scaling are internally inconsistent.**  
   The paper alternates between “per 1,000” and “per 100,000” population for S60 search rates. Table 1 reports pre-treatment mean S60 stops of 0.48 or 2.04/0.17 per 100,000, while the text claims a Callaway-Sant’Anna ATT of -7.8 “per 1,000 population,” which is impossible on the scale of the reported means. Either the units are wrong, the table labels are wrong, or the estimates are misreported. This is not cosmetic: the paper’s headline claim is a weak first stage, and right now the magnitude is not interpretable. The authors must reconcile the units, define exactly whether the outcome is S60 searches or S60 authorizations, and show the raw series by force and over time.

2. **The main crime results are not causally persuasive because the key outcome fails pre-trends and the treated forces are highly non-comparable.**  
   The paper openly states that the pre-trends test for weapons possession fails with \(p=0.000\). That should substantially weaken any causal claim about the weapons outcome. More broadly, the seven treated forces are precisely the largest urban forces with the highest knife-crime salience; with only 15 pre months, force fixed effects and linear trends are not enough reassurance. If the most policy-relevant outcome fails parallel trends, the paper cannot present “null crime effects” as a clean causal conclusion. At best, the paper has a more credible statement about implementation failure in the first stage.

3. **Inference and design are too thin for the displacement claim, and the economic meaning is underdeveloped throughout.**  
   The displacement test is effectively a small-sample difference-in-differences among 35 later-treated forces over one four-month window. That is a very low-powered design, especially given likely serial correlation and measurement noise in force-level “weapons possession” counts. The paper should be much more cautious: it does not show “no displacement,” only no detectable displacement at this aggregation level. More generally, the paper needs to translate estimates into economically meaningful margins—e.g., how many additional S60 searches would the confidence interval permit, and what knife-crime reduction would that imply under plausible elasticities? Right now the paper tends to equate statistical insignificance with policy irrelevance too quickly.

4. **Suggestions**

The paper is closest to being convincing as a reduced-form paper on policy implementation failure, not as a causal paper on knife crime or displacement. I would lean into that reframing. The title and abstract already move in this direction, but the results section still occasionally overstates what can be learned about crime. If the reform truly did not change S60 use, then the clean claim is about institutional non-response to administrative relaxation. That is interesting. But to make that claim persuasive, the first-stage evidence must become much more transparent.

First, fix the data architecture and reporting. There are too many inconsistencies for an AER: Insights-style paper: 42 versus 43 forces; 35 versus 36 in Cohort 2; per-1,000 versus per-100,000; “authorizations” versus “searches”; “bicycle theft” in the text but “other theft” in the table; the abstract says 43 forces while the body says 42. These are red flags because they make the reader worry that the codebook and estimands are not yet stable. I strongly recommend a short data appendix table listing exactly which forces are included, which are excluded, why, and the number of force-month observations. Also provide one figure with aggregate monthly S60 searches for pilot and non-pilot forces, both in levels and per-capita rates. If the first stage is truly absent, that figure should immediately show it.

Second, extend the pre-period back to 2017, as proposed in the manifest, unless there is a compelling data-quality reason not to. With only 15 pre-treatment months, you are inviting skepticism, especially because 2018 was exactly the period of heightened knife-crime salience and likely mean reversion in the pilot forces. If police.uk data are available from 2017, there is little justification for not using them. More pre-period data will not solve selection into treatment, but it will allow a more informative event-study and better assessment of whether the pilots were already on different trajectories.

Third, the paper needs better outcome validation. “Possession of weapons” is not the same as knife crime, and “violence and sexual offences” is far too broad. If narrower Home Office series on knife-enabled offences or knife-enabled robbery/assault exist at the force-month level, even for a shorter period, use them as corroborating outcomes. At minimum, provide evidence that “possession of weapons” comoves with known knife-crime patterns across forces. Otherwise the paper risks answering a different question from the one policymakers care about.

Fourth, clarify what the first stage actually is. Section 60 is an authorization power, but your data appear to contain realized stops, not authorizations. That distinction matters. A reform could increase authorizations without increasing recorded searches, or vice versa. If authorizations are unavailable, say so explicitly and stop referring to “authorization volumes.” Then interpret the result as no increase in realized S60 search activity. Also consider showing extensive-margin effects: probability a force-month has any S60 search, not just the rate. Given many zeroes, a binary or count model summary may be more revealing than a linear rate alone.

Fifth, the magnitudes need benchmarking. For example: if the pre-period mean S60 rate is 0.48 per 100,000 per month, then an estimate of -7.8 on that scale is nonsensical. If instead it is per million, say that. More substantively, tell the reader how many searches per month this implies in the Metropolitan Police versus a smaller force. The policy relevance hinges on whether the confidence interval rules out meaningfully large increases in S60 use. Right now I cannot tell whether the paper shows “no increase” or “too noisy to know.”

Sixth, treat the weapons-possession reduced-form result as secondary unless you can do more to address the failed pre-trends. Useful options include: matching or synthetic-control style weighting of the seven pilot forces against later-treated forces; re-estimating on a narrower sample of more comparable urban forces; presenting cohort-specific event studies rather than only aggregated ATTs; and running Rambachan-Roth style sensitivity to non-parallel pretrends if feasible. If those exercises still show instability, the paper should say plainly that the crime outcomes are inconclusive while the first stage is the robust result.

Seventh, the displacement design should be toned down and strengthened where possible. I would avoid the phrase “spatial displacement tests yield null results” without qualification. At force-month granularity, neighboring-force displacement is a blunt object: crime could shift across boroughs, transport corridors, or neighborhood boundaries within the same force. A better presentation would be: “we find no evidence of displacement across police-force boundaries.” If possible, exploit the street-level police.uk geography more directly, for example comparing areas near pilot-force borders with interior areas, or at least using local-area aggregates around boundaries. That would align better with the original idea and would be much more compelling than force-level adjacency alone.

Eighth, if the IV from the manifest has been abandoned, either remove discussion suggesting that endogeneity of policing intensity has been solved, or add a brief section explaining why the IV was not pursued. A simple statement would suffice: because the reduced-form policy shock produced no detectable increase in S60 searches, an IV for search intensity is not informative in this setting. That would honestly connect the final paper to the original design.

Finally, the paper would benefit from a more restrained and empirically grounded interpretation section. “Institutional inertia” is plausible, but it is not identified. Other explanations include measurement error in police.uk stop-search data, substitution toward Section 1 PACE rather than Section 60, concentration of use in small hotspots that do not move force totals, or anticipatory behavior before April 2019. You can propose institutional inertia as a leading interpretation, but not as the established mechanism. A helpful addition would be to examine total weapon-related stop-searches under all legal authorities. If Section 60 did not rise but weapon searches under Section 1 did, that would substantially change the interpretation.

In short: there is a potentially publishable paper here, but it is narrower than advertised. If the authors clean up the data inconsistencies, reinforce the first-stage evidence, back away from strong causal claims on weapons crime, and present the contribution as one about implementation failure of a highly salient policing reform, the paper will be much stronger.
