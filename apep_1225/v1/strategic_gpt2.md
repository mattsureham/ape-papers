This paper asks a simple policy question with unusually high political salience: when England and Wales relaxed one of the broadest stop-and-search powers in 2019, did police actually use it more, and did that reduce weapons crime? The headline answer is not “stop-and-search doesn’t work,” but something narrower and potentially more interesting: the reform barely changed realized Section 60 search activity at all, so the policy failed at implementation before it could be tested on crime.

That is a potentially publishable idea, but in its current form the paper is still caught between three different papers: a stop-and-search deterrence paper, a spatial displacement paper, and an implementation/organizational inertia paper. The first is weakened by the admitted pre-trend problems; the second is undercut by the absent first stage; the third is the live one, but it is still more asserted than truly framed and developed. Right now the paper has a result. It does not yet have the strongest story for AER.

## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

### What is this paper about?

This paper studies a nationally important policing reform in England and Wales that made suspicionless Section 60 stop-and-search easier to authorize. It finds that the reform did not meaningfully increase actual Section 60 searches, and correspondingly finds no detectable change in weapons crime or violence—suggesting that formal legal discretion is not enough to change frontline police behavior.

Why should a busy economist care? Because the paper’s real lesson is broader than stop-and-search: policy reforms often fail not because the treatment has no effect, but because the state never meaningfully delivers the treatment. That is a state capacity / implementation gap question, not just a policing question.

### Does the paper articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Not quite. The introduction opens as if this is mainly a knife-crime / stop-and-search effectiveness paper, then pivots into a displacement paper, and only by paragraph 4 does the actual central result emerge: the first stage is weak. The paper is making the reader walk through the authors’ original research design rather than the paper they ended up with.

The first two paragraphs should instead say, upfront:

1. governments often respond to crises by loosening formal constraints on frontline officials;
2. whether this matters depends on whether frontline behavior actually changes;
3. here, a major UK policing reform did not.

### The pitch the paper should have

In 2019, England and Wales relaxed Section 60 stop-and-search powers in response to a knife-crime panic, with the explicit goal of increasing searches and reducing violence. Using the staggered rollout across police forces, we show that the reform did not materially increase realized Section 60 searches; correspondingly, we find no detectable reduction in weapons or violent crime. The paper’s main lesson is about policy implementation: changing legal authority on paper need not change state behavior on the ground.

That is the pitch. The displacement material can appear later as a secondary implication, not as the motivating frame.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper’s contribution is to show that a prominent policing reform failed primarily because lowering authorization thresholds did not induce greater frontline use of the power, highlighting an implementation failure rather than a deterrence failure.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest papers?

Only partially.

The paper does differentiate itself from the London stop-and-search literature by using a national policy change and explicit not-yet-treated controls. But it does not yet clearly separate itself from at least two nearby literatures:

- “stop-and-search and crime” papers, where it risks sounding like another null effect paper;
- “policy implementation / street-level bureaucracy” papers, where it invokes the language but does not really deliver direct evidence on mechanism.

It needs to decide which literature it is mainly contributing to. Right now it says “three literatures,” which is often a sign that the contribution is not sharply prioritized.

### World question or literature gap?

At its best, it is framed as a world question: when governments expand coercive policing authority, does that actually change policing behavior? That is strong.

Too often, though, the paper lapses into literature-gap framing: no prior paper has done DiD on the 2019 S60 relaxation; no prior English paper has tested displacement. Those are fine supporting points, but they are not why AER readers should care.

The paper should lead with the world question. “Does relaxing legal constraints on police produce more policing?” is bigger than “there is no DiD paper on the 2019 UK reform.”

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?

Currently, maybe not cleanly. They might say:

> “It’s a DiD paper on a UK stop-and-search reform, and the main results are null.”

That is not enough.

You want them to say:

> “It shows that the reform didn’t really change police behavior, so the policy failed at the implementation margin; the lesson is that expanding formal discretion can have little effect when frontline agents don’t take it up.”

That’s the memorable version. The introduction needs to produce that sentence.

### What would make the contribution bigger?

Most importantly: make the implementation claim less inferential and more substantive.

Right now “institutional inertia” is the interpretive gloss placed on a weak first stage. That may be right, but it is not yet shown. To make the paper bigger, I would want one of the following:

- **Direct implementation outcomes:** authorizations, officer deployment, use of S60 by rank/command, internal force guidance, supervisory approvals, or within-force heterogeneity in take-up.
- **Mechanism on organizational behavior:** evidence that forces with prior heavy reliance on S60, different leadership, or different racial scrutiny responded differently.
- **A broader conceptual comparison:** place this alongside other reforms where legal authority expanded but behavior did not—policing, welfare administration, inspections, prosecutors, immigration enforcement, etc.
- **A stronger outcome framing:** less “did crime fall?” and more “when does loosening formal rules change state action?” That makes the paper about public economics/political economy/state capacity, not just British policing.

The single biggest way to enlarge the contribution is to turn “weak first stage” from a nuisance finding into the object of study.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

### Closest neighbors

Based on the paper’s own citations and field, the closest neighbors appear to be:

- McCandless et al. / HMICFRS-style official evaluations of stop-and-search intensity

- MacDonald, Fagan, and Geller (2016) on proactive policing / stop-and-frisk in the US

On the implementation side, the conceptual neighbor is:
- Lipsky (1980) and the street-level bureaucracy literature

And if the paper wants to matter to economists beyond crime:
- papers on implementation, state capacity, bureaucratic discretion, and compliance with centrally mandated reforms.

### How should it position itself relative to those neighbors?

Mostly **build on and redirect**, not attack.

- Relative to the stop-and-search/crime papers: “We complement prior work on whether searches deter crime by showing that even a high-profile reform may not generate the intended increase in searches.”
- Relative to displacement/hot spots papers: “We bring spatial spillovers into the design, but the absent first stage means the more important lesson is about treatment delivery.”
- Relative to implementation papers: “This is a policing case study of a general implementation problem: legal authority is not self-executing.”

The paper currently spends too much energy distinguishing itself on method and not enough on the shift in substantive question.

### Is it positioned too narrowly or too broadly?

Oddly, both.

- **Too narrowly** in the empirical setup: Section 60, England and Wales, 2019, 42 forces.
- **Too broadly** in rhetorical ambition: deterrence, displacement, implementation, racial equity, police patrol intensity, and public administration all appear, but none is fully developed.

For AER, it needs one broad frame and one empirical setting. The broad frame should be implementation of coercive state power.

### What literature does it seem unaware of?

It feels underconnected to economics literatures on:

- state capacity and bureaucratic implementation;
- principal-agent problems in public organizations;
- compliance with top-down reforms;
- law-on-the-books versus law-in-action;
- public sector personnel and organizational behavior.

It also might usefully speak to literatures on:
- policing as state presence;
- discretion under public scrutiny;
- politically salient but weakly implemented reforms.

The paper currently knows criminology and policing reasonably well, but it does not yet sound like it is in conversation with mainstream economics on how institutions translate central policy into frontline action.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?

Not yet. The highest-impact conversation is not “does stop-and-search reduce knife crime?” That is interesting, but the paper’s own design limitations and null first stage mean it cannot be the strongest possible version of that conversation.

The better conversation is:

> Why do high-profile legal reforms often fail to alter frontline state behavior?

That is more surprising, broader, and more generalizable.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

The world before the paper: knife crime is politically salient; governments often respond by expanding police powers; advocates assume easier authorization leads to more searches and less violence.

### Tension

But there are two tensions here, and the paper currently muddles them.

1. Substantive tension the paper wants to start with: deterrence vs displacement.
2. Actual tension generated by the findings: reforms on paper may not change behavior in practice.

The second is the real tension. The first is mostly a design motivation that the results render secondary.

### Resolution

The policy relaxation did not materially increase realized S60 searches. With little or no first-stage response, crime effects and displacement effects are also null.

### Implications

The important implication is not simply “S60 doesn’t reduce crime.” It is: when evaluating public policy, especially policing, one must first ask whether the state actually changed what it does. Reforming formal authority without changing organizational behavior may accomplish very little.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

Not fully. It reads like a collection of results from a project whose original story was overtaken by the data.

You can feel the original plan:
- test deterrence;
- test displacement;
- maybe use an IV;
- then discover the first stage is weak.

The paper has partially adapted, but not completely. It still carries around the scaffolding of the earlier story.

### What story should it be telling?

The story should be:

1. Politicians often equate expanded formal powers with stronger enforcement.
2. That assumption is testable.
3. In this case it fails: easing legal thresholds did not change realized enforcement.
4. Therefore the relevant margin is implementation inside the organization, not legal design alone.
5. This has implications for how economists evaluate reforms and think about state capacity.

That is a coherent setup-tension-resolution-implications arc.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?

I would lead with:

> England and Wales loosened one of its broadest stop-and-search powers in the middle of a knife-crime panic—and actual use of that power did not rise.

That is the hook.

Not “we estimate a Callaway-Sant’Anna ATT of -7.8.”  
Not “there is no displacement.”  
The striking fact is that the treatment barely happened in behavior.

### Would people lean in?

Some would. Especially economists interested in state capacity, public organizations, policing, or implementation. But only if the paper frames this as a failure of policy transmission, not just another null crime paper.

If framed as “we found null crime effects for stop-and-search,” many readers will indeed reach for their phones.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Immediately:

> Why didn’t police use the power more?

That is the central audience question. And right now the paper cannot answer it beyond speculation: officer culture, supervisory caution, community concerns, etc.

That is exactly why the current version feels one step short of top-journal impact. It has a provocative reduced-form fact, but limited evidence on the mechanism that would make the fact travel.

### If the findings are null or modest, is the null interesting?

Yes—but only the first-stage null is genuinely interesting. The crime nulls are not the main event, especially given the pre-trend issues on weapons possession.

The paper should make a much more forceful case that learning “the reform did not change delivered enforcement” is valuable in its own right. It should stop apologizing for the null and instead elevate it:

- policymakers believed legal relaxation would increase policing;
- that prediction fails;
- this is informative about implementation constraints.

Done right, this is not a failed experiment. It is a revealing implementation result.

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

### What would make it read better?

A lot, mostly by simplification and reprioritization.

#### 1. Rewrite the introduction around the first-stage fact
The first stage should appear in paragraph 1 or 2, not paragraph 4 after a long excursion through knife-crime politics and displacement.

#### 2. Cut or demote the displacement setup
The “displacement illusion” concept is overdeveloped relative to what the paper can actually say. Because the first stage is null, the displacement exercise becomes a secondary corroborating check. Right now the paper invests too much conceptual capital in an issue the results never really reach.

This material should be condensed substantially in the introduction and conceptual framework.

#### 3. Shorten the institutional background
The legal details are useful, but too much of the front half reads like a policy brief. For AER, the background should be brisk and instrumentally tied to the economics question.

#### 4. Move some caveats earlier and some technical detail later
The paper is admirably candid about failed pre-trends for weapons possession. Good. But the introduction currently spends a lot of space narrating estimates and caveats in detail. That belongs later. The intro should sell the question and preview the core finding.

#### 5. Put the strongest figure/table immediately up front
If there is an event-study figure or a simple time series showing no rise in S60 searches in pilot forces relative to controls, that belongs in the main text very early. A picture of “the treatment didn’t move behavior” will do more work than pages of estimator discussion.

#### 6. Relegate some robustness and estimator exposition
The paper spends too much visible space defending design choices for results it itself says are not the main causal contribution. The mechanics of Callaway-Sant’Anna and robustness can be tighter in the main text.

#### 7. Tighten the conclusion
The conclusion mostly summarizes. It should end with the general lesson: economists should distinguish policy adoption from policy delivery, particularly when reforms operate through frontline discretion.

### Is the paper front-loaded with the good stuff?

No. The reader has to wade through too much before getting to the real punchline.

### Are there results buried that should be in the main text?

Potentially yes, if there is heterogeneity in take-up or any descriptive evidence on which forces did or did not respond. That would help the implementation story much more than yet another robustness table.

### Is the conclusion adding value?

Some, but not enough. It should generalize harder and more convincingly.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

Be honest: in current form, this is not yet an AER paper.

### What is the gap?

Mostly a combination of **framing problem**, **scope problem**, and **ambition problem**.

#### Framing problem
The paper’s best idea—formal authority changes did not alter frontline enforcement—is not the organizing frame. The paper still acts like a stop-and-search deterrence paper with some implementation observations attached.

#### Scope problem
If the paper wants to claim “institutional inertia,” it needs more than a weak first stage. It needs richer evidence on the implementation mechanism, or a broader comparative framing that makes the reduced-form fact itself more general.

#### Ambition problem
The paper is competent and self-aware, but it is still a fairly safe policy evaluation of one UK reform. To excite the top people in the field, it needs to make a larger claim about the state, discretion, and policy delivery.

### Is it a novelty problem?

Somewhat. “Null effects of stop-and-search on crime” is not novel enough. “A salient coercive reform failed because the implementing organization did not change behavior” is much more novel and broader.

### Single most impactful advice

If the author can only change one thing:

**Rebuild the paper around implementation failure as the primary question, and bring direct evidence or much sharper analysis on why legal relaxation did not change police behavior.**

Everything else is second-order. If the paper cannot do that, it will remain a respectable null-result policing paper rather than a broadly interesting economics paper.
