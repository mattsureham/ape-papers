### 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether the world's largest subsidized student mobility program, Erasmus+, inadvertently undermines the European Union's multi-billion euro effort to reduce regional inequality (Cohesion Policy). Using a shift-share IV at a granular geographic level, the author finds that every student who leaves a peripheral region via Erasmus reduces that region’s share of young professionals, effectively turning a "study abroad" program into a "subsidized brain drain."

**Evaluation:** The paper actually does a decent job in the first two paragraphs of setting the stakes. However, it leans a bit too heavily on the "paradox" of EU policy. 
**The pitch it *should* have:** "Governments worldwide spend billions to keep talent in lagging regions while simultaneously subsidizing programs that make it easier for that talent to leave. Does subsidized mobility necessarily come at the cost of spatial equity? This paper uses the scale of the Erasmus program to show that for the poorest regions, the 'brain drain' effect is not just a theoretical risk—it accounts for a substantial portion of the human capital gap Cohesion Policy is trying to close."

---

### 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**The Contribution:** The paper provides the first regional-level equilibrium evidence that subsidized student mobility causes a net reduction in the human capital stock of peripheral regions.

- **Differentiated?** Yes. Most Erasmus papers (Parey & Waldinger, etc.) look at individual returns. This shifts the focus to the *sending region*.
- **Question about the World or Literature?** It frames it as a policy trilemma (World), which is its greatest strength.
- **Explainable?** A smart economist would say: "It's the first paper to show that the EU is paying for people to leave the very places it's trying to fix."
- **Bigger Contribution?** To make this truly "Big AER," the author needs a sharper **mechanism** check. Is it that students find jobs abroad (pull), or that they never intended to return (selection), or that their home labor market is too weak to absorb their new skills (mismatch)?

---

### 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

- **Neighbors:** Parey & Waldinger (2011) [Individual returns]; Docquier & Rapoport (2012) [Brain drain]; Rodríguez-Pose (2018) [Place-based policy].
- **Positioning:** It should **synthesize** the individual-level labor literature with the regional growth literature. Currently, it positions itself as a "critique" of Erasmus. It would be stronger if it framed itself as a paper on the **unintended consequences of mobility subsidies in the presence of agglomeration externalities.**
- **Missing Literature:** It needs to speak to the **Economic Geography** crowd (Krugman/Venables). The "escalator" effect mentioned is good, but the formal tension between "mobility as a right" and "spatial equity" is a deep philosophical debate in urban econ that would elevate the paper.

---

### 4. NARRATIVE ARC

- **Setup:** The EU pours money into poor regions (Cohesion) and student travel (Erasmus).
- **Tension:** These two goals may be diametrically opposed. If talent leaves, the return on Cohesion investment (infrastructure, etc.) drops to zero.
- **Resolution:** In poor regions, the drain is real and significant (1.01 pp drop in tertiary share per unit of outflow). In rich regions, there is no effect.
- **Implications:** Subsidizing mobility without return incentives is a transfer from the periphery to the core.

**Evaluation:** The narrative arc is very strong. It’s not just a collection of results; it’s a focused investigation of a specific policy conflict.

---

### 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

- **The Dinner Party Fact:** "The EU spends nearly 400 billion euros to fix poor regions, and then spends 26 billion euros to help the smartest people in those regions leave."
- **Reaction:** People will lean in. This is a "common sense" paradox that hasn't been quantified.
- **Follow-up:** "Does it happen because they find spouses abroad, or because the jobs back home just aren't there?" (This reinforces the need for mechanism work).

---

### 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

- **Front-loading:** The "Go/No-Go" diagnostic (Section 4.4) is brilliant for a top-tier paper. It anticipates the referee's first objection (within-country variation) and kills it before it can take root. Keep this prominent.
- **Appendices:** The NUTS2 long-difference vs. Panel results are a bit muddled in the text. I would move the secondary specifications to a single "Robustness/Dynamics" section to keep the NUTS3 results as the primary anchor.

---

### 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The gap is currently **Empirical Weight vs. Causal Finality.** The author is very honest: the Randomization Inference p-value is 0.44. In a top-5 journal, that is a "death star" result. It suggests the instrument is driven by the *shares* (who you were already connected to) rather than the *shocks* (exogenous changes in destination).

**Single Biggest Advice:** To save the causal claim, the author needs to find a **policy shock**—a sudden change in Erasmus grant levels for certain countries or a change in university bilateral agreements—to supplement the Bartik. If the "suggestive" nature isn't hardened, it might be a better fit for *AEJ: Economic Policy* or *JUE*. 

**To make it AER:** Connect the human capital loss to **actual regional outcomes**. Does a higher Erasmus drain lead to lower local tax revenue or lower firm entry in those regions? Connecting the "drain" to the "pain" makes the story undeniable.
