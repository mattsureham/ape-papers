## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether Erasmus — one of Europe’s most celebrated mobility programs — has an unintended distributional consequence: helping talented students leave poorer regions and not come back. Using regional data across Europe, it argues that student mobility may raise individual opportunity while widening the gap in human capital between the European core and periphery.

A busy economist should care because this is a sharp policy tradeoff, not just another program evaluation: the same EU state can subsidize mobility with one hand and subsidize regional cohesion with the other. If true, that is a first-order insight about how integration policy reshapes economic geography.

**Does the paper articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Almost, but not cleanly enough. The paper has the ingredients of a strong pitch, but the opening quickly slides into literature review, method, and caveats. The central idea is stronger than the current exposition: **a flagship education policy may be systematically redistributing talent away from lagging regions.** That should be the unmistakable lead.

**What the first two paragraphs should say instead:**

> Erasmus is typically framed as a success story in European integration: it expands opportunity, builds skills, and fosters cross-border ties. But mobility policies can have uneven spatial consequences. When students from poorer regions study in richer ones, they may acquire not just education but also information, networks, and a pathway to permanent relocation.  
>   
> This paper asks whether Erasmus inadvertently contributes to regional divergence in Europe by accelerating the movement of educated young people from peripheral to core regions. That question matters because it connects two central concerns in modern economics — mobility and spatial inequality — and because the answer bears directly on the coherence of EU policy: a program designed to build Europe may simultaneously undermine the cohesion of Europe’s left-behind regions.

That is the pitch the paper should own.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution in one sentence:**  
The paper’s contribution is to shift the evaluation of Erasmus from individual returns to regional incidence, arguing that student mobility may contribute to human-capital divergence across European regions, especially between poorer peripheral areas and richer cores.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?
Only partly. The paper identifies adjacent literatures, but the differentiation is too diffuse and too long. The introduction lists many papers and several literatures, but the reader does not come away with a crisp sense of exactly what is new relative to the closest neighbors.

The relevant differentiation is:

1. **Relative to Erasmus papers**: existing work studies participants’ wages, employability, identity, and later international mobility; this paper studies **origin-region consequences**.
2. **Relative to brain-drain papers**: much of that literature studies migration **across countries**; this paper studies brain drain generated by a **publicly subsidized educational mobility channel within an integrated economic area**.
3. **Relative to spatial inequality papers**: those document divergence; this paper tries to link divergence to a specific policy mechanism.

That triad is strong. It should be stated in about six lines, not six paragraphs.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or filling a gap in a LITERATURE?
The paper does both, but it drifts too often into “here is a gap in literature X, Y, Z.” The stronger frame is about the world:

- Do mobility programs create a tradeoff between individual opportunity and place-based equity?
- Can a celebrated integration policy accelerate regional divergence?

That is much stronger than “the Erasmus evaluation literature has not looked at regional outcomes.”

### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what’s new?
Right now, maybe, but only after some work. They would probably say:  
“It's a shift-share paper on Erasmus outflows and regional tertiary attainment.”

That is not enough. The desired reaction is:  
“It says Erasmus may function as a publicly subsidized brain-drain mechanism from Europe’s periphery to its core.”

That is memorable. That is a claim about the world.

### What would make this contribution bigger?
Several possibilities:

- **Better outcome framing**: the tertiary share is serviceable, but not maximally compelling. - **Stronger comparison**: compare Erasmus to other forms of mobility or to regions with similar youth out-migration absent Erasmus. That would help distinguish “another migration paper” from “a paper about a specific policy architecture.”
- **Sharper policy framing**: make this a paper about the internal contradiction of EU policy — mobility versus cohesion. That is bigger than “brain drain in Europe.”
- **Cleaner scope**: right now the paper is trying to be substantive, policy-relevant, and methodologically self-conscious. The biggest version is substantive: **How should governments think about mobility policies when talent is spatially sticky?**

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Europe celebrates Erasmus as a mobility and integration success. At the same time, Europe is increasingly worried about divergence between prosperous metropolitan/core regions and poorer peripheral regions.

### Tension
Mobility may benefit participants while harming origin places. A program designed to expand opportunity may also make it easier for skilled young people to leave lagging regions permanently. The unresolved question is whether Erasmus is part of the solution to European integration — or part of the problem of regional divergence.

### Resolution
The paper finds that regions with greater Erasmus outflows experience lower tertiary attainment among young adults, with stronger effects in poorer and net-sending regions. The results are consistent with the interpretation that Erasmus can act as a stepping stone from peripheral regions to thicker labor markets in the core.

### Implications
The implication is not “Erasmus is bad.” The implication is that **mobility policy has incidence across places**, and governments may need to redesign such programs if they care about both individual opportunity and regional cohesion.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
It has one, but it is buried under too much exposition and too much methodological self-consciousness. The paper repeatedly interrupts its own story to explain the instrument, preview robustness, discuss randomization inference, and recite significance levels. That is referee-facing writing, not editor-facing or reader-facing writing.

At present it reads somewhat like **a collection of competent empirical results looking for a top-journal narrative**. The story it should tell is simpler and better:

> Erasmus is a mobility subsidy. Mobility subsidies can reallocate talent. In an unequal geography, talent reallocation may worsen divergence. This paper asks whether that happened in Europe.

That is the narrative spine. Everything else should serve it.

The interesting claim here is not “we definitively estimate the causal effect.” It is:

> “There is meaningful evidence that a celebrated mobility program may have regressive spatial incidence.”

That is already interesting. The paper should defend why that is valuable to know.
