## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
This paper examines how national bans on fur farming in Europe—which prohibit production but not consumption—affect the global geography of the industry. Using a staggered difference-in-differences design, it asks whether these "moral" regulations successfully reduce production or simply drive it to "animal welfare havens" like Poland. It finds that while domestic exports collapse by 74% in banning countries, production surges in neighboring non-banning jurisdictions, mirroring the "pollution haven" effect but with the added clarity of absolute legal prohibitions.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this reasonably well, but the second paragraph spends too much time on the Copeland-Taylor framework and not enough on the "So What?" for a general economist.

**The pitch the paper should have:** 
"When a country bans the production of a globally traded good for moral reasons but continues to allow its sale, does it actually reduce the activity or just export the 'sin'? This paper uses the wave of European fur farming bans to provide a uniquely clean test of regulatory leakage. Because these bans are absolute prohibitions rather than marginal tax increases, they offer a 'laboratory' setting to see how mobile capital responds to the total legal evaporation of a domestic industry."

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**One-sentence contribution:** The paper provides the first empirical evidence of an "animal welfare haven" effect, demonstrating that absolute production bans in integrated markets lead to near-perfect trade diversion to neighboring jurisdictions with weaker regulations.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It differentiates itself from the environmental literature (Levinson, Greenstone, etc.) by highlighting the *binary* nature of the regulation. Most "pollution haven" papers struggle with the "marginality" of carbon taxes or scrubbers; this is an "on/off" switch.
*   **Question vs. Literature:** It is currently framed as "filling a gap" in the animal welfare/pollution haven literature. It needs to be framed as a question about **Globalized Morality**: Can a nation have a clean conscience without changing global outcomes?
*   **What would make it bigger?** To reach the AER, the paper needs to move beyond "Poland's exports went up." It needs to quantify the **Net Global Effect**. If 10 million minks were saved in the UK/Netherlands, but 11 million more were raised in Poland/China, the "moral" regulation has a negative global return. This welfare calculation is what's missing.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
*   **Closest Neighbors:** Copeland & Taylor (2004) on trade/environment; Greenstone (2002) on regulatory costs; Vogel (1995) on the "California Effect."
*   **Positioning:** It should **attack** the traditional environmental literature for its "mushy" identification. The author has a "killer" institutional detail: a total ban. Use that to show why previous estimates of "leakage" might be understated because they dealt with mere price changes.
*   **Unexpected Connection:** The paper should speak to the **Political Economy of Symbolic Legislation.** Why do politicians pass bans that they *know* will lead to leakage? Is it about actual animal welfare, or "washing" the domestic image for voters?

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC
*   **Setup:** Europe moves toward higher moral standards for animal treatment.
*   **Tension:** The "Mink Coat" remains legal. If I can't farm it here, I'll farm it ten miles across the border.
*   **Resolution:** Production doesn't die; it migrates to Poland.
*   **Implications:** Unilateral moral regulation in a trade-integrated world is largely performative unless accompanied by import bans.

**Evaluation:** The arc is clear but currently feels like a technical "check the box" exercise. The "Tension" needs to be more dramatic—highlighting the irony of the "moral" consumer in Amsterdam wearing a coat from a Polish farm that only exists *because* of the Dutch ban.

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
At a dinner party: "When the Netherlands banned fur to save animals, Poland's exports tripled almost overnight. The animals didn't get saved; they just moved to a country with even fewer inspectors."
*   **Reaction:** People lean in. It's a "cynical but true" economic fact that challenges the efficacy of popular social movements.
*   **Follow-up:** "Why didn't they ban the *imports* too?" (This leads to a great discussion on WTO rules and trade law).

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
*   **The Appendix:** Move the "Denmark COVID cull" results into the main text as a "Natural Experiment" section. It’s a massive exogenous shock that provides a beautiful comparison to the "legislated" bans.
*   **The Results:** Table 3 (Poland vs. Finland) is more interesting than Table 2 (the DiD). I want to see a map. A heat map of European mink production in 2000 vs. 2022 would be the "Money Shot" of the paper.
*   **Conclusion:** Needs to be less about "limitations" and more about the "EU 2027" policy implication mentioned on page 8.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The paper is currently a very solid *Journal of Public Economics* or *Journal of Environmental Economics and Management* paper. To make it **AER**, it needs to become a paper about **The Limits of Unilateralism**.

**Single most impactful piece of advice:** 
Construct a "Global Welfare Leakage" index: calculate the total number of animals "saved" in banning countries versus the "extra" animals raised in haven countries, and show how the **intensity of leakage** is a function of trade costs (distance to the border).
