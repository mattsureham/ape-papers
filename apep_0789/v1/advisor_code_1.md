**Summary**

This paper studies the price effects of post-Fukushima nuclear reactor restarts across Japan’s regional electricity markets. Exploiting staggered NRA approval dates and the limited inter-regional transmission imposed by the 50Hz/60Hz split, the author estimates a Callaway–Sant’Anna staggered DiD using 2.5 million JEPX half-hourly observations and finds that restarts lower wholesale prices by 0.60 ¥/kWh—only a fraction of the shutdown-induced price spike, implying a “restart deficit.” The paper documents supportive robustness checks (e.g., donut specifications, dosage, heterogeneity) and suggests that renewable deployment and LNG contracting during the hiatus created an asymmetric merit-order response.

**Essential Points**

1. **Credibility of the Parallel Trends Assumption & Treatment Timing Exogeneity:** The paper relies critically on the claim that NRA restart timing reflects reactor-specific safety progress and is exogenous to contemporaneous regional demand or pricing trends. However, the discussion of potential confounders—such as region-specific economic shocks, demand shifts, or regulatory coordination that might influence both restart pacing and prices—is limited. The pre-trend evidence is summarized in a table of means but not formally tested using event-study plots or placebo coefficients. The author should provide rigorous pre-treatment event-study estimates for each cohort and, if feasible, add placebo treatments (e.g., pseudo-restarts in the control group) to demonstrate the absence of pre-trends. If the assumption is tenuous, the causal interpretation of the restart deficit weakens substantially.

2. **Treatment Definition & Dosage Variation Over Time:** The analysis treats the onset of any restart in a region as a binary switch to treatment, despite reactors restarting at different times within the same region (e.g., Kansai’s restarts spanning 2016–2023). This binary approach may misstate exposure—especially where restarts were intermittent or overlapped with substantial capacity changes. The dosage specification in Table 4 is a step in the right direction, but more systematic modeling of intensity (e.g., cumulative MW online) within the staggered framework is necessary. A missing mass of untreated capacity in early cohorts could bias the ATT downward. The authors should re-estimate main results with a continuous treatment variable that tracks megawatts online and carefully reconcile it with the assumptions of the Callaway–Sant’Anna estimator (e.g., weighting by cumulative capacity) to ensure the estimated effect truly reflects the marginal impact of returning capacity.

3. **Inference Given Limited Number of Clusters & Permutation Approach:** While the paper implements wild-cluster bootstrap and exact randomization inference, several concerns remain unresolved. The RI procedure reassigns the observed timing to different regions but evaluates the TWFE coefficient, not the heterogeneity-robust ATT. Since the TWFE estimator is known to be biased under treatment effect heterogeneity, the permutation distribution may not accurately reflect the null distribution of the preferred estimator, undermining the validity of the reported $p$-values. The authors should either (a) derive an RI distribution for the CS estimator (perhaps via a simulation-based approximation) or (b) clarify why the TWFE-based RI still provides valid inference for the ATT. Additionally, the wild-cluster bootstrap implementation should report whether the bootstrap procedure respects the staggered nature of treatment (e.g., by re-sampling residuals at the region level) and whether results are sensitive to alternative distributions (Rademacher, Webb 6-point). Otherwise, the robustness of the statistical significance claim is uncertain.

If these essential issues cannot be satisfactorily resolved, especially the validity of the parallel trends assumption, the paper’s main causal claims would be undermined sufficiently to recommend rejection. If addressed, the contribution becomes compelling.

**Suggestions**

1. **Strengthen Pre-Trend Documentation:** Beyond reporting similar past means, include visual event-study graphs showing group-time ATTs estimated via the Callaway–Sant’Anna approach (or the Sun–Abraham method) for each cohort. Overlaying confidence intervals would clarify whether any treatment groups were already trending differently before their restarts. Additionally, presenting placebo tests—such as artificially imposing a “pre-restart” treatment that occurs before any reactor came back online—would further bolster confidence in the parallel trends assumption.

2. **Model Treatment Intensity More Explicitly:** The heterogeneity and dosage discussion suggest that pricing effects depend on the quantity of restarted capacity and the timing of each reactor within a region. Consider re-estimating the main specification with a treatment variable equal to cumulative gigawatts of online nuclear capacity (per region-month), normalized by peak demand so that coefficients are interpretable as price impacts per GW. Alternatively, switch to a continuous DiD framework (e.g., “Borusyak et al. 2021” or “de Chaisemartin & D’Haultfœuille 2024” estimators) that flexibly handles varying intensities. This would also allow you to explore nonlinear dosage effects (e.g., diminishing returns) and reconcile the binary/TWFE and dosage results.

3. **Address Structural Changes & Demand-Side Covariates:** While time fixed effects absorb national reforms, regional demand could evolve heterogeneously and correlate with both restart schedules and prices. For example, Kyushu and Kansai differ in industrial composition and economic growth post-2015. Including region-specific linear trends, controlling for region-month demand proxies (manufacturing output, temperature-adjusted load), or interacting observable covariates with time effects would help. If demand data are unavailable, argue more explicitly why demand shocks are unlikely to coincide with restart timing (e.g., resume of high-growth industries in treated regions after restarts).

4. **Clarify Spillover Argument & Role of Frequency Barrier:** The 1.2GW interconnection capacity is cited as limiting spillovers, but the paper should quantify what share of regional demand this represents and explain how flows are allocated during high-price differentials. Providing descriptive evidence—e.g., scatterplot of price differences between treated and control regions before and after restarts—would demonstrate that the barrier effectively segments the markets. If hourly transmission data exist, supply them to show that flows remained constrained even during stressed periods. If not, justify why the limited capacity plausibly preserves the no-spillover assumption.

5. **Expand Mechanism Discussion with Demand-Side Evidence:** The restart deficit is attributed to solar deployment, LNG contracting, and higher nuclear marginal costs. While plausible, these remain speculative. Use available data to illustrate the mechanism: show solar generation or capacity trends (national or regional) before and after restarts to demonstrate structural shifts. Alternatively, examine the price effect’s persistence over time—does the coefficient decline as LNG contracts roll off? Another idea is to test whether restarts change the distribution of marginal generator types (if dispatch data are accessible) or whether fossil fuel price pass-through differs across regions post-restart. Any direct evidence would elevate the mechanism section from conjecture to substantiated reasoning.

6. **Robustness to Alternate Control Groups:** The choice of four never-treated regions is natural, but consider alternative control sets (e.g., excluding regions that are most similar in demand patterns or performing synthetic control-style weighting to mimic treated-region pre-trends). Conduct a leave-one-out analysis where each treated region is dropped in turn to evaluate whether a single region drives the aggregate result. This would also address concerns that Kyushu (with the earliest and largest restart) dominates the estimate.

7. **Transparency on Data & Replication:** Provide summary code snippets or pseudo-code in the appendix demonstrating how region-month aggregates were constructed from half-hourly data, how treatment dates were assigned, and how the RI/treatment permutation was implemented. If some data sources are proprietary (e.g., JEPX), clarify access instructions. Given the autonomous generation claim, ensuring reproducibility (e.g., by sharing scripts and cleaned datasets) would greatly increase credibility.

8. **Discuss Alternative Outcomes:** Although the focus is on wholesale prices, consider adding auxiliary outcomes if data permit—such as spot volumes traded, capacity factors of fossil plants, or even emissions (the manifest mentioned prefectural emissions). If nuclear restarts reduced fossil-fuel generation, this would strengthen the case for a broader decarbonization effect. At minimum, signal how emissions data could be incorporated in future work.

9. **Refine the Narrative Around the “Restart Deficit”:** The concept is compelling, but the paper should be cautious about treating the restart effect as a universal constant. Rephrase sections that assert the deficit is “permanent” or “systemic” unless supported by dynamic analysis. Consider incorporating a dynamic specification (e.g., allowing treatment effects to vary with years since restart) to show whether the dividend grows, shrinks, or stabilizes over time, which would clarify whether the deficit reflects short-term hysteresis or enduring structural change.

Implementing these suggestions would strengthen the paper’s identification, clarify its empirical strategy, and enhance its contribution to energy-economics literatures.
