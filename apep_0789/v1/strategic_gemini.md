## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
The paper examines the "reversibility" of energy shocks by looking at Japan’s nuclear restart program (2015–2024). It asks: if shutting down 30% of a nation's power capacity doubled prices, does bringing it back online restore the old price equilibrium? It finds a significant "restart deficit," where the price reduction is an order of magnitude smaller than the initial shutdown penalty, suggesting that energy systems undergo irreversible structural changes (like solar expansion and contract stickiness) during a hiatus.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this well in the second and third paragraphs. However, the first paragraph is a bit slow on the "history." The pitch should be sharper.
**The Pitch the Paper Should Have:** 
"When a major energy source is removed, the system adapts. Does it 'un-adapt' when that source returns? Exploiting the staggered, exogenous restart of Japan’s nuclear fleet, I show that the resulting price relief is 90% smaller than the original shutdown cost, revealing a fundamental path-dependency in energy transitions."

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
The paper identifies a "restart deficit"—a form of energy market hysteresis where the price benefits of a technology are permanently diminished once the grid has adapted to its absence.

- **Differentiation:** It moves beyond the "cost of Fukushima" literature (e.g., Neidell et al. 2021) by focusing on the *asymmetry* of the return. It’s not just about the cost of losing nuclear; it's about why gaining it back doesn't help as much as expected.
- **Question vs. Literature:** It frames itself as answering a question about the world (reversibility of transitions), which is strong.
- **Smart Economist Test:** A reader would explain this as the "Hysteresis Paper." It’s more than a DiD—it’s a test of the "Merit Order" stability.
- **Making it bigger:** To be truly AER-sized, the paper needs more granular data on the *mechanisms*. The "Peak vs. Off-Peak" test is a clever proxy, but the contribution would be massive if it used hourly generation mix data to prove *which* fuel was displaced (or wasn't).

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
The paper sits at the intersection of Environmental/Energy Econ and Applied Micro (DiD methodology).

- **Neighbors:** Neidell et al. (2021) on Fukushima; Davis & Hausman (2016) on nuclear closures; Fabra & Reguant (2014) on merit orders.
- **Positioning:** It builds on Neidell but essentially complicates the narrative. It should "attack" the implicit assumption in policy modeling that marginal effects are symmetric.
- **Niche/Broad:** It’s currently a bit "Energy-Specific." To reach the AER, it needs to lean harder into the "Path Dependence/Hysteresis" literature (Arthur 1989), framing this as a general lesson for any large-scale industrial reversal (e.g., bringing manufacturing back to the US).

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC
- **Setup:** Japan loses 30% of its power; prices skyrocket.
- **Tension:** 14 reactors come back, but the "energy bill" remains high. Why?
- **Resolution:** The grid has changed. Solar and LNG contracts have "locked in" a new cost structure.
- **Implications:** "Green" transitions create a one-way street; you can't go home again to cheap baseload once the merit order has shifted.

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong. It’s a "mystery" story (Where did the price relief go?).

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The paper is highly competent but currently feels like a "very good" *AEJ: Policy* or *JPE: Micro* paper. To reach AER:
1.  **Ambition:** It needs to bridge the gap between "Japan's electricity prices" and "The General Theory of Industrial Hysteresis."
2.  **Scope:** It needs to move from "Price" (the symptom) to "Quantity/Mix" (the cause). If the author can show the actual displacement of MWh by fuel type, the "Restart Deficit" moves from a statistical observation to a structural proof.

**Single Biggest Advice:** Shift the framing from "Japan's nuclear restarts" to "The Irreversibility of Energy Transitions," and use the Japanese data as the definitive proof of a general economic phenomenon.
