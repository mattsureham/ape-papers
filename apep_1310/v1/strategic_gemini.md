**EDITORIAL MEMO: STRATEGIC POSITIONING**
**To:** Board of Editors, American Economic Review
**From:** Editor
**Re:** "The Compression Shock: Sector-Level Employment Responses to an Extreme Minimum Wage Increase"

---

### 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
This paper examines the employment effects of a "mega-shock" to the minimum wage—a 39% increase in Lithuania—contrasted against its Baltic neighbors. It asks whether the empirical consensus of "small-to-zero" disemployment effects is merely a function of the modest (5-15%) increases typically studied, or if it holds even when the wage floor is pushed to 75% of a sector's average wage.

**Evaluation:** The paper does a decent job in the first two paragraphs, but it leads too much with the specific institutional details of Vilnius dishwashers. For the AER, the pitch needs to be about the *shape of the labor demand curve* and the *bounds of the monopsony model*.

**The pitch the paper should have:**
"Does the labor market's resilience to minimum wage hikes break down when the shock is extreme? While the literature has converged on near-zero employment elasticities for marginal adjustments, we lack evidence on whether this resilience is a structural feature of labor markets or a domain-specific result of small shocks. This paper exploits a unique 39% 'compression shock' in Lithuania to identify the employment response at the intensive binding frontier, finding that extreme hikes generate substantial disemployment that moderate reforms do not."

---

### 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**One-sentence contribution:** The paper identifies a "domain restriction" for the minimum wage consensus, showing that extreme hikes (Kaitz index > 0.5) trigger significant employment losses that are absent in moderate regimes.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It differentiates itself from *Cengiz et al. (2019)* by focusing on the tail of the shock distribution and from *Harasztosi and Lindner (2019)* by using a cross-national sector-level DiD rather than firm-level data.
*   **World vs. Literature:** It is currently framed as "answering a question about the world" (what happens in Lithuania), but to reach the AER, it needs to be framed as a "stress test of a fundamental economic consensus."
*   **The "So What":** A smart economist would say, "It's a DiD on an outlier event." To make the contribution bigger, the author needs to move beyond the *fact* of the decline and more deeply into the *mechanism* (e.g., why did Hungary absorb a 57% shock while Lithuania didn't? Is it the tax reform confound mentioned on page 4?).

---

### 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
The paper sits at the intersection of the "New Minimum Wage Research" (Card/Krueger, Dube) and the newer literature on "Large Shocks" (Jardim et al. 2022 on Seattle; Harasztosi/Lindner 2019 on Hungary).

*   **Closest Neighbors:** *Dustmann et al. (2022)* on Germany; *Cengiz et al. (2019)* on U.S. gaps; *Zilio & Lozej (2023)*—which is a major threat as it uses the same shock with better (micro) data.
*   **Strategy:** The paper should **attack** the universality of the "small effects" consensus. It needs to position itself as the "Upper Bound" study. 
*   **Missing Conversations:** The paper is silent on the *Search and Matching* literature. If firms can't absorb the cost, do they exit, or do they stop hiring? The "dynamic adjustment" mention (Sorkin, Meer & West) is a good start but needs more weight.

---

### 4. NARRATIVE ARC
*   **Setup:** The "39% hike" is a fantastic hook.
*   **Tension:** The tension is high—existing theory (monopsony) suggests firms should absorb this, but common sense (and standard competitive models) suggests a 39% spike must break something.
*   **Resolution:** It finds a massive structural break in 2019.
*   **Implications:** The "domain restriction" is the key takeaway.

**Evaluation:** The narrative is currently "Here is a big shock, and I found a big effect." To be AER-caliber, it needs a "But why?" arc. Why does Lithuania look different from the Hungarian case? Is it because of the simultaneous tax reform? The paper treats the tax reform as a "caveat," but in the AER, that *is* the story.

---

### 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
*   **The Lead Fact:** "Lithuania raised the minimum wage by 39% and employment in hotels fell by 60% relative to the trend."
*   **The Reaction:** People lean in. That is a "scary" number for policymakers.
*   **The Follow-up:** "Wait, didn't they also change the tax code at the exact same time?" (Page 4). If the author can't disentangle the "grossing up" of wages from the cost shock, the dinner party ends early.

---

### 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
*   **Move to Appendix:** The "Binary" treatment (Table 3, Col 2) is weak and adds little.
*   **Front-load:** The event study (Table 4) is the most important visual. It shows a worrying pre-trend (2013-2014). This needs to be addressed in the *first* results section, not as a "threat to validity" later.
*   **Expand:** The "Wage Compression" section (page 11) is currently a "null" result. This is a disaster for the paper. If a 39% hike didn't raise average sector wages, the identification (Kaitz index) is arguably not capturing the intended shock. This needs micro-data or a much better explanation of "compositional changes."

---

### 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The distance is currently **Medium-to-Far**. The use of sector-level aggregate data (ILO STAT) is very thin for a modern AER paper, especially when *Zilio & Lozej (2023)* already exist using Lithuanian administrative micro-data.

**The single most impactful piece of advice:** 
The author must pivot from "identifying the effect" to "reconciling the conflict." Specifically: Why does this study find large negative effects when the Hungary 57% shock study (AER 2019) found firms just passed costs to consumers? Is it the Baltic labor market structure, the simultaneous 2019 tax reform, or the 2020 COVID timing?

### STRATEGIC ASSESSMENT

*   **Current framing quality:** Adequate (too institutional, needs more theory)
*   **Contribution clarity:** Somewhat fuzzy (struggles with the wage-effect null)
*   **Literature positioning:** Well-positioned (hits the right names)
*   **Narrative arc:** Serviceable
*   **AER distance:** Far (Data is too aggregate; identification is threatened by the 2019 tax reform)
*   **Single biggest improvement:** Use micro-data to prove that the 39% hike actually increased labor costs for low-wage workers specifically, rather than being masked by the "grossing-up" tax reform.
