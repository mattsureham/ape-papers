## 1. Idea Fidelity

The paper clearly pursues the core question in the manifest: whether HomeBuilder generated genuinely additional construction or mainly shifted timing. It uses the intended ABS building approvals data, emphasizes the policy-relevant “hangover” concept, and implements two of the three proposed designs: an interrupted time series and a within-location houses-versus-apartments comparison.

That said, the paper departs from the original design in ways that matter for identification. Most importantly, the manifest’s strongest cross-sectional idea was to exploit heterogeneity in the $750$K cap across **GCCSAs** (e.g., Sydney/Melbourne versus Perth/Adelaide), but the paper collapses this to **eight states** and does not really use the richer 16-GCCSA variation. That is a lost opportunity, since the price-cap mechanism is central to the paper’s causal story. Relatedly, the “DDD” is not actually a triple-difference in the sense promised in the manifest; as written, it is a two-group difference-in-differences with dwelling-type-by-state fixed effects and time fixed effects. The paper also does not use the lending data mentioned in the manifest, which could have helped validate timing and mechanism. So the paper is faithful to the main research question, but it does not fully deliver on the most compelling identification strategy originally proposed.

## 2. Summary

This paper studies whether Australia’s 2020–21 HomeBuilder grant increased new housing construction or merely pulled approvals forward. Using national interrupted time series and a house-versus-apartment panel design across states, the paper finds a sizable increase in house approvals during the program and little evidence of a post-program “hangover,” concluding that HomeBuilder generated substantial net additionality at relatively low fiscal cost per extra dwelling.

The question is important and timely, and the descriptive patterns are interesting. However, the current causal design does not yet convincingly isolate the effect of HomeBuilder from coincident pandemic-era shifts in demand composition, and several quantitative claims are stronger than the evidence currently supports.

## 3. Essential Points

1. **The main identification strategy is not yet credible enough for the paper’s causal claims.**  
   The preferred specification compares houses to apartments during the HomeBuilder period, but this requires that, absent the program, approvals for houses and apartments would have evolved similarly within states after netting out common time shocks. That seems doubtful in 2020–21. COVID plausibly caused a sharp reallocation from dense, developer-led apartments toward detached houses because of remote work, migration, investor conditions, border closures, and financing differences. Those shocks are exactly type-specific and time-varying, so they are **not** removed by month fixed effects. The paper needs to confront this directly with event-study evidence, pre-trends by dwelling type, and ideally a design that exploits the cap’s differential bite across locations rather than relying primarily on apartments as the counterfactual.

2. **The paper over-interprets the absence of a sharp post-program drop as evidence of net additionality.**  
   A weak or absent “hangover” in approvals is not sufficient to conclude the subsidy created new housing. The post-program period also saw strong macro tailwinds, unusually low interest rates, changed housing preferences, and continued supply bottlenecks. Those forces can mask displacement that would otherwise appear as below-trend approvals. More fundamentally, approvals are not completions, and timing shifts could occur with lags longer than the short post window emphasized here. The paper should substantially soften its interpretation and, if possible, estimate cumulative excess approvals relative to a defensible counterfactual over a longer horizon rather than infer additionality from the sign of a post-period trend.

3. **Several implementation and reporting issues undermine confidence in the estimates.**  
   There are internal inconsistencies in the reported magnitudes: the abstract says 47%, Table 3 reports 0.468, while the additionality table converts this to 59.7%; summary-stat means also differ between text and table. More importantly, inference with only eight state clusters is fragile; leave-one-state-out is not a substitute for appropriate small-cluster methods. The paper should use wild cluster bootstrap or randomization inference, clarify the level of aggregation and sample period, and make the estimating equations and treatment coding fully transparent. Until these issues are fixed, the cost-per-additional-dwelling calculation is premature.

## 4. Suggestions

This is a promising paper on an important policy episode, and I think it could become much stronger with a tighter design and more disciplined interpretation. My suggestions below are aimed at helping the authors get there.

First, I would re-center the empirical strategy around the **price-cap heterogeneity** rather than the houses-versus-apartments contrast. The institutional narrative throughout the paper is that HomeBuilder should have been more effective where the $750$K cap was non-binding. That is a much more persuasive source of variation than apartment approvals. The manifest itself points to GCCSAs, and I strongly encourage the authors to use the full 16-region panel. A natural design would compare house approvals in more-versus-less exposed regions before, during, and after the program, with exposure measured using pre-period house-price distributions or the pre-program share of transactions/new builds below the cap. Even if the exposure measure is imperfect, that approach is more closely tied to the policy rule and less vulnerable to the “houses and apartments are structurally different markets” critique.

Second, if the authors retain the houses-versus-apartments design, they need to present it much more cautiously and validate it much more carefully. At a minimum, show an event-study with interactions for house relative to apartment by month, normalized before June 2020. This will reveal whether the differential break begins exactly with HomeBuilder or earlier, for example around the first COVID shock. Also show pre-2020 trends by dwelling type within each state or GCCSA. If parallel trends are poor, that result should push the paper away from treating the dwelling-type comparison as the preferred specification.

Third, there is a useful way to combine the two ideas: estimate a **triple-difference that is actually a triple-difference**. For example, compare houses versus apartments in high-exposure versus low-exposure regions during HomeBuilder. That would better isolate HomeBuilder from general detached-housing demand shifts, because the latter should affect houses broadly, while the grant should affect houses more strongly where the cap is less binding. This seems especially well suited to the AER: Insights format because it sharpens the causal claim without requiring a huge amount of additional data work.

Fourth, the interrupted time series should be presented as descriptive unless the authors can do more to justify it. June 2020 is not just a policy break; it is embedded in an extraordinary macroeconomic episode. Month fixed effects and a linear trend are not enough to make ITS persuasive here. If the authors keep the ITS, I would recommend explicitly reframing it as evidence on timing and aggregate patterns rather than as a causal estimate. A more informative ITS exercise would plot cumulative excess approvals relative to pre-trend and report sensitivity to different pre-periods, alternative trends, and exclusion of the volatile early-pandemic months.

Fifth, the paper needs a more serious discussion of **timing from contracts to approvals**. The reduced-grant period appearing stronger than the full-grant period is explained as a lag, but this is currently ad hoc. This timing issue is central, because the treatment is assigned by contract date, while the outcome is approval date. The paper would benefit from a simple conceptual timing diagram and a distributed-lag/event-time specification showing the dynamic response month by month. If ABS lending or loan commitment data can proxy for purchase/build initiation, those data could be very helpful for validating when decisions were actually made.

Sixth, the current additionality calculation is too mechanical relative to the evidence. I would not multiply the preferred coefficient by the pre-period mean and immediately interpret the result as the number of genuinely additional dwellings. At best, this is an estimate of **excess approvals relative to a specific counterfactual**, and the uncertainty should be made explicit. Please provide confidence intervals for additional approvals and cost per approval, and distinguish clearly between “additional approvals,” “additional starts,” and “additional completed dwellings.” In the present version, the welfare language goes beyond what the data can support.

Seventh, the paper should exploit more institutional variation within the program. The phase-down from $25$K to $15$K and the announced expiration date are potentially informative. For example, one might examine whether there is bunching in approvals/contracts around the December 2020 and March 2021 deadlines, and whether that bunching is stronger in more exposed regions. This could speak directly to intertemporal substitution. If there is no post-deadline collapse even in high-exposure areas, that would be more compelling than the aggregate “no hangover” result.

Eighth, I recommend much clearer reporting of the data and units. The paper variously refers to states, GCCSAs, national totals, and 2018–2023 versus 2016–2025 samples. The summary table numbers do not line up cleanly with the text, and the coefficient transformations are inconsistent. For a short paper, this matters a lot. I would include one concise appendix table listing: sample period, unit of observation, exact number of observations, dwelling-type coding, treatment windows, and whether outcomes are original or seasonally adjusted. Also make clear whether “other residential” includes townhouses that may have some exposure to HomeBuilder; if so, that weakens the placebo interpretation.

Ninth, I would strengthen the inference strategy. With eight state clusters, conventional clustered standard errors are not very reliable. Wild cluster bootstrap-t is the obvious fix. If the paper moves to 16 GCCSAs, that is still not many clusters, but better; randomization inference based on treatment exposure rankings or permutation of the treatment window could also be informative. The paper should not present p-values to three decimals from conventional clustered inference as if they were definitive.

Tenth, I encourage the authors to use the **mechanism evidence** more carefully. The current conclusion invokes affordability thresholds and supply elasticity, but these are not directly shown. Some light-touch validation would help: heterogeneity by region affordability, by detached-house prevalence, by pre-period approval volatility, or by areas with more greenfield supply. If lending or first-home buyer finance approvals are available, one could also test whether owner-occupier construction finance rose disproportionately in exposed areas. That would align the empirical work more closely with the paper’s stated mechanism.

Finally, I would moderate the headline claims. “HomeBuilder created housing, not just a calendar rearrangement” is too strong for the present evidence. A more defensible claim is that the program was associated with a large rise in house approvals, and that the available evidence suggests at least some meaningful net additionality, though precise magnitudes remain uncertain because the policy coincided with major pandemic-era changes in housing demand. That is still a useful and publishable contribution if the identification is tightened.

In short, the paper asks an excellent question and has promising raw material, but the empirical design needs to align more closely with the policy’s actual margins of variation. The strongest path forward is to lean into the cap-based geographic heterogeneity, use the richer GCCSA panel, provide dynamic/event-study evidence, and substantially temper the welfare conclusions until the causal estimates are more secure.
