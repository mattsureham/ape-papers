## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether Australia’s 2020 HomeBuilder grant actually increased residential construction or simply shifted projects forward in time. That is a question economists should care about because it speaks to a central issue in fiscal policy: when temporary subsidies generate real new activity versus merely reshuffling timing, especially in housing where affordability thresholds and supply elasticities may make stimulus unusually potent.

The paper **does articulate a pitch**, and the first two paragraphs are stronger than most submissions. But they are still not quite the pitch an AER paper needs. The introduction currently oscillates between three frames: pandemic stimulus evaluation, intertemporal substitution, and housing supply policy. Those are related, but the paper has not yet chosen which is the lead story. Right now the reader gets “HomeBuilder mattered,” “temporary subsidies may create real activity,” and “housing is different,” but not a single sharp thesis.

### The first two paragraphs should say instead

A stronger opening would be:

> Temporary subsidies often raise purchases today by stealing them from tomorrow. That logic underlies classic findings on cars, investment, and tax credits, and it makes policymakers skeptical that short-lived stimulus creates much real activity. This paper asks whether housing is different.  
>  
> I study Australia’s 2020 HomeBuilder grant, a large temporary subsidy for new home construction. I show that the program substantially increased house approvals relative to apartment approvals, and—crucially—there is little evidence of the post-program slump that would indicate simple pull-forward. The central implication is that temporary subsidies can create net new activity when they help households cross discrete affordability thresholds in a sector where supply can expand.

That is the paper’s best version of itself: not “an evaluation of one Australian program,” but “a test of when temporary subsidies create activity rather than re-time it.”

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper’s contribution is to show that a temporary construction subsidy in Australia appears to have generated substantial **net additional housing approvals**, not merely intertemporal substitution, suggesting that temporary subsidies can create real activity in markets with threshold-crossing demand and elastic supply.

### Is this clearly differentiated from the closest papers?

Only partly. The paper names broad analogs—Cash for Clunkers, bonus depreciation, investment tax credits—but the differentiation is still too conceptual and not concrete enough. The paper says, in essence, “those papers find pull-forward in other markets; I find less of it in housing.” That is a plausible contribution, but the author needs to make much clearer:

1. **Why housing is an informative test case rather than just a new setting**.
2. **Why HomeBuilder is especially useful** for testing the additionality-versus-timing question.
3. **What exactly is new relative to existing housing-subsidy evidence**—which is not yet crisply laid out.

At present, a smart economist might summarize it as: “It’s a DiD/DDD paper on a housing subsidy during COVID showing a positive effect on house approvals.” That is not enough. The author wants the summary to be: “It uses HomeBuilder to show that temporary subsidies need not just shift timing; in housing they may create net new activity because they move people across ownership/buildability thresholds.”

### World question or literature-gap question?

Mostly framed as a **world question**, which is good: do temporary subsidies create activity or simply rearrange timing? That is much stronger than “there is no formal causal evaluation of HomeBuilder.” The paper should downplay the “no one has evaluated this program” angle; that is publication logic for a field journal, not AER logic.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the intro?

They could explain the broad idea, but not with enough precision. The introduction does not yet cleanly isolate the novelty. The novelty should be something like:

- a direct empirical test of net additionality for a temporary housing construction subsidy,
- in contrast to literatures where temporary subsidies mostly shift timing,
- with implications for when stimulus works.

Right now the paper risks reading as “another reduced-form policy evaluation,” because the introduction spends a lot of space listing methods and estimates rather than building the conceptual novelty.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Most importantly: **make the object of interest net creation more central and more visible**.

Specific ways to make it bigger:

- **Bring completions, starts, or downstream supply outcomes closer to the center**, if possible. Approvals are an intermediate outcome. For AER-level ambition, “approvals rose” is weaker than “the housing stock rose.”
- **Develop the threshold-crossing mechanism more explicitly.** If the contribution is that temporary subsidies create activity when they relax discrete constraints, then the paper should show where that mechanism bites most strongly: lower-price states, first-home-buyer-heavy areas, fringe suburbs, or price distributions near the cap.
- **Frame the comparison more sharply against classic intertemporal substitution episodes.** The question is not “did this subsidy work?” but “why did this temporary subsidy generate additionality when so many others do not?”
- **Emphasize general lessons for subsidy design.** New-build targeted, owner-occupier targeted, in relatively elastic markets, with caps that screen out supply-inelastic locations.

The biggest upgrade would be to move from “effect of HomeBuilder on approvals” to “conditions under which temporary subsidies create real quantity responses rather than timing shifts.”

### How should the paper position itself?

It should **build on and qualify** the temporary-subsidy literature, not just import its citations. The right move is:

- “Classic studies show temporary subsidies often re-time behavior.”
- “Housing has features that make that logic less automatic.”
- “This paper provides evidence consistent with genuine net creation in that setting.”

That is stronger than trying to “attack” the prior literature. The prior literature is not wrong; this paper’s ambition should be to define boundary conditions.

### Is the current positioning too narrow or too broad?

Oddly, both.

- **Too narrow** because it can read as a country-specific Australian policy evaluation.
- **Too broad** because it gestures at macro stimulus, housing supply, intertemporal substitution, affordability thresholds, and pandemic reallocation all at once.

The paper needs to choose a primary audience. The best audience is: **applied micro/macro economists interested in stimulus design and housing supply responses**. Everything else should be subordinate.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?

It seems under-engaged with at least three relevant conversations:

1. **Housing demand subsidies versus supply responses**  
   There is a large literature on mortgage subsidies, first-time buyer credits, and whether housing policies capitalize into prices versus quantities. The paper mentions Glaeser-type arguments but not in a sufficiently grounded way.

2. **Pandemic housing reallocation / remote work / suburbanization**  
   This matters not just as a caveat but as a rival narrative. The author mentions it in discussion, but if that is the main competing explanation, it belongs much earlier in the framing.

3. **Policy design / incidence of housing subsidies**  
   If the claim is “construction-targeted subsidies in elastic markets can work,” then the paper should speak to incidence and supply elasticity more directly.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?

Almost. But the highest-value conversation is not “Australian housing policy” and not even “pandemic policy evaluation.” It is:

> **When do temporary subsidies create net new real activity instead of merely pulling activity forward?**

That is the conversation that gives this paper a chance at broad interest.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Economists are skeptical of temporary subsidies because many simply shift purchases across time. Meanwhile, policymakers use short-lived subsidies precisely because they are easy to deploy in recessions. Housing is a major sector, but it is unclear whether temporary construction subsidies create new supply or just accelerate planned projects.

### Tension

Housing could go either way. On one hand, temporary subsidies should induce intertemporal substitution. On the other, housing decisions involve lumpy constraints, financing hurdles, and potentially elastic supply at the urban fringe, so a subsidy might trigger projects that otherwise would not happen at all. HomeBuilder offers a natural test.

### Resolution

The paper finds a sizeable increase in house approvals relative to apartments during the program and little evidence of a post-program collapse. The author interprets this as evidence of genuine additionality rather than simple pull-forward.

### Implications

Temporary subsidies may work better in housing than in markets like autos or capital goods, especially when they target new construction and operate where supply can respond. That has implications for both macro stabilization and housing policy design.

### Does the paper have a clear arc?

**Serviceable, but not fully disciplined.** There is a story here, but the paper keeps interrupting it with “result catalog” prose. The narrative is strongest when the paper contrasts pull-forward with threshold-crossing. It gets weaker when it lapses into listing empirical strategies, point estimates, and literature buckets.

At moments it feels like a collection of positive findings looking for the most ambitious interpretation. The story it should be telling is simpler:

1. Temporary subsidies usually re-time activity.
2. Housing is a hard test of that logic because the purchase margin is lumpy and supply may be elastic.
3. HomeBuilder suggests real additionality rather than pure timing.
4. Therefore, the effectiveness of temporary subsidies depends on market structure and the margin being subsidized.

That is the paper.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?

I would lead with:

> “Australia paid people to build houses during COVID, and approvals jumped sharply with little sign of the usual post-subsidy hangover.”

That is the one-sentence hook.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

They would **lean in initially**, because “no hangover after a temporary subsidy” cuts against a well-known baseline intuition. But the very next question would come fast:

> “How do you know that isn’t just the pandemic-induced shift from apartments to detached houses?”

That is the paper’s central strategic vulnerability. Not the econometrics per se, but the narrative vulnerability. The paper knows this and mentions it, but it is currently too much of a caveat tucked into the discussion rather than a first-order rival interpretation confronted head-on.

### If findings are modest or null, is that interesting?

The findings are not null. But they are still intermediate in an important sense: they are about **approvals**. So the dinner-party follow-up after the COVID-shift question is:

> “Did it increase actual housing supply, or just paper approvals?”

That does not kill the paper, but it limits excitement. For AER, the paper needs a more forceful answer to why approvals are the right object or else stronger downstream evidence.

The **cross-state heterogeneity tied to the price cap** is potentially more interesting than it is currently treated. If the paper’s mechanism is threshold-crossing plus supply elasticity, that heterogeneity is not peripheral robustness—it is part of the main story.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

This paper’s current gap to AER is mainly a combination of **framing problem + ambition problem**, with some novelty risk.

- **Framing problem:** The paper has not fully decided whether it is about Australian housing policy, temporary stimulus design, or a broader theory of when subsidies create activity. It should be the third.
- **Ambition problem:** Approvals are a useful start, but by themselves they are not yet a top-journal object unless tied to a much sharper conceptual contribution.
- **Novelty problem:** “A temporary housing subsidy increased approvals” is not enough. “Temporary subsidies can create net new activity when they relax lumpy constraints in elastic markets” is much closer.

If I imagine the top 10 people in this field reading it, they would likely say: “Interesting setting, sensible design, plausible result—but what is the general lesson, and how do I know it is not just pandemic composition plus an approvals artifact?” Until those questions are answered in the framing, the paper feels strong seminar-level rather than AER-level.

### Single most impactful advice

**Reframe the paper around a general economic question—when temporary subsidies create net new activity rather than timing shifts—and organize every section around showing that housing is a boundary case where the standard pull-forward logic breaks down.**

That one change would do the most to elevate it from a competent policy evaluation to a paper with broader intellectual stakes.
