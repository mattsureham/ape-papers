## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple, important incidence question: when environmental regulation forces dirty power plants to retire, do consumers pay through higher electricity bills or do producers absorb the cost? Using variation from the MATS-induced coal retirement wave, the paper argues that the answer depends on electricity market structure: regulated states pass costs through to ratepayers, while deregulated states do not.

That is a potentially AER-relevant question because it links environmental policy to market design and incidence. Busy economists should care if the same federal rule has different distributional consequences solely because of institutional structure.

**Does the paper itself articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Pretty well, actually. The opening is better than most submissions. It gets to the question quickly and gives the main answer in paragraph two. The weakness is that it still sounds a bit like “here is a policy episode and I estimate a price effect,” whereas the real hook is broader: **environmental regulation does not have a single incidence; institutions determine who pays**.

### The pitch the paper should have
“Who pays for environmental regulation when dirty capital must shut down? This paper studies the U.S. coal plant retirement wave triggered by the Mercury and Air Toxics Standards and shows that the incidence of compliance costs depends critically on electricity market design. In regulated states, coal retirements raised retail electricity prices because utilities passed replacement and stranded-asset costs through to captive customers; in deregulated states, the same federal mandate had little effect on retail prices, implying that the burden fell mainly on producers rather than consumers.”

That is the version to lead with. The current introduction is close, but it should lean harder into **incidence under alternative market structures**, not just “price effects of MATS.”

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:**  
The paper shows that the consumer incidence of coal-plant retirement costs from a common federal environmental regulation differed sharply across regulated and deregulated electricity markets, with pass-through to retail prices occurring only in regulated states.

### Is this clearly differentiated from the closest papers?
Only partially. The paper names some neighbors, but the differentiation is still somewhat “to my knowledge no one has estimated exactly this.” That is a weak mode of claiming contribution. A stronger version is:

- prior work studies whether environmental regulation changes plant retirement/investment behavior;
- prior work studies welfare or firm-level consequences of restructuring;
- this paper studies **incidence**, specifically **how market structure determines whether compliance costs are borne by consumers or producers**.

That is a real conceptual distinction. Right now the paper understates it and drifts into “there is a gap.”

### WORLD question or LITERATURE gap?
It starts with a world question, which is good: who paid for coal retirements? But then the formal contribution paragraph slips into literature-gap language (“fills an empirical gap”). That weakens the paper. The introduction should remain anchored on a world question:

- When polluting capital is forced to retire, how are the transition costs allocated?
- Does competition shield consumers from environmental compliance costs?
- Do regulated utility systems socialize transition costs in ways merchant systems do not?

That is much stronger than “no prior work estimates...”

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new?
Yes, but only if they are charitable. At present they might say: “It’s a DiD on MATS retirements and electricity prices, with heterogeneity by regulated versus deregulated states.” That is competent but not memorable.

You want them to say:  
**“It shows that the incidence of environmental regulation depends on market structure: regulated utility systems pass retirement costs to customers, competitive systems don’t.”**

That is the sellable new idea.

### What would make the contribution bigger?
A few concrete ways:

1. **Reframe around incidence, not price effects.**  
   “Price effects” sounds small and sectoral. “Incidence of environmental transition costs” sounds broader and more important.

2. **Show the producer side more directly.**  
   The paper asserts that in deregulated markets costs fell on generator owners. That is plausible, but right now it is more an interpretation than a demonstrated result. Even one clean producer-side outcome—merchant plant revenues, margins, equity exposure of utility holding companies, stranded asset write-downs—would make the contribution much bigger.

3. **Tie more directly to broader decarbonization policy.**  
   If the lesson is not about MATS per se but about forced fossil retirement generally, say so and structure the paper around that general lesson.

4. **Sharpen the mechanism evidence.**  
   The current replacement-investment/rate-base discussion is intuitive but somewhat verbal. Evidence on capital expenditure, utility rate cases, or generation replacement by ownership form would make the story feel much more first-order.

The biggest upside is not a different outcome variable per se; it is **demonstrating both sides of incidence** rather than only the consumer side.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
A major federal environmental rule forced many coal plants either to install controls or retire. Coal retirements were large and costly. The environmental benefits are clear, but the incidence of the economic costs is not.

### Tension
The same federal policy hit states with different electricity market institutions. That creates a natural tension: if the engineering shock is similar, why should consumer outcomes differ? Are consumers actually paying for clean air, or are firms?

### Resolution
The paper finds that states more exposed to MATS-induced coal retirements saw higher retail electricity prices, but only in traditionally regulated markets. In deregulated markets, there is little or no retail price effect.

### Implications
Market structure determines the distribution of transition costs. Environmental policy design cannot be evaluated apart from the institutional structure of the industries it regulates.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
Yes, more than many papers. But it is still somewhat **a collection of sensible results orbiting an underdeveloped central idea**. The central idea should be incidence. The plant-level retirement analysis, state-level price results, and mechanism discussion then become pieces of one story:

1. MATS forced retirement of technically vulnerable coal plants.
2. Those retirements imposed transition costs.
3. Market structure determined whether those costs hit retail customers.
4. Therefore, institutional design mediates the incidence of environmental policy.

Right now the paper spends a bit too much time convincing the reader that inefficient plants retired and not enough time elevating the broader implication. The first-stage evidence is useful, but strategically it is a support act, not the headline.

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

### What fact would I lead with at dinner?
“Its core finding is that the same federal clean-air rule raised electricity prices in regulated states but not in deregulated ones—so market design determined whether consumers or producers paid for coal plant retirements.”

That is a good dinner-party fact. Economists will lean in more than they will for “MATS increased prices by X percent.”

### Would people lean in?
Moderately, yes. Not because MATS itself is thrilling, but because the implication is broad: **incidence of climate/environmental policy depends on industrial organization**. That is a live question.

### What follow-up would they ask?
Immediately:  
“Then where did the costs go in deregulated states—producer profits, wholesale prices, reliability margins, entry, or some combination?”  

And that is exactly where the paper is a bit exposed. It asserts the answer but does not show it directly enough.

### If findings are modest, is the modesty itself interesting?
Yes, but only if framed correctly. A 2–3 percent retail price effect is not huge. That is fine. The interesting part is not that MATS massively raised prices; it’s that **the incidence pattern flips by market structure**. If the paper continues to foreground magnitude alone, it risks sounding modest. If it foregrounds heterogeneity in who pays, the result becomes much more interesting.

So the paper should not sell “environmental regulation greatly increased electricity prices.” It should sell:  
**“Environmental regulation did not have a common consumer cost; institutions determined incidence.”**

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

This is currently **closer to a solid field-journal paper than an AER paper**. The gap is not primarily technical; it is strategic.

### What is the main gap?
Mostly a **framing problem**, with some **scope/ambition problem**.

- **Framing problem:** The paper is better than its self-description. It has a broader idea than “electricity prices after MATS,” but it does not fully own that idea.
- **Scope problem:** It only really nails the consumer side of incidence. For a top journal, one wants stronger evidence on the alternative margin—who bears the cost when consumers do not.
- **Ambition problem:** The paper is careful and competent, but still somewhat safe. It stops at a plausible and useful finding instead of pushing toward a more general statement about transition policy and market design.

### Novelty problem?
Not fatal, but real. Many papers study coal retirements, MATS, electricity restructuring, or price effects. The novelty here is in the interaction—**environmental regulation × market structure × incidence**. That novelty needs to be made unmistakable, or else readers will indeed file it as “another DiD paper about a regulation.”

### Single most impactful advice
**Rewrite the paper around the incidence question and bring direct evidence on who bears the costs in deregulated markets, so the paper is about institutional determination of transition-cost incidence—not just about retail price effects of MATS.**

If the author can only change one thing, that is it.
