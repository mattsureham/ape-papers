---

### 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether procurement set-asides for small businesses actually bring new firms into the economy or if they simply induce existing, well-connected firms to acquire new labels. Using a sharp enforcement shock in the Dominican Republic, the author finds that "compliance" was achieved not through new entry, but through a "relabeling illusion" where incumbents captured the set-asides, actually leading to a more consolidated and less diverse supplier base. 

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this pitch very well in the first two paragraphs. It immediately frames the tension between the "straightforward logic" of policy and the empirical reality of "relabeling." The current pitch is strong, but to make it "AER-grade," the first paragraph should emphasize that this is a general failure mode of **categorical targeting** in any weak-institutional setting, not just a procurement story.

---

### 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**The Contribution:** The paper identifies "relabeling"—strategic certification by incumbents—as a primary mechanism through which targeted industrial policies fail to achieve substantive inclusion despite achieving nominal compliance.

*   **Differentiation:** It moves beyond the standard "cost of bid preferences" literature (Marion, 2007; Krasnokutskaya and Seim, 2011) by treating the supplier pool as endogenous. It distinguishes itself from the "corruption" literature by showing that even "clean" enforcement can lead to "compliant waste."
*   **Framing:** It is framed as a question about the WORLD (Does set-aside enforcement work?), which is strong.
*   **Explainability:** A smart economist would say: "It's the paper that shows set-asides just make old firms get new stamps, actually hurting market entry."
*   **How to make it bigger:** To truly clear the AER bar, the author needs to link this to **State Capacity**. The contribution would be bigger if it explored *why* agencies consolidate (is it a fixed cost of monitoring MIPYMEs?) and if it could provide a theoretical boundary condition for when relabeling is more attractive than entry.

---

### 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

*   **Closest Neighbors:** Bandiera et al. (2009) on passive waste; Best et al. (2023) on procurement and state capacity; Athey et al. (2013) on set-asides.
*   **Strategy:** The paper should **synthesize** the procurement literature with the **targeting literature** (e.g., Alatas et al. on community targeting). 
*   **Narrow vs. Broad:** Currently a bit narrow (Procurement). It should speak more to the **Industrial Policy** revival. This is a "cautionary tale" for the new industrial policy era.
*   **Missing Conversations:** The paper needs to speak to the "Missing Middle" and firm dynamics literature (Hsieh & Klenow). Is relabeling a tax on growth or a subsidy for staying small?

---

### 4. NARRATIVE ARC

*   **Setup:** Global popularity of SME set-asides as a tool for inclusive growth.
*   **Tension:** A "dead letter" mandate is suddenly enforced. Does the market open up?
*   **Resolution:** No. Market diversity drops. Incumbents "relabel" themselves to capture the rents.
*   **Implications:** Compliance metrics are "illusions"; policy must target firm *age* or *entry* rather than just *size* or *status*.

**Evaluation:** The arc is excellent. It has a "detective story" feel—the data shows a successful policy (MIPYME share up), but the "relabeling" analysis reveals the body in the closet.

---

### 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

*   **Lead Fact:** "When the government enforced a 20% small-business quota, the number of unique suppliers actually *fell* by 7%, and two-thirds of the 'small' winners were just the same old guys with new paperwork."
*   **Reaction:** Lean in. It confirms a cynical intuition with rigorous data.
*   **Follow-up:** "Did the government pay more for the same goods?" (The paper needs to answer the price/quality trade-off if the data allows).

---

### 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

*   **Front-loading:** The decomposition in Table 3 is the "money shot." It should be teased even earlier.
*   **Consolidation Section:** The finding that agencies reduce the number of processes (consolidation) to manage the mandate is fascinating but slightly buried. This "administrative burden of compliance" deserves its own subsection as a secondary mechanism.
*   **Appendix:** Standardized effect sizes (Table 6) belong in the appendix.
*   **Conclusion:** Needs to move away from the Dominican Republic and provide a "Manual for Policy Designers" to avoid the relabeling illusion.

---

### 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The science is here, and the "Relabeling Illusion" label is catchy. The gap is **Generalization**. To be a "Top 5" paper, it needs to move from "this happened in the DR" to "this is an inherent risk of any policy that creates a binary eligibility status."

**Single Most Impactful Advice:** Quantify the **efficiency cost**. If relabeling occurred, did the government pay a "certification premium" in terms of higher prices or lower quality compared to the pre-period? If the author can show that relabeling led to *worse* procurement outcomes (price/speed), it becomes an unbeatable paper on the unintended consequences of industrial policy.
