This paper has a real paper in it. The hook is potentially strong: procurement set-asides are ubiquitous, but we do not actually know whether they broaden access for small firms or simply induce incumbents to sort into the favored category. That is a world question, not just a literature gap, and it is exactly the kind of reframing that can make a familiar policy area newly interesting.

That said, in its current form the paper is still closer to a good field-journal paper than an AER paper. The main reason is not that the empirical object is unimportant; it is that the paper has not yet fully decided whether it is about procurement design, policy implementation, state capacity, or regulatory arbitrage. Right now it gestures at all four. AER papers usually force the reader to leave with one clean belief update.

## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

### What is this paper about?

This paper asks whether small-business procurement set-asides actually expand access for new small firms, or whether they mainly benefit incumbent suppliers who obtain the right certification once enforcement tightens. Using the Dominican Republic’s sudden post-2020 enforcement surge of a long-dormant SME procurement quota, the paper argues that stronger compliance increased the share of certified winners but did not broaden entry; instead, much of the shift reflects “relabeling” by existing suppliers and procurement consolidation.

Why should a busy economist care? Because governments around the world use procurement quotas as a major industrial policy tool, and the central policy question is not whether compliance rises on paper but whether the policy changes who can actually sell to the state.

### Does the paper articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Mostly yes, and better than many submissions. The first paragraph is good. The second paragraph, however, steers too quickly into “identification is difficult” and the U.S. auction literature. That is a literature move, not a why-this-matters move. The paper’s strongest pitch is not “I have a quasi-experiment in a place where identification is possible.” It is “we have been measuring the wrong outcome.”

### What should the first two paragraphs say instead?

The opening should foreground a more general proposition:

> Governments reserve billions in procurement contracts for small firms on the theory that set-asides help new and disadvantaged businesses overcome barriers to selling to the state. But the key policy question is not whether agencies meet the quota. It is whether set-asides actually expand market access, or instead induce incumbent suppliers to acquire the favored label and capture the benefits.
>
> This paper studies that question in the Dominican Republic, where a long-ignored 20 percent small-business procurement mandate began to be aggressively enforced after the 2020 presidential transition. I show that stronger enforcement substantially increased the share of certified winners, but did not broaden the supplier base. Much of the post-reform shift reflects incumbent suppliers obtaining MIPYME certification, while agencies with the largest compliance increases actually contracted through fewer suppliers. The broader lesson is that mandate-based industrial policy can generate nominal compliance without substantive inclusion.

That is the pitch the paper should own.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### Contribution in one sentence

The paper’s contribution is to show that enforcing small-firm procurement set-asides can raise the share of certified winners without expanding genuine small-firm entry, because incumbents relabel themselves to capture the certification premium.

### Is this clearly differentiated from the closest papers?

Partially, but not sharply enough. The paper says prior work studies costs, allocation, discretion, corruption, and monitoring, whereas this paper studies supplier pool composition and relabeling. That is the right direction. But the differentiation still feels generic because the introduction lists literatures rather than defining a precise frontier.

The paper needs to distinguish itself from at least four nearby conversations:

1. **Set-asides and bidding/cost distortions**  
   Marion (2007), Krasnokutskaya and Seim, Athey et al., Nakabayashi-style work.  
   Distinction: those papers ask how preferences affect prices, bidding, or allocation conditional on the market; this paper asks whether the policy changes who enters and who benefits.

2. **Procurement discretion/corruption/state capacity**  
   Bandiera, Prat, and Valletti (2009); Decarolis et al.; Szucs; Colonnelli et al.  
   Distinction: those papers focus on inefficiency, favoritism, or implementation quality; this paper argues that even clean-looking compliance can fail through strategic certification by incumbents.

3. **Targeting and program capture / regulatory arbitrage**  
   This is the most underexploited comparison. The “relabeling illusion” is basically a form of category-based policy capture. The paper should make more of that.

4. **Firm entry and market access to the state**  
   The paper should speak more directly to how policies shape the extensive margin of supplier participation, not just procurement composition.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a WORLD question or filling a LITERATURE gap?

It begins with a world question, which is good, but repeatedly slips into literature-gap framing. The stronger frame is: “Policymakers think quotas bring new firms into procurement; we show they may instead reward incumbents who can cheaply comply.” That is a belief about the world.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?

They could get there, but the risk is they would say: “It’s a DiD on SME set-asides in the Dominican Republic showing some crowd-out and relabeling.” That is not yet memorable enough. You want them to say: “This paper shows quota compliance can be fake progress—certified winners rise, but actual market access does not.”

The phrase “relabeling illusion” is actually useful branding. The paper should build the whole introduction around it, not just use it as a clever title.

### What would make the contribution bigger?

Three possibilities:

- **Make substantive access the main outcome, not supplier counts alone.**  
  The biggest version of this paper is not “supplier diversity fell.” It is “set-asides did not increase the participation of truly new, young, or previously excluded firms.” Supplier count is a somewhat indirect object. The decomposition is more novel than the log-suppliers result.

- **Connect relabeling to policy design choices.**  
  For example: when certification is easy for incumbents and hard for new firms, quotas produce relabeling rather than entry. This turns a case study into a general proposition.

- **Show distributional incidence more clearly.**  
  Not just awards to “new MIPYME” vs. “relabeled,” but who among small firms gains: young firms, small-value contracts, first-time participants, geographically peripheral firms, women-led firms. AER wants the deeper margin, not just one decomposition table.

If the author could enlarge one thing substantively, it would be the direct measurement of whether the policy changed access for the intended beneficiaries on multiple margins.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

### Closest neighbors

The closest neighbors seem to be:

- **Marion (2007)** on affirmative action / disadvantaged business enterprise procurement and bidding outcomes.
- **Athey, Levin, and Seira / related procurement preference papers** on set-asides and allocation in auctions.
- **Krasnokutskaya and Seim / Nakabayashi** on procurement auctions and small business programs.
- **Bandiera, Prat, and Valletti (2009)** on active and passive waste in procurement.
- **Decarolis et al. / Coviello et al. / Szucs** on procurement rules, publicity, discretion, and corruption.

Depending on exact reference list quality, the paper may also need to engage work on **government demand shaping firm dynamics**, **program take-up / certification frictions**, and **policy targeting under endogenous category choice**.

### How should it position itself relative to those neighbors?

Mostly **build on them, not attack them**. The tone should be:

- The existing procurement literature has taught us a lot about prices, costs, and allocation.
- But to evaluate set-asides as an inclusion policy, we also need to know whether they change the composition of suppliers and induce entry.
- This paper shows a previously underappreciated margin: strategic recategorization of incumbents.

That is stronger than implying prior work “takes the supplier pool as given,” which is true enough but slightly cheap as positioning.

### Is it too narrow or too broad?

Currently a bit **too broad in aspiration and too narrow in execution**.

Broad, because it invokes procurement, corruption, transparency, state capacity, industrial policy, and implementation.  
Narrow, because the actual evidence is from one policy in one country with a specific mechanism.

The right move is to narrow the framing around one central conversation: **how category-based procurement preferences change actual market access**. Then, in the discussion, connect that to implementation and state capacity.

### What literature does it seem unaware of?

It seems somewhat underconnected to:

- **Regulatory arbitrage / endogenous sorting into policy categories**
- **Targeting and beneficiary misclassification**
- **Government procurement as industrial policy / market access**
- **Extensive-margin participation in public markets**
- Possibly **firm certification and administrative burden** literatures

The paper has the beginnings of a “nominal vs. substantive compliance” idea. That could connect productively to state-capacity and implementation literatures, but it needs citations from beyond procurement.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?

Not quite. The current conversation is “procurement plus corruption plus monitoring.” The more impactful conversation is:

**When policy targets categories rather than underlying constraints, beneficiaries may change labels rather than the policy changing opportunity.**

That is a much bigger idea, and procurement is a strong application of it.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Many governments reserve procurement for small firms to lower barriers and broaden participation.

### Tension

Observed compliance with such mandates may not mean the policy is achieving its substantive objective, because firms and agencies can adapt in ways that satisfy the rule while leaving market access largely unchanged.

### Resolution

In the Dominican Republic, stricter enforcement increased the share of certified MIPYME winners, but much of that increase came from incumbent suppliers obtaining certification; agencies with the biggest compliance increase did not expand their supplier base and may even have consolidated procurement.

### Implications

Quota-based procurement policy can produce nominal compliance without genuine inclusion. Policymakers should evaluate who enters and who benefits, not just whether the quota is met.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

Yes, more than most. But the arc weakens in the middle because the paper is unsure what its central reveal is.

There are actually two candidate stories:

1. **Set-asides reduce supplier diversity via procurement consolidation.**
2. **Set-asides increase certified participation mainly through relabeling, not true entry.**

The second is the much stronger story. The first may be a channel or supplementary consequence, but it is not the memorable headline. Right now the paper almost leads with the supplier decline and then later gets to the more original decomposition. That is backwards.

If this were an AER-oriented revision, the story should be:

- Policy intends to broaden access.
- Enforcement raises certified participation.
- But certified participation is a misleading metric.
- The increase comes mostly from relabeled incumbents, not intended entrants.
- Therefore compliance metrics overstate substantive inclusion.

That is a clean narrative. The supplier-count result can sit inside that story as one manifestation, not the paper’s organizing principle.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?

“Enforcing a small-business procurement quota raised the share of certified winners a lot, but for every genuinely new small-firm entrant there were roughly two incumbent suppliers who simply got certified.”

That is the fact. Not the 2.4 fewer suppliers.

### Would people lean in?

Yes, moderately. It has the right kind of surprise: the policy appears to work by official metrics, but those metrics are misleading. Economists like that kind of inversion.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Likely one of these:

- “Is this just a Dominican Republic story, or is relabeling a general feature of set-asides?”
- “Are these incumbents actually small firms that had not certified before, or larger firms gaming the system?”
- “What policy design would prevent this?”
- “Did costs, prices, or contract performance change?”

That last question matters strategically. Even if referees handle causality, the editor has to ask whether the paper leaves too much on the table by not speaking to efficiency or welfare margins. AER readers will want to know whether relabeling is merely distributionally disappointing or also socially costly.

### If findings are modest, is the null interesting?

The paper is not a null-results paper. Its strongest result is conceptual: the key observed policy success metric is potentially misleading. That is interesting. But the paper needs to sell the non-result on entry more forcefully as the core substantive point, not as “we didn’t find much on first-time winners.”

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

### What would make it read better?

  The introduction currently emphasizes the supplier decline first. That is likely not the best front-door result.

- **Cut literature review in the introduction by 30–40 percent.**  
  Too many citations, not enough sharpening.

- **Move most robustness prose out of the introduction.**  
  The intro currently spends too much real estate reporting p-values and leave-one-out results. That is not where top-journal readers decide whether to care.

- **Make the main results section shorter and more hierarchical.**  
  Start with one figure or table that tells the whole story:
  1. compliance rises,
  2. certified-winner share rises,
  3. true new-small-firm participation barely rises / is dominated by relabeling.
  
  Then supplier counts, HHI, and process consolidation come after.

- **Demote some standard validation material.**  
  Event studies and robustness can stay, but they should not crowd the narrative center.

- **Rewrite the conclusion to add value.**  
  Right now it mostly restates findings. It should instead crystallize the broader lesson: policies that target administrative categories can alter labels faster than opportunities.

### Is the paper front-loaded with the good stuff?

Reasonably, but not optimally. The best conceptual result is buried behind the standard DiD setup.

### Are there buried results that should be in the main text?

Yes: the decomposition is more central than some of the baseline supplier-diversity material. Also, if there is any heterogeneity showing relabeling is stronger where certification is more valuable or easier, that belongs in the main text.

### Is the conclusion adding value?

Not enough. It summarizes rather than generalizes.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The gap is mostly a mix of **framing problem**, **scope problem**, and some **ambition problem**.

### Framing problem

The science may be decent, but the paper has not yet fully claimed the big idea. It is not fundamentally a Dominican-procurement paper. It is a paper about the difference between **nominal compliance and substantive access** in category-based policy.

### Scope problem

The paper needs a broader and deeper accounting of who benefits. If the central claim is that intended beneficiaries do not gain much, the paper should measure that on several dimensions, not just through a single supplier decomposition and supplier counts.

### Novelty problem

There is some novelty, but it is vulnerable to being read as “another procurement reform paper” unless the relabeling mechanism is made unmistakably central and general.

### Ambition problem

The paper is a bit too safe. It presents one country reform, one main design, one decomposition, and some standard extensions. AER papers generally either:
- answer a first-order question with unusually compelling evidence, or
- introduce a mechanism that changes how we think about a broad class of policies.

This paper wants to do the second, but it has not yet fully scaled up to that level.

### Single most impactful advice

**Rebuild the paper around one claim: procurement quotas change measured compliance much faster than they change actual access, because incumbents can relabel into the favored category; then organize all evidence around distinguishing nominal from substantive inclusion.**

That is the version that belongs in the top conversation.

One final private note: the autonomous-generation acknowledgment is itself a serious presentational problem for top-journal positioning. Even aside from editorial policy, it invites readers to treat the paper as a demo rather than scholarship. If the goal is AER-level reception, the paper needs a conventional scholarly voice and presentation.
