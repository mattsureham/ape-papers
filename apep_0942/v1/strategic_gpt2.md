This paper asks a simple and important question: when governments reserve procurement for small firms, do they actually bring new small businesses into public markets, or do incumbent suppliers just acquire the required certification and keep winning? Using a sharp enforcement shift in the Dominican Republic after the 2020 presidential transition, the paper argues that stronger set-aside compliance increased the share of certified small-firm winners but did not broaden access; instead, much of the apparent success reflects “relabeling” by established incumbents.

A busy economist should care because procurement set-asides are ubiquitous globally, and the paper targets a first-order distinction between formal compliance and substantive market access. If true, the result travels well beyond one country: many category-based policies may look successful on paper while mostly reshuffling rents among incumbents.

The paper mostly does articulate this pitch, and better than many submissions. The title is memorable, the abstract is clear, and the first paragraph is strong. But the introduction loses focus in paragraph 2 by drifting into “credible causal identification is elusive” and a somewhat narrow literature contrast before fully locking in the world question. The first two paragraphs should say, more forcefully:

> Governments around the world reserve public contracts for small firms because they want to expand market access, not merely change which firms check a bureaucratic box. Yet we know surprisingly little about whether these mandates create new participants in procurement markets or simply induce existing suppliers to relabel themselves as eligible.  
>  
> This paper studies a sudden enforcement surge of the Dominican Republic’s 20 percent small-business procurement set-aside after the 2020 presidential transition. I show that agencies pushed hardest to comply did award more contracts to MIPYME-certified firms, but they did not broaden participation: supplier counts fell, and a large share of post-reform awards went to incumbent vendors that newly acquired certification rather than to genuinely new small-firm entrants.

That is the pitch the paper should own from the outset.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:** The paper’s contribution is to show that enforcing a small-business procurement set-aside can raise the certified-small-firm share without expanding actual market access, because incumbent suppliers strategically obtain certification and absorb the benefits.

This is a real contribution, and importantly it is framed mostly as a question about the world rather than as a literature-gap exercise. That helps. “Do set-asides create new suppliers or merely redistribute to relabeled incumbents?” is much stronger than “the literature hasn’t examined X.” The paper is at its best when it says exactly that.

That said, the differentiation from the closest literature is not yet sharp enough. Right now, a smart economist could say: “It’s a DiD paper on procurement set-asides in a developing country, showing limited entry effects.” That is not enough. The paper needs to say much more explicitly that the novelty is **not** merely another estimate of the effects of affirmative procurement or SME targeting. The novelty is the distinction between:
1. nominal compliance versus substantive access,
2. certified status versus true entry,
3. beneficiary relabeling versus market expansion.

That distinction is potentially publication-worthy at AER level if framed as a general problem in category-based policy design.

Could a smart economist explain what is new after reading the current introduction? Yes, but only approximately. They would probably say: “The paper finds that set-asides in Dominican procurement mostly benefited incumbents who got SME certification, not new small firms.” That is good. But the paper still risks being heard as “another procurement reform paper” rather than “a paper about how targeted policies get captured through relabeling.”

What would make the contribution bigger?

- **Different framing:** Elevate this from procurement to a general political economy / industrial policy design point: when benefits are tied to a certifiable category, incumbents with lower compliance costs capture them.
- **Different comparison:** Compare “newly certified incumbents” to “young firms already eligible but not participating” or to “truly new entrants” in a more conceptually central way. The decomposition is the heart; it should become the paper, not a mechanism subsection.
- **Different outcome variable:** The biggest missing outcome is not another concentration metric; it is a cleaner measure of **extensive-margin access**. First-time winners is there, but it is buried and underdeveloped. If the paper can make “who newly enters procurement?” the centerpiece, the contribution gets much bigger.
- **Different mechanism emphasis:** “Procurement consolidation” may be true, but strategically it is a distraction unless it is central to the conceptual contribution. The bigger idea is relabeling/capture, not agency purchasing organization per se.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

Closest neighbors likely include:

- Marion (2007), on affirmative action / disadvantaged business enterprise programs in procurement
- Krasnokutskaya and Seim (2011), on bid preferences and set-asides in procurement
- Athey, Coey, and Levin (2013), on set-asides and subsidies in auctions
- Nakabayashi (2013), on small business set-asides in procurement/auction settings
- Decarolis, Coviello, and related procurement discretion/rules papers
- Bandiera, Prat, and Valletti (2009), on procurement waste
- Colonnelli, Prem, and Teso (2020), on corruption and politically connected firms in procurement

How should the paper position itself? It should **build on** the auction/set-aside literature but argue that those papers mostly study price, allocation, efficiency, or bidder composition holding the supplier pool conceptually fixed. This paper adds the extensive-margin participation question. It should also **connect to** state capacity / implementation / policy evasion literatures by arguing that enforcement can improve compliance metrics without achieving policy intent.

The paper should not “attack” the prior literature; rather, it should say: existing work asked whether set-asides distort prices or allocation among participants, while this paper asks whether they change who participates at all—and whether measured success reflects real entry or bureaucratic relabeling.

Current positioning is a bit split. It is somewhat too broad in the sense that it gestures at procurement, corruption, monitoring, state capacity, and industrial policy without clearly choosing the main conversation. At the same time, it is too narrow where it matters, because the most powerful framing is not yet fully exploited.

The paper seems insufficiently engaged with at least three broader literatures:

1. **Policy implementation / Goodhart’s law / multitasking in bureaucracy**: agents optimize to measured targets rather than true objectives.
2. **Program capture / regulatory gaming / bunching around eligibility thresholds**: incumbents reorganize to qualify for targeted benefits.
3. **Industrial policy and firm dynamics**: whether government demand policies create new firms versus subsidize incumbents.

The most impactful conversation may actually be with a literature the paper only hints at: **the difference between formal eligibility targeting and real beneficiary incidence**. That is a larger and more interesting conversation than procurement narrowly construed.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

The setup is strong: governments worldwide use procurement set-asides to help small firms overcome barriers to contracting.

The tension is also good: observed increases in small-business procurement could reflect real market opening—or just incumbents obtaining certification. We usually cannot tell because these policies are longstanding and the relevant margin is hard to observe.

The resolution: after enforcement in the Dominican Republic intensified, certified-small-firm winning rose sharply, but supplier participation did not broaden; instead, many awards went to relabeled incumbents, especially in large agencies.

The implications: compliance metrics can be deeply misleading. Policy design that targets certifiable categories may reward those best able to satisfy paperwork rather than those most excluded from the market.

So yes, there is a narrative arc here. More than many submissions, this paper has an actual story. But it is still not fully disciplined. The paper currently tries to tell two stories:

1. set-asides create a relabeling illusion;
2. agencies respond by consolidating procurement processes.

The first is the much stronger story. The second may be part of the channel, but strategically it muddies the paper’s identity. If I remember this paper a week later, I will remember “relabeling of incumbents,” not “process consolidation.” The manuscript should be organized around the former and demote the latter to supporting evidence unless the authors can show that consolidation is itself central to why substantive access declines.

Right now it is not a collection of random results, but it does risk becoming a collection of related procurement facts. The story it should tell is:

- governments want entry,
- they measure certification,
- agencies and incumbent firms respond to the metric,
- reported success therefore overstates actual market opening.

That is a clean AER-type narrative if developed properly.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

At a dinner party of economists, the lead fact is:

> “When the Dominican Republic started enforcing its small-business procurement quota, the share of certified small-firm winners jumped—but most of that increase came from incumbent suppliers getting certified, not from new small firms entering.”

That would get people to lean in, at least initially. It is intuitive, counterintuitive in the right way, and generalizable. The follow-up question would be immediate:

> “Is that just this one country’s certification bureaucracy, or is it a general failure mode of set-asides?”

And that is precisely where the paper needs to lean harder. Right now the evidence is country-specific but the framing as a general mechanism is not yet developed enough. The paper needs to earn the claim that this is not merely a Dominican procurement anecdote.

A second follow-up question would be:

> “Does the policy hurt entry, or merely fail to help it?”

The paper seems to suggest both at different moments. It should be precise. “No increase in genuine entry, plus substitution toward relabeled incumbents” is already interesting. It does not need to oversell to “crowding out” unless that is the central empirical pattern.

These are not null findings, and that helps. The paper has a positive factual result—compliance rises, but entry does not—which is better than a pure null. The authors should present the core finding as a discrepancy between the policy metric and the policy objective, not as a failed experiment.

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

- **Shorten the introduction after the core pitch.** The first 2–3 paragraphs are close to enough. The detailed robustness paragraph does not belong in the introduction at this length. It dilutes the story.
- **Move some design detail later.** The introduction currently spends too much time on the empirical setup relative to the conceptual point.
- **Front-load the decomposition.** The decomposition table is the paper’s intellectual center of gravity. It should appear earlier in the Results or even be previewed in the introduction more prominently. Right now the introduction does this somewhat, but the logic of the whole paper should revolve around it.
- **Demote robustness rhetoric.** This memo is not about identification, but as an editor thinking strategically: the paper reads too much like it wants to reassure skeptical referees before winning the reader’s interest. That is backwards.
- **Clarify whether “procurement consolidation” is core or secondary.** If secondary, move it later and shorten. If core, then it needs to be conceptually tied to the relabeling story rather than appearing as a parallel mechanism.
- **The discussion section should do more synthesis.** Right now it partially repeats results and partially wanders into caveats. It should instead broaden the lesson: category-based targeting often induces strategic sorting by incumbents.
- **The conclusion adds some value** because it generalizes beyond the setting, but it could be sharper and less repetitive.

Most importantly: the reader should not have to wade through tables to learn the big idea. On that score the paper is better than average, but it can still be tightened.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The main gap is **not** primarily a framing problem, though framing needs work. It is a combination of **scope and ambition**.

The idea is good enough for AER-level interest: nominal targeting versus real incidence, with a memorable relabeling mechanism. But in current form, the paper still feels like a competent single-country reduced-form paper rather than a field-defining statement. The risk is novelty slippage: readers may ask whether this is just one more setting where a quota did not do exactly what intended.

What separates an AER paper from a solid field journal paper here would be:

- a stronger claim about a **general economic mechanism**,
- cleaner emphasis on **entry versus relabeling** as the central outcome,
- tighter positioning in a broader conversation about **policy targeting, implementation, and incumbent capture**,
- less time spent on standard procurement outcomes that do not advance the headline contribution.

If I had to classify the gap:
- **Framing problem:** yes, somewhat.
- **Scope problem:** yes, importantly.
- **Novelty problem:** not fatal, but the paper must work harder to show this is a new question, not merely a new setting.
- **Ambition problem:** yes. The paper currently undersells the conceptual stakes and oversells some secondary results.

**Single most impactful advice:** Rebuild the paper around one big claim—**set-asides measured through certification can produce the appearance of inclusion without real entry because incumbents are better able to sort into the targeted category**—and make every section serve that claim.

That means the decomposition is not a mechanism add-on; it is the main result. The supplier-count and consolidation results then become supporting evidence about how the market adjusts around that central phenomenon.
