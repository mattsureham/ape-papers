## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper exploits a 2023 nationwide policy shift in Wales—reducing the default urban speed limit from 30 to 20 mph—to provide the first causal evidence of how broad-based speed limit reductions affect road safety. Using a difference-in-differences design against England, the author finds a 30% reduction in pedestrian serious injuries, concentrated precisely where physics and the policy intended. It is a rare "clean" test of how changing a regulatory default can shift high-stakes public safety outcomes on a national scale.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this well in the abstract and the first paragraph. However, the current intro focuses a bit too much on the UK institutional context. 
**The pitch the paper should have:** "While the nonlinear relationship between impact speed and pedestrian survival is well-known in physics, economists know little about whether changing a nationwide regulatory *default* can successfully realize these safety gains. Using the 2023 divergence between Wales and England, I show that lowering the default urban speed limit reduces serious pedestrian injuries by 30%, providing a proof-of-concept for the 'power of defaults' in life-and-death public infrastructure."

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution:** The paper provides the first nationwide causal evaluation of a default urban speed limit reduction, demonstrating that the primary welfare gain is a 30-80% reduction in serious pedestrian injuries rather than a general reduction in all traffic collisions.

- **Differentiation:** It is well-differentiated. Most literature (Ashenfelter & Greenstone) focuses on *increases* on highways to estimate the Value of Statistical Life (VSL). This looks at *reductions* in urban settings where the margin is pedestrian survival, not just driver time-costs.
- **World vs. Literature:** It frames itself as answering a question about the WORLD (Does this policy save lives?), which is high-impact.
- **Explainability:** A smart economist would say: "It’s the Wales 20mph paper; it proves that lowering the default works for pedestrians, even if it doesn't stop every fender-bender."
- **Bigger Contribution:** To make this "AER big," the paper needs to move beyond the physical result and more deeply into the **Economic Trade-off**. We need a back-of-the-envelope calculation of the VSL saved versus the aggregate time-cost of slower commutes for the entire Welsh population. Without the cost side, it's a public health paper; with the cost side, it's an AER paper.

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

- **Neighbors:** Ashenfelter & Greenstone (2004) on VSL; Madrian & Shea (2001) on Defaults; Li & Graham (2016) on London 20mph zones.
- **Positioning:** It should **synthesize** the safety literature with the behavioral "defaults" literature. The "Default" framing (Section 5.1) is the paper's strongest intellectual hook. 
- **Missing Literature:** It should speak to the **Urban/Transport Economics** literature on "Vision Zero" and the "Value of Time" (VOT). 
- **Right Conversation?** Currently, it’s a "policy evaluation" conversation. It would be more impactful if it were a "Value of a Statistical Life" conversation. If we save X lives but slow down Y million commutes, what is the implicit price Wales is paying per life saved?

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

- **Setup:** Global urban speed limits are usually 30mph/50kph, based on legacy standards.
- **Tension:** Physics says 20mph is much safer, but politicians fear the "war on drivers" and believe people won't comply anyway.
- **Resolution:** Wales forced the change via a default shift; pedestrians got significantly safer, and compliance (though incomplete) was enough to move the needle.
- **Implications:** Other nations can use "defaults" to bypass the political gridlock of road-by-road changes.

**Evaluation:** The arc is clear. However, the "tension" could be heightened by highlighting the massive political backlash in Wales (the 469,000-signature petition). This turns a dry DiD paper into a study of a highly contentious policy that actually worked.

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

- **Lead Fact:** "Lowering the speed limit to 20mph reduced serious pedestrian injuries by 30%—and up to 80% near the border—with almost no effect on car-to-car collisions."
- **Reaction:** Lean in. Everyone has an opinion on speed limits.
- **Follow-up:** "But how much did it screw up traffic?" This is the question the author *must* answer to keep the AER audience's attention.

---

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

- **Front-load the Border Results:** The border analysis (Section 4.2) is much more compelling than the full-sample results, which suffer from parallel trend issues. I would move the border results to the primary position.
- **The "Slight Injuries" result:** Move the decomposition of fatal/serious/slight (currently in 4.4) earlier. It’s a vital part of the story—the policy shifts the distribution of severity.
- **Appendix:** The "Standardized Effect Sizes" table is good for meta-analysis but can stay in the appendix.

---

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The gap is **Economic Welfare Analysis**. 

An AER reader doesn't just want to know if the policy worked; they want to know if it was *optimal*. The author mentions "time-costs" implicitly but doesn't quantify them. To get into the AER, the author needs to use the GoSafe speed data to estimate the total increase in travel time across Wales and compare that to the social value of the 4.5 serious injuries prevented per month.

**Single Biggest Improvement:** Add a formal Welfare/Cost-Benefit section that weighs the VSL of reduced pedestrian injuries against the aggregate increase in travel time.
