## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Congress faces a fixed constitutional deadline. As the end of a Congress approaches, legislative activity compresses and leaders must allocate scarce floor time.

### Tension
We know deadlines affect legislative timing and strategy, but do they also degrade the *procedures* that make lawmaking accountable and deliberative? Congress may appear productive while quietly sacrificing transparency.

### Resolution
The paper finds that laws enacted near the end of a Congress are less likely to receive recorded roll-call votes and less likely to go through conference committees.

### Implications
Institutional deadlines may create a hidden quality cost in democratic governance: lawmaking can continue, but under weaker public accountability and less formal scrutiny.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
It has a **serviceable** arc, but not a fully satisfying one. The paper has the setup and the result. What is weaker is the *tension* and the *implication*.

Why weaker?

- The tension is not yet framed as a major puzzle. It is almost intuitive that late-session lawmaking uses shortcuts. So the paper needs to explain why this is surprising, consequential, or underappreciated.
- The implication is underspecified because the measured outcomes are proxies. The paper wants readers to conclude something about deliberative quality, but the actual evidence is about procedural form.

So at present it risks reading as **a collection of plausible procedural facts searching for a larger meaning**.

### What story should it be telling?
The best story is:

> Democratic procedures are costly safeguards. When institutions impose hard deadlines, lawmakers economize on those safeguards. The result is not simply delayed policymaking or omnibus legislating, but a systematic substitution away from transparent, accountable forms of lawmaking.

That is a real story. But the paper must discipline itself around it.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Almost certainly:

- “Does this matter for policy outcomes, or is it just procedural symbolism?”
- Then: “How much of this is just that trivial consensus bills get cleaned up at the end?”
- And: “Is this unique to Congress, or a general deadline phenomenon?”

Those are exactly the questions the current draft does not fully answer.

The findings are not null, but they are modest in the sense that they are procedural and indirect. The paper does make a decent case that “less recorded voting” matters for accountability. But unless it can show either (a) broader procedural degradation, or (b) downstream policy consequences, some readers will see this as an elegant documentation exercise rather than a major insight.

So the “so what” is **promising but incomplete**.
