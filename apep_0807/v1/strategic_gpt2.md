## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether hard end-of-session deadlines change how Congress makes law. Using all enacted public laws since 1973, it shows that legislation passed in the final month of a Congress is much less likely to receive a recorded roll-call vote or go through conference, suggesting that looming deadlines shift lawmaking toward faster, less transparent procedures.

A busy economist should care because this is, at root, a paper about how hard deadlines shape organizational behavior inside a core democratic institution. If real, the result says that constitutional design affects not just how much Congress produces, but the procedural form through which policy is made and accountability is delivered.

### Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Only partially. The opening anecdote is vivid, but the introduction quickly settles into a somewhat descriptive political-science framing. The problem is not that the paper lacks a result; it is that the introduction undersells the broader economic question and overclaims the meaning of the proxies. “Deliberation deficit” is memorable, but the paper has not yet earned that label. What it clearly shows is a decline in procedural transparency/formality near the deadline, not necessarily a decline in actual deliberative quality.

### The pitch the paper should have

> Democratic institutions operate under hard deadlines, yet we know little about how deadlines change the process by which policy is produced. This paper studies a fixed constitutional deadline in the U.S. Congress and shows that laws enacted near the end of a Congress are substantially less likely to receive recorded votes or formal bicameral reconciliation, implying that time pressure shifts lawmaking toward faster and less publicly accountable procedures.
>
> The broader point is that institutional design affects not only legislative output but also the transparency and procedural scrutiny of policymaking. By showing that a constitutional calendar systematically changes how laws are enacted, the paper speaks to economists interested in organizations under time pressure, political accountability, and the design of democratic institutions.

That is cleaner, more general, and more defensible.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### Contribution in one sentence

The paper documents that the fixed end-of-Congress deadline is associated with a large within-Congress shift away from recorded votes and conference committees for enacted laws.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?

Not enough. The paper cites legislative productivity, omnibus lawmaking, and agenda-setting, but the differentiation remains fuzzy. A reader could still come away thinking: “This is a competent empirical note showing that late-session bills use more shortcuts.” That is not yet a top-journal contribution.

The paper needs to distinguish itself more sharply from:
- work on **unorthodox lawmaking** as a secular institutional trend,
- work on **omnibus bills and coalition-building**,
- work on **legislative productivity/gridlock**,
- work on **transparency/accountability from recorded votes**.

Right now it is adjacent to all four literatures without fully claiming a distinctive lane.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a gap in a literature?

Mixed, but too often the latter. The stronger question is: **Do hard deadlines degrade procedural transparency in lawmaking?** That is a world question. The current introduction spends too much time saying prior work studied “how much” Congress produces rather than “conditions” of production. That is a literature-gap formulation, and a relatively weak one.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?

They could explain the basic result, but they might still summarize it as: “It’s another reduced-form paper showing that end-of-session pressure changes congressional procedure.” That is a warning sign. The paper is understandable, but not yet memorable at the level of contribution.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Several possibilities, in descending order of impact:

1. **Show substantive consequences, not just procedural correlates.**  
   If end-of-session laws are more likely to be amended quickly, corrected by technical amendments, litigated, delayed in implementation, or reversed, then the paper becomes about policy quality, not just process. That would be much bigger.

2. **Reframe around accountability/transparency rather than “deliberation quality.”**  
   Recorded votes are directly tied to public accountability. Conference use is a formal procedural checkpoint. A paper about deadline-induced reductions in legislative accountability is cleaner than one claiming to measure “deliberation.”

3. **Exploit comparative institutional variation.**  
   If state legislatures, chambers, or periods with different calendar rules show different patterns, the paper could move from “interesting pattern in Congress” to “general evidence on institutional design.”

4. **Open the black box of what gets fast-tracked.**  
   Are these low-salience consensus bills, distributive bills, major omnibus packages, or last-minute fixes? A more economically meaningful characterization of the affected legislation would enlarge the contribution.

5. **Connect to broader organization economics.**  
   The insight is not just about Congress. It is about teams facing hard deadlines and substituting toward less information-revealing, less formal processes.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Congress faces a fixed constitutional endpoint. As deadlines approach, legislative business must be completed or dies. Prior work has emphasized productivity, gridlock, and strategic procedure, but less is known about how deadlines affect procedural form.

### Tension

Hard deadlines may force a tradeoff: finish more legislation quickly, or preserve formal and transparent procedures. Yet it is not obvious whether deadline pressure actually reduces scrutiny, because many important bills also arrive late and might receive more, not less, procedural attention.

### Resolution

Laws enacted near the deadline are less likely to receive recorded roll calls and less likely to go through conference, even after accounting for some observed bill characteristics.

### Implications

Institutional deadlines may systematically reduce transparency and procedural accountability in democratic lawmaking. Constitutional design therefore shapes not only whether policy gets made, but how it gets made.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

Serviceable, but not strong. It has a coherent empirical pattern, but the story is still too close to “collection of results looking for a larger meaning.” The core issue is that the paper wants the resolution to be “deadline pressure degrades deliberative quality,” but the evidence really supports “deadline pressure changes procedural form in ways that likely reduce transparency and formality.”

That mismatch weakens the narrative.

### What story should it be telling?

The strongest story is:

1. **Hard deadlines are ubiquitous in organizations and institutions.**
2. **When deadlines bind, organizations substitute away from slower, information-rich procedures.**
3. **In Congress, this means fewer recorded votes and less formal reconciliation near session end.**
4. **This substitution reduces public accountability in a central democratic process.**

That is a much sharper narrative than “we document a deliberation deficit.”

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?

“Laws enacted in the final month of a Congress are about half as likely to receive a recorded roll-call vote as laws enacted earlier.”

That is the cleanest, most memorable fact.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

A mixed verdict. Political economists and public economists would lean in initially because the fact is striking and intuitive. But many would then immediately ask: **Is this just because low-salience consensus bills get cleared at the end?** If the answer remains “we control for some observables and do placebo tests,” interest may fade. The paper gets attention; it does not yet hold it.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Almost certainly:  
**Does this matter for actual policy quality or accountability outcomes?**

Secondary follow-ups:
- Are the late-session bills substantively different?
- Is this about speed, transparency, or actual deliberation?
- Does anything bad happen because of this?

Those are exactly the questions the current draft cannot fully answer.

### If the findings are modest, is the result itself interesting?

Yes, but only if framed correctly. A null on midpoint placebo and a sizable decline in roll calls near the real deadline is not a failed experiment. But the paper must make a more convincing case that learning this is important because recorded votes produce accountability, not because they are a generic proxy for “quality.”

As written, the paper risks feeling like a pattern in procedure rather than a consequential institutional fact.
