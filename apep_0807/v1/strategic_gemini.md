## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
The U.S. Constitution imposes a hard deadline every two years (Jan 3), forcing a massive volume of legislation through a narrow temporal bottleneck. This paper asks: does this "calendar pressure" systematically degrade the transparency and scrutiny of the laws being passed? By analyzing 50 years of data, the author finds that end-of-session laws are nearly 50% less likely to receive a recorded roll-call vote, effectively shielding legislators from individual accountability for last-minute passage.

**Evaluation:** The paper does a decent job in the first two paragraphs, but it leans a bit too heavily on a specific 2022 anecdote. It should pivot faster from the "Omnibus" story to the universal institutional constraint.

**The Pitch the Paper Should Have:**
"Every two years, the Twentieth Amendment creates a constitutional 'guillotine' for all pending U.S. legislation. This paper documents a systematic 'deliberation deficit' caused by this deadline: laws enacted in the final 30 days are 10.5 percentage points less likely to receive a recorded roll-call vote, halving the baseline rate of transparency. By exploiting this exogenous institutional clock, I show that the constitutional design itself creates a predictable cycle of diminished democratic accountability."

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**Contribution:** The paper identifies an exogenous institutional source (the 20th Amendment) for the decline of legislative transparency, moving beyond "strategic" or "partisan" explanations for "unorthodox lawmaking."

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It is well-differentiated from Sinclair (2012) by focusing on *timing* as a driver rather than *strategy*. 
*   **World vs. Literature:** It frames itself as a question about the WORLD (how laws are made under pressure), which is its greatest strength.
*   **Explanation:** A smart economist would say: "It’s the paper that shows the Jan 3rd deadline kills roll-call votes." It is not "just another DiD"; it’s a compelling descriptive discovery.
*   **Making it Bigger:** To move the needle for AER, the "So What?" needs more teeth. What is the *cost* of this deficit? Do these late-passed laws get amended more often later? Are they more likely to be challenged in court? Linking "process" to "product quality" is the missing link.

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
*   **Closest Neighbors:** Sinclair (2012) on unorthodox lawmaking; Cox & McCubbins (2005) on agenda setting; Snyder & Groseclose (2000) on roll-call signaling.
*   **Positioning:** It should **synthesize** the "unorthodox lawmaking" literature with "institutional design." It currently "builds," but it could "challenge" by suggesting that even if we fixed partisan polarization, the "deliberation deficit" would remain due to the calendar.
*   **Missing Conversations:** The paper needs to speak to the **Political Economy of Deadlines** (e.g., Gneezy et al. on task completion/quality).
*   **Right Conversation?** Yes, but it feels more like a *Political Science* paper than an *Economics* paper. To make it "AER-economist-friendly," it needs more on the **incentives** of the leadership and the **opportunity cost** of floor time.

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC
*   **Setup:** The U.S. Constitution sets a hard exit date for Congress.
*   **Tension:** As the date approaches, the opportunity cost of time skyrockets. Do we pass more stuff poorly, or less stuff well?
*   **Resolution:** We pass stuff poorly (no recorded votes, no conferences).
*   **Implications:** Our "most important" last-minute deals are often the ones with the least individual accountability.

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong. It is a "story" paper, not just a "results" paper.

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
*   **The Fact:** "One in ten laws normally gets a roll call; at the end of the session, that rate is cut in half, regardless of how important the bill is."
*   **Reaction:** People will lean in. It confirms a cynical suspicion with hard data.
*   **Follow-up:** "Do the 'bad' laws passed in the dark actually fail more often?" 
*   **Null Results:** The placebo (mid-session) is excellent and vital; it proves this isn't just about "hurrying" in general, but specifically about the *expiration* of power.

---

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
*   **Front-loading:** The paper is well-structured. Table 1 and 2 are the stars.
*   **Appendix:** The "Standardized Effect Sizes" (Table 5) feel a bit like filler and could move to an appendix. 
*   **Mechanisms:** I want to see a section on **Bill Salience**. Does this happen to "Must-pass" bills only, or everything? The author touches on "naming bills," but a more granular look at bill importance (using page counts or media mentions) would be better.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The gap is **Ambition**. Currently, it's a very high-quality "fact-check" of congressional procedure. To be AER, it needs to show that this matters for **outcomes**.

**Single Most Impactful Advice:** 
Show me the **consequences** of the deficit: do bills passed in the final 30 days have a higher rate of technical corrections in the *next* Congress, or do they correlate with higher market volatility/uncertainty? If "deliberation" is low, the "product" should be worse—prove it.
