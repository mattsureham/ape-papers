## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
The paper evaluates whether state-level Medicaid reimbursement for doula services improves population-level birth outcomes. Despite clinical evidence showing doulas drastically reduce C-section rates for individuals, this paper finds that policy adoption across eight states has had a near-zero impact on statewide maternal health metrics. It introduces the concept of the "coverage-to-care gap" to explain why financial coverage fails to translate into clinical results due to supply-side constraints and low take-up.

**Evaluation:** The paper actually does a commendable job of articulating this in the first two paragraphs. It immediately hooks the reader with the high stakes of C-sections and the "one-in-three" Medicaid statistic. However, the first two paragraphs could be punchier by explicitly framing the "coverage-to-care gap" not just as a doula problem, but as a fundamental challenge for all Medicaid expansions.

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
The contribution is the empirical demonstration that expanding insurance coverage for a high-efficacy clinical service (doulas) fails to move population-level outcomes because of massive implementation and supply-side frictions.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It is well-differentiated. While Kozhimannil et al. (2013/2016) show the *potential* of doulas for Medicaid recipients, this is the first "macro" evaluation of the actual policy rollout.
*   **Question about the WORLD:** It successfully frames this as a question about why policy "leaks." It’s not just a gap in the literature; it’s a warning to the 20+ states currently implementing these programs.
*   **The "Smart Economist" Test:** A reader would explain this as: "Doulas work in trials, but the Medicaid policy failed in the real world because the reimbursement is too low and no one knows about it." 
*   **How to make it bigger:** The paper needs to push harder on the *mechanism* of the failure. To make this an AER-level contribution, the author should use the variation in reimbursement rates ($450 vs $3,000) mentioned on page 4 more formally. If the $3,000 states (CA) show movement while the $450 states (FL) don't, the paper moves from a "null result" to a "theory of optimal reimbursement."

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
The paper is currently a "Health Economics" paper. To be an "AER" paper, it needs to be a "Public Economics" paper.

*   **Closest Neighbors:** Wherry et al. (2016) on Medicaid expansions; Dave et al. (2015) on take-up; and the clinical doula literature (Bohren et al. 2017).
*   **Positioning:** It currently "joins a growing literature." It should instead seek to *anchor* the literature on supply-side constraints in social insurance.
*   **Missing Conversations:** The paper should speak more to the **Labor Economics** of the "thin market." Why don't doulas enter? Is it just the rate, or is it the "Medicaid administrative burden" (the "Sludge" literature)?
*   **Unexpected Connection:** Connecting this to the literature on **"Orphan Drugs"** or **"Service Deserts"**—where price signals fail to induce supply—would elevate the narrative.

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC
*   **Setup:** Doulas are a "miracle drug" for birth outcomes; Medicaid started paying for them to save lives and money.
*   **Tension:** The "miracle" hasn't happened. The numbers haven't budged.
*   **Resolution:** The "Coverage-to-Care Gap." Financial access $\neq$ Functional access.
*   **Implications:** Coverage is a necessary but insufficient condition for health equity.

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong but the "Resolution" is currently a bit of a "shrug." The author concludes that "more data is needed" or "states must pair reimbursement with investment." To be AER-worthy, the resolution needs to be more assertive: the paper should prove *why* the gap exists (e.g., by correlating state-level reimbursement rates or doula registries with the point estimates).

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
At a dinner party, I’d lead with: *"States are spending millions to cover doulas because they reduce C-sections by 40%, but the actual effect on the population is exactly zero."*
*   **Reaction:** People will lean in. It’s a classic "policy failure" story.
*   **Follow-up:** *"Is it because doulas don't work, or because no one is actually using the benefit?"* 
*   **The Null Result:** The null here is highly interesting because it contradicts the very strong clinical consensus. It’s a "precisely estimated zero" on a high-stakes topic.

---

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
*   **Front-loading:** The discussion of the $450 vs $3,263 reimbursement (page 4) is the most interesting part of the institutional background. This should be a central part of the analysis, not just a footnote.
*   **Appendix:** The methodological defense of Callaway-Sant'Anna is necessary but can be streamlined. 
*   **New Section:** A section explicitly attempting to measure "Take-up" (even with proxy data like Google Trends or state-level Medicaid claims if available) is essential. Without it, we don't know if the doulas failed or if the policy failed to produce doulas.

---

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
**The Gap:** Currently, it's a very high-quality "Policy Evaluation" that belongs in *JPE* or *JHE*. To make it an *AER* paper, it needs more **General Interest Theory.**

**The Single Most Impactful Advice:** Shift from saying "there is a gap" to "explaining the elasticity of supply." If the author can show that the treatment effect scales with the *generosity* of the reimbursement, the paper becomes a foundational study on how to price social services to ensure they actually reach the poor.
