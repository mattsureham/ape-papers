## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper examines the impact of the 1924 Johnson-Reed Act—the largest immigration restriction in U.S. history—on the upward occupational mobility of native-born workers. Using a linked census panel of 10 million men, it asks whether removing millions of unskilled foreign competitors allowed natives to climb the economic ladder. This is a first-order question because the "lump of labor" intuition remains the primary political justification for modern immigration restriction.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this pitch exceptionally well. The opening hook regarding the Italian quota (91% cut) is visceral, and the "factory gate" metaphor provides a clear narrative anchor. It identifies the tension between intuition and evidence immediately. No rewrite of the pitch is necessary.

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:** The paper provides the first individual-level panel evidence that the massive 1924 immigration supply shock failed to induce occupational upgrading for native workers, instead depressing local economic dynamism (homeownership).

**Evaluation:**
- **Differentiation:** It is clearly differentiated from **Tabellini (2020)** by moving from aggregate city-level data to an individual linked panel, and from **Ager and Hansen (2017)** or **Abramitzky et al.** by focusing specifically on the native response to restriction rather than immigrant assimilation.
- **World vs. Literature:** It is framed as a question about the world: "Does restriction help natives?" This is the strongest possible framing for a top-tier journal.
- **Narrative vs. Method:** A smart economist would describe this as "the definitive evidence that the 1924 quotas didn't help natives move up," rather than "a DiD paper."
- **Bigger contribution:** To make this even bigger, the author should lean harder into the "General Equilibrium" cost. The homeownership result is currently a "side finding" in Section 5.2, but it is actually the key to the story. If restriction didn't just fail to help, but actually *hurt* local demand, that is a much more powerful AER-level story than a simple null.

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

The paper sits at the intersection of Economic History and Labor Economics.

- **Closest Neighbors:** Tabellini (2020) on 1920s immigration; Peri (2012) on complementarity; Abramitzky, Boustan, & Eriksson on historical record linkage; and Clemens et al. (2018) on the Bracero exclusion.
- **Positioning:** The paper correctly builds on Tabellini and Peri. It uses the modern linkage technology of Abramitzky et al. to solve a measurement problem that previous generations couldn't.
- **Missing Conversations:** The paper could better connect to the **Urban/Geography** literature. If native mobility is hindered by a lack of local demand (as suggested by the homeownership result), the paper should speak to the "Spatial Equilibrium" literature (e.g., Moretti, Diamond).
- **The Right Conversation?** Yes. It is refuting a central tenet of restrictionist political economy using the highest-quality data available.

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

- **Setup:** The Age of Mass Migration has created a massive foreign-born workforce competing with natives.
- **Tension:** Politicians pass the 1924 Act to "protect" native jobs. If the "lump of labor" theory holds, we should see natives flooding into the vacancies left by Italians and Poles.
- **Resolution:** We see exactly zero upgrading. In fact, the counties that were doing best (complementarity) saw their momentum stalled.
- **Implications:** Restriction is not a shortcut to native prosperity; it is a demand-side shock that stifles local growth.

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong. However, the "Resolution" is currently framed as a "precisely estimated null." In the AER, a "null" is often less exciting than a "negative." The author should frame the arc around the **disruption of complementarity**.

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

**The Dinner Party Fact:** "When the U.S. cut Italian immigration by 90% in 1924, not a single native-born American in those affected counties moved into a better job as a result—but they did stop buying houses."

**The Reaction:** People lean in. The "lump of labor" is so intuitive that a precise 10-million-person refutation is shocking. 

**Follow-up:** "Why didn't they move? Did the jobs just disappear?" This points to the need for the author to emphasize the mechanism (complementarity vs. demand).

---

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

- **Front-loading:** The paper is well-structured, but Table 1 is a bit dry. A map showing the "Quota Exposure" would be a much better visual for the first 5 pages to show the reader the "treatment" they are buying into.
- **Appendix:** Move the "Standardized Effect Sizes" (Table 7) into the main text of Section 5.1. In a "null" paper, the most important thing is proving you have the power to see an effect if it existed.
- **Conclusion:** The conclusion is a bit brief. It should more aggressively tie the 1924 findings to modern debates (H-1B, etc.) to cement the paper’s relevance.

---

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The gap is **Ambition/Scope.** Right now, it is a very high-quality "No" to a simple question. To be a "slam dunk" AER paper, it needs to explain the **General Equilibrium** consequences more deeply. 

**The single most impactful piece of advice:** Elevate the "Homeownership/Local Demand" result from a secondary finding to a primary pillar of the paper's narrative. Don't just show that restriction *failed to help*; show that it *actively harmed* the local economic ecosystem by destroying the demand-side benefits of a growing population.
