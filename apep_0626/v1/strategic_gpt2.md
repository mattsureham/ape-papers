## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple, policy-relevant question: when the United States sharply restricted immigration in 1924, did native-born workers move into better occupations? Using linked census records for about 10 million native-born men and cross-county variation in exposure to the quota shock, the paper says no: closing the door to immigrants did not improve natives’ occupational mobility, and may have reduced local economic dynamism as reflected in lower homeownership transitions.

A busy economist should care because this is one of the cleanest historical settings in which immigration restriction was large, sudden, and politically salient. If even here restriction does not generate native upgrading, that matters for how economists think about immigration, labor complementarity, and the perennial political claim that fewer immigrants means better jobs for natives.

**Does the paper articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Mostly, but not optimally. The current opening is vivid and readable, but it gets pulled too quickly into institutional detail and empirical strategy. The paper’s best asset is not that quotas for Italians fell by 91 percent; it is that this episode lets the author speak directly to a durable claim about the world: whether shutting out immigrants helps natives climb the job ladder.

**What the first two paragraphs should say instead:**  
The introduction should lead with the broader question and the answer, not the quota arithmetic. Something like:

> Immigration restriction is often justified by a straightforward claim: if immigrants compete with natives for jobs, shutting off immigration should improve natives’ labor-market opportunities. This paper tests that claim in perhaps the most consequential historical episode of immigration restriction in U.S. history, the 1924 Johnson-Reed Act, which drastically reduced inflows from Southern and Eastern Europe.

> Linking 10 million native-born men across the 1920 and 1930 censuses and exploiting cross-county variation in exposure to the groups most affected by the quotas, I find that restriction did not push natives into better occupations. The main effect on occupational upgrading is essentially zero, while homeownership transitions fall in more exposed places. The broader implication is that reducing immigration did not deliver the occupational gains restrictionists promised, and may instead have dampened local economic dynamism.

That is the pitch the paper should have.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution in one sentence:**  
The paper shows that the largest immigration restriction shock in U.S. history did not improve native-born men’s occupational mobility in more exposed local labor markets.

That is a potentially good contribution. But in its current form, the paper does not yet differentiate that contribution sharply enough from adjacent work.

### Is the contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?
Not fully. The introduction cites the right broad literatures, but the differentiation is still generic: “historical setting,” “large shock,” “individual linked data.” That is not enough. A smart economist could still summarize this as “another local immigration paper, but historical and using linked census data.”

The paper needs to state more crisply what exactly is new relative to the nearest neighbors:

- **Tabellini (2020, AER)**: immigration exposure improved natives’ outcomes in early 20th-century cities. This paper is not about immigration inflows; it is about **restriction** and whether shutting off immigration helps natives. That is the natural mirror-image policy question, and the author should lean into that.
- **Sequeira, Nunn, and Qian (2020, Review of Economic Studies)**: historical immigrants generated long-run local gains. This paper is about **short-run individual mobility of natives after restriction**, not long-run place effects.
- **Peri and Ottaviano / Peri and Sparber**: modern immigration may induce task specialization and complementarity. This paper offers a **historical test under a massive negative supply shock**.
- **Boustan (2009)** and related work on immigration and internal labor-market adjustment: this paper is about **occupational mobility of native men**, not just wages, employment, or racial displacement.

### World question or literature gap?
The paper mostly frames itself around a **world question**, which is good: does restricting immigration help natives? That is the right instinct. But it sometimes slips back into “this contributes to several literatures” mode, which weakens it. The world question is stronger and should dominate.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?
At present: somewhat, but not sharply. They would probably say: “It’s a linked-census paper on the 1924 quotas showing no occupational gains for natives.” That’s decent, but still sounds like a competent application rather than an AER-level conceptual contribution.

To elevate it, the paper needs a more memorable novelty claim:
- not “I use big linked data,” but
- “I test the central political promise of immigration restriction in the canonical U.S. restriction episode—and it fails.”

That is much stronger.

### What would make this contribution bigger?
A few possibilities:

1. **Reframe around restriction, not immigration.**  
   The paper’s distinctive angle is not just another immigration shock; it is a direct test of what immigration **restriction** achieves. That framing is underexploited.

2. **Promote the homeownership result if it is real and central.**  
   Right now it feels tacked on, but strategically it may be the bridge from “null on occupations” to “restriction had visible economic costs.” If the paper can make this the first non-labor-market margin of adjustment—local demand, housing, construction, neighborhood dynamism—it becomes more than a null paper.

3. **Make occupational mobility more conceptually rich.**  
   “OCCSCORE moved by zero” is dry. “Restriction did not move natives from laboring to craft, clerical, supervisory, or owner-occupied status” is more compelling. A bigger contribution would turn occupational upgrading into an interpretable labor-market ladder.

4. **Lean harder into complementarity as the substantive result.**  
   The introduction currently says null post-period + positive pre-period suggests complementarity. That may be the economic story the paper wants to tell, but it is not yet fully developed as the paper’s contribution. If that is the story, build the paper around it.

**Build on and invert**, not attack.

The best positioning is:

- This paper asks the reverse policy question: when immigration is forcefully cut off, do natives gain?
- The answer is no, which complements those papers and bears directly on a live policy argument.

It should not posture as overturning the whole immigration literature. That would feel overbroad and invite skepticism. Its sweet spot is: **historical restriction as a decisive test of a prominent mechanism**.

### Is the paper positioned too narrowly or too broadly?
It is oddly both.

- **Too narrowly** in the sense that it reads like a historical labor paper about the 1924 Act and occupational income scores.
- **Too broadly** when it gestures at “the debate over immigration harms native workers” in general without clearly defining what margin it speaks to.

The right audience is not just economic historians. It should speak to labor economists, migration economists, and political economists interested in restriction. But to do that, it needs a tighter core message.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?
Two literatures feel underused:

1. **The economics of immigration restriction / policy backlash / political economy of exclusion.**  
   The paper should speak more directly to work on why immigration restrictions arise and what they deliver, not only to the labor-demand side of immigration.

2. **Spatial equilibrium / local demand / housing-market adjustment.**  
   Since the homeownership result is the one non-null finding, the paper should connect to literatures on how migration shapes local housing demand, construction, and urban growth. Right now that result hangs on a single citation and feels under-theorized.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?
Not quite yet. The current conversation is “historical immigration and native outcomes.” The more impactful conversation is:

> “What does immigration restriction actually buy for natives?”

That is the right conversation, and it is more distinctive.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Before the paper: immigration restriction is commonly defended on the grounds that fewer immigrants should improve opportunities for native workers, especially in lower-skill occupations.

### Tension
We have lots of evidence on immigration inflows, but less direct evidence on whether a sharp, durable restriction actually creates occupational advancement for natives in exposed places. The 1924 Act is the canonical setting to test that political claim.

### Resolution
It does not. Natives in more exposed counties do not experience meaningful occupational upgrading. Meanwhile, homeownership transitions fall, suggesting that restriction may have reduced local dynamism rather than helping natives advance.

### Implications
Economists and policymakers should update away from a simple substitution story. Large-scale immigration restriction did not mechanically open better occupational ladders for natives; if anything, it may have shut down complementary economic activity.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
It has the ingredients, but not a fully controlled arc. Right now the paper is slightly torn between three stories:

1. **A big null result on occupational mobility**
2. **A complementarity story suggested by pre-1924 patterns**
3. **A local-demand / homeownership story**

Those can fit together, but the paper has not yet decided which is the spine. As written, it risks feeling like a collection of related findings surrounding a null main effect.

### What story should it be telling?
The best story is:

> Restriction was sold as a way to help natives move up. In the biggest historical test of that idea, it did not. Instead, the evidence points away from substitution and toward complementarity and lost local dynamism.

That is a coherent narrative. The occupational null is the core fact; the pre-period pattern and homeownership result are supporting pieces, not coequal stories.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?
“After the 1924 immigration quotas slashed the main immigrant inflows, natives in the most exposed counties did not move into better occupations.”

That is the headline.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
Economists would lean in initially because the setting is strong and the question is first-order. But they will only stay engaged if the paper quickly makes clear why this is more than a high-powered null.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Probably: “So what *did* happen instead?”  
That is where the paper currently needs more discipline. If the answer is “nothing on occupations, but less homeownership and maybe lost complementarity,” then the paper must present that as a coherent substantive implication, not as an appendix-worthy side result.

### If the findings are null or modest: is the null itself interesting?
Yes—potentially very interesting. This is exactly the kind of setting where one might expect restriction to matter if the standard political rhetoric were right. A null in a weak setting is forgettable; a null in a powerful, canonical setting can be important.

But the paper must make the case more sharply:
- why this null is informative,
- why this is the right margin on which restriction should have shown up,
- and what beliefs should change because of it.

Right now it comes close, but it still sometimes sounds like “we found no effect” rather than “we tested the central promised benefit of restriction and it failed to materialize.”

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

2. **Move empirical strategy language out of the introduction.**  
   The intro currently spends too much real estate on “continuous-treatment difference-in-differences,” sample size, and power language. Some is fine, but too much makes the paper feel technical before it feels important.

3. **Front-load the result and why it matters.**  
   The best line in the paper is essentially “closing the golden door did not open the factory gate.” The paper should earn that line immediately and then organize the rest around it.

4. **Bring the homeownership result forward if it is part of the core story.**  
   It appears in the abstract and intro, so it is clearly meant to matter. If so, it should not feel like a secondary outcome buried after several nulls. It should be integrated as the main implication of “what happened instead.”

5. **Be careful with the placebo section narratively.**  
   Without getting into identification, the paper’s current rhetorical handling is awkward. It says the placebo “supports” one thing and then the table shows a strong positive pre-period relationship. That creates confusion. Strategically, the paper should stop calling this a placebo that supports the design and instead call it **evidence on pre-restriction complementarity** or **evidence on the pre-1924 trajectory in immigrant-dense places**. That is a narrative fix more than a technical one.

6. **Cut the power discussion in the introduction by half.**  
   One sentence on precision is enough. Multiple sentences on minimum detectable effects and confidence intervals read defensively.

7. **The conclusion is mostly summary.**  
   It should do more interpretive work. A stronger conclusion would say what the paper changes in the broader debate over immigration restriction.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

At present, the gap is mostly **framing and ambition**, with some **scope** issues.

### Framing problem?
Yes, definitely. The science may be competent, but the paper has not yet found the highest-value framing. Its core asset is not “individual-level linked census evidence.” Its core asset is that it speaks directly to the central promised benefit of immigration restriction in the most important historical U.S. episode.

### Scope problem?
Somewhat. The main result is a null on one primary labor-market margin. For AER, that can work only if the paper either:
- makes that margin feel unquestionably central, or
- broadens the picture of what restriction did instead.

The homeownership result hints at a broader equilibrium story, but right now it is underdeveloped. If the paper could make a compelling two-part contribution—**no occupational gains, plus evidence of reduced local dynamism**—it becomes much more publishable at the very top.

### Novelty problem?
Not fatal, but present. There is a lot of work on historical immigration and native outcomes. What saves this paper is the **restriction** angle. It should exploit that much more aggressively.

### Ambition problem?
Yes. The paper feels a bit too safe and a bit too pleased with being large-N. AER papers need to do more than document a careful null; they need to change the terms of the conversation. This paper could, if it more boldly stakes out the claim that immigration restriction failed on its own stated objective.

### Single most impactful advice
**Rebuild the paper around the question “What did immigration restriction actually deliver for natives?” and make the answer—not technical design, not sample size—the organizing principle of the introduction and results.**

That one change would force better choices everywhere else: what to emphasize, what to cut, which literature to foreground, and how to interpret the null.
