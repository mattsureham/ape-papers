## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple, important question: when the 1924 Johnson-Reed Act sharply curtailed immigration from Southern and Eastern Europe, did native-born American workers move into better jobs? Using linked census records for more than 10 million native-born men, the paper’s headline claim is no: one of the largest immigration restrictions in U.S. history did not generate occupational upgrading for natives, and may instead have reduced local economic dynamism.

A busy economist should care because the paper speaks directly to one of the oldest and still most politically salient claims in labor economics and political economy: whether shutting out immigrants helps incumbent workers. The historical setting is potentially powerful because the policy shock was large, salient, and central to American economic history.

### Does the paper articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Mostly, but not optimally. The current opening is vivid and readable, but the introduction then drifts too quickly into design language and too early into “continuous-treatment DiD” exposition. More importantly, the paper has **two competing stories**:

1. “A huge immigration restriction did not help natives.”
2. “Immigration may have been complementary, and the restriction disrupted beneficial local dynamics.”

Those are related, but the introduction has not yet decided which is the main story. Right now it wants both the clean null and the complementarity reinterpretation, and the result is a slightly unstable pitch.

### The pitch the paper should have

Here is what the first two paragraphs should say instead:

> The central political promise of immigration restriction is straightforward: if fewer immigrants arrive, native workers should face less competition and move into better jobs. The 1924 Johnson-Reed Act offers an unusually stark test of that claim. By slashing immigration from Southern and Eastern Europe, it imposed one of the largest and most durable immigration restrictions in U.S. history.
>
> This paper asks whether native-born workers in places most exposed to that restriction experienced greater upward mobility during the 1920s. Linking more than 10 million native-born men across the 1920 and 1930 censuses, I find they did not. The headline result is a precise absence of occupational gains for natives in more exposed counties, alongside suggestive evidence that restriction reduced broader local economic dynamism. The historical lesson is that closing the door to immigrants did not deliver the labor-market benefits restrictionists promised.

That is the AER pitch: a first-order question about the world, a historically consequential policy, huge data, and a clear overturning of a common presumption.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper’s contribution is to show, using linked individual census data around the 1924 Immigration Act, that one of the largest immigration restrictions in U.S. history did not improve native occupational mobility in more exposed local labor markets.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest papers?

Not yet clearly enough. The introduction cites the right broad literatures, but the differentiation is still a bit generic: “historical setting,” “large shock,” “individual linked data,” “corroborates complementarity.” That is not enough on its own because a smart reader can still walk away thinking: “this is another immigration-shock paper finding little harm to natives.”

The paper needs sharper differentiation from at least four strands:

- **Tabellini (2020)** on the economic and political effects of historical immigration to U.S. cities.
- **Abramitzky, Boustan, Eriksson and coauthors** on immigrant assimilation and long-run historical labor markets using linked census data.
- **Card / Peri / Ottaviano-type immigration incidence papers** arguing limited adverse impacts on natives.
- **Boustan (2009)** and adjacent work on historical labor-market competition/complementarity involving immigrants and Black workers.

Right now the novelty is partly there, but not packaged tightly enough. The real differentiator is not just “I use a historical shock.” It is:

- a **major policy-induced immigration cutoff**, not an inflow episode;
- **individual mobility outcomes**, not just local employment/wage aggregates;
- a test of the **restrictionist counterfactual promise** rather than the effect of immigration itself.

That framing is much stronger.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a literature gap?

It starts with a world question, which is good. But by the middle of the introduction it slips into literature-recitation mode. The strongest version of this paper is not “I extend X with linked data.” It is “Here is a historically decisive test of whether immigration restriction improves native upward mobility.”

That world-facing framing is absolutely the right one. The paper should lean harder into it.

### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what’s new?

At present, only imperfectly. They would probably say:

> “It’s a paper on the 1924 Immigration Act using linked census data; it finds no occupational upgrading for natives.”

That is decent, but still perilously close to “another paper on immigration and natives.” What they should instead be able to say is:

> “It tests the canonical political claim behind immigration restriction using the biggest historical restriction shock we have, and finds that natives didn’t move up.”

That is cleaner and more memorable.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Three possibilities:

1. **Shift the center of gravity from occupational scores to broader economic adjustment.**  
   The homeownership result may actually be more provocative than the null occupational result because it suggests a real cost of restriction. If there are other local-demand or household-balance-sheet margins available, that would make the paper feel larger.

2. **Make the paper explicitly about the failure of restrictionist promises, not just complementarity.**  
   That is a bolder and more policy-resonant framing. “Did restriction deliver?” is bigger than “Are immigrants complements?”

3. **Clarify mechanism through task structure / occupational hierarchy.**  
   If the paper can show more directly that immigrant-heavy places were precisely where natives historically climbed into supervisory, skilled, or clerical jobs, then the pre/post contrast becomes a bigger story. Right now the complementarity claim is plausible, but not yet narratively fully earned.

- Build on Peri/Ottaviano by testing complementarity in a dramatic historical restriction episode.
- Reframe the modern immigration debate by saying: this is not another setting-specific marginal shock; it is a test of the central political claim behind restriction.

An “attack” posture would be wrong unless the paper had much more definitive evidence overturning specific prior estimates. It does not need to do that.

Somewhat too broadly in citation, but too narrowly in felt audience.

The intro currently names several literatures, but the paper still feels like it is mainly speaking to economic historians and immigration economists. For AER, it should more explicitly speak to:

- labor economists interested in adjustment to supply shocks,
- political economists of backlash and policy promises,
- macro/regional economists interested in local equilibrium and demand spillovers.

It should probably engage more explicitly with:

- **Labor-market adjustment and local shocks** beyond immigration per se.

- **General equilibrium / local demand spillovers** literature, since the homeownership result points in that direction.

There is also an underused connection to the literature on **whether negative labor supply shocks mechanically raise native outcomes**. That is a broader economics question than immigration alone.

Almost, but not quite. The highest-impact conversation is not “another estimate in the immigration-and-natives literature.” It is:

> “Do large immigration restrictions actually deliver labor-market gains to natives, or do they mainly destroy complementary and demand-side benefits?”

That conversation is larger and more current.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Before this paper, the prevailing public intuition is that immigrants compete with natives, so restricting immigration should improve natives’ labor-market prospects. Economists, meanwhile, have often found limited adverse impacts of immigration and some evidence of complementarity, but most evidence comes from modern inflow shocks and aggregate outcomes.

### Tension

The 1924 Act provides a rare, large, clear-cut restriction shock. If the restrictionist logic is right anywhere, it should show up here. But it is not obvious whether natives actually benefited, particularly on individual upward mobility rather than wages or employment aggregates.

### Resolution

The paper finds no occupational upgrading for natives in more exposed places. It also finds a negative effect on homeownership transitions and reports pre-1924 patterns consistent with immigrant-heavy places having previously delivered more native upgrading.

### Implications

The implication is that cutting immigration did not produce the promised occupational gains for natives and may have reduced local economic vitality. More broadly, labor markets may be characterized less by simple immigrant-native substitution than by complementarity and equilibrium spillovers.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the ingredients, but not yet the discipline. Right now it reads partly like a results package in search of a settled story.

The biggest narrative problem is the treatment of the placebo/pre-trend evidence. The paper initially says:

- a null placebo would support the assumption,

but then finds a **positive placebo** and tries to convert that into substantive evidence of complementarity. That may ultimately be the right interpretation, but narratively it creates whiplash. In the introduction the paper even says “The positive pre-trend and null post-trend together suggest restriction disrupted a beneficial labor market dynamic,” which is interesting, but then later it still sometimes talks as if the placebo supported design assumptions. That inconsistency weakens the story.

### What story should it be telling?

The story should be:

1. Restrictionists promised occupational gains for natives.
2. The 1924 Act was a real-world test of that promise.
3. Those gains did not materialize.
4. In fact, immigrant-heavy places had previously been places of native advancement, suggesting the policy interrupted a complementary growth process.

That is a coherent narrative. But then the paper must fully embrace that this is **not** a clean “placebo passed” story. It is a “historical dynamics suggest these were exactly the places where immigration was part of upward mobility” story. Once embraced, that is actually more interesting.

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

### What fact would I lead with?

I would lead with:

> “When the U.S. slammed the door on Southern and Eastern European immigration in 1924, natives in the most exposed places did not move into better occupations.”

That is the dinner-party line.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

Economists would lean in at first because the setting is big and the question is first-order. The 1924 Act is historically important, and the policy relevance is obvious.

But the second sentence matters enormously. If the next sentence is too technical, phones come out. If the next sentence is:

> “And the paper suggests those places may actually have lost economic dynamism instead,”

people stay interested.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Probably:

- “Is the paper saying immigration was complementary?”
- “How do you know this isn’t just that immigrant-dense counties were different growth places?”
- “What exactly adjusted if not occupations?”
- “Is the homeownership result the more important one?”

Those are all signs that the paper has a live conversation available.

### If the findings are null or modest, is the null itself interesting?

Yes — in principle very much so. A null can absolutely be AER-worthy when it comes from a huge, historically central, policy-relevant setting that should have generated effects if the standard claim were right.

But the paper needs to make the null feel like a **definitive answer to an important claim**, not just “I estimated zero very precisely.” The current draft sometimes oversells “precision” instead of selling the importance of the claim being tested.

The best way to make the null interesting is:

- emphasize that this was a **massive policy intervention**;
- emphasize that the predicted sign was central to restrictionist rhetoric;
- show that not only is there no gain, but there are hints of actual costs.

That transforms the null from absence-of-evidence to substantive evidence against an influential worldview.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

This is not primarily an identification memo, so on strategic grounds the main gap is:

### Mostly a framing problem, secondarily a scope/ambition problem.

The paper has the bones of an AER paper because:

- the question is big,
- the historical event is major,
- the data are unusually large and interesting,
- the headline is policy-relevant and potentially surprising.

But in current form it still reads more like a strong field-journal paper than a must-read general-interest paper. Why? Because it has not fully decided what its conceptual payload is.

Is it:
- a paper about the labor-market effects of immigration restriction?
- a paper about occupational mobility?
- a paper about complementarity?
- a paper about the failure of restrictionist economic promises?
- a paper about general-equilibrium losses from immigration cuts?

At present it is trying to be all of them at once.

### The gap between current form and a paper that excites the top 10 people in the field

Top people would likely say: “Interesting setting, good scale, plausible headline — but what is the paper’s one big idea?”

The answer should be:

> “A historically massive immigration restriction did not produce the incumbent-worker gains it was supposed to produce.”

Everything else should serve that. The complementarity evidence and homeownership evidence should be marshaled as explanation and implications, not competing main contributions.

### Is it a novelty problem?

Not exactly. The question is not wholly new, but the setting and lens are sufficiently distinctive that novelty is not the primary issue.

### Is it a scope problem?

Somewhat. If the paper wants to be more than a precise null on occupational scores, it likely needs a somewhat richer picture of what did adjust — housing, mobility, sectoral transitions, local demand, household formation, or other margins. Right now the one negative homeownership result does a lot of conceptual work by itself.

### Is it an ambition problem?

A little. The paper is competent and readable, but there is an element of “safe execution of an obvious design.” AER papers need either a bigger conceptual claim or a more striking empirical architecture. The setting gives it that chance, but the writing has not fully claimed it.

### Single most impactful piece of advice

**Reframe the paper around a single bold question — whether one of the largest immigration restrictions in U.S. history delivered the promised gains to native workers — and make every section serve that claim, with the pre/post complementarity evidence and homeownership result used as supporting interpretation rather than as parallel stories.**
