**Summary**

This paper studies whether the 1924 Immigration Quota Act induced native-born occupational upgrading by exploiting county-level exposure to cuts in Southern and Eastern European immigration and tracking 10 million native-born men across the 1920 and 1930 censuses. Using a continuous-treatment DiD with state and baseline-occupation fixed effects, the author finds precisely estimated null effects on occupational income scores and related mobility outcomes, while finding that quota exposure reduced native homeownership transitions. A 1910–1920 placebo and robustness checks support the credibility of the identifying assumptions.

**Essential Points**

1. **Parallel Trends and Functional Form:** The identifying assumption rests on the exposure variable being exogenous after conditioning on controls. Yet \Cref{tab:placebo} shows a large, significant raw effect in 1910–1920 (columns appear to report different exposures), which raises questions about pre-existing correlations. The paper interprets the placebo as null, but the reported coefficient of 10.41 (column 1) is both large and significant. Please clarify whether this specification conditions on the same fixed effects as the main regressions and why that result should not undermine identification. If that estimate persists with the full set of controls, it suggests exposure is correlated with other trends, so the main DiD may not be clean.

2. **Treatment Variation and Comparison Group:** The logic rests on comparing counties with more versus fewer restricted-origin immigrants. Yet high-exposure counties are very different (urban, high baseline OCCSCORE). Even with occupation FE, treatment varies across non-random counties, potentially confounding the causal interpretation. Consider (a) showing event-study-like evidence (or a flexible function of exposure interacted with time) using aggregate data to demonstrate parallelism over shorter intervals, (b) adding county-level time trends or more granular controls (e.g., county-level manufacturing employment shares or industrial composition from 1920), or (c) instrumenting for exposure using earlier settlement patterns (e.g., machine distance from entry ports). Without such checks, it is hard to rule out that the null reflects offsetting trends rather than the causal effect of immigration restriction.

3. **Interpretation of the Null:** The paper concludes that immigration restriction failed to benefit natives and even hurt homeownership. Yet the null estimate, while precisely zero, still allows for small positive or negative effects within the confidence interval. Moreover, the first-stage reduction in immigrant inflows is described qualitatively but not quantified in the regression context. Please provide (a) evidence that higher exposure indeed led to larger declines in immigrant inflows (first-stage) to support the interpretation of null as causal, and (b) perhaps bounds or power calculations framed in terms of substantively meaningful occupational gains to contextualize the null—if the reduction in immigrant labor was large, even small coefficients might imply little effect; but this needs to be made explicit.

**Suggestions**

1. **Clarify the Placebo Results:** As noted, columns (1) and (3) in \Cref{tab:placebo} show significant coefficients for 1910–1920 when using 1920 exposure. If these estimates disappear once you include the occupation fixed effects and controls (matching the main specification), that should be emphasized. If not, the main specification needs to address potential spurious correlations. Consider reestimating the placebo with the exact same set of fixed effects and controls as in column (4) of \Cref{tab:main}, and present this side-by-side. This will bolster the claim that there were no differential trends.

2. **First-Stage Evidence or Event Study:** The manuscript should more explicitly document the variation in immigrant inflows post-1924. For instance, show that counties with high 1920 exposure experienced larger declines in new restricted-origin arrivals in the 1930 census (or in earlier years if available) relative to controls. A table or figure illustrating the relationship between exposure and immigrant inflow declines would make the shock’s magnitude concrete and reassure readers about the treatment’s relevance.

3. **Mechanism and Composition:** The null on OCCSCORE is interesting, but that score may be measured with error or insufficiently sensitive to actual wage or occupational quality changes. While the paper also examines farm exits and mobility, consider adding (if data allow) changes in industry or class of worker, average hours, or any wage-related variable. If richer outcomes are unavailable, a discussion of measurement noise in OCCSCORE and its implications for attenuation bias would help; the appendix could report placebo estimates for these alternative outcomes as well.

4. **Local Economic Conditions and Dynamics:** To address concerns about confounders, incorporate additional county-level controls or heterogeneity analyses. For example, interact quota exposure with baseline industrial concentration (manufacturing vs. agriculture) or with immigration intensity in neighboring counties. This would help to show that the null holds across different economic contexts, mitigating concerns that high-exposure counties simply followed different secular paths.

5. **Homeownership Finding:** The negative impact on homeownership transitions is intriguing. Expand on it by discussing potential mechanisms (demand vs. credit vs. housing supply). Could the fall in homeownership be driven by reduced construction activity, lower total income, or different mortgage access? If possible, provide supporting evidence (e.g., changes in housing occupancy of immigrants, housing starts, or municipal finance). Alternatively, situate the finding in the literature on immigration and local demand to make the interpretation more compelling.

6. **Communication of Effect Sizes:** The main text notes that a one-standard-deviation increase in exposure implies a change of 0.006 standard deviations in OCCSCORE. While this is very small, some readers might benefit from seeing this expressed in more intuitive employment or wage terms—for example, how would such a change compare to moving from a skilled to an unskilled occupation? The appendix table is helpful; consider moving a brief version into the main text or caption to make the null more salient.

7. **Robustness to Alternative Clustering:** The paper clusters standard errors at the county level (and reports state clustering). Given the treatment is measured at the county level and the regressions use individual data, consider clustering at a higher level (e.g., county-year or state-county) or using multi-way clustering explicitly (county × year). Also, since the treatment varies only at the county level, a randomization inference approach or wild cluster bootstrap might strengthen inference given the large number of counties.

8. **Discussion of General Equilibrium Interpretation:** The paper interprets the null as evidence of complementarity. To make this argument sharper, discuss alternative explanations (e.g., natives may have responded via wage adjustments, hours, or geographic moves not fully captured by OCCSCORE). Additionally, the homeownership result suggests a contraction in demand; consider linking this back to complementarity by explaining how immigrant demand for housing could affect native occupational choices (e.g., via local demand for goods and services).

Overall, the paper addresses an important question with rich data and a clean research design. Addressing the above points—especially clarifying pre-trends, documenting first-stage variation, and elaborating on mechanisms—will greatly strengthen the causal claims and the contribution to the immigration literature.
