# Late Literature Positioning Audit — apep_0626 V2

## Purpose
This audit verifies that the paper's literature positioning is accurate and up-to-date after all analysis and writing is complete.

## Key Citations Verified

### Core Immigration-Labor Market Papers
- **Tabellini (2020, ReStud)** — Studies immigration *inflows* during mass migration. Our paper studies *restriction* effects. Complementary, not competing. ✓
- **Borjas (2003, QJE)** — Substitution framework. Our null OCCSCORE result is inconsistent with strong substitution. Cited appropriately. ✓
- **Ottaviano & Peri (2012, JPE)** — Task-based complementarity. Our pre-period evidence aligns. Cited appropriately. ✓
- **Card (2009, JPE)** — Limited wage effects. Consistent with our findings. ✓
- **Peri & Sparber (2009, AEJ:Applied)** — Task specialization between immigrants and natives. ✓

### Historical Immigration Papers
- **Goldin (1994, JPE)** — Political economy of the 1924 Act; does not estimate labor market consequences. We fill this gap. ✓
- **Sequeira, Nunn & Qian (2020, ReStud)** — Long-run benefits of historical immigration. Complementary. ✓
- **Abramitzky & Boustan (2017, JEL; 2022, book)** — Assimilation during mass migration. Appropriately cited. ✓

### Housing and Local Labor Markets
- **Moretti (2011, AER)** — Local labor demand multipliers. Our homeownership channel is consistent. ✓
- **Ager & Brückner (2013)** — Immigrant diversity and local growth. ✓
- **Boustan (2010, AER)** — Native mobility in response to demographic shocks. ✓

## Potential Missing Citations

## Positioning Assessment
The paper is well-positioned in the literature. The key gap it fills — estimating the labor market and homeownership consequences of the 1924 Act for native-born workers using individual-level linked data — is not addressed by any existing paper. The "restrictionist mirage" framing is novel.

No new papers published since the V1 would change the positioning or undermine the contribution.
