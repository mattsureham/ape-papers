## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a historically and politically resonant question: when the 1924 Johnson-Reed Act sharply curtailed immigration from Southern and Eastern Europe, did native-born American workers actually benefit economically in the way restrictionists promised? Using linked census data, the paper argues that the answer is no: there is no detectable occupational upgrading for natives in more exposed counties, and there is evidence of a decline in entry into homeownership, especially for young urban natives.

Why should a busy economist care? Because this is a rare case where the U.S. ran a large-scale, sudden immigration restriction, and the paper tries to evaluate the core empirical claim behind restrictionist labor-market rhetoric: that cutting immigration helps natives move up.

Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs? Mostly, but not optimally. The introduction is forceful and readable, but it is too eager to declare a verdict (“the truth is worse than a broken promise”) before it has crisply established the broader economic question. It also overbrands the result as “restrictionist mirage,” which reads more like op-ed framing than AER framing. The first two paragraphs should more plainly state: this paper studies a major policy shock, asks whether natives gained in the dimensions restrictionists emphasized, and shows that the canonical promised benefit is absent while an unexpected margin worsens.

### The pitch the paper should have

“In 1924, the United States sharply restricted immigration from Southern and Eastern Europe under the Johnson-Reed Act, with proponents arguing that reduced immigrant competition would improve native workers’ economic outcomes. This paper studies that claim using linked census panels covering over 10 million native-born men and county-level variation in exposure to the quota shock.

We find no evidence that natives in more exposed counties experienced greater occupational upgrading between 1920 and 1930. Instead, the main detectable effect is lower transition into homeownership, especially among young and urban natives. The results suggest that one of the most consequential immigration restrictions in U.S. history failed to deliver its central promised labor-market benefit, and may have reduced local economic dynamism instead.”

That is the AER version: cleaner, more world-facing, less sloganized.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### Contribution in one sentence

The paper’s contribution is to provide new evidence that the 1924 immigration quotas did not improve native occupational mobility in more exposed local labor markets, while plausibly reducing entry into homeownership.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?

Partly, but not sharply enough. The paper says it studies “the reverse experiment” relative to inflow papers, which is true, but that is not yet a full differentiation strategy. The closest neighbors are broadly historical immigration papers on inflows, assimilation, and long-run effects; the paper distinguishes itself by focusing on a major restriction episode and short-run native outcomes. That’s a real distinction. But the introduction does not yet make clear why this setting delivers something qualitatively new rather than simply “another local labor market immigration paper, except with negative inflows.”

The differentiation problem is especially acute because the headline main effect is a null on occupational upgrading. A null can be important, but only if the paper makes unmistakably clear why this null is surprising, informative, and decision-relevant. Right now the contribution feels split between:
1. a null on the primary promised margin, and
2. a significant effect on a secondary margin, homeownership.

That can work, but only if the paper makes the second result feel like a central economic consequence rather than a consolation prize.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a gap in a literature?

It is mostly framed as a question about the world, which is good: did immigration restriction actually help natives? That is much stronger than “there is no paper on Johnson-Reed and county occupational mobility.” The paper should lean even harder into the world question and strip back some of the literature-gap language.

### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what’s new?

Right now they could probably say: “It’s a historical immigration paper on the 1924 quotas; they find no occupational gains for natives and maybe some housing downside.” That is decent, but not yet memorable enough for AER. The risk is exactly that the paper sounds like “another shift-share/local exposure paper about immigration,” albeit in a historical setting.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Several ways:

- **Make homeownership the co-equal headline, not an add-on.** If the paper’s real insight is that restriction did not help labor-market advancement but did depress a broader indicator of household economic progress, then that needs to be structured as a deliberate test of competing margins of adjustment, not a secondary finding discovered along the way.
- **Connect more directly to local economic dynamism.** If the real mechanism is complementarity through urban production and housing supply, then outcomes tied to construction, rents, housing stock, building permits (if available historically), local self-employment, household formation, or city growth would make the contribution much larger.
- **Sharper comparison of promised vs actual margins.** The paper should explicitly separate outcomes restrictionists talked about (jobs, wages, occupational status) from outcomes they ignored but that matter for welfare (homeownership, local asset accumulation, mobility).
- **Better conceptual framing of the null.** The null is not “nothing happened”; it is “restriction failed on its own stated objective despite being unusually large and salient.” That is bigger than a generic null.
- **A stronger synthesis result.** The paper could be larger if framed as evaluating whether immigration restriction operates through labor-market substitution or local production complementarities, with the 1924 Act as the test case.

Also relevant:

- **Lewis** on local production responses and task adjustment.
- Potentially **Ager and Hansen** / **Ager and Brückner** type historical immigration and local growth papers, depending on exact match.

### How should the paper position itself relative to those neighbors?

Mostly **build on and reframe**, not attack.

- Relative to **Tabellini** and other inflow papers: “Those papers study what happens when immigrants arrive; we study what happens when they are abruptly removed. This directly tests the core restrictionist counterfactual.”
- Relative to **Goldin**: “Goldin explains why restriction happened politically; we ask whether its economic promise was true.”
- Relative to **Sequeira et al.**: “Their long-run gains suggest immigration may have durable benefits; we show that even in the short-to-medium run, the canonical gains from shutting immigration down are hard to find.”
- Relative to **Borjas/Card/Ottaviano-Peri**: “The historical episode provides a salient test of substitution vs complementarity under a large, policy-induced negative immigration shock.”

That is a coherent conversation. The paper should stop sounding like it is adjudicating modern theory from a single historical case, though. It can inform that debate, not settle it.

### Is the paper currently positioned too narrowly or too broadly?

At present it is a little **too broad in aspiration and too narrow in actual evidentiary base**.

It wants to speak to:
- economic history,
- modern immigration,
- local labor markets,
- housing,
- political economy.

That is potentially good, but the empirical core is still fairly narrow: one historical policy, one main labor-market outcome, one strong secondary outcome, and a suggestive pre-period contrast. The paper should choose its center of gravity more clearly.

My advice: center it as **economic history + immigration + local adjustment**, with housing/homeownership as the key unexpected margin. Do not oversell it as a general verdict on all immigration restriction debates.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?

Two gaps stand out:

1. **Housing and urban economics.**  
   If homeownership is the paper’s most interesting positive result, it needs to speak more seriously to housing supply, urban development, local demand spillovers, and asset accumulation. Right now the housing result appears without a fully developed housing literature anchor.

2. **Historical labor-market adjustment and structural transformation.**  
   The occupational mobility framing would benefit from engagement with work on sectoral reallocation, urbanization, and task specialization in historical settings, not just modern immigration wage debates.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?

Not quite yet. The current conversation is “immigration restriction and native occupational mobility.” That is reasonable but somewhat cramped, especially since the occupational result is null.

The more promising conversation is: **what happens to local economies when immigrant labor is abruptly withdrawn?** Under that framing, the occupational null and homeownership decline become jointly interpretable. That conversation connects immigration economics to local multiplier and housing literatures, which makes the paper feel larger.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Before this paper, we know that the 1924 Act was politically consequential and that the Age of Mass Migration generated both backlash and economic effects. We also know modern debates often hinge on whether immigrants substitute for or complement native workers. But we have less direct evidence on whether a major historical restriction episode actually delivered the native gains its proponents promised.

### Tension

Restrictionists promised clear labor-market benefits for natives. Yet much of the immigration literature finds small or mixed labor-market effects of inflows and some evidence of complementarities. So the tension is: when immigration was sharply curtailed in one of the most important U.S. policy shifts of the 20th century, did natives finally experience the gains restrictionists predicted?

### Resolution

No clear occupational gains appear in more exposed counties. Instead, the paper finds lower transitions into homeownership, concentrated among young and urban natives, and a positive pre-period association between immigrant presence and native occupational upgrading.

### Implications

The paper suggests that severe immigration restriction may fail even on its own terms and can reduce local economic dynamism on margins not captured by standard occupation-based outcomes.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the elements of one, but the arc is still somewhat unstable. The paper currently reads like:

- historical motivation,
- null on occupational outcomes,
- surprising homeownership result,
- placebo/pre-trend evidence interpreted as complementarity,
- implications.

That is close to a compelling story, but still somewhat like a collection of findings searching for hierarchy. The core problem is that the paper does not fully decide whether the story is:

1. “restriction did not help natives,” or
2. “restriction hurt natives through housing/local dynamism,” or
3. “the case reveals immigrant-native complementarity.”

All three are present, but only one can be the backbone.

### What story should it be telling?

The best version is:

**The Johnson-Reed Act provides a rare test of the central restrictionist claim. It fails that test on the margin proponents cared most about—occupational advancement—and the detectable adjustment occurs elsewhere, through reduced household asset accumulation and local dynamism.**

That is one clean story. The complementarity evidence then supports interpretation rather than carrying the whole paper.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?

I would lead with: **One of the largest immigration restrictions in U.S. history produced no detectable occupational gains for native-born men in the places most exposed to the shock.**

Then I would add: **The main measurable effect is actually lower entry into homeownership among young urban natives.**

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

Economists would lean in at the first sentence because the policy episode is big and the question is first-order. But they may start reaching for their phones if the conversation turns into a generic “null effect in local labor markets” paper.

They lean back in once you mention the homeownership result, because that is less expected and suggests a broader equilibrium story.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Almost certainly: **Why homeownership?**  
And closely after: **Is this really telling us about complementarity/local production, or is homeownership just an incidental correlated outcome?**

That tells you where the paper’s strategic problem lies. The question the paper naturally invites is not “what was the coefficient?” but “what is the mechanism that links immigration restriction to homeownership?” The current paper only partially answers that.

### If the findings are null or modest, is the null itself interesting?

Yes, potentially very much so. The null is interesting because:
- the policy shock is historically large;
- the political claims were explicit;
- the setting is salient and policy-relevant.

But the paper needs to make the null feel like a decisive empirical rebuke to a strong ex ante prediction, not like a failed search for effects. Right now it mostly succeeds, but the rhetoric gets ahead of the analytical structure. For a top journal, the null has to be framed as the main object: **a major, policy-induced contraction in immigration did not generate the gains restrictionists advertised.**

That is worth knowing.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

### What is the gap between current form and an AER paper?

This is not mainly a competence problem. It is a **framing-plus-scope problem**.

- **Framing problem:** The paper has a publishable question and an interesting historical setting, but the current framing oscillates between advocacy and contribution. It needs to sound less like it is prosecuting restrictionism and more like it is answering a major economic question.
- **Scope problem:** The main outcome is a null, and the main positive finding is on homeownership. For AER, that positive finding needs either deeper mechanism, broader outcome support, or stronger integration into a general-equilibrium local adjustment story.
- **Novelty problem:** The setting is novel enough; the issue is not novelty per se. The issue is whether the paper makes sufficiently clear why this setting changes what we know.
- **Ambition problem:** The paper is ambitious in rhetoric but somewhat safe in empirical payoff. To excite the top people in the field, it needs to either deepen the mechanism or widen the consequences.

### What is the single most impactful piece of advice?

**Rebuild the paper around the question “What happens to local economies when immigrant labor is abruptly withdrawn?” and make homeownership/local dynamism a central outcome rather than a side result appended to a null occupational-mobility paper.**

That one change would do several things at once:
- turn the null into a meaningful part of the story rather than the whole story;
- connect the paper to broader literatures;
- make the contribution feel bigger than “yet another immigration DiD with a null.”

If the authors do not want to broaden the mechanism/outcomes, then the fallback is to make the paper much more disciplined as a clean historical test of the restrictionist claim. But in current form, the most AER-like version is the broader local-adjustment story.
