## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple, important question: when the United States sharply restricted immigration in 1924, did native-born workers get the labor-market gains restrictionists promised? Using linked census data, the paper argues that the answer is no: the quotas did not improve natives’ occupational mobility, and they may have reduced homeownership in the places most exposed to the immigrant decline.

A busy economist should care because this is a rare historical case where the U.S. actually ran the policy experiment that modern immigration skeptics often imagine—large-scale restriction—and the paper claims the canonical benefits did not materialize.

**Does the paper articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Reasonably, but not optimally. The current introduction is vivid and readable, but it oversells with rhetoric (“the truth is worse,” “mirage”) before it has earned the reader’s trust, and it muddies the core contribution by jumping too quickly from the main question to the homeownership result. The paper should lead with the policy question and why this historical episode is uniquely informative, then state the two key findings cleanly.

**What the first two paragraphs should say instead:**

> The 1924 Johnson-Reed Act was one of the largest immigration restrictions in U.S. history. Its supporters made a concrete economic claim: by sharply reducing immigration from Southern and Eastern Europe, the law would improve native-born Americans’ labor-market outcomes. This paper tests that claim using linked census records that follow more than 10 million native-born men across counties before and after the quotas.
>
> We find little evidence that the restriction delivered the promised gains. Counties more exposed to the immigrant decline did not experience greater native occupational upgrading between 1920 and 1930. If anything, the policy appears to have reduced homeownership transitions, especially among young and urban natives. Taken together, the results suggest that the economic effects of immigration restriction operated less through relieving labor-market competition than through weakening complementary local economic activity.

That is the pitch. Cleaner, less sloganized, more world-question driven.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Restrictionists in 1924 promised that cutting low-skill immigration would help native workers move up. The Johnson-Reed Act was a major policy intervention that sharply reduced inflows from Southern and Eastern Europe.

### Tension
We have a lot of work on immigration inflows, but much less on what happens when immigration is sharply curtailed. If the restrictionist logic is right, this episode should be one of the best places to see native gains.

### Resolution
The paper finds no occupational upgrading for natives in more exposed counties and instead finds reduced homeownership transitions, especially among young and urban natives. Pre-period patterns are suggestive of complementarity rather than substitution.

### Implications
The core economic promise of immigration restriction may be overstated or wrong; cutting immigration may fail to help natives on the expected margins and can impose costs through local equilibrium channels.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
It has the ingredients, but the arc is not fully under control. At present it reads like:

1. Main null result on occupational mobility  
2. Surprise negative result on homeownership  
3. Suggestive placebo/complementarity evidence  
4. Lots of robustness and auxiliary results

That is serviceable, but it still feels somewhat like a collection of results searching for the highest-impact story.

### What story should it be telling?
The story should be:

> The 1924 quotas were sold as a way to help native workers advance. In one of the cleanest historical tests of that claim, we find that natives did not move up occupationally where immigration fell most. Instead, the main detectable effect is lower entry into homeownership, especially among young urban natives. The broader lesson is that immigration restriction may weaken complementary local activity rather than freeing natives to prosper.

That is much more coherent than the current “restrictionist mirage” branding, which feels a bit too editorialized. The phrase is catchy, but it currently substitutes for narrative discipline.

Then, if they’re still listening:

> “The only clear effect is that natives in those places, especially young urban men, were less likely to become homeowners.”

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
The first sentence gets attention because the policy episode is famous and the question is first-order. The second sentence gets more attention because it is unexpected.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Probably one of these:
- “If not occupations, what margin adjusted?”
- “Why homeownership?”
- “Is the story really complementarity, or is homeownership just capturing broader urban shocks?”
- “How different is this from existing historical immigration papers?”

That tells you where the paper needs to be strongest narratively: **why homeownership, and why this is not just another historical immigration design.**

### If the findings are null or modest, is the null itself interesting?
Yes—**if framed correctly**. A null is interesting here because the policy promise was large, explicit, and politically central. “One of the biggest immigration restrictions in U.S. history did not deliver the labor-market gains its advocates promised” is inherently interesting.

But the paper must lean into that logic more cleanly. Right now the null sometimes feels like a setup for the non-null housing result. It should instead feel like a direct falsification of a historically important policy claim.
