### 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper tests whether the most significant immigration restriction in U.S. history—the 1924 Johnson-Reed Act—delivered the "occupational upgrading" promised to native workers by its architects. Using a massive linked census panel (10M+ observations), it finds a precisely estimated null effect on native occupational gains, but documents a significant "hidden cost": a sharp decline in homeownership entry among young urban natives, likely due to the loss of complementary immigrant labor in construction and services.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this pitch excellently. The first two paragraphs provide a "hook" (the gap between political rhetoric and economic reality) and immediately present the "Headline Null" followed by the "Surprising Cost." No rewrite is necessary; the authors have clearly identified their "Restrictionist Mirage" branding.

### 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution:** The paper provides the first large-scale, individual-level causal evaluation of the 1924 Quota Act’s impact on native life-cycle outcomes, overturning the "competition" narrative in favor of a "complementarity" framework.

*   **Differentiation:** It is well-differentiated. While **Goldin (1994)** handles the *why* of the Act and **Tabellini (2020)** handles the *inflow* period, this paper handles the *interruption* of those flows. It moves beyond the "wages/employment" debate by looking at **homeownership**, a high-stakes welfare metric.
*   **Framing:** It is framed as answering a question about the **WORLD** (the persistent logic of restrictionism) rather than just a gap in a literature.
*   **Clarity:** A smart economist would call this "The paper that showed the 1924 quotas didn't actually help workers and actually hurt the housing market."
*   **Expansion:** To make the contribution bigger, the authors could more explicitly link the "homeownership" result to **local credit markets** or **housing supply elasticity** in the 1920s. If they can show that housing prices didn't fall (but construction did), the mechanism becomes ironclad.

### 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

*   **Closest Neighbors:** **Tabellini (2020)** on backlash; **Abramitzky, Boustan, & Eriksson (2014/2022)** on immigrant assimilation; **Peri (2016)** on complementarity; **Sequeira et al. (2020)** on long-run gains.
*   **Positioning:** It acts as a "Mirror Image" to the literature on the Age of Mass Migration. If immigration is good (Sequeira), its removal should be bad. This paper completes that logical circle.
*   **Conversation:** It is having the right conversation. It bridges Economic History and modern Labor/Urban Economics. It should perhaps lean harder into the **Urban/Housing** literature (e.g., **Moretti**) to explain why the homeownership effect is the "smoking gun" of the paper.

### 4. NARRATIVE ARC

*   **Setup:** 1920s America passes a draconian law based on the promise of "protecting the American worker."
*   **Tension:** If the law worked, we should see natives moving up. If it didn't, why?
*   **Resolution:** Null results for job titles, but a "thud" in the housing market. The "Mirage" is that the competition wasn't there; the complementarity was.
*   **Implications:** Restrictionists often focus on the wrong margins (jobs) and ignore the general equilibrium costs (asset accumulation).
*   **Evaluation:** The arc is strong. It isn't just a list of coefficients; it is a "detective story" where the primary suspect (job competition) is acquitted and a new one (housing/dynamism) is found.

### 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

*   **Dinner Party Fact:** "When the U.S. shut the door on 80% of European immigrants in 1924, native workers didn't get better jobs—they just stopped being able to buy houses."
*   **Reaction:** People lean in. The homeownership angle is the "kicker" that prevents this from being "just another historical null result."
*   **Follow-up:** "Wait, if they didn't get better jobs, did their rents at least go down?" (This is a question the authors should address in the "Mechanisms" section).

### 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

*   **Front-loading:** The paper is well-structured. The transition from Table 2 (The Null) to Table 3/5 (The Cost) is the heart of the paper.
*   **Appendix:** The "Ladder Up" result (Table 10) actually shows a small *negative* effect. This is more than a null—it’s a reversal. This should be moved from the appendix to the main body to strengthen the "Mirage" argument.
*   **Conclusion:** The conclusion is a bit standard. It could be more "ambitious" by discussing how these historical findings should inform the "substitution vs. complementarity" parameters used in modern DSGE models of migration.

### 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The gap is **Ambition**. A "Null on OCCSCORE" is a 2nd-tier journal paper. The "Homeownership Cost" is a top-5 journal paper. To make this a definitive AER piece, the authors need to prove the **Housing Mechanism** is not just a correlation but the definitive reason for the "Mirage."

**Single Most Impactful Advice:** Explicitly test the "Construction Channel." Can you link the county-level quota exposure to a decline in building permits or the number of people employed as "Carpenters/Masons" in the 1930 census? If you show the immigrants who were "kept out" were the ones who would have built the houses for the young natives, the paper is a "slam dunk."
