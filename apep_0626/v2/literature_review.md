# Literature Novelty Check — apep_0626 V2

## Key Papers in the Literature

### Immigration and Native Labor Markets
- **Tabellini (2020)** — Studies immigration *inflows* during mass migration era; finds political backlash coexisted with modest economic benefits. We study the reverse: *restriction* effects on natives.
- **Borjas (2003)** — Emphasizes substitution; our null on OCCSCORE and positive pre-period are more consistent with complementarity.
- **Ottaviano & Peri (2012)** — Task-based complementarity framework; our results align with their prediction that removing immigrants hurts native outcomes.
- **Peri & Sparber (2009)** — Immigrants and natives specialize in different tasks; restriction disrupts this specialization.
- **Card (2009)** — Limited wage effects of immigration; consistent with our occupational null.

### Historical Immigration Restriction
- **Goldin (1994)** — Political economy of the 1924 Act; does not estimate labor market consequences. We fill this gap.
- **Sequeira, Nunn & Qian (2020)** — Long-run benefits of historical immigration; we show short-run complementarity was also present.
- **Abramitzky & Boustan (2017, 2022)** — Assimilation and integration during mass migration; we document what happened when the door closed.

### Homeownership and Local Economies
- **Moretti (2011)** — Local labor demand multipliers; our homeownership channel is consistent with lost multiplier effects.
- **Ager & Bruckner (2013)** — Immigrant diversity and local economic growth in this era; our mechanism aligns.
- **Boustan (2010)** — Native mobility responses to demographic shocks.

## Novelty Assessment
**No existing paper** estimates the occupational and homeownership consequences of the 1924 Act for native-born workers using individual-level linked census data. The closest is Tabellini (2020), who studies immigration *inflows* rather than *restriction*. Our contribution is distinct: we test the explicit promise of restriction and document its failure.

The "restrictionist mirage" framing — the gap between promised and actual effects — is novel in the literature. No prior paper combines the occupational null, homeownership harm, and pre-restriction complementarity evidence into a unified narrative about the consequences of restriction.
