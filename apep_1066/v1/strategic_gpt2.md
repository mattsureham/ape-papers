## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple, policy-relevant question: when states explicitly ban hair-texture discrimination through CROWN Acts, do Black workers see measurable gains in earnings or employment? Using staggered state adoption, the paper’s headline result is that these laws do not appear to move aggregate labor-market outcomes, suggesting a gap between documented interpersonal discrimination and the ability of narrow anti-discrimination statutes to change economic outcomes at scale.

A busy economist should care because this is not really a paper about hair. It is a paper about when anti-discrimination law bites, when symbolic or narrow legal reforms fail to translate into market outcomes, and how much aggregate labor markets respond to targeted civil-rights expansions.

### Does the paper articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Not quite. The introduction is competent, but the first two paragraphs steer the reader toward “hair discrimination exists” rather than toward the broader economic question: **can narrowly targeted anti-discrimination laws change aggregate labor-market outcomes?** The first paragraph is vivid, but the second becomes a somewhat diffuse evidence dump. The paper reaches the real stakes only in paragraph three.

### What the first two paragraphs should say instead

The introduction should open with the policy puzzle, not the social background alone. Something like:

> States have rapidly adopted CROWN Acts to prohibit discrimination based on hair texture and protective hairstyles, closing a legal gap left by courts’ narrow interpretation of race discrimination. These laws are politically salient and normatively resonant, but their economic significance is unknown: can a narrowly targeted anti-discrimination statute meaningfully improve Black workers’ labor-market outcomes?
>
> This paper provides the first evidence on that question using staggered state adoption of CROWN Acts from 2019 to 2023. I find that, despite well-documented evidence of hair-based discrimination in surveys and experiments, CROWN Acts did not measurably increase Black workers’ earnings or employment at the state level. The broader implication is that legal recognition of a specific discriminatory channel may be insufficient to move aggregate outcomes when enforcement is weak, exposure is limited, or the targeted margin is not economically central.

That is the pitch the paper should have. Right now the paper undersells the broader question and oversells the narrow setting.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper’s contribution is to show that state CROWN Acts—explicit protections against hair-texture discrimination—did not produce detectable improvements in Black workers’ aggregate earnings or employment, thereby informing the broader debate over the effectiveness of narrow anti-discrimination laws.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest papers?

Only partially. The paper does distinguish itself from the experimental and survey literature documenting hair discrimination, and from broader anti-discrimination-law papers. But the differentiation is still mostly of the form “no one has studied this policy yet,” which is weaker than “this changes how we think about anti-discrimination policy.”

The closest differentiation problem is that a reader could easily summarize the paper as: **“another staggered-adoption state-policy paper finding a null effect on aggregate outcomes.”** That is not enough for AER unless the null teaches something general and surprising.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a gap in the literature?

Too much as a literature gap. “First causal evaluation of CROWN Acts” is fine, but that is not a top-journal contribution by itself. The stronger framing is:

- When do anti-discrimination protections generate aggregate labor-market effects?
- Are narrow, identity-specific legal protections economically consequential?
- How much of documented discrimination is economically binding at market scale?

Those are world questions. The paper should lean there much harder.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?

Right now: maybe, but not crisply. They would likely say, “it’s a DiD/DDD paper on CROWN Acts and Black earnings, and it mostly finds nothing.” That is not enough.

The goal should be for them to say: “It uses CROWN Acts to test whether narrow anti-discrimination statutes can move aggregate labor outcomes; apparently not, which suggests either weak enforcement or that many documented discriminatory slights are not the margins driving earnings.”

That is a much stronger takeaway.

### What would make the contribution bigger?

Most importantly, the paper needs a **bigger object of interest** than state-level earnings and employment.

Specific ways to make the contribution bigger:

1. **Move closer to the policy’s first-order margin.**  
   Earnings and employment are very distal outcomes for a law about grooming discrimination. More compelling outcomes would be:
   - occupational sorting into customer-facing/professional jobs,
   - hiring complaints or EEOC/state agency filings,
   - workplace discipline/separation,
   - self-employment, sector choice, or public-facing occupations,
   - school discipline or educational settings if the paper broadened beyond labor markets.

2. **Sharpen mechanism through exposure.**  
   The paper needs a reason some labor markets should be more exposed:
   - industries with formal grooming standards,
   - occupations requiring customer interaction,
   - states with stronger state-level civil-rights enforcement,
   - places with larger Black female professional populations.

3. **Use the setting to answer a broader policy question.**  
   For example: are anti-discrimination laws effective only when they create new enforcement leverage, not merely new protected language? That could connect CROWN Acts to a larger class of symbolic or low-enforcement statutes.

4. **Reframe from “hair discrimination” to “narrow vs. broad civil-rights protections.”**  
   That would make the paper legible to labor, public, and law-and-economics audiences simultaneously.

It should mostly **build on** the hair-discrimination literature and **discipline** it with aggregate evidence, while **speaking directly to** the anti-discrimination-law literature.

The right stance is not “attack experimental studies”—that would be misguided and probably wrong. The right stance is:

- Experimental and survey evidence shows discrimination exists.
- This paper asks whether legal prohibition of one specific channel changes market outcomes.
- The answer appears to be no, at least on aggregate earnings/employment margins.
- Therefore existence of discrimination does not imply that a narrow legal remedy will shift equilibrium outcomes.

That is a productive bridge.

### Is the current positioning too narrow or too broad?

It is oddly both.

- **Too narrow** in setting: hair-texture discrimination sounds niche.
- **Too broad** in claims: “limits of symbolic legislation” is potentially large, but currently underdeveloped and somewhat tacked on.

The paper needs to pick a lane and earn it. The best lane is: **a test of whether narrow civil-rights expansions without major enforcement changes affect labor-market outcomes.**

It seems underconnected to several relevant conversations:

- **Civil-rights law as enforcement design**, not just protected-category design.
- **Targeted identity regulations** versus broader labor-market reforms.

- Potentially **personnel economics / workplace norms** literatures on subjective professionalism standards.

The paper should also more explicitly connect to the literature on the gap between micro discrimination evidence and macro labor-market effects. That is an important tension and would give the paper more intellectual heft.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?

Not yet. Right now it is mostly in conversation with “hair discrimination exists” and “this policy hasn’t been studied.” The better conversation is:

> Why do some anti-discrimination reforms produce measurable labor-market effects while others do not?

That is the conversation AER readers might care about.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

There is substantial evidence that Black workers, especially Black women, face discrimination linked to natural hairstyles and race-coded grooming norms. In response, states adopted CROWN Acts to extend explicit legal protection.

### Tension

But it is unclear whether recognizing this form of discrimination in statute changes labor-market outcomes. The policy is highly visible and morally salient, yet potentially narrow, weakly enforced, and targeted at a specific channel whose aggregate economic importance is uncertain.

### Resolution

The paper finds little evidence that CROWN Acts increased Black workers’ earnings or employment at the state level.

### Implications

The implication is not that hair discrimination is unreal. It is that **documented discrimination plus legal recognition does not automatically imply aggregate economic change.** Narrow anti-discrimination protections may fail to move outcomes when they lack enforcement bite, affect only a small subset of workers, or target a margin that is not first-order for earnings.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

Serviceable, but not fully. At present it reads a bit like a collection of empirical exercises in search of a larger claim. The strongest story is present, but the paper doesn’t commit to it early or consistently enough.

In particular, the paper wobbles between three possible stories:

1. Hair discrimination is real, but CROWN Acts do not matter economically.
2. Symbolic legislation often has limited labor-market effects.
3. Aggregate state-level outcomes may be too coarse to capture the relevant benefits.

All three can be true, but the paper needs one spine. The spine should be #2, with #1 as context and #3 as caution.

### What story should it be telling?

The paper should be telling this story:

> CROWN Acts provide a clean test case for whether narrowly targeted civil-rights expansions can move labor-market outcomes. They address a real and salient form of discrimination, but do so without major new enforcement tools. The fact that they do not shift aggregate earnings or employment suggests that the effectiveness of anti-discrimination law depends not just on recognizing harms, but on targeting economically central margins with enforceable mechanisms.

That is coherent, memorable, and bigger than the specific statute.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?

I would say:  
“States rapidly passed CROWN Acts to ban hair-texture discrimination, but there’s no evidence those laws raised Black workers’ earnings or employment.”

That is the lead. It is clear and mildly surprising.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

Some would lean in, but not all. The problem is that the current version sounds like a niche policy null. For broad economics audiences, the second sentence needs to be:

> “So the interesting question is why a legally meaningful civil-rights expansion failed to move aggregate labor outcomes.”

That is what gets people to lean in.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Almost certainly:  
**“Is that because the law had no bite, or because hair discrimination isn’t quantitatively important for earnings?”**

That is the key follow-up, and the paper currently cannot answer it decisively. Strategically, the paper should organize itself around this question rather than around the baseline coefficient alone.

### Are the null findings interesting?

Potentially yes, but only if the authors make a stronger case that this is an informative null rather than a failed attempt to detect an effect.

Right now the paper tries to do this with “well-powered null,” but that is not enough editorially. The case must be conceptual:

- The policy was salient.
- The harmed margin is documented elsewhere.
- The law changed legal coverage but not enforcement architecture.
- Therefore the null is evidence about the conditions under which anti-discrimination law does or does not matter.

If framed that way, the null is interesting. If framed as “first study of CROWN Acts finds no effect,” it feels minor.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

Bluntly: in current form, this is not an AER paper. The issue is not competence. It is **scale of question relative to design and outcome choice**.

### What is the gap?

Mostly:

- **Framing problem:** the paper is about a bigger question than it currently admits.
- **Scope problem:** the outcomes are too aggregate and distant from the policy margin.
- **Ambition problem:** the paper is content to be “the first paper on CROWN Acts” instead of using CROWN Acts to answer a broader question about anti-discrimination law.
- Some **novelty problem:** the empirical move—state policy staggered adoption with aggregate outcomes—is now familiar. So the conceptual payoff must be unusually sharp.

### What would excite the top 10 people in this field?

Not “a null on CROWN Acts.” What might excite them is:

> a persuasive demonstration that anti-discrimination policies only matter when they alter enforceability or target economically central margins, using CROWN Acts as a revealing test case.

To get there, the paper likely needs either:
- outcomes closer to the policy’s mechanism, or
- a much stronger comparative framing across types of anti-discrimination statutes, or
- sharper evidence on where one would expect effects if enforcement or exposure were the key constraint.

### Single most impactful advice

**Stop selling this as the first paper on CROWN Acts and start selling it as a paper about the limits of narrow anti-discrimination law—then restructure the evidence around that broader claim.**

That is the one change that most improves its strategic positioning. If the authors can’t do that persuasively, the paper likely belongs in a solid field journal rather than AER.
