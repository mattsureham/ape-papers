## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper evaluates the labor market impact of the CROWN Act, a recent wave of state laws prohibiting discrimination based on hair texture. Using a triple-difference design, the author finds that these laws have failed to move the needle on Black workers' median earnings or employment. A busy economist should care because this is a "stress test" for symbolic versus substantive anti-discrimination law: does targeting a specific, culturally-salient channel of bias actually work in the aggregate?

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this well, but the first two paragraphs focus a bit too much on the legal history and "texture penalty" anecdotes. To meet AER standards, the pitch should more aggressively emphasize the *economic* trade-off between narrow, conduct-specific protections and broad, outcome-based anti-discrimination frameworks.

**Suggested Pitch:**
> While experimental evidence suggests significant labor market penalties for natural Black hairstyles, it remains an open question whether legal prohibitions targeting such specific channels can alter aggregate outcomes. This paper provides the first causal evaluation of the CROWN Act, exploiting its staggered adoption across twenty-two states. I find a precisely estimated null effect on earnings and employment, suggesting that narrow anti-discrimination statutes may lack the enforcement teeth or scope to overcome deeper systemic biases.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Statement of Contribution:** The paper establishes that the first generation of state-level hair-discrimination laws (CROWN Acts) has had no detectable impact on the aggregate earnings or employment of Black workers.

- **Differentiation:** It differentiates itself from the "beauty premium" literature (Hamermesh) by moving from *measuring* a penalty to *evaluating a policy* meant to fix it.
- **Framing:** It is currently framed as filling a gap in the CROWN Act literature (which is niche). It would be stronger if framed as a "limits of law" paper—asking why specific legal remedies fail when experimental evidence suggests the underlying bias is large.
- **AER Quality?** Currently, a smart economist might say "it's a DiD on a new law with a null result." To make the contribution "AER-sized," the author needs to dig deeper into the *why*. Is it a lack of awareness? Do firms just switch to other forms of discrimination?
- **Bigger Contribution:** The paper would be more impactful if it looked at **firm-level behavior** or **search behavior**. Do Black women change their "conformity" behavior (hair straightening) even if earnings don't change? That would capture a welfare gain (reduced costs) that the current paper misses.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

- **Closest Neighbors:** Donohue & Heckman (1991) on Title VII; Bertrand & Mullainathan (2004) on resume bias; Doleac & Hansen (2020) on Ban-the-Box.
- **Positioning:** The paper should position itself as a modern successor to the "Ban-the-Box" literature. Just as that literature showed that removing one signal (criminal records) can lead to unintended statistical discrimination, this paper suggests that protecting one signal (hair) might be insufficient or ignored.
- **Unexpected Connection:** The paper should speak to the **"Incomplete Contracts"** or **"Multitasking"** literature (Holmstrom & Milgrom). If you regulate one dimension of employer bias, do they simply shift their "effort" to discriminate along unregulated dimensions (e.g., "culture fit")?

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

- **Setup:** A world where hair discrimination is a documented, costly barrier for Black women.
- **Tension:** A massive legislative wave (CROWN Acts) sweeps the country to fix this, but it relies on old enforcement mechanisms for a very specific, mutable trait.
- **Resolution:** Total silence in the data. No change in the earnings gap.
- **Implications:** "Expressive" law isn't enough; if we want to move aggregate numbers, we need either broader enforcement or a different policy tool.

**Evaluation:** The arc is clean but a bit "flat." The "Implications" section needs more teeth. It feels like a report on a failed policy; it needs to be an inquiry into the *nature* of modern discrimination.

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

**The Dinner Party Fact:** "Twenty-two states passed laws specifically to stop hair discrimination, and five years later, it hasn't changed the Black-white earnings gap by even one percent."

**The Follow-up:** "Is that because the law is weak, or because hair isn't actually the primary driver of the earnings gap?" This is the question the paper *must* answer to get through the AER.

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

- **Front-load the heterogeneity:** The Black woman vs. White woman comparison (Table 4) is the most compelling part. It shouldn't be a "robustness check"; it should be a central pillar.
- **The "Cost of Conformity":** Page 2 mentions Black women spend $473 billion on hair care. This is a massive "compliance cost." If the author could find a way to proxy for hair care spending (using Consumer Expenditure Survey data), a null result on earnings combined with a *decrease* in spending would be a major AER-level welfare finding.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The gap is **Ambition**. Currently, it’s a very competent policy evaluation. To be an AER paper, it needs to be a paper about the **Economics of Symbolic Legislation.**

**The Single Most Impactful Advice:**
Broaden the scope beyond the binary "did it work?" and investigate the **mechanism of failure**. Specifically, try to test if the law changed *behavior* (e.g., Google Search trends for natural hair care in CROWN states vs. others) without changing *outcomes*. If people changed their behavior but the market didn't reward them, you have a much more profound story about the limits of legal protection.
