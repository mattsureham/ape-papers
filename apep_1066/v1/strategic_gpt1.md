## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple policy question: when states explicitly ban discrimination against natural Black hairstyles through CROWN Acts, do Black workers see measurable gains in earnings or employment? Using staggered state adoptions from 2019–2023, the paper’s answer is no: despite substantial evidence that hair-based discrimination exists, these laws do not appear to move aggregate labor-market outcomes.

A busy economist should care because this is, at least in principle, a test of a broader question: when does anti-discrimination law translate into economically meaningful changes, and when is it mostly symbolic? That broader question is important. The current paper, however, is only intermittently written at that level.

**Does the paper articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Not quite. The introduction opens with an anecdote and then a claim that the “texture penalty” is well documented. That is fine, but the paper gets pulled too quickly into the niche object—hair discrimination—before establishing the bigger economic question. As written, the paper sounds like an evaluation of a very specific law in a very specific domain. For AER positioning, the introduction should instead begin with the general issue of how narrow anti-discrimination protections affect labor-market outcomes, then present CROWN Acts as a sharp test case.

**What the first two paragraphs should say instead:**

> Anti-discrimination law often expands by targeting increasingly specific channels of bias. But an open question is whether these narrower protections meaningfully change economic outcomes, or instead mainly express social norms while leaving labor markets largely unchanged. This question matters for both economists and policymakers: if discrimination operates through many margins, laws that address only one may have limited aggregate effects even when the underlying discrimination is real.
>
> State CROWN Acts provide a useful test. Enacted rapidly across U.S. states since 2019, these laws explicitly prohibit discrimination based on hair texture and protective hairstyles associated with Black identity. This paper asks whether those protections improved Black workers’ earnings and employment. Using staggered state adoption, I find little evidence that they did. The result suggests a broader lesson: targeted anti-discrimination statutes may close a legal gap without relaxing the main economic constraints facing workers.

That is the pitch the paper should have.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:**  
The paper provides the first evidence on whether CROWN Acts—state laws banning hair-texture discrimination—improve Black workers’ aggregate labor-market outcomes, and finds no detectable effect on earnings or employment.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?
Only partially. The paper says it is the “first causal evaluation” of CROWN Acts, which may well be true, but “first paper on this policy” is not by itself a strong AER contribution. The more important differentiation should be relative to:
1. broad anti-discrimination law evaluations,
2. appearance/beauty penalty papers,
3. correspondence/audit studies on discrimination,
4. symbolic law / expressive law work.

Right now the paper differentiates itself by object, not by idea. “No one has studied this statute yet” is much weaker than “this statute lets us learn when anti-discrimination laws fail to move market outcomes.”

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or filling a gap in a LITERATURE?
Mostly as a literature gap. The paper repeatedly says “first causal evaluation” and “contributes to three literatures.” That is standard, but not strong. The stronger framing is about the world:

- Hair-based discrimination is real.
- Policymakers responded with targeted legal protection.
- Did that protection alter labor-market outcomes?
- If not, what does that imply about the margins on which discrimination matters and the limits of narrow legal reforms?

That is a world question. The paper currently knows this, but does not consistently foreground it.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?
They could probably say: “It’s a DDD paper on CROWN Acts and Black earnings, and it finds null effects.”  
That is not enough. The risk is exactly that it reads as “another DiD/DDD paper about a new state law.”

### What would make this contribution bigger?
Several possibilities:

1. **Shift the outcome from aggregate earnings to more proximate margins.**  
   If the theory is about appearance-based discrimination in hiring, customer-facing occupations, professionalism norms, or disciplinary actions, state-level median earnings is a very indirect place to look. A bigger paper would track outcomes closer to the policy margin: occupational sorting, public-sector employment, complaints filed, hiring in appearance-sensitive sectors, managerial/professional jobs, or self-reported workplace treatment.

2. **Leverage the obvious gender asymmetry more centrally.**  
   The paper acknowledges Black women are the most relevant group, yet the headline specification pools men and women and then later backs away from the more interesting heterogeneity result. Strategically, that makes the paper look cautious rather than insightful. If the core substantive claim is about Black women’s workplace presentation, the entire paper should be organized around that.

3. **Make the contribution about the limits of narrow anti-discrimination law.**  
   The paper should compare CROWN Acts conceptually to broader anti-discrimination interventions. Why do some legal changes bite and others not? Without that comparative lens, this looks like a narrow policy null.

4. **Show whether the law changed anything at all, even if not earnings.**  
   If employment and earnings do not move, did complaints rise? Did firms change handbook language? Did media salience shift? Did social norms change? AER papers can survive nulls if they reveal the margin on which policy did or did not operate. This one currently says mostly: no effect on two broad outcomes.

### How should the paper position itself?
It should **build on** the anti-discrimination law literature and **translate** the management/legal literature into an economic policy question. It should not “attack” the hair-discrimination literature. The paper is at risk of being read as saying the underlying problem is not important because aggregate earnings did not move. That would be both strategically unwise and substantively too strong.

The right positioning is:
- The experimental and qualitative evidence establishes that hair-based bias exists.
- This paper asks whether a targeted legal response changes market outcomes at scale.
- The answer informs the design and likely limits of narrow anti-discrimination laws.

### Is it positioned too narrowly or too broadly?
Paradoxically, both.

- **Too narrowly** in object: it is very much about CROWN Acts per se.
- **Too broadly** in claims: it gestures at “beauty premium,” “symbolic legislation,” and “anti-discrimination law” without deeply integrating any of them.

The paper needs one primary conversation. The best one is: **what makes anti-discrimination law economically effective?**

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?
It should probably engage more with:
- the economics of legal enforcement and compliance,
- expressive law versus enforceable law,
- implementation/administrative capacity,
- discrimination margins beyond wages and employment,
- sociological and management work on respectability norms, grooming codes, and workplace identity performance.

It also needs to be careful with the beauty-premium literature. That connection is plausible, but currently feels borrowed rather than earned. Hair-texture discrimination is not just generic attractiveness; it is racialized professionalism. The paper should probably connect more to racialized labor-market sorting and employer discretion than to beauty per se.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?
Not yet. The most impactful framing is probably **not** “beauty premium” and not even “hair discrimination.” It is:

> targeted anti-discrimination law as a policy technology: when does closing a legal gap change equilibrium outcomes?

That is a more general and more economic conversation.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
There is credible evidence that Black workers, especially Black women, face penalties tied to natural hairstyles and grooming norms. Courts historically left a gap in protection by treating hairstyle as mutable rather than racial. States responded with CROWN Acts.

### Tension
If hair discrimination is real and the legal gap mattered, then explicitly banning this form of discrimination should improve labor-market outcomes. But it might not, either because the law is weakly enforced, because hair-based bias affects dignity more than earnings, or because discrimination operates through broader channels that this statute does not touch.

### Resolution
The paper finds no detectable effects on median earnings or employment for Black workers at the state level after CROWN adoption.

### Implications
Targeted anti-discrimination statutes may fail to change aggregate labor-market outcomes even when the underlying discriminatory behavior is real. The likely reasons are narrow scope, indirect enforcement, or mismatch between the protected margin and the outcomes measured.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
It has the skeleton of one, but the execution is uneven. Too much of the paper feels like:
- “here is the policy,”
- “here is the DDD,”
- “here is the null,”
- “here are robustness checks.”

That is not a full narrative; it is a competent empirical sequence.

### What story should it be telling?
The story should be:

1. **Modern anti-discrimination law increasingly targets specific practices.**
2. **CROWN Acts are a clean, salient example.**
3. **They address a real documented form of discrimination.**
4. **Yet they do not shift broad labor-market outcomes.**
5. **Therefore, the economically relevant question is not whether discrimination exists, but which legal interventions can actually move equilibrium outcomes.**

That is a real story. Right now the paper is still too attached to the object-level novelty of hair discrimination.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at dinner?
“More than twenty states explicitly banned workplace hair-texture discrimination, but there’s no sign those laws raised Black workers’ earnings or employment.”

That is the right lead.

### Would people lean in?
Some would. The topic is vivid and culturally salient. But after the initial interest, the next reaction from economists would be: “Interesting—but is that because the policy truly doesn’t bite, or because the outcomes are too aggregated and far from the mechanism?”

That follow-up comes immediately.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Almost certainly:
- “Did it affect Black women specifically?”
- “Did it change complaints, hiring, promotions, or customer-facing occupations?”
- “Is median state-level earnings just too blunt an outcome?”
- “What do we learn beyond this one niche statute?”

Those questions expose the current paper’s vulnerability.

### Is the null itself interesting?
Potentially yes. A null can be very interesting when:
1. the underlying problem is well established,
2. the policy intervention was salient and widely adopted,
3. the paper can say something sharp about why no aggregate effects emerged.

This paper has (1) and some of (2), but only gestures toward (3). As written, the null is moderately interesting, not compelling. It risks feeling like a failed search for effects because the mechanism-to-outcome chain is too long and the outcome too coarse.

For the null to feel valuable rather than disappointing, the paper must make the central claim:
> Even highly salient legal reforms may not matter economically unless they alter enforcement or target a margin large enough to affect actual labor-market decisions.

That would make the null informative.

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

### What would make it read better?

1. **Rewrite the introduction around the general question, not the anecdote.**  
   Keep the anecdote if desired, but it should not lead. Open with the broad issue: narrow anti-discrimination protections and their economic bite.

2. **Move the “well-powered null” idea earlier, but more carefully.**  
   The paper currently declares a “well-powered null” before fully convincing the reader that the chosen outcomes are the right place to look. That can read as overclaiming. The paper should first clarify what kind of effects it can plausibly detect and on what margins.

3. **Shorten the institutional background.**  
   The legal background is useful but can be leaner. The key facts are simple: federal legal gap, state response, adoption timing, enforcement via existing mechanisms. The rest can be condensed.

4. **Trim the methods exposition in the main text.**  
   The paragraph explaining why the interaction survives the fixed effects is unnecessary at current length for an AER-oriented narrative memo style paper. That material can be shorter or shifted to appendix. The main text should privilege the economic question over econometric bookkeeping.

5. **Bring the Black women issue to the front.**  
   This is not a side heterogeneity exercise. It is central to the substantive question. If the paper believes the cleanest test is Black women versus white women, that should be elevated, not buried in robustness.

6. **Do not bury the most intellectually interesting result in the discussion.**  
   The strongest idea in the paper is that anti-discrimination laws can be real legally, real symbolically, and still too weak or narrow to move aggregate outcomes. That should be in the introduction and conclusion, not mostly left to the discussion section.

7. **Cut the standardized effect sizes appendix table.**  
   Strategically, it does not add much and in places creates more noise than value. Labels like “moderate negative” for imprecisely estimated near-zero effects are unhelpful and potentially distracting.

8. **Conclusion should do more than summarize.**  
   Right now it mostly recaps findings. It should end with a sharper take-home message about policy design: narrowing protected categories is not the same as changing enforcement or economic incentives.

### Is the paper front-loaded with the good stuff?
Reasonably, but not optimally. The broad lesson is not front-loaded enough. The reader learns the empirical result early, but not the bigger reason to care.

### Are there results buried in robustness that should be in the main results?
Yes: the Black women versus white women comparison is much closer to the policy’s obvious target and belongs in the main text, not as an afterthought in robustness.

### Is the conclusion adding value?
Some, but not enough. It should be more interpretive and more comparative.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

At present, this is **not** primarily an identification problem for editorial purposes. It is a **framing and ambition problem**, with some **scope** issues.

### What is the gap?

#### 1. Framing problem
The paper has a potentially important idea—narrow anti-discrimination statutes may be largely symbolic in labor markets—but it presents itself as an evaluation of a niche policy. That is too small.

#### 2. Scope problem
The outcomes are broad and distal relative to the mechanism. If you want to claim the limits of legal reform, you need either:
- outcomes closer to the margin the law should affect, or
- a stronger conceptual case that aggregate earnings/employment are the right sufficient statistics.

Right now the paper has neither fully.

#### 3. Novelty problem
“First paper on CROWN Acts” is not enough. The broader contribution has to be to what economists understand about discrimination, legal design, and policy efficacy.

#### 4. Ambition problem
The paper is competent but safe. It asks the narrowest evaluative question and then stops at a null. The ambitious version would use CROWN Acts to test a broader theory of anti-discrimination law: scope, enforcement, salience, and the difference between symbolic and behavior-changing policy.

### What is the single most impactful piece of advice?
**Reframe the paper as a general test of when targeted anti-discrimination laws change economic outcomes, and reorganize the evidence around the margins where such laws should bite—especially Black women and more proximate workplace outcomes.**

If the author can only change one thing, it should be the framing. Without that, this remains an interesting niche null. With that, it has a chance to become a paper about the design limits of modern anti-discrimination policy.
