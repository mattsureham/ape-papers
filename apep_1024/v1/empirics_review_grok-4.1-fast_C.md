### 1. Idea Fidelity
The paper faithfully pursues the core idea from the manifest: applying a bunching estimator to ADEME's 14.4 million DPE diagnostics to detect strategic responses at the 420 (F/G) and 330 (E/F) kWh/m²/year rental ban thresholds, with geographic heterogeneity by rental market tightness (tight urban vs. rural/owner-dominated areas) and a placebo at 110 (B/C). It delivers on static bunching, difference-in-bunching over time (for 420), and geographic splits, using the correct data source and fields. However, it misses key promised elements: (i) no analysis of the July 2024 small-property reform as a natural experiment (difference-in-bunching for properties <40m² post-reform); (ii) limited temporal dynamics (only 420 shown, not increasing bunching at 330 toward 2028 or minimal at 250 until 2034); and (iii) no explicit GHG placebo or full rural-urban splits beyond IDF/tight markets. The sample is restricted to ~1.2M observations near thresholds (appropriate but narrower than the manifest's full universe), yielding a solid but incomplete execution of the identification strategy.

### 2. Summary
This paper applies the bunching estimator to French DPE diagnostics around rental ban thresholds, finding no aggregate excess mass at the imminent 420 kWh/m²/year (G-ban) cutoff due to offsetting positive bunching in tight urban rental markets (renovation) and negative bunching (missing mass) elsewhere (retreat). Stronger bunching appears at the later 330 kWh/m²/year (F-ban) threshold, with geographic heterogeneity providing the key economic insight. Results highlight how quantity regulations like rental bans split housing markets along local rental premia, with policy implications for EU-wide minimum energy standards.

### 3. Essential Points
**1. Absence of density plots undermines all inference.** No figures show the raw binned densities, fitted counterfactuals, or exclusion windows—standard in every bunching paper (e.g., Saez 2010; Kleven 2016). Without visuals, readers cannot assess polynomial fit, manipulation window plausibility, or whether "missing mass" truly reflects retreat vs. density truncation. AER: Insights demands figures; include at least one per threshold (pooled/geographic splits) showing 95% CIs from bootstrap.

**2. Large placebo bunching at 110 (b=1.076, SE=0.048) and across-threshold variation indicate dominant mechanical artifacts not credibly differenced out.** If DPE methodology generates ~100% excess mass at non-regulatory cuts (plausible from Collins & Curtis 2018 on assessor discretion/rounding), the "behavioral" b=0.549 at 330 or b=0.107 in IDF are not excess relative to baseline—they could be smaller than mechanical. Difference-in-bunching over time (Table 2) shows no significant trend (p=0.63) and volatile estimates (e.g., 2024 b=-2.608), contradicting the manifest's prediction of rising intensity. Must subtract placebo-adjusted b (e.g., regulatory minus 110) or run formal diff-in-diff (threshold × post-policy announcement), with SEs clustered by year/region.

**3. Extreme polynomial sensitivity (Table 5: b from -0.231 to +0.535) signals poor identification of counterfactual density.** With complex underlying density (renovate/retreat cancellation), 7th-order fits are arbitrary; nearby bins likely non-smooth due to DPE algorithm kinks. This flips signs/magnitudes, making aggregate b=-0.013 unreliable (even if intended as "null"). Heterogeneity helps but inherits the bias. Essential fix: (i) use local polynomial (e.g., 4th-order, ±30-50 kWh/m² per Kleven & Waseem 2013); (ii) report optimal order via fit diagnostics (e.g., AIC on held-out bins); (iii) bound b via sensitivity plots (Bertanha et al. 2023). Without this, reject for AER: Insights.

### 4. Suggestions
**Data and Sample Enhancements (20% weight).** Expand to full 14.4M per manifest for global densities, but keep threshold windows for power. Clarify sample construction: Table 1 N=1.18M seems pooled across windows (overlaps?), but Table 2 has 432K for 420—report per-window Ns consistently. Add covariates: control for building age (`annee_construction`), type (`type_batiment`), size (to proxy small-property exposure pre-reform), and GHG emissions (`emission_ges_5_usages_par_m2`) in stratified bunching, as dual-criterion DPE could induce joint bunching. Deduplication is good (appendix), but test for repeat diagnostics (same building-ID fields) as strategic re-testing post-renovation—tabulate share >1 per building/year. Incorporate manifest's ~36K communes for finer geography: replace coarse IDF/"tight" dummies with rental share (INSEE data) or HHI vacancy quartiles for continuous heterogeneity plot (b vs. log-rent premium).

**Identification Refinements (30% weight).** Fully execute promised tests: (i) Small-property reform (July 2024): split sample by surface area <40m², estimate diff-in-bunching pre/post (expect drop in 420 bunching for small units only). (ii) Multi-threshold dynamics: replicate Table 2 for 330/250, plotting b_t × distance-to-deadline (e.g., years to 2025/2028/2034). Positive slope validates behavior. (iii) GHG placebo: bin on emissions thresholds (e.g., F/G at ~high kgCO2/m²), expect no rental-ban response there. (iv) Round-number falsification: test 400/450 (non-thresholds), as in robustness appendix mention. For retreat interpretation, compute implied supply drop: missing mass / total near-threshold mass ≈ 5% rental exit in non-IDF (economic? Cross-check with rental listings data, e.g., SeLoger API).

**Estimation and Robustness (25% weight).** Magnitudes are plausible conditionally: b=0.1 implies ~10% of marginal properties renovate ~5-20 kWh/m² (cheap insulation/seals, €2-5K cost recoverable in tight markets at €25/m² rents). But b=0.549 at 330 is large (55% excess; ~7K obs shift), possibly mechanical—normalize by placebo or report unnormalized B / total N (e.g., 0.4-1% of F-stock affected). SEs appropriate (Poisson bootstrap standard), but cluster by diagnostician/postal code if ID available. Add: (i) Dynamic bunching (Kleven & Landais 2017): fit time-varying windows. (ii) Alternative counterfactuals: quantile regressions or change-in-changes (narrow bins only). (iii) Subsample robustness: pre-2023 (no ban anticipation), owner-only proxies (rural), new builds (no renovation possible). Table 5 sensitivity is transparent but plot it (b vs. order)—shows cancellation, strengthening heterogeneity claim.

**Economic Interpretation and Presentation (25% weight).** Result is clear/economically meaningful: bans induce renovate (urban) vs. retreat (rural), splitting markets—novel for quantity regs (vs. prices/subsidies). Quantify: for 50m² unit, b=0.1 at 420 ≈ 1-2K buildings renovated in IDF (0.3% stock); missing mass ≈ 0.5% supply drop non-IDF. Add back-of-envelope: cost per kWh saved = renovation cost / (B × avg shift); compare to €/tCO2. Discussion model is intuitive—formalize as Figure 7 (theory plot: b vs. rental premium). Trim LaTeX bloat (e.g., unused packages); add 3-4 figures (densities, heterogeneity map, time trends). Standardized effects (Appendix Table 6) good but arbitrary SD(Y)=1—use SD(consumption)=147 (Table 1) for SDE≈0.08 at 420 (small). Intro/conclusion punchy; cite EPBD more (e.g., cost-benefit sims). Overall, strong AER: Insights candidate post-fixes—heterogeneity delivers policy punch absent in Collins/Curtis.
