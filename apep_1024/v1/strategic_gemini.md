## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper investigates how landlords respond to "quantity regulations" in housing—specifically, the French ban on renting energy-inefficient properties. Using a bunching design on 1.2 million energy audits, it asks whether these thresholds induce building improvements or lead to the withdrawal of housing supply, finding that the answer depends entirely on local market tightness. It matters because it provides the first major empirical test of Minimum Energy Performance Standards (MEPS), a policy currently being rolled out across the European Union.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this very well. The first paragraph of the introduction sets the stakes (half a million properties becoming illegal) and the trade-off (renovate vs. strand) immediately. It avoids the "gap in the literature" trap until the third page.

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**The Contribution:** The paper demonstrates that energy-efficiency quantity mandates (rental bans) trigger a bifurcation of the housing market where landlords renovate in high-rent districts but exit the market in low-rent districts.

*   **Differentiation:** It differentiates itself from the standard "energy efficiency gap" literature (Allcott, Greenstone, etc.) by shifting from price/subsidy instruments to outright bans. It distinguishes itself from Irish EPC bunching papers by focusing on a regulatory notch (the ban) rather than just information labels.
*   **Framing:** It is framed as a question about the world—specifically, the unintended consequences of climate-motivated housing policy.
*   **Clarity:** A smart economist would say: "This is the paper that shows French rental bans actually kill supply in poor areas while fixing buildings in rich ones."
*   **Expansion:** To make it "bigger," the author needs more data on the "retreat" side. Where do the units go? Are they sold to owner-occupiers, or do they sit vacant? The current evidence for "retreat" is "missing mass," which is suggestive but technically a residual.

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

*   **Closest Neighbors:** Kleven & Waseem (2013) on notches; Allcott & Greenstone (2014) on energy gaps; Best & Kleven (2018) on property taxes.
*   **Strategy:** It builds on the bunching toolkit but synthesizes it with urban economics (market tightness).
*   **Conversation:** It is speaking to Environmental and Urban Econ. It could benefit from speaking to the **Housing Supply** literature (e.g., Gyourko or Glaeser) regarding how regulation creates "shadow" inventory or accelerates gentrification.
*   **Unexpected Connection:** It could connect to the literature on **Stranded Assets** in finance. These properties are essentially the "brown" assets of the residential world.

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

*   **Setup:** France introduces a phased ban on renting "G," "F," and "E" rated homes to save the planet.
*   **Tension:** In the aggregate, the data shows nothing. No bunching at the first deadline. A "failed" policy?
*   **Resolution:** The aggregate null is a lie. By splitting the data, we see two massive, opposing behaviors. In Paris, everyone renovates. In the provinces, everyone quits. 
*   **Implications:** Uniform climate standards across heterogeneous markets create geographic inequality in housing supply.

**Evaluation:** The arc is exceptionally strong for a bunching paper. It moves from a "puzzling null" to a "heterogeneous epiphany."

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

*   **Dinner Party Fact:** "The French climate ban didn't actually lead to renovations across most of the country; it just made it illegal to be a landlord in poor neighborhoods."
*   **Response:** People will lean in. It’s a classic "unintended consequences of virtuous policy" story.
*   **Follow-up:** "Where did the poor people living in those G-rated units go?" (The paper doesn't answer this yet, which is its main weakness).

---

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

*   **Front-loading:** The paper is well front-loaded. The "Small-property amendment" mentioned on page 4 is a goldmine—if the author can get more power there, it should be a centerpiece, not a footnote.
*   **Section 5.4 (Robustness):** Table 5 shows the result is sensitive to polynomial order (Order 5 vs Order 9). This is the "bunching Achilles heel." The author should move some of this to the appendix and focus the main text on the *difference* in bunching between regions, which is more robust than the *level*.
*   **Conclusion:** It’s good, but it should lean harder into the EU-wide implications.

---

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The gap is **Ambition/Scope**. Currently, it's a very clever "Applied Micro" paper using a standard tool. To be an AER paper, it needs to prove the "Retreat" mechanism. 

**The single most impactful change:** Track the "missing" units. If the author can link the DPE registry to transactions data or tax records to show that "missing mass" in the rental market appears as "excess mass" in the sales market (owner-occupiers) or vacancy, the paper becomes a definitive study on the general equilibrium effects of energy regulation.
