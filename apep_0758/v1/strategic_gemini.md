## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
This paper evaluates the impact of the largest administrative expansion of SNAP (food stamps) in modern history—Broad-Based Categorical Eligibility (BBCE)—on both program take-up and labor supply. Using staggered state-level adoption, it tests the central "access-efficiency" tradeoff: does making it easier for the working poor to get benefits increase enrollment without triggering a massive withdrawal from the labor force?

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this well in the first paragraph, specifically framing it as an empirical test of a 50-year-old welfare economics tension. However, the first paragraph is a bit "textbook." It should lead with the scale of the policy (the 40 million people/$100 billion mentioned later) to establish immediate stakes.

**The pitch the paper should have:** 
"Welfare design is haunted by the fear that expanding access necessarily erodes work incentives. This paper exploits the largest administrative expansion of the U.S. SNAP program—Broad-Based Categorical Eligibility—to provide a 'pure' test of this tradeoff. By decoupling eligibility from benefit levels, I isolate whether extending a safety net to 3.1 million additional households actually discourages work or merely provides consumption insurance to the working poor."

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**One-sentence contribution:** The paper identifies the causal effect of SNAP eligibility thresholds on labor supply, separately from benefit levels, using a staggered difference-in-differences design.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It is well-differentiated from Anders and Rafkin (2025) and Wang (2026) by shifting the focus from *enrollment* to *labor supply*.
*   **Framing:** It is currently framed as answering a question about the WORLD (the "access-efficiency tradeoff"), which is the right move for the AER.
*   **AER Quality?** At first glance, a smart economist might say, "It's another staggered DiD on SNAP." To avoid this, the paper needs to lean harder into the "pure eligibility" aspect—the idea that this is a unique laboratory to see a threshold move while the phase-out rate stays constant.
*   **Making it bigger:** To be a "slam dunk" AER paper, it needs to look at the **compositional shift**. Who are the new enrollees? Are they "hustlers" who keep working, or "marginal exiters"? Using the state-level ACS is fine for the headline, but the contribution becomes "AER-sized" if it can show *why* the labor supply didn't move (e.g., using QWI data to show it's mostly full-time workers in low-wage sectors).

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
*   **Neighbors:** Moffitt (2002) on theory; Hoynes and Schanzenbach (2016) on SNAP effects; Callaway and Sant’Anna (2021) on methods.
*   **Strategy:** The paper should **synthesize** these. It positions itself as the missing empirical link in the Moffitt framework.
*   **Missing Conversations:** The paper needs to speak more to the **"Administrative Burden"** literature (Herd & Moynihan). BBCE isn't just about income thresholds; it’s about the *humiliation* of asset tests. Connecting the lack of labor supply response to the reduction in "psychic costs" of the program would be a high-level AER move.
*   **Breadth:** Currently, it’s a bit US-centric. A brief nod to how other OECD countries manage "cliffs" would broaden the appeal.

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC
*   **Setup:** States had a rigid, "humiliating" eligibility gate for SNAP.
*   **Tension:** In the 2000s, they found a "loophole" (BBCE) to swing the gate open. Critics (and the Trump admin) argued this would destroy work incentives.
*   **Resolution:** The gate opened, participation rose, but—crucially—work didn't collapse.
*   **Implications:** The "efficiency cost" of the safety net may be vastly overstated for households near the poverty line.

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong. It feels like a "myth-busting" paper, which is a classic AER narrative.

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
*   **The Dinner Party Fact:** "The Trump administration tried to kick 3 million people off food stamps because they thought it made them lazy. It turns out, giving them those benefits didn't change their work hours at all."
*   **Response:** People will lean in. The "So What" is the high-stakes political conflict (2019 rule change) currently mentioned on page 5.
*   **Follow-up:** "If labor didn't change, did they spend the money on better food or just save it?" (The paper should anticipate this in the discussion).

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
*   **Front-loading:** The "Conceptual Framework" (Section 3) is a bit dry. I’d move the "Political Economy" (page 5) much earlier. The policy fight is the hook; the math is the proof.
*   **Appendix:** Table 7 (Standardized Effect Sizes) is a bit "social science-y" for the AER. Keep the focus on raw, interpretable units (percentage points of employment).
*   **Buried Treasure:** The mention of QWI data (low-education workers) in the appendix should be a main-text figure. Showing that even the *most* vulnerable workers didn't quit is a stronger result than the general population mean.

---

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
**The Gap:** Currently, it’s a very competent "Applied Micro" paper. To be an AER paper, it needs **Mechanisms**. 

A null result on labor supply is only exciting if you can explain *why* it’s null. Is it because the substitution effect perfectly cancels the income effect? Or is it because these households are "locked in" to jobs by other factors (childcare, fixed costs)?

**Single Biggest Advice:** Use the heterogeneity section to go beyond "High Poverty vs. Low Poverty." Categorize states by the **type of BBCE** they implemented (Income threshold vs. Asset test removal). If removing asset tests increases enrollment *without* affecting work, that is a massive, specific policy takeaway for the design of all means-tested programs.
