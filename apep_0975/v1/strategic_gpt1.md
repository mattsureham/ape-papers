## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper studies whether faster cross-border prosecutorial cooperation deters crime. Using staggered adoption of the European Investigation Order across EU countries, it asks a simple but important question: when states make it much easier for prosecutors to obtain evidence from abroad, do cross-border crimes fall—or do authorities simply uncover more of them?

A busy economist should care because this is a rare attempt to evaluate a “back-end” law-enforcement reform: not more police, harsher sentencing, or surveillance, but procedural state capacity in prosecution. If the paper is right, it suggests that improving legal cooperation may change measured crime through detection rather than actual offending—an idea with implications well beyond EU criminal justice.

**Does the paper itself articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Not quite. The current opening is vivid and competent, but it starts with institutional detail before landing the broader economic question. It reads like a legal-institutions paper first and an economics paper second. The paper should lead with the general issue—what happens when governments reduce frictions in prosecution—and only then introduce the EIO as the setting.

**What the first two paragraphs should say instead:**

> Criminal deterrence depends not only on police presence and punishment severity, but on whether the state can actually turn investigations into prosecutable cases. In cross-border crime, that link often breaks down: suspects, transactions, and evidence span jurisdictions, while prosecutors remain nationally bounded. This raises a basic question with broad relevance for economics: does improving prosecutorial cooperation across jurisdictions reduce crime, or mainly increase detection and recording of crimes that were already occurring?
>
> This paper studies that question using the European Investigation Order, an EU reform that sharply reduced the time required for one member state to compel investigative evidence from another. Exploiting staggered implementation across countries, I show that the reform did not reduce reported fraud, drug offences, or theft, but did increase reported cross-border crime relative to domestic crime. The central implication is that enforcement-capacity reforms may affect measured crime through detection more than deterrence.

That is the pitch. It is broader, cleaner, and more economic.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### What is the setup?
Cross-border crime creates a mismatch between where criminal activity occurs and where evidence is stored. Traditional enforcement systems are territorially fragmented, so prosecutors struggle to obtain admissible evidence from foreign jurisdictions in time.

### What is the tension?
Economic theory says raising the chance of conviction should deter crime. But many institutional reforms improve the state’s back-end capacity in ways offenders may not observe. So does better prosecutorial cooperation actually deter crime—or simply surface more of it?

### What is the resolution?
The paper finds no decline in reported fraud, drugs, or theft after EIO adoption, but it does find that reported “cross-border-relevant” crimes rise relative to domestic crimes. The paper interprets this as evidence consistent with detection rather than deterrence.

### What are the implications?
Evaluating enforcement reforms using reported crime alone may be misleading. Governments may improve prosecution capacity and uncover more crime without generating immediate deterrence; if so, conventional crime-rate metrics can perversely make successful reforms look ineffective.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
**Mostly serviceable, but not yet fully disciplined.** The ingredients are there, but the paper still feels somewhat like a set of estimates in search of a hierarchy:
- null effects on three categories,
- a placebo anomaly,
- a DDD result,
- a detection interpretation.

The real story should be much clearer:  
**This is a paper about why backend enforcement reforms may fail the deterrence test but succeed on detection.**

Right now, the paper spends too much energy proving it is a standard causal paper on the EIO and not enough building that conceptual arc. The null-by-category results should be treated as setup; the key object is the tension between deterrence and detection.

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

**What fact would I lead with at a dinner party?**

That is a decent opener. People would probably lean in for a moment, especially if phrased as: “Better prosecution can make crime statistics look worse.”

**Would people lean in or reach for their phones?**  
Lean in—briefly. But only if the presenter immediately makes the broader point. “An EU directive had no effect on fraud” is phone-grabbing. “Improving enforcement capacity may raise measured crime because you detect more of it” is interesting.

**What follow-up question would they ask?**  
Almost certainly: **“How do you know that’s detection rather than just reclassification or unrelated reporting changes?”**

That is the central strategic vulnerability. Not because of identification minutiae, but because the paper’s most interesting claim currently rests on indirect evidence. If the paper cannot answer that at a conceptual level, the finding remains intriguing but not fully persuasive.

**If findings are null or modest, is the null itself interesting?**  
Yes, but only if framed correctly. A null on deterrence is interesting here because the reform is large in administrative terms and directly targets prosecution frictions. The paper does make some case for why “this didn’t deter” matters. But it should press harder on the notion that visible and invisible enforcement margins may have very different behavioral effects. That turns a null into a substantive result rather than a failed experiment.
