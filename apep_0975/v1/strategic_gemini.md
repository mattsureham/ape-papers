---

### 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper examines whether a massive reduction in the cost and time of judicial cooperation—specifically, the European Investigation Order (EIO) which slashed evidence-sharing timelines from 15 months to 90 days—actually deters crime. Using the staggered transposition of this EU directive, the author finds no evidence of deterrence in fraud, drugs, or theft, but instead finds a significant increase in *reported* cross-border crime, suggesting that better enforcement tools might initially increase measured crime by making more cases "prosecutable."

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this well, but it spends a bit too much time on the "how" (staggered DiD) and not enough on the "why" in the opening hook.

**The Pitch the Paper Should Have:**
"Can procedural efficiency in the courtroom deter criminals on the street? We exploit a 'paradigm shift' in European law enforcement—the EIO—which mandated a 500% speed-up in cross-border evidence gathering. While the classic Beckerian model predicts a deterrence effect via increased conviction probability, we find that streamlining the 'back-end' of the justice system primarily serves to bring previously 'invisible' cross-border crimes onto the books, creating a detection dividend rather than a deterrent effect."

---

### 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution:** The paper provides the first causal evidence that procedural "back-end" judicial reforms (evidence-gathering speed) may be invisible to offenders and thus fail to provide deterrence, while simultaneously biasing measured crime rates upward through increased detection.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It is well-differentiated. Most crime literature focuses on "front-end" policing (more cops, more cameras). This looks at the "plumbing" of the legal system.
*   **World vs. Literature:** It frames itself as answering a question about the WORLD (Does the EIO work?), but it leans heavily on the "staggered DiD" literature for its technical legitimacy.
*   **The "So What":** A smart economist would say: "It's a paper about how judicial efficiency might actually make crime stats look worse because we're finally catching people we used to ignore."
*   **Bigger Contribution:** To make this truly "AER-big," the author needs to move beyond "I found a null for deterrence" and more deeply prove the "detection" mechanism. Is there data on case filings vs. street crime? Without it, the "detection channel" is a very plausible guess, but still a guess.

---

### 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

*   **Closest Neighbors:** Becker (1968) on the theory; Chalfin & McCrary (2017) on deterrence vs. detection; Agan, Doleac, & Harvey (2023) on prosecution effects.
*   **Positioning:** It builds on Becker but challenges the *visibility* of the conviction-probability channel. It should more aggressively position itself as a critique of using "reported crime" as a metric for policy success in institutional reforms.
*   **The Conversation:** The paper is currently in a "Crime & Law" conversation. It could gain traction by speaking to the "State Capacity" literature—how increasing the legibility of the state (its ability to see across borders) changes the data the state itself produces.

---

### 4. NARRATIVE ARC

*   **Setup:** The "Prosecution Gap"—cross-border crime is easy to commit but hard to prove because of 19th-century-style diplomacy.
*   **Tension:** The EU fixes this with a "paradigm shift" (the EIO). This *should* deter criminals if they are rational.
*   **Resolution:** Deterrence is nowhere to be found. In fact, cross-border crime "increases" by 98% relative to domestic crime.
*   **Implications:** The EIO isn't a failure; it’s a mirror. It’s showing us crime that was always there. We need better metrics than "reported crime" to evaluate judicial reforms.

---

### 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

**The Dinner Party Fact:** "The EU made it 5x faster to catch cross-border criminals, and the result was that the crime rate 'doubled' because the police finally had the paperwork to acknowledge the crimes were happening."

**The Follow-up:** "If it didn't deter them, is it because criminals are too dumb to know the law changed, or because the 90-day window is still too slow?" This is the core tension the paper needs to explore.

---

### 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

*   **Front-loading:** The "Triple-Difference" (Table 4) is the most interesting part of the paper by far. The "Category-by-category nulls" (Table 3) feel like a preamble. Move the DDD higher.
*   **Appendix:** The "Old vs. New Member States" in Table 6 is a goldmine. If the EIO worked better in "New" members with weaker prior institutions, that's a story.
*   **The "Detection" Proof:** The author admits they don't have "case clearance rates." Without this, the narrative is "results looking for a story." Finding even a small proxy for "prosecutorial effort" would anchor the whole paper.

---

### 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

**The Gap:** Currently, it's a very high-quality "Policy Evaluation" paper that would likely sail into *JPE Micro* or *AEJ: Policy*. To hit the *AER*, it needs to move from "Evaluating a Directive" to "A General Theory of Back-End Deterrence."

**The Single Biggest Advice:** Abandon the modesty of a "well-powered null." Reframe the paper entirely around the **"Measurement Trap of State Capacity."** Argue that the most effective institutional reforms are the ones that are most likely to be judged as "failures" by standard crime statistics. This turns a "failed" deterrence paper into a "must-read" paper for anyone doing empirical work on institutions.
