### 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper uses a boundary discontinuity design (BDD) to test whether the sharp, formula-driven reductions in UK police officer numbers (2010–2018) led to localized increases in crime. While a cross-sectional analysis suggests a massive "effect," the author uses a 14-year panel to show that the crime gap at these borders is entirely pre-existing and unresponsive to both the "austerity" cuts and the subsequent "uplift" hiring. It is a cautionary tale about the limits of geographic RDDs when administrative boundaries coincide with historical economic "fault lines."

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this clearly. The first two paragraphs effectively set the scene (the "fiscal accident" of austerity) and the stakes (15 million people in "left behind" regions). It doesn't bury the lead—it admits in the abstract and the intro that this is essentially a "null result" paper regarding the causal effect, but a "positive result" paper regarding methodological pitfalls.

### 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**The Contribution:** The paper demonstrates that the spatial correlation between police funding and crime in the UK is driven by historical geographic sorting rather than a causal policing channel, providing a methodological template for using temporal "pre-trends" to validate spatial RDDs.

*   **Differentiation:** It differentiates itself from the "police prevent crime" literature (Levitt 1997, Draca 2011) by showing why a common identification strategy (BDD) fails in this specific, high-stakes context.
*   **Question about the World vs. Literature:** It starts with the world (Austerity in the UK) but quickly pivots to a question about the literature (Can BDD actually identify this?).
*   **"Another DiD/RDD paper":** A smart economist would see this as a "Skeptic’s RDD." It’s not just another application; it’s an autopsy of an application.
*   **Making it bigger:** To make this an AER-level contribution, the author needs to move beyond "this specific BDD failed" to "here is a generalizable framework for when and why administrative boundaries are endogenous to economic outcomes."

### 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

*   **Closest Neighbors:** Dell (2010) on *mita* boundaries; Keele and Titiunik (2015) on geographic RDD; Draca, Machin, and Witt (2011) on UK policing.
*   **Positioning:** The paper "respectfully undermines" the naive application of the Dell-style BDD. It builds on Keele and Titiunik’s formal warnings by providing a "real-world" case study of failure.
*   **The Right Conversation?** Currently, it’s a bit too focused on the UK austerity context. To reach the AER’s general interest audience, it should lean harder into the **econometrics of spatial design**. It should speak to people doing RDDs in urban economics, political economy, and development.

### 4. NARRATIVE ARC

*   **Setup:** The 2010 UK austerity program created a natural experiment with massive, varying cuts to police forces.
*   **Tension:** A simple spatial comparison suggests a large effect, but the direction is "wrong" (less police = less crime), creating a theoretical puzzle.
*   **Resolution:** The event study (Figure 4) reveals the "effect" is a permanent geographic fixture, not a response to policy.
*   **Implications:** Proximity does not equal comparability. We must use time-series diagnostics to "placebo-test" spatial discontinuities.

**Evaluation:** The arc is exceptionally strong. It’s a "detective story" where the detective realizes the prime suspect has a rock-solid alibi (the pre-existing gap).

### 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

*   **The Fact:** "If you look at the border between a high-cut and low-cut police force in England, there's an 18% crime gap that hasn't changed a bit in 14 years, despite the police force sizes swinging by 30%."
*   **Reaction:** People will lean in. It’s a counter-intuitive finding that challenges the "common sense" narrative often found in the *Guardian* or *BBC* about austerity causing the crime wave.
*   **Follow-up:** "If it's not the police, what *is* the boundary actually marking?" (The author touches on this—it’s the "fault lines" of the UK economy—but could go deeper).

### 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

*   **Front-loading:** Figure 4 is the "money shot." The paper does a good job of getting there by page 17, but the "Conceptual Framework" (Section 3) could be tighter to move the results up.
*   **Appendix:** The "Donut RDD" and "Placebo Cutoffs" are vital for the skepticism narrative; they are currently in the appendix but the most interesting parts should be woven into the main text to reinforce the "Why the BDD Fails" section.

### 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The gap is **ambition**. Right now, it’s a very high-quality "Comment" or "Short Paper" on the UK context. To be a full AER article, it needs to be the **definitive paper on the endogeneity of administrative borders.**

**The Single Most Impactful Piece of Advice:**
Shift the framing from "UK Police Austerity" to "The Endogeneity of Administrative Geography." Use the police data as the primary case study, but add a secondary analysis (perhaps using the same borders with a completely unrelated variable like house prices or health outcomes) to prove that these borders are "systemically endogenous" across multiple dimensions of the UK economy.
