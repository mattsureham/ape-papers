**Referee Report**

**1. Idea Fidelity**
The paper largely adheres to the original research design outlined in the Idea Manifest. It correctly employs the Callaway-Sant'Anna (CS) staggered DiD estimator on state-level alcohol consumption data (NIAAA) to estimate the effect of "large" (≥$0.25/pack) state cigarette tax increases. It delivers the promised beverage-type decomposition (beer, wine, spirits) and performs a welfare computation using an external alcohol externality cost. However, the paper deviates in two notable ways from the original plan: 1) It restricts the analysis to the *first* large tax increase per state, analyzing 49 events rather than the 143 events mentioned in the Manifest. This choice, while simplifying the staggered design, discards a significant portion of the identifying variation and may affect external validity. 2) The Manifest suggested testing a specific mechanism (complements vs. substitutes via social vs. home consumption). While the beverage decomposition is presented, the paper does not formally develop or test this mechanism hypothesis; the interpretation remains speculative. Overall, the core identification strategy and research question are faithfully executed, albeit with a more restrictive treatment definition.

**2. Summary**
This paper provides a rigorous, null-result estimate of the cross-substance spillover from state cigarette excise taxes to per capita alcohol consumption in the US (2001-2019). Using a modern staggered difference-in-differences design, the author finds a precisely estimated near-zero effect, bounding potential substitution or complementarity effects to be economically small. The primary contribution is to demonstrate that, for the purpose of setting optimal Pigouvian cigarette taxes, policymakers can likely ignore cross-market spillovers to alcohol without introducing substantial welfare error.

**3. Essential Points**
The following critical issues must be addressed for publication:
1.  **Threats to Identification and Robustness of the Null:** The central finding is a null effect. The burden of proof for a credible null is high, requiring a persuasive case that the design had sufficient power to detect a reasonable alternative and that the null is not an artifact of identification failures. The paper must: (a) Conduct a formal power analysis or sensitivity analysis (e.g., using methods from Rambachan and Roth, 2023) to state the Minimum Detectable Effect (MDE). What magnitude of cross-elasticity *could* the design have detected? (b) More aggressively address the threat that states implementing cigarette tax hikes may have contemporaneously changed other policies (e.g., alcohol taxes, smoking bans, public health funding) that independently affect alcohol consumption. A simple check excluding three states is insufficient. The author should systematically test for correlation between cigarette tax increases and changes in state-level alcohol taxes, using data from the Alcohol Policy Information System (APIS), and control for them or show their changes are orthogonal.
2.  **Treatment Definition and Welfare Implications:** The decision to use only the *first* "large" tax increase is consequential and under-justified. It discards valuable within-state variation and may select for a non-representative set of policy shocks (e.g., early adopters vs. late adopters). The welfare calculation is then based on the ATT from this selected subset. The author must either: (a) Robustly justify this choice over a design that uses all tax increases (perhaps using the "stacked" estimator or continuous treatment), or (b) Show that the estimated ATT and the ensuing welfare conclusion are not sensitive to this choice. For instance, does using a continuous measure of the tax change (dollars per pack) yield a similar null conclusion on the intensive margin?
3.  **Interpretation of Beverage-Specific Results and Mechanism:** The paper posits that effects concentrated in beer would support a "social complementarity" mechanism but finds imprecise negative estimates for all beverages. The pre-trends for spirits are concerning (significant coefficients at k=-2, -1). The author must: (a) Formally address the validity of the parallel trends assumption for spirits, perhaps by showing results are robust to using only beer and total consumption. (b) Move beyond speculative interpretation. If the goal is to test the social vs. home consumption mechanism, the analysis could be enriched by incorporating state-year level moderators (e.g., per-capita bar/restaurant employment from the BLS, survey data on drinking location). Without this, the beverage decomposition is merely descriptive.

**4. Suggestions**
*Suggestions for Improvement (Non-Essential but Highly Recommended):*
*   **Heterogeneity Analysis:** The aggregate null could mask offsetting effects in different subpopulations. The author should explore heterogeneity by: (1) **Baseline smoking prevalence:** Use BRFSS or CDC STATE data to split states into high/low smoking rate groups. Spillovers may be stronger where more consumers are at the margin. (2) **Tax Magnitude:** Interact the treatment with the size of the tax increase (e.g., above/below median). Large increases may trigger different behavioral responses. (3) **Border State Status:** Create an indicator for states with neighboring states that have substantially lower cigarette taxes, as cross-border shopping could attenuate the effective price shock.
*   **Refine the Welfare Calculation:** The welfare computation in Section 5.1 is back-of-the-envelope. To be useful, it should: (1) Explicitly derive the modified Pigouvian tax formula with a cross-elasticity term, citing theoretical work (e.g., the multi-good optimal taxation framework from Gruber & Köszegi, 2004). (2) Calculate the *range* of optimal tax adjustments implied by the confidence interval of the ATT, not just the point estimate. (3) Acknowledge and discuss the limitation that the alcohol externality cost estimate ($2.05/drink) is an average; the marginal drinker affected by a cigarette tax may have higher or lower external costs.
*   **Address Measurement and Power Concerns:** (1) **Measurement Error:** Acknowledge that the NIAAA "apparent consumption" data include sales, not necessarily consumption, and could be noisy due to tourism and cross-border shopping. Consider a robustness check using self-reported consumption from national surveys (like the BRFSS, aggregated to the state-year level), despite its limitations, as an alternative outcome. (2) **Statistical Power:** The standard errors are sizable. Could precision be improved by using a more efficient estimator (e.g., the doubly-robust estimator of Callaway & Sant'Anna) or by incorporating pre-treatment outcome lags as covariates?
*   **Presentation and Nuance:** (1) **Abstract/Conclusion:** The abstract and conclusion should more clearly frame the null result as a *positive* contribution—informing policy that complex cross-good corrections are unnecessary—rather than a failure to find an effect. (2) **TWFE Comparison:** The discussion of TWFE vs. CS is good. Elaborate briefly on *why* the weights might be negative in this context (e.g., if early-treated states have different trends or effect sizes). (3) **Alternative Explanations for Null:** Expand Section 5.2. For example, discuss the potential role of addiction models: if cigarette addiction is strong, the income effect from a price increase may be minimal, leaving little budget to reallocate to alcohol.
*   **Minor Technical Points:** (1) In Table 2 (Event Study), label the pre-period coefficients as a "Parallel Trends Test" in the note. (2) In the robustness table (Table 3), report the number of *treatment events* (state-cohorts) for each threshold, not just the number of states. (3) The standardized effect sizes in the Appendix are not conventionally labeled. "Large negative" for an SDE of -0.2 is misleading; these are small to moderate effects. Use a standard scale (e.g., Cohen's d) or remove the labels. (4) Ensure all citations in the reference list are complete and match the text.

Overall, this is a well-executed paper on a policy-relevant question. The use of a rigorous DiD estimator is a strength. The core task for revision is to transform the presented null result from a potentially underpowered or confounded finding into a definitive, well-defended contribution to the literature on sin tax interdependence.
