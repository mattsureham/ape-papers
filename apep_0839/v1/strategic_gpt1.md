## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple, policy-relevant question: when the U.S. permanently raised SNAP benefits by the largest amount in program history, did that materially increase participation or reduce deprivation? Its headline claim is narrower but potentially interesting: the permanent increase was swamped by the withdrawal of much larger temporary pandemic-era supplements, producing a sharp “take-up cliff” in SNAP caseloads when Emergency Allotments ended.

A busy economist should care because this is not really just a SNAP paper. It is about a broader design problem in social insurance: temporary crisis expansions may reset reference points and participation incentives in ways that modest permanent expansions cannot undo.

**Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Not quite. The opening starts with the largest permanent increase in SNAP history and invites the reader to expect a paper about poverty and benefit generosity. But the paper then quickly concedes that the poverty analysis is not convincing and, indeed, not the right outcome given the official poverty measure. The real paper is about the interaction between permanent benefit reforms and the unwind of temporary emergency programs. That should be the lead, not the third-paragraph reveal.

**What the first two paragraphs should say instead:**

> In 2021, the federal government permanently increased SNAP benefits by 21 percent, the largest lasting benefit increase in the program’s history. But for many households, that reform arrived while pandemic-era Emergency Allotments were still delivering even larger temporary supplements. This creates a sharp policy question: when a permanent expansion overlaps with the withdrawal of a much larger temporary one, what happens to program participation?
>
> This paper shows that the unwind dominated the reform. Using cross-state variation in the timing of Emergency Allotment expiration, I find that SNAP caseloads fell sharply when temporary supplements ended, even though the permanent Thrifty Food Plan increase was already in place. The broader lesson is that crisis-era safety-net expansions can create benefit floors and participation norms that later permanent reforms are too small to sustain.

That is the paper’s best story. Lead with it.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution in one sentence:**  
The paper argues that the 2021 permanent SNAP benefit increase did not prevent a substantial drop in SNAP participation when larger temporary pandemic Emergency Allotments were withdrawn, implying that temporary benefit expansions can create participation levels that modest permanent reforms cannot maintain.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest papers?
Only partially. Right now the paper presents itself as contributing to three literatures at once—take-up elasticity, pandemic safety-net unwinds, and poverty effects of SNAP—but only one of those is really carrying weight. The poverty piece is not persuasive enough to anchor a contribution, and the take-up elasticity angle is too indirect and imprecise in the baseline design. The distinctive piece is the **interaction of a permanent formula reform with the unwind of temporary emergency generosity**. That needs to be much more clearly differentiated from generic SNAP take-up or generic COVID safety-net papers.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a gap in a literature?
It starts as a world question, then lapses into literature-gap language. The stronger framing is the world question:

- **How do beneficiaries respond when a large temporary expansion expires but a smaller permanent increase remains?**
- **Can permanent reforms preserve the participation gains generated by emergency expansions?**

That is stronger than “there is limited evidence on the transition from pandemic-era to steady-state policy.”

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the intro?
At present, maybe not cleanly. They might say: “It’s another state-level DiD on SNAP after COVID, with a null poverty result and a triple-diff on EA timing.” That is not what you want. You want them to say: “It shows that the permanent SNAP increase was too small to offset the participation drop when emergency benefits ended—more generally, emergency expansions create cliffs when policy normalizes.”

### What would make this contribution bigger?
Several concrete possibilities:

1. **Use outcomes that better match the claim.**  
   If the paper is about a “take-up cliff,” then participation/caseload dynamics, recertification, churn, exits, and re-entry are first-order outcomes. Poverty is not.

2. **Show the margin more directly.**  
   The paper currently infers take-up and value thresholds from state-level caseloads. A bigger paper would distinguish whether the cliff came from:
   - reduced entry,
   - increased exits,
   - failed recertifications,
   - composition changes among households near the income threshold.

3. **Connect to welfare-relevant outcomes.**  
   If participation fell, did food hardship rise? Did food spending fall? Did charitable food use rise? That would turn a program-administration fact into an economic fact.

4. **Make the comparison explicit.**  
   The paper’s core insight is comparative: **temporary expansions may be much more consequential than permanent formula changes**. That is stronger than just documenting one caseload decline. Lean into the contrast between a 21% permanent increase and the much larger effective drop induced by EA expiration.

5. **Generalize the framework.**  
   A bigger contribution would not just say “this happened in SNAP,” but “this is a recurring feature of social policy transitions when temporary generosity exceeds permanent generosity.” The conclusion gestures at this; the introduction should build the paper around it.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
The government permanently raised SNAP benefits by an unprecedented amount in 2021. Standard intuition says higher benefits should increase program value, possibly participation, and perhaps reduce hardship.

### Tension
But the reform occurred during the pandemic, when many households were already receiving much larger temporary Emergency Allotments. So the nominally historic permanent increase may have felt, in practice, like a cut once emergency supplements disappeared. The key puzzle is whether permanent reform could preserve participation once temporary generosity ended.

### Resolution
The poverty analysis does not deliver much. But the triple-difference suggests a large decline in SNAP caseloads associated with EA withdrawal, and the permanent TFP increase did not offset it.

### Implications
Temporary crisis expansions can create participation plateaus or benefit expectations that smaller permanent reforms cannot sustain. This has implications well beyond SNAP for how policymakers design exits from emergency programs.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
Only in embryonic form. Right now it reads like:
1. big reform,
2. null poverty result,
3. maybe take-up,
4. actually the interesting thing is the triple-diff.

That is a **collection of results looking for a story**. The story is there, but it is misordered. The paper should not pretend the poverty analysis is the centerpiece. It should say:

- Here is a major permanent reform.
- Here is why its timing makes naïve evaluation misleading.
- Here is the overlooked institutional variation that lets us learn about the transition from emergency to permanent benefits.
- Here is the key finding: the unwind dominates.

That would produce an actual narrative arc.

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?
“Even the largest permanent increase in SNAP history did not stop caseloads from falling sharply once the much bigger temporary pandemic supplements ended.”

That is the line.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
Some would lean in—because it speaks to a broader post-COVID policy question. But they will lean in only if the presenter immediately makes clear that this is about **policy cliffs after emergency expansions**, not just one more reduced-form SNAP paper.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Almost certainly:  
**“Is that because household need fell, because benefits became less attractive relative to the pandemic peak, or because states that ended early also changed administration or politics?”**

That is the natural follow-up. The paper anticipates some of this, but because the central contribution is basically one triple-diff estimate, readers will immediately interrogate interpretation. Again, I am not evaluating identification, but strategically that means the paper must define the claim carefully: not “SNAP doesn’t matter,” but “the normalization of benefits can generate large participation losses that permanent increases do not fully offset.”

### Are the modest/null findings interesting?
The poverty null is not interesting in its current form. In fact, the paper itself admits that official poverty is the wrong measure for a direct effect of SNAP because SNAP is excluded from OPM income. Once the paper says that, the reader wonders why they spent so much time on poverty at all. That section weakens the paper strategically.

The null on the direct take-up effect of the TFP increase could be interesting if framed as:
- large permanent changes in generosity may have modest participation effects in the short run, **especially when overshadowed by much larger temporary benefits**.

But that is not yet developed sharply enough.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

At present, this is **not an AER paper in strategic positioning**. The gap is mostly not econometric polish; it is that the paper is trying to be a larger paper than the evidence currently supports, while under-selling the one thing that might actually travel.

Primarily:
- **a framing problem**, and
- **an ambition/scope problem**.

#### Framing problem
The best contribution is buried. The paper should stop presenting itself as a paper about whether the 2021 SNAP reform reduced poverty. That is not the right question, not the right outcome, and not the right headline.

#### Scope problem
Even with better framing, the current evidence base feels thin for AER because the paper leans heavily on a single aggregate caseload result. To excite top people in this field, it likely needs either:

- a more powerful general claim tied to broader policy transitions.

The broad domain—COVID-era safety net unwinds—is active. So the novelty has to be the **specific conceptual insight about transition design**, not just another state-level treatment-effect estimate.

#### Ambition problem
The paper is competent but safe. It documents an interesting empirical pattern, but it does not yet convert that pattern into a major economics question.

### Single most impactful advice
**Rewrite the paper as a paper about the economics of policy unwinds—using SNAP’s 2021 reform and Emergency Allotment expiration as a case study—and demote the poverty analysis from centerpiece to sidebar.**

That one change would immediately improve contribution clarity, audience, and narrative coherence.
