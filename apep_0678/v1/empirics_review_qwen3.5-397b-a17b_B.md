1. **Idea Fidelity**

The paper deviates significantly from the Original Idea Manifest in three critical dimensions, altering the statistical power and specificity of the proposed research. First, the **unit of analysis** shifted from 371 Local Authorities (LAs) to 11 regions/countries. The manifest promised ~4,081 observations using LA-level data (NOMIS NM_161_1 and NRS council data); the paper delivers 121 observations using aggregated Government Office Regions. This drastically reduces degrees of freedom and weakens the staggered DiD design. Second, the **outcome variable** changed from K70 (Alcoholic Liver Disease) specifically to broad "Alcohol-Specific Deaths" (including acute intoxication, mental disorders, etc.). While broader outcomes capture more harm, K70 was specified in the manifest as the primary mechanism for chronic harm among heavy drinkers targeted by MUP. Third, the **data sources** shifted from NOMIS/NRS direct downloads to OHID Fingertips and ONS bulletins. While these are authoritative, the aggregation to regional levels appears to be a convenience choice rather than a data availability constraint, as LA-level alcohol-specific mortality is publicly available from ONS/NOMIS. These deviations transform a high-powered local-level study into a low-powered regional comparison, fundamentally changing the contribution relative to the proposed plan.

2. **Summary**

This paper evaluates the causal effect of Minimum Unit Pricing (MUP) on alcohol-specific mortality using the staggered adoption in Scotland (2018) and Wales (2020) versus England. Using a regional panel (11 units) and difference-in-differences estimators, the authors find a statistically significant reduction in mortality rates in treated nations relative to England, estimating approximately 140 deaths averted annually in Scotland. The study contributes timely evidence on the durability of MUP effects through 2023 and highlights distributional consequences via deprivation gradients. However, the aggregation of data to the regional level limits statistical inference and obscures local heterogeneity promised in the initial design.

3. **Essential Points**

1.  **Statistical Power and Unit of Analysis:** The reduction from 371 Local Authorities to 11 regions severely compromises the identification strategy. With only two treated units (Scotland, Wales) and nine control regions, the Callaway-Sant'Anna estimator and TWFE rely on extremely few clusters. The reported cluster-robust standard errors with 11 clusters are known to be biased downward (Angrist and Pischke 2009); the permutation test on three countries yields a non-significant p-value (0.164). The claim of causal identification is weakened by this aggregation, as unobserved regional shocks (e.g., specific health board policies in Scotland vs. English regions) cannot be disentangled from the treatment effect with so few units.
2.  **Outcome Specificity and Mechanism:** The shift from K70 (liver disease) to broad alcohol-specific deaths dilutes the test of the specific MUP mechanism. MUP is theorized to reduce chronic harm among heavy drinkers of cheap high-strength alcohol (linked to K70), whereas broad measures include acute deaths (e.g., poisoning) which may be less price-elastic or driven by different behaviors. Without showing that the effect is driven by chronic conditions (K70) rather than acute incidents, the link between the price floor and the specific physiological mechanism remains ambiguous.
3.  **Pre-Trend Volatility and COVID Confounding:** While the linear pre-trend F-test passes (p=0.619), the event study coefficients show substantial volatility in the pre-period (e.g., significant positive spikes in 2014–2015 for Scotland). Furthermore, the Wales treatment coincides almost exactly with the COVID lockdown (March 2020). The paper acknowledges this but retains Wales in the main staggered estimator. Given the anomalous 2022 coefficient for Wales (+1.3), including Wales in the aggregate ATT may introduce bias rather than precision. The robustness check excluding 2020–2021 helps, but the primary specification should arguably focus on Scotland as the cleanest treated unit.

4. **Suggestions**

The following recommendations aim to strengthen the causal claims, improve statistical validity, and align the paper more closely with the high-powered design originally proposed.

**Recover Local Authority-Level Data:** The most critical improvement would be to return to the LA-level design outlined in the manifest. ONS publishes alcohol-specific death rates at the Local Authority level (England and Wales) and NRS provides this for Scottish Councils. Aggregating to 371 units would allow for unit-level fixed effects and robust cluster-robust inference (clustered by LA). This would also enable a more rigorous Callaway-Sant'Anna implementation with many treated units rather than just two countries. If LA-level rates are suppressed due to small counts, consider pooling over 2-3 years or using negative binomial count models on raw death counts with population offsets, which preserves variation better than rates.

**Refine the Outcome to K70:** To better test the theoretical mechanism, please present results specifically for K70 (Alcoholic Liver Disease) alongside the broad measure. The manifest identified K70 as the key outcome because it reflects chronic damage most associated with the cheap, high-strength alcohol targeted by MUP. If the effect is driven primarily by acute causes (e.g., F10 mental/behavioral disorders or accidental poisoning), the policy implication changes. A table comparing effect sizes across ICD-10 subgroups (K70 vs. F10 vs. Accidents) would significantly deepen the analysis and address the mechanism concern.

**Improve Inference for Few Clusters:** If the authors retain the regional panel, standard cluster-robust standard errors are inappropriate for 11 clusters. I recommend implementing the wild-cluster bootstrap (Cameron, Gelbach, and Miller 2008) or Conley spatial HAC standard errors to account for spatial correlation across regions. Additionally, the permutation test on the country-level panel (3 units) should be interpreted with extreme caution; consider dropping the country-level TWFE from the main results and focusing on the regional panel with corrected inference, or explicitly framing the country-level results as descriptive rather than causal.

**Re-evaluate the Wales Cohort:** Given the severe COVID confounding for Wales (treatment start March 2020), consider presenting Scotland-only estimates as the primary specification. Wales can be included in a secondary specification or appendix. The current staggered estimator averages the Scotland and Wales effects; if Wales is biased due to pandemic mortality shocks (which affected alcohol deaths non-linearly), the aggregate ATT is contaminated. A "Scotland vs. England" DiD with 2018 treatment is cleaner and should be the headline result, with Wales added as a robustness check.

**Address Synthetic Control Failure:** The paper honestly reports that the synthetic control fails due to poor pre-fit (Scotland's baseline is too high). However, including this in the main robustness table alongside successful tests may confuse readers about the paper's overall validity. I suggest moving the synthetic control to an appendix with a detailed explanation of why DiD is preferred in this specific context (level differences vs. trend differences), citing Abadie (2021) as the paper does, but not presenting it as a parallel robustness check that "failed."

**Clarify Deprivation Mechanism:** The deprivation gradient analysis is compelling but currently descriptive (England only). To strengthen the causal claim regarding equity, interact the MUP treatment indicator with baseline deprivation levels at the LA or region level. If MUP effects are larger in high-deprivation areas (as theory predicts), a triple-difference specification (Treatment × Post × HighDeprivation) would provide direct causal evidence of the distributional impact, rather than inferring it from England's worsening trend alone.

**Data Transparency and Code:** Given the deviation from the manifest's data sources, please provide a detailed data appendix linking the specific OHID/NRS indicators used to the manifest's proposed NOMIS codes. Ensure that the age-standardization methods are perfectly harmonized across the three nations; slight differences in standardization populations (ESP 2013 vs. others) can create artificial trends. Code for the harmonization process should be made available to verify that the "England vs. Scotland" gap is not an artifact of differing statistical definitions.

**Discussion of Substitution Effects:** The manifest proposed a drug substitution test (F11-F19 deaths). The paper mentions this in the manifest but does not report results in the LaTeX source. Including this test is vital to address the "substitution toward other substances" critique mentioned in the introduction. If alcohol deaths fell but drug deaths rose in Scotland relative to England, the net welfare benefit is ambiguous. Adding this analysis would round out the policy evaluation significantly.

**Title and Abstract Alignment:** Finally, update the title and abstract to reflect the regional-level analysis rather than implying the LA-level precision suggested in the manifest. Phrases like "across UK Nations" are accurate, but ensure the abstract clarifies the unit of observation (regions vs. local authorities) to manage reader expectations regarding statistical power.

By implementing these suggestions, particularly recovering the LA-level data and refining the outcome measure, the paper can fulfill the promise of the original idea and make a definitive contribution to the economics of public health policy.
