This is a promising paper with a real idea in it, but in its current form it reads more like a competent historical application of an existing mechanism than a field-defining paper. The strategic question is whether the author can make this feel like a paper about how immigration policy reshaped the technology of household production and thereby altered the path of women’s market work in America—not just a historical reverse-Cortes-Tessada exercise.

## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether shutting off low-skill immigration in 1924 increased or decreased native-born women’s labor supply. Its core claim is that the Johnson-Reed Act reduced women’s labor force participation in places that had relied more heavily on Southern and Eastern European immigrants, because those immigrants supplied domestic labor that made market work feasible for native women.

Why should a busy economist care? Because the paper flips the standard “restriction helps natives by reducing labor market competition” story, and it suggests that immigration policy can affect labor supply not only through labor-market substitution but through household production and care/service complementarities.

### Does the paper articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Not quite. The current introduction has the ingredients, but the first two paragraphs are trying to do too many things at once: historical setup, Collins, Cortes-Tessada, and a prediction about unmarried women. The paper’s opening should be simpler, sharper, and more world-facing.

Right now the introduction feels like: “there are two channels, we test them.” That is fine for a workshop paper; it is not yet an AER-caliber opening. The best version is: “Immigration policy changed the supply of household labor, and that changed native women’s ability to work.” That is the big idea. Everything else should serve that.

### The pitch the paper should have

A stronger first-two-paragraph pitch would be something like:

> Immigration policy does more than change who competes for jobs. It also changes who performs the household services—cleaning, laundry, childcare, cooking—that enable other people to supply labor. This paper studies that broader channel in one of the most consequential immigration restrictions in U.S. history: the 1924 Johnson-Reed Act, which sharply reduced immigration from Southern and Eastern Europe, a major source of domestic servants.
>
> I show that places more exposed to this restriction saw lower labor force participation among native-born white women, not higher. The reason is that immigration restriction withdrew a key input into household production. Rather than freeing native women from immigrant competition, the Act reduced the availability of domestic service that had enabled women’s market work. The paper therefore reframes immigration restriction as a shock to household production, with consequences for the evolution of female labor supply.

That is the version a busy economist will remember.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### Contribution in one sentence

The paper’s contribution is to show that the 1924 immigration restriction reduced native-born women’s labor supply in more exposed places because it contracted the supply of immigrant domestic labor, implying complementarity rather than simple labor-market substitution.

### Is this clearly differentiated from the closest papers?

Only partially. The paper repeatedly says “historical Cortes-Tessada,” which is directionally useful but strategically risky. If that is all readers remember, then the paper sounds derivative: a reverse-shock historical replication of a known mechanism.

The paper needs to distinguish itself more clearly from at least four neighboring strands:

1. **Cortes and Tessada (2011)** — modern low-skilled immigration and high-skilled native women’s labor supply via household services.
2. **Furtado and Hock / Furtado-type literature** — immigration, household services, childcare, and female labor supply.
3. **Collins (1997)** and related historical immigration-restriction work — native upgrading and labor-market competition after restriction.
4. **Tabellini / Abramitzky-Boustan-Eriksson type historical immigration papers** — local labor market and longer-run impacts of immigration.

At present, the contribution is differentiated mainly by time period and data scale. That is not enough for AER. It needs to be differentiated conceptually.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a literature gap?

Mostly literature-gap. The paper says “first individual-level evidence on the Cortes-Tessada channel in the historical setting,” which is exactly the kind of sentence that undersells itself.

The stronger framing is world-facing: **What happens to women’s work when immigration policy removes market substitutes for household production?** That is a substantive question about the world. The historical episode is the setting, not the reason the paper matters.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?

Right now they might say: “It’s a linked-census DiD showing that immigration restriction reduced women’s labor supply because domestic servants disappeared.” That is decent, but still perilously close to “another Bartik paper about immigration.”

You want them to say: “It shows that one of the most famous immigration restrictions in U.S. history functioned as a shock to household production, not just to labor-market competition, and that this helps explain women’s labor supply.” That is bigger.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Most importantly: **elevate the object of study from domestic servants to household production and female labor supply.**

Specific ways to enlarge the contribution:
- **Focus more on heterogeneity that maps to household production needs**, especially married women with children or family structure proxies. The paper gestures at this, but the contribution would look much bigger if the central empirical contrast were between women more and less likely to need outsourced household labor.
- **Clarify whether the contribution is about the path of the gender revolution.** If so, lean in. Right now that claim appears but is not central.
- **Say less about “historical validation of Cortes-Tessada” and more about overturning the canonical restriction-benefits-natives story for women.**
- **Separate the two channels more narratively**: labor-market competition vs household-production complementarity. The paper currently mentions both, but the competition channel feels like a straw prediction rather than a deeply engaged rival framework.
- **Use outcomes or framing tied to work-family constraints**, not just LFP and domestic-service employment. Even if the available outcomes are limited, the paper can conceptually center the work/home margin.

The biggest upgrade would be to make the paper feel like it changes how we think about **immigration policy as family economics**, not just labor economics.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

### Closest neighbors

The nearest papers/fields seem to be:

- **Cortes and Tessada (2011, JPE/AER-era canonical paper)** on low-skilled immigration and high-skilled U.S. women’s labor supply via household services.
- **Barone and Mocetti (2011/2012-type European evidence)** on service immigration and female labor supply.
- **Farré, González, Ortega / related immigration-household service-female labor supply papers** in Europe.
- **Collins (1997)** on immigration restriction and native occupational upgrading.
- **Tabellini (2020)** and broader historical immigration literature on local labor market effects.
- Likely also **Abramitzky, Boustan, Eriksson and coauthors** on historical immigration, local impacts, and linked census data.
- On women’s labor supply and household technology: **Goldin (1990, 2006)**, **Greenwood-Seshadri-Yorukoglu (2005)**, possibly **Jones, Greenwood et al.** on household production and structural change.

### How should the paper position itself relative to those neighbors?

- **Build on Cortes-Tessada, but do not sell itself as a simple historical extension.**
- **Directly challenge the Collins-style competition framing**, at least for women. That is where the tension is.
- **Bridge immigration economics and family economics.** This is probably the most valuable conversation.
- **Use historical context to show the mechanism is structurally deep, not modern and elite-specific.** That is the strongest “why history?” answer.

So the posture should be: **build on household-service/female-labor-supply papers, challenge the labor-competition reading of restriction, and connect immigration policy to long-run family labor supply.**

### Is the paper positioned too narrowly or too broadly?

Currently, slightly too narrowly in mechanism and slightly too broadly in ambition.

Too narrow because it sounds like a paper for historians of immigration plus people who already know the domestic-service literature.

Too broad because it invokes “gender revolution” and sweeping implications without fully making that case in the narrative.

The better lane is narrower than “this explains the gender revolution” but broader than “this is about domestic servants in the 1920s”: namely, **immigration shocks to household production alter women’s market work.**

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of or under-engaged with?

Even without doing a full literature audit, the paper should probably speak more explicitly to:
- **Family economics / household production** beyond the handful of citations.
- **Care economy and marketization of household tasks**.
- **Structural transformation in women’s work**.
- Possibly **race and stratification in household service**, even if the sample is limited to native-born white women. The domestic service literature is historically inseparable from race and class. Ignoring that makes the story thinner and potentially historically flattened.
- **Immigration and local service prices / household-service availability** literature, where relevant.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?

Almost, but not quite. It is having a decent conversation with immigration economics, but the more powerful conversation is with **family economics and the economics of household production**.

That is the unexpected connection that could make the paper matter beyond immigration specialists.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Before this paper, the natural story about immigration restriction is that it helps natives by reducing labor-market competition. Separately, modern evidence suggests that low-skilled immigration can increase some native women’s labor supply by expanding household services.

### Tension

These two ideas point in opposite directions for the 1924 Act. If immigrant women were competing with natives for jobs, restriction could help native women. But if immigrant women were supplying household labor that enabled other women to work, restriction could hurt native women.

### Resolution

The paper finds that more exposed counties saw lower native-born women’s labor force participation and lower domestic-service employment, with effects especially visible among married women and larger raw effects among unmarried women.

### Implications

Immigration restriction can reduce native labor supply by removing complementary household services. More broadly, immigration policy affects labor supply through the household as well as the workplace.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the bones of one, yes. But it still feels somewhat like a collection of plausible results orbiting a known mechanism rather than a tightly scripted argument.

The main reason is that the paper alternates between three stories:
1. historical reverse-Cortes-Tessada,
2. challenge to Collins/competition,
3. contribution to the gender revolution.

All three are defensible, but they dilute each other. The paper needs one master story and the others as implications.

### What story should it be telling?

The master story should be:

**The 1924 Act was not just a labor-supply shock to immigrant workers; it was a shock to the market provision of household production, and that shock reduced native women’s market work.**

Then the subplots are:
- this challenges the competition view of restriction for women;
- it shows the household-service mechanism is historically deep;
- it implies immigration policy belongs in the story of women’s labor supply.

That is a clean arc.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?

I would lead with: **The 1924 immigration quotas appear to have reduced, not increased, native-born women’s labor force participation in places that had relied more on immigrant domestic servants.**

That is the fact that gets attention.

### Would people lean in?

Yes—initially. The sign reversal relative to the default “restriction helps natives” intuition is interesting. The historical setting also helps.

But the next 30 seconds matter a lot. If the presenter immediately says “this is like Cortes-Tessada in reverse,” some people will mentally downgrade it to a clever historical replication. If instead the presenter says “the policy removed a key market substitute for household production,” they will lean in more.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Probably: **Is this really about household production rather than general local economic decline in immigrant-heavy places?**

As editor, I am not adjudicating that here. But strategically, the paper must anticipate that this is the audience’s first question and structure the story so the mechanism is front and center, not an afterthought.

### If the findings are modest, is the result still interesting?

Yes, the magnitudes are not enormous, but the sign and mechanism are what matter. This is not a failed experiment. The paper can credibly argue that even modest average effects are important because they reveal the direction of the underlying force and because they bear on a canonical policy episode.

That said, the paper should not oversell small standardized effects as if they are transformative in magnitude. The interest is conceptual, not because these are huge coefficients.

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

### What would make it read better?

1. **Rewrite the introduction around one big question.**
   - Drop the “this paper tests both hypotheses” feel.
   - Lead with the substantive paradox: restriction lowers competition but also removes household labor.

2. **Move faster to the headline result.**
   - The paper already does this reasonably well, but the introduction still spends too much time on framing-by-citation and too little on framing-by-question.

3. **Trim the historical background.**
   - It is useful but standard. A top-journal reader does not need quite so much scene-setting before the paper starts making its argument.
   - Keep the quota shock and the prevalence of domestic service; compress the rest.

4. **Elevate the mechanism section.**
   - Right now the mechanism table is central to the story and should feel central in the narrative too.
   - If there are any mechanism results currently treated as secondary, bring them forward.

5. **Demote defensive material.**
   - Long discussions of caveats, data-linkage limitations, and method labels should not dominate the main text’s rhetorical energy.
   - This is especially true for the appendix-style “standardized effect sizes” table, which adds little to the main strategic case.

6. **Shorten or eliminate claims that are not fully earned.**
   - The “early phase of the gender revolution” framing is intriguing, but unless the paper truly shows meaningful implications for that broader process, it should be toned down or made more precise.

7. **Rework the conclusion.**
   - The current conclusion is punchy, perhaps too punchy.
   - “It locked women inside the house” is memorable, but also a little slogan-like. Better to end on the broader economic implication: immigration policy changes labor supply by changing the organization of household production.

### Is the paper front-loaded with the good stuff?

Mostly yes. But the best conceptual payoff—immigration restriction as a household-production shock—is still not as front-loaded as it should be. That phrase, or something like it, should appear early and repeatedly.

### Are there results buried that should be in the main results?

The mechanism is already in the main text, which is good. If there is sharper heterogeneity tied to household burdens, that should be in the main results and not buried anywhere.

### Is the conclusion adding value?

Some, but not enough. It summarizes and offers a line. It should instead crystallize what economists should now believe differently.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

At the moment, the gap is mostly a **framing and ambition problem**, with some **scope problem**.

- **Not primarily a framing-only problem:** the idea is good, but the current manuscript does not yet make it feel foundational.
- **Partly a scope problem:** the paper would be stronger if it more fully exploited the household-production angle, especially through heterogeneity and implications.
- **Also a novelty problem at the margin:** “historical reverse-Cortes-Tessada” is interesting, but not by itself enough for AER. The paper must make readers feel it is answering a broader first-order question.
- **Definitely an ambition problem:** the paper is careful and competent, but it currently aims for “nice historical application” rather than “reframing how we think about immigration restriction and women’s work.”

### The single most impactful advice

**Reframe the paper as showing that immigration restriction was a shock to household production that altered native women’s labor supply, rather than as a historical test of the Cortes-Tessada mechanism.**

That one change would improve the introduction, the literature positioning, the narrative, and the paper’s perceived ambition all at once.
