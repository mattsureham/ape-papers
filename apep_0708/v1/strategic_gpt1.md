## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether shutting down immigration in 1924 increased or decreased native-born women’s labor supply. Its core claim is that the Johnson-Reed Act reduced women’s employment in places that had relied more on immigrant domestic servants, suggesting that immigrant household labor complemented rather than displaced native women’s market work.

A busy economist should care because this is a clean, historically important reversal of the standard “restriction helps natives” intuition, and because it links immigration policy to the long-run evolution of female labor supply through household production rather than wage competition alone.

### Does the paper articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Not quite. The ingredients are there, but the introduction opens with historical scene-setting and then immediately pivots into “conventional wisdom” versus “Cortes-Tessada mechanism.” That is intelligible to specialists, but it is not yet a sharp top-journal pitch. The first two paragraphs should more explicitly foreground the big world question:

- When immigration is cut off, do native workers benefit because competition falls?
- Or do some natives lose because immigrants provide complementary services that make their work possible?

Right now the paper feels like “a historical application of Cortes-Tessada plus a response to Collins.” It should instead feel like “a paper about what immigration restriction does to household production and female labor supply.”

### The pitch the paper should have

“In 1924 the U.S. abruptly cut off immigration from Southern and Eastern Europe, including many women who worked in domestic service. This paper shows that in places more exposed to that shock, native-born white women became less likely to work, not more: restricting immigrant labor removed the household services that enabled women’s market employment. The broader lesson is that immigration policy affects native labor supply not only through competition in labor markets, but also through the supply of complementary household inputs.”

That is the AER version of the pitch: one policy shock, one surprising fact, one general lesson.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper’s contribution is to show that the 1924 immigration restriction reduced native-born women’s labor force participation in more exposed counties because immigrant domestic servants were complements to, not substitutes for, women’s market work.

Only partially. The paper names Collins, Cortes-Tessada, Tabellini, and Abramitzky, but the differentiation is still fuzzy.

The likely reader’s takeaway now is:
- “historical immigration restriction paper”
- “using linked census data”
- “with women as the outcome”
- “and domestic service as mechanism”

That is decent, but not yet sharply differentiated. The introduction should more crisply separate the paper from four neighboring literatures/questions:

1. **Immigration restriction and native labor market outcomes**  
   Those papers are mostly about wages, occupational upgrading, or native employment effects broadly.
2. **Immigration and household services**  
   Cortes-Tessada and follow-ons are modern and mostly about high-skill women / childcare / services.
3. **Women’s labor supply in history**  
   Goldin and others study the gender revolution and household technology, but not immigration restriction as a household input shock.
4. **Historical linked-data immigration papers**  
   The data contribution is useful, but should not be sold as a main contribution.

It is mixed, and it should be much more world-first. The strongest version is:

- **World question:** What happens to native women’s labor supply when a major source of household labor disappears?

The current draft too often slips into literature-gap language (“first individual-level evidence,” “contributes to three literatures,” “demonstrates the value of IPUMS MLP”). That is not AER-forward framing.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?

They could get close, but many would still summarize it as:  
“Another shift-share historical immigration paper, but for women’s labor supply.”

That is the warning sign. The paper needs to ensure the colleague says instead:  
“It shows that immigration restriction backfired for native women because it cut off domestic service inputs.”

Several possibilities, in descending order of importance:

1. **Make the household-production angle much sharper.**  
   The paper would feel bigger if it showed not just women’s LFP and domestic service employment, but variation exactly where household-service dependence should matter most: mothers with young children, higher-income households, urban households, or households employing servants pre-shock. 2. **Push beyond domestic service as an occupation category toward a broader household-services equilibrium.**  
   If the story is really about household inputs, the paper would be stronger with outcomes that map more directly to household outsourcing constraints rather than only labor-force participation and occupation score.

3. **Frame the paper as changing how we think about immigration restriction, not just women’s labor supply.**

4. **Clarify who is affected and why.**  
   The unmarried result is currently large but strategically awkward because it muddies the story. If the headline is really about household help enabling market work, then the married-with-household-responsibilities margin should lead the paper. Right now the larger unmarried effect makes the contribution seem less conceptually clean.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

Possibly also:
- **Farré, González, Ortega / Barone and Mocetti / Furtado and Hock** type papers on immigration and female labor supply through household production channels.

- **Build on Cortes-Tessada**, not merely “run it in reverse.” “Running the mechanism in reverse” is a useful hook, but it sounds derivative if overused. Better to say the paper identifies the same underlying economic force in a radically different institutional setting: a national immigration shutdown rather than gradual local inflows.
- **Revise Collins rather than attack him.** The paper should say: restriction may have helped some natives on competition margins, but for women there was an offsetting or dominant complementarity channel through household production.
- **Connect to Goldin more deeply.** This could be a paper about one underappreciated force shaping the path of women’s market work in the interwar period.
- **Use the linked-data literature instrumentally, not as audience.** Data scale is not the conversation; economics is.

### Is the paper positioned too narrowly or too broadly?

Currently it is oddly both:

- **Too narrow** in that it sounds like a specific historical immigration paper about a specific occupation.
- **Too broad** in its laundry list of “three literatures,” which diffuses the message.

The right audience is narrower but higher-value: economists interested in immigration, labor, family economics, and economic history around a single conceptual point—**complementarity through household production**.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?

Not unaware, but under-engaged with:

- The broader **family economics / household production** literature.
- Work on **service outsourcing and women’s labor supply** beyond the canonical immigration papers.
- Potentially literature on **labor-saving household technologies** and the cost of household production as a determinant of female work.
- Possibly **economic history of domestic service** more directly; the historical background is a bit generic for a paper whose mechanism depends heavily on that institution.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?

Mostly, but not optimally. The most impactful conversation is not “historical effects of Johnson-Reed” per se. It is:

**How does immigration policy affect native labor supply when immigrants perform tasks inside the household economy?**

That is a stronger and more portable framing. It lets the paper speak to modern immigration debates and to household economics simultaneously.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Before this paper, a reader might think immigration restriction should help native workers by reducing labor-market competition. At the same time, modern work suggests low-skilled immigrants can raise native women’s labor supply by providing household services. The 1924 Act offers a historical setting in which those forces can be tested against each other.

### Tension

Which force dominates when immigration is suddenly cut off? Do native women benefit from reduced job competition, or are they harmed because household help becomes scarcer?

### Resolution

The paper finds that women’s labor force participation fell more in counties more exposed to the restricted immigrant groups, and domestic service employment declined in those places as well. The paper interprets this as evidence that the complementarity channel dominated.

### Implications

Immigration restriction can reduce native employment rather than increase it, because immigrants may supply inputs that enable natives’ labor supply. More broadly, household production constraints mattered for women’s employment long before the modern service economy.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the bones of one, but the arc is not fully disciplined. The paper currently feels like it has:

- one good big result,
- one mechanism result,
- several robustnesss,
- and then a discussion that tells the story more cleanly than the introduction.

That is backward. The story should be visible immediately:

1. Restriction reduces competition but also removes complements.
2. Domestic servants are the critical historical margin.
3. The paper shows the complementarity effect dominated, especially for married women.

Right now the unmarried result complicates the narrative substantially. It may be true and important, but it weakens the elegance of the main story because the causal narrative for unmarried women is partly “loss of domestic-service jobs,” while for married women it is “loss of household help.” Those are two related but distinct channels. The paper should decide which is the story and organize around it.

My instinct: **lead with married women and household production.** Then present unmarried women as additional evidence that restriction also shrank a female-intensive employment sector. That is a cleaner two-margin story than the current equal-weight treatment.

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

### What fact would I lead with?

“I’d lead with: when the U.S. shut down immigration from Southern and Eastern Europe in 1924, native-born women worked less in the places most exposed to the cut, because the country had choked off the supply of domestic servants.”

That is a sentence economists will understand immediately.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

They would lean in—at least initially—because the sign is surprising and the mechanism is intuitive. The combination of immigration restriction, household production, and women’s labor supply has real bite.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Probably one of these:

- “Is this really about married women losing household help, or mostly about unmarried women losing domestic-service jobs?”
- “How much does this change our interpretation of Johnson-Reed more broadly?”
- “Is this a narrow domestic-service story or evidence of a general complementarity mechanism?”

Those are strategic questions, not referee questions, and they matter. The first one is especially important because it reveals the paper’s current vulnerability: the headline mechanism is cleaner for married women, but the largest effect is for unmarried women.

### If findings are modest, is the modesty okay?

Yes. The effects are not huge in percentage-point terms, but they are interpretable against low baseline rates and a major policy shock. The issue is not effect size; it is conceptual sharpness. A modest but surprising historical fact can absolutely be AER material if it revises a broad intuition. This paper has that possibility.

This paper is not obviously far away in topic or potential. The question is live enough, the historical setting is attractive, and the sign of the result is genuinely interesting. But in its current form, the paper feels more like a solid field-journal paper than an AER paper because the framing is still narrower and safer than the finding warrants.

### What is the main gap?

Primarily **a framing problem**, with some **scope/ambition problem**.

- **Framing problem:** The science the paper wants to sell is bigger than the paper’s current self-description. It should be a paper about how immigration restriction affects native labor supply through household production, not mainly a historical reverse-Cortes-Tessada exercise.

It is **less a novelty problem** than it may look. The historical setting plus the policy reversal plus the household-production framing do create novelty. But the paper must insist on that contribution with more discipline.

### What would excite the top 10 people in this field?

A version of this paper that made them say:

“This changes how we think about restriction. Immigration policy doesn’t just shift wages by changing labor supply in the market; it changes whether native women can work at all by shifting the availability of household substitutes.”

To get there, the paper needs to show that this is not just a quaint domestic-service fact from the 1920s, but a deeper economic mechanism.

**Reframe the paper around one big claim: the 1924 Act reduced native women’s labor supply because immigration restriction removed household-production complements, and organize the entire introduction and results around the married-women/household-services margin rather than around a generic historical immigration design.**
