## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
This paper examines how the 1924 Johnson-Reed Act—a massive restrictive immigration shock—inadvertently suppressed the labor force participation of native-born American women by eliminating the supply of immigrant domestic servants. Using a linked panel of 7.65 million women, it shows that the loss of "cheap help" raised the shadow price of market work, forcing native women (especially married ones) back into the home. It is a "Cortes-Tessada" story in reverse, proving that the complementarity between low-skilled immigrant labor and high-skilled native labor is a foundational, century-old feature of the US economy.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this pitch quite well. The first two paragraphs successfully bridge the gap between historical narrative and modern labor theory. The current pitch is strong, but to make it "AER-grade," the first paragraph should more aggressively highlight the *irony* of the 1924 Act: a policy designed to protect native workers actually hampered the "Quiet Revolution" of women's entry into the workforce.

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**Contribution:** The paper provides the first individual-level evidence that historical immigration restrictions reduced native women’s labor supply by destroying the domestic service market, reversing the "competition" narrative often applied to this era.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It differentiates itself well from Tabellini (2020) and Abramitzky et al. (2023) by focusing on the gendered labor supply response rather than general manufacturing wages or assimilation.
*   **Question vs. Gap:** It frames the contribution as answering a question about the *world* (Did immigration restrictions "free" or "trap" native women?), which is excellent.
*   **Clarity:** A smart economist would likely say "It’s a historical version of Cortes-Tessada using a massive new IPUMS panel." This is a clear, "sticky" contribution.
*   **Bigger Contribution:** The paper would be more ambitious if it explored the *long-run* path of these women. Did this temporary "trap" in the 1920s permanently alter their career trajectories in the 1940s?

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
The paper sits at the intersection of the **Economic History of Immigration** (Abramitzky, Boustan, Tabellini) and the **Evolution of Women’s Labor Supply** (Goldin, Costa).

*   **Closest Neighbors:** Collins (1997) on the Great Migration; Goldin (2006) on the Quiet Revolution; Cortés and Tessada (2011) on domestic service; Tabellini (2020) on the 1920s quotas.
*   **Positioning:** It currently "synthesizes" these. It should more aggressively "attack" the competition-centric view of the 1920s (Collins) by showing that for women, complementarity was the dominant force.
*   **Unexpected Connection:** It could benefit from connecting to the literature on **"Engines of Liberation"** (Greenwood et al., 2005). If appliances were the technological engine of liberation, immigrant servants were the *human* engine. The 1924 Act was a "wrench" thrown into that engine.

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC
*   **Setup:** The early 20th century saw women beginning to enter the workforce, supported by a vast pool of Southern/Eastern European domestic help.
*   **Tension:** The 1924 Act cut this supply by 87%. Did this help native women (by removing competition) or hurt them (by removing help)?
*   **Resolution:** It hurt them. LFP fell, particularly where the domestic service market collapsed.
*   **Implications:** Policy changes in the low-skill labor market have immediate, massive spillover effects on the labor supply of supposedly "unrelated" demographic groups (native women).

**Evaluation:** The arc is exceptionally clear. It is a "revisionist history" story, which is the most compelling kind for a top-five journal.

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
At an economics dinner party, you lead with: *"Did you know the 1924 immigration quotas—designed to protect American jobs—actually drove millions of native women out of the workforce because they lost their housekeepers?"* 

People would lean in. The follow-up question would be: *"Did this accelerate the adoption of household appliances (washing machines, vacuums) to compensate?"* This is the "missing chapter" of the paper.

---

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
*   **Front-loading:** The results in Table 2 and 3 are the "meat." Table 3 (Mechanism) is actually more interesting than Table 2 (Main Result) and should be integrated more tightly into the primary narrative.
*   **The "Unmarried" Puzzle:** The paper notes a larger effect for unmarried women but attributes it to them losing *jobs* in domestic service. This creates two different stories: one about "household help" (married) and one about "disappearing sectors" (unmarried). These need to be more clearly separated or more elegantly unified.
*   **Appendix:** The "Standardized Effect Sizes" (Table 5) are helpful but belong in an appendix.

---

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The paper is currently a very high-quality *Applied Economics* or *Journal of Economic History* paper. To make it **AER**, it needs to bridge the gap between "historical fact" and "general economic lesson."

**The Gap:** The paper currently feels like a "case study" of 1924. 
**The Single Most Impactful Advice:** Add an analysis (or at least a robust discussion) of **technological substitution**. If the "help" disappeared, did women in those counties buy more appliances? If the author can show that immigration restriction acted as a catalyst for the "Engines of Liberation" (household tech), the paper becomes a foundational study on the interaction between labor supply, immigration, and technological change.
