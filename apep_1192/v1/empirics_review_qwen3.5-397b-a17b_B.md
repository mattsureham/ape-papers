1. **Idea Fidelity**

The paper largely adheres to the core conceptual framework outlined in the manifest: using NHTSA investigation queue congestion to identify the causal effect of regulatory capacity on safety outcomes. However, there are notable deviations in execution. First, the sample size differs significantly; the manifest proposed 5,330 investigations, while the paper utilizes 1,362 closed PE/EA investigations. The reasoning for excluding approximately 75% of the unique investigation records (likely open cases or those with missing dates) requires clearer justification to ensure selection bias is not introduced. Second, the primary outcome shifted from "recall delays" (as emphasized in the manifest's injury cost narrative) to "recall probability" (forgone recalls). While this is a potentially stronger finding—suggesting abandonment rather than just delay—it diverges from the original promise to quantify injury costs resulting from *timing* delays. Finally, the identification strategy shifted from a proposed IV approach to a preferred reduced-form specification due to weak instrument concerns, yet the appendix presents IV results with strong first-stage statistics, creating confusion about the final empirical choice.

2. **Summary**

This paper provides causal evidence that regulatory capacity constraints at the NHTSA reduce the likelihood of vehicle safety recalls. By exploiting exogenous variation in the volume of concurrent investigations at other manufacturers, the author finds that each additional concurrent case reduces recall probability by 0.14 percentage points, primarily through triage at the Preliminary Evaluation stage. The results suggest a measurable "triage tax" where bandwidth constraints lead to the abandonment of lower-profile safety cases rather than mere delays.

3. **Essential Points**

1.  **Contradictory Instrument Strength:** There is a critical inconsistency regarding the first-stage F-statistic. The main text claims the IV first-stage F-statistic is 4.0 ("below conventional thresholds"), motivating the reduced-form preference. However, Appendix Table 1 reports an F-statistic of 82.958 for the same first-stage relationship. This discrepancy undermines confidence in the empirical strategy. The authors must reconcile these numbers immediately—either the text or the appendix is incorrect, and the justification for preferring reduced-form over 2SLS hinges on this value.
2.  **Sample Selection and Censoring:** The reduction from the manifest's 5,330 investigations to the paper's 1,362 closed investigations raises concerns about censoring bias. Excluding open investigations means removing cases that are currently consuming bandwidth but have not yet reached an outcome. If queue congestion causes cases to remain open longer (or indefinitely), excluding them biases the sample toward cases that resolved quickly. The authors must address whether excluding open cases correlates with the instrument.
3.  **Exclusion Restriction Plausibility:** The identification assumes that concurrent investigations at *other* manufacturers affect the focal case only through bandwidth. However, industry-wide safety shocks (e.g., a new type of battery failure affecting multiple manufacturers simultaneously) could increase the queue *and* increase the likelihood of recalls across the board due to heightened regulatory scrutiny. While year fixed effects help, they may not absorb quarter-specific industry shocks. The authors need to demonstrate that the instrument is not picking up correlated safety trends.

4. **Suggestions**

**Resolve the Empirical Inconsistency**
The contradiction between the text (F=4.0) and Appendix (F=82.9) is the most urgent issue. If the F-statistic is indeed 82.9, the weak instrument argument collapses, and 2SLS estimates should be foregrounded rather than treated as secondary. If the F-statistic is 4.0 in the preferred specification (perhaps due to additional fixed effects not shown in the appendix), the text must specify exactly which specification yields the weak result. For an *AER: Insights* paper, clarity on the identification strength is paramount. I recommend standardizing the reporting: present the first-stage regression explicitly in the main text or a dedicated appendix table that matches the text description. If the reduced-form is preferred for robustness, cite \citet{chernozhukov2008reduced} consistently, but ensure the reader understands why the IV is being downplayed despite the appendix suggesting strength.

**Address Sample Selection Bias**
The drop from 4,254 unique PE/EA investigations to 1,362 closed investigations needs a transparent flowchart or table. Specifically, how many cases were excluded because they were "open" at the time of data extraction? If the queue instrument measures *current* congestion, excluding currently open cases creates a mechanical link between the instrument and the sample selection. I suggest two robustness checks: (1) Include open cases with a censored duration model (e.g., Cox proportional hazards) to see if congestion affects the *hazard* of closure; (2) Show a balance table comparing included vs. excluded investigations on observable characteristics (manufacturer, component, year) to argue that exclusions are random conditional on fixed effects. If the excluded cases are systematically different (e.g., newer, more complex cases), the results may not generalize to the current regulatory environment.

**Strengthen the Exclusion Restriction**
To bolster the claim that the "other-manufacturer queue" is exogenous, the authors should control for industry-wide demand shocks that might drive both complaints and regulatory attention. For example, include controls for total Vehicle Miles Traveled (VMT) or gas prices at the year-quarter level, as these affect accident rates and complaint volumes across all manufacturers. Additionally, consider a placebo test where the instrument is constructed using *future* queue congestion (which should not affect current outcomes) or using queue congestion from a different agency (e.g., EPA investigations) if data permits. Another valuable addition would be to interact the instrument with a measure of "industry-wide recall trends." If the effect persists even when controlling for the total number of recalls issued industry-wide in that quarter, it strengthens the bandwidth channel argument over the correlated shock argument.

**Quantify the Welfare Cost**
The manifest promised an estimate of the "injury cost of investigation delays," but the paper focuses on recall probability. To fulfill the original contribution promise and enhance policy relevance, I recommend a back-of-the-envelope welfare calculation. Using the estimate of 65 forgone recalls (mentioned in the Introduction) and the average number of injuries per recall (available in the complaint data linked to recalls), estimate the number of injuries potentially attributable to congestion. Even a rough estimate (e.g., "Queue congestion may be associated with X additional injuries per year") would make the "triage tax" concept concrete for policymakers. If injury data is too noisy, use the number of vehicles affected (median 100,000 per recall) to estimate the number of consumers left unprotected. This aligns the paper closer to the manifest's goal of informing NHTSA staffing decisions.

**Deepen the Mechanism Analysis**
The finding that the effect is concentrated in Preliminary Evaluations (PE) rather than Engineering Analyses (EA) is compelling but deserves more exploration. Why is the PE stage more sensitive? Is it because PE requires less technical resource intensity, making it easier to close quickly under pressure? Or is it because EA cases have already passed a severity threshold? I suggest adding a interaction term between the queue instrument and a measure of "complaint severity" (e.g., presence of death/injury flags in the underlying complaints). If congestion only affects cases with zero injury flags, this supports the "rational triage" narrative. If it affects high-severity cases equally, it suggests a more troubling breakdown in safety prioritization. The current heterogeneity table shows effects for high severity, but the discussion downplays this. Clarifying whether high-severity cases are truly protected or just less numerous is crucial for the safety implication.

**Refine for AER: Insights Format**
*AER: Insights* papers are typically concise (often under 5,000 words) and focus on clear policy takeaways. The current draft is well-structured but could be tightened. The Institutional Background section is slightly long; consider merging it with the Introduction to save space for robustness checks. The IV appendix is essential but should be referenced more clearly in the main text to avoid the confusion noted above. Finally, ensure the policy implication in the Conclusion is specific: rather than just "increase staffing," suggest a marginal cost-benefit analysis (e.g., "Hiring 10 additional investigators would cost $X but potentially prevent Y recalls"). This moves the paper from documenting a problem to offering a solution.

**Data and Reproducibility**
Given the autonomous generation note, ensure that the data cleaning steps are fully documented in a public repository. Specifically, the logic for collapsing 153,998 records to 1,362 investigations must be scriptable and transparent. For an empirical paper relying on administrative data, providing the code used to construct the "concurrent investigations" variable is critical, as the timing logic (e.g., how "open" is defined on the exact opening date) can significantly impact the instrument's variation. A link to a GitHub repository with the cleaning script should be included in the footnote or data section.

Table 1 (Summary Statistics) should include the instrument ("Other-mfr queue") as a row to match the text description. Table 2 (Reduced-Form) is clear, but consider adding a column with the IV estimate alongside the RF estimate for direct comparison, even if RF is preferred. This allows readers to see the magnitude difference immediately. In Table 3 (Heterogeneity), ensure the sample sizes for Low vs. High Severity sum to the total; currently, 1,019 + 342 = 1,361, which matches the total 1,362 (allowing for one missing observation), but this should be noted. Consistency in sample sizes across tables builds trust in the data handling.

By addressing the IV statistic contradiction, clarifying the sample selection, and quantifying the welfare implications, this paper has the potential to be a significant contribution to the literature on regulatory economics and auto safety. The core intuition is strong, but the empirical execution needs to be airtight to support the causal claims.
