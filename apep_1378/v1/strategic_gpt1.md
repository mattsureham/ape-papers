## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple, policy-relevant question: when a country pushes its minimum wage up very far, very fast, do the small-employment-effect findings from the classic U.S. literature still hold? Using Slovakia’s unusually large minimum-wage increases and variation across sectors in how binding the wage floor is, the paper argues that employment losses are modest on average but meaningful in the most exposed low-wage sectors, which matters because Europe is now moving toward similar 60%-of-median wage floors.

That is a perfectly viable pitch. But the paper does not yet deliver it cleanly in the first two paragraphs. The current opening has energy, but it mixes several claims at once—largest increase in the EU, EU Directive relevance, U.S. debate relevance, “natural experiment,” and sectoral design—before crisply stating the core question and headline answer. It also overstates the degree to which “Slovakia offers a natural experiment large enough to resolve this question,” which is rhetorically risky and invites skepticism.

### What the first two paragraphs should say instead

A better opening would be something like:

> Economists have learned a great deal about the effects of modest minimum wage increases, mostly from settings where the policy changes are relatively small and the wage floor remains far below median earnings. Much less is known about what happens when a country raises the minimum wage repeatedly until it approaches 60 percent of the median wage—the threshold now encouraged by the European Union’s 2022 Minimum Wage Directive.  
>   
> This paper studies that question using Slovakia, where the statutory minimum wage rose from €327 to €750 between 2012 and 2024. I exploit the fact that a common national minimum wage binds much more strongly in some sectors than others: accommodation, retail, and other low-wage sectors are far more exposed than finance or ICT. The main finding is that large minimum-wage increases have small effects in low-exposure sectors but generate economically meaningful employment declines in highly exposed sectors, suggesting that employment effects become more salient when the wage floor reaches very high levels relative to sector wages.

That is the pitch the paper should have.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper’s contribution is to provide evidence, from Slovakia’s unusually large minimum-wage increases, that employment effects remain modest overall but become meaningfully negative in sectors where the wage floor is highly binding, speaking directly to the consequences of pushing minimum wages toward 60% of median wages.

### Is this clearly differentiated from the closest papers?

Not yet. Right now the contribution is only partly differentiated. The paper repeatedly says: “first causal estimate in Slovakia,” “guidance for the EU Directive,” and “validates the Kaitz-based approach in a new context.” Of these, only one has broad-journal potential: evidence about the consequences of very high minimum-wage ratios. “First in Slovakia” is not an AER contribution. “Validation of the Kaitz approach” is methodological housekeeping unless tied to a bigger economic claim. “Guidance for the EU Directive” is potentially important, but only if framed as a general question about the shape of labor-demand responses at high minimum-wage levels.

The paper needs to differentiate itself from the existing minimum-wage literature along one clear dimension: **this is about the regime where the minimum wage becomes very high relative to typical pay**, not about minimum wages per se.

### World question or literature gap?

At present it is split awkwardly between the two. The stronger framing is the world question:

- What happens when a country raises the minimum wage to around 60% of the median wage?
- Do the “small or zero employment effects” results from modest increases generalize to large, repeated escalations?
- Are employment effects nonlinear in the bite of the wage floor?

That is much stronger than “there is no paper on Slovakia.”

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new?

Right now, not confidently. Too many readers would summarize this as: “It’s another sector-level DiD on minimum wages using Kaitz exposure.” That is the danger.

To avoid that, the introduction must make the novelty unmistakable:
1. **This is not about whether minimum wages matter at all.**
2. **It is about whether effects change once the floor gets very high relative to sector wages.**
3. **The paper uses Slovakia as a case study of a policy regime Europe is about to replicate.**

### What would make the contribution bigger?

Most importantly: **reframe around the high-ratio regime**, not around Slovakia.

Then, substantively, the contribution would become bigger if the paper could do one or more of the following:
- Show more directly that effects change as the minimum wage approaches or exceeds salient thresholds like 50%, 60%, 65% of sector median wage.
- Emphasize extensive-margin sectoral reallocation, not just within-sector employment losses. Do workers move elsewhere, or do low-wage sectors shrink relative to others?
- Speak more directly to the EU policy question by comparing Slovakia’s path to countries approaching the Directive threshold.
- Add outcomes that sharpen the economic mechanism of adjustment in this high-bite regime: vacancies, hours, firm entry/exit, or occupational composition. Even one of these would help the paper say more than “employment falls somewhat.”

The biggest payoff would come from making the paper a statement about **nonlinearity / regime change** rather than a country study with a standard design.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

### Closest neighbors

The natural neighboring conversation includes:
- **Card and Krueger (1994)** and the broader “small employment effects” debate.
- **Neumark and Wascher** on more negative employment effects.
- **Cengiz, Dube, Lindner, and Zipperer (2019)** on the distributional and employment effects of minimum wages.
- **Dustmann, Lindner, Schönberg, Umkehrer, and vom Berge (2022)** on Germany’s minimum wage and labor-market adjustment.
- Likely also **Harasztosi and Lindner (2019)** on Hungary, since that is a much closer neighbor both geographically and in terms of large wage shocks in a Central/Eastern European context.
- Possibly **Dube (2019)** or related syntheses on what we know from modern minimum-wage research.

If the author wants a European-policy audience, the Germany and Hungary papers are especially important comparators. If the author wants a broad labor audience, Card/Krueger, Neumark/Wascher, Cengiz et al., and Dube are unavoidable reference points.

### How should the paper position itself?

Build on, don’t attack. The paper should say:

- The modern literature has taught us that modest increases often have limited employment effects.
- But that literature is less informative about **large, repeated increases in a lower-wage economy**, especially when the minimum wage approaches 60% of median earnings.
- This paper extends the conversation into that region of the policy space.

That is the right posture. An attack—“the standard North American literature may not generalize”—is directionally fine, but the paper currently sounds too eager to claim that Slovakia “resolves” the question. Better to say it **tests the external validity** of the modest-effects consensus in a more extreme policy environment.

### Too narrow or too broad?

Currently both, oddly.

- **Too narrow** because it leans on “first paper on Slovakia,” which is niche.
- **Too broad** because it invokes the U.S., developing countries, all of Europe, and labor-demand theory in general without tightly linking the evidence to those literatures.

The paper needs a more disciplined audience: labor economists and public economists interested in the consequences of high minimum-wage ratios and the EU Directive. That is enough.

### What literature is it missing?

The paper seems under-engaged with:
- The modern minimum-wage literature on distributional margins and wage compression.
- European minimum-wage studies, especially Germany and Hungary.
- Broader literature on wage-setting institutions in Europe.
- Literature on threshold-based policy design and nonlinear treatment effects.
- Potentially structural or equilibrium incidence papers if the author wants to say anything about “boundary” or “regime.”

The phrase “validate the Kaitz-based sector heterogeneity approach” also suggests a literature on exposure designs, shift-share-type logic, or sectoral exposure methods that the paper should acknowledge more seriously if it wants to use that language.

### Is it having the right conversation?

Almost, but not quite. The most impactful framing is not “here is evidence from Slovakia.” It is:

**What do minimum wages do when policymakers stop making small adjustments and instead push the floor toward a high share of median pay?**

That conversation intersects:
- minimum-wage labor economics,
- European policy design,
- and the external validity of the “small effects” consensus.

That is the right conversation.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

The world before this paper: the best-known evidence on minimum wages mostly comes from modest changes, often in the U.S., and has led many economists to think employment effects are small or concentrated in narrow margins. Meanwhile, the EU is encouraging member states to move minimum wages toward 60% of the median wage, which is a more ambitious policy regime than the settings most studied.

### Tension

We do not know whether the “small effects” result survives when the wage floor gets much higher relative to underlying wages, especially in lower-wage economies. Slovakia is useful because it experienced exactly this kind of escalation.

### Resolution

The paper finds that high-exposure sectors experience economically meaningful employment declines, while low-exposure sectors do not. The implied message is not “minimum wages are disastrous,” but rather “the costs become more visible in highly exposed sectors once the floor becomes very binding.”

### Implications

The implication is that the employment consequences of minimum wages may be **state-dependent**: mild at low levels of bite, more salient at high levels. That matters for EU implementation, for the generalizability of the U.S.-based literature, and for how economists think about nonlinear policy effects.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

Serviceable, but not sharp enough. It currently reads too much like a collection of standard empirical components:
- policy background,
- data,
- baseline result,
- heterogeneity,
- robustness,
- discussion.

The story is there, but the paper keeps diluting it with less important claims:
- “first in Slovakia,”
- “validates Kaitz approach,”
- “largest increase in EU history,”
- “natural experiment large enough to resolve this question.”

Those are not all equally useful, and some are distracting.

### What story should it be telling?

The story should be:

1. Economists mostly know about small increases.
2. Europe is moving toward much larger relative minimum wages.
3. Slovakia got there first.
4. The key empirical fact is sectoral divergence by bite.
5. The broader conclusion is that minimum-wage effects are modest at low bite and more negative at high bite.

That is a coherent AER-style narrative. Right now the paper has the ingredients but not the discipline.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?

I would lead with:

> “When Slovakia pushed its minimum wage from 40% to more than 60% of median pay, employment effects remained near zero in high-wage sectors but turned meaningfully negative in the low-wage sectors where the floor was actually binding.”

That is the memorable fact.

### Would people lean in?

Some would. Labor economists and applied microeconomists would at least pay attention, because the 60%-of-median threshold is an active policy frontier. But the current presentation would not maximize that reaction, because it sounds too much like a familiar design in a small country. The dinner-party version needs to sound like a result about **policy regimes**, not about Slovakia per se.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Likely one of these:
- “Is this telling us there’s a nonlinear threshold where minimum wages start to bite harder?”
- “How generalizable is Slovakia to the rest of Europe?”
- “Are these losses offset by wage gains or reallocation to other sectors?”
- “Is this really about the 60% threshold, or just about accommodation and retail being special?”

Those are exactly the questions the paper should be trying to anticipate in its framing.

### If findings are modest, is that still interesting?

Yes, if handled correctly. The paper’s estimates are not dramatic enough to support a “minimum wages cause large job loss” headline. But they are interesting if framed as:
- a limit to the modest-effects consensus,
- evidence of heterogeneity by exposure,
- and guidance about a high-policy-threshold regime.

What does not work is oscillating between “moderate but meaningful” and language that suggests a more sweeping overturning of prior beliefs. The paper is strongest when it says: **costs are concentrated, not catastrophic; but they become salient when the floor gets high enough.**

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

Bluntly: in current form, this is not an AER paper. The problem is not just execution; it is mainly **ambition and framing**.

### What is the gap?

#### 1. Framing problem
Yes, definitely. The paper has a potentially interesting question but frames itself too much as:
- a Slovakia study,
- with a standard sector-exposure design,
- producing modestly negative estimates.

That is not enough. It needs to be framed as evidence on **what happens when the minimum wage enters a high-bite regime**.

#### 2. Scope problem
Also yes. For a top general-interest journal, one country, 22 sectors, and one primary outcome is thin unless the conceptual contribution is very sharp. The paper would feel much bigger if it either:
- more convincingly establishes a nonlinear regime change,
- or broadens the set of outcomes to show the full adjustment process.

#### 3. Novelty problem
Moderately. Minimum wage papers using exposure heterogeneity are not new. “Evidence from another country” is not a top-journal novelty unless the setting speaks to a substantially new margin. The only strong novelty available here is the unusually high minimum-wage ratio.

#### 4. Ambition problem
Yes. The paper is competent but safe. It is content to document an elasticity in a new setting instead of pushing toward a bigger conceptual claim.

### Single most impactful advice

If the author could change only one thing, it would be this:

**Rewrite the paper around the claim that minimum-wage effects are regime-dependent and become materially more negative when the wage floor approaches 60% of median earnings; stop selling it as “the Slovakia paper.”**

Everything else follows from that. If the paper cannot make that broader claim convincingly, then it is likely a solid field-journal paper rather than an AER paper.
