**SUBJECT:** Strategic Positioning of "Escalating the Floor: Slovakia’s Minimum Wage"

---

### 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
This paper examines the employment consequences of a massive, sustained policy shock: a 129% nominal increase in Slovakia’s minimum wage over 12 years. By exploiting sectoral variation in how "binding" this floor is (using the Kaitz index), the author finds that while effects are negligible in high-wage sectors, they are significant and negative in low-wage sectors (e.g., retail and hotels) as the floor approaches 60-80% of median wages. This serves as a "stress test" for the North American consensus of null employment effects, specifically in the context of the new EU-wide minimum wage mandates.

**Evaluation:** The intro is decent, but it spends too much time on the "what" of Slovakia and not enough on the "why" for a global theorist. It frames itself as a "policy experiment hiding in plain sight."
**The Pitch the Paper Should Have:** "Does the law of demand eventually bite if you raise the price of labor high enough? While the literature finds null effects for marginal increases, Slovakia’s 129% escalation offers a rare look at the 'out-of-sample' consequences of aggressive wage floors. This paper provides the first empirical boundary for the 60% median-wage threshold now being mandated across the European Union, demonstrating that the 'null effect' regime of minimum wages has a clear expiration point."

---

### 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**One-sentence contribution:** The paper identifies an "inflection point" in minimum wage policy where effects transition from null to negative as the wage floor moves from 40% to 60%+ of the median wage.

- **Differentiators:** It moves beyond the "Cengiz et al. (2019)" style of small-gap analysis into "deep" policy changes. It differs from the German minimum wage literature (e.g., Dustmann et al. 2022) by looking at a sustained 12-year escalation rather than a single introduction.
- **World vs. Literature:** It currently leans heavily on "filling a gap in CEE labor economics." To be AER, it must be framed as answering a question about the **limits of firm optimization** in the face of extreme price floors.
- **The "Smart Economist" Test:** A reader would likely say, "It’s a Kaitz-based DiD on a very large shock." To avoid the "just another DiD" tag, the author needs to emphasize the *magnitude* as a unique feature of the data, not just the method.
- **Bigger Contribution:** The paper needs to look at **firm-level margins**. If employment fell by 2-3%, did firms go bankrupt? Did they automate? Without a mechanism (Section 5 is currently just speculation), it’s a "reduced-form fact" looking for a theory.

---

### 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
- **Closest Neighbors:** *Cengiz et al. (2019)* on US bunching; *Dustmann et al. (2022)* on the German introduction; *Harzthoff & Manning (2020)* on the Kaitz index's theoretical properties.
- **Strategy:** It should **stress-test** the US-centric consensus. It shouldn't just "build" on them; it should "warn" them.
- **Missing Conversations:** The paper is silent on the **Automation/Capital Substitution** literature (e.g., Lordan & Neumark). If you raise wages 129%, did the hotels in Bratislava buy check-in kiosks? That is the AER-level conversation.
- **Narrowness:** Currently too focused on "EU Directives." This makes it feel like a policy brief for the European Commission. It needs to speak to the broader theory of the firm.

---

### 4. NARRATIVE ARC
- **Setup:** The world (labor econ) thinks minimum wages are a "free lunch" (or at least low-cost) because most studies look at 5-10% hikes.
- **Tension:** But surely there is a limit. What happens if you double the wage? Does the labor market break, or do firms have infinite "slack"?
- **Resolution:** The labor market in Slovakia didn't break, but it started to fray. At 60% of the median, the "null effect" vanishes.
- **Implications:** The "60% of median" rule-of-thumb is not a safe harbor; it is the beginning of the danger zone.

---

### 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
At a dinner party, you lead with: *"Slovakia doubled its minimum wage in a decade, and surprisingly, the economy didn't collapse—but the hospitality sector lost 3% of its workforce."*
- **Reaction:** People will lean in. The follow-up: *"Did the workers who lost jobs end up better off because the ones who stayed made so much more?"* (The paper currently cannot answer this—it needs welfare/income analysis).

---

### 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
- **Move to Appendix:** The "Standardized Effect Sizes" table (Table 6) is overkill for the main body.
- **Expand:** Section 5 (Mechanisms) is the weakest part. It’s 100% prose. It needs even a simple back-of-the-envelope calculation on firm profitability or a look at *hours worked* vs. *headcount*.
- **Front-loading:** Move the 2012-2024 escalation graph (Table 1 info) to Figure 1. The visual of that "staircase" is the paper's best asset.

---

### 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The gap is **Ambition.**
Right now, this is a very high-quality *Regional Science and Urban Economics* or *Labour Economics* paper. To be AER, it needs to move from "What happened in Slovakia?" to "What does Slovakia teach us about the elasticity of substitution between capital and low-skill labor?"

**Single biggest improvement:** Use the Eurostat data to look at **Total Hours Worked** and **Number of Firms** (business entry/exit) by sector. If the "disemployment" is just people moving from small shops to big supermarkets, the story changes entirely.
