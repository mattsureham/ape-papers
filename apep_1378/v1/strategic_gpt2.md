## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple and policy-relevant question: when a country pushes its minimum wage up very aggressively, do the usual “small employment effects” conclusions still hold? Using Slovakia’s unusually large minimum-wage increases from 2012 to 2024 and cross-sector differences in how binding the wage floor is, the paper argues that employment losses are modest on average but meaningfully concentrated in low-wage sectors like accommodation and retail.

A busy economist should care because the interesting claim is not “minimum wages matter in Slovakia”; it is “the employment effects of minimum wages may be nonlinear and become visible once the wage floor gets high relative to typical wages.” That is potentially a world question, and a live one in Europe given the EU directive.

### Does the paper articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Not quite. The current opening is energetic, but it is still too much “Slovakia is a policy experiment” and not enough “this paper informs the broader debate over whether the small-effects consensus survives large hikes.” The introduction leads with the country episode rather than the general economic question. It also overstates the resolving power of a single-country sectoral design (“large enough to resolve this question”), which invites skepticism.

### What the first two paragraphs should say instead

The paper should open with the broader tension in the minimum-wage literature:

> A large literature finds that modest minimum-wage increases often have small employment effects. But policymakers in Europe are now considering or implementing much larger increases that raise the wage floor toward 60 percent of the median wage, and we know much less about whether those findings extrapolate to that regime. The central question is therefore not whether small minimum-wage changes matter, but whether employment effects become economically meaningful once the wage floor becomes highly binding.

Then introduce Slovakia as the setting:

> Slovakia provides an unusually useful setting to study that question. Between 2012 and 2024, its statutory minimum wage more than doubled, pushing the wage floor from roughly 40 to over 60 percent of the national median wage. Exploiting differences across sectors in how exposed they were to the rising wage floor, this paper finds that employment effects remain small in low-exposure sectors but become noticeable in high-exposure sectors such as accommodation and retail.

That is the pitch the paper should have.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper’s contribution is to show that very large minimum-wage increases in a lower-wage European economy generate modest but nontrivial employment losses concentrated in sectors where the minimum wage is most binding, suggesting that disemployment effects become more salient at higher Kaitz ratios.

### Is this clearly differentiated from the closest papers?

Only partially. Right now the paper’s “fourfold contribution” is too diffuse and includes at least two contributions that are not AER-level contributions:

- “first causal estimate in Slovakia”
- “validate the Kaitz-based approach in a new context”

Those are publishable-journal contributions, not top-field-journal contributions.

The paper’s potentially important contribution is narrower and stronger: evidence on **large-hike/high-Kaitz regimes**. But the introduction does not sharply distinguish this from prior cross-sector minimum-wage studies or from the broader literature finding small average effects. A reader could easily come away with: “This is another reduced-form minimum-wage paper using variation in exposure.”

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world or filling a gap in the literature?

Mixed, but too much literature-gap framing. “First causal estimate of Slovakia” is literature-gap and country-gap framing. That is weak. The strongest framing is about the world: **what happens when minimum wages rise into a more binding range?** The EU Directive makes that world question immediate. The paper should lean almost entirely into that.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?

At present, not cleanly. They would likely say: “It’s a sector-level DiD on Slovakia showing higher disemployment in high-Kaitz sectors.” That is competent, but not exciting.

What you want them to say is: “It speaks to whether the low-elasticity consensus breaks down when the wage floor gets close to 60 percent of median wages.”

That is a much better paper.

### What would make the contribution bigger?

Most importantly: make the paper about **regime change / nonlinearity**, not about Slovakia per se.

Specific ways to make it bigger:
1. **Center the threshold question.** Is there something special about moving from, say, 40% to 60% of median wages? Right now the paper hints at this but never really structures itself around it.
2. **Exploit more mechanism-relevant margins.** If the story is adjustment in highly exposed sectors, outcomes like hours, vacancies, firm entry/exit, wage compression, or occupational mix would make the contribution feel much larger than employment alone.
3. **Connect to policy calibration.** The paper should more explicitly translate findings into the debate over high minimum-wage ratios. “What happens as the Kaitz index approaches 0.6, 0.7, 0.8?” That is more useful than “Slovakia had a big increase.”
4. **Comparison/framing upgrade.** If possible, contrast Slovakia with the “modest increase” benchmark from the existing literature conceptually, even if not empirically. The paper needs to tell us why its setting moves us into a part of the policy space prior studies do not cover well.

### How should the paper position itself relative to those neighbors?

Build on them, not attack them.

The right move is:
- **Build on the modern consensus** that modest minimum-wage increases often have small aggregate employment effects.

- Use Slovakia as evidence from that understudied region of the policy space.

The paper should not posture as overturning the literature. It does not have the scope to do that. It should instead say: “Our evidence is consistent with the view that employment effects are small at low exposure but become more visible in high-exposure/high-Kaitz settings.”

Oddly, both.

It should occupy the middle ground: a paper about **large minimum-wage hikes in Europe’s new 60%-of-median policy environment**.

Not just labor-demand / minimum wage. It should also speak to:

### Is the paper having the right conversation?

Almost, but not yet. The most impactful conversation is not “what happened in Slovakia?” It is “what do we learn about the consequences of pushing the minimum wage into a new, more binding regime?” That is the right conversation.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

The pre-paper world is one in which the modern minimum-wage literature has persuaded many economists that modest increases often have limited employment effects, but most of that evidence comes from settings where the wage floor is not extremely high relative to median wages.

### Tension

Policymakers are now moving into a different regime: much larger increases and explicit targets around 60% of the median wage. The open question is whether the low-elasticity findings continue to hold there, especially in lower-wage economies and low-wage sectors.

### Resolution

In Slovakia, where the minimum wage rose dramatically, sectors with greater exposure to the wage floor experienced meaningful employment declines, while low-exposure sectors did not. The results suggest modest average effects can coexist with nontrivial losses in highly exposed sectors.

### Implications

Economists and policymakers should update from a blanket statement—“minimum wages usually have little employment effect”—to a more conditional one: effects depend on how binding the wage floor is. For policy, moving toward 60% of median wages may be feasible, but it is not costless, and the costs are concentrated.

### Does the paper currently have a clear narrative arc?

Serviceable, but not disciplined. Right now it reads somewhat like a collection of plausible claims:
- Slovakia is a big increase
- High-Kaitz sectors are more affected
- The EU directive matters
- This is the first Slovak estimate
- The method works

That is too many stories.

### What story should it be telling?

One story only:

> The modern minimum-wage literature is mostly about modest hikes. Slovakia lets us study a much more binding path. The evidence suggests employment effects remain limited in low-exposure sectors but become economically meaningful in sectors where the wage floor approaches a large share of typical pay. That matters because Europe is explicitly moving policy into that range.

If the paper commits to that narrative, it will read much more like a top-field paper.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party?

“I’ve got a paper suggesting that once the minimum wage rises toward 60 percent of median pay, the usual ‘little to no employment effect’ conclusion starts to break: the losses show up in the sectors where the floor is actually binding.”

That is the one-line hook.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

Lean in, if presented that way.

If presented as “I estimate the effect of the Slovak minimum wage using sector-level Eurostat data,” they will reach for their phones.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Immediately: “Is this really about Slovakia, or does it tell us something general about high minimum wages?”  
Second: “Is the key result nonlinearity—i.e., that the effects emerge only at high Kaitz ratios?”  
Third: “Are firms adjusting through employment, hours, prices, or margins like firm exit?”

Those are good questions. The paper should tee them up, not wait for the audience to infer them.

### If findings are modest, is the modesty itself interesting?

Yes, but only if framed correctly. The paper’s results are not earth-shattering magnitudes. Their interest comes from being **conditional and policy-calibrated**:
- modest overall
- concentrated where the wage floor binds
- relevant precisely because policymakers are now aiming for 60%

That is valuable. But the paper currently sometimes oversells the magnitude (“surprisingly large,” “clear results”) in a way that weakens credibility. The better framing is: **not catastrophic, not zero, and importantly concentrated**.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

At the moment, the gap is mainly **framing plus ambition**, with some **scope** issues.

### Is it a framing problem?

Yes, strongly. The paper’s best idea is there, but it is hidden under a country-study wrapper. AER is not interested in “the Slovak minimum wage paper.” It might be interested in “evidence that the employment effects of minimum wages become more visible when policy pushes the wage floor into a much more binding range.”

### Is it a scope problem?

Also yes. Employment alone, using 22 sectors in one country, is thin for AER unless the framing is truly first-order and the evidence feels decisive. To get closer, the paper would ideally broaden the set of margins or sharpen the nonlinear/exposure dimension much more powerfully.

### Is it a novelty problem?

Partly. “High-Kaitz sectors are more affected by minimum-wage hikes” is not a novel idea by itself. The novelty has to come from the policy regime: the unusually large escalation and the explicit connection to the 60%-of-median benchmark. If the paper cannot make that feel genuinely new, it will read as an incremental application.

### Is it an ambition problem?

Yes. The paper is competent but safe. It does not yet seize the biggest question it could answer. It is content to be “a careful paper on Slovakia,” when it should be trying to be “a paper about the limits of extrapolating the small-effects consensus.”

### Single most impactful advice

**Rewrite the paper around the question of whether minimum-wage effects become economically meaningful once the wage floor approaches 60 percent of median pay, and treat Slovakia only as the setting that lets you answer that question.**

That is the one change that would most improve its odds.
