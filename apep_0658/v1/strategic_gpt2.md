## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether taxing one form of housing wealth can redirect investment rather than simply suppress it. Using Norway’s 2022 increase in the wealth-tax assessment of secondary homes, it argues that municipalities more exposed to second-home ownership saw more building permits, more firm entry, and more out-migration—suggesting that taxes on existing vacation or investment properties may reallocate capital toward new construction.

Why should a busy economist care? Because the paper is trying to overturn a familiar instinct: that higher taxes on housing reduce housing investment. If credible and well framed, that is a potentially important idea for public finance, urban, and housing economists.

Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs? Not quite. The opening is reasonably vivid, but it gets bogged down quickly in institutional detail and “first causal evidence” language. The sharper pitch is not “here is a Norwegian reform and my design,” but “taxing existing housing wealth can increase construction by shifting portfolios across housing assets.” That needs to be the front-door claim.

### The pitch the paper should have

A stronger first two paragraphs would say something like:

> Economists typically think taxes on housing reduce investment in housing. But when tax policy changes the relative attractiveness of different housing assets—say, existing second homes versus newly built units—the effect may be reallocation rather than contraction. Whether housing taxes reduce supply overall or redirect investment across segments of the housing market is a first-order question for both tax policy and housing affordability.
>
> This paper studies Norway’s 2022 reform that increased the wealth-tax valuation of secondary dwellings. I show that municipalities more exposed to second-home ownership experienced a disproportionate rise in residential construction, along with more business formation and population outflows. The central implication is that taxing existing housing wealth can redirect capital toward new building, suggesting that the structure of housing taxation may matter as much as its level.

That is the story. Right now the paper knows this, but does not lead with it hard enough.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### Contribution in one sentence

The paper’s contribution is to argue that a tax increase on existing secondary homes can reallocate real-estate investment toward new construction, rather than simply reducing housing investment overall.

### Is this clearly differentiated from the closest papers?

Only partially. The paper cites wealth-tax response papers and broad housing-supply papers, but the differentiation is still mushy. It currently sounds like a combination of “wealth taxes matter” plus “housing supply responds,” without sharply locating the novelty. The distinct claim is not merely “wealth taxes have real effects” or “housing taxes affect housing.” It is:

1. **the margin of adjustment is within real estate,**
2. **the treated asset is existing secondary homes,**
3. **the response is increased new construction,**
4. **so the relevant object is reallocation across housing asset types, not aggregate savings or evasion.**

That distinction is potentially interesting, but the introduction does not yet separate it cleanly from adjacent work on tax-induced mobility, property taxation, or wealth taxation.

### Is the contribution framed as a question about the world, or a gap in a literature?

Mostly as a literature gap. The stronger version is a world question:

- **Current framing:** “This contributes to wealth taxation, housing supply, and tax-induced mobility.”
- **Better framing:** “When governments tax existing housing wealth more heavily, do they depress housing investment, or do they push capital into new supply?”

The latter is much stronger and much more AER-like.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?

At present, maybe, but not crisply. Too many would summarize it as: “It’s a DiD on a Norwegian wealth tax reform and housing permits.” That is not enough.

The introduction needs to make the reader say: “Ah—the paper claims that taxing one type of housing asset can increase construction by shifting portfolios within housing.” That is memorable.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Several possibilities:

1. **Different outcome variable:**  
   The paper leans heavily on building permits. For a top-journal contribution, “permits” can feel one step removed from the economically big object. If they could show actual starts, completions, unit mix, sales composition, or whether the new units are primary vs. secondary dwellings, the contribution becomes much larger.

2. **Stronger mechanism outcome:**  
   The paper’s mechanism is portfolio rebalancing, but the evidence is indirect. Anything on who builds, what gets built, whether corporate/developer activity rises, whether existing second-home transactions fall, or whether primary-dwelling registrations rise would materially enlarge the contribution.

3. **Different comparison:**  
   The paper should more directly contrast “tax on existing stock” versus “tax on new supply.” Right now it speaks vaguely about housing taxation. The sharper and bigger point is about **differential taxation across housing assets**.

4. **Different framing:**  
   The paper should be framed less as “wealth taxation in Norway” and more as “how asset-specific housing taxation shapes the composition of housing supply.” That gives it broader portability.

That framing connects to affordability, vacancy, second-home regulation, and efficient tax design. That is a better and larger conversation.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Before this paper, the standard intuition is that taxes on housing wealth reduce incentives to invest in housing. Wealth-tax work has focused on savings, evasion, and mobility; housing work has focused on regulation, geography, and user cost.

### Tension

But when a tax change targets one segment of the housing stock—existing secondary homes—the effect on overall investment is ambiguous. Does it depress real-estate activity, or does it redirect capital toward untaxed or relatively lightly taxed forms of housing, such as new primary dwellings?

### Resolution

The paper reports that more exposed municipalities experience more building permits, more enterprise formation, and more out-migration after the reform. It interprets this as reallocation from taxed secondary dwellings into new construction.

### Implications

If true, the structure of housing taxation shapes not just how much housing investment occurs, but where within the housing market it goes. That has implications for wealth-tax design, second-home policy, and housing-supply policy.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the ingredients, but not a fully disciplined arc. At present it still feels somewhat like a collection of interesting reduced-form results—permits, enterprises, migration—searching for a single story. The migration result in particular risks distracting from the main contribution unless tightly integrated.

### What story should it be telling?

The story should be:

1. Governments often want to discourage second-home or investor use of housing without choking off supply.
2. A differential tax on existing secondary homes creates exactly that test.
3. The reform appears to have shifted activity toward new building.
4. Therefore, differentiated housing taxation may be a supply-shifting instrument, not merely a revenue tool.

Everything else should serve that story. If the paper cannot integrate the migration result tightly, it should demote it.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The main gap is a mix of **framing problem** and **scope/mechanism problem**.

- **Framing problem:** The paper has a better idea than it currently advertises. It should be framed as a paper on the design of housing taxation and the reallocation of investment within housing markets, not mainly as a Norwegian wealth-tax event study.
- **Scope/mechanism problem:** For AER, the reader will want more direct evidence that the observed construction increase really reflects substitution from taxed secondary dwellings toward new supply, rather than just correlated local shocks.

It is **not primarily a novelty problem**—the sign reversal is interesting. Nor is it mainly an ambition problem—the paper is trying to say something big. The issue is that the current draft does not yet fully cash out that ambition.

### The single most impactful advice

If the author could change only one thing, it would be this:

**Reframe the paper around a single big question—whether differential taxation across housing assets can increase new supply by redirecting investment—and then reorganize every section to support that claim, especially with more direct evidence on what was built and why.**

That one change would improve both the introduction and the paper’s claim to broad interest.
