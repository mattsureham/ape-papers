## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether taxing one form of housing wealth—secondary dwellings and vacation homes—changes where capital goes within real estate markets. Using Norway’s 2022 increase in the wealth-tax assessment of secondary dwellings, it argues that municipalities more exposed to the reform saw more new construction, suggesting that taxes may reallocate housing investment rather than simply depress it.

A busy economist should care because the paper is trying to overturn a simple intuition: higher taxation of housing should reduce housing investment. If instead differential taxation across housing assets pushes investors from existing second homes into new construction, that has implications for wealth taxation, housing supply, and local development.

### Does the paper itself articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Not well enough. The current opening starts with Hemsedal and gets to the core idea fairly quickly, but the paper still reads too much like “a Norwegian tax reform produced surprising permit responses” and not enough like “this is about how taxes reshape the composition of housing investment.” The current introduction also overclaims “first causal evidence” too early and too confidently, before the reader is persuaded that the broader question is important.

### What the first two paragraphs should say instead

The introduction should lead with the economic question, not the municipality anecdote:

> Governments increasingly tax housing wealth, but we know little about whether such taxes reduce housing investment overall or simply redirect it across assets. This distinction matters: if higher taxes on existing second homes push investors toward new construction, then wealth taxation may increase some forms of housing supply even while making other housing assets less attractive.

> This paper studies that reallocation margin using Norway’s 2022 reform, which increased the wealth-tax assessment of secondary dwellings from 90 to 100 percent of market value while leaving primary dwellings much more lightly assessed. I show that municipalities with greater pre-reform exposure to secondary dwellings experienced substantially larger increases in residential building permits after the reform. The paper’s central claim is not just that a tax changed local outcomes, but that differential taxation within housing can reallocate capital from taxed existing properties toward new development.

That is the pitch the paper should have. It is cleaner, world-facing, and gives the reader a reason to care before any details about Norway.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper claims that increasing wealth taxation on secondary dwellings can reallocate investment within real estate markets toward new residential construction.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest papers?

Only partially. The paper distinguishes itself from wealth-tax papers on avoidance, reported wealth, and mobility, but the differentiation is still too generic. Right now the contribution sounds like: “existing papers study wealth taxes; I study one more outcome.” That is not enough for AER positioning.

The sharper distinction is:

- prior wealth-tax work emphasizes **tax base erosion, saving, avoidance, and migration**;
- this paper emphasizes **asset reallocation within the housing sector**;
- prior housing-tax work often asks whether taxes reduce housing demand/investment;
- this paper asks whether **relative taxation across housing asset classes changes the composition of supply**.

That distinction is present, but it is not driven home with enough force.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a literature gap?

Mostly as a literature gap. The introduction has three “I contribute to the literature” paragraphs in standard seminar format. That is serviceable, but not compelling.

The stronger world question is: **When governments tax existing housing wealth, do they shrink housing investment or redirect it into new supply?** That is a real question about the world. The introduction should be built around that.

### Could a smart economist explain what is new after reading the introduction?

At present, maybe, but only vaguely. They would probably say: “It’s a DiD on a Norwegian wealth-tax reform showing more permits in high–vacation-home municipalities.” That is not yet the kind of distilled novelty that travels.

What you want them to say is: “Interesting paper—it suggests wealth taxes can reallocate capital within housing from existing second homes to new construction.” That is much stronger.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Several possibilities:

1. **Make the reallocation margin concrete.**  
   Right now “portfolio rebalancing” is an interpretive gloss. The paper would be strategically bigger if it could speak more directly to what was built and for whom. Even descriptive evidence on permit type, unit size, location within municipality, or developer entry would help the contribution feel like a housing-market paper rather than a reduced-form policy note.

2. **Focus more tightly on composition, less on miscellaneous local outcomes.**  
   Building permits are the big result. New enterprises and migration are supporting pieces, but at present they make the paper look scattered. If the authors could show that the construction response is concentrated in the most plausible substitute category, the contribution becomes much larger.

3. **Tie to a broader policy design question.**  
   The broader claim should be about **the structure of housing taxation** rather than one Norwegian reform. The paper could be framed as evidence that tax wedges between primary residences, second homes, and new construction shape the allocation of capital across housing uses.

4. **Show whether supply rises where housing scarcity matters.**  
   As written, the action appears concentrated in resort municipalities. That makes the paper interesting, but still niche. To become bigger, it needs to explain why economists beyond the “vacation homes in Norway” crowd should care. One route is to say this is a general lesson about tax-induced reallocation in segmented housing markets, even if the Norwegian case is an extreme version.

   - Kleven et al. (2014), Agrawal and Foremny / Agrawal et al. on relocation responses.
   - But this is secondary, not core.

### How should it position itself relative to those neighbors?

Mostly **build on and redirect**, not attack.

- Build on the wealth-tax literature by saying: it has taught us that wealth taxes affect reporting, avoidance, and location, but much less about how they reshape the composition of real investment.
- Build on housing-tax models by saying: standard user-cost logic often predicts less housing investment, but segmented tax treatment within housing creates a reallocation margin that can reverse that comparative static for some forms of construction.
- Do **not** oversell as overturning the housing taxation literature. That will read as naïve. Better to say it identifies a margin those models can accommodate but empirical work has not emphasized.

### Is the paper positioned too narrowly or too broadly?

Oddly, both.

- **Too narrowly** because the empirical narrative is very Norway-specific and very tied to resort municipalities and vacation cabins.
- **Too broadly** because the literature review sprawls into wealth taxation, housing supply, migration, tax salience, and local business formation without a clear center.

The right audience is narrower but clearer: **public finance + urban/housing**. The paper should own that intersection.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?

A few gaps in conversation:

1. **Asset substitution and tax arbitrage within household portfolios**  
   The paper gestures toward this, but it should engage more directly with tax-induced portfolio choice and capital reallocation, not just wealth-tax incidence.

2. **Property tax and housing composition**  
   There is a large public-finance / urban literature on how property taxation affects land use, structure, redevelopment, and tenure margins. Even if not exact substitutes, those are more useful neighbors than generic migration papers.

3. **Second-home / amenity housing / tourism real estate**  
   Since the treated municipalities appear to be resort-heavy, the paper should probably acknowledge work on seasonal housing, amenity demand, and tourism-driven development. Otherwise the reader suspects the authors do not fully understand the economic environment generating the result.

4. **Tax notch / discontinuity evidence in housing or wealth taxation**  
   If there is micro evidence on bunching, valuation, or ownership form responses, this paper should at least mention how its municipal evidence complements that.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?

Not quite. It is currently trying to have three conversations at once:
- wealth taxes,
- housing supply,
- mobility.

The paper’s best conversation is: **public finance meets urban economics: differential housing wealth taxation changes the composition and location of housing investment.**

That is a better conversation than “wealth tax also affects migration and entrepreneurship.”

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Housing wealth is taxed differently across assets, but most empirical work asks whether taxes reduce wealth, induce avoidance, or trigger migration. Standard intuition says taxing housing more should reduce investment in housing.

### Tension

When only one segment of housing is taxed more heavily—secondary dwellings, not primary residences—investors may not leave housing altogether. They may shift into a more lightly taxed segment, including new construction. We have little evidence on that reallocation margin.

### Resolution

The paper finds that municipalities more exposed to taxed secondary dwellings experienced larger post-reform increases in building permits, along with some increase in business formation and out-migration.

### Implications

The structure of housing taxation—not just its overall level—may shape whether capital remains in housing and where it goes. Wealth taxes may reallocate investment rather than simply suppress it.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the bones of one, but right now it still feels somewhat like a collection of municipal outcomes around a reform. The building-permit result is the real story. Enterprise formation and migration are currently satellites that threaten to dilute the narrative rather than deepen it.

The story it should be telling is:

1. Tax reform increases the relative cost of holding existing secondary dwellings.
2. In places where those assets matter most, investors need a substitute.
3. New construction is a plausible substitute because it is a different tax object and can be channeled into more lightly taxed uses.
4. Therefore, differential housing taxation can shift capital from old assets to new supply.

That is a proper setup-tension-resolution arc. At the moment, the paper gets there, but only after a lot of regression summary.

And that is the strategic issue. Even before referee-level identification questions, the paper needs a more compelling answer to the conceptual version of that question. If the response is just “the timing and monotonicity are consistent with rebalancing,” that is not enough to make the story travel.

Not relevant here; the findings are large and surprising. The problem is not modesty—it is interpretability and scope.

   The paper currently does this somewhat, but then quickly falls into coefficient recital. The intro should say:

   Enterprise formation and migration should be demoted unless they are essential for mechanism. Right now they read like “more outcomes from the same design.” That is not helping the narrative.

4. **Shorten the institutional background.**  
   The institutional section is useful but too leisurely relative to the paper’s current level of ambition. Compress the boilerplate on the Norwegian wealth tax and move some details to an appendix or footnotes.

5. **Trim robustness from the main text.**  
   This draft devotes a lot of precious real estate to inferential reassurance and robustness cataloguing. For an editorially strong draft, the main text should emphasize the substantive pattern and its interpretation; some of the specification variants can be moved or compressed.

6. **Expand the discussion of what the outcome means economically.**  
   Not more caveats, but more interpretation:
   - what types of municipalities drive the effect,
   - what “building permits” means in these markets,
   - why permits are the right manifestation of reallocation.

7. **Rebuild the conclusion.**  
   The current conclusion is honest but too hedged and repetitive. It should end on a sharper point about policy design: taxes that discriminate across housing uses may redirect capital in unintended or intended ways.

### Are interesting results buried?

The most interesting result is already in the abstract and main table. The problem is not burial; it is dilution. The paper has one strong result and keeps surrounding it with weaker companion outcomes.

### Is the conclusion adding value?

Only modestly. It mostly summarizes and reiterates caution. The conclusion should instead translate the result into a broader policy lesson and explicitly state what belief should change if the finding is true.
