## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple policy-economy question: when governments impose disclosure mandates on large firms, do those regulations chill entry by smaller, exempt firms through perceived compliance burdens or industry-wide spillovers? Using England’s 2022 calorie-labeling mandate and Scotland as a control, the paper finds essentially no effect on new food-service business formation, suggesting that a prominent industry claim about market-wide deterrence was overstated.

A busy economist should care because the broader issue is not calorie labels per se; it is whether “light-touch” regulation creates hidden barriers to entry. That is a first-order question in regulatory economics and political economy.

**Does the paper articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Not quite. The current opening is competent, but it starts too close to the policy details and too quickly slides into “this literature has not studied X.” The strongest version of this paper is not “first causal estimate of calorie labeling on entry.” That sounds narrow and incremental. The stronger pitch is: **do disclosure mandates create the kind of supply-side distortions opponents routinely claim?**

### The pitch the paper should have

Here is what the first two paragraphs should say instead:

> Opponents of disclosure regulation often make a specific economic claim: even if a mandate directly targets only large firms, compliance costs and industry-wide uncertainty will spill over to deter entry by smaller firms. This claim matters well beyond food policy. If true, seemingly modest transparency rules could reduce competition and entrepreneurship; if false, a central argument against disclosure mandates loses force.
>
> This paper studies that claim using England’s 2022 mandatory calorie-labeling law, which applied only to food businesses with 250 or more employees. Comparing food-service entry in England and Scotland before and after the reform, relative to other sectors, I find no evidence that the mandate deterred new business formation among exempt firms. The result suggests that one common political-economy objection to disclosure regulation—market-wide entry deterrence—may be more rhetoric than reality.

That is the AER-worthy version of the story. It elevates the paper from “a null result on calorie labels” to “evidence on the incidence of regulatory burden narratives.”

2. broader disclosure-regulation studies,

### What would make this contribution bigger?
Most importantly: **broaden the object of interest from calorie labeling to disclosure mandates as a class of regulation.** Specific ways to do that:

The paper’s biggest limitation as a contribution is not the null; it is the narrowness of the outcome and framing.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Industry groups often claim that new regulations, even informational ones, will impose burdens that ripple through an industry and reduce entrepreneurship. England’s calorie-labeling law provides a case where such warnings were explicit.

### Tension
The regulation directly applied only to large firms, but opponents argued broader market consequences. That creates a genuine empirical and conceptual tension: can a targeted disclosure rule deter entry by exempt small businesses?

### Resolution
The paper finds no detectable decline in new food-service incorporations in England relative to Scotland and other sectors.

### Implications
If the result is taken seriously, it suggests that one common argument against disclosure mandates—entry deterrence beyond the directly regulated firms—may be overstated. That matters for regulatory design and for how economists interpret industry lobbying.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
It has the skeleton of one, but not a polished arc. At present it reads somewhat like:
1. policy background,
2. data,
3. DDD,
4. null result,
5. discussion.

That is functional, but the storytelling is not fully in control. The paper’s best story is not “here is a natural experiment and here is a null.” The story should be:

- **Claim in the world:** disclosure mandates will deter entry.
- **Why this is plausible:** spillovers, expectations, strategic responses.
- **Why this setting is useful:** large-firm mandate, exempt small entrants, public industry warnings.
- **What we learn:** those spillovers do not appear.
- **Why beliefs should update:** some compliance-cost narratives are not empirically consequential at the entry margin.

The current draft has results looking for a bigger story. The story exists, but it is under-exploited.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?
“England forced large chains to post calories, the industry warned this would hurt the whole sector, and I find basically zero effect on small-business entry.”

That is the right lead.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
Mixed. They will lean in for about 30 seconds because the broad issue—whether regulation suppresses entry—is interesting. They will reach for their phones if the paper then stays at the level of “restaurant incorporations in England vs Scotland.” To hold attention, the presenter has to pivot immediately from the policy detail to the general proposition about disclosure regulation and compliance-cost politics.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Probably one of these:
- “Is this really about calorie labels, or about disclosure mandates generally?”
- “Why should I believe entry is the margin where these costs would show up?”
- “If not entry, where would the burden appear—prices, margins, outlet openings, product mix?”
- “Is the real contribution that industry cost claims are exaggerated?”

The paper should anticipate these and answer them in the introduction, not wait until the discussion.

### If the findings are null or modest: is the null itself interesting?
Potentially yes—but only if the paper makes the null do conceptual work. A null is interesting when it rules out an important feared mechanism. Here that mechanism is market-wide entry deterrence.

The paper is close to making that case, but not fully there. At moments it still reads like “we looked and found nothing.” That is not enough. The author must argue that:
1. this was a prominent ex ante policy claim,
2. the setting is one where spillovers were plausible,
3. the confidence interval is informative enough to matter,
4. the implication is not “regulations never matter,” but “this specific anti-regulatory argument has limited bite here.”

Done well, that is a publishable null. Done weakly, it feels like a failed experiment.
