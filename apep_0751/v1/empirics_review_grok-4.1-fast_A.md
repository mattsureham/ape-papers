### 1. Idea Fidelity
The paper deviates substantially from the original idea manifest. The manifest promised a tract-level panel from the unexploited USDA SNAP Retailer Historical Database (2005-2025, 300K+ retailers, geocoded with entry/exit dates), estimating causal effects of retailer exits on food security and health outcomes (e.g., CDC PLACES obesity/diabetes/food insecurity) using two strategies: (1) chain-level corporate shocks as IVs (e.g., national closures from mergers/bankruptcies) and (2) differential exposure to the 2016 stocking rule DiD for small-format SNAP retailers. Instead, the paper uses county-level Census County Business Patterns (CBP) data on total food retailers (NAICS 445110/445120, not SNAP-specific), a single continuous-treatment DiD with 2015 convenience share as intensity, no IVs, no tract-level analysis, no SNAP authorization/exit data, and no health/food access outcomes. The research question narrows to total convenience store counts (not SNAP exits or downstream food access/health), missing the manifest's supply-side causal novelty, policy relevance to 2025 rules via health impacts, and feasibility-checked data (SNAP DB, CDC PLACES). This is a fundamental mismatch, undermining the paper's claimed contributions.

### 2. Summary
This paper tests whether the 2016 USDA SNAP stocking rule, which raised minimum variety requirements for authorized retailers, caused exits among small-format (convenience) stores using a county-level continuous-treatment difference-in-differences design. Treatment intensity is the pre-reform (2015) share of convenience stores in Census CBP data, interacted with a post-2018 indicator; event studies reveal pre-trends violating parallel trends, so state-by-year fixed effects yield the preferred null estimate (-0.11 log points on convenience stores, insignificant). The null suggests adaptation or weak enforcement rather than exit, with implications for the 2025 proposed rule tightening standards further.

### 3. Essential Points
**(1) Identification strategy lacks credibility due to non-SNAP-specific data and persistent endogeneity threats.** CBP counts *all* food retailers, not SNAP-authorized ones, so the design cannot distinguish SNAP deauthorizations (the policy margin) from total closures/entry. A convenience store dropping SNAP but staying open appears as a non-exit, attenuating effects conservatively—but the paper cannot quantify SNAP-specific impacts or test if non-SNAP shifts biased results. Even with state-year FEs, the continuous treatment (pre-2015 convenience share) plausibly correlates with unobserved county trends (e.g., rural depopulation, Dollar General targeting specific counties), as evidenced by violated pre-trends and urban-rural heterogeneity. Authors must provide direct evidence (e.g., correlations between CBP changes and SNAP data) that CBP proxies SNAP dynamics credibly, or switch to SNAP data; otherwise, causal claims are untenable.

**(2) Empirical approach mismatches the research question on food access unraveling.** The question implies downstream effects (e.g., food deserts, health), but outcomes are only aggregate retailer counts—no measures of SNAP-accepting access (e.g., distance to stores), food insecurity, or health (as promised). County-level aggregation masks tract-level variation in low-income areas, where corner stores matter most. Null on counts does not imply null access effects if surviving stores adapt stock or if exits cluster in deserts. Authors must either add access/health outcomes (e.g., USDA Food Access Atlas, CDC PLACES) with matching geography/scale or reframe narrowly to "total food retail exit."

**(3) Short post-period and low power undermine null result informativeness.** Only 4 post-years (2018-2021) versus 8 pre-years, with COVID disruptions unaddressed (e.g., 2020-21 retail shifts). Preferred estimate has SE=0.094 (p=0.24), wide CIs (-0.30 to 0.07) compatible with large effects; no power calculations or minimum detectable effects. Event-study post-coefficients are noisy (e.g., k=3 SE=0.067). Authors must report power (e.g., for 10-20% exit effect), extend data if possible (CBP to 2022?), or conduct simulations bounding minimum effects consistent with data.

### 4. Suggestions
The paper is well-written, transparent about limitations (e.g., pre-trends, measurement), and AER:Insights-appropriate in length/style, with strong diagnostics (event studies, placebo, Poisson QMLE). The null framing is policy-relevant amid 2025 debates, complementing demand-side work (Allcott et al.). To elevate it, prioritize aligning with the manifest's strengths while shoring up the current design.

**Data and measurement improvements (top priority):** Download/process the SNAP Retailer Historical Database (Shannon repo confirmed feasible) for a tract-level panel of SNAP-specific entries/exits (auth_date, end_date, store_type). This directly measures the policy margin: exit/deauthorization rates for small-format SNAP retailers, geocoded to ~75K tracts x 20 years. Merge with CDC PLACES (tract-level food insecurity/obesity 2020-23 via API) and ACS (poverty/vehicle access) for outcomes. Replicate DiD at tract level with small-SNAP-share as intensity—finer geography better isolates low-income effects. Validate CBP as proxy by regressing CBP changes on SNAP exits (supplemental table). For 2025 relevance, preview 2022-25 SNAP exits post-proposal.

**Bolster identification:** Implement manifest's Strategy 1: corporate shock IVs. Identify ~30-50 chain-level shocks (e.g., Walmart 2016 closures, Dollar General safety violations via SEC/press) as instruments for tract/county SNAP exits—exclusion via HQ decisions uncorrelated with local health. First stage: chain exit share per tract; reduced form on health/access. Test overID with multiple shocks. For current DiD, interact convenience share with rural/poor-tract dummies *pre-trends* (e.g., Roth et al. 2023 methods) to formally correct/confirm state-year FEs suffice. Add county linear trends or synthetic controls for heterogeneity.

**Outcomes and heterogeneity:** Expand to food access: compute tract-level average distance to SNAP retailer (pre/post) or % tracts >1mi from SNAP store (Food Access Atlas). Test health spillovers (CDC PLACES diabetes/obesity, controlling baselines). Heterogeneity table: triple interactions with baseline food desert status (RUCC/ACS), SNAP usage (ACS), or vehicle access—does null mask effects in deserts? Bound intensive margin: if feasible, proxy stock quality via CBP subcategory shifts (e.g., NAICS produce specialists).

**Robustness and presentation:** Power analysis: simulate for β=-0.10 (10% exit) under clustered SEs—report min detectable effect (e.g., via simulations in R/Stata). COVID robustness: exclude 2020-21 or add COVID×share. Balance table: pre-trends by convenience-share deciles (graph + stats). Appendix: full event study for supermarkets/total; SDE table good, but clarify "Moderate negative" ignores insignificance—use CIs. JEL/keywords fine; cite more SNAP lit (e.g., Hoynes on access).

**Broader enhancements:** Discuss adaptation channels empirically: regress SNAP exits on pre-stock (if available) or proxy via firm size (CBP payroll/employment). Policy section: quantify 2025 rule's implied exposure (e.g., % small SNAP retailers below 28 items). If null holds with SNAP data, it's a strong contribution—supply shocks small vs. demand. Total revisions could make this publishable; current version reject/revise heavily due to data/ID gaps.
