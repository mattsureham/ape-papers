The submitted manuscript focuses solely on a county-level continuous-treatment difference-in-differences using CBP data to test for retailer exit after the 2016 SNAP stocking rule. **Summary**

The paper evaluates whether the 2016 USDA SNAP stocking rule, which raised minimum variety requirements from 3 to 7 items per category, reduced convenience store access across U.S. counties. Using a continuous-treatment DiD that interacts county-level pre-reform convenience shares with a post-2018 indicator, the author finds that baseline specifications suffer from strong pre-trends, and the most credible specification—including state-by-year fixed effects—yields a statistically insignificant negative effect on convenience store counts. The paper highlights the difficulty of identifying supply-side effects of USDA quality requirements and stresses the importance of robustness checks in continuous-treatment DiD designs.

**Essential Points**

1. **Identification strategy does not target SNAP-authorized exits or food access effects.** The paper measures total convenience store counts rather than SNAP-authorized retailer presence, and the treatment intensity is based on historical convenience shares rather than exogenous variation (e.g., corporate shocks or rule-induced compliance margins). This weakens the link between the research question (“SNAP retailer exits and food access”) and the empirical strategy. The authors should either reframe the research question to match the data (e.g., “Did counties with more convenience stores lose more food retailers after the stocking rule?”) or, preferably, incorporate the SNAP Retailer Historical Database and the IV approach promised in the manifest to directly observe authorization dynamics.

2. **Causal interpretation remains tenuous despite state-by-year fixed effects.** While the paper acknowledges pre-trends and adjusts by adding state-by-year fixed effects, it still relies on cross-county variation in pre-2017 convenience shares to proxy for treatment intensity. This assumes no other shocks (e.g., Dollar General expansion, rural depopulation, broadband access) differentially correlated with convenience shares within states. Without a more exogenous source of variation—such as exploiting chain-level shocks or compliance differentials—the preferred specification may still reflect persistent heterogeneity rather than the policy effect. The authors must make a stronger case for why the remaining variation plausibly isolates the stocking rule’s impact or find an alternative strategy (e.g., using administrative SNAP data to see which stores actually faced compliance pressure).

3. **Measurement of the outcome is disconnected from the policy mechanism.** The policy affects SNAP participation, not necessarily total food retailers. A convenience store that drops SNAP authorization but stays open is not captured as a decline in CBP counts. Thus the paper tests for retailer closure rather than SNAP-specific exit, limiting the policy relevance of the findings. Without SNAP-specific data, the null result may simply reflect that stores can remain open after losing SNAP. The authors should better justify why CBP counts are a valid proxy or, ideally, supplement the analysis with SNAP retailer data that records authorization and termination dates as in the manifest.

Given these critical concerns, especially the mismatch between the stated research question/identification plan and the implemented empirical strategy, I would recommend rejection unless the authors substantially rework the paper to align more closely with the original identification strategy or convincingly defend the validity of the current approach.

**Suggestions**

1. **Leverage the promised SNAP Retailer Historical Database.** The manifest highlights a tract-level panel covering 2005–2025 with authorization and end dates for 300K+ retailers. This dataset is essential to observe the actual margin of interest—SNAP exits. Incorporating it would allow you to directly measure retailer deauthorizations/exits post-2016, rather than relying on CBP counts that mix SNAP and non-SNAP stores. You could still aggregate to counties or tracts, but the key is using retailer-level authorization changes. This would also enable linking exposure to health/diet outcomes from CDC PLACES, aligning with the bigger-picture policy stakes.

2. **Implement the pre-registered dual identification strategy.** The manifest proposes two strategies: (i) chain-level corporate shocks as instrumental variables for retailer exits and (ii) a stocking rule DiD exploiting the 2016 rule’s differential impact on small-format stores. The current draft only carries out the latter in a simplistic form. Pursuing the IV approach (e.g., exogenous closures or mergers of large chains) would strengthen the causal claim about retailer exit. Explicitly modeling how a chain-wide shock affects a tract's SNAP retailer count would also better map onto the “extensive margin of food access” mentioned in the manifest.

3. **Reframe treatment intensity and address endogenous sorting.** If you continue with a continuous-treatment DiD, consider devising a more credible treatment variation. For instance, interact the rule with an indicator for counties where a high share of convenience stores are SNAP-authorized as of 2016—this ties treatment to the affected population. Alternatively, use an event-study exploiting differential rollout of compliance inspections or enforcement intensity (if such data exists). Whatever the treatment is, ensure it truly reflects the policy’s bite and is orthogonal to other concurrent trends. Presenting falsification tests (e.g., using retail sectors unaffected by the rule) would also help.

4. **Measure the policy’s effect on SNAP-specific outcomes.** The motivation is that tightening requirements might reduce access to SNAP benefits for vulnerable communities. Even if SNAP-specific data is unavailable for the entire period, consider using partial data or case studies. For example, combine USDA data on SNAP retailer deauthorizations with CBP counts to estimate how many exits were SNAP-related. Additionally, if tract-level health measures are available, explore whether deviations in food insecurity or diet-related health align with retailer dynamics—this would tie the supply-side changes to the broader welfare question.

5. **Clarify the scope and limitations transparently.** The paper presently states that the null result is informative but doesn’t fully acknowledge that the outcome deviates from the stated question. A dedicated section discussing how CBP counts map (or fail to map) to SNAP retailer access, and how much attenuation/measurement error this introduces, would improve transparency. Similarly, be explicit about the mechanism assumed in the preferred specification: is it compliance costs leading to closure, or the rule pushing stores to exit SNAP? Spell out these assumptions and their implications for interpretation.

6. **Enhance robustness with additional diagnostics.** The event study convincingly shows pre-trends, but the state-by-year fixed effects specification is relatively opaque. Consider presenting the event study under the preferred (state × year) specification to show whether the pre-trends persist when using the variation you ultimately rely on. Also, report placebo tests using other industries (e.g., pharmacies) or other policy dates to demonstrate that the null is not driven by general retail cycles.

7. **Tie results back to policy.** The manifest emphasizes the proposed 2025 rule. Use the empirical findings (or limitations) to inform that policy debate: if the 2016 rule had no detectable effect on total convenience store counts, what does that imply about possible future costs of stricter rules? Are there reasons to expect a stronger response under the 2025 proposal (e.g., magnitude of change, enforcement, adoption delays)? Providing this forward-looking discussion would help policymakers understand how to interpret your null in context.

By aligning the empirical strategy more closely with the original idea, improving measurement of the treatment and outcome, and strengthening diagnostics, the paper could make a substantive contribution to understanding how SNAP retailer standards affect food access.
