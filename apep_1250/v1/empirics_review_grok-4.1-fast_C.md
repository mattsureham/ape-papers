### 2. Summary
This AER: Insights-style paper examines whether the UK's 2015 payday lending cap increased formal housing distress, measured by landlord vs. mortgage possession claims in English and Welsh courts, exploiting regional variation in post-cap HCSTC penetration. Using a triple-difference design on LA-quarter data (2003Q1–2019Q4), it finds no evidence of a post-cap rise in high-exposure areas: the baseline interaction is negative (-0.0030, SE=0.0010, implying 12% lower private-minus-mortgage differential across the exposure range), but weakens with trends (p=0.204 permutation). The disciplined null challenges claims that payday loans smoothed rent arrears on this margin, contributing to debates on high-cost credit regulation and housing insecurity.

### 3. Essential Points
1. **Post-treatment exposure measure undermines identification.** The FCA HCSTC loans-per-1,000-adults is observed in 2017–2018, over 2.5 years post-cap, making it a noisy proxy for pre-cap penetration rather than a predetermined dose. This invites classical measurement error (attenuating toward zero), reverse selection (high-exposure regions may reflect post-cap survivors), and post-treatment bias (e.g., if cap effects altered regional reliance nonlinearly). Authors must source or proxy pre-cap exposure—e.g., via FCA PSD006 aggregates, firm-level reports, or lender disclosures pre-2015—or explicitly falsify with pre-policy predictions (e.g., regress post-cap measure on pre-2015 observables like poverty rates). Absent this, the DiD assumption fails, and results are suggestive at best.

2. **Pre-trend violation and few-cluster inference require resolution.** The event study shows a significant positive blip at t-2 (0.0022***, SE=0.0003), violating parallel trends in the private-minus-mortgage differential. With only 10 regions (clusters), clustered SEs are unreliable (over-reject prone per Cameron et al. 2008; MacKinnon 2022), and the permutation p=0.204 (on trends spec) correctly disciplines claims—but baseline ** significance misleads without upfront disclosure. Authors must: (i) test/adjust pre-trends (e.g., include region-linear × pre-period trends); (ii) report wild cluster bootstrap or CRT p-values alongside permutation; and (iii) bound effects under sensitivity (e.g., Rambachan & Roth 2023). If pre-trends persist, downgrade to stacked synthetic control or reject DiD.

3. **Omission of renter-specific confounders biases toward null.** Private-minus-mortgage differencing absorbs common shocks but not renter-unique policies like UC rollout (2013–2018, LA-varying, directly targeting rent arrears) or 2019 tenant fees ban. These likely covary with regional exposure (higher North/South West deprivation), contaminating post-2015 private claims. Include LA-quarter controls for UC penetration (ONS rollout schedules), regional house prices/unemployment (Land Registry/NOMIS), and leads/lags of fees ban. If unbalanced, use triple-differencing with national shocks or matching.
