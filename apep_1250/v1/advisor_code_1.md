The empirical strategy remains a continuous-treatment DiD that compares outcomes across local authorities with differing exposure, even though the published version pivots to a private-minus-mortgage log-difference outcome rather than analyzing landlord claims alone. **Summary**

The paper tests whether the 2015 UK payday-loan price cap increased possession claims by exploiting regional variation in HCSTC intensity and a national policy shock. Using local-authority-quarter data on private-landlord versus mortgage possession claims, the preferred triple-difference specification finds a negative—not positive—interaction between exposure and the post-cap period, although this estimate weakens once region-specific trends are added and a permutation test yields a p-value of 0.204. The conclusion is a disciplined null: the data do not show that the cap heightened formal housing distress in areas that were relatively dependent on payday credit.

**Essential Points**

1. **Pre-treatment validity of the treatment variable remains tenuous.** The regional HCSTC intensity used as the “dose” is observed only in the July 2017–June 2018 window, more than two years after the cap. The justification that this post-period exposure proxies for pre-cap reliance rests on the assumption of stable regional rankings, but this is not tested or documented. The paper needs to strengthen this argument—either through auxiliary evidence (e.g., comparing share of outlets, payday-loan branch count, or other indicators from before 2015) or sensitivity checks showing that plausible re-rankings would not overturn the conclusions—or else emphasize more clearly that identification is contingent on it. This is the central identifying assumption, and its opacity undermines the causal interpretation.

2. **Treatment variation occurs at a very coarse level with only 10 effective clusters, so inference must be handled with utmost care.** While the paper acknowledges the few-cluster problem and reports permutation p‑values and region-specific trends, the main text still relies heavily on conventional standard errors and a point estimate that changes substantially once flexible trends are introduced. It would strengthen the paper to foreground the permutation-based confidence intervals (e.g., percentile or randomization-based intervals) and to make clear that the baseline clustered SEs are unreliable. Without that, readers may overstate the precision of the estimates.

3. **Control for concurrent policies affecting renters is insufficient.** Universal Credit rollout, welfare reforms, and tenant-fees bans all shifted incentives for landlords and tenants during the study period and could differentially affect private versus mortgage possession claims across regions. The private-minus-mortgage differential helps but does not eliminate the concern, especially if these reforms had regionally heterogeneous timing or intensity correlated with HCSTC exposure. The paper needs to either show that these reforms are uncorrelated with the exposure measure or explicitly control for them (e.g., include LA-specific timing for Universal Credit rollout, benefit cap exposure, or tenant-fees implementation) to bolster the claim that the estimated effects are driven by the payday cap rather than confounders.

**Suggestions**

1. **Strengthen the measurement story for HCSTC exposure.** Consider merging in any pre-2015 indicators of payday lending activity at the regional or local level—such as FCA branch-level authorizations, commercial registers of HCSTC outlets, or even credit-bureau aggregates if accessible—to demonstrate that regions ranked similarly before the cap. Alternatively, use a bounding exercise: simulate how much re-ranking (e.g., correlation of 0.7 versus 0.9) would be necessary to reverse the sign of the estimate, and report the plausibility of such misclassification.

2. **Report uncertainty in a way that reflects the few-cluster structure.** Move the permutation p-value into the main panel or consider presenting randomization-based confidence intervals for the treatment effect. Another possibility is to implement the wild cluster bootstrap with region clusters or report the entire empirical distribution of the treatment effect from the permutation exercise. That would help readers see how close the estimate is to what one would expect under the null without relying on conventional SEs.

3. **Revisit the decision to focus on the private-minus-mortgage differential and connect it more directly to the theory.** The differential mitigates common shocks but also introduces its own interpretation challenges. For instance, a decline in the differential could arise from mortgage claims rising rather than landlord claims falling. Adding a more formal decomposition or presenting results from models that interact exposure with post-period for private and mortgage claims separately (with the appropriate caveats) would clarify through which margin the effect operates. Table 2 already presents separate regressions, but linking them back to the theoretical expectation in a structured way would improve interpretability.

4. **Address the pre-trend at $t-2$ more thoroughly.** The positive, statistically significant coefficient two years before the cap is a concern for the parallel-trends assumption. Consider re-estimating the model excluding the pre-cap period from which that coefficient arises (e.g., start in 2005) or adding pre-trends interacted with exposure to soak up any differential trajectories. You could also present the event study with confidence bands based on the permutation distribution to demonstrate how unusual that pre-trend is in the context of few clusters.

5. **Expand robustness to alternative outcomes and samples.** Since the original idea concerned rent arrears and housing distress broadly, it would be valuable to show that the null holds (or not) for other related measures—for instance, the log of private claims alone with controls for unemployment, or using mortgage claims as an outcome but weighting by regional renter share. Similarly, although the paper rightly stops in 2019 to avoid COVID bias, presenting a short-window version (2010–2019) with covariates accounting for macro shocks would reassure readers that the null result is not driven by the long pre-trend.

6. **Clarify the policy story in the discussion.** The concluding paragraphs would benefit from an explicit recognition that the results speak to a narrow slice of the consumption-smoothing argument—capturing only formal court filings, not arrears that never reach court or informal arrangements. Emphasize that the absence of a rise in possession claims does not necessarily mean that financially distressed households were unaffected; they may have faced hardship in less visible ways. Doing so would align the empirical findings with the careful interpretation already suggested in the text.

7. **Consider adding falsification exercises if data permit.** One option is to test whether HCSTC exposure predicts possession claims in periods before the cap (using the same specification but defining “post” to begin earlier). Another is to use other court outcomes that should, in theory, be unaffected by HCSTC reforms (e.g., civil claims unrelated to housing) to demonstrate no spurious correlation. While data limitations may constrain these exercises, any such checks would reinforce the credibility of the null.

Taken together, these suggestions aim to make the paper’s restrained conclusion more convincing by bolstering the identifying assumptions, clarifying the mechanism, and presenting uncertainty in a manner compatible with the data structure.
