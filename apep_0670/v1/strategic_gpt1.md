## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple and policy-relevant question: when federal agencies give the public more time to comment on proposed regulations, do more people actually participate? Using proposed rules and comment counts, it argues that longer comment windows substantially increase participation for the vast majority of routine rules, while making little difference for already-salient major regulations.

A busy economist should care because this is really a paper about whether procedural design shapes political participation in the administrative state. If true, the paper says that a seemingly technical rule — 30 days versus 60 days — changes who shows up in regulatory politics.

**Does the paper articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Not quite. The current opening is competent, but it is written like a narrow administrative-law paper filling an evidentiary gap. It does not quite elevate the question into a broader economics question about participation, salience, and state capacity. The phrase “first empirical test” is not enough to make the paper feel important.

**What the first two paragraphs should say instead:**  
> Much of economic policy is made not in Congress but through administrative rulemaking, where agencies propose rules and invite public comment before finalizing them. A basic but unanswered question is whether this participatory process is substantively shaped by procedure: does giving the public more time to respond actually broaden participation, or do only already-mobilized interests show up regardless?  
>  
> This paper shows that time matters. Using federal rulemakings matched to comment counts, I find that longer comment periods substantially increase participation for ordinary, low-salience rules, but have little effect on major high-profile regulations. The implication is that procedural design affects democratic input precisely where public attention is otherwise weakest — in the routine rulemakings that make up most of the regulatory state.

That is the pitch. It is about **procedural frictions in regulatory participation**, not merely about compliance with ACUS recommendations.

That is bigger, cleaner, and more AER-like.

   That is a much bigger idea than one procedural parameter in administrative law.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
The administrative state makes a huge amount of policy through rulemaking, and public comment is the formal channel for participation.

### Tension
We know comments can matter, and we know participation is unequal — but we do not know whether a core procedural parameter, the length of the comment window, actually changes participation, especially for the many routine rules that attract little attention.

### Resolution
Longer windows are associated with substantially more participation overall, and the effect is concentrated in routine, non-significant rules rather than major headline-grabbing ones.

### Implications
Procedure is not neutral: it shapes who participates in regulation. Reforming comment deadlines may meaningfully broaden participation where attention is scarce, even if it does little for major rules that already attract mobilized actors.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
It has the raw ingredients, but the arc is only **partially realized**. The paper currently feels like: institutional background, then regressions, then some heterogeneity. The most interesting story — that **time matters only when salience is low** — is buried as a heterogeneity result rather than elevated into the central tension/resolution.

So yes, there is a story here, but the paper is still somewhat a **collection of sensible results looking for a bigger narrative**.

**What story should it be telling?**  
Not “do longer periods get more comments?”  
But rather:  
**“In regulatory politics, participation is constrained not only by preferences and stakes but by procedural frictions. Those frictions matter for ordinary rules, not marquee ones.”**

That is a stronger arc, and it makes the significant/non-significant split the headline rather than an add-on.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?
I would lead with:  
**Doubling the comment window from 30 to 60 days is associated with roughly two-thirds more comments — but only for the boring, low-profile rules that make up most of the administrative state.**

That is a decent hook.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
Some would lean in — especially political economy, public, and law-and-econ people — but many macro/applied micro economists might still reach for their phones unless the paper makes clearer why comment counts matter for actual policy. The current version is interesting, but not yet irresistible.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Almost immediately:  
**“Are these extra comments meaningful, or just more form letters?”**  
And then:  
**“Does this change who comments, or only how many comments arrive?”**

That is the pressure point. The paper anticipates this a bit, but not enough. For a top-journal audience, the answer cannot just be “comment volume is a standard participation proxy.”

### If findings are modest, is the result itself interesting?
The findings are not null, but they are still vulnerable to seeming modest because the outcome is count of comments. The paper does make a plausible case that this matters, but it needs to make a **sharper normative and economic case** for why participation at the comment stage is an important object in itself.

Otherwise, readers will say: “Yes, giving people more time yields more submissions. What else is new?”
