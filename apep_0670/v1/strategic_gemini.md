## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
The paper examines whether the length of the public comment period for federal regulations—a foundational element of administrative law—actually determines the volume of public participation. Using five years of Federal Register data, it finds that while high-profile "significant" rules attract engagement regardless of the window, "routine" rules (which constitute 86% of the regulatory state) are highly sensitive to time, with a 10-day extension increasing participation by 18%. This suggests the statutory 30-day floor is a binding constraint on democratic participation for the vast majority of the administrative state.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this reasonably well, but the first two paragraphs lean a bit heavily on the "ACUS recommendation" and legal proceduralism.
**The Pitch the Paper Should Have:** "In the American regulatory state, public participation is mediated by a 'notice and comment' window that is often as short as 30 days. This paper provides the first large-scale empirical evidence that this temporal constraint serves as a bottleneck for democratic input. While 'headline-grabbing' regulations are immune to these constraints, the vast majority of 'workhorse' federal rules see participation increase by 65% when the window is extended from 30 to 60 days, suggesting that administrative 'speed' comes at a direct cost to public voice."

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**Contribution:** The paper establishes the first empirical dose-response relationship between regulatory comment period length and the quantity of public participation, identifying it as a binding constraint for non-significant rules.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It differentiates well from the qualitative legal literature and the "influence" literature (Yackee), which assumes participation but doesn't study its temporal determinants.
*   **Question vs. Literature:** It frames itself as answering a question about the WORLD (Does participation depend on time?), which is a strength.
*   **Smart Economist Test:** A reader would explain this as "the paper that shows the 30-day rule suppresses comments on boring-but-important regulations."
*   **Bigger Contribution:** To make this "AER-big," it needs to show **who** is being constrained. Is it individual citizens vs. corporations? If the 30-day window specifically keeps out the "public" while "organized interests" are already ready to fire, the democratic story becomes much more explosive.

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
The paper sits at the intersection of Political Economy (Regulatory Governance) and Law & Economics.

*   **Closest Neighbors:** Yackee (2006) on interest group influence; Libgober (2020) on informational lobbying; Balla et al. (2022) on mass comment campaigns.
*   **Positioning:** It builds on these by looking at the "input" side of the equation. It should position itself more aggressively as a study of *barriers to entry* in the regulatory marketplace.
*   **Missing Literature:** It misses the broader "Cost of Participation" literature in political science and the "Rational Ignorance/Attention" literature in Econ. It should speak to the **Industrial Organization of Lobbying**—how time constraints favor incumbents with "shelf-ready" legal teams over reactive public interests.

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC
*   **Setup:** Agencies are required by the 1946 APA to listen to the public, usually for 30 days.
*   **Tension:** We don't know if 30 days is "enough." If it isn't, the procedural "floor" is actually a ceiling on participation.
*   **Resolution:** For 86% of rules, it is a binding constraint. For the top 14%, it doesn't matter.
*   **Implications:** Changing a simple procedural number (30 to 60) could fundamentally change the representative nature of the administrative state.

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong. It’s not just a collection of results; it’s a focused investigation into a specific institutional parameter.

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
**Dinner Party Fact:** "Extending a comment period from 30 to 60 days increases public participation by 65% for the average federal rule."
**Reaction:** People lean in. They will ask: "Who are these people commenting? Is it just more spam, or is it actual useful information?"
**The Null Result:** The null result for "significant" rules is actually the most interesting part—it suggests that for the rules we *think* are important, the procedure is irrelevant. The procedure only matters where the light is dim.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The gap between this and the AER is **mechanisms and "Who."** Currently, it counts "comments." But are these 1,000 extra comments from a single NGO's email list, or 10 technical experts who finally had time to read the 500-page rule?

**Single Biggest Improvement:** Decompose the comment counts by *type of commenter*. If you can show that longer periods specifically enable "diffuse interests" (the public) to catch up to "concentrated interests" (industry), this becomes a major paper on the political economy of regulation.
