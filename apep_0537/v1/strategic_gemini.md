## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper investigates whether the adoption of Artificial Intelligence is reshaping the seniority structure of the U.S. labor market by substituting for entry-level "training" tasks while complementing senior-level judgment. Using public BLS and O*NET data, it documents a massive 4.5 percentage point shift away from entry-level employment since 2015, concentrated in AI-exposed industries—though it crucially finds that this trend significantly predates the release of ChatGPT.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this pitch quite well in the first three paragraphs, particularly in the third, which frames the "seniority-biased technological change" against the canonical SBTC literature. However, the first two paragraphs are a bit too "newsy" about ChatGPT adoption.
**The pitch it should have:** "While economists have long studied how technology shifts demand between high and low-skilled workers, this paper explores a different margin: seniority. I document a large, decade-long reallocation of U.S. employment from entry-level to senior roles and show that this 'seniority-biased' shift is concentrated in industries and occupations most exposed to AI, starting well before the generative AI era."

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution:** The paper provides the first economy-wide evidence using public administrative data that AI exposure correlates with a hollowing out of entry-level positions relative to senior roles, while identifying that this is a long-term structural trend rather than a discrete shock from Large Language Models.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It is clearly differentiated from Hosseini Maasoum and Lichtinger (2025) by using public rather than proprietary data and by testing (and failing) the "GenAI shock" timing.
*   **Question vs. Literature:** It frames itself as answering a question about the world (Are career ladders disappearing?), which is a strong AER-style hook.
*   **Clarity:** A smart economist would say: "It's an 'honesty' paper. It finds the correlation people expected, but proves the timing doesn't fit the current ChatGPT hype."
*   **Bigger Contribution:** To make this bigger, the paper needs to move beyond the "failed" GenAI timing and more deeply explore the *consequences* of the 4.5pp shift. If entry-level roles are disappearing, where are those young workers going? Are they underemployed, or are they entering as "seniors" earlier?

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

*   **Neighbors:** Acemoglu et al. (2022) on AI vacancies; Autor, Levy, and Murnane (2003) on RBTC; Hosseini Maasoum and Lichtinger (2025) on seniority-bias; Deming (2017) on social skills.
*   **Positioning:** The paper should position itself as the **empirical reality check** for the "Seniority-Biased Technological Change" hypothesis. It builds on the theory but complicates the narrative by showing the trend is a slow-burn automation story, not a "Big Bang" 2022 event.
*   **Breadth:** It is positioned well—broad enough to interest labor and macro economists.
*   **Right Conversation?** Yes. It connects the "Task-Based Approach" (Autor/Acemoglu) to the "Experience/Seniority" margin.

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

*   **Setup:** Technology usually hurts the "unskilled" or "routine" workers.
*   **Tension:** Generative AI seems to help the "unskilled" (novices) most in experiments, yet firms seem to be hiring *fewer* of them.
*   **Resolution:** The paper confirms that high-AI industries are indeed shedding entry-level shares, but the event study shows this didn't start with ChatGPT—it’s a pre-existing condition of the AI/automation era.
*   **Implications:** We are witnessing the "death of the apprenticeship," but we can't blame it all on the latest shiny LLM.

**Evaluate:** The narrative arc is very strong because it has a "twist." The reader expects a ChatGPT paper, but receives a decade-long structural change paper.

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

*   **Lead Fact:** "The U.S. economy lost 7 million entry-level positions relative to trend in the last decade, and it's happening exactly where AI is most prevalent."
*   **Reaction:** Lean in. The "death of the entry-level job" is a high-anxiety, high-interest topic for anyone with children or students.
*   **Follow-up:** "If we aren't hiring juniors to do the 'grunt work' because of AI, how will the next generation ever learn enough to become seniors?"

---

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

*   **Front-loading:** The paper is well-structured. The "honest" event study (Figure 2) is the heart of the paper and is placed correctly to modulate the DiD results.
*   **Appendix:** The SEC EDGAR search (Section 3.5) is a bit of a distraction in the main text; it confirms what we already know (GenAI is new) and could be moved to the appendix to keep the focus on the labor data.
*   **Mechanisms:** Section 7.4 (Alternative Mechanisms) is crucial. It needs more data. Can the author use CPS data to control for "Demographic Aging" or "Credentialization" more formally?

---

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The gap between this and a "Revise and Resubmit" at the AER is **mechanistic depth**. Currently, the paper says "There is a trend, it correlates with AI, but it's not a ChatGPT shock." 

To be AER-quality, the paper needs to answer: **If it's not GenAI, what is it?** Is it the "Machine Learning" wave of 2012-2018? Is it a change in how we *define* entry-level? 

**Single most impactful advice:** Turn the "failed" 2022 identification into a "success" for a 2015-2024 structural story. Use the AIOE index to look at earlier "shocks" (e.g., the 2017 transformer paper, the rise of cloud computing) to see if you can find the *start* of this trend. If you can show when the "Seniority-Bias" began, you've discovered a new law of modern automation.
