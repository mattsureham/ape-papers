**Re:** Strategic Positioning of "The Conscription Tax: Wartime Military Service and Academic Achievement in Colombia"

---

### 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether the threat of being drafted into an active civil war discourages young men from investing in their education long before they ever put on a uniform. Using a triple-difference design centered on the 2016 FARC peace agreement in Colombia, the author finds that young men in high-conflict areas score significantly lower on high school and university exit exams, with the effect appearing even before the age of enlistment. This suggests that "conscription" isn't just a tax on a soldier's time—it’s a shadow cast over their entire adolescence that erodes human capital.

**Evaluation:** The paper does a decent job in the first two paragraphs, but it leans a bit too heavily on the "Colombia civil war" narrative rather than the "economic mechanism."
**The revised pitch should be:** 
> "Mandatory military service is often modeled as a one-to-two-year tax on labor market experience. However, when conscription involves active combat, the 'tax' may begin much earlier through anticipatory disinvestment in human capital. By exploiting the sudden reduction in combat risk following the 2016 Colombian peace accord, this paper identifies a significant penalty in standardized test scores for draft-eligible males that manifests years before actual service."

---

### 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution:** The paper identifies an "anticipatory" human capital penalty of wartime conscription that occurs before actual service, distinguishing it from the direct disruption of the draft itself.

*   **Differentiation:** It moves beyond Angrist (1990) and Galiani (2011) by focusing on *wartime* (vs peacetime) and *pre-service* (vs post-service) outcomes.
*   **World vs. Literature:** It is currently framed as answering a question about the **world** (the hidden costs of war), which is a strength for the AER.
*   **"Another DiD paper?":** A smart economist would see this as more than "just another DiD." The finding that the effect exists *prior* to the draft age is the "hook" that prevents it from being a standard labor paper.
*   **Bigger Contribution:** To make this truly "AER big," the author needs to bridge the gap between the test score drop and lifetime earnings. A back-of-the-envelope calibration—linking these test score deltas to the returns to education literature—would turn this from a "schooling paper" into a "macro-relevant growth/development paper."

---

### 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

*   **Closest Neighbors:** Angrist (1990) [Conscription/Earnings], Galiani et al. (2011) [Conscription/Crime/Earnings], Blattman & Annan (2010) [Child Soldiering], and Prem et al. (2022) [Conflict/Peace in Colombia].
*   **Strategy:** The paper should **synthesize** the labor literature on conscription with the development literature on conflict.
*   **Missing Links:** It should speak more to the **Human Capital Investment under Uncertainty** literature (e.g., Levhari & Weiss, 1974). If the "risk" of death or service increases, the option value of education changes.
*   **Right Conversation?** Yes, but it could be broader. By framing this as "Institutional Barriers to Investment," it speaks to a wider audience interested in why developing countries stay poor, beyond just those interested in Colombia.

---

### 4. NARRATIVE ARC

*   **Setup:** Conscription is an established economic cost.
*   **Tension:** Most studies look at the "time away" from work. But what if the mere *threat* of war makes school feel pointless before you even go?
*   **Resolution:** Test scores drop for males in high-risk zones, even at age 16 (pre-draft).
*   **Implications:** Peace deals have a "human capital dividend" that starts working immediately on the next generation of students.

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong. The "tension" (results appearing at age 16) is the most compelling part of the paper.

---

### 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

*   **Lead Fact:** "In Colombia, the threat of being drafted into the war zone caused high schoolers' math scores to drop by a third of the urban-rural gap—before they were even old enough to be drafted."
*   **Reaction:** Economists will lean in. The "anticipatory" effect is the counter-intuitive result that makes for good dinner conversation.
*   **Follow-up:** "Does this mean the kids who *don't* get drafted still suffer the 'tax'?" (Answer: Yes, and that's the point).

---

### 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

*   **Front-loading:** The *libreta militar* discussion (Page 5) is crucial. It should be moved up or emphasized even more in the intro. It's the "smoking gun" for why the effect is anticipatory—you can't even get into university without the booklet.
*   **Appendix:** The English score results (Table 3) are less compelling and distract from the Math results. Move them to an appendix.
*   **Selection:** The Saber Pro results (university level) are heavily selected. The author acknowledges this, but more work is needed to show that the -2.40 point drop isn't just because the "best" students fled the country.

---

### 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The gap is **Ambition**. Currently, it is a very competent, well-executed empirical exercise. To be an AER paper, it needs to move from "This happened in Colombia" to "This is a fundamental feature of how human capital responds to state-mandated risk."

**Single Most Impactful Advice:** Quantify the "Option Value of Education." Use the test score drop to estimate the implied increase in the "discount rate" or "risk premium" young men are applying to their future. If you can show that the draft acts like a 15% interest rate hike on educational loans, you’ve written a classic.
