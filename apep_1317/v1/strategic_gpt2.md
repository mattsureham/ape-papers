## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether the threat of being drafted into a live civil conflict depresses young men's educational investment before they ever serve. Using Colombia's 2016 peace agreement, regional variation in conflict intensity, and the fact that only men were draft-eligible, the paper argues that wartime conscription operated like a hidden tax on human capital, lowering male test scores in conflict-heavy places even on exams taken before draft age.

A busy economist should care because the paper is trying to move the conscription literature from “service lowers later earnings” to a broader claim: coercive state institutions can distort schooling decisions ex ante, not just labor market outcomes ex post.

**Does the paper articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Not quite. The current opening is competent but too literature-first and too diffuse. It starts with Colombia’s war, then quickly pivots into the Angrist draft literature, but the real hook is not “another conscription paper.” The hook is: **the prospect of compulsory wartime service may lower educational investment before induction, and we can see that in standardized test scores.** That is the startling fact. The introduction should lead with that.

### The pitch the paper should have

“Can the threat of military conscription reduce human capital accumulation before anyone is actually drafted? This paper studies Colombia’s long civil war and shows that young men in high-conflict areas performed worse academically when draft eligibility meant a meaningful risk of wartime service, with the gap shrinking after the 2016 peace agreement. The central message is that conscription in wartime acts not only as a tax on labor market experience, but also as an ex ante tax on schooling through expectations, uncertainty, and institutional barriers.”

Then paragraph two should say:

“This matters beyond Colombia. Most of the economics literature measures the effects of military service after induction, typically in peacetime or through draft-lottery designs focused on earnings. But if the threat of service changes educational investment before labor market entry, then standard estimates understate the social cost of conscription. Colombia provides a setting where that anticipatory channel should be especially strong: only men face the draft, conflict intensity varies sharply across regions, and the 2016 peace accord changed the salience of draft-related combat risk.”

That is much cleaner, more world-question-driven, and more memorable.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Before this paper, economists largely think of conscription as imposing costs through actual service: forgone experience, later earnings losses, disrupted careers. Conflict-and-education papers show war hurts schooling, but generally through violence, displacement, and school disruption.

### Tension
If wartime conscription is salient and dangerous, then the costs may begin before service itself, through expectations, uncertainty, and institutional bottlenecks. But this channel is hard to see because draft exposure is tangled up with general conflict.

### Resolution
The paper presents evidence that male students in more conflict-intense Colombian departments performed worse in the conflict era relative to after the peace deal, and that the pattern appears even on exams taken before draft age. That timing is the key result.

### Implications
The social cost of conscription may be understated if we only measure post-service labor market effects. Wartime state coercion can distort educational investment upstream in the human-capital pipeline.

### Does the paper have a clear arc?
**Serviceable, but not yet sharp.**  
There is a story here, but the draft is trying to tell two stories at once:

1. Wartime conscription lowers human capital.
2. Colombian conflict/peace changed educational outcomes for men.

The second can easily swallow the first. The paper needs to organize itself around a single narrative:

> “The threat of risky compulsory service causes anticipatory educational losses, and Colombia’s peace agreement reveals that channel.”

Right now several sections read like a collection of plausible interpretations attached to a DDD estimate. The strongest narrative is not “we estimate a wartime conscription penalty.” It is “we observe a pre-service educational response to coercive draft risk.”

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Almost certainly:
- “Why is this conscription rather than just boys in violent places facing different incentives?”
or
- “What makes the peace deal variation a draft-risk shock rather than a bundle of region-specific changes?”

Again, that is not a referee-style identification objection here; it is a positioning issue. The paper’s current title and framing promise more conscription-specificity than the narrative can comfortably support.

### If findings are modest, is the modesty itself interesting?
Yes, but only if the paper leans into the mechanism and timing. A 0.04 SD high-school effect is not by itself dinner-party material. What makes it interesting is:
- it appears before service;
- it is male-specific;
- it speaks to expectations and the human-capital pipeline.

If the paper does not foreground that, it will feel like a modest regional education result.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The biggest gap is **framing plus ambition**.

This is not obviously an AER paper in current form because the paper is caught between a narrow setting and a broad claim. The current title and rhetoric imply a clean estimate of the effect of conscription, but the body of the paper more carefully describes a reduced-form “draft environment” effect. That mismatch weakens the contribution.

### What is the main problem?
Mostly **a framing problem**, with some **scope problem**.

- **Framing problem:** The paper’s true comparative advantage is the anticipatory human-capital story, not simply “conscription lowers scores.”
- **Scope problem:** Test scores alone may feel too narrow unless the paper makes a compelling case that they reveal an upstream margin the literature has missed.

### Is it a novelty problem?
Not fatally, but somewhat. Conscription, conflict, and education are all established literatures. What is novel here is their intersection through anticipation. If that is not made central, the paper will feel derivative.

### Is it an ambition problem?
Yes, a bit. The paper is competent and careful, but it is still playing small-ball with a DDD estimate and administrative test scores. To be an AER-level story, it needs to claim and demonstrate something conceptually larger: that coercive policy risk changes forward-looking human-capital investment.

### The single most impactful advice
**Rewrite the paper around one headline claim: the threat of wartime conscription depresses education before service begins, and Colombia provides evidence on that anticipatory channel.**

Everything else should support that. If the paper cannot own that channel cleanly, then it should scale back the “conscription tax” branding and present itself more modestly as evidence on male-specific educational costs of the wartime draft environment after all. But it cannot have it both ways.
