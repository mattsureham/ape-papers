**Idea Fidelity**

The paper departs substantially from the manifest. The original proposal aimed to exploit the sharp IMG compensation boundary at the four-digit postcode (“PC4”) level and the postcode-specific eligibility rule to estimate capitalization using a spatial regression discontinuity design with dense transaction-level data. The submitted paper instead adopts a neighborhood-level difference‑in‑differences approach, classifying areas as “high exposure” based on cumulative peak ground acceleration (PGA) and comparing WOZ assessments before and after the 2020 announcement. This misses three critical manifestations of the original design: the discrete IMG postcode boundary, the availability of exact compensation eligibility (including retroactivity and compensation percentages), and the focus on transaction prices within a spatial RDD framework. As a result, the empirical strategy no longer isolates the policy discontinuity and the data no longer directly reflect the compensation mechanism. Consequently, the paper does not faithfully pursue the original idea as outlined in the manifest.

**Summary**

The paper studies whether the September 2020 IMG compensation announcement for Groningen earthquake losses capitalized into property values. Using annual neighborhood-level WOZ assessments and a binary indicator for “high exposure” to past seismicity, it estimates a difference-in-differences specification and reports a null effect of compensation on assessed values post-announcement. Event studies, a placebo year, province-restricted samples, and dose-response tests all fail to reveal any statistically significant impact, leading the author to conclude that the retroactive, sale-contingent compensation did not alter neighborhood property values.

**Essential Points**

1. **Identification Strategy Mis-specification:** The central claim is that compensation eligibility creates a discontinuity at the IMG postcode boundary, yet the empirical implementation does not exploit this boundary. Instead, treatment is defined as “above-median cumulative PGA,” which bears only an indirect relationship to actual compensation eligibility. Without explicitly incorporating the postcode-level eligibility rule, the estimator cannot capture the sharp policy-induced discontinuity and risks conflating exposure with eligibility. This undermines the causal interpretation of the null result.

2. **Outcome Measure Mismatch with Policy Mechanism:** The policy compensates sellers upon transaction, so capitalization should manifest in transaction prices rather than annual WOZ assessments, which are smoothing mechanisms updated once per year and calibrated to past sales. WOZ values may therefore fail to capture the immediate effect of an announcement tied to prospective sales. This mismatch raises concerns that the null could reflect measurement error or timing lags rather than an absence of capitalization.

3. **Lack of Direct Evidence on Compensation Eligibility:** The paper does not use the precise set of 150 eligible postcodes or the 20% claim threshold that defined inclusion in the Waardedalingsregeling. Without leveraging this administrative rule (and the associated compensation percentages), it is unclear whether the treatment group actually gained eligibility. The use of a median split on estimated PGA introduces potential misclassification that could attenuate any true effect. The paper needs to show that its high-exposure classification closely maps to the IMG eligibility map or, better yet, estimate treatment effects using the actual eligible boundary.

Given these issues, I cannot recommend publication without major revisions addressing the identification and measurement concerns.

**Suggestions**

1. **Exploit the Spatial Boundary and Administrative Eligibility Directly:** Return to the original idea: exploit the sharp IMG boundary at the PC4 level and the explicit 20% claim-rate rule. The dataset described in the manifest (PC4 shapefiles, BAG coordinates, Kadaster/NVM transaction prices) is well suited for this. A spatial regression discontinuity design or boundary fixed effects estimator comparing transactions just inside versus just outside the eligible postcode boundary would align closely with the research question and provide a credible source of exogenous variation. Include mapping of the actual boundary and demonstrate that the boundary is sharp (i.e., that households cannot easily choose their postcode), which would strengthen identification.

2. **Use Transaction-Level Prices to Match the Policy Mechanism:** Since the compensation is triggered by a sale, the natural outcome is the transaction price (or log price) of houses sold after January 25, 2013. The manifest indicates data availability from NVM/Kadaster and BAG building coordinates; these should be employed to construct a transaction-level panel with precise location, sale date, sale price, and property characteristics. At minimum, supplement the WOZ results with transaction-based evidence; ideally, base the main specification on transactions to capture the immediate capitalization effect tied to selling.

3. **Leverage Policy Heterogeneity and Timing:** The Waardedalingsregeling pays different percentages (2.07–12.22%) depending on the postcode, and it is retroactive to sales after 2013 but announced in 2020. These features allow for clean placebo tests (e.g., comparing sales just before vs. after the announcement within eligible areas) and heterogeneity tests (correlating capitalization with the promised compensation percentage). If the null holds even when comparing pairs of postcodes that are both eligible but receive different compensation rates, that would support the argument. Additionally, using the retroactivity can allow for falsification: capitalized effects should be visible only after the 2020 announcement; sales before 2020 within eligible zones should not exhibit a premium. The current approach conflates exposure and eligibility and ignores these rich policy dimensions.

4. **Address Sorting and Composition More Directly:** While the paper argues sorting is unlikely because the announcement occurred months before the 2021 WOZ reference date, transaction-level data would permit an explicit test for differential mobility or sales volume changes around the boundary. Even in the current setup, the paper could show that neighborhood characteristics (population, owner-occupancy, socioeconomic controls) do not change discontinuously at the boundary or over time. This would bolster the parallel trends assumption.

5. **Clarify Standard Errors and Power Calculations:** With neighborhood-level data and only seven years of post-announcement data, the study may face limited statistical power, especially with clustered standard errors at the buurt level. A formal discussion of statistical power (perhaps using anticipated effect sizes based on compensation percentages) would help readers understand whether the null is due to lack of effect or lack of precision. Engage with the possibility that measurement error (using WOZ instead of transactions, classification via PGA, etc.) could attenuate estimates further; an explicit sensitivity analysis would be useful.

6. **Explain How PGA-Based Exposure Maps to Compensation Eligibility:** If you retain the exposure-based definition, show quantitatively how it correlates with the actual IMG eligibility map. Provide figures illustrating the overlap between high-PGA areas and eligible postcodes. If misalignment exists, consider using a fuzzy RDD or instrumental variables approach where exposure predicts eligibility, and show first-stage strength. Without this, the treatment becomes an indirect proxy and the causal claim is weakened.

7. **Enrich the Interpretation of the Null:** The discussion offers three possible explanations for the null. To strengthen this section, consider checking whether the compensation was indeed sale-contingent in practice (did buyers receive anything, or only sellers?). If possible, estimate whether transaction volumes declined or whether sellers in eligible areas received the transfer as expected (e.g., by looking at municipal payout records). Additionally, compare to other contexts where temporary transfers did or didn’t capitalize to demonstrate external validity. A more thorough normative discussion could also consider whether the lack of capitalization affects the incentive structure for future compensation programs.

8. **Consider Alternative Outcomes or Mechanisms:** If WOZ values are too coarse, perhaps other outcomes (e.g., listing prices, sale-to-list price ratios, time-on-market) could be analyzed to see if buyers respond to the compensation announcement even if assessed values do not change. This broader empirical scope could help determine whether the null effect reflects genuinely absent capitalization or simply a measurement limitation.

9. **Be Transparent About Data Limitations:** The appendix mentions missing 2022 data due to format changes. Explain how you handle this missing year (do you impute, drop, or use interpolation?). Similarly, clarify whether the KNMI earthquake catalog includes events post-2020 and how that affects exposure measures. Explicitly address any potential selection issues introduced by excluding certain neighborhoods (e.g., those with fewer than 20 dwellings) and whether the results are sensitive to these choices.

10. **Align the Abstract and Contribution with the Revised Strategy:** If the paper continues with the neighborhood-level DID design, revise the abstract and introduction to be upfront about the departure from a sharp boundary design and make clear that the paper evaluates whether high- vs. low-exposure neighborhoods, as defined by past seismicity, responded differently to the 2020 announcement. If you move to the original spatial RDD strategy, the contributions should highlight how it isolates the policy boundary and how it incorporates compensation percentages.

Addressing these suggestions would bring the empirical strategy closer to the original policy question and improve the credibility of the null finding.
