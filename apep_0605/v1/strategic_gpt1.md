## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple, important question: when a local economy gets a large natural-resource windfall, does it enjoy lasting gains or suffer the classic boom-bust “resource curse”? Using the US fracking revolution across counties from 2001–2023, the paper argues that shale counties saw large employment and earnings gains that persisted through two major downturns, suggesting that in a high-capacity institutional environment, resource booms can generate durable local prosperity rather than temporary spikes.

Why should a busy economist care? Because the paper is trying to convert a diffuse, often cross-country idea—“resources are a curse”—into a concrete, testable dynamic prediction about local labor markets: do gains reverse when the boom ends? That is a real economic question about how local economies adjust to large shocks, not merely a sector-specific fracking paper.

**Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Not quite. The current opening is competent, but it starts with the resource-curse literature in an overly general way and only gradually gets to the sharp empirical question. The strongest version of the paper is not “we study fracking” and not even “we estimate employment effects.” It is: **the resource curse makes a dynamic prediction about persistence, and fracking gives an unusually clean setting to test it.** That should be front and center immediately.

### The pitch the paper should have

A better first two paragraphs would say something like:

> Natural-resource booms are widely thought to leave places worse off in the long run, whether through Dutch disease, volatility, or institutional distortions. But that broad claim has a sharper empirical implication: if the curse is real at the local level, then employment and income gains during the boom should dissipate—or reverse—once prices collapse.  
>   
> The US fracking revolution provides an unusually revealing test. Fracking generated a large, geographically concentrated resource boom across US counties, and the subsequent oil-price collapses of 2015–16 and 2020 create a natural opportunity to ask whether boom-era local gains endured. We show that shale counties experienced large increases in employment and earnings, and that these gains persisted through the busts rather than unwinding. The core fact is not simply that fracking raised local employment; it is that the gains survived the downturn.

That is the AER-facing pitch: a first-order question, a setting that credibly speaks to it, and a finding that changes how we think about resource booms in advanced economies.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:**  
The paper’s contribution is to recast the “resource curse” as a testable prediction about boom-bust asymmetry in local labor markets and to show, using US fracking counties, that employment gains persisted through major busts rather than reversing.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?
Only partially. The introduction names several obvious neighbors, but the differentiation is still too incremental-sounding:

- relative to **Weber (2012)** / **Maniloff and Mastromonaco (2017)**: “we extend the sample through the bust”
- relative to **Feyrer, Mansur, and Sacerdote (2017)**: “we confirm positive local effects”
- relative to **Allcott and Keniston (2018)**: “we look at total employment rather than Dutch disease composition”
- relative to **Black, McKinnish, and Sanders (2005)** / **Jacobsen and Parker**: “we examine persistence”

That is all true, but it reads like extension rather than a conceptual advance. The paper needs to insist that what is new is **not just more years of data**, but a **sharper organizing question**: persistence versus reversal after the shock turns.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or filling a gap in a LITERATURE?
It is mixed, but still too literature-gap coded. The strongest world question is:

- When a place gets a resource windfall in a modern, integrated economy, are the gains temporary or durable?

That is stronger than:

- The fracking literature has not yet followed outcomes through the full bust cycle.

The latter is publishable in a field journal; the former is what gives it broader ambition.

### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what’s new?
At present, they might still say: “It’s another fracking DiD paper, but with longer-run data showing gains persist.” That is not enough.

The introduction should make them say instead:  
**“This paper argues that the decisive test of the local resource curse is whether boom gains unwind in the bust, and in US fracking they don’t.”**

That is memorable.

### What would make this contribution bigger?
Several possibilities:

1. **Broaden the outcome beyond employment levels to local welfare or adjustment margins.**  
   Right now the paper’s main fact is employment persistence. That is useful, but for AER stature, one wants either broader welfare accounting or more compelling structural implications. If rents rise but housing costs, congestion, or mortality also rise, “no resource curse” becomes too strong a headline. The paper itself acknowledges these omitted margins; strategically, that acknowledgement weakens the title claim.

2. **Show sectoral reallocation more sharply.**  
   If the paper wants to rebut Dutch disease or transitory boom logic, it should show what happens to the sectors that theory says ought to be hurt, not just total and non-mining employment. Manufacturing, tradables, construction, local services, and public-sector outcomes would tell a bigger story.

3. **Make the mechanism of persistence central.**  
   The current mechanism section is generic multiplier language. The real question is: why didn’t employment gains unwind? Population retention? Business formation? Infrastructure? Wage ratcheting? Human capital? Industry diversification? Without a sharper mechanism, “persistent gains” risks sounding like a descriptive update rather than a deeper contribution.

4. **Reframe the paper as about shock persistence in local economies, with resource booms as the setting.**  
   That would connect it to the much larger conversation on local labor-market adjustment, hysteresis, and place-based persistence. That framing is bigger than resource economics alone.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

### Closest neighbors
The closest papers appear to be:

- **Black, McKinnish, and Sanders (2005)** on the Appalachian coal boom-bust
- **Marchand (2012)** on local labor-market impacts of the Canadian oil and gas boom
- **Michaels (2011)** on long-run effects of oil abundance in the US South
- **Feyrer, Mansur, and Sacerdote (2017)** on geographic spillovers from shale
- **Allcott and Keniston (2018)** on Dutch disease and local labor markets
- likely also **Weber (2012)** and **Maniloff and Mastromonaco (2017)** on early fracking employment effects
- possibly **Jacobsen and Parker / Jacobsen et al.** on energy booms and persistence

### How should the paper position itself relative to those neighbors?
Mostly **build on and synthesize**, with one clear contrast.

- Build on the fracking papers by saying: they established short-run gains; we ask whether those gains survive the bust.
- Build on resource-boom persistence papers by saying: prior work often studies one episode or one region; we use the national fracking cycle to test persistence across multiple cohorts and busts.
- Contrast directly with **Black et al. (2005)**, because that gives the paper real tension: coal looked like boom-bust reversal; fracking does not.

The paper should not “attack” prior papers. It should instead say prior evidence is mixed because resource booms differ across institutional contexts, technologies, and adjustment environments. This paper helps sort out **when** the curse appears rather than claiming the literature is wrong wholesale.

### Is the paper currently positioned too narrowly or too broadly?
Oddly, both.

- **Too narrowly** because much of the paper reads like a standard applied-energy paper about fracking counties.
- **Too broadly** because the title and framing claim “No Resource Curse,” which sounds like a sweeping rejection of a vast literature based on one country, one period, and one outcome family.

That mismatch is dangerous. The best positioning is more precise:  
**“A test of boom-bust persistence in local resource economies: evidence from US fracking.”**

That is broad enough to matter and narrow enough to be credible.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of, or under-engaged with?
At least four broader conversations are underused:

1. **Local labor markets / place-based persistence / hysteresis**  
   This is probably the most important missing conversation. The finding is fundamentally about whether a large positive shock creates persistent local effects. That belongs alongside work on local multipliers, spatial equilibrium, adjustment frictions, and persistent place effects.

2. **Agglomeration and dynamic adjustment**  
   If the argument is that temporary shocks can trigger durable local expansion, then agglomeration or adjustment-cost frameworks are relevant.

3. **Resource dependence versus resource abundance**  
   The “resource curse” literature often distinguishes institutions, rents, volatility, and political economy. This paper collapses all of that into a local labor-market persistence test. That is okay if explicit, but it needs to say it is testing one **specific margin** of the curse, not the whole concept.

4. **Welfare and externalities literature on fracking**  
   The paper cites Bartik et al., but only in passing. Since the title makes a sweeping claim, it needs to be more careful in relation to papers showing substantial environmental and health costs. Otherwise readers will feel bait-and-switched: “no curse” based on employment only.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?
Not fully. The highest-return reframing would be to connect resource booms to the general economics of **persistent local shocks**. That is the unexpected literature that could make the paper more interesting. Right now it is mostly speaking to energy and resource economists. For AER, it needs to speak to labor, urban, public, development, and macro people interested in regional adjustment.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Before this paper, the broad belief is that resource booms are dangerous: they may temporarily enrich places, but often at the cost of volatility, crowd-out, and eventual decline. In the fracking context, previous work largely established sizable short-run gains.

### Tension
The unresolved question is whether those gains were temporary. Fracking delivered a major boom, but then two serious busts arrived. If the local resource curse is real in advanced-economy local labor markets, this is when it should show up.

### Resolution
The paper finds that shale counties retained their employment and earnings gains through the busts; gains did not unwind.

### Implications
The implication is not “the resource curse is false.” The implication is narrower and more credible: **resource booms need not be self-reversing in places with strong institutions, mobility, and broad local linkages.** That matters for how economists think about local adjustment, the persistence of shocks, and the conditions under which natural resources become an asset rather than a liability.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
It has the ingredients, but the execution is uneven. Too often it feels like a set of standard empirical sections—main result, event study, mechanisms, robustness—wrapped in a grand title. The paper’s strongest story is dynamic and comparative: **boom, then bust, then what remained?**

That should govern the structure more tightly. Right now the paper sometimes slips back into “fracking raises employment,” which is not the interesting part. The interesting part is **what happened after prices collapsed**.

A stronger story would be:

1. The resource curse makes a dynamic prediction.
2. Fracking plus two busts gives an ideal test.
3. The core fact: gains persist.
4. The persistence is broad-based, not just in mining.
5. Therefore, local resource booms in high-capacity environments can create lasting level shifts.

That is a coherent paper. The current version is close, but it has not fully committed to that storyline.

Those would matter more than leave-one-out tables.

Moderately, but not enough. The good stuff is there by page 2; still, it is diluted by too much framing around estimators and too many claims packed together.

If they could change only one thing, that is it. The current paper is trying to win by negating a huge literature with a narrow set of outcomes. That claim is both more credible and more interesting.
