## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple, important question: when a major local resource boom arrives, do communities enjoy durable gains or suffer the classic boom-bust pattern associated with the “resource curse”? Using the US fracking revolution, the paper argues that shale counties saw large employment and earnings gains that persisted through subsequent energy busts, suggesting that in a high-income, institutionally strong setting, resource booms may generate lasting local benefits rather than temporary spikes.

Why should a busy economist care? Because this is not really a paper about fracking per se; it is a paper about whether one of economics’ most influential narratives about natural resources survives contact with modern subnational evidence in the United States. If true, the result matters for regional economics, labor markets, natural resources, and how economists think about when “resource curse” logic travels across contexts.

**Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Partly, but not well enough. The current opening starts with the grand “resource curse” literature, then pivots to fracking, but it does not crisply define the empirical and conceptual question in a way that immediately signals why this is bigger than another local fracking paper. The introduction also gets pulled too quickly into method and geological exogeneity. That is not what an editor or broad AER reader most needs in paragraph 1.

**What the first two paragraphs should say instead:**

> Economists have long argued that natural resource booms may leave places worse off in the long run: they can crowd out other industries, create fragile local expansions, and unravel when prices fall. Yet much of what we know comes either from cross-country comparisons or from settings where weak institutions and limited labor-market adjustment are part of the story. A basic question remains unsettled: in a rich economy with mobile workers and strong institutions, does a resource boom create a temporary spike or a lasting improvement in local economic activity?
>
> We answer that question using the US fracking revolution. Exploiting the staggered arrival of commercially viable shale extraction across counties, and following local labor markets through both the boom and subsequent oil-price collapses, we show that fracking counties experienced large increases in employment and earnings that did not unwind in the bust. The core finding is not merely that fracking boosted local economies during the boom, but that the gains persisted—suggesting that the dynamic “boom-bust” version of the resource curse is far less universal than often implied.

That is the pitch the paper should have.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:**  
The paper’s contribution is to show that the local employment gains from the US fracking boom persisted through subsequent busts, challenging the dynamic boom-bust version of the resource curse in a strong-institutions, high-mobility setting.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?
Not sharply enough. The paper names relevant neighbors, but the differentiation is still too generic: “we extend the sample through the bust,” “we do a formal asymmetry test,” “we use modern DiD.” That reads like an incremental update rather than a new conceptual contribution.

The paper needs to distinguish itself more forcefully from:
- early fracking-boom papers showing positive local employment effects,
- Dutch disease papers showing sectoral reallocation but net gains,
- classic boom-bust local resource papers,
- long-run subnational resource papers showing persistence in other settings.

Right now the reader can too easily summarize it as: **another county-level fracking DiD, but with later years.**

### Is the contribution framed as a question about the WORLD or as filling a gap in a LITERATURE?
It is mostly framed as a world question, which is good: do resource booms persist or reverse? That is stronger than “the literature has not yet studied the full bust cycle.” But the paper keeps sliding back into literature-gap language and estimator language. The strongest version is not “we revisit fracking with Callaway-Sant’Anna”; it is “we test whether a canonical prediction about local resource booms actually holds in modern America.”

### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain what’s new?
A smart economist could probably say, “It’s a fracking paper arguing the gains were persistent rather than temporary.” That is decent, but not yet memorable. The current draft does not make the novelty vivid enough.

What you want them to say is:
> “It’s the paper showing that the US fracking boom did not produce the expected local bust—employment gains stuck, which changes how we think about the resource curse in advanced economies.”

That is a much stronger takeaway.

### What would make this contribution bigger?
Specific possibilities:

1. **Make persistence itself the object, not just another outcome.**  
   Right now persistence is presented as a statistical comparison of boom and bust coefficients. That is a bit thin. A bigger paper would show what exactly persisted:
   - population,
   - establishments,
   - occupational composition,
   - wages in non-extractive sectors,
   - housing stock,
   - public finances,
   - tradable versus nontradable employment,
   - business formation and survival.

   If the paper could show that fracking generated a durable restructuring of local economies rather than a temporary employment bubble, the contribution becomes much larger.

2. **Sharpen the mechanism behind persistence.**  
   The discussion gestures at multipliers, hysteresis, infrastructure, mobility, and institutional quality. But it does not decide. The paper would be more important if it could say **why** these gains persisted:
   - because non-mining activity remained elevated,
   - because population stayed,
   - because infrastructure lowered costs,
   - because royalty income or local fiscal capacity translated into durable demand,
   - because labor-market thickening outlasted extraction intensity.

3. **Reframe from “resource curse” generally to “when does the resource curse fail?”**  
   That conditional framing is much more ambitious and more honest. The current title and rhetoric imply a broad refutation—“No Resource Curse”—while the actual evidence is narrower: one country, one technology, one period, county-level labor-market outcomes. The bigger contribution is not disproving the resource curse everywhere; it is identifying a setting in which its dynamic local-labor-market prediction does not hold.

4. **Use sectoral composition more aggressively.**  
   If the paper wants to engage Dutch disease and local development, it should not stop at mining/non-mining. Distinguish tradables vs nontradables, manufacturing vs services, construction, health, retail, logistics. That would let the paper say whether fracking produced genuine diversification or merely a service halo around extraction.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

### Closest neighbors
The closest papers appear to be:

- **Black, McKinnish, and Sanders (2005)** on the Appalachian coal boom and bust.
- **Michaels (2011)** on oil abundance and long-run development in the US South.
- **Feyrer, Mansur, and Sacerdote (2017)** on the geographic dispersion of fracking effects.
- **Allcott and Keniston (2018)** on Dutch disease and local labor market effects of shale booms.
- **Weber (2012)** and **Maniloff and Mastromonaco (2017)** on local employment/income effects of shale development.
- Potentially also **Jacobsen and Parker (2016)** and later work on Wyoming/resource cycles.

If I were handling this paper, those are the names I would expect it to be in direct dialogue with.

### How should the paper position itself relative to those neighbors?
Mostly **build on and adjudicate among them**, not attack them.

- Relative to **Feyrer/Weber/Maniloff**: “Those papers established large boom-period local gains; we ask whether those gains survive the bust.”
- Relative to **Allcott and Keniston**: “They show compositional costs alongside net gains; we ask whether net gains persist when prices collapse.”
- Relative to **Black et al.**: “Their coal setting became the canonical local boom-bust template; fracking lets us test whether that pattern generalizes.”
- Relative to **Michaels**: “Our evidence complements long-run persistence in historical oil settings by showing persistence even within a shorter but complete modern boom-bust cycle.”
- Relative to **Jacobsen/Parker**: “We contribute evidence on dynamic persistence in energy-dependent labor markets, but in a broader, multi-play setting.”

The paper should not present “formal asymmetry test” as if prior papers were conceptually underdeveloped. That is a methodological flourish, not the intellectual center.

### Is it currently positioned too narrowly or too broadly?
Paradoxically, both.

- **Too narrowly** in method and application: much of the presentation reads like a county-level fracking DiD paper.
- **Too broadly** in rhetoric: “No Resource Curse” suggests a sweeping claim the evidence cannot carry.

The right lane is: **a broad question answered in a disciplined, bounded way**.  
Namely: “What does the US fracking experience teach us about the persistence of local resource shocks in advanced economies?”

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of, or under-engaged with?
A few conversations deserve more prominence:

1. **Regional adjustment and persistence after local shocks**
   - Autor-Dorn-Hanson style local shock persistence literature
   - Greenstone/Hornbeck/Moretti type local multipliers and place-based persistence
   - recovery after large local demand shocks

   This is important because the paper is fundamentally about whether local shocks leave permanent scars or permanent gains.

2. **Agglomeration/hysteresis/path dependence**
   The paper uses the word “hysteresis” once, but this could be a real intellectual home: do temporary shocks create durable local level shifts?

3. **Place-based policy and local development**
   If fracking creates lasting local gains, that matters for how economists think about localized positive shocks more broadly—not because fracking is policy, but because it informs what kinds of local booms can permanently raise regional activity.

4. **Environmental/resource tradeoff literature**
   The paper mentions externalities, but only defensively at the end. It does not need to become an environmental economics paper, but it should acknowledge that the right welfare question is not “employment only.” Without that, some readers will view the framing as one-dimensional.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?
Not quite. It is currently having a conversation with the fracking literature plus the resource-curse literature. That is necessary, but not sufficient.

The more impactful conversation is:
**temporary shocks versus permanent local transformation**.  
That brings in regional and urban economics, local multipliers, persistence, and structural change. That is the conversation that gives the paper a broader AER-style audience.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
The world before this paper: economists often talk as if resource booms are dangerous for local economies because they distort sectoral composition and leave places exposed to painful reversals when prices fall. We know fracking generated large boom-time gains, but it is unclear whether those gains lasted.

### Tension
The tension is real and potentially powerful: earlier work shows positive local effects of fracking during the boom, but the central dynamic critique of resource dependence is about what happens after the boom ends. If the gains evaporate in the bust, then boom-period estimates overstate long-run benefits. If they persist, then the canonical boom-bust narrative is too crude.

### Resolution
The paper’s resolution is that employment and earnings gains remained elevated through major busts, with non-mining activity also higher, implying persistence rather than reversal.

### Implications
The implication should be: resource booms are not mechanically self-undermining; under certain conditions they can permanently raise local economic activity. That would update beliefs about resource dependence, local adjustment, and what kinds of positive shocks have durable place-based effects.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
It has the ingredients, but the arc is not fully controlled. The draft currently feels like:
1. big claim about resource curse,
2. standard fracking design,
3. a set of employment results,
4. then a “formal asymmetry test” presented as the centerpiece.

That is serviceable but not elegant. The paper needs a stronger story spine:
- **Question:** Are local resource booms temporary or durable?
- **Test case:** US fracking gives a clean full-cycle setting.
- **Key fact:** gains survive the bust.
- **Why:** the shock spills into broader local economic activity.
- **Meaning:** resource-curse logic is conditional, not universal.

At present, the “formal asymmetry test” is doing too much narrative work. It is not itself a story. It is a tool for summarizing the story.

Also, the title overshoots the narrative. “No Resource Curse” is punchy, but it invites pushback because the paper only studies a subset of possible curse margins and one institutional setting. A better title would preserve the boldness while narrowing the claim, something like:
- **The Fracking Boom Without the Bust: Persistent Local Employment Gains in US Shale Counties**
- **Do Resource Booms Reverse? Evidence from the US Fracking Revolution**
- **When Resource Booms Persist: Local Labor Market Effects of US Fracking**

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?
I would say:
> “US fracking counties got a big employment boost—and those gains did not disappear even after the oil busts.”

That is the right leading fact. It is intuitive, surprising, and easy to remember.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
Economists would lean in, but only for a moment. The next 20 seconds matter enormously.

If the presenter immediately says:
> “So the paper is a staggered DiD across 3,140 counties...”
phones come out.

If instead the presenter says:
> “That matters because the canonical worry is not whether booms help in the short run, but whether they leave places worse off once the bust comes. This paper says: in modern America, they didn’t.”
then people stay engaged.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Likely one of these:
1. **What exactly persisted—just extraction jobs, or broader local activity?**
2. **How does this square with Dutch disease and environmental harms?**
3. **Why is fracking different from coal or other resource booms?**
4. **Is this about institutions, labor mobility, or the specific economics of shale?**

Those are good questions. The paper should be organized to answer them fast.

### If the findings are modest or null
The findings are not null. They are economically meaningful. But the persistence claim is still somewhat modestly framed in the current draft because it is reduced to “cannot reject equality of boom and bust effects.” That is not the most persuasive or exciting way to sell it. “Not significantly different” is weak rhetoric for a top-journal pitch. The paper should instead emphasize the positive and sustained post-bust levels, and use the asymmetry test as confirmation rather than the headline.

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

### Without rewriting the paper, what would make it read better?

1. **Cut method from the introduction.**  
   The Callaway-Sant’Anna discussion appears too early and too prominently. For editorial positioning, that is dead weight. AER readers need the question, the key finding, and why it matters before they need the estimator.

2. **Move most robustness material out of the introduction.**  
   The opening pages spend too much time preemptively defending the design. That is referee-facing, not reader-facing. In the introduction, one sentence on credibility is enough.

3. **Front-load the key fact more sharply.**  
   By the end of page 1, the reader should know:
   - fracking caused large local gains,
   - those gains persisted through two major busts,
   - this challenges a canonical prediction about resource booms.

4. **Do not bury the non-mining result.**  
   This is one of the most important facts in the paper. If only mining employment rose and stayed high, the result would feel narrower. The persistence of non-mining employment is what makes the story interesting for a broad economics audience.

5. **Reorganize results around questions, not tables.**  
   The results section should probably follow:
   - Did total employment rise?
   - Did gains persist through the bust?
   - Were gains broader than mining?
   - What mechanism best explains persistence?
   - How does this relate to prior resource-boom cases?

6. **Be careful with the discussion of pre-trends.**  
   Even setting identification aside, from a narrative perspective it is a mistake to tell readers “there are significant negative pre-trends, but that strengthens our case.” That is not persuasive writing. If the event-study has awkward features, the introduction should not invite attention to them. Again, that is for the econometric discussion, not the strategic positioning memo.

7. **Shorten the conclusion.**  
   The conclusion currently mostly summarizes and then qualifies. It would be more useful if it ended with one sharp statement of what belief should change:
   - not whether fracking is good on net,
   - but whether resource booms necessarily imply local boom-bust dynamics.

8. **Trim the title.**  
   “No Resource Curse” is catchy but too absolute. A title that is precise and strong would improve credibility and broaden receptivity.

### Are there results buried in the robustness section that should be in the main results?
Not really the robustness checks themselves. But the **leave-one-out heterogeneity across plays** suggests a more interesting substantive point: the persistence result may differ across geological/economic contexts. If developed substantively rather than as a sensitivity exercise, that could be main-text material.

### Is the conclusion adding value or just summarizing?
Mostly summarizing. It adds some sensible contextual moderation, but not enough synthesis. The paper needs a stronger final paragraph on **conditional resource effects** and **durable local adjustment**.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The gap is mostly **framing plus ambition**, with some scope.

This is not obviously a dead-on-arrival paper. The underlying fact pattern is potentially interesting. But in current form it reads more like a solid field-journal paper with an inflated title than like a paper that would excite the top people in labor/regional/resource economics.

### What is the main problem?

#### 1. Framing problem
Yes. The science may be competent, but the story is not yet pitched at the right level. The paper wants to be about the resource curse, but what it actually has is evidence on the persistence of local labor-market gains in one major resource boom. That can be a strong AER-type contribution if framed as a test of a broader economic idea: **when do local shocks create durable changes rather than transient fluctuations?**

#### 2. Scope problem
Also yes. For a top-journal version, employment and average earnings alone may be too narrow to support a sweeping claim. The paper needs a richer picture of what persisted. Without that, “persistent employment gains” risks feeling like a narrow margin.

#### 3. Novelty problem
Somewhat. Fracking’s local positive effects are well known. “We extend through 2023” is not enough. The persistence angle is the novelty, but it must be made much more conceptually central and empirically richer.

#### 4. Ambition problem
Yes. The paper is competent but safe. It takes a standard county-level policy-evaluation template and attaches a big claim to it. A truly exciting version would do more to explain why persistence happened and what that implies for theories of local adjustment and resource dependence.

### Single most impactful piece of advice
**Reframe the paper around a bigger question—whether temporary resource shocks create durable local economic transformation in advanced economies—and then marshal evidence that what persisted was not just mining employment, but broader local economic activity.**

If they only change one thing, that is the thing.
