### 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
This paper tests the "resource curse" hypothesis by examining whether the massive employment gains from the US fracking revolution were reversed during major oil price collapses in 2015 and 2020. Using a staggered difference-in-differences design across all US counties, it finds that the 21% gain in total employment and 12% gain in earnings were not just temporary windfalls but persistent level shifts that survived the bust. This matters because it challenges the "boom-bust" inevitability of resource-led development in advanced economies with strong institutions.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates the pitch reasonably well in paragraph 2, but it spends too much time on the "how" (staggered DiD, Callaway & Sant’Anna) rather than the "why it matters." 

**The pitch the paper *should* have:**
"Does striking it rich in natural resources inevitably lead to a long-run economic hangover? While the 'resource curse' suggests that communities are often left worse off after the bust, we show that the US fracking revolution created a permanent upward shift in local prosperity. By tracking the full lifecycle of the boom and two subsequent major busts, we provide the first comprehensive evidence that in the presence of modern institutions, resource wealth can be a durable blessing rather than a fleeting curse."

---

### 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**The Contribution:** The paper provides a formal statistical test of boom-bust asymmetry, finding that employment gains from fracking are fully persistent through price downturns, contradicting the dynamic resource curse.

- **Differentiation:** It differentiates itself from Feyrer et al. (2017) and Allcott & Keniston (2018) by moving past the "boom" phase. While those papers look at the *mechanisms* of the upside, this paper looks at the *permanence* of the equilibrium shift.
- **Question vs. Literature:** It frames itself as answering a question about the world (Is the resource curse real in the US?), which is a strength.
- **The "DiD" trap:** A smart economist might initially say "it's another fracking paper," but the "bust-period ATT" is the specific hook that elevates it.
- **Bigger Contribution:** To be a "slam dunk" AER paper, the contribution needs more on *why* it's persistent. Is it human capital accumulation, infrastructure, or a permanent change in the local industrial mix? Just showing it "stayed up" is a fact; explaining the "stickiness" is a theory.

---

### 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
The paper sits at the intersection of Resource Economics and Urban/Labor Economics.

- **Closest Neighbors:** Sachs & Warner (1995) on the curse; Black et al. (2005) on the Appalachian coal bust; Feyrer et al. (2017) on fracking multipliers; Michaels (2011) on long-run oil effects.
- **Positioning:** It builds on Michaels (2011) to attack the "pessimism" of Black et al. (2005). It uses modern "heterogeneity-robust" DiD tools to update a conversation that was previously based on older, potentially biased TWFE models.
- **Missing Literature:** It could speak more to the *Hysteresis* literature in macro/labor and the *Agglomeration* literature in urban economics. If the gains are permanent, it implies fracking triggered a new local steady state.

---

### 4. NARRATIVE ARC
- **Setup:** Resource booms are traditionally seen as "curses" that leave behind "ghost towns" (Appalachian coal).
- **Tension:** Fracking created the biggest boom in a century, but then oil prices crashed twice. Did the ghost town narrative repeat, or has the world changed?
- **Resolution:** The gains didn't go away. The Wald test shows the "bust ATT" is statistically identical to the "boom ATT."
- **Implications:** The resource curse is conditional on institutions. In the US, the "blessing" is permanent.

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong. It’s not just a collection of results; it’s a focused "myth-busting" exercise.

---

### 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
At a dinner party, I’d lead with: **"Even after oil prices crashed in 2015 and 2020, fracking counties kept 100% of the jobs they gained during the boom."**

People would lean in. The follow-up would be: "Why didn't they leave? Did they just become service-sector hubs?" The paper's finding that non-mining employment grew by 18% and *stayed* there is the most provocative fact. It suggests a "Big Push" style of local development.

---

### 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
- **Front-load the Asymmetry:** Move the formal Wald test (Table 2, Panel B) and the discussion of "Persistence vs. Reversal" earlier.
- **Appendices:** The "Standardized Effect Sizes" (Table 5) are useful for Meta-analysis but feel like "journal-filler." Keep them in the appendix.
- **Mechanisms:** Section 5.3 is currently too speculative. It needs a table showing the shift in the *composition* of employment over time. Did the county go from "Mining" to "Retail/Healthcare" and stay there?

---

### 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The paper is currently a very high-quality "Empirical Note." To be a full-length AER article, it needs to solve the **Ambition Problem.**

**Single most impactful piece of advice:** 
The authors must explain the *source* of the persistence. Use the QWI data to look at worker flows or business churn. If mining jobs disappeared (which they did, to some extent), but total employment stayed high, *who* is hiring those people now? Is it "Dutch Disease in reverse," or did the boom fund a permanent upgrade in local productivity?
