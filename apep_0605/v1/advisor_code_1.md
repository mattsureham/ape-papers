**Summary**

The paper studies employment and earnings impacts of the US fracking boom across 3,140 counties from 2001–2023. Using the Callaway and Sant’Anna staggered DiD estimator, it estimates that counties over shale plays gained roughly 21 % in total employment, with mining jobs more than doubling and non-mining employment also rising, and it performs a Wald test showing boom and bust ATTs are statistically indistinguishable. The contribution is framed as a rebuttal to the “resource curse” by documenting persistence of fracking gains through two major downturns and by formally testing for asymmetry between boom and bust effects.

**Essential Points**

1. **Pre-treatment trends appear inconsistent with parallel trends.** The reported event-study table shows sizeable, statistically significant negative coefficients for several pre-treatment event times (e.g., $k=-9$ through $-4$). These imply shale counties were already diverging—losing employment—relative to controls before treatment. Without a convincing explanation or adjustment (e.g., matched controls, flexible trends, or additional covariates), the key identifying assumption is on shaky ground. The authors should either (a) show that these pre-trends disappear when using the appropriate Callaway–Sant’Anna pre-period weighting, (b) re-specify the control group or include county-specific time trends, or (c) provide robustness where places with similar pre-trends are matched.

2. **The asymmetry test hinges on an unclear regime delineation and mixes estimators.** Panel B of Table 2 reports a TWFE interaction of Post × Boom/Bust, yet the paper’s preferred estimator is CS-DiD. Relying on TWFE for the critical resource curse test undermines the defensibility of the result, especially in this setting with heterogeneous treatment timing. Moreover, defining “boom” and “bust” periods by fixed calendar years (e.g., 2014 vs. 2015) may not reflect when each play actually experienced downturns. The authors should conduct the asymmetry test within the CS-DiD framework (e.g., by aggregating ATTs over pre-defined “boom” and “bust” event-time windows) and perform sensitivity analyses with play-specific bust timing.

3. **Omitted-variable concerns and spillovers remain unaddressed.** The geological assignment is plausibly exogenous, but shale counties may differ systematically along recent economic trajectories (e.g., urbanization, infrastructure investment). The current specifications include only county and year fixed effects, and robustness checks do not add time-varying covariates such as county-level oil prices, infrastructure capacity, or commuting areas. Likewise, positive spillovers to nearby counties are likely and could bias the estimated ATT downward if nearby counties serve as controls. Providing sensitivity analyses that (a) include lagged covariates or state×trend fixed effects, and (b) drop counties in the same commuting zone as treated counties or use buffer zones, would bolster causal claims.

**Suggestions**

1. **Clarify and improve the pre-trends evidence.** The event-study coefficients in Table 3 show statistically significant negative effects several years before fracking begins, which is disconcerting. If these estimates are artefacts of the TWFE version of the event study, re-run them using the cohort-averaged CS-DiD event study so that each cohort contributes only to its own relative time. Alternatively, acknowledge that the pre-period window is largely constructed from areas with fewer mining jobs and provide a graph with normalized levels and the same weighting as the estimation sample. If pre-trends persist, consider reweighting the control group (e.g., via entropy balancing) to achieve better pre-treatment balance.

2. **Conduct the asymmetry test within the unmatched CS-DiD framework.** The currently reported regression uses Post × Boom/Bust interactions in a TWFE model (Table 2, Panel B) to compute the Wald test, which reintroduces the issues TWFE is supposed to avoid. Instead, define boom and bust windows in terms of event time (e.g., event times 0–4 for boom, ≥5 for bust) and aggregate the cohort-specific ATTs within those windows using the CS-DiD weighting. Then compare those aggregated ATTs with a bootstrap-based test that respects the estimator’s structure. Present both the point estimates and confidence intervals, and report the sensitivity of the test to alternative window lengths and to play-specific bust years (e.g., oil price crash in 2015 for Bakken vs. 2019 for some other plays). This would transform the asymmetry test into one that is coherent with the main estimation strategy.

3. **Explore alternative control groups or impose more flexible trends.** Although never-treated counties are the baseline control group, they are geographically diverse and could differ in unobservable ways. The authors already mention robustness with “not-yet-treated” counties but do not show results. Present the ATT estimates when (a) the control group is restricted to counties within the same commuting zone or state that never see shale development, and (b) the sample is limited to counties adjacent to shale counties but not themselves over shale (a spatial placebo). Additionally, consider adding county-specific linear or quadratic trends (or state×year interactions) to account for differential secular changes, and show how ATTs shift. Because the key policy implication hinges on persistence, any specification that materially alters the magnitude or significance of the effect must be discussed.

4. **Address potential measurement issues with treatment timing and intensity.** Treatment is coded as a binary switch in the “first significant production” year for each play, but fracking intensity likely ramped up over multiple years with heterogeneous trajectories across plays. Provide a table or figure showing annual drilling activity, rig counts, or production volumes to justify the chosen timing. Consider estimating a continuous-treatment version (e.g., cumulative wells per county or log of well permits), or at least include an intensity-weighted ATT that interacts the binary treatment with measures of drilling intensity in a given year. This would help assess whether the persistence is a function of sustained extraction activity or of early investments that left a permanent footprint.

5. **Expand discussion of potential negative externalities.** The conclusion emphasizes the absence of the resource curse, but the introduction already acknowledges that the analysis focuses only on employment and earnings. To fortify the argument, briefly discuss how these positive local labor-market outcomes fit with the broader literature on fracking’s environmental and health costs, perhaps citing \citet{bartik2019externalities} more extensively. Even if these dimensions are outside the paper’s scope, acknowledging them explicitly in the results/discussion would temper claims and situate the findings within the larger cost-benefit debate.

6. **Improve exposition of robustness checks and leave-one-out results.** Table 4 presents leave-one-out estimates without any uncertainty metrics except SEs, but it could mislead readers into thinking each play contributes equally. Add a brief paragraph explaining why dropping the Bakken lowers the ATT the most and whether there is heterogeneity in the timing or strength of the effect across plays. Include a visualization with confidence intervals for those leave-one-out estimates to show the range. In addition, report the not-yet-treated control group specification explicitly (with table and/or figure) rather than stating the point estimate is “identical,” and explain how standard errors were computed when the control group includes soon-to-be-treated units.

7. **Consider alternative outcomes to reinforce persistence.** To buttress the argument that gains persisted beyond the mining sector, include additional outcomes such as population (to capture in-migration), housing starts, or business establishments, if data are available. Even if these outcomes are only available for a subset of counties, their inclusion could demonstrate that the aggregate employment effects are not driven solely by mining employment (which might be more cyclical) but reflect broader structural change. If obtaining such data is infeasible, discuss the limitations explicitly and perhaps show that non-mining employment dynamics track total employment over time.

8. **Discuss potential spillovers and general equilibrium.** The identification assumes control counties are unaffected, but fracking can have spillovers through commuting and supply-chain linkages. Consider estimating the treatment effect on adjacent counties to test for spatial spillovers (which could bias the baseline ATT downward). Alternatively, include a placebo regression where “treatment” occurs in pre-fracking years for neighboring counties to check for anticipatory effects. Including even a short discussion of these mechanisms would acknowledge potential interdependencies and strengthen the causal narrative.

By addressing these points, the paper would more convincingly claim that the fracking boom generated persistent employment and earnings gains without the usual resource curse dynamics.
