**SUBJECT:** Strategic Positioning of "The Fortress Premium: Exchange Rate Pass-Through and Tourism Demand"

---

### 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper exploits the 2015 "Swiss Franc Shock" to estimate the price elasticity of international tourism—a massive but understudied component of global services trade. By comparing Eurozone visitors to domestic Swiss visitors within the same hotels, the author finds a large demand elasticity (approx. -2.0) that is significantly higher than typically found in goods trade and suggests that high-end "premium" destinations are paradoxically the most vulnerable to currency appreciation.

**Evaluation:** The paper does a decent job, but it is currently too "applied" in its opening. It leads with a travelogue description of Switzerland rather than the economic puzzle.

**The Pitch the Paper Should Have:**
"International trade in services accounts for a quarter of global trade, yet our understanding of its exchange-rate pass-through remains dominated by models of physical goods. This paper exploits the 2015 Swiss National Bank’s surprise removal of the currency floor as a natural experiment to identify the price elasticity of tourism demand. Using a within-hotel, across-nationality design, I find that services demand is more elastic than goods demand, particularly for luxury leisure segments, revealing a 'fortress premium' where high-cost positioning creates extreme vulnerability to monetary shocks."

---

### 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution:** The paper identifies that tourism (a service consumed at the point of production) has a significantly higher exchange rate elasticity than goods trade, with sharp heterogeneity between leisure and business segments.

*   **Differentiated?** Yes, from Auer et al. (2021) which looked at Swiss prices/goods, and from the tourism literature which lacks clean identification.
*   **World vs. Literature?** Currently framed more as a "gap in literature" (services trade is understudied). It needs to be framed as a "Question about the World": *How does a luxury service economy survive a 15% overnight price hike?*
*   **The "Smart Economist" test:** A smart economist would say, "It’s a DiD using the SNB shock to show tourism is way more price-sensitive than we thought."
*   **How to make it bigger:** The "Fortress Premium" concept is the most AER-ready part. The author should lean into the **industrial organization** of tourism. Is this elasticity high because of the availability of substitutes (Innsbruck vs. St. Moritz)? The paper would be bigger if it linked the findings to a model of **comparative advantage in high-cost environments.**

---

### 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

*   **Closest Neighbors:** Auer, Burstein, & Lein (2021); Gopinath, Itskhoki, & Rigobon (2010); and the Amiti/Itskhoki/Konings trade literature.
*   **Positioning:** It should **attack** the assumption that goods-trade elasticities are a good proxy for services. It should **synthesize** trade and tourism econ.
*   **Niche vs. Broad:** Currently a bit niche (Tourism Economics). It needs to speak more to **International Finance** and **Monetary Policy**.
*   **Missing Conversations:** The paper needs to engage with the **"Dutch Disease"** and **"Real Exchange Rate"** literatures. If services are this elastic, the "Dutch Disease" is far more terminal for the service sector than for manufacturing.

---

### 4. NARRATIVE ARC

*   **Setup:** Switzerland is a high-cost, high-quality tourism "fortress."
*   **Tension:** The SNB drops a bomb (the 2015 shock). If the "fortress" is built on quality, does price even matter?
*   **Resolution:** Price matters immensely. Eurozone stays crater by 24%. The "fortress" is actually a glass house.
*   **Implications:** Central banks are ignoring the most volatile part of their trade balance by focusing on manufacturing.

**Evaluate:** The arc is "Serviceable," but the tension is weak. The author needs to emphasize that Swiss tourism *should* have been inelastic because it is "high-end." The fact that it crashed so hard is the surprise.

---

### 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

*   **The Lead Fact:** "When the Franc rose 12%, German skiing trips to Switzerland didn't just dip—they dropped by 35%."
*   **Reaction:** People will lean in because everyone has a "Switzerland is expensive" story.
*   **Follow-up:** "Does this mean the SNB accidentally killed the Alpine economy to save the watchmakers?"

---

### 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

*   **Front-loading:** The event study (Table 3) is actually more interesting than the main DiD (Table 2) because it shows the *persistence* of the shock. Move a version of the event study plot to the first 3 pages.
*   **Appendix:** The Bartik design (Table 2, Col 3) is weak and yields a null result—move it to the appendix. It clutters the main table.
*   **Mechanisms:** Spend less time on the SNB history and more time on the "Substitutability" mechanism. Are people going to Austria instead? If the author can show a "Mirror" effect in Austrian hotel data, the paper becomes a "lock" for a top journal.

---

### 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The gap is **Ambition**. Right now, it’s a very good empirical note on a specific event. To be an AER paper, it needs to be a paper about **the nature of services trade.**

**The Single Most Impactful Advice:**
Stop calling it a "Tourism" paper; call it a "Services Trade and Exchange Rate Pass-Through" paper, and prove that the high elasticity is driven by the fact that services lack the "distribution margins" and "pricing-to-market" buffers that exist in the goods trade.
