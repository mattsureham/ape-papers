**Idea Fidelity**

The paper closely adheres to the manifest. It implements the proposed estimator—state survey agency stringency measured by the leave-one-out mean scope-severity score—as an examiner-leniency instrument for facility-level deficiency severity, exploits multi-state chains to isolate regulatory exposure from management, and studies staffing and turnover outcomes with CMS data (deficiencies, PBJ staffing, and quality measures). All key elements of the original identification strategy, data sources, and policy question appear in the draft.

**Summary**

This paper exploits quasi-random variation across state survey agencies to instrument for nursing home deficiency severity, identifying how regulatory citation pressure affects nurse staffing. The IV results indicate that stricter enforcement—reflected in higher severity scores induced by stringent states—reduces staffing and raises turnover, especially among for-profit chains. The paper interprets this as a “compliance crowding” mechanism whereby administrative burdens generated by citations displace clinical care.

**Essential Points**

1. **Exclusion Restriction Requires Stronger Support.** The instrument is state-level stringency, so any state characteristic that affects staffing directly (e.g., Medicaid reimbursement generosity, nurse wage premia, labor market tightness, demographics of residents, or general regulatory culture) risks violating the exclusion restriction. The within-chain specification mitigates corporate-level confounding but cannot rule out state-specific demand/supply factors that simultaneously induce harder citations and lower staffing. The paper needs to demonstrate more convincingly that these state-level confounders do not drive the second stage—e.g., by incorporating state-level controls, interacting the instrument with observable state characteristics, or using alternative sources of variation (such as federal resurvey discrepancies) that are plausibly orthogonal to state staffing determinants. At present, the compliance crowding interpretation might instead reflect unobserved state attributes that lower staffing and raise citation severity.

2. **Interpretation of the Local Average Treatment Effect Needs Clarification.** The IV identifies the effect of severity on staffing for facilities whose citations fluctuate with state stringency. But the discussion emphasizes a general “compliance crowding” mechanism as if it applied to all nursing homes. The paper should clarify the population to which the effect generalizes and discuss whether those marginal facilities differ systematically (e.g., lower baseline staffing) from others. It would also help to relate the instrumented variation to observable thresholds (e.g., marginal citation types or states close to policy changes) to demonstrate that the mechanism truly reflects regulatory pressure rather than heterogeneity in unobserved quality.

3. **Causal Pathway from Citations to Staffing is Not Fully Established.** The empirical strategy treats mean deficiency severity as an endogenous treatment but does not fully disentangle how citations translate into staffing changes. For example, facilities with staffing shortages may attract more citations and also reduce staffing further—producing a negative relationship that the instrument captures. The compliance crowding mechanism would be more convincing with evidence that severity induces administrative hours (e.g., time spent on plans of correction) or that staffing changes occur after citations rather than simultaneously. The paper should either provide temporal evidence (e.g., staffing before/after the most recent survey) or exploit survey timing to bolster the causal ordering implied by the mechanism.

**Suggestions**

1. **State-Level Balance and Controls.** Expand the balance tests to include more state-level variables such as Medicaid reimbursement rates, median nurse wages, urbanization shares, federal inspection frequency, or resident acuity. Even if the instrument is aggregated at the state level, showing that it is uncorrelated with a richer set of observable state characteristics would strengthen the exclusion argument. Where imbalance remains, include these variables (or their residuals) as controls in both stages, or interact them with chain fixed effects to soak up remaining variation.

2. **Alternative Instrument or Overidentification.** Consider constructing a second, independent measure of state stringency—for example, the average gap between state and CMS federal resurvey citations, or timing of federal enforcement actions. An overidentified IV system would allow overidentification tests that assess whether different sources of exogenous variation point to the same staffing effect, lending credibility to the exclusion restriction.

3. **Leverage Timing of Surveys.** If possible, use the exact survey date to distinguish pre- and post-survey staffing. For example, compare staffing in the month preceding the survey (or the most recent PBJ quarter) to staffing in subsequent quarters to see if staffing declines after the survey in stricter states. This would clarify the causal pathway and help rule out reverse causality or omitted facility-level trends.

4. **Instrument Strength Robustness.** While the first-stage F-statistics are very high, the Anderson–Rubin test reported is relatively weak (F = 5.12). Provide the AR confidence set (or intervals) to show that the main conclusion (negative effect) holds even under weak instrument assumptions. Alternatively, report LIML or estimation with jackknife IV to verify that the magnitude is not overly sensitive to the many-control, many-state structure.

5. **Mechanism Evidence Beyond Staff Composition.** The compliance crowding story would benefit from direct measures of administrative burden—e.g., the number of plans of correction submitted, time spent on documentation, or the incidence of follow-up surveys. If these are unavailable, consider proxying via the number of citations requiring plans of correction or the frequency of higher-severity tags (G–L), and show that they predict staffing reductions even conditional on mean severity.

6. **Explore Heterogeneous Responses by Market Conditions.** Since staffing costs and labor supply vary across states, the effect of citations on staffing may differ where labor markets are tight. Interact the instrument (or estimated severity) with state-level nurse wage indexes or staffing vacancy rates to see if the negative effect is concentrated where it is costliest to reallocate staff. This would also lend nuance to the policy message: compliance crowding may be more severe in constrained markets.

7. **Clarify the Role of Multi-State Chains.** The within-chain results are compelling, but some discussion on how much of the variation is between-state versus within-state (e.g., do chain facilities often face multiple states) would help readers assess the plausibility of quasi-random assignment. Provide summary statistics showing the proportion of chains with facilities in multiple states and the average number of states per chain. This will reinforce the claim that chain fixed effects indeed purify the instrument.

8. **Cross-Validate with Quality Outcomes.** Although the paper focuses on staffing, it would be informative to compute the reduced-form effect of state stringency on clinical quality measures (e.g., pressure ulcers, falls). If compliance crowding reduces staffing without improving quality, the reduced-form effects might be neutral or negative; showing this would emphasize the policy implications and align with the crowding narrative.

9. **Discuss Policy-Relevant Magnitudes Carefully.** The policy translation (e.g., 16 fewer nursing hours for 80 residents) is striking but depends on the assumption that the IV effect is uniform. Frame these magnitudes as illustrative and tie them to the local nature of the IV. Also, consider comparing the estimated margin (0.73 HPRD per severity point) to typical staffing ratios to give policymakers a sense of scale.

10. **Be Transparent About Generalizability.** In the conclusion, reiterate that the results speak to the causal effect for facilities affected by state-level stringency variation and that effects might differ under broader standardization reforms or federal enforcement changes. This will avoid overclaiming while preserving the paper’s policy relevance.

Overall, the paper tackles an important question with novel data and a creative IV strategy. Addressing these points will strengthen the credibility of the identification and deepen the policy implications.
