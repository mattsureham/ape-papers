### 2. Summary
The paper identifies the causal effect of nursing home deficiency citations on staffing levels by using state-level examiner stringency as an instrument for citation severity. Contrary to the intuition that stricter regulation improves care, the author finds that a one-unit increase in instrumented severity reduces total nurse staffing by 0.73 hours per resident per day (approx. 5.2% of the mean). The results suggest a "compliance crowding" mechanism where administrative requirements and staff turnover divert resources away from frontline clinical care.

### 3. Essential Points
1.  **Exclusion Restriction and State-Level Confounders:** The paper relies on the assumption that state-level survey stringency only affects staffing through the issuance of deficiencies. However, states that are "strict" in surveying often have other systematically different policies, most notably **Medicaid reimbursement rates** and **state-specific minimum staffing mandates**. If "strict" states also have lower reimbursement rates (tightening budgets) or different labor market conditions, the IV estimate is biased. While the chain fixed effects (FE) help, they only control for *corporate* strategy; they do not control for the fact that the Utah facility in a chain faces Utah’s Medicaid rates while the Texas facility faces Texas’s rates. The author must explicitly control for state-level Medicaid generosity and state staffing laws.
2.  **Definition of the "Treatment" Variable:** The endogenous variable is the "mean severity score" across all citations in a survey. This is a problematic construct because a facility with 20 "D" citations (minor) might have the same *mean* as a facility with 1 "D" citation, yet the administrative burden of 20 plans of correction is orders of magnitude higher. The "Total Count" of deficiencies or the "Max Severity" are more standard measures of regulatory pressure. The author should test if the results are robust to using the *count* of deficiencies as the endogenous variable, as "compliance crowding" is likely driven by the volume of paperwork rather than the average grade of that paperwork.
3.  **Timing and Simultaneity:** Nursing home surveys happen at a specific point in the year (recorded in the CMS data). For an annual cross-section (2025), is the staffing measured *before* or *after* the survey? If the staffing data is an annual average and the survey happened in month 11, the "effect" on staffing might actually be the "cause" of the deficiencies (low staffing leads to more citations). The author needs to demonstrate a clear temporal lag—showing that a high-severity survey in Quarter $T$ leads to staffing drops in Quarters $T+1$ and $T+2$.

### 4. Suggestions
*   **The Chain FE Specification:** This is your strongest result. I suggest making Table 3, Column 3 your "headline" result. It effectively compares two identical "Genesis" homes where one randomly gets a "strict" state inspector and the other a "lenient" one. This kills 90% of the selection concerns regarding management quality.
*   **Administrative vs. Clinical Staff:** The "compliance crowding" theory suggests that while *clinical* hours drop, *administrative* hours (e.g., Director of Nursing time spent on paperwork) might actually increase. CMS data includes "Administrative" or "Medical Director" hours. Finding an *increase* in those hours alongside a *decrease* in CNA hours would be the "smoking gun" for your mechanism.
*   **Turnover Mechanism:** The result that turnover increases by 12.7 percentage points is extremely large (nearly a 25% increase relative to the mean). This suggests a "morale" channel or "burnout" channel. You should discuss whether this is driven by management firing staff to save money for fines, or staff quitting due to the stress of a "Plan of Correction" regime.
*   **Sample Selection:** You mention 14,703 facilities but analyze 11,427. Is there a systematic reason why 3,000 facilities have missing staffing data? Often, the worst-performing or smallest facilities fail to report PBJ data correctly. Check for "Non-random Missingness" balance.
*   **Monotonicity Check:** Standard in the judge-IV literature is testing if the "strict" states are strict for all types of homes (For-profit vs. Non-profit). It is worth ensuring that your instrument doesn't have a "sign flip" for different types of facilities.
*   **Policy Nuance:** Clarify that "Severity A-L" is a CMS standard. While the *state* does the survey, they use a *federal* form. Mentioning that CMS conducts "Federal Monitoring Surveys" (where feds re-survey a state's work) would add great institutional flavor to your background section and justify why state-level variation exists despite federal standards.
*   **Literature:** You cite Einav et al. (2025) and GAO reports. You should also look at **Johnson (2020, American Economic Review)** on the effects of OSHA inspections. The tension between "deterrence" and "compliance costs" is a classic theme there that maps well to your nursing home setting.
*   **Data Year:** The paper identifies the data as "2025" and the current date as "2026." While this is a simulated future date, ensure that if this were for current publication, you address the lingering effects of COVID-19 on staffing volatility, which remained a major factor in nursing home operations through 2024.
