### 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

The paper evaluates the impact of real-time, electronic transaction monitoring on tax compliance and reported economic activity using Croatia’s 2013 "fiscalization" mandate. By exploiting a staggered sectoral rollout and cross-country comparisons, it finds that mandatory digital receipts increased the VAT-to-GDP ratio by 1.1 percentage points and boosted reported GVA in hospitality by 35%. This matters because it provides a "proof of concept" for high-tech tax enforcement in economies with high informality, directly informing the EU’s "VAT in the Digital Age" policy.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this well, though it could be punchier. The second paragraph currently dives a bit deep into the "Law on Fiscalization of Cash Transactions" (legal citations). 
**The "Better" Pitch:** "Can a digital eye on every cash register eliminate the shadow economy? This paper uses Croatia’s 2013 mandate for real-time electronic receipts to show that technological monitoring can permanently increase VAT revenue by 10%. We find that the 'fiscal receipt dividend' is largest where cash is king—hospitality—proving that technology can bridge the state-capacity gap in high-informality settings."

### 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:** The paper quantifies the long-run fiscal and reporting gains from universal, real-time digital transaction monitoring, showing these gains are durable and concentrated in cash-intensive sectors.

*   **Differentiation:** It differentiates well from Naritomi (2019) (consumer lotteries) and Pomeranz (2015) (B2B paper trails) by focusing on **B2C real-time digital monitoring**.
*   **Framing:** It is currently framed as "filling a gap in the literature" regarding electronic monitoring. It should be framed as a question about **State Capacity**: "Can technology replace traditional auditing in weak-institution environments?"
*   **Clarity:** A smart economist would understand this as the "Croatia cash register paper."
*   **Bigger Contribution:** To make this an AER heavyweight, the author needs to explore **spillovers**. Does fixing the VAT leak lead firms to under-report labor or over-report costs (as in Carrillo et al.)? If they only show the "dividend" without the "distortion," it’s a policy evaluation. If they show the "cat-and-mouse" game of tax evasion, it’s an AER paper.

### 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

*   **Neighbors:** Naritomi (2019), Pomeranz (2015), Kleven et al. (2011), and Slemrod (2019).
*   **Positioning:** It should **synthesize** these. It builds on the "information trail" logic but introduces a new "real-time" dimension.
*   **Missing Conversations:** The paper is silent on the **Industrial Organization (IO)** of these firms. Did the 35% GVA increase come from existing firms reporting more, or did small, informal "mom-and-pop" shops go out of business (exit), replaced by larger, compliant ones? This "selection vs. behavior" conversation is essential for the AER.

### 4. NARRATIVE ARC

*   **Setup:** High informality in transition economies makes traditional tax enforcement impossible.
*   **Tension:** Does expensive digital infrastructure actually catch "cheats," or do they just find new ways to hide?
*   **Resolution:** It works. Not only does revenue go up, but it stays up for a decade.
*   **Implications:** The "VAT gap" is a choice, not a destiny; digital enforcement is a viable path for the EU and developing nations.

**Evaluation:** The arc is clean. However, the "tension" is a bit low because the results are almost *too* good. The author needs to highlight the "gradual accumulation" (Table 2, Col 4) as a surprise—why didn't it happen overnight? That’s where the interesting narrative lies.

### 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

*   **Lead Fact:** "Croatia forced every café to link their cash register to the tax office in real-time, and reported hospitality revenue jumped 35%."
*   **Reaction:** Lean in. It’s a massive number.
*   **Follow-up:** "Did prices go up for consumers?" or "Did the cafés just start paying their waiters under the table to offset the tax?"
*   **Nulls:** The null pre-trends are vital here to prove this isn't just Croatia's 2013 EU accession talking.

### 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

*   **Move to Appendix:** The detailed legal citations and the "March 2012 VAT rate change" discussion (page 4) can be compressed.
*   **Front-load:** The event study (Table 5) is the "money shot." It shows the durability. It should be Figure 1, not Table 5.
*   **Expand:** Section 5.2 (Sector-Level) needs more on firm size. Are the gains coming from the biggest tax evaders or the smallest?

### 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The gap is **Mechanism and General Equilibrium (GE) effects**. 
Right now, it’s a very high-quality "Case Study of a Reform." For the AER, we need to know: **What happens to the rest of the firm?** 
If I'm a café owner and I can no longer hide my coffee sales, do I start lying about my wage bill? Do I reduce my investment? If the author can use the Eurostat data (or better yet, firm-level administrative data) to show how firms re-optimize across other margins of evasion, it’s a slam dunk.

**Single biggest piece of advice:** Move beyond the "revenue dividend" and look for the "informality migration"—show us where that 30% of "hidden" GVA went once the cash registers were turned on.
