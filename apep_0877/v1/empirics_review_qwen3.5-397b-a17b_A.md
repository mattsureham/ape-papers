\section*{Referee Report}

\subsection*{1. Idea Fidelity}

The paper largely adheres to the core research question outlined in the Original Idea Manifest: estimating the causal effect of Croatia's 2013 fiscalization mandate on tax compliance and economic activity. However, there are notable deviations in the empirical strategy. The Manifest proposed a Callaway-Sant'Anna staggered Difference-in-Differences (DiD) exploiting the three-month gaps between sector phases (January, April, July) as the \emph{primary} identification strategy. The Paper shifts the primary identification to a cross-country DiD (Croatia vs. 5 controls) and admits that annual data frequency prevents exploiting the within-year stagger effectively. Additionally, the Manifest proposed using Eurostat Structural Business Survey (SBS) data to test size-class heterogeneity (Strategy 3); the Paper instead uses National Accounts (NACE sections) and drops the size-class analysis entirely. While the topic remains faithful, the key identification advantage highlighted in the Manifest (clean within-country temporal variation) is substantially diluted in the execution.

\subsection*{2. Summary}

This paper estimates the impact of Croatia's 2013 mandatory electronic cash register mandate on VAT revenue and sectoral value added. Using a cross-country difference-in-differences design and a triple-difference specification, the author finds that fiscalization increased Croatia's VAT-to-GDP ratio by approximately 1.1 percentage points and raised reported gross value added in treated sectors by 10 percent, with heterogeneous effects concentrated in hospitality. The results suggest that real-time transaction monitoring generates sustained compliance gains, offering relevant evidence for ongoing EU digital tax reforms.

\subsection*{3. Essential Points}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Inference with Few Clusters:} The primary cross-country DiD specification relies on only six countries (one treated, five controls). Clustering standard errors at the country level with $G=6$ clusters leads to severe size distortions and over-rejection of the null hypothesis (Cameroon, Gelbach, and Miller 2008). While the paper acknowledges this limitation, leave-one-out exercises do not correct the standard error bias. For a causal claim of this magnitude to be credible, the author must employ inference methods robust to few clusters, such as wild cluster bootstrap percentile-t procedures or permutation tests (Conley and Taber 2011). Without this, the significance stars in Table 2 are likely unreliable.

    \item \textbf{EU Accession Confounding:} Croatia's EU accession occurred on July 1, 2013, coinciding exactly with Phase 3 of the fiscalization rollout. Accession involved profound structural changes (customs unions, regulatory harmonization, funding flows) that likely affected VAT collection and reported economic activity independently of fiscalization. The paper argues that country$\times$year fixed effects absorb this, but this is incorrect in the cross-country DiD: the treatment effect \emph{is} the Croatia$\times$Post interaction. If EU accession caused a level shift in Croatia's VAT capacity relative to non-EU controls (e.g., Romania was already in, others were members), this confounds the treatment. The author must either isolate Phase 1 and 2 effects (pre-accession) more rigorously or use a Synthetic Control Method to construct a counterfactual that better matches Croatia's accession trajectory.

    \item \textbf{Data Frequency vs. Stagger Identification:} The Manifest emphasized the three-month stagger as a key identification advantage. The Paper admits annual data renders this temporal variation unusable, reducing the design to cross-sectional sector variation (high-cash vs. low-cash sectors) within a single treatment year. This weakens the causal claim because sector-specific shocks in 2013 (e.g., tourism booms) could drive the results. The author should temper claims about the "staggered design" and explicitly frame the sector results as intensity-based variation rather than temporal stagger, or seek quarterly turnover data to recover the original identification strategy.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection*{4. Suggestions}

The following recommendations are intended to strengthen the paper's empirical robustness and clarity. While not strictly essential for publication, addressing them would significantly elevate the quality of the analysis and align it closer to the high standards of \textit{AER: Insights}.

\textbf{Robust Inference Techniques.} Given the small number of clusters, I strongly recommend replacing the standard cluster-robust standard errors in the cross-country DiD with wild cluster bootstrap confidence intervals. This is now standard practice in macro-DiD settings with few treated units. Alternatively, consider reporting Conley et al. (2012) spatial HAC standard errors if there is reason to believe errors are correlated across neighboring Central European economies. For the triple-difference specification, where the unit of observation is sector-country-year, clustering at the country level is still conservative but worth double-checking with two-way clustering (country and sector) to ensure serial correlation within sectors over time is accounted for.

\textbf{Synthetic Control Method (SCM).} Given that Croatia is a single treated unit in the aggregate analysis, SCM offers a compelling alternative to the standard DiD. Constructing a synthetic Croatia from a donor pool of OECD or transition economies could better account for the unique shock of EU accession. The author could match on pre-treatment VAT/GDP trajectories, GDP growth, and informality estimates. This would provide a visual and statistical counterfactual that demonstrates whether the 2013 break is unique to Croatia or part of a regional trend. Even if the main results remain DiD, adding an SCM appendix would bolster confidence in the aggregate findings.

\textbf{Quarterly Data Exploration.} The loss of the within-year stagger is a significant limitation. I suggest checking if Eurostat's Short-Term Business Statistics (STS) or Croatian Tax Administration public reports offer quarterly turnover data for NACE sections. Even a shorter time series (e.g., 2012--2014 quarterly) would allow the author to exploit the January vs. April vs. July variation as originally proposed. If quarterly data is unavailable, the author should explicitly discuss this data constraint in the introduction rather than the results section, managing reader expectations about the identification strategy upfront.

\textbf{Disentangling EU Accession.} To address the accession confound more directly, consider adding Romania and Bulgaria to the control group if data permits (they joined in 2007, so 2013 is post-accession for them) or excluding them if their transition dynamics are too different. Alternatively, interact the treatment with a measure of "cash intensity" at the sector level. If the effect is truly driven by fiscalization, it should be stronger in sectors with higher pre-reform cash usage, regardless of EU accession. The paper touches on this with hospitality, but a continuous measure of cash intensity (e.g., from household budget surveys) would strengthen the mechanism test.

\textbf{Outcome Variable Nuance.} The paper uses VAT revenue as a share of GDP. This ratio can fluctuate due to denominator effects (GDP contractions) rather than numerator effects (revenue increases). Given Croatia's post-crisis recovery, ensure the results are not driven by GDP volatility. I suggest presenting results for log VAT revenue (numerator only) as a robustness check, controlling for log GDP separately. Additionally, National Accounts GVA is often modeled based on tax data; if fiscalization improved tax data, it might mechanically improve GVA estimates without real economic growth. The author should acknowledge this reflection problem: are we measuring more compliance or more output? Discussing this distinction is crucial for policy interpretation.

\textbf{Mechanism Evidence.} The paper infers mechanism from sector heterogeneity. If possible, include descriptive evidence on the number of fiscal receipts issued or the volume of electronic transactions from Croatian National Bank reports. Even a simple figure showing the jump in electronic payment volume in 2013 would corroborate the claim that the mandate changed behavior rather than just reporting practices. This would help distinguish between "formalization of existing activity" versus "reduction of evasion on existing activity."

\textbf{Clarifying the Stagger Claim.} In the Introduction and Identification sections, revise the language regarding the staggered design. Currently, the abstract claims the rollout created "clean within-country variation," but the results section admits annual data prevents exploiting this. I recommend reframing the contribution: the paper provides evidence on \emph{sectoral heterogeneity} in compliance responses rather than temporal stagger effects. This aligns the claims with the actual empirical execution and avoids overpromising on identification.

\textbf{Policy Context Expansion.} The discussion on EU "VAT in the Digital Age" (ViDA) is timely. Expand this slightly to discuss cost-benefit implications. If the compliance gain is 1.1pp of GDP, what were the implementation costs? Even rough estimates of the cost per registered business would help policymakers weigh the "fiscal dividend" against administrative burdens. This would make the paper more actionable for the target audience of \textit{AER: Insights}.

\textbf{Visual Enhancements.} Consider adding a event-study plot for the sector-level triple difference, not just the aggregate VAT. Showing the dynamics of GVA in hospitality vs. agriculture over time would visually reinforce the parallel trends assumption for the sectoral analysis. Currently, Table 5 shows the aggregate event study; a sector-level equivalent would be highly informative given the reliance on sectoral variation for the mechanism.

\textbf{Data Appendix Transparency.} Finally, ensure the Data Appendix clearly lists any imputations or interpolations used for missing Eurostat values. National Accounts data sometimes undergoes revisions; specifying the vintage of the data (e.g., "downloaded March 2026") is good practice, but noting if any series were spliced or adjusted for ESA2010 consistency would add to reproducibility.

By addressing the inference issues and clarifying the identification limitations regarding the stagger and EU accession, this paper has the potential to be a definitive reference on electronic fiscalization in transition economies. The core finding is policy-relevant and novel, but the empirical scaffolding needs to be as robust as the conclusion suggests.
