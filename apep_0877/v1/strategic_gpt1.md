## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether real-time digital monitoring of retail transactions can materially increase tax compliance. Using Croatia’s 2013 electronic cash register mandate, it argues that forcing firms to transmit cash sales to the tax authority in real time increased reported activity in cash-intensive sectors and raised VAT revenue at the national level.

A busy economist should care because this is a first-order state-capacity question: can governments buy tax capacity with digital technology, especially in sectors where evasion is easiest? That is a broader and more important question than “did Croatia’s reform work?”

### Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Not really. The paper opens with the VAT gap and quickly moves into “causal evidence is thin” and “Croatia is a rare exception.” That is competent, but it frames the contribution as a methods opportunity rather than a substantive economic question. The introduction also gets into design details too early. A top-journal introduction should first tell me what big economic problem is at stake, what general mechanism is being tested, and what was learned about the world.

### The pitch the paper should have

Here is the pitch the paper should give in the first two paragraphs:

> Governments increasingly hope that digitizing tax administration can convert hidden economic activity into taxable activity, but we still know surprisingly little about how much revenue real-time transaction monitoring actually generates. The key economic question is whether evasion at the point of final sale is a major margin of informality that can be shut down with technology, or whether firms simply adapt around new reporting systems.
>
> This paper studies Croatia’s 2013 electronic fiscal receipt mandate, which required cash-register transactions to be transmitted to the tax authority in real time. I show that the reform substantially increased reported activity in the sectors where cash evasion should be most prevalent and raised VAT revenue at the national level, with especially large effects in hospitality. The broader lesson is that digital tax enforcement can generate meaningful gains in state capacity when it targets final-sale cash transactions.

That framing is stronger because it is about the world: the enforceability of cash taxation, the limits of state capacity, and the promise of administrative digitization.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### Contribution in one sentence

The paper’s contribution is to show that a nationwide real-time electronic receipt system can substantially increase reported economic activity in cash-intensive sectors and raise VAT revenue, suggesting that digitized monitoring can build tax capacity by reducing final-sale evasion.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest papers?

Only partially. The author cites the usual neighbors—Pomeranz, Naritomi et al., Carrillo et al., Almunia and Lopez-Rodriguez—but the differentiation is still too schematic: “they study X, I study Croatia.” The introduction needs a crisper statement of what is conceptually different here.

The paper’s real distinction is not just geography or institutional detail. It is that:

1. **This is a universal administrative monitoring technology**, not a selective audit, threshold effect, or consumer-incentive program.
2. **It targets the final-sale cash margin**, which is precisely where VAT paper trails are weakest.
3. **It speaks to state capacity in an advanced/transition European setting**, not just compliance responses in Latin American tax administrations.
4. **It studies medium-run persistence**, not just short-run reporting responses.

Those are the differentiators. Right now they are present, but not cleanly organized.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a gap in a literature?

Too much as filling a gap in the literature. There is a lot of “causal evidence is thin” and “rare exception.” That is fine, but secondary. The stronger framing is:

- How much evasion sits at the cash-register margin?
- Can real-time monitoring change firms’ reporting behavior durably?
- When does digitization genuinely build tax capacity rather than just reshuffle reporting?

That is a world question. The paper should lean into that much harder.

### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what’s new?

They could probably say: “It’s a DiD on Croatia’s fiscal cash-register reform and finds VAT and reported output go up, especially in hospitality.” That is decent, but still too close to “another DiD paper about a tax reform.”

The introduction should make it easier for the reader to say:

> “This paper shows that if the state can digitally observe every final cash sale in real time, compliance jumps a lot—especially where paper trails are weakest. So digital tax administration may be a major state-capacity tool, not just an administrative tweak.”

That is the version with AER potential.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Most importantly, make the paper less about one reform episode and more about a general proposition:

- **Different framing:** position it as evidence on whether digitization can substitute for traditional enforcement in hard-to-tax sectors.
- **Different outcomes:** if available, link to firm entry/exit, employment, prices, card adoption, business closures, or tax remittance composition. Right now “VAT/GDP + sectoral GVA” is suggestive but narrow.
- **Different mechanism:** sharpen whether this is increased reporting of existing activity versus real output expansion, sectoral reallocation, or formalization. Even descriptive mechanism evidence would help.
- **Different comparison:** benchmark Croatia against countries with similar electronic monitoring reforms later on, or against sectors with differing cash intensity more explicitly.
- **Different implication:** say what this implies for where digitization works best—high-cash sectors, high-informality countries, low-third-party-reporting environments.

As written, the contribution is respectable. To feel AER-sized, it needs to become a paper about **the economics of digital state capacity**.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Before this paper, economists believe tax compliance improves when governments obtain third-party information, but the final cash sale remains a classic blind spot. Governments are increasingly adopting digital reporting systems, yet evidence on whether these systems truly generate durable revenue gains is limited.

### Tension

There are two tensions the paper could exploit, but it currently uses only one weakly.

1. **Economic tension:** cash retail transactions are precisely where evasion should be easiest, but it is unclear whether digitization can really shut down that margin rather than shifting behavior elsewhere.
2. **Policy tension:** governments are spending heavily on digital tax administration, but we do not know whether these systems produce substantial, persistent gains or just short-lived reporting spikes.

Those are strong tensions. The paper currently emphasizes a narrower one: “national reforms are hard to identify causally.” That is a research-design tension, not an economic one.

### Resolution

The reform increased VAT revenue and reported value added, especially in hospitality, with effects that appear persistent. The implied interpretation is that real-time monitoring makes final-sale evasion materially harder.

### Implications

The implications should be:

- Digital state capacity can generate meaningful revenue gains.
- The gains are concentrated where cash intensity and underreporting are highest.
- Real-time reporting may complement, or in some settings substitute for, more traditional audit-based enforcement.
- The payoff to digitization is heterogeneous across sectors and institutional contexts.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has a serviceable arc, but it is still too much a collection of empirical results around a reform. The narrative is not fully earned because the paper alternates between three possible stories:

1. Croatia-specific VAT reform.
2. Electronic receipts as a compliance technology.
3. Digital state capacity in modern tax administration.

The third is the strongest, but the paper has not fully committed to it. It should.

### What story should it be telling?

The story should be:

> The final cash sale is one of the hardest places for the state to observe economic activity. Croatia’s fiscalization reform created an unusually clean test of whether digital transaction-level monitoring can close that information gap. It did—and most strongly in precisely the sectors where theory predicts evasion should be most responsive to detection. That makes the paper about the limits and possibilities of digital state capacity, not just about one VAT reform.

Somewhat, but in the wrong way. It is front-loaded with **estimates**, not with the **idea**. The reader learns many numbers quickly, but not the deepest reason to care.

Only modestly. It mostly summarizes. It should instead answer:
- What does this change about our understanding of tax enforcement?
