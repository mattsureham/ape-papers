## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether real-time digital monitoring of retail transactions can materially increase tax compliance and reported economic activity. Using Croatia’s 2013 mandate that cash transactions be recorded through electronically connected cash registers, the paper argues that governments can generate a sizable “fiscal receipt dividend”: higher VAT revenue and higher reported value added, especially in cash-intensive sectors like hospitality.

Why should a busy economist care? Because this is a first-order state-capacity question: can digital monitoring meaningfully reduce informality at the point of sale, and if so, how large are the gains? That is a broad, policy-relevant question with implications for tax capacity, informality, and the design of digital enforcement systems now being debated across Europe.

### Does the paper articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Not quite. The current opening is competent, but it starts from the EU VAT gap and then moves quickly into the institutional details of Croatia’s rollout. That makes the paper sound like a well-executed policy evaluation of one reform, rather than a paper about a larger economic question: how much hidden activity can states surface when they digitize monitoring at the final sale.

The first two paragraphs should do less institutional narration and more intellectual framing. Right now the introduction is too close to “here is a nice setting with staggered rollout.” AER readers need to understand immediately that the paper is about the power and limits of digital state capacity.

### The pitch the paper should have

> A central problem in public finance is that final retail transactions are hard to monitor, making cash-intensive sectors a persistent locus of tax evasion and informality. This paper asks whether digital real-time transaction monitoring can close that gap: when the state can observe every receipt as it is issued, do firms simply comply, and how large are the resulting gains in reported activity and tax revenue?
>
> I study Croatia’s 2013 fiscalization reform, which required cash-register receipts to be transmitted electronically to the tax authority in real time. I show that the reform generated a large and persistent increase in VAT revenue and reported value added, with especially large effects in hospitality, suggesting that digital monitoring can substantially expand effective tax capacity where cash evasion is pervasive.

That is the AER version of the paper. Start with the world problem, not the institutional details.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper’s contribution is to provide evidence that real-time electronic monitoring of cash transactions can produce large, sustained increases in reported economic activity and VAT revenue, especially in cash-intensive sectors.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?

Only partially. The paper cites a sensible cluster—Pomeranz, Naritomi et al., Carrillo et al., Almunia and Lopez-Rodriguez—but the differentiation is still somewhat mechanical: “they study X, I study Croatia and universal real-time monitoring.” That is not yet sharp enough.

The paper should be clearer that it is not merely another paper on tax enforcement technology. Its distinctive claim is something like:

- Pomeranz is about the paper trail and self-enforcement in intermediate transactions.
- Naritomi is about consumer incentives to request receipts.
- Carrillo is about firm responses to discrepancy information.
- This paper is about a different enforcement margin altogether: the state observing the final sale in real time.

That distinction matters. The hardest place to enforce VAT is often the final cash transaction, where third-party reporting is weakest. If this paper is really about that margin, that should be the centerpiece.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a gap in a literature?

It is mixed, but too often it slips into literature-gap framing. The stronger version is the world question: can digital monitoring make retail tax evasion meaningfully harder? The current introduction spends too much time on “credible counterfactual” and “rare exception” language. That is useful for referees, but it is not what makes the paper matter.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?

Right now, they might say: “It’s a DiD paper showing Croatia’s e-receipts raised VAT revenue.” That is not enough. The author wants them to say: “It shows that real-time digital monitoring at the point of sale can materially increase tax capacity, and that the gains are concentrated exactly where final-sale evasion is easiest.”

At present, the paper is at risk of sounding like “another policy evaluation of a tax compliance reform” rather than “evidence on a major enforcement technology.”

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Specific ways to enlarge it:

1. **Make the object of interest more fundamental than Croatia.**  
   The paper should explicitly frame the reform as a test of whether states can close the final-sale evasion margin. That is bigger than “Croatia had a reform.”

   The hospitality result is the best fact in the paper. If the argument is that digital monitoring matters where cash opacity is greatest, then the paper should foreground a gradient by pre-reform cash intensity or evasion risk, not just phase categories.

3. **Clarify whether reported GVA is a compliance margin or a real activity margin.**

If the paper can’t separate these, it should avoid overclaiming “economic activity” and instead say “reported economic activity.”

4. **Connect more directly to tax capacity/state capacity.**  
   The paper briefly does this, but too late and too lightly. If the true contribution is that digital infrastructure can permanently raise state extractive capacity, that is the big-picture claim.

5. **Potentially broaden outcomes conceptually.**

The unexpected but valuable reframing is: this is not just about a tax instrument; it is about how digital infrastructure changes what the state can observe. That is a much more AER-like conversation.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Before this paper, we know that VAT enforcement often relies on paper trails, audits, or third-party reporting, but the final cash sale remains hard to monitor. Governments are increasingly adopting digital enforcement technologies, yet there is limited evidence on whether real-time monitoring of point-of-sale transactions actually yields large and durable compliance gains.

### Tension

The core tension is: digital monitoring sounds powerful in theory, but is it transformative in practice or merely administrative theater? And even if tax revenues rise after such a reform, is that because hidden activity is being surfaced where evasion was common, or because of unrelated macro and policy changes?

### Resolution

The paper’s answer is that Croatia’s electronic receipt mandate generated a substantial increase in VAT revenue and in reported value added in treated sectors, with the strongest gains in hospitality. The pattern is consistent with the reform biting hardest where cash evasion opportunities were greatest.

### Implications

The implications are that digital real-time monitoring can materially expand tax capacity in cash-heavy environments, and that the returns to such systems are likely heterogeneous—largest where baseline opacity and informality are high. That matters for governments weighing the costs and benefits of digital tax administration reforms.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the ingredients, but the arc is **not yet fully coherent**. Right now it reads a bit like:

- Here is the reform.
- Here are two empirical designs.
- Here are several positive results.
- Here is why that matters for EU policy.

That is serviceable, but not great. The paper needs a more disciplined story:

1. **Problem:** final-sale evasion is a central blind spot of tax enforcement.
2. **Intervention:** real-time fiscal devices are designed to close that blind spot.
3. **Test:** Croatia offers unusual evidence.
4. **Finding:** large gains, especially where cash opacity is highest.
5. **Meaning:** digital monitoring can raise state capacity, but primarily in places with lots of evasion to begin with.

At present, the paper is a bit too close to “a collection of positive estimates around a reform.” The story should be about a specific enforcement margin and why this reform identifies it.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The main gap is **framing plus ambition**, with some novelty risk in the background.

This is not obviously a bad paper. In fact, it has a potentially strong fact. But in its current form, it feels more like a solid field-journal public finance paper than an AER paper, because it is too attached to the institutional case and not sufficiently committed to the larger question.

### What is the main problem?

Mostly **a framing problem**, secondarily **an ambition problem**.

- **Framing problem:** The paper undersells its core question. It should be about digital state capacity and the final-sale evasion margin, not mainly about Croatia’s reform.
- **Ambition problem:** The paper presents two estimands and several robustness checks, but it does not fully convert them into a broader claim about how digital monitoring changes compliance behavior and where it should matter most.

### Is there also a novelty problem?

Potentially. The tax compliance literature already has many papers on monitoring, reporting, and VAT enforcement. So the author cannot just say “electronic enforcement matters.” The genuinely new angle must be: **real-time point-of-sale monitoring of final cash transactions**, with sectoral heterogeneity lining up with the enforcement story.

If that distinction is not made crisply, the paper will feel incremental.

### Is there a scope problem?

Somewhat. The paper may be trying to do too much with limited outcomes: aggregate VAT/GDP and sectoral GVA. That is enough for a paper if the story is sharp. But if the author wants a top-journal pitch, the scope has to come from conceptual reach, not just one extra table. The author should resist turning this into a kitchen-sink exercise and instead make the existing evidence speak more clearly to the big question.

### The single most impactful advice

**Rewrite the paper around one claim: real-time digital monitoring can meaningfully reduce final-sale tax evasion and raise state capacity, with the clearest evidence coming from the concentration of gains in cash-intensive sectors.**

Everything else should serve that claim.
