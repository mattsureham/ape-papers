## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple policy question: when Congress extended SNAP work requirements to low-income adults ages 50–54, did those older adults actually end up working more? Using the 2023 Fiscal Responsibility Act as a national policy shock, the paper argues the answer is no: the reform appears to have reduced access to food assistance without generating measurable employment gains, implying that for this group work requirements function more as benefit cuts than as labor-market policy.

A busy economist should care because this is a live, high-salience policy debate with broader implications for how we think about conditionality in the safety net. If credible, the paper speaks not just to SNAP, but to the general question of whether work requirements induce work or merely screen people out.

### Does the paper articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Reasonably well, but not optimally. The introduction leads with the policy change and gets to the zero-employment-result quickly, which is good. But it muddies the message in two ways:

1. It frames the contribution partly as a methodological lesson about DDD pre-trends too early.
2. It introduces the paper’s coined term, “compliance gap,” before the reader is fully convinced that this is a substantive empirical object rather than branding.

The first two paragraphs should be more direct, sharper, and more world-facing. Right now the opening is good enough for a field journal; for AER, it needs to feel like a paper about a first-order fact in the world.

### The pitch the paper should have

Congress expanded SNAP work requirements to adults ages 50–54 on the premise that conditioning food assistance on work would increase employment and self-sufficiency. This paper asks whether that premise is true. Exploiting the 2023 federal age expansion and cross-state differences in whether the requirement actually bound, I find essentially no employment response among older adults: for this population, work requirements appear to reduce benefits without increasing work.

This matters beyond SNAP. Policymakers increasingly use work requirements across means-tested programs, but the core empirical question is whether these policies create employment or simply exclude vulnerable recipients. The 2023 SNAP expansion provides unusually policy-relevant evidence on that question for a group facing substantial labor-market barriers and for a provision Congress must soon decide whether to make permanent.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper’s contribution is to show, using the 2023 federal expansion of SNAP work requirements to ages 50–54, that extending work requirements to older low-income adults does not measurably raise formal employment and therefore operates primarily as a benefit cut rather than an employment policy.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest papers?

Partially, but not enough. The paper says prior work studies waiver transitions and administrative discretion while this paper uses a federal statutory age expansion. That is a real distinction. But at present the differentiation still sounds like “another quasi-experimental SNAP work requirement paper, but with cleaner variation.”

For AER, the paper needs to differentiate on substance, not only design:
- Prior work: work requirements reduce SNAP receipt.
- This paper: here is a nationally important episode where policymakers explicitly claimed employment gains, and for a newly covered, older population, those gains do not materialize.

That is stronger than “cleaner variation.”

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a gap in a literature?

It is mixed. The strongest parts are world-facing: “Do work requirements for older adults create jobs?” The weaker parts are literature-facing: “This contributes to literature questioning employment effects” and “contributes methodologically by showing pre-trends in DDD.”

The world-facing question is much stronger and should dominate. The paper should not sell itself primarily as “a DDD cautionary tale.” That is a side lesson, not the main event.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?

Yes, but only barely. Right now they could say either:
- “It shows the 2023 SNAP age expansion didn’t raise employment for older adults,” which is good; or
- “It’s a DDD paper on SNAP work requirements where the naive estimate disappears after detrending,” which is much less exciting.

The paper is perilously close to being remembered as the second rather than the first.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

A few concrete ways:

1. **Measure the first stage directly.**  
   The paper’s central claim is “benefit cuts, not jobs,” but it only observes jobs. It imports the benefit-cut margin from prior literature and policy reports. Strategically, this is the biggest limitation in the paper’s current contribution. If the author could directly show that SNAP participation or exits changed in this exact setting, then “compliance gap” becomes an actual result rather than an inferred rhetorical frame.

2. **Connect to broader outcomes of self-sufficiency.**  
   If employment is zero, what about earnings, UI-covered job attachment, job stability, or transitions across sectors? The paper has hires and earnings, which helps, but more direct markers of labor-market attachment would enlarge the scope.

3. **Lean into the age margin as the core novelty.**  
   The older-adult dimension is the most distinctive aspect. The paper should more forcefully argue that whether work requirements function differently at older ages is itself a first-order policy question, not a subgroup footnote.

4. **Reframe from SNAP-specific to conditionality in the safety net.**  
   The broader contribution is not “one more SNAP paper”; it is evidence that conditionality may fail precisely for populations with low employment elasticity and high compliance burdens.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Policymakers increasingly justify work requirements as tools for promoting self-sufficiency. In SNAP, the 2023 FRA expanded those requirements to adults ages 50–54, explicitly betting that conditioning benefits on work would increase employment.

### Tension

We know work requirements often reduce program participation, but it is much less clear whether they actually increase work—especially for older adults with health limitations and labor-market barriers. The core tension is between the policy’s stated goal (employment) and its likely operative mechanism (benefit loss through noncompliance or inability to satisfy requirements).

### Resolution

The paper finds essentially no employment response in formal-sector data, despite the policy’s likely bite on SNAP access. The implication is that the expansion did not achieve its labor-market objective.

### Implications

If true, policymakers should view this reform as a benefit-cutting device, not an employment program. More broadly, economists should update toward a view that work requirements may operate mainly through exclusion rather than labor supply, at least for older low-income adults.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the bones of one, but the arc is undercut by two things:

1. **The paper is too eager to tell a methods story.**  
   The “naive DDD says yes, event study says pre-trend, detrending says zero” sequence is analytically important, but it becomes the plot. That is not the best plot. The plot should be “Congress expanded work requirements to older adults to create employment; employment did not rise.”

2. **The “compliance gap” outruns the evidence currently shown.**  
   Since the paper does not directly observe SNAP exits here, the narrative resolution is a bit overstated. It can say “consistent with a large compliance gap” more comfortably than “the compliance gap appears nearly complete,” unless it adds direct program-response evidence.

So: not a collection of unrelated results, but still somewhat a paper with a strong result looking for the biggest story. The right story is conditionality versus self-sufficiency, not DDD diagnostics.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?

“Congress expanded SNAP work requirements to adults in their early 50s, and the paper finds basically zero employment gain.”

That is the fact.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

They would **lean in**, because the policy is salient and the result is intelligible. This is exactly the sort of finding economists can immediately map into larger debates over welfare design.

But then they would ask the obvious next question.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

“Okay, if jobs didn’t rise, did SNAP receipt actually fall in this setting?”

And that is where the paper is currently vulnerable. The author has a strong answer indirectly, via prior literature and policy estimates, but not directly in the paper’s own empirical setting. For a paper centered on a “compliance gap,” that missing direct link is strategically costly.

### Is the null result itself interesting?

Yes—conditionally. This is not a failed experiment; it is a highly policy-relevant null. The paper does a decent job explaining why a well-measured zero matters. The confidence interval argument is useful, and the policy sunset sharpens relevance.

Still, to make the null feel important rather than anticlimactic, the author should emphasize:
- the policy was explicitly sold as an employment intervention;
- this was a major federal reform, not a local pilot;
- the paper can rule out even modest positive effects; and
- the most plausible alternative mechanism is exclusion from benefits, not increased work.

That last point again would be much stronger with direct first-stage evidence.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

This is not mainly a competence problem. The paper is readable, topical, and has a clean central empirical takeaway. The gap is that the paper in current form feels more like a strong field-journal paper than an AER paper because the **story is narrower than the result could support**.

### What is the main gap?

Mostly:
- **a framing problem**, and
- **a scope problem**.

Less so a novelty problem. The question is not wholly new, but this particular federal expansion and age margin are new enough. The problem is that the paper has not yet turned that into a sufficiently broad and consequential statement.

### More specifically

1. **Framing problem**
   The paper is framed too much as:
   - a paper about SNAP ABAWD rules, and
   - a paper about fixing a misleading DDD estimate.

   It should be framed as:
   - a paper about whether work requirements generate employment or simply ration benefits.

2. **Scope problem**
   The claim “benefit cuts, not jobs” is bigger than the evidence shown because the paper only directly measures the “not jobs” part. To make the title-level claim fully persuasive, the paper needs direct evidence on benefit loss or at least much tighter linkage to it in the same setting.

3. **Ambition problem**
   The paper is a bit too safe in what it chooses to observe. Employment alone is not enough to carry the broader political and economic interpretation the author wants.

### Single most impactful piece of advice

**Add direct evidence on SNAP participation loss in the same policy episode, so the paper can truly show the wedge between benefit loss and employment gain rather than inferring half of that wedge from other studies.**

That is the one change that would most increase the paper’s claim to AER-level importance. If the author can’t do that, then the paper should narrow its rhetoric: it can confidently say the expansion did not raise formal employment, and only more cautiously say this implies work requirements likely acted as benefit cuts.
