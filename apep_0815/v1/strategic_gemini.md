---

### 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper exploits the 2023 Fiscal Responsibility Act (FRA) to test whether expanding SNAP work requirements to older adults (aged 50-54) actually increases employment. Using a triple-difference design, the author finds that while a naive estimate suggests employment gains, these are entirely explained by pre-existing trends; the true causal effect on employment is a precise zero. This creates a "compliance gap" where half a million people lose benefits without gaining jobs, suggesting the policy functions purely as a transfer cut rather than a labor supply incentive.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this clearly. The first two paragraphs of the current draft are excellent. They move from the policy change (FRA 2023) to the economic mechanism (work requirements as incentives vs. screening) to the headline result (zero employment effect). No rewrite of the pitch is necessary.

---

### 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Statement:** The paper provides a "precisely estimated zero" for the employment response to SNAP work requirements for older adults, demonstrating that the policy’s only measurable effect is a reduction in the social safety net.

*   **Differentiation:** It differentiates itself from Gray et al. (2023) and Harris (2021) by focusing on a specific, high-barrier demographic (older adults) and exploiting a very recent, clean statutory change rather than historical administrative variation.
*   **Question vs. Literature:** It frames itself as answering a question about the **world** (specifically, the efficacy of the FRA 2023). This is a strength.
*   **Clarity:** A smart economist would understand the "Compliance Gap" immediately. It is not "just another DiD"; it is a "cautionary DDD" that uses a methodological lesson to debunk a politically salient "finding."
*   **Bigger Contribution:** To make this bigger, the paper needs to bridge the gap to **fiscal externalities**. If these people lose SNAP but don't work, do they show up in ERs? Do they apply for SSI/SSDI? The paper hints at this in the discussion, but bringing even suggestive data on other safety net interactions would move this from a "labor supply paper" to a "public finance masterpiece."

---

### 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

*   **Neighbors:** Gray et al. (2023, *AEJ: Policy*), Deshpande and Li (2019, *AEJ: Policy*), Bitler et al. (2006, *AER*), and the methodological work of Rambachan and Roth (2023).
*   **Positioning:** It should position itself as the "stress test" of work requirement theory. By looking at 50–54-year-olds (a group with higher health barriers), it tests the *limits* of labor supply elasticity to benefit sanctions.
*   **Missing Conversations:** The paper should speak more to the **Political Economy** of "sunset provisions." The FRA sunsets in 2030; this paper should frame itself as the "evidence-based audit" for that specific legislative deadline.
*   **Unexpected Connection:** Connecting to the literature on **"Ordeal Mechanisms"** (e.g., Kleven and Kopczuk) would be smart. Is this a "good" ordeal that screens out the non-needy, or a "bad" ordeal that just punishes the vulnerable?

---

### 4. NARRATIVE ARC

*   **Setup:** Proponents of work requirements claim they promote self-sufficiency.
*   **Tension:** Older adults have higher health barriers; does the "nudge" of a benefit cut actually overcome the "friction" of age discrimination and chronic pain?
*   **Resolution:** No. A naive look says "yes," but rigorous de-trending shows "no."
*   **Implications:** The policy fails its stated goal. It is a tax on the poor, not a ladder to the middle class.

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong. The "tension" is particularly well-handled by showing the spurious naive result first—this creates a "mini-mystery" for the reader to solve.

---

### 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

*   **The Lead Fact:** "Congress expanded work requirements to 500,000 older people to get them jobs, but the actual number of jobs created was effectively zero."
*   **Reaction:** People will lean in. This is a "Policy Fail" story, which is always more provocative than a "Policy Success" story.
*   **Follow-up:** "So where did those 500,000 people go? Are they starving, or did they just move to other programs?" This confirms that the "So What" is high, but the "What next?" needs more data.

---

### 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

*   **QWI Limitations:** The 45-54 age bin dilution (Section 4.4) is a significant hurdle. The author handles it with a "rescaling" argument, but this needs to be more prominent. A table showing the ACS age distribution to justify the "divide by 2" logic is essential.
*   **Front-loading:** The paper is well-front-loaded.
*   **Appendix:** The methodological lesson in Section 6 (the "cautionary tale" about DDD) is interesting but potentially distracting. I would move the heavy methodological preaching to a shorter "Technical Note" or appendix to keep the focus on the SNAP results.

---

### 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The gap between this and an AER publication is **Scope and Mechanisms**. 

Currently, it is a very clean "No" to a policy question. To be AER, it needs to explore the "Why" and the "Where." 
1.  **Why:** Can the author use the QWI to look at different industries? Do we see a zero in *all* sectors, or do we see a shift from high-physical-demand to low-physical-demand jobs that nets to zero?
2.  **Where:** Does the zero effect vary by local labor market tightness? 

**Single most impactful piece of advice:** Use the 2030 sunset provision as your "Hook"—frame the entire paper as a "Cost-Benefit Analysis for the 2030 Reauthorization," and try to pull in any data (even if imperfect) on Medicaid or Disability applications to show where the "Compliance Gap" spills over.
