## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple policy question: when Congress extended SNAP work requirements to adults ages 50–54 in 2023, did those older adults actually work more? Using the federal age expansion and cross-state variation in whether the requirement was actually enforced, the paper’s headline claim is no: the policy appears to reduce access to food assistance without generating measurable employment gains.

A busy economist should care because this is a clean test of a central premise behind work requirements in the safety net. If the policy does not increase employment even in the short run, then its practical function is redistribution-by-exclusion, not labor supply encouragement.

### Does the paper articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Reasonably well, but not sharply enough. The current opening is competent and policy-relevant, but it spends too much time on the population’s health profile and too little on the big economic stakes: this is a test of whether work requirements are labor-market policy or benefit cuts. The phrase “compliance gap” is potentially useful, but right now it reads more like branding than a concept the reader immediately needs.

The first two paragraphs should do three things more forcefully:
1. Start with the broad question economists care about: do work requirements induce work?
2. Explain why this episode is unusually informative.
3. State the answer immediately and plainly.

### The pitch the paper should have

“Do work requirements in the safety net actually increase employment, or do they mainly reduce program receipt? This paper studies the 2023 federal expansion of SNAP work requirements to adults ages 50–54 and finds that, in states where the rule was enforced, formal employment did not rise. The implication is stark: for older adults, SNAP work requirements appear to operate primarily as benefit cuts rather than as effective employment policy.”

That is the paper’s story. Everything else should serve it.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper provides evidence from the 2023 federal SNAP age expansion that extending work requirements to adults ages 50–54 produced essentially no formal employment response, implying that the policy mainly reduced eligibility rather than increased work.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?

Not yet clearly enough. The paper says prior work finds limited employment effects of SNAP work requirements and substantial reductions in participation, and then says its contribution is “cleaner variation” from a federal statutory change. That is fine, but it is still a second-order contribution unless the author makes clearer why this episode materially changes what we know.

Right now the differentiation is something like: “another paper showing SNAP work requirements do not do much, but in a cleaner setting and for older adults.” That is publishable in a field journal. It is not automatically AER.

To rise above that, the paper needs to press one of two distinctions much harder:

- **Population distinction:** older adults are the crucial margin, and the paper reveals that work requirements are especially mismatched to populations with health limitations and lower job-finding rates.
- **Policy-design distinction:** this is not just another SNAP paper; it is a test of whether statutory work requirements, when expanded by Congress at the federal level, function as employment policy at all.

At present it gestures at both, but neither is fully developed.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a gap in a literature?

Mostly about the world early on, which is good. But then it slides into “cleaner variation than prior work” and “extends warnings about pre-trends to DDD,” which sounds like a literature gap. The stronger paper is the world paper: **What do work requirements actually do?** The methodological point should be subordinate, not coequal.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?

They could probably say: “It studies the 2023 SNAP work-requirement expansion for older adults and finds no employment effect once you account for trends.”

That is decent. But they might also say: “It’s another quasi-experimental SNAP work-requirements paper with a null.” That is the danger.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Be specific:

- **Better outcome framing:** The paper’s current main outcome is formal employment in QWI. That is sensible, but narrow. The contribution gets bigger if the paper can directly connect the employment null to actual SNAP disenrollment or benefit loss in the same design, rather than importing the first stage from other papers. Right now “compliance gap” is partly asserted, not directly shown.
- **Mechanism through margin of adjustment:** If the paper could distinguish whether people comply through paperwork exemptions, churn, informal work, or noncompliance leading to exit, the story becomes much more informative.
- **Broader conceptual framing:** Position this as evidence on the economics of conditionality in social insurance, not just on SNAP’s ABAWD rules. Then the paper speaks to work requirements in Medicaid, TANF-like redesigns, and broader debates over screening versus incentive effects.
- **Heterogeneity that matters substantively:** The older-adult angle could be much bigger if the paper showed why this population is different—e.g., stronger nulls in low-employment states, weaker labor markets, or places with older-worker barriers. Even one compelling heterogeneity dimension could turn a narrow null into a broader lesson about when work requirements fail.

The biggest missing piece is that the paper wants to claim “benefit cuts, not jobs,” but only directly measures the “not jobs” side.

- Potentially the emerging literature on **Medicaid work requirements** and administrative burdens, even if not currently cited.

### How should the paper position itself relative to those neighbors?

Build on them, not attack them. This is not a paper overturning the existing literature; if anything, it reinforces a growing consensus that work requirements often reduce enrollment more than they increase employment. The paper’s angle should be:

- prior evidence mostly studies waiver changes, administrative reinstatements, or younger populations;
- this paper studies a salient federal statutory expansion to older adults;
- the result generalizes and sharpens the case that work requirements are weak labor-market instruments.

That is a “build and sharpen” posture, not a frontal challenge.

### Is the paper currently positioned too narrowly or too broadly?

Slightly too narrowly in substance and slightly too broadly in rhetoric.

- **Too narrow in substance:** It is a paper about one SNAP provision, one age group, one labor-market dataset, six post quarters.
- **Too broad in rhetoric:** It sometimes sounds like it has settled the general economics of work requirements, and the phrase “benefit cuts, not jobs” overstates what is directly shown.

The right balance is: this is a clean, policy-relevant test of a central mechanism in a major federal program, with implications for the broader debate over conditionality.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?

Two omissions stand out:

1. **Administrative burden / take-up / program compliance** literature.  
   The paper’s “compliance gap” is really a version of a well-known issue: conditions can screen people out without changing underlying behavior. It should engage that literature more directly.

2. **Medicaid work requirements / health insurance conditionality** literature.  
   The broader debate is not just SNAP. There is a cross-program conversation about whether work requirements produce employment gains or simply reduce access. The paper should join that conversation.

Potentially also:
- labor supply effects of transfer program conditions versus sanctions,
- older-worker employment frictions,
- the economics of tagging and screening in social policy.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?

Not quite. It is currently having a conversation with the SNAP ABAWD literature plus a side conversation with DiD methods. The more impactful conversation is: **what are work requirements for?** Are they incentive devices, targeting devices, or political devices? That broader frame is much more AER-friendly than “here is another clean estimate for SNAP ABAWD.”

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Policymakers justify work requirements as tools to increase employment and self-sufficiency. SNAP’s 2023 age expansion created a new test case by exposing older adults to the same requirements.

### Tension

The tension is that this population may be especially unlikely to respond with higher employment, yet policymakers still describe the policy as employment-promoting. Existing evidence suggests work requirements cut caseloads, but cleaner evidence on whether they create jobs—especially for older adults—is limited.

### Resolution

The paper finds that once one accounts for differential pre-trends, the employment response is essentially zero. Hires and earnings also do not move.

### Implications

The implication is that for this group, work requirements do not function as labor-market policy. They likely reduce assistance without improving employment outcomes, which matters for the 2030 sunset and for broader debates over conditioning benefits on work.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has one, but it is not yet fully disciplined. The paper currently has **two competing stories**:

1. a substantive story about work requirements for older adults; and  
2. a methodological story about how DDD can be misleading when there are pre-trends.

The second is useful but not strong enough to be the main story. Right now the paper risks feeling like “I found a positive result, then it went away after de-trending, and here is a lesson about event studies.” That is not an AER narrative.

The paper should more clearly tell this story:

- Society uses work requirements because it believes they create work.
- Congress just expanded one in a major federal program.
- This policy change gives us a chance to test that premise for an economically important margin: older low-income adults.
- The answer is no; the policy cuts support without moving employment.
- Therefore, economists and policymakers should rethink the function of work requirements in the safety net.

That is a coherent narrative. The DDD pre-trend issue belongs inside the resolution, not as a parallel paper.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?

“Congress expanded SNAP work requirements to about half a million older adults, and the paper says the employment effect was basically zero.”

That is a good opening fact.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

They would lean in initially, because the policy is salient and the result is intuitive but important. But the interest will last only if the presenter can answer the next question convincingly.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Almost certainly: **“Okay, if employment didn’t rise, what happened instead? Did people actually lose benefits?”**

That is the paper’s core strategic problem. Its title and framing promise a comparison between benefit cuts and jobs, but it directly estimates only the jobs part. The “benefit cuts” part is inferred from outside evidence. That weakens the payoff.

### Is the null result itself interesting?

Yes, potentially very much so. A well-identified null on a major federal policy can be highly informative, especially when the policy’s political justification is precisely employment. The paper does a decent job arguing that the null is well-powered and policy-relevant.

But to make the null feel consequential rather than deflationary, the author must show more forcefully that this is not a failed attempt to find an effect. It is a direct test of the policy’s stated mechanism. The null is the result.

Still, the paper needs to better justify why six post-treatment quarters are enough to evaluate that mechanism, or else frame the result as a short-run employment null rather than a total assessment.

- answer a much bigger question,
- reshape a literature,

This paper is not there yet.

**Either directly document the policy’s effect on SNAP participation/benefit loss for the same treated population, or retitle and reframe the paper around the narrower but defensible claim that the 2023 SNAP work-requirement expansion did not increase employment among older adults.**

That is the fork in the road. As written, the paper promises more than it directly shows.
