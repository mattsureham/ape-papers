**Idea Fidelity**

The submitted paper only partially pursues the original manifest. The manifest proposed exploiting firm-level variation in pre-GIPP price-walking intensity as a continuous treatment to separate the waterbed effect from economy-wide claims inflation. Instead, the paper estimates a line-of-business difference-in-differences that treats entire product lines (motor liability, motor other, property) as binary “treated” groups relative to other non-life lines. The continuous treatment logic, which was intended to exploit cross-insurer heterogeneity in price-walking behavior, is absent. Consequently, the paper does not capitalize on the manifest’s central identification insight and misses the opportunity to isolate the GIPP effect from confounding claims-cost dynamics at the firm level. 

**Summary**

The paper studies the FCA’s January 2022 ban on insurance price-walking (GIPP) by comparing treated lines (motor and property insurance) to other non-life lines using Bank of England aggregate data and firm-level FCA value measures. The author finds no aggregate premium waterbed but documents a motor-specific increase in the loss ratio accompanied by falls in claims frequency and rises in complaints, which is interpreted as “claims compression,” while property insurance exhibits a textbook waterbed effect. Firm-level data are used to argue that claims-handling responses shifted surplus extraction once the pricing margin was constrained.

**Essential Points**

1. **Credibility of the DID comparison.** The identifying variation comes from treating entire insurance lines as treated versus controls. However, motor and property differ markedly from other lines (market size, competition, claim volatility, regulatory exposure, COVID sensitivity). The paper should show more than a placebo and event-study; it needs to demonstrate that pre-existing trends in motor/property were comparable to controls not just in aggregate totals but also in the components of interest (premiums and loss ratios). In particular, the property result is flagged as sensitive to pre-trends and control group choice. Without additional diagnostics (e.g., invariance tests using alternative outcomes, synthetic controls, or matching on historical trends), the causal interpretation of the property effect is weak. Given that the central policy conclusion hinges on cross-market heterogeneity, this is a critical issue.

2. **Distinguishing pricing versus composition effects in aggregate data.** Net written premiums combine intensive (price per policy) and extensive (policies written) margins. Responding to GIPP could involve insurers reducing policy count in treated lines (e.g., by exiting markets or tightening underwriting) rather than raising prices. The paper acknowledges this but offers no empirical check. For example, could motor premium flatness stem from reduced policy volume, allowing premium per policy to rise while total NWP stays constant? The loss-ratio spike is consistent with tighter underwriting as well as claims compression. The absence of policy counts or premiums-per-policy data weakens the claim that pricing was constrained by competition and that only claims margins adjusted. The authors need to either show stability in policy volumes or acknowledge and quantify the ambiguity.

3. **Interpretation of firm-level claims results.** The firm-level evidence uses band midpoints to approximate continuous outcomes; while understandable, this introduces measurement error. The magnitudes reported (e.g., −7.75 percentage points in claims frequency) are large and drive the claims-compression narrative. Yet no discussion is provided on how shifts in product mix or reporting practices across firms could influence these banded measures. Moreover, the complaint increase is presented as evidence of “friction” in claims handling, but alternative explanations (e.g., firms aggressively steering customers to non-GIPP products) are not ruled out. Since these firm-level results underpin the policy implications about “complaint displacement,” the authors must better justify the measurement strategy (maybe via robustness using only firms/products consistently reporting a given band) or triangulate using additional data (e.g., complaints from FCA Aggregate Complaints data mentioned in the manifest).

**Suggestions**

1. **Revisit identification strategy to align with the manifest’s continuous-treatment idea.** The paper would be far more compelling if it leveraged the available firm-level pre-GIPP price-walking metrics to construct a treatment intensity variable. Even if the line-level DID remains the primary analysis, incorporating firm-level heterogeneity would allow the author to test the key insight: firms that walked prices aggressively pre-GIPP should show larger post-GIPP adjustments, whereas firms that were already near parity should be unaffected. This would also address concerns about claims-cost inflation affecting all firms equally. A triple-difference specification (firm × line × time) or interaction of the line-level treatment with pre-GIPP renewal-to-new price ratios could serve this purpose.

2. **Provide richer diagnostics for parallel trends and common shocks.** In addition to reporting event study coefficients, the author could estimate the model on placebo “pseudo-treated” lines that share structural similarities with the treated lines (e.g., large-volume lines exposed to high claims volatility) to see if similar effects appear. Another constructive step is to decompose the loss ratio into claims incurred and premiums earned separately; if the premium component is unaffected while claims jump, that lends plausibility to the claims-compression story. Displaying weighted pre-trends (e.g., synthetic control) for motor and property individually would also reassure readers.

3. **Disentangle price versus quantity effects in the Bank of England data.** If the raw data lack policy counts, consider using publicly available aggregates (e.g., policy counts from FCA regulatory returns or published sectoral statistics) to approximate whether the number of policies changed post-GIPP. Alternatively, the author could reframe the narrative to clarify that the estimates apply to aggregate revenue, not price per policy. Explicitly acknowledging and, if possible, quantifying how much of the observed changes could be due to volume shifts would make the claims more transparent.

4. **Strengthen the firm-level claims analysis.** The current regressions only compare high-GIPP products to others within firms, but they do not account for possible product reclassification, measurement error from midpoints, or differential reporting over time. The author could (a) show that the set of firms reporting banded measures is stable across years, (b) re-estimate the models using only firms present in all years or only products that consistently fall into the same bands pre-treatment, and (c) consider transforming the band variables into ordered categories with ordinal regressions, which might better reflect the nature of the data. Additionally, linking the claims metrics to the BoE aggregate loss-ratio results via a decomposition (e.g., mapping the firm-level changes into implied loss-ratio shifts weighted by market share) would help tie the two pieces of evidence together.

5. **Address the complaint-displacement channel more directly.** The manifest mentioned FCA aggregate complaints data, but the current paper does not use them. Incorporating the aggregate complaints dataset (or other sources, such as FCA case studies) to show that complaints rose post-GIPP—especially in motor/home lines—would substantiate the claim that tighter claims handling drove the loss-ratio increase. If the aggregate complaints data are too coarse, at least report summary trends or descriptive statistics to show that complaints trended upward after the reform.

6. **Clarify policy implications with nuance.** The conclusion currently suggests that fairness regulation must monitor the entire surplus chain, but without quantifying the welfare effects, it is unclear whether the compression channel is “bad” for consumers (e.g., are claimants worse off, or are there efficiency gains from reduced moral hazard?). The author could draw on the conceptual framework to note that some tightening of claims (leading to more complaints) may reflect overzealous underwriting rather than deliberate consumer harm. Providing even rough calculations (e.g., the implied dollar loss from a 12 percentage point jump in the loss ratio given a motor market size) would help readers assess the magnitude of the regulatory tension.

7. **Expand related literature and contextualize GIPP within it.** The paper references key works but could better connect to recent papers on fairness regulation, dynamic pricing, and regulatory arbitrage in insurance. For instance, mention work on loyalty penalties or consumer inertia in other regulated markets, and situate the GIPP reform among broader EU or international efforts to limit price discrimination. This would underscore the generalizability of the findings beyond the UK setting.

Overall, the paper tackles a timely and important question. With additional attention to the identification strategy, more detailed diagnostics, and tighter integration between the aggregate and firm-level evidence, the paper could make a strong contribution to the literature on insurance regulation and price discrimination.
