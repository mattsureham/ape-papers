Across French communes, places more exposed through car dependence were already trending toward the RN before carbon pricing began, suggesting that what looks like tax-induced backlash is largely the electoral expression of deeper rural and periurban decline.

That framing is cleaner, bigger, and more world-facing.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper’s contribution is to argue that the celebrated France case does not show that carbon taxation itself caused far-right electoral gains; instead, the apparent relationship is largely explained by pre-existing political realignment in car-dependent places.

Only partially. The paper names nearby literatures and says prior work documents opposition or coarser regional correlations, while this paper uses finer geographic data and emphasizes pre-trends. That is a legitimate distinction, but as currently framed it risks sounding like: “same question, smaller geography, added placebo.” That is not enough for AER-level distinctiveness unless the paper makes clearer that it overturns a central stylized fact or policy inference.

Right now the differentiation is methodological and empirical in a modest sense, not conceptual in a big sense. The paper needs to say more explicitly: prior literature and public debate infer that carbon pricing creates populist backlash; this paper shows that in the flagship case, the electoral evidence does not support that causal narrative. That is a more consequential claim than “we account for pre-trends.”

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a gap in a literature?

Mostly about the world, which is good. The strongest version is: “Do carbon taxes actually generate far-right voting?” The weaker version, which the paper occasionally slips into, is: “the literature has not tested pre-trends carefully enough.” The latter is referee bait, not editorial excitement.

AER wants the former.

### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what’s new?

They could, but with some hesitation. Best-case version: “This paper says the French carbon-tax/populism link is mostly a misreading of pre-existing rural RN trends.” Worst-case version: “It’s a commune-level reduced-form paper saying a famous correlation disappears once you look at pre-trends.”

Right now too many readers will land on the second summary.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Three possibilities:

1. **Make the object of interest broader than France.**  
   Frame the paper as a test of a general proposition in climate politics: whether carbon pricing creates geographically concentrated right-populist electoral backlash in exposed places. France is the most salient case, not just a case.

2. **Lean harder into belief revision.**  
   The paper should explicitly say what widely held belief changes if its findings are right. Not “the correlation is confounded,” but “the canonical example used to argue carbon pricing is politically toxic does not, in fact, demonstrate that toxicity.”

3. **Strengthen the mechanism/framing around political channels rather than only vote shares.**  
   The 2022 result is potentially very interesting because it suggests the politics may run through movement mobilization, salience, or elite framing rather than direct pocketbook exposure. If the authors can credibly build that distinction into the narrative, the paper becomes much more ambitious conceptually: not “no effect,” but “the political pathway is indirect and contingent.”

If I had to choose one enlargement: the paper should pivot from “we debunk a France narrative” to “we distinguish direct policy backlash from backlash mediated by broader place-based decline and later political mobilization.”

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

There is a widely accepted narrative that France’s carbon tax raised fuel costs for car-dependent households, triggered anger in the periphery, and helped fuel the electoral rise of the far right.

### Tension

The places most exposed to fuel costs are also the places already experiencing longer-run economic and political marginalization. So the same geography that makes the backlash story plausible also makes it hard to know whether the tax caused the voting shift.

### Resolution

Using pre-tax car dependence and commune-level electoral changes, the paper finds that more car-dependent places did not experience larger RN gains from 2012 to 2017; if anything they saw smaller gains, and these places were already trending RN before the tax existed.

### Implications

The canonical case for “carbon taxes cause populism” is weaker than commonly claimed. The political problem may be less the direct incidence of carbon pricing and more the underlying distress of peripheral places and the later mobilization of that distress.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the ingredients of one, but the arc is not fully disciplined. Right now the paper is caught between two stories:

1. **Debunking story:** carbon tax did not cause Le Pen.
2. **Nuance story:** maybe the gilets jaunes later translated grievance into RN voting by 2022.

Those two stories can coexist, but the paper has not fully integrated them. As written, the 2022 result feels a bit tacked on—interesting, but narratively destabilizing. It creates a “well maybe it did eventually matter” coda that weakens the force of the main message unless handled carefully.

The story it should be telling is:

- The direct ballot-box effect during the tax-escalation period is not what conventional wisdom claims.
- The core cleavage predates the tax.
- Later political mobilization may have transformed that underlying grievance into electoral gain, but that is a different mechanism from the simple incidence story.

That is a coherent and much more interesting narrative.

Those are exactly the questions the current paper raises but does not fully answer. That is okay, but it means the framing should anticipate them and position the paper as clarifying one mechanism rather than closing the case on all climate backlash.

### If the findings are null or modest, is the null itself interesting?

Yes—because the null challenges a high-profile narrative that is repeatedly invoked in policy debates. This is not a failed experiment. But the paper needs to sell the null more carefully as **belief-revising** rather than merely “we didn’t find the expected sign.”

The key is to emphasize that this is the canonical case people cite when arguing carbon pricing is politically toxic. Showing that the canonical case does not actually establish the claim is important.
