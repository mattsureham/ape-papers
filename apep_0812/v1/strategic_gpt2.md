## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a timely, policy-relevant question: did France’s rising carbon tax help drive support for the far right by disproportionately hurting car-dependent places? Using commune-level variation in pre-tax car dependence, the paper argues no: the places most exposed to higher fuel costs were already trending toward Le Pen before the tax, and between 2012 and 2017 they did not become more pro-RN because of the tax.

A busy economist should care because the paper is taking on one of the most widely repeated claims in the political economy of climate policy: that carbon pricing mechanically produces populist backlash. If that claim is wrong, the implications for climate-policy feasibility are important.

**Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Mostly yes, but not as sharply as it could. The current opening is vivid and topical, but it spends too much of its capital on the protest narrative before crystallizing the broader question. The introduction should get to the world-level question faster: *Do carbon taxes actually create far-right electoral backlash, or do they merely appear to because they fall on places already on that trajectory?*

**What the first two paragraphs should say instead:**

> Carbon taxes are often said to be politically self-defeating: by raising fuel costs for car-dependent households, they are believed to fuel support for anti-system and far-right parties. France is the canonical case. The rise of the *gilets jaunes* and Marine Le Pen has become exhibit A in the argument that ambitious climate policy triggers populist backlash.
>
> This paper tests that claim directly. Using commune-level variation in pre-tax car dependence in France, I ask whether places more exposed to the 2014–2018 carbon-tax increase shifted more strongly toward the National Rally. They did not. If anything, they shifted less between 2012 and 2017, and the reason the tax looks politically toxic in cross section is that car-dependent places were already moving toward the far right before the tax existed.

That version gives the paper a cleaner claim: not “here is a French case study,” but “the canonical case for carbon-tax backlash may be misread.”

- regional papers correctly document **spatial correlation**

That is a more sophisticated and more credible stance. It avoids overstating what the paper disproves.

### Is the paper positioned too narrowly or too broadly?
Currently it is oddly both:
- **too narrow** in design terms: commune-level France, one tax, one party, one electoral window
- **too broad** in rhetorical ambition when it jumps to “political economy studies routinely…” or “climate policy worldwide”

The right balance is: **a sharp reinterpretation of the best-known case of carbon-tax backlash**. That is a clearly bounded but high-value audience.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?
It needs stronger engagement with:
- the literature distinguishing **protest participation** from **electoral behavior**
- the literature on **place-based political realignment** and rural/peripheral discontent
- possibly work on **salience, mobilization, and issue ownership**, because the *gilets jaunes* episode may have changed politics without changing immediate RN voting in 2017
- the literature on **policy feedbacks**: when do taxes create durable partisan effects versus transient protest?

Right now the paper is speaking mainly to carbon-tax acceptance and populism. It should also speak to political behavior and political geography.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?
Not quite. The most impactful conversation is not “does this specific French tax correlate with RN votes?” It is:

**When does climate policy produce protest, and when does it produce electoral realignment?**

That is the unexpected but fertile connection. France is the perfect setting because conventional wisdom conflates those two. The paper can matter more if it disentangles them.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
The world before this paper: carbon taxes are widely viewed as politically dangerous, and France is taken as the prime example because fuel-tax increases coincided with the *gilets jaunes* and the continued rise of Le Pen.

### Tension
The key tension is that the places most exposed to the tax are also places already experiencing long-run economic and political alienation. So when we observe more RN support in car-dependent France, are we seeing carbon-tax backlash or just the continuation of peripheral realignment?

### Resolution
The paper’s resolution is that the tax itself does not appear to explain extra RN gains between 2012 and 2017. The observed correlation is largely pre-existing; car-dependent communes were already moving toward the RN before carbon pricing began.

### Implications
The implications are potentially important:
- climate policy may not be as electorally toxic as commonly claimed
- protest backlash and electoral backlash are not the same thing
- analysts should be cautious in inferring policy effects from spatial correlations between exposure and voting

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
It has the ingredients, but the arc is not fully disciplined. The strongest version of the story is:

1. France is the canonical cautionary tale about carbon taxes.
2. But the canonical tale conflates grievance, geography, and electoral causality.
3. Once we ask the right question, the tax does not explain the 2017 RN shift.
4. Therefore the lesson for climate politics is subtler: what looks like tax backlash may often be underlying place-based resentment.

That is a strong story.

At present, however, the paper sometimes reads like a collection of regressions:
- main result
- pre-trend
- placebo
- 2022 sign reversal
- robustness

The **2022 result especially muddies the arc**. It introduces a second story—maybe the grievance mattered later through *gilets jaunes* mobilization—but without enough development to resolve it. Either make that delayed-effects story central and well-developed, or keep the paper tightly focused on debunking the “2014–2017 tax caused 2017 RN surge” claim.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?
I would say:  
**“The canonical French example of carbon-tax backlash may be wrong: the most car-dependent communes did not shift more toward Le Pen between 2012 and 2017, and those places were already moving RN before the tax existed.”**

That is a good lead fact. Economists will lean in.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
They would lean in initially, because the topic is salient and politically charged. France plus carbon taxes plus Le Pen is inherently attention-grabbing.

But the second question they will ask is decisive:  
**“So what did the tax do politically, if not that?”**

And here the paper currently has only a partial answer. It says:
- not 2017 RN voting
- maybe later grievance via *gilets jaunes*
- maybe protest rather than votes

That is interesting, but underdeveloped.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Most likely one of these:
- “If not RN vote share, did it affect protest, turnout, incumbent punishment, or issue salience?”
- “Are you showing no backlash, or just backlash that did not show up in 2017 first-round presidential votes?”
- “Is France actually evidence that climate policy creates protest but not durable electoral realignment?”

Those are stronger questions than the paper currently poses to itself. It should anticipate them.

### If the findings are null or modest, is the null itself interesting?
Yes—**if framed correctly**. A null against a famous narrative is interesting. “France is not the clean causal horror story people think it is” is a meaningful negative result.

But the paper must make the case that this is not merely a failed attempt to find an effect. It is a **revision of a powerful conventional wisdom**. The introduction should say this more directly and confidently.
