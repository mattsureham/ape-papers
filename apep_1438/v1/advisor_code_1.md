**Idea Fidelity**

The manuscript devotes itself to the core idea laid out in the manifest: linking the universe of 2019 mayoral campaign donors to subsequent procurement contracts and to municipal Saber 11 outcomes, and asking whether identifiable pay-to-play affects education quality. That promise is fulfilled, although the identification strategy departs from the manifest’s emphasis on close-mayor election RDDs. The authors instead implement a straightforward two-way fixed-effects DiD using a binary indicator for mayoral donor-contractor overlap. The exploration of donor-contractor intensity and heterogeneity across municipality size is novel, but the more ambitious strategy of exploiting electoral closeness to isolate exogenous treatment variation (as in Gulzar et al. 2022) is absent. This raises concerns about whether the key identifying leverage—near-random treatment within tight election margins—is being missed, especially given the extremely sparse treated sample (48 municipalities). The data linkage, outcome construction, and welfare question are handled faithfully.

**Summary**

The paper compiles a new panel that merges 2019 mayoral donor information, SECOP II procurement records, and municipal Saber 11 test scores. It defines treatment as whether an elected mayor’s donor list contains any individual who later appears as a contractor in 2020–2022, and it estimates a DiD with municipality and year fixed effects. The estimated effects on Saber 11 global, math, and reading scores are precisely zero, with point estimates within ±0.07 standard deviations.

**Essential Points**

1. **Identification without plausibly exogenous variation is weak.** Treatment is not randomized, and the choice of which mayor attracts donors who become contractors is likely correlated with unobserved municipal characteristics (such as political machines, fiscal capacity, or incumbent strength) that also affect education outcomes. Without exploiting the close-election RDD mentioned in the manifest, it is hard to interpret the DiD coefficient causally. The small treated sample (48/948 municipalities) exacerbates concerns that observable confounders drive the result.

2. **Magnitude interpretation lacks context.** The main effect is a negligible point estimate with a large clustered SE, but the paper leans on “within ±0.07 SD” to claim economic insignificance. Given the low power from only 48 treated units, failure to detect an effect may reflect imprecision rather than an innocuous null. More importantly, the heterogeneity analysis suggests a potentially large negative effect in small municipalities (−5.7 points, about 0.28 SD), albeit imprecise; this should not be waved away. The authors should quantify the minimum detectable effect given the treatment prevalence to assess whether the “null” is meaningful.

3. **Standard errors and inference are thin.** Clustering at the municipality level is appropriate, but with only ~48 treated municipalities and 942 controls, inference remains fragile. The event-study coefficients (Table 5) include implausibly large estimates in 2018–2022 with huge SEs, and yet the authors claim “pre-trends pass” without addressing whether those wide confidence intervals make the test uninformative. Given the low treatment variation, they should consider alternative inference strategies (e.g., wild-bootstrap, randomization inference) to demonstrate robustness.

**Suggestions**

1. **Revisit identification with close elections.** The manifest proposes a close-mayor RDD to harness quasi-random treatment assignment, which would be more credible than cross-sectional DiD with a rare treatment. Even if the existing linkage is sparse, one can still test whether within the subsample of close elections (say, margin < 5 pp), treated mayors (those whose donor pool overlaps with contractors) differ in pre-treatment characteristics or trends. If there are insufficient treated cases to run an RDD, the paper should transparently explain why (e.g., “in the close-election bandwidth only N treated cities exist, so RDD is infeasible despite the manifest plan”). Alternatively, the authors could use the close-election specification to instrument for donor-contractor links (first stage: probability a randomly selected close election cohort has such links).

2. **Clarify treatment definition and scope.** The treatment is defined broadly (“mayor whose donors became contractors anywhere in Colombia”), which mixes local capture with national-level relationships. The paper should report how many treated municipalities had donor-contractors operating within the same municipality versus outside it, and whether the latter are materially different. A more focused treatment—donors who received contracts from the mayor’s own municipality—would better capture the intended pay-to-play in local procurement. If data scarcity prevents that, discuss how the broader definition might dilute the effect and what assumptions justify it.

3. **Assess power and minimum detectable effects.** Compute the minimum effect size that the current sample can detect with 80% power (perhaps using a placebo treatment or simulations). This would help readers judge whether the null result is due to the effect truly being zero or simply too small to be picked up. Reporting the confidence interval width in standardized units is good; extend this by showing, for example, that detectable effects of 0.2 SD are ruled out at 95%, but 0.05 SD are not. That would also contextualize the heterogeneity result in small municipalities, where point estimates are large but imprecise.

4. **Strengthen inference in the event study.** The reported pre-period coefficients (e.g., 2018 estimate of 14.5 with SE 13.6) are noisy enough to render the parallel-trends test nearly meaningless. Present a plot of the entire event study with confidence intervals and consider imposing restrictions (e.g., exclude highly noisy early years or collapse them). Alternatively, aggregate the pre-periods to reduce noise (e.g., average 2014–2018) and test whether the average difference is zero. Transparency about how sensitive the results are to the choice of time frame would help build confidence.

5. **Expand robustness checks.** The binary treatment has a very low incidence. Try placebo tests where treatment is assigned randomly to 48 municipalities to verify that the empirical strategy would not systematically produce null effects. Also explore alternative specifications: (a) limited dependent variable (e.g., dummy for having any donor-contractor link within the municipality) and (b) controls for baseline municipal characteristics (e.g., pre-2020 spending, political party of mayor). If data permit, use a stacked event-study (sun and Abraham) to handle dynamic treatment effects even with rare treatment.

6. **Discuss covid-era disruptions more thoroughly.** The post-treatment window (2020–2022) overlaps with the pandemic, which dramatically disrupted schooling and testing. Although year fixed effects absorb average shocks, the pandemic may have affected testing participation differently across municipalities depending on their treatment status (e.g., municipalities with elite capture might have weaker public health responses). Provide evidence that the pandemic did not differentially affect treated municipalities (perhaps by showing similar variations in test participation rates or alternative outcomes).

7. **Consider alternative outcomes or longer horizons.** If test scores respond slowly, then focusing on cumulative scores may miss faster-moving channels (e.g., municipal education spending, teacher hiring, infrastructure completion). If feasible, examine intermediate outcomes available in administrative data, or extend the horizon beyond 2022 if data become available. At minimum, discuss how the limited window might bias the estimates toward zero and whether any lagged effects could be detectable in the future.

8. **Make heterogeneity analysis more systematic.** The split by municipality size suggests heterogeneous effects, but it should be accompanied by a formal test (e.g., interaction with size) and corrected for multiple comparisons if other splits are considered. Consider additional dimensions, such as political party alignment with the central government, baseline fiscal capacity, or urban/rural classification, to better characterize where the pipeline might matter most.

Overall, the paper offers a valuable data linkage and a transparent null result, but it should fortify its identification strategy, inference, and interpretation before asserting that the welfare cost of pay-to-play is negligible.
