### 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
The paper asks how trade liberalization—specifically the 2000 PNTR grant to China—affected the Black-White earnings gap in the U.S. manufacturing sector. It argues that because historical occupational segregation concentrated Black workers in industries with high "NTR gaps" (like textiles and apparel), the China shock acted as a massive, unintended regressive tax on Black labor. 

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this very clearly. The first two paragraphs successfully bridge 1930s tariff policy, 20th-century racial sorting, and 21st-century inequality. It is a "big idea" pitch.

### 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**One-sentence contribution:** The paper identifies the "China Shock" as a causal driver of the widening Black-White earnings gap, mediated by historical occupational segregation.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It is well-differentiated. While Pierce & Schott (2016) gave us the NTR gap and Autor et al. (2013) gave us the local labor market effects, this paper adds a demographic layer that has been surprisingly absent from the core "China Shock" canon.
*   **World vs. Literature:** It frames itself as answering a question about the **world** (the racial equity of trade policy), which is its strongest asset.
*   **Clarity:** A smart economist would say: "It’s the Pierce-Schott paper, but for the racial wage gap."
*   **Bigger Contribution:** To make this even bigger, the paper should lean harder into the "last-hired, first-fired" mechanism. If the author can show that trade shocks don't just hit industries, but specifically trigger the *liquidation* of the progress made by Black workers during the mid-century manufacturing boom, the narrative becomes much more powerful.

### 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
The paper sits at the intersection of International Trade (Pierce & Schott 2016, Autor et al. 2013) and Labor/Race (Bound & Freeman 1992, Neal & Johnson 1996). 

*   **Positioning:** It should **synthesize** these. Currently, it feels a bit like a Labor paper using a Trade instrument. It needs to speak more to the Trade literature's "Adjustment Costs" conversation (e.g., Dix-Carneiro). 
*   **Missing Conversations:** The paper should connect to the "Spatial Mismatch" literature. If these textile plants were the primary employers of Black men in certain Southern counties, their closure isn't just an earnings hit; it’s a total collapse of the local economic ladder.
*   **Audience:** The audience is broad, which is good for the AER.

### 4. NARRATIVE ARC
*   **Setup:** Black workers found a tenuous middle-class foothold in specific manufacturing subsectors following the Great Migration.
*   **Tension:** Trade policy (PNTR) was designed without regard for these sorting patterns, creating a "policy-induced" shock targeted at Black-heavy industries.
*   **Resolution:** Black workers were disproportionately purged from the manufacturing workforce (extensive margin), widening the racial gap.
*   **Implications:** Trade liberalization has heterogeneous costs that mirror historical injustices.

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong. It isn't just "results looking for a story"; the story is the heart of the paper.

### 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
At a dinner party, I’d lead with: *"Did you know the 2000 China trade deal widened the Black-White wage gap by 8% in exposed industries just because of where Black workers were sorted in the 1930s?"*
People would lean in. The follow-up would be: *"Where did those workers go? Did they move to the service sector or just disappear from the labor force?"* (The paper’s biggest weakness is its inability to track individuals, but the aggregate flow data is a good start.)

### 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
*   **Front-loading:** The paper is well-front-loaded.
*   **Appendices:** The "Asian worker placebo" is interesting but should be handled carefully; it's currently in the main text and works well to show it's not a "generic minority" effect.
*   **Mechanisms:** Section 5.3 (Mechanisms) is currently very lean. This needs to be the "meat" of the paper to satisfy AER referees. I want to see more on the *type* of jobs (production vs. non-production).

### 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The gap between this and a "Safe Reject" is **ambition regarding mechanisms.**
The science is there, but the AER wants to know *why* this happened. Is it just industry sorting? Or is it that within the *same* industry, firms fired Black workers first? The paper touches on "last-hired-first-fired," but needs to prove it.

**Single biggest piece of advice:** Move beyond the "Industry-level" story and use the QWI flows to show that even in the same county/industry cells, the *exit rate* for Black workers was higher than for White workers during the shock.
