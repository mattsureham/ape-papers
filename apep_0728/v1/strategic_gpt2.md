## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether the China PNTR shock widened racial inequality within U.S. manufacturing by hitting industries where Black workers were disproportionately employed. Using newly available race-by-industry administrative data, it argues that trade liberalization increased the Black-White earnings gap in manufacturing primarily by destroying Black employment in the most exposed sectors.

Why should a busy economist care? Because the paper tries to connect one of the central shocks in modern labor/trade economics—the China shock—to one of the central distributional facts in labor economics—the persistence of racial inequality. If that connection is real and well-framed, it is potentially important well beyond the trade literature.

Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs? Not quite. The first paragraph is strong and vivid. The second paragraph immediately drops into design language and identification mechanics, which is too early and too narrow. The paper should first make the world-level claim: a race-neutral national policy can have racially unequal effects when it interacts with historically segregated labor market structures.

### The pitch the paper should have

“Did the China shock widen racial inequality in the United States? This paper shows that it did within manufacturing: the industries most exposed to China’s PNTR accession were also the industries where Black workers had been concentrated by decades of occupational segregation. As a result, a policy that was formally race-neutral generated systematically unequal earnings and employment losses by race.

Using newly released administrative data that track earnings and employment by race, county, industry, and quarter, I show that PNTR increased the Black-White manufacturing earnings gap, with effects driven mainly by disproportionate job loss among Black workers in the most exposed sectors. The broader lesson is that the incidence of trade shocks depends not just on skills and geography, but also on the racial organization of work inherited from earlier labor market discrimination.”

That is the AER version of the introduction. The current version is too eager to reassure the reader about the design before fully establishing why the question matters.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### Contribution in one sentence

The paper’s contribution is to show that the China PNTR shock had racially heterogeneous effects within U.S. manufacturing, widening the Black-White earnings gap because Black workers were disproportionately concentrated in the most trade-exposed industries.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest papers?

Only partly. The paper says it extends the China shock literature to racial heterogeneity, but the differentiation is still a bit generic. Right now the contribution can sound like: “take a canonical trade shock and split the sample by race.” That is not enough. The more distinctive contribution is narrower and stronger:

- not just heterogeneity by race,
- but heterogeneity generated by **historical occupational segregation across industries**,
- measured with newly available administrative race-by-industry data,
- with the main margin being **employment destruction rather than only wage effects**.

That is the differentiated contribution. The paper should hammer that more explicitly.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a gap in a literature?

Mixed, but too much “literature gap” language. The strongest version is a world question: **Can race-neutral trade policy widen racial inequality because labor markets are already segregated?** That is much better than “the China shock literature has not looked at race.” AER papers lead with the world, not with an omitted subgroup analysis.

### Could a smart economist explain what is new after reading the intro?

At present, maybe, but not cleanly. A smart reader might say: “It’s a DiD/DDD on PNTR showing bigger adverse effects for Black workers.” That is not enough. You want them to say: “It shows that the China shock propagated through preexisting racial sorting across industries, so trade policy amplified racial inequality through the structure of labor market segregation.”

That latter sentence is a real contribution. The current intro only intermittently earns it.

### What would make the contribution bigger?

Several possibilities:

1. **Shift framing from ‘earnings gap’ to ‘racial incidence of trade shocks’.**  
   The current title and framing focus on the earnings gap, but the paper’s own mechanism discussion suggests the bigger idea is unequal exposure and unequal adjustment. That is broader and more important.

2. **Make employment and reallocation the core outcome, not a side mechanism.**  
   If the extensive margin is doing most of the work, that is the paper. “Trade widened racial inequality because Black workers were more likely to lose manufacturing jobs in exposed sectors” is bigger and cleaner than a somewhat muddled earnings-gap claim.

3. **Connect beyond manufacturing.**  
   Even if the data constrain the main analysis to manufacturing, the framing should speak to a broader question: when sectoral shocks hit a segregated labor market, who bears the costs? That would resonate with labor, trade, urban, and public economics audiences.

4. **Sharpen the mechanism as historical segregation rather than contemporaneous race heterogeneity.**  
   The paper should say this is not just race as a demographic category; it is race as an organizing feature of industrial allocation. That makes it less “another heterogeneity paper” and more “a paper about the transmission mechanism.”

5. **Potentially compare race to skill/geography rather than just Asians as placebo.**  
   Strategically, the paper would feel bigger if it positioned racial sorting as a first-order complement to the classic geography and education channels in the China shock literature.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

### Closest neighbors

The obvious neighbors are:

- **Autor, Dorn, and Hanson (2013)** on local labor market effects of import competition
- **Pierce and Schott (2016)** on PNTR and U.S. manufacturing employment
- **Acemoglu et al. (2016)** on aggregate labor market impacts of Chinese imports
- **Autor et al. (2014)** on adjustment and labor market frictions after trade shocks
- On racial inequality / structural labor market change:
  - **Bound and Freeman / Bound and Holzer-era work** on Black-White labor outcomes in manufacturing and deindustrialization
  - **Neal (1996)** on the role of industrial shifts in Black-White wage inequality
  - Potentially **Wilson (1987/1990s)** on disappearing work and the importance of manufacturing for Black workers

There may also be a nearby literature on:
- deindustrialization and Black workers,
- labor market polarization and race,
- place-based racial inequality,
- and newer work on distributional effects of trade across demographic groups.

### How should the paper position itself relative to those neighbors?

Mostly **build on**, not attack.

It should not imply that the China shock literature somehow missed race due to a conceptual blind spot. The better tone is:
- the trade literature established the shock,
- the racial inequality literature established that manufacturing mattered disproportionately for Black workers,
- this paper links the two and shows the mechanism connecting them.

That is a synthesis contribution, and it is respectable. Overclaiming novelty here would backfire.

### Is it currently positioned too narrowly or too broadly?

Oddly, both.

- **Too narrowly** in the econometric framing: too much attention to the DDD apparatus, fixed effects, placebo mechanics, and not enough to the conceptual contribution.
- **Too broadly** in some rhetorical lines like “trade policy is racial policy by accident.” That is a striking sentence, but the paper as currently framed does not yet support a claim of that breadth.

The right positioning is: a focused empirical paper with broader conceptual implications about how ostensibly race-neutral shocks interact with segregated labor markets.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?

It seems under-connected to at least four conversations:

1. **Structural change / deindustrialization and racial inequality**  
   This is arguably the most natural adjacent literature. The paper should be speaking to people who study the disappearance of middle-wage jobs and its unequal incidence.

2. **Segregation and labor market sorting**  
   Not only racial earnings-gap papers, but work on occupational segregation, industrial sorting, and differential exposure to shocks.

3. **Distributional incidence of trade policy**  
   The broader public finance/political economy framing: who bears the incidence of trade liberalization when factor markets are segmented?

4. **Race and local labor market adjustment**  
   There is likely relevant work on displacement, mobility constraints, discrimination, and post-displacement trajectories by race.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?

Not quite. It is currently having the conversation: “China shock, but by race.” The more impactful conversation is: **How do major economic shocks propagate through socially structured labor markets?** That is the unexpected but fruitful bridge. The paper should be less “subset analysis of PNTR” and more “a case study in how historical segregation shapes the incidence of economic change.”

That would make the paper matter to economists who are not trade economists.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Before this paper, we know the China shock hurt U.S. manufacturing, and we know Black workers historically depended heavily on manufacturing jobs for middle-class earnings. We also know labor markets were racially segmented across industries and occupations.

### Tension

What we do not know is whether these facts are linked: did the China shock merely reduce manufacturing overall, or did it systematically widen racial inequality because Black workers were concentrated in the most exposed segments of manufacturing? In other words, was the shock’s incidence racially uneven because the labor market was already segregated?

### Resolution

The paper’s answer is yes: more exposed industries saw a larger widening of Black-White earnings gaps, apparently driven mainly by disproportionate Black job loss and separation from those sectors.

### Implications

The implication is that economic shocks and policies can generate racial inequality even without race-specific rules, because they operate through inherited structures of segregation and unequal mobility. That matters for trade policy, adjustment assistance, and more generally for how economists think about incidence in segmented labor markets.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the ingredients, but not yet the discipline. Right now it feels like a paper with a strong opening paragraph, then a quick slide into specification-speak, then a pile of results with some interpretive instability. There is a story here, but the paper has not fully committed to it.

The biggest narrative weakness is that the main text oscillates between two stories:

1. **earnings-gap story**, and
2. **employment destruction / extensive-margin story**.

The latter is actually stronger and more intuitive. The paper should decide whether its real story is:
- “PNTR widened racial earnings gaps,” or
- “PNTR imposed larger labor market losses on Black manufacturing workers because of occupational segregation.”

The second is better. It naturally nests the first.

As written, some of the coefficient presentation muddies the narrative. When the “preferred” specification differs from the headline DDD and the sign changes across columns, the story feels fragile or under-controlled rather than inevitable. That is not a refereeing point; it is a storytelling point. A reader should not have to be walked through coefficient sign reversals in the main results section to understand the contribution.

### What story should it be telling?

This one:

- Manufacturing had become a key pathway to stable earnings for Black noncollege workers.
- Black workers were not evenly spread across manufacturing; they were concentrated in the industries most exposed to PNTR.
- PNTR therefore did not just reduce manufacturing jobs; it hit Black workers disproportionately.
- The main manifestation was job loss and separation, which then widened earnings inequality within manufacturing.
- More broadly, race-neutral policy can have racially unequal effects when labor markets are historically segregated.

That is a coherent AER-style story.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?

I would say: “The China PNTR shock appears to have widened the Black-White manufacturing gap because Black workers were disproportionately sorted into the industries that got hit hardest.”

That is the lead. Not the DDD. Not the standard errors. Not the new dataset. The substantive fact.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

Some would lean in. This is a real topic with broad appeal. Trade economists, labor economists, and economists interested in inequality will care. But they will lean in only if the paper sounds like it is telling them something genuinely new about the incidence of the China shock, rather than just reporting another demographic split.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Probably one of these:

- “Is this really about race per se, or just about industry sorting?”
- “How much of the effect is job loss versus wage changes?”
- “What happened to displaced Black workers afterward?”
- “Is this specific to Black workers because of segregation, or would you find analogous patterns by education, gender, or region?”
- “Does this change how we think about the aggregate incidence of trade policy?”

Those are good follow-up questions because they point to the paper’s real intellectual opportunity.

### If the findings are modest or null

The findings are not null, but the paper still needs to make clear why the magnitude changes beliefs. The current draft uses assertive language, but it does not yet fully calibrate why an economist should revise priors. The key is not just statistical significance; it is the claim that one of the major labor market shocks of the last 30 years also had a meaningful racial incidence that prior work underappreciated.

That is potentially interesting. The paper needs to sell it as such, more clearly and more soberly.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The gap is mostly a **framing and ambition problem**, with some **novelty risk**.

### Framing problem

The science may be competent, but the story is not yet presented at the right altitude. The paper is still framed as an application of a familiar shock to a new subgroup. That is not enough for AER. It needs to become a paper about how major economic shocks transmit through historically segregated labor market structures.

### Scope problem

Moderate. The paper has earnings, employment, hires, and separations, which is good. But it still feels confined to “within manufacturing.” That may be data-driven, but conceptually it needs to claim something larger about incidence and adjustment.

### Novelty problem

Real but not fatal. Many papers have already studied the China shock. The author must therefore show that this is not just a descriptive decomposition by race. The novelty lies in linking historical segregation to exposure and in using race-by-industry administrative data to do so. If that is made explicit and central, the paper has a better chance.

### Ambition problem

Yes. The paper is more ambitious in rhetoric than in conceptual architecture. It wants to say something big, but it still reads structurally like a careful field-journal paper: identify a shock, estimate a DDD, run placebos, list literatures. An AER paper needs a cleaner and more daring conceptual claim.

### Single most impactful piece of advice

**Reframe the paper from “PNTR widened the Black-White earnings gap” to “historical occupational segregation caused a race-neutral trade shock to have racially unequal labor-market incidence,” and make employment loss—not just earnings—the core manifestation of that claim.**

That one change would improve the title, introduction, narrative arc, literature positioning, and broad relevance all at once.
