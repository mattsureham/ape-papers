## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether the China PNTR shock widened racial inequality inside U.S. manufacturing, and argues that it did because Black workers were disproportionately concentrated in the industries most exposed to the post-2000 surge in Chinese import competition. A busy economist should care because the paper’s broader claim is not just “trade hurt manufacturing,” but that ostensibly race-neutral policy shocks can generate racial inequality when they hit an economy with pre-existing occupational segregation.

The paper does **not** articulate this pitch as clearly as it should in the first two paragraphs. The opening has the ingredients, but the introduction pivots almost immediately into design and identification language. That is too early. For an AER audience, the first two paragraphs should foreground the economic question and the broader lesson about how aggregate shocks propagate through stratified labor markets.

### The pitch the paper should have

“Do race-neutral trade shocks widen racial inequality? This paper studies whether granting China Permanent Normal Trade Relations in 2000 increased the Black-white earnings gap in U.S. manufacturing. The key economic idea is that large aggregate shocks do not hit an undifferentiated workforce: because Black workers were historically sorted into the manufacturing industries most exposed to Chinese import competition, PNTR may have transmitted through inherited occupational segregation and widened racial disparities.

Using newly available race-by-industry panel data from the QWI, I show that the China shock did not just reduce manufacturing employment overall; it disproportionately lowered Black workers’ earnings and employment within exposed manufacturing industries. The paper’s broader contribution is to show how trade policy can reshape racial inequality even without explicitly targeting race, because the incidence of adjustment depends on who was sorted where before the shock.”

That is the story. Only after that should the paper say, briefly, “I estimate this using variation in industry exposure from the Pierce-Schott NTR gap.”

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper claims that China’s PNTR widened the Black-white earnings gap in U.S. manufacturing by hitting industries with high import exposure where Black workers were disproportionately employed due to historical occupational segregation.

### Evaluation

#### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?

Only partially. Right now, the paper situates itself as:
1. “China shock, but by race”
2. “racial earnings gap, but with trade as a cause”
3. “here is a new race-by-industry dataset”

That is not yet sharp enough. A smart reader will immediately map this onto Pierce-Schott/Autor-Dorn-Hanson plus subgroup heterogeneity. The paper needs to distinguish itself more forcefully from:
- the canonical China shock papers on local labor-market effects,
- papers documenting differential incidence of labor-market shocks across demographic groups,
- papers on racial inequality in manufacturing decline/deindustrialization.

The real differentiation is **not** “I use a DDD” or “I use QWI released in 2023.” The real differentiation is: **pre-existing racial sorting across industries changes the incidence of trade shocks inside manufacturing, so trade policy can alter racial inequality through industrial composition.**

That is stronger and more memorable.

#### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or filling a gap in a LITERATURE?

At present, too much of the introduction is framed as filling a literature gap. The stronger framing is world-facing:

- Weak: “The China shock literature has not examined racial heterogeneity.”
- Strong: “When workers are segregated across industries, a race-neutral trade reform can widen racial inequality.”

That latter framing is AER-caliber because it generalizes beyond this episode.

#### Could a smart economist explain what is new after reading the introduction?

Not cleanly enough. Right now, many would summarize it as: “It’s another China-shock paper, this time on Black-white manufacturing earnings gaps.” That is too generic.

You want them to say:  
**“It shows that the incidence of import competition depends on racial sorting across industries, so trade shocks can mechanically become racial inequality shocks.”**

That is much better.

#### What would make the contribution bigger?

Most importantly: **raise the level of abstraction.** The paper should not just be about Black versus White manufacturing workers in 2000s America. It should be about **how aggregate shocks interact with pre-existing social stratification.**

More specifically, the contribution would become bigger if the paper did one or more of the following in its framing:

1. **Emphasize incidence, not just heterogeneity.**  
   The question is not merely whether effects differ by race, but whether racial sorting determines who bears adjustment costs from globalization.

2. **Show the reallocative consequence beyond manufacturing earnings.**  
   The current mechanism discussion leans on employment, hires, and separations. Strategically, the paper would feel bigger if it connected these losses to where displaced workers go: nonemployment, lower-paying sectors, local earnings declines, or persistent scarring. Without that, the paper risks feeling like a within-manufacturing accounting exercise.

3. **Frame occupational segregation as the core mechanism, not as a control variable in prose.**  
   The paper should be built around the proposition that historical segregation creates differential exposure to modern shocks.

4. **Connect to a broader class of shocks.**  
   Even if the empirical setting remains PNTR, the introduction should explicitly state that the mechanism applies to automation, energy transitions, place-based decline, and industrial policy. That broadens the paper’s conceptual reach.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

### Closest neighbors

The clearest neighbors are:

1. **Autor, Dorn, and Hanson (2013, AER)** on local labor market effects of Chinese import competition.
2. **Pierce and Schott (2016, AER)** on PNTR and U.S. manufacturing employment via trade policy uncertainty.
3. **Acemoglu et al. (2016, JPE)** on broader labor-market and welfare consequences of Chinese imports.
4. **Autor, Dorn, Hanson, and Song (2014, AER/Papers & Proceedings? broader adjustment-cost literature)** or related worker-adjustment papers on earnings losses and mobility frictions after shocks.
5. On racial inequality and manufacturing/deindustrialization, likely **Bound and Freeman/Bound and Holzer**, **Neal**, and **Wilson** are the right older anchors, though the paper likely needs more modern labor/race references.

Depending on what exists in the current literature, it may also be near work on:
- race and exposure to routine-task displacement,
- race and regional labor demand shocks,
- racial inequality in deindustrialization,
- distributional incidence of trade shocks across demographic groups.

### How should it position itself relative to those neighbors?

Mostly **build on** the China-shock literature, but **pivot** it. The right move is not to “attack” Autor-Dorn-Hanson or Pierce-Schott for overlooking race. That would feel cheap and historically unfair; those papers were not trying to answer that question. Instead:

- “The existing literature established that PNTR and Chinese import competition reduced U.S. manufacturing employment.”
- “This paper shows that those losses were not demographically neutral because workers were not evenly distributed across exposed industries.”
- “The missing margin is the interaction between product-market shocks and labor-market stratification.”

That is a constructive extension.

Relative to racial inequality literature, the paper should say:
- “The literature has studied discrimination, skills, deindustrialization, incarceration, and geography as drivers of racial inequality.”
- “This paper adds globalization as another force, operating through historical industrial sorting.”

### Is the paper positioned too narrowly or too broadly?

Right now it is oddly both:
- **Too narrow empirically**: “Black-white earnings in manufacturing after PNTR.”
- **Too broad rhetorically** in places: sweeping claims about trade policy as racial policy.

The solution is to make the framing broader in concept but disciplined in claim:
- broader concept: shocks interacting with segregation,
- narrower claim: this paper shows that mechanism in manufacturing during PNTR.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?

It seems under-engaged with at least three conversations:

1. **Distributional incidence of shocks across demographic groups**  
   There is a broad labor literature on heterogeneous effects by race, education, geography, and mobility constraints. The paper should speak more directly to that.

2. **Deindustrialization and racial inequality**  
   The paper gestures to Wilson and classic race-gap work, but it needs stronger integration with the literature on the decline of manufacturing as a pillar of Black middle-class employment.

3. **Segregation/sorting as economic structure**  
   The paper says “historical occupational segregation” but treats it almost as background context. There is a deeper literature on segregation, job ladders, networks, and industrial sorting that should be brought to the front.

Potentially also:
4. **Political economy / distributional consequences of globalization**  
   If the paper wants to matter beyond trade economists, it should connect to work on who bears the costs of liberalization and how those costs are mediated by institutions and historical inequality.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?

Not quite. It is currently having the conversation: “here is a racialized China-shock estimate.”

The more impactful conversation is:  
**“How do large aggregate shocks map into inequality when workers are segregated across sectors and places?”**

That is the right conversation for an AER audience.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Before this paper, economists know that PNTR/China trade exposure hurt U.S. manufacturing, and they know that Black and White workers were unevenly distributed across sectors due to historical occupational segregation.

### Tension

What is missing is whether these two facts interacted. Did globalization merely reduce manufacturing employment in the aggregate, or did it also widen racial inequality because the shock hit sectors where Black workers were disproportionately located?

### Resolution

The paper’s answer is yes: industries with larger PNTR exposure saw a larger widening in the Black-white earnings gap, with the mechanism operating largely through employment losses, lower hiring, and higher separations for Black workers.

### Implications

The implication is that trade policy can widen racial inequality even without explicit discrimination, because the incidence of economic shocks depends on pre-existing labor-market sorting. More broadly, policies and shocks should be evaluated not only by average effects but by who is structurally positioned to absorb them.

### Evaluation of arc

There **is** a narrative arc here, and it is a potentially strong one. But the paper does not yet execute it cleanly. The current manuscript often reads like:
- setup,
- then straight into specification,
- then a bundle of coefficient tables,
- then a discussion that states the broader lesson after the fact.

That is backwards. The broader lesson should be the spine of the paper from page 1.

At present, there is some risk that this is a **collection of results looking for a story**, especially because:
- the introduction overemphasizes design,
- the dataset itself is treated almost as a contribution,
- the results section spends energy explaining coefficient sign changes across specifications.

The story it should be telling is much simpler:

1. **Historical segregation sorted Black workers into specific manufacturing industries.**
2. **PNTR hit those industries harder.**
3. **Therefore a formally race-neutral trade reform widened racial inequality.**
4. **This reveals a general mechanism: shocks inherit the unequal structure of the economy they hit.**

That is the paper.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?

I would lead with something like:

**“The China PNTR shock did not just reduce U.S. manufacturing employment; it widened the Black-white earnings gap because Black workers were concentrated in the industries most exposed to Chinese import competition.”**

That is the most interesting fact.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

Economists would lean in **if** the paper is presented that way. Race plus trade plus historical sorting is genuinely interesting. But if presented as “a triple-difference paper using QWI to estimate Black-white earnings effects of PNTR,” they will reach for their phones.

The topic is strong enough. The current packaging undersells it.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Almost certainly:

**“Is this about within-job wages, job loss, or reallocation into worse jobs elsewhere?”**

And second:

**“Is this specific to Black workers because of occupational segregation, or just because they were in weaker labor markets more generally?”**

Strategically, those are exactly the questions the introduction should anticipate at a high level.

### If findings are modest or null

The findings are not null, but they are currently presented in a way that creates confusion because the “preferred” estimate differs in form from the race-level DDD and some sign patterns look awkward. Strategically, that undermines the headline. The paper needs one clean empirical object that readers can carry away.

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

### What would make it read better?

1. **Rewrite the introduction around the question, not the econometrics.**  
   The first two pages should be mostly economic motivation and headline findings. Right now, the design appears too soon and too mechanically.

2. **Front-load the main fact more aggressively.**  
   The introduction should state the result in intuitive terms once, clearly, and then move immediately to why it matters conceptually.

3. **Shorten or demote the explanation of specification sign reversals.**  
   Any paragraph that says “the coefficient changes sign across specifications because…” is dangerous in the main text unless absolutely necessary. It makes the paper feel messy. The paper should present one preferred estimand cleanly and move the algebraic interpretation issues elsewhere.

4. **The “new dataset” language should be toned down.**  
   New data can be useful, but “the dataset opens the door…” is not a top-journal contribution in itself. It reads like a data note. Keep it, but subordinate it.

5. **Discussion should do more than summarize.**  
   The discussion currently restates the findings and offers a broad implication. It should be sharper on what beliefs change:
   - trade shocks are not demographically neutral,
   - incidence depends on historical sorting,
   - analyses of adjustment should integrate labor-market stratification.

6. **Conclusion should be less slogan-forward and more economically disciplined.**  
   “Trade policy is racial policy by accident” is rhetorically effective but a bit too op-ed for the AER. A less sloganized version would land better: race-neutral policy can have race-specific incidence because of inherited sorting.

### Are there results buried that belong in the main text?

Conceptually, yes:
- anything that directly shows Black overrepresentation in the most exposed industries should be in the main text as a simple figure or table,
- any visual that links pre-PNTR racial industrial sorting to NTR-gap exposure would be extremely valuable and should probably appear very early.

That figure may be more important for strategic positioning than several regression tables.

### Is the paper front-loaded with the good stuff?

Not enough. The main intellectual contribution is front-loadable, but the paper instead front-loads setup plus identification talk. Readers should learn the central fact and broader takeaway by page 2.

### Is the conclusion adding value?

Some, but not enough. It currently adds rhetoric more than analytical value. The conclusion should explicitly generalize:
- this mechanism applies when any shock hits a segregated labor market,
- distributional analysis should incorporate demographic sorting,
- adjustment policy should be targeted where historical sorting concentrates exposure.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

There is a real paper here. The main gap between the current version and an AER-caliber paper is primarily a **framing and ambition problem**, with a secondary **scope problem**.

### What is the gap?

#### 1. Framing problem
The science is presented as if the contribution is “racial heterogeneity in a classic trade setting.” That is publishable somewhere, but AER wants the broader idea. The paper needs to argue that it reveals a general mechanism by which aggregate shocks translate into inequality through inherited labor-market structure.

#### 2. Ambition problem
The paper is competent but currently safe. It documents a subgroup effect in a well-studied setting. To feel like AER, it must make readers think they have learned something broader about economics, not just about this episode.

#### 3. Scope problem
The paper is close to being about incidence, but not quite there. It would feel bigger if it more clearly established whether the key effect is:
- within-manufacturing wage changes,
- disproportionate job loss,
- reallocation into worse sectors,
- or persistent earnings scars.

Even if the existing data constrain this, the framing should lean toward the broader incidence question.

#### 4. Novelty problem
Not fatal, but real. “China shock with race heterogeneity” is not by itself enough. “Trade shocks propagate through racial sorting” is much more novel.

### Single most impactful advice

**Reframe the paper around a general economic mechanism—race-neutral shocks widen racial inequality when workers are historically segregated across exposed industries—rather than around a subgroup estimate of the China shock.**

That one change would improve the title, introduction, literature review, result presentation, and conclusion all at once.
