## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether Medicare’s bed-count thresholds distort how large hospitals choose to be, and shows that they do—especially the 25-bed cutoff for Critical Access Hospitals, where an extraordinary share of rural hospitals pile up exactly at the eligibility limit. A busy economist should care because this is a vivid example of how reimbursement design shapes real productive capacity in health care, not just coding, billing, or spending.

The paper does articulate a pitch quickly, and the opening line is strong. But the first two paragraphs are still too “look at this spike” and not enough “why this matters for the organization of health care.” The current introduction also overinvests early in listing thresholds and underinvests in the broader question: how payment rules shape provider capacity and market structure.

### The pitch the paper should have in the first two paragraphs

“Medicare does not just pay hospitals differently; it induces them to organize themselves differently. In the United States, several major reimbursement programs create sharp bed-count thresholds, and hospitals appear to respond by choosing capacity to qualify for more favorable payment regimes. This paper asks how much these payment cliffs distort the hospital size distribution, and whether the 25-bed Critical Access Hospital rule is merely an administrative detail or a major force shaping rural hospital capacity.

Using the universe of Medicare hospital cost reports from 2010–2023, I show that the 25-bed threshold creates an enormous concentration of hospitals exactly at the cutoff, far larger than at the 50- and 100-bed thresholds elsewhere in Medicare. The core message is simple: reimbursement design has materially reshaped the physical capacity of U.S. hospitals, with the Critical Access Hospital program acting as a de facto cap on rural hospital size.”

That is the AER-worthy version of the story: not “here are three bunching estimates,” but “payment system design changes the real organization of production in health care.”

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper shows that Medicare’s bed-based reimbursement thresholds, especially the 25-bed Critical Access Hospital cutoff, substantially distort hospital capacity choices and thus the size distribution of U.S. hospitals.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?

Not yet clearly enough. The paper says “first unified bunching analysis” and “first comparative estimates,” but that is a literature-gap formulation, not a world-question formulation. The reader is left with the sense that the novelty is mainly packaging: applying bunching to hospital beds at three thresholds and adding a heaping adjustment. That is not nothing, but it is not by itself an AER-level contribution.

The paper needs to differentiate itself from at least four nearby conversations:

1. **Critical Access Hospital policy evaluations**: papers on whether CAH status affects costs, quality, closure risk, or access.
2. **Hospital responses to payment incentives**: work showing that provider reimbursement shapes service mix, coding, ownership, admissions, and investment.
3. **Bunching/notches literature**: studies using distributional responses to infer behavioral distortion around policy cutoffs.
4. **Organization of capacity / provider supply**: work on how regulation changes real capacity, not just expenditure.

Right now the paper mostly says: no one has done this exact multi-threshold bunching exercise. That is true but too incremental.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or filling a gap in a LITERATURE?

Mostly framed as filling a gap in a literature. That weakens it.

The stronger world question is: **To what extent do reimbursement thresholds function as hard capacity constraints in U.S. health care?** If that is the question, the paper becomes about the real allocation of hospital capacity under public payment design. That is more important than “this is the first unified bunching analysis.”

### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what’s new?

At present, they might say: “It’s a bunching paper showing hospitals stack up at 25 beds because of CAH.” That is understandable, but it still sounds like “another DiD/bunching paper about a threshold.”

The introduction needs to make the novelty more conceptual:
- not just that hospitals bunch,
- but that **Medicare appears to have engineered a durable hospital-size distribution**, especially in rural America.

That is a memorable claim.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Most importantly: connect the bunching fact to a first-order consequence economists care about. Right now the paper stops at the distributional distortion. To feel AER-sized, it needs one of the following:

1. **Capacity consequences**  
   Show that the bed cap affects admissions, occupancy, transfers, service lines, or local access—not just reported bed counts.

2. **Market-structure consequences**  
   Show that the threshold shapes which hospitals survive, convert, merge, or remain independent.

3. **Geographic welfare relevance**  
   Link the bunching to local hospital deserts, travel times, emergency access, or patient sorting.

4. **Theory/framing upgrade**  
   Explicitly frame this as a paper on “regulatory design and real capacity choice under public reimbursement,” not a descriptive threshold exercise.

If forced to choose one: the biggest upgrade would be to show that hospitals at 25 beds are not just reporting strategically but operating differently in ways that matter for patients or local markets.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Medicare contains several sharp payment thresholds tied to hospital bed counts. Policymakers designed these programs to support particular providers, especially rural hospitals.

### Tension

If payment rules jump discretely at specific bed counts, hospitals may choose capacity to satisfy the rules rather than local demand or efficient scale. Yet we do not know how much these thresholds actually shape the size distribution of hospitals, or whether the 25-bed CAH rule is a marginal administrative feature or a major structural force.

### Resolution

The paper finds enormous excess mass at 25 beds and much smaller responses at 50 and 100 beds. The 25-bed threshold appears to be a dominant organizing feature of rural hospital capacity.

### Implications

Medicare reimbursement design may function as a de facto cap on hospital scale in rural areas. That matters for policy design, for understanding hospital organization, and potentially for access and welfare.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the ingredients of one, but currently it reads more like a well-executed set of results than a fully shaped story. The narrative is strongest around the 25-bed CAH threshold. The 50- and 100-bed thresholds add comparators, but they do not feel equally essential to the paper’s central point. In fact, they may dilute the story unless they are explicitly used to sharpen the main claim.

### What story should it be telling?

Not “three thresholds, three estimates.”

It should be telling:
- Medicare created several bed-based incentives.
- Most of them matter somewhat.
- One of them—the 25-bed CAH rule—fundamentally reorganized rural hospital scale.
- Therefore, the design of provider reimbursement can directly shape real capacity in health care.

That is a story. Right now the paper often sounds like a ranking exercise: 25 is bigger than 50 and 100. True, but ranking alone is not enough.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Probably one of these:
1. “Is this just reporting or real capacity?”
2. “Does it matter for patients—access, quality, transfers, closures?”
3. “Why didn’t policymakers smooth the payment rule?”
4. “Is the hospital actually too small because of the rule, or would it have shut down otherwise?”

Those are exactly the questions the paper should anticipate and frame around, even if it cannot fully answer all of them.

### If findings are modest or null

Not relevant for the 25-bed result; that result is not modest. But the 50- and especially 100-bed pieces are more modest and seem included mainly for completeness. Their value is comparative—they establish that the 25-bed program is qualitatively stronger, not just statistically nonzero. The paper should say this more directly and stop trying to make each threshold equally central.
