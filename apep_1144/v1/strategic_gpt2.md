## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple, policy-relevant question: do patent grants create local jobs, or do places with strong economies simply both patent more and employ more? Using quasi-random variation from USPTO examiner leniency aggregated to the county level, the paper argues that the well-known positive patents-employment correlation is not causal: marginal patent grants do not generate detectable local employment gains.

Why should a busy economist care? Because a large amount of innovation policy is implicitly sold as place-based job creation policy, and this paper says that premise may be wrong. If true, that is a useful corrective to how economists and policymakers talk about patents, innovation subsidies, and local development.

**Does the paper articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Reasonably clearly, yes. The opening does a decent job of stating the question and the basic empirical idea. But it still leads a bit too much with the instrument and not enough with the broader stakes. The first two paragraphs should more sharply foreground the mismatch between the rhetoric of innovation policy and what patents may actually do for local labor markets.

**The pitch the paper should have:**

> Governments often justify innovation policy on the grounds that more patenting means more local jobs. But patents may be a marker of innovative places rather than a cause of local employment growth. This paper asks whether the marginal patent grant actually creates jobs in the inventor’s county.
>
> To answer that question, we exploit quasi-random variation in patent grants generated by differences in USPTO examiner leniency and aggregate it to the county level. We find that while patenting and employment are positively correlated in OLS, examiner-induced patent grants have no detectable effect on county employment, suggesting that the local “jobs payoff” from marginal patent grants is far smaller than policy discussions imply.

That version puts the world question first, the policy relevance second, and the research design third.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution in one sentence:**  
The paper argues that marginal patent grants, while correlated with local employment in observational data, do not causally increase employment in the inventor’s county.

That is a clean contribution. The issue is not whether there is a contribution, but whether it is big enough and sharply enough distinguished from adjacent work.

### Is the contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3-4 papers?
Partly, but not fully. The paper distinguishes itself from firm-level examiner-leniency studies by saying: they study firms; we study local employment aggregation. That is a real distinction. It also distinguishes itself from geography-of-innovation work by claiming those papers document spatial correlation or spillovers without isolating the causal effect of a patent grant itself.

Still, the introduction currently risks sounding like: “we take an existing IV and run it on county employment.” That is not fatal, but it is exactly the sort of reaction AER readers will have if the framing is not elevated. The contribution needs to be differentiated conceptually, not just by unit of observation. The key conceptual distinction is: **the paper is testing whether the private or innovative effects of patents translate into place-based labor market effects.** That is stronger than “this is another examiner-leniency paper.”

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a gap in a literature?
Mostly about the world, which is good. The best parts of the introduction ask whether patents create jobs. The weaker parts revert to “we contribute to three literatures,” including a methods contribution about Bartik. That methods angle feels second-order here and weakens the main message.

### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what's new?
Yes, but only barely. A smart reader could say: “They use examiner leniency to show patents don’t seem to raise county employment.” That’s good. But they could also just as easily say: “It’s a Bartik-IV paper on patents and local jobs with a null result.” The paper is not yet sufficiently protected against that reductive summary.

### What would make this contribution bigger?
Several possibilities:

1. **Broaden the outcome from employment alone to local economic adjustment more generally.**  
   If the core finding is “patents do not raise local jobs,” the natural follow-up is: do they raise wages, establishment growth, productivity, firm entry, or shift employment composition? Right now the paper is boxed into a fairly narrow null on one outcome. A broader set of economically coherent outcomes would make the paper feel less like a single null estimate.

2. **Lean harder into the distinction between private returns and local returns.**  
   This is the most promising angle. Prior work says patents matter for recipients; this paper says those gains do not show up as local job creation. That wedge is interesting. The paper should make it central.

3. **Sharpen the policy framing: innovation policy is not the same as place-based policy.**  
   This is potentially AER-worthy if properly developed. The result need not be “patents do nothing”; it can be “the relevant margin of patent policy does not operate through local employment creation.”

4. **Explore displacement versus aggregation more explicitly in framing.**  
   Even without adding heavy empirical machinery, the introduction could emphasize that patents may reallocate activity across firms, sectors, or space without changing total local employment. That gives the null a more economically interesting interpretation.

The biggest expansion would be from “patents don’t create local jobs” to **“innovation policy and place-based job creation are often conflated, but they operate on different margins.”**

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

### Closest neighbors
The paper’s closest neighbors appear to be:

- **Sampat and Williams (2019)** or closely related examiner-leniency papers on patents and follow-on innovation / gene patents.
- **Farre-Mensa, Hegde, and Ljungqvist (2020)** on patent rights and startup growth.
- **Galasso and Schankerman (2015)** on patents and cumulative innovation.
- **Moretti (2010)** on local multipliers and the local labor market effects of innovation/tradable sectors.
- **Moretti (2019)** / geography-of-innovation framing.
- Potentially **Autor, Dorn, and Hanson (2013)** only as a shift-share design analogy, though that is not really a substantive neighbor.

### How should the paper position itself relative to those neighbors?
It should **build on** the examiner-leniency papers, not “attack” them. The paper is strongest when it says: existing work establishes firm-level or innovation-level effects of patents; we ask whether those effects aggregate into local labor demand. That is a natural next question.

Relative to local labor market papers, it should position itself as a **disciplining test** of a common inference rather than a repudiation of the broader innovation literature. In other words: innovative places do better, but that does not imply a marginal patent grant creates local jobs.

### Is the paper currently positioned too narrowly or too broadly?
It is oddly both.

- **Too narrowly** in empirical framing: county employment, one horizon, one treatment margin.
- **Too broadly** in rhetoric: “The patent payroll illusion” risks sounding like it overturns a much larger set of ideas than the evidence really does.

The title and repeated branding of the “illusion” are catchy, but somewhat overmarketed relative to what the evidence appears to identify. That is dangerous at AER, where editors and readers are sensitive to overclaiming off one design and one margin.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?
A few conversations could be drawn in more effectively:

1. **Place-based policy and spatial equilibrium.**  
   If patents create rents but not employment, that fits a spatial-equilibrium or reallocation logic. Even a light engagement with that literature would improve the economic interpretation.

2. **Innovation and labor demand beyond headcount.**  
   The paper should connect more directly to work on automation, skill demand, task composition, superstar firms, and innovation-driven concentration. The null on total employment might be consistent with important compositional changes.

3. **Agglomeration and local spillovers.**  
   The paper cites some geography work, but it could do more to place itself in the agglomeration/spillovers conversation: maybe patenting matters for knowledge production or wages without creating net local jobs.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?
Not quite. It is currently having a conversation with:
- examiner-leniency papers,
- Bartik-method papers,
- and innovation/local employment papers.

The **best** conversation is actually:  
**When do micro-level innovation gains translate into local general-equilibrium labor market gains?**

That is a bigger, more interesting question. It connects innovation economics, labor, urban, and public policy. Right now the paper understates that broader conceptual hook.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### What is the setup?
Policymakers and economists often treat patenting as evidence of local economic vitality and sometimes as a mechanism for creating local jobs. Patenting and employment are positively correlated across places.

### What is the tension?
That correlation may not reflect a causal jobs effect from patent grants. Patents may matter for firms or inventors without generating measurable county-level employment growth. The paper asks whether the marginal patent grant actually changes local labor market outcomes.

### What is the resolution?
Using examiner-leniency-induced variation in patent grants, the paper finds that OLS shows a positive association, but the causal estimate is near zero. The marginal patent grant does not appear to increase local employment.

### What are the implications?
Innovation policy should not be casually sold as local job creation policy. Economists should distinguish between patents as markers of innovative places, patents as private rights affecting firms, and patents as drivers of local employment.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
A serviceable one, yes. But it is still more **result-forward** than **story-forward**. There is a clear central result, but the paper sometimes reads like a sequence of estimates organized around a null rather than a developed economic narrative.

The main narrative weakness is that the paper jumps quickly from result to branding (“patent payroll illusion”) without fully developing the economic interpretation. Why might patents fail to create local jobs? Because effects may be too small, too slow, too concentrated, too nonlocal, too compositional, or offset by displacement. The paper gestures at some of this, but the story would be stronger if it explicitly framed the null as informative about aggregation and incidence.

### If it is a collection of results looking for a story, what story should it be telling?
The story should be:

1. **Patents have known micro effects.**
2. **Policymakers often extrapolate from those micro effects to local job creation.**
3. **That extrapolation is unwarranted.**
4. **The missing link is aggregation: private innovation returns need not become local employment gains.**
5. **Therefore, innovation policy and local labor market policy should not be conflated.**

That is much better than “we found a null and called it an illusion.”

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

### What fact would you lead with at a dinner party of economists?
“I can instrument patent grants using examiner leniency, and once I do, the positive correlation between county patenting and county employment disappears.”

That is the right lead. It is easy to understand and provocative enough.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
They would lean in initially. Patents, local jobs, and sign reversal from OLS to IV is a good opening. The likely reaction is not boredom; it is skepticism followed by interest. That is promising.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Almost certainly:  
**“So do patents matter only for firms and innovation, but not for local labor markets?”**  
Or:  
**“Is this about marginal low-quality patents, or about patents more generally?”**

Those are exactly the questions the paper should be engineered to answer more clearly in the framing.

### If the findings are null or modest: is the null itself interesting?
Yes, potentially very much so. But the paper has to make the null feel like a **substantive correction to a widely used inference**, not just an inability to find an effect. It is on the right path, because the OLS-IV sign reversal gives the null bite. Nulls are much more publishable when they overturn a pervasive interpretation of a positive observational relationship.

That said, the current draft sometimes overstates certainty while understating the narrowness of the margin identified. The null is interesting if framed as:
- the local employment effect of **marginal examiner-induced patent grants** appears negligible,
- therefore observed patent-job correlations should not be read causally.

That is valuable. But if the paper tries to imply “patents don’t create jobs” in a broad sense, it will trigger resistance rather than persuasion.

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

### Without rewriting the paper, what would make it read better?

1. **Shorten the methods exposition in the introduction.**  
   The first page is already pretty strong; keep the empirical design description to one sentence in the intro and move the mechanics to later. The opener should be about the economic question, not the full architecture of the shift-share IV.

2. **Reduce the “contributes to three literatures” paragraph.**  
   It is standard and somewhat deflationary. Replace it with one paragraph on what economists would learn about innovation policy, local labor markets, and aggregation if the result is true.

3. **Move some design-defense language out of the data section.**  
   The caveat about granted-patent geography is important, but it lands with a thud in the data section and interrupts flow. Keep the point, but integrate it more strategically into a limitations or design discussion.

4. **Bring interpretation earlier.**  
   The strongest interpretive point is buried later: the design identifies the effect of marginal patents, not necessarily breakthrough patents. That belongs much earlier, probably at the end of the introduction. It is crucial for setting expectations.

5. **Reorganize the results section around the main economic message.**
   Suggested order:
   - Main fact: OLS positive, IV zero.
   - Why that matters: observational patent-job correlations are not causal.
   - Interpretation of margin identified.
   - Broader outcomes / mechanisms.
   - Robustness.

   Right now the paper slips too quickly into conventional robustness formatting.

6. **Do not bury potentially interesting non-employment outcomes.**  
   If hires and earnings are in the paper, decide whether they matter to the story. If they matter, elevate them into the main narrative. If they do not, cut or demote them. Right now the earnings result is mentioned but then waved away; that creates clutter and invites confusion.

7. **Tone down the branding.**  
   “Patent payroll illusion” is memorable, but repeated too often it reads as sloganizing. Use it once, maybe in the conclusion or title, but let the economics do the work.

8. **The conclusion should do more than summarize.**  
   It should explicitly state the conceptual implication: innovation policy may produce welfare gains without producing local employment gains; these are different margins and should be evaluated differently.

### Is the paper front-loaded with the good stuff?
Mostly yes. The sign reversal is introduced early, which is good. But the reader still gets a little too much machinery before the broader interpretive payoff is fully developed.

### Are there results buried in robustness that should be in the main results?
Yes: the **marginal-patent interpretation** is buried in interpretation rather than integrated into the headline. Also, if the longer-horizon null is important, mention it briefly in the main results narrative, not as an afterthought.

### Is the conclusion adding value or just summarizing?
Mostly summarizing. It should do more conceptual synthesis and a bit less restating of coefficients.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

At the moment, this feels like a competent, interesting paper with a publishable null result, but not yet an obvious AER paper.

### What is the gap?
Primarily an **ambition and framing problem**, with some **scope problem**.

- **Not mainly a framing-only problem:** the story could be improved a lot, yes, but there is also a substantive scope issue. One county-level null on employment is narrower than what AER typically wants unless it is attached to a really important and broadly interesting conceptual claim.
- **Not mainly a novelty problem:** the question is not trivial, and the sign reversal is meaningful. But the paper is using a familiar empirical ingredient in a new aggregation context.
- **Mostly ambition problem:** the paper currently makes a modest point cleanly when it needs to make a larger point convincingly.

The paper would be more AER-like if it framed itself as identifying a broader disconnect between:
- patent-induced private innovation effects,
- and place-based labor market effects.

It also needs a richer account of what does or does not adjust locally when patents are granted. Employment alone may be too thin a lens.

### Be honest: what is the gap between current form and something that would excite the top 10 people in this field?
The top people will ask:
1. Is this a deep new fact or just a null on one margin?
2. What does this teach us about the geography of innovation, not just about one reduced-form coefficient?
3. Does it change how we think about innovation policy versus place-based policy?
4. Is the non-result itself theoretically illuminating?

Right now the paper gets partway there, but not all the way. It shows a useful fact; it does not yet fully explain why that fact revises our understanding of the world.

### Single most impactful piece of advice
**Reframe the paper around the broader economic question—why firm-level patent effects fail to aggregate into local labor market gains—rather than around the narrower claim that one IV estimate of county employment is null.**

That one shift would improve the introduction, literature positioning, interpretation, and perceived ambition all at once.
