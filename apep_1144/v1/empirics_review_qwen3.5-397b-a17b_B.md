1. **Idea Fidelity**

The paper largely adheres to the core identification strategy and data sources outlined in the Original Idea Manifest. It successfully implements the Bartik shift-share instrument using quasi-random examiner leniency shocks (LOO grant rates within art-unit-year) interacted with pre-determined technology shares. The data sources match the manifest (USPTO PatEx via BigQuery and Census QWI). However, there are two notable deviations from the manifest parameters. First, the manifest proposed a **state-level** analysis ("State-level patent grant shocks," "~15,300 state x NAICS x year cells"), whereas the paper executes a **county-level** analysis (876 counties). While this deviation increases granularity and statistical power, it diverges from the specified unit of analysis. Second, the manifest specified a sample period of 2001–2015, while the paper uses 2004–2012, likely due to the share-construction window and QWI availability constraints. These deviations are methodologically sound but should be explicitly justified as improvements over the original plan rather than limitations.

2. **Summary**

This paper investigates whether patent grants causally increase local employment by exploiting quasi-random examiner assignment at the USPTO within a Bartik shift-share framework. The authors find a stark divergence between OLS and IV estimates: while OLS suggests a positive elasticity of employment to patents, the IV estimate is statistically indistinguishable from zero, termed the "patent payroll illusion." The study contributes credible null evidence that marginal patent grants do not drive local labor market dynamics, challenging policy assumptions linking innovation infrastructure directly to job creation.

3. **Essential Points**

1.  **Interpretation of the Local Average Treatment Effect (LATE):** The IV strategy identifies the effect of *marginal* patents granted due to examiner leniency, not the effect of patents per se. These marginal patents may differ systematically from infra-marginal patents (e.g., lower quality, weaker commercial potential, or firms less poised to scale). The paper must ensure the "Patent Payroll Illusion" framing does not overgeneralize to imply *no* patents create jobs. The conclusion should explicitly caveat that this null applies to the examiner-induced margin, distinguishing it from the potential effects of breakthrough innovations that would be granted regardless of examiner assignment.
2.  **Exogeneity of Examiner Shocks and Workload Assignment:** The identification relies on the assumption that examiner leniency shocks are orthogonal to local economic conditions. While assignment is quasi-random, examiners are often allocated based on workload and backlog within art units. If technology booms in specific counties (e.g., software in Santa Clara) increase application volume in specific art units, and examiner workload adjustments correlate with grant rates (e.g., rushed examinations during high volume), the shock may not be fully exogenous to local demand shocks. The authors should strengthen the defense of shock exogeneity, perhaps by testing whether art-unit application volumes correlate with examiner leniency.
3.  **Measurement Error in Share Construction:** The paper acknowledges that technology shares ($s_{ca}$) are constructed using *granted* patent locations (PatentsView) rather than *application* locations, because geocoded application data is limited. This introduces potential measurement error if grant rates vary geographically for reasons correlated with county characteristics (violating the "no dominant shares" or share exogeneity intuition). While Borusyak et al. (2022) emphasize shock exogeneity, measurement error in shares attenuates the first stage and can bias IV estimates if shares correlate with unobserved county trends. The authors should discuss whether using application-level geography (if available via alternative linkage) or correcting for this measurement error alters the first-stage strength.

4. **Suggestions**

1.  **Power Analysis and Minimum Detectable Effect (MDE):** Given the null result, readers will naturally ask whether the study is sufficiently powered to detect economically meaningful effects. The standard error on the main IV estimate is 0.044. I recommend adding a formal power calculation or MDE table in the appendix. Specifically, calculate the minimum elasticity the study could detect with 80% power at the 5% significance level. If the MDE is larger than the OLS estimate (0.010), the null is less informative. If the MDE is well below the OLS estimate, the null is robust. This quantification would significantly strengthen the credibility of the "null" claim in the *AER: Insights* format, where policy relevance is paramount.
2.  **Heterogeneity by Firm Age and Size:** The Original Idea Manifest highlighted the potential to examine heterogeneity ("Small-entity vs large-firm patents"). The paper currently splits outcomes by sector (Exposed vs. Local Service), but QWI data contains rich firm demographics (firm age classes, firm size classes). Examiner leniency might have heterogeneous effects: a marginal patent might be crucial for a startup's survival (and hiring) but irrelevant for a large incumbent's employment levels. Utilizing QWI's firm age/size tabulations to test whether the null holds for young firms (e.g., <5 years old) versus mature firms would address the LATE concern directly. If the null persists even for young firms, the "illusion" claim is much stronger.
3.  **Extended Lag Structure:** The baseline specification measures employment at $t+1$. Commercialization of patented technology often involves significant lags between grant date, product development, and hiring. A one-year lag might be too short to capture the employment effect, leading to a false null. I suggest extending the outcome window to $t+3$ and $t+5$. If the effect remains zero at longer horizons, it reinforces the conclusion that these marginal patents do not drive real economic expansion. Conversely, if effects emerge at $t+5$, it suggests the mechanism is simply slower than anticipated.
4.  **Refining the Share Construction:** To address the measurement error concern in Essential Point 3, consider whether the PatEx data on BigQuery can be linked directly to inventor addresses on the *application* rather than the grant. While PatentsView is optimized for grants, the raw XML or transaction tables in PatEx often contain inventor addresses at the time of filing. Re-constructing the shares ($s_{ca}$) using application-level geography rather than grant-level geography would eliminate the potential endogeneity of shares driven by geographic variation in grant rates. Even if this reduces the sample slightly, it would purify the instrument.
5.  **Nuancing the "Illusion" Framing:** The term "Patent Payroll Illusion" is catchy but risks being misinterpreted by policymakers as "patents do not create jobs." I recommend refining the abstract and conclusion to emphasize that the *marginal* grant decision is what lacks employment impact. Consider adding a sentence quantifying the implication: e.g., "This suggests that policies aimed at increasing patent grant rates (e.g., examiner hiring or throughput incentives) are unlikely to yield local employment dividends, even if underlying innovation remains constant." This ties the empirical null directly to a specific policy lever (USPTO throughput) rather than innovation policy broadly.
6.  **Placebo Tests on Examiner Characteristics:** To further validate the shock exogeneity, run a placebo test where you interact the shares with examiner characteristics that should *not* matter (e.g., examiner tenure, gender, or age) instead of leniency. If these characteristics also predict employment, it suggests the shares are picking up some other correlated trend. If only leniency matters (and even then, only for patents, not employment), it bolsters the claim that the instrument is isolating the specific variation in grant propensity.
7.  **Comparison to Existing Literature:** The introduction cites firm-level examiner IV papers (Farre-Mensa et al., 2020). It would be valuable to explicitly reconcile the findings. Firm-level papers find effects on firm growth/survival; this paper finds no effect on local employment. Is this because firms substitute labor (hire skilled workers, fire others)? Or because firm-level effects are too small to aggregate? A brief discussion reconciling the micro (firm) vs. macro (local labor market) findings would help position the paper within the broader innovation economics literature.
8.  **Data Appendix Transparency:** Given the complexity of linking PatEx, PatentsView, and QWI, I recommend adding a detailed data appendix (even if online) describing the exact linkage rates. What percentage of patent applications could be successfully matched to counties? What percentage of QWI counties were dropped due to the "20 applications" rule? Transparency on sample attrition is crucial for assessing external validity, particularly since the sample is restricted to "invention-active" counties, which may differ systematically from rural areas where policy intervention might be most desired.
