## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple, important question: when a government labels an area as groundwater-stressed and formally subjects extraction to permitting requirements, does anything actually change underground if enforcement is near zero? Using a large panel of groundwater monitoring wells in India, the paper argues that the answer is no: regulatory classification without credible enforcement does not slow aquifer depletion.

A busy economist should care because this is potentially a sharp test of a broad proposition about state capacity: environmental regulation is not just about rules on the books, but about whether those rules alter behavior in common-pool resource settings. If the result is credible and well framed, it speaks beyond groundwater and beyond India.

**Does the paper articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Not quite. The current introduction has urgency and scale, but it takes too long to get to the real question. It starts as a groundwater-crisis paper, then becomes an India-institutions paper, then an evaluation of a specific classification regime. The core idea is stronger than the opening makes it sound.

**What the first two paragraphs should say instead:**  

> Governments in many low-capacity settings respond to environmental degradation by creating regulatory categories, permit requirements, and formal restrictions. But if violations are rarely detected or punished, do these legal designations change behavior at all? This paper studies that question in the context of India’s groundwater crisis, one of the world’s largest common-pool resource problems.  
>   
> India’s central groundwater authority classifies local areas as “overexploited” when extraction exceeds recharge, a designation that triggers permit requirements for non-drinking groundwater use. Yet official audit evidence shows that most regulated users do not comply. Using two decades of monitoring-well data, I test whether expansion of this overexploitation regime slows groundwater depletion. I find no detectable reduction in depletion rates, suggesting that regulation without enforcement can be largely irrelevant even when the resource problem is severe.

That is the pitch. Right now the paper has the ingredients, but not the clean opening.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:**  
The paper claims to show that India’s flagship groundwater regulatory regime—classification of areas as overexploited and associated permitting requirements—did not measurably reduce groundwater depletion, implying that environmental regulation without enforcement may be behaviorally inert.

### Evaluation

**Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?**  
Only partially. The paper says it contributes to environmental regulation in developing countries, common-pool resources, and Indian groundwater policy. But those are literatures, not a differentiation strategy. A reader still does not know whether this is new because:
1. it studies groundwater rather than pollution,
2. it studies **regulatory designation** rather than inspections/audits/pricing,
3. it studies **state capacity failure** in a common-pool resource setting,
4. or it studies the limits of top-down command-and-control regulation when agriculture is de facto exempt.

Those are different claims. The paper needs to pick one main dimension of novelty and hammer it.

**Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a gap in a literature?**  
Mostly the former, which is good. The strongest world question is: *Do unenforced environmental regulations matter at all?* The weaker framing is: *There is little evidence on groundwater regulation in India.* AER wants the first, not the second.

**Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what’s new?**  
Right now, maybe not cleanly. They might say: “It’s a DiD on India groundwater regulation and finds no effect.” That is not enough. They should instead say: “It’s evidence that formal regulatory designation by itself doesn’t change extraction when the state can’t enforce and the main users are politically protected.” That is sharper.

**What would make this contribution bigger?**  
Most importantly, the paper needs to tighten the object of study. Right now the evidence is not actually on the sharp policy margin the rhetoric emphasizes. The institutional story is about **block-level notification and NOC requirements**, but the empirical treatment is **state-level crossing of a 15% overexploitation threshold** that appears to be the author’s construct, not the legal trigger itself. That creates strategic drift: the paper sounds like “effect of regulation,” but reads like “effect of living in a state with many overexploited blocks.”

Specific ways to make the contribution bigger:
- **Match treatment to actual notification/enforcement geography.** This is the single biggest issue. Without it, the paper’s substantive claim outruns its design.
- **Separate users or margins.** If agriculture is effectively exempt and industry is regulated, can the paper say anything about industrial zones, urban/peri-urban blocks, or deeper aquifers where industrial extraction matters?
- **Show behavioral substitution or political economy mechanisms.** For example: does classification shift drilling deeper, increase borewell intensity, or simply have no salience among extractors?
- **Frame as a state-capacity paper, not just a groundwater paper.** The broader question is when legal designation substitutes for actual implementation—and when it does not.

   Neighbor on Indian groundwater and common-pool dynamics.

4. **Ostrom (1990), Governing the Commons**  
   More conceptual than empirical, but central for the coordination/common-pool framing.

5. Potentially also papers on **state capacity and implementation failure** in development/public economics—e.g., **Pritchett-style implementation capability**, **Burgess et al.** on bureaucracy/state effectiveness, or applied work on “paper laws” versus actual enforcement.

If the field-specific groundwater literature is richer—and it is—the paper should likely engage work on groundwater institutions, electricity pricing, borewell externalities, and irrigation policy in India. Right now that side feels too thin.

### How should the paper position itself?
**Build on**, not attack. The right message is:
- Duflo et al. show enforcement technology can make regulation meaningful.
- This paper shows the opposite case: absent enforcement capability, a formal regulatory regime may do essentially nothing.
- The combination of the two is the insight: **rules matter when paired with credible implementation; otherwise not.**

That is better than pretending to overturn a literature.

### Is it positioned too narrowly or too broadly?
At present, oddly both.
- **Too narrowly** in the institutional details of CGWB/CGWA classification.
- **Too broadly** in the claim that this says something sweeping about environmental governance generally.

It needs a narrower empirical claim and a broader conceptual frame.

### What literature does it seem unaware of?
It under-engages:
- broader **state capacity / implementation / law-on-the-books vs law-in-action** literatures,
- empirical work on **common-pool regulation vs price instruments**,
- the Indian political economy of **electricity subsidies and groundwater extraction**,
- possibly water resource economics outside development.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?
Not fully. The current conversation is “environmental regulation in developing countries.” That is fine, but the more interesting conversation may be:
- **Can legal designation change behavior without enforcement?**
- **When does the state substitute symbolic regulation for actual implementation?**
- **Why are common-pool resources especially resistant to paper regulation?**

That conversation is bigger and more AER-relevant than “one more groundwater policy evaluation.”

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
India faces severe groundwater depletion, and the government’s main response is to classify areas as overexploited and require permits for extraction.

### Tension
The legal regime looks substantial on paper, but audit evidence suggests compliance and enforcement are extremely weak. So the central puzzle is whether formal designation itself has any bite through deterrence, salience, or norm formation—even without meaningful enforcement.

### Resolution
The paper finds no detectable slowdown in depletion rates after regulatory classification.

### Implications
If correct, the result implies that formal environmental regulation without enforcement capacity may be functionally irrelevant, especially for common-pool resources where the main users are politically difficult to regulate.

### Evaluation
The paper **does have** a narrative arc, but it is undermined by a mismatch between story and empirical object. The narrative says:
- block-level designation,
- legal restrictions,
- noncompliance,
- paper tiger.

But the empirics test:
- state-level threshold crossing in overexploited block share.

That gap matters enormously for editorial positioning. It makes the paper feel like it has a story first and is reaching for a design second. In top-journal terms, it risks reading like “a collection of suggestive nulls with a strong title.”

**What story should it be telling?**  
Not “India’s groundwater regulation does nothing,” at least not yet.  
Better: “Broad regulatory designation in a low-enforcement setting does not measurably alter aggregate groundwater depletion.”  
That sounds less dramatic, but it is more faithful—and paradoxically more credible.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

**What fact would I lead with at dinner?**  
“India’s main groundwater regulatory regime appears to have had no measurable effect on groundwater depletion, despite covering some of the most water-stressed areas in the world.”

That is a good opening fact. People would lean in—initially.

**Would people lean in or reach for their phones?**  
They would lean in for the first minute, because groundwater depletion in India is first-order and the “paper tiger” framing is vivid. But the follow-up question would come quickly:

**What follow-up question would they ask?**  
“Are you actually estimating the effect of the legal regulation, or just the effect of states becoming more overexploited?”

And that is exactly the right question. Right now the paper does not have a fully satisfying strategic answer.

**Is the null result itself interesting?**  
Yes, potentially very much so. A well-established null on a major policy regime can be AER material. But null papers need one of two things:
1. a design that cleanly maps to a policy margin everyone cares about, or
2. a conceptual contribution showing why the null changes how we think.

This paper has the beginnings of (2), but not enough yet, and it weakens (1) by using a coarse treatment. So at present the null feels more like “failure to detect an effect in a noisy institutional environment” than “convincing evidence of policy irrelevance.”

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

Without rewriting the paper, several changes would improve readability and strategic force.

### Front-load the real result
The paper does a decent job here, but it still spends too much time on background before clarifying what exactly is being tested. The introduction should get to:
- the institutional trigger,
- the enforcement gap,
- the empirical proxy for treatment,
- and the headline null,
all by the end of page 2.

### Be much more explicit about the treatment mismatch
This is currently buried in “Threats to Validity” and “Limitations.” That is too late and too defensive. It is actually the central interpretive issue. Move it up and make it transparent:
- legal policy operates at block level,
- data only permit state-level exposure,
- therefore the paper estimates aggregate effects of expansion in the regulatory environment, not the local causal effect of block notification.

That honesty would improve credibility.

### Shorten generic background
The institutional background can be tighter. There is too much descriptive material on governance architecture relative to what is needed for the paper’s strategic purpose.

### The mechanisms section is weak
As written, it is a list of plausible stories the author cannot test. That is fine for a working paper, but for top-journal positioning it reads as hand-waving. Shorten it unless stronger evidence can be brought in.

### Robustness is too visible relative to the main idea
Because this is not a referee report, I’ll say bluntly: the paper reads too much like it wants credit for having many checks. For editorial purposes, that does not help. The robustness section should be compressed unless one of those results materially deepens the substantive takeaway.

### Conclusion
The conclusion is punchy, but mostly summarizing. It should do more synthesis:
- what this teaches us about regulation under weak state capacity,
- why groundwater is a hard case,
- and what conditions would make such a regime work.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The gap is mostly **a framing problem plus a scope/mapping problem**.

- **Not mainly an econometrics problem** for purposes of this memo.
- **Not mainly a writing problem** either.
- The central issue is that the paper’s strongest claim—“groundwater regulation without enforcement changes nothing”—is larger than what the current empirical design cleanly identifies.

### What is the paper’s current limitation?
It is caught between two papers:

1. **A narrow policy evaluation** of a specific Indian groundwater regime.  
   If that’s the paper, the treatment needs to map tightly to actual notification/enforcement.

2. **A broad state-capacity paper** about when regulation on paper fails in practice.  
   If that’s the paper, it needs stronger conceptual framing and ideally some comparative leverage or mechanism evidence.

Right now it is not fully succeeding as either.

### Is it a novelty problem?
Partly. “Another null DiD on a policy that wasn’t enforced” is not enough. What rescues novelty is the importance of the setting and the possibility that the paper becomes a canonical example of symbolic environmental regulation. But to get there, it has to be much sharper.

### Is it an ambition problem?
Yes. The paper is competent but safe. It takes a large important setting, then settles for a coarse treatment and a broad conclusion. An AER paper would either:
- get much closer to the true regulatory margin, or
- build a stronger, more general argument about state capacity and common-pool resource governance.

### Single most impactful advice
**Align the empirical object with the rhetorical claim: either estimate the effect of actual block-level notification/enforcement exposure, or rewrite the paper so it explicitly studies state-level expansion of a weak regulatory environment rather than “the effect of regulation.”**

That is the one change that most increases the paper’s chance of belonging in this journal.
