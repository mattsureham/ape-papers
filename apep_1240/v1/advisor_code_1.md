**Idea Fidelity**

The manuscript closely follows the original idea manifest. It employs the CGWB monitoring-well data and staggered assessment rounds to study the causal impact of overexploitation classification on depletion rates, as promised. The core identification strategy—using state-level treatment timing when states cross a 15%-overexploited threshold—is implemented, and attention is given to the prospective mechanism (classification without enforcement). A minor omission is that the sharper design comparing notified vs.\ non-notified blocks (Design B in the manifest) is not fully realized; the analysis stays at the state level rather than exploiting the 14 notified blocks explicitly. This choice should be justified more clearly given the manifesto emphasis on that sharper comparison.

**Summary**

The paper assesses whether India’s CGWB overexploitation classification slows groundwater depletion by estimating a staggered difference-in-differences model on 28,074 groundwater monitoring wells over 1996–2017. The preferred specification uses annual changes in depth-to-water to show no detectable post-classification slowdown—the point estimate is positive (faster decline) but statistically indistinguishable from zero. Robustness checks across subgroups, geographic neighbors, and placebo timings support the null, leading the author to conclude that the CGWA/CGWB system is a “paper tiger” absent enforcement.

**Essential Points**

1. **State-Level Treatment Obscures Causal Mechanism**: The treatment—CGWA notification of specific overexploited blocks—is at the block level, whereas the empirical design aggregates to states crossing a 15% threshold. This risks conflating the effect of being in an overexploited state with the regulatory treatment itself, especially since only 162 out of ~736 overexploited blocks are formally notified. The paper needs to show that state-level timing reasonably proxies for block-level treatment: for example, how closely aligned are the dates for notified blocks versus the state threshold? Can the treatment variable be refined to exploit block-level notification status (Design B from the idea manifest) or, at minimum, show that the state-level event captures the policy change the paper aims to study?

2. **Parallel Trends for Depletion Rates Require More Support**: The paper argues that the rate specification sidesteps endogeneity because classification is triggered by high depletion levels. However, parallel trends in depletion rates are a strong assumption, and the existing placebo (fake 2000 treatment) indicates some pre-trends in levels. The paper should present visual/event-study evidence for the rate specification, not just a p-value, and consider additional controls or leads to demonstrate that treated and control states were not diverging in depletion rates before treatment. Without this, readers may worry that the null is driven by differential dynamics in the treated states even before they cross the threshold.

3. **Inference Concerns with Few Clusters and State-Level Variation**: There are only 24 state clusters, and the treatment variation comes from even fewer states (the nine that ever cross 15%). Standard errors clustered at the state level may be unreliable, and the leave-one-state-out exercise in the main text shows coefficients ranging widely (0.46–0.96) but remains positive—suggesting sensitivity. The paper should employ more rigorous inference (e.g., wild cluster bootstrap) and explicitly report how many treated vs.\ control states contribute to identification. Clarifying whether the standard errors meaningfully account for few treated clusters will bolster confidence in the null result.

**Suggestions**

1. **Implement Block-Level or Notification-Based Treatment**: Since the key policy question is whether CGWA notification and the subsequent NOC requirement deter extraction, the paper should make every effort to link wells to notified overexploited blocks. Even if official block-level notification dates are not perfectly aligned with the assessment rounds, the author could construct a pseudo-treatment indicator based on whether a well falls inside a block that was ever notified, or use continuous treatment (fraction of notified blocks in the district) to get closer to the actual policy. At minimum, summarize how many wells fall into notified versus non-notified blocks and whether there is any change in depletion trends in the notified subset.

2. **Enhance Graphical/Event-Study Evidence**: Include plots of the estimated event-study coefficients for the depletion-rate outcome, showing both pre- and post-treatment periods with confidence intervals. This will help assess the parallel-trends assumption visually and demonstrate the lack of post-treatment deviation. In addition, consider plotting aggregate depletion rates for treated and control states before and after treatment to give readers an intuitive sense of the trajectories.

3. **Clarify Treatment Timing and Intensity**: Table 3 in the current draft reports overexploited block counts by state and round, but the analysis hinges on a 15% share threshold. Provide a figure or table showing when each state crosses the threshold and how many overexploited/notified blocks they had at that time. If the threshold is arbitrary, discuss robustness to alternative thresholds (e.g., 10% or 20%). Also consider interacting treatment with the share of overexploited blocks to capture variation in treatment intensity, which could reveal whether stronger exposure yields any effect.

4. **Address Potential Spillovers and Control Group Validity**: Since groundwater dynamics are spatially correlated, control states adjacent to treated ones may not be valid counterfactuals. The adjacent-state specification in Table 5 is interesting, but it would help to systematically test for spatial spillovers (e.g., using bordering districts) and to assess whether controls experience “contamination” through groundwater flows or farmer behavior. A falsification test using a neighboring district across a state border could either strengthen the identification or highlight a limitation.

5. **Discuss Limitations of Inference and Power**: The paper mentions the minimum detectable effect but should elaborate on what that implies for the substantive conclusions. For instance, if the MDE is 0.58 m/year—a large number relative to the mean depletion rate—the null may simply reflect low power to detect smaller but policy-relevant effects. The author should discuss whether such smaller effects would still be meaningful for groundwater sustainability and whether the sample has adequate power at the block/district level. Reporting confidence intervals or standardized effect sizes (already in the appendix) in the main text can help readers interpret the null more fully.

6. **Triangulate Enforcement with Additional Data**: The paper relies on the CAG audit to establish enforcement gaps. If possible, supplement this with state-level proxy measures (e.g., number of NOCs issued per state over time, CGWA inspection visits, or media coverage of enforcement) to show that enforcement was indeed negligible after classification. Even descriptive correlations between CAG compliance indicators and depletion trends could strengthen the story that the regulation was born a paper tiger.

7. **Consider Alternative Outcomes or Mechanisms**: While the depletion rate is the natural outcome for causal identification, the paper could also look at complementary outcomes such as reported well drilling activity (if available), electricity consumption for irrigation, or nightlight intensity in rural areas to see if the classification affected broader water-intensive economic behavior. If the classification had no groundwater impact but did influence other inputs, that would offer additional nuance to the “paper tiger” conclusion.

8. **Fix Minor Issues in Tables and Text**: In Table 1, the standard deviations are enclosed in brackets with no units; clarifying the notation (SD in parentheses) would help. Also, clarify in the main text whether depth-to-water is measured from ground level downward (i.e., higher values mean greater depletion) to avoid confusion for readers unfamiliar with hydrogeology. Finally, the abstract and discussion mention both “classification” and “notification”; be consistent about which policy action is being evaluated.

By addressing these points, the paper can more convincingly argue that India’s overexploitation classification lacks enforcement teeth and thus fails to affect groundwater depletion.
