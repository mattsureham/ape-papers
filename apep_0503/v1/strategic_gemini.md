## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a fundamental question for environmental policy: do energy efficiency labels move markets because they provide information, or because they act as the "on-ramp" for hard regulation? By exploiting France’s phased rental ban—where only specific energy-score thresholds carry legal consequences—the paper uses a multi-cutoff RDD to show that price discounts appear only where the "teeth" of a rental ban exist, while purely informational labels have zero effect on prices.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this very well. The first two paragraphs clearly set up the "bright-line" regulatory cutoff and the "information vs. regulation" horse race. 

**The Pitch the Paper Should Have:** The current pitch is excellent. If I were to sharpen it further to make it "more AER," I would emphasize that this is a paper about the **micro-foundations of the energy efficiency gap.** It isn't just about French houses; it's about why information disclosure policies often fail to induce investment unless paired with mandates.

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:** The paper identifies that energy efficiency capitalization in housing is driven entirely by regulatory restrictions (rental bans) rather than the informational value of the labels themselves.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It differentiates itself cleanly from Sejas-Portillo et al. (2025) by having a setting with heterogeneous regulatory bite across cutoffs.
*   **World vs. Literature:** It frames itself as answering a question about the WORLD (how do we get people to renovate?), which is a major strength.
*   **Explanation to a colleague:** A smart economist would say: "It's the paper that proves energy labels need teeth to matter for prices."
*   **Making it bigger:** To make this "Big AER," the paper needs to bridge the gap between the *price effect* and the *welfare/investment effect*. Does this price discount actually lead to more renovations, or just a reshuffling of ownership? (See Section 7 for more on this).

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

*   **Closest neighbors:** *Allcott & Greenstone (2014)* on the efficiency gap; *Chetty et al. (2009)* on salience; *Myers (2022)* on sorting; and *Sejas-Portillo (2025)* on multi-cutoff EPCs.
*   **Positioning:** The paper correctly builds on the capitalization literature but essentially "attacks" the interpretation of prior work that attributed price premiums solely to information.
*   **Conversation:** It is having the right conversation, but it could speak more loudly to the **Public Economics** literature on "Regulation vs. Pigouvian Taxes." Is a rental ban an efficient way to internalize an externality compared to a carbon tax?
*   **Unexpected connection:** The paper could lean into the **Contract Theory/Incomplete Markets** side. The "rental option" is a specific stick in the bundle of property rights. Why is the market pricing the loss of the *option* to rent even for owner-occupiers?

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

*   **Setup:** Energy labels are everywhere, but we don't know why they work (or if they do).
*   **Tension:** If it’s just information, every label change should move the needle. If it’s regulation, only the "banned" labels should matter.
*   **Resolution:** Only the "banned" labels (G-rating) show a price drop. Anticipated bans (F and E) show nothing yet.
*   **Implications:** Disclosure alone is a weak tool for climate goals.

**Evaluation:** The narrative arc is very strong. However, the "Tension" in the current draft is slightly weakened by the non-monotonicity at the G/F boundary (the sign flip at narrow bandwidths). The author spends a lot of time defending the sign of the coefficient rather than the existence of the discontinuity.

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

*   **The Fact:** "In France, if you label a house 'inefficient,' nothing happens to the price. But the moment you say 'you can't rent this out,' the price drops 4.6%."
*   **Response:** People will lean in. It’s a clean, intuitive result that confirms a cynical view of "nudge" policies.
*   **Follow-up:** "Does the 4.6% cover the cost of the renovation?" (The paper answers this: No, the market is under-reacting or anticipating a rollback).

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

*   **The "Ecological RDD" Problem:** The use of commune-level average prices instead of individual transaction prices is a major hurdle for a top-5 journal. While the author argues it is conservative, referees will hammer this. The author needs to be much more upfront about the *compositional* risks here.
*   **Front-loading:** Figure 2 (the bin-scatters) is the "money shot." It should be moved earlier or referenced more heavily in the intro.
*   **The Sign Discrepancy:** The discussion on pages 17-18 about the sign flipping from negative to positive as the bandwidth widens is currently a "defensive" section. It should be reframed as an interesting finding about **selection** or **market thinness** near the boundary.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

**The Gap:** Currently, this is a very high-quality "Applied Economics" paper (think *AEJ: Applied* or *JPE: Micro*). To make it *AER*, it needs more **Economic Theory** or a broader **Welfare Calculation**. 

**The Single Biggest Improvement:** Expand the "Conceptual Framework" (Section 3) into a more formal model of property valuation under "Regulatory Risk." If the market is anticipating a ban, but we don't see an effect at the F/E boundary (the 2028 ban), what does that tell us about the discount rate or the perceived credibility of the French state? 

**The Advice:** Stop apologizing for the data linkage and start exploiting the **Anticipation vs. Enforcement** puzzle. The fact that the 2025 ban is priced but the 2028 ban isn't (despite the 2028 ban already causing "sorting" by assessors) is a fascinating behavioral result. Is it hyperbolic discounting, or just zero credibility? That is a "Big E" Economics question.
