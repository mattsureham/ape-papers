## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether the chronic circadian misalignment created by time-zone boundaries makes people more vulnerable to extreme heat. Using U.S. counties near time-zone borders, it finds no evidence that places on the late-sunset side suffer higher heat-related mortality, suggesting that one plausible vulnerability channel linking sleep disruption to climate risk is weaker than many would expect.

Why should a busy economist care? Because the paper sits at the intersection of two high-interest areas—climate damages and the economic consequences of sleep/circadian disruption—and tries to say something broader than either literature alone: whether a common institutional distortion meaningfully amplifies climate mortality risk.

Does the paper articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs? Mostly yes, but with two problems. First, it gets to the hypothesis quickly, which is good. Second, it immediately frames the contribution as “this sharp prediction is wrong,” which is punchy but too self-satisfied given that the outcome is a broad premature mortality measure rather than heat-specific or elderly mortality. The intro is clear, but the pitch is currently stronger than the actual object being measured.

### The pitch the paper should have

“Time-zone boundaries create a one-hour discontinuity in social time but not solar time, generating chronic circadian misalignment for otherwise similar communities. This paper asks whether that institutional misalignment increases vulnerability to extreme heat—a question that links the growing evidence on sleep-related health harms to the economics of climate mortality. Using counties near U.S. time-zone boundaries, I find little evidence that late-sunset communities experience larger summer mortality losses, suggesting that chronic circadian disruption is not an important population-level amplifier of heat mortality in these data.”

That is more accurate, more credible, and still interesting.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:**  
The paper’s contribution is to test whether chronic circadian misalignment induced by time-zone assignment amplifies the mortality effects of heat exposure, and it reports a null result.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?
Only partially. The paper is reasonably clear that it is not another paper on the direct health effects of time-zone position; it is about an interaction with heat. That helps. But the introduction does not yet do enough to explain why this is a first-order question rather than a clever cross-product of two literatures.

Right now, the contribution reads like: “Take a known treatment from one literature, interact it with a known risk from another literature, test for heterogeneity.” That can be publishable, but for AER the author needs to make the interaction itself feel conceptually central. Why should economists update on climate vulnerability or on time policy if this interaction is zero?

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or filling a gap in a LITERATURE?
It is trying to be about the world, which is the right instinct: does social time affect climate vulnerability? But it repeatedly falls back into “this contributes to three literatures” language, which weakens the piece. The stronger version is a world question:

- Do institutional arrangements that shift sleep and circadian timing meaningfully change climate damages?
- Are climate vulnerability estimates missing an important social-time dimension?
- Should time-zone reform be seen as part of climate-health adaptation?

Those are world questions. The current framing only intermittently leans into them.

### Could a smart economist explain what is new after reading the intro?
They could, but not crisply enough. Best case, they would say: “It uses time-zone boundaries to test whether social jetlag worsens heat mortality, and finds no amplification.” That is decent. Worst case, they would say: “It’s another time-zone-boundary paper, except the outcome is interacted with heat.”

That latter reaction is the danger. The paper needs to preempt it.

### What would make the contribution bigger?
Very specifically:

1. **A better mortality outcome.**  
   The biggest limitation to strategic positioning is that the paper studies YPLL before age 75, while heat mortality is heavily concentrated among the very old. This creates a mismatch between the hypothesis and the measured outcome. A paper claiming “the clock does not kill through the heat channel” cannot rest mainly on an outcome that excludes many of the likely victims. This is the single biggest obstacle to AER-level excitement.

2. **Heat-specific or cause-specific outcomes.**  
   Cardiovascular mortality, emergency visits, heat illness, all-age mortality, or age-stratified mortality would make the question feel much more direct.

3. **A mechanism margin economists care about.**  
   The paper currently gestures at adaptation but does not really elevate it into the contribution. If the author could show that any potential physiological vulnerability is offset in places with greater adaptation capacity, then the paper becomes not just “null interaction” but “institutions affect biology less than adaptation technology does.” That is a bigger claim.

4. **A reframing around climate-damage heterogeneity.**  
   Instead of “sleep deprivation doesn’t amplify heat mortality,” the paper could be framed as “not every plausible biological vulnerability scales into population climate damages.” That is a broader and more useful message.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

### Closest neighbors
The nearest neighbors appear to be:

1. **Giuntella and Mazzonna (2019)** on sunset time and health / circadian misalignment.  
2. **Giuntella and Mazzonna (or related earlier work, 2015)** on sleep and time zones.  
3. **Jin and Ziebarth / related recent papers** on time zones, earnings, or human capital consequences of circadian misalignment.  
4. **Barreca et al. (2016)** on adaptation to temperature and mortality.  
5. **Deschênes and Greenstone (2011)** and **Carleton et al. (2022)** on temperature and mortality / climate damages.

There is also a neighboring literature on daylight saving time, sleep disruption, and health/productivity that the paper should at least nod to, even if DST is not the design here. That literature helps establish that clock time can matter for behavior and health.

### How should the paper position itself relative to them?
**Build on, not attack.**  
This is not a paper that overturns Giuntella-style results. Nor does it overturn the heat-mortality literature. It asks whether the mechanisms documented in one domain materially spill over into another. The right positioning is:

- We know time-zone position affects sleep and some chronic health outcomes.
- We know heat kills.
- It is natural to think these facts interact.
- Here is evidence that, at least at the population level and with these outcomes, they do not meaningfully interact.

That is a useful synthesis paper, not a takedown.

### Is the paper positioned too narrowly or too broadly?
It is currently **positioned somewhat too narrowly in design and too broadly in conclusion**.

Too narrowly because the paper is organized around the specific institutional setting and estimation machinery. Too broadly because lines like “the clock does not kill through the heat channel” oversell what is, in truth, evidence using one broad mortality measure in one institutional context.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?
Not fully unaware, but underconnected to:

- **Daylight saving time / clock-time policy literature**
- **Sleep and labor/productivity literature**
- **Public health work on heat vulnerability by age and cause**
- **Climate adaptation heterogeneity literature**, especially papers emphasizing that biological risk need not map one-for-one into realized mortality because adaptation is endogenous

### Is the paper having the right conversation?
Almost, but not quite. Right now it is having the conversation: “Here is a null interaction between two established literatures.” The more impactful conversation would be:

**Which individual-level physiological vulnerabilities actually matter for aggregate climate damages once adaptation and behavior are allowed to respond?**

That is a much better conversation for AER. It connects this paper to a larger economic question: when do biologically plausible risks scale into social damages?

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Time-zone boundaries generate chronic social jetlag. Heat increases mortality. Medical intuition suggests that sleep-deprived or circadian-misaligned populations may be more physiologically vulnerable to heat.

### Tension
Despite that strong prior, it is unknown whether this vulnerability matters at the population level. The tension is not merely “nobody has studied this interaction,” but rather “a biologically plausible and policy-relevant amplifier may or may not survive contact with real-world adaptation.”

### Resolution
The paper finds little evidence that counties on the late-sunset side of time-zone boundaries experience higher mortality when summers are hotter.

### Implications
Potentially: climate damages may be less sensitive to this type of chronic circadian disruption than one might think; time-zone reform should not be justified primarily through heat-mortality concerns; and economists should be careful about extrapolating from plausible biomedical mechanisms to realized population damages.

### Does the paper have a clear arc?
Yes, but only in outline. The paper does have setup-tension-resolution. What it lacks is a persuasive **implications** section. The discussion mostly offers excuses for the null rather than elevating what the null teaches.

That is a common problem in null-result papers: the author is half-defending the result and half-apologizing for it. For AER, the paper needs to own the null more confidently but also more carefully. Not “we found nothing,” but “we learned that a plausible vulnerability channel does not appear to matter much at population scale, which disciplines how economists think about climate-risk heterogeneity.”

### If it is a collection of results looking for a story, what story should it tell?
The story should be:

**Many mechanisms that look important in the lab or in chronic disease settings may not materially amplify aggregate climate mortality because behavioral and technological adaptation buffer them.**

That is a real economic story. The current paper has pieces of it, but it does not yet tell it cleanly.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?
“I expected counties on the late-sunset side of time-zone borders to be more vulnerable to heat because of chronic circadian misalignment. In fact, I can’t find evidence that they are.”

That is a decent opener. Some people will lean in.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
At first, they would lean in. The premise is clever and the institutional setting is elegant. But the second question will come immediately, and if the answer is weak, interest will fade fast.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Almost certainly:  
**“What mortality measure are you using, and does it actually capture the people who die from heat?”**

And that is where this paper currently gets into trouble. The answer—YPLL before age 75—is not fatal, but it is strategically damaging because it makes the null feel less dispositive than the author wants it to.

If the paper’s findings are null or modest: is the null itself interesting?  
Yes, potentially. Nulls are interesting when:

- the prior was strong,
- the design is clean,
- the estimated object is central,
- and the paper changes how we think.

This paper has the first two. It is shakier on the third and fourth. The null could be valuable, but only if the paper more honestly defines the object as “broad premature mortality” rather than “heat mortality” full stop, or upgrades the outcome.

As written, it sometimes feels less like “X doesn’t matter” and more like “I looked in an imperfect place and didn’t see much.” That is not enough for AER.

Reasonably, yes. The question appears early. The result appears in paragraph two. That is good.

### Are there results buried in robustness that should be in the main results?
Not really. The bigger issue is the opposite: too much effort is spent documenting that the null survives specification changes, and not enough effort is spent extracting meaning from it.

### Is the conclusion adding value?
Not much. It is mostly a compressed abstract. It should be doing interpretive work.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

Bluntly: in current form, this is **not yet an AER story**.

Why?

### The gap to AER
This is mainly a **scope and ambition problem**, with some framing issues.

- **Framing problem:** The paper’s core question is interesting, but it is framed a bit too much as a clever test and a bit too little as a substantive statement about climate-damage heterogeneity.
- **Scope problem:** The outcome does not cleanly match the claim. That shrinks the paper’s ambition.
- **Novelty problem:** The institutional variation is familiar, and interaction-null papers need a very strong object or a very strong conceptual payoff.
- **Ambition problem:** The paper is competent, but safe. It does not yet produce a takeaway that the top people in environmental, health, or labor economics would feel they must grapple with.

If the paper had all-age mortality, elderly mortality, cause-specific mortality, or utilization outcomes that directly map to heat vulnerability, then the same design could support a much more consequential claim. As it stands, the result is more “interesting exercise” than “field-shaping paper.”

### Single most impactful advice
**Either upgrade the outcome to one that directly captures heat vulnerability—especially elderly or all-age mortality—or recast the paper much more modestly as evidence that circadian misalignment does not noticeably affect broad premature mortality responses to heat in these data.**

That is the fork in the road. If the author wants an AER shot, I would strongly prefer the first path.
