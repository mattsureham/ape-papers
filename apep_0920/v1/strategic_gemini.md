## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether legalizing Medical Aid in Dying (MAID) generates broad "cultural spillovers" that shift end-of-life care from expensive, aggressive inpatient interventions toward lower-cost hospice care for the general population. Using a staggered difference-in-differences design across seven U.S. states, the author finds that despite the intense fiscal hopes of proponents and fears of opponents, MAID legalization has a precisely estimated null effect on Medicare spending patterns. Economists should care because end-of-life care accounts for 25% of Medicare spending, and this study provides a rigorous test of "option value" and symbolic policy effects in health markets.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this well, but it spends too much time on the "how" (staggered DiD, Callaway-Sant’Anna) in the second paragraph rather than the "why." 

**The pitch the paper should have:**
"Does the legal right to die change how the living are treated? While fewer than 0.5% of patients utilize Medical Aid in Dying (MAID), proponents argue its presence 'normalizes' palliative care conversations, potentially shifting the 25% of Medicare spending concentrated at the end of life away from aggressive inpatient care. This paper provides the first causal evidence that this 'exit option' hypothesis—the idea that a policy's symbolic availability outweighs its mechanical use—fails to materialize in administrative spending data, suggesting that the MAID debate should remain centered on ethics rather than fiscal outcomes."

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:** The paper provides a precisely estimated null result showing that MAID legalization does not induce broader behavioral or systemic shifts in end-of-life Medicare spending.

- **Differentiation:** It differentiates well by moving beyond the descriptive/case study nature of existing health policy literature (Emanuel et al.) into a modern causal inference framework. 
- **World vs. Literature:** It frames itself as answering a question about the WORLD (Does the exit option work?), which is its strongest asset.
- **"Just another DiD":** A smart economist would recognize this as a "precisely estimated null" paper. The "what's new" is the debunking of a widely held fiscal intuition in health policy.
- **Making it bigger:** To be truly AER-sized, the paper needs more on the *mechanism of the failure*. Is the null because physicians don't change their behavior, or because patients' preferences for aggressive care are inelastic? Adding data on advance directive completion rates or "Goals of Care" billing codes would turn this from a spending paper into a behavior paper.

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

- **Closest Neighbors:** Riley & Lubitz (2010) on EOL spending; Finkelstein & Notowidigdo (2019) on option value/take-up; Callaway & Sant’Anna (2021) on methods.
- **Positioning:** It should **synthesize** the health spending literature with the "option value" literature. Currently, it feels a bit like a health-specialist paper that happened to use the latest DiD tools.
- **Narrow vs. Broad:** It is currently a bit narrow (Health Econ). It should speak more broadly to **Public Economics** regarding the limits of "symbolic" policy and the stickiness of high-expenditure healthcare norms.
- **Missing Conversations:** The paper should connect to the literature on **Physician Agency**. If the "exit option" doesn't work, is it because physicians are the gatekeepers of these conversations and their incentives (FFS) remain unchanged by the law?

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

- **Setup:** End-of-life care is an intractable fiscal burden for Medicare.
- **Tension:** MAID is a polarizing policy, but both sides agree it *should* change the culture of dying. Proponents see a "hospice surge"; opponents see "incentives to undertreat."
- **Resolution:** Neither happens. The needle doesn't move.
- **Implications:** Policy debates over MAID should be decoupled from fiscal projections.

**Evaluation:** The arc is clear but lacks a "climax." The "methodological finding" about TWFE bias (the sign flip in ER visits) is treated as a side-note but is actually a very compelling part of the story. It shows how "bad" economics created a false narrative of increased ER use that this paper corrects.

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

- **Lead Fact:** "Legalizing the right to die has exactly zero impact on how much Medicare spends on the other 99.5% of people."
- **Reaction:** Moderate lean-in. The "precisely estimated null" is the hero here.
- **Follow-up:** "If the law doesn't change behavior, what does? Is it just a legal protection for a tiny fringe, or are doctors ignoring it?"

---

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

- **Front-load the Methodological Lesson:** The sign flip from positive/significant (TWFE) to null (CS) is a great "teaching moment" for the AER audience. Move it closer to the start of the results.
- **Appendix to Main:** The "Standardized Effect Sizes" (Table 5) are crucial. In a null-result paper, the most important thing is proving you had the power to see an effect if it existed. This should be in the main body.
- **Conclusion:** It’s a bit dry. It should lean harder into the "symbolic vs. mechanical" policy distinction.

---

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The gap is **Ambition**. It is a very "clean" paper—competent, robust, and well-executed. But the AER usually wants to see a deeper dive into *why* the hypothesis failed. 

**Single most impactful advice:** Connect the null spending result to a measure of *information* or *preferences*. If the author can show that "the law passed, public interest (Google Trends) spiked, but physician billing for 'End of Life Counseling' didn't move," they have a complete story about why the "exit option" failed to bridge the gap from law to bedside.
