## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper argues that an important, overlooked margin in the prescription-opioid crisis was not just how many pills were shipped, but how strong those pills were. Using the rollout of higher-dose generic oxycodone products by Mallinckrodt and preexisting geographic exposure to that manufacturer, the paper shows that some places were differentially exposed to an increase in opioid potency because of ordinary generic-market product-line decisions.

A busy economist should care because the paper tries to reframe a major public-health episode as partly a market-design and industrial-organization story: competition in generic pharmaceuticals changed the composition of supply, with potentially large external consequences.

**Does the paper articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Mostly yes. The opening is actually better than many submissions: it stakes out a neglected margin and gives the basic fact quickly. But it then jumps too fast from “the literature studies volume” to “manufacturers competed on potency, not price,” which is stronger than what the paper, as currently written, seems positioned to establish. The introduction needs to lead with the world-level question—why did some places get stronger opioids?—rather than with the implicit literature complaint and an IO claim that invites demands for price evidence.

**What the first two paragraphs should say instead:**

> The first wave of the U.S. opioid crisis is usually described as a story about quantity: how many pills were marketed, prescribed, and shipped into different communities. But for overdose risk and abuse potential, composition matters too. Two counties can receive the same number of oxycodone pills yet face very different exposure if one receives systematically stronger formulations.
>
> This paper shows that local opioid potency was shaped by a generic-manufacturer product-line expansion. When Mallinckrodt introduced higher-dose immediate-release oxycodone products in 2008, counties that were more exposed to Mallinckrodt before the launch experienced larger subsequent increases in oxycodone strength per pill. The broader point is that standard competition in generic drug markets can reallocate not just volume, but potency—and that this was an important, previously underappreciated feature of the geography of the opioid epidemic.

That pitch is cleaner, more defensible, and more obviously about the world.

The paper differentiates itself from work on physician marketing, prescribing regulation, insurance, and dispensing incentives by saying those papers study volume while this one studies potency composition. That is a real distinction. But the paper needs to do more to explain why this is not just “another supply-side opioid paper with a new treatment variable.” Right now, a reader may still summarize it as: *a quasi-experimental paper on opioid supply using ARCOS and a manufacturer shock*. The novelty is there, but it is not yet distilled into one memorable sentence.

**Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a gap in a literature?**  
It starts with the world, which is good, but then quickly reverts to gap-filling: “the literature treats generic supply as homogeneous.” The stronger frame is: **Why did the strength of opioid exposure vary across places, and what market forces determined that?** That is a world question. The literature gap is secondary.

**Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what’s new?**  
They could, but only if they are attentive. A good reader would say: “It’s about the geography of opioid strength, not just pill counts, and ties that to generic product entry.” A less attentive reader would indeed say: “It’s another opioid DiD/shift-share paper.” That is a danger signal for AER positioning.

**What would make this contribution bigger?**  
Three possibilities:

1. **Sharper outcome framing:**  
   Potency per pill is interesting, but still one step removed from what most economists care about. If the paper can show that the compositional shift materially changed the share of high-risk formulations, or total high-dose opioid exposure per capita, the result feels more consequential.

2. **Mechanism beyond “new products existed”:**  
   The most interesting mechanism here is not just product proliferation; it is the interaction of manufacturer product strategy with sticky distributor/pharmacy networks. That could be elevated much more. Right now it is present, but not central enough.

3. **A broader conceptual contribution:**  
   The paper could be framed as an instance of a more general proposition: in regulated markets with bioequivalence-based generic approval, firms may compete along dimensions that are clinically consequential but weakly regulated. That would move the paper from opioid episode to market-design insight.

A smaller but important point: the claim that firms “competed on potency, not price” is too big for the current paper. Unless the paper actually documents price responses or the absence of price competition, that line weakens credibility. Better to say they competed **through product-line expansion into higher-dose formulations**.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
The existing understanding is that the first wave of the opioid crisis was driven by aggressive expansion in prescription-opioid supply—marketing, prescribing incentives, regulation, insurance, pharmacy behavior.

### Tension
But that literature mostly treats opioid supply as homogeneous. If overdose risk depends on potency and formulation, then counting pills may miss a crucial margin. So why did some places get stronger opioid supply than others?

### Resolution
The paper argues that one answer is generic manufacturer product-line expansion. Mallinckrodt’s introduction of higher-dose oxycodone products increased local potency more in places already tied to Mallinckrodt through distribution networks.

### Implications
The implication is that competition and regulation in generic pharmaceutical markets can produce public-health-relevant compositional changes in supply that regulators do not directly target. That matters for how we think about opioid policy and, potentially, regulation of generics more broadly.

### Evaluation
There is **a real narrative arc here**, which is a strength. This is not just a bag of regressions. But the arc is not yet fully disciplined.

The main problem is that the paper wants two climaxes:

1. “Generic firms competed on potency.”
2. “Shift-share based on a single corporate event gives a clean design.”

The second is not a climax for AER readers. It is supporting architecture. The paper should tell one story: **a neglected but important margin in the opioid epidemic was product strength, and generic market dynamics determined its geography**.

Also, the paper overstates the resolution a bit. It shows Mallinckrodt’s expansion shifted local potency. That is a nice and publishable result. But the narrative sometimes slides toward “this reshaped the opioid crisis” in a way the current outcome evidence cannot fully carry. It needs to calibrate claims: this paper identifies **one mechanism shaping the geography of opioid strength**, not the whole geography of the crisis.

### What fact would I lead with?

That is the memorable fact.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
**Lean in, initially.**  
The topic is important, and “potency rather than quantity” is a nice twist. But the second question will come quickly:

> “Does this matter for actual health outcomes, or is it just a compositional fact?”

That is the central strategic issue for the paper. Even if the paper intentionally stops short of downstream outcomes, it needs to make the case that potency composition is itself an economically meaningful object, not just an intermediate variable.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Most likely one of these:

1. “Did stronger local supply translate into more overdoses or misuse?”
2. “Was Mallinckrodt unusual, or is this a general feature of generic competition?”
3. “Why should we think this was potency competition rather than just product availability?”

The paper needs a better answer to #2 and #3 in the framing, and ideally at least a partial conceptual answer to #1.

### If findings are modest: is the modesty okay?
Yes, potentially. The estimated effect is not gigantic, but that is not fatal. AER will publish moderate effects if the margin is conceptually important and the fact changes how economists think about the problem. The paper’s job is to argue that **composition is first-order even when average shifts are moderate**.

Right now it partly succeeds, but the rhetoric overshoots. Calling the effect “profound” and “arms race” risks making the estimates look smaller than advertised. The paper should let the conceptual point do the work rather than trying to inflate the magnitude.

Then give one sentence on design. Save the battery of diagnostics for later.

**2. Move the shift-share-literature contribution to the back of the intro or cut it substantially.**  
As written, it dilutes the paper’s identity. This is not a methods paper.

**3. Front-load the most interesting substantive result.**  
The most interesting substantive result is not the exact coefficient. It is:
- counties more exposed to Mallinckrodt got stronger oxycodone after the high-dose rollout;
- this operated through the 20mg+ range;

That should appear before discussion of coefficient stability.

**4. Tighten the institutional background.**  
The background is generally fine, but some details on corporate lineage and product counts can be compressed. More emphasis should go to why distributors create persistent local exposure and why that matters economically.

**5. Be selective about robustness in the main text.**  
The leave-one-state-out exercise and some ancillary decomposition can probably be shortened or appendix’d in the narrative version. The hydrocodone placebo feels important enough for the main text because it supports the substantive interpretation.

**6. Rework the conclusion.**  
The conclusion is mostly summary plus a policy nod. It should do more conceptual work:
- what economists should now think differently about,
- what this implies for regulated generic markets more generally,
- and what the key limitation is.

A good conclusion for this paper should broaden the lesson beyond opioids.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

At present, the paper feels like a **good field-journal paper with a top-journal premise**. The gap is not primarily technical. It is mainly one of **framing and ambition**, with some residual scope concerns.

### What is the main gap?

**Mostly a framing problem, secondarily an ambition problem.**

- **Framing problem:** The paper has a better idea than its current self-presentation. It should be about the economics of product composition in a major public-health market, not about a clean shift-share shock involving one company.

There is also a mild **novelty risk**: opioid-supply papers are crowded territory. The paper needs to make the reader feel this is not “one more ARCOS paper,” but rather “a different way of thinking about pharmaceutical supply.”

### What would excite the top 10 people in this field?
Either of two versions:

1. **Big-concept version:**  
   Use the opioid setting to make a broader point about how firms in regulated generic markets compete through product attributes with public-health externalities.

2. **High-consequence version:**  
   Show that the potency shift had meaningful downstream implications—prescribing behavior, misuse proxies, substitution patterns, overdose outcomes, or even law-enforcement/supply-chain responses.

The current paper hints at both, but fully delivers neither.

### Single most impactful piece of advice
**Reframe the paper around a bigger economic question—how competition in regulated pharmaceutical markets changes the composition, not just the quantity, of risky supply—and make “Mallinckrodt’s rollout” the vehicle for that claim rather than the claim itself.**
