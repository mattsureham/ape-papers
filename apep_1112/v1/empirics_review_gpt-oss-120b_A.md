**2. Summary**  

The paper investigates whether antitrust‑mandated unwinding of airline coordination reverses the fare increases generated by that coordination. Using a two‑shock DiD on 310 JetBlue–American routes in the Northeast, the author finds that fares rose about 7 % after the alliance formed and remained 4–5 % above the pre‑alliance level after the court‑ordered dissolution—a persistence (“ratchet’’) of roughly 60 %. The effect is concentrated on high‑volume routes and appears to stem from lasting changes in market structure (fewer competing carriers).

**3. Essential Points**  

1. **Parallel‑Trends Assumption and COVID Shock**  
   *Issue:* The event‑study coefficients show large pre‑treatment movements (e.g., +17 % in Q2 2020) driven by the pandemic, raising doubts about the parallel‑trends required for DiD. Simply “excluding the COVID quarters’’ (Table 5, row 5) is not sufficient because the pandemic may have permanently altered demand trends on hub‑airport routes.  
   *Required fix:* Provide a more convincing test of parallel trends. One option is to construct a synthetic‑control‑type weighted combination of control routes that matches the treated trends pre‑2020, then re‑estimate the DiD. Alternatively, include route‑specific time trends *and* interact them with a pandemic dummy to capture differential COVID dynamics, and show that the treatment coefficients remain robust.  

2. **Potential Spillovers to Control Routes**  
   *Issue:* The control group consists of other routes serving the same four airports. Capacity reallocation, slot swaps, or strategic shifts by non‑NEA carriers could affect these routes, biasing the estimated treatment effect downward (i.e., the “ratchet’’ may be larger than reported).  
   *Required fix:* Conduct a spillover robustness check using (i) a “clean‑control’’ sample of routes that operate exclusively from non‑NEA airports, and (ii) a distance‑based approach (e.g., exclude routes within 50 nm of the hub airports). Demonstrate that the main results hold when spillover‑prone controls are removed.  

3. **Interpretation of the “Ratchet’’ (β₂) Coefficient**  
   *Issue:* The paper interprets β₂ (the post‑dissolution dummy) as the *remaining* fare premium. However, β₂ is estimated relative to the *pre‑alliance* baseline, not relative to the “formation’’ level. Because the formation effect (β₁) is estimated with a single dummy that averages across ~2.5 years, any within‑formation dynamics (e.g., a gradual price increase) are obscured, making the ratio β₂/β₁ a noisy measure of persistence.  
   *Required fix:* Re‑estimate the model with separate leads and lags for the formation period (as done in the event study) and compute the *difference* between the average formation coefficient (e.g., average of Q1 2021–Q2 2023) and the post‑dissolution coefficient. This yields a clearer “reversion’’ metric and allows you to test formally whether the post‑dissolution level is statistically different from the formation level.

If the authors cannot satisfactorily address any of the three points above, the paper should be **rejected** for lack of credible identification.

**4. Suggestions**  

*These recommendations are non‑essential but will considerably strengthen the manuscript.*

1. **Refine the Treatment Definition**  
   - Consider imposing a minimum share threshold (e.g., each carrier must account for at least 10 % of quarterly passengers on the route) rather than a flat ticket count. This guards against treating routes where one carrier’s presence was marginal.  
   - Report robustness to alternative thresholds (e.g., 30 tickets, 5 % share) in an appendix.

2. **Alternative Outcome Measures**  
   - The paper already presents median fare and fare‑per‑mile robustness checks. Adding **price dispersion** (e.g., interquartile range or Gini of fares) as a primary outcome would directly test the first mechanism (sticky pricing norms).  
   - Provide a figure showing the evolution of dispersion across the three periods; this will make the “ratchet’’ narrative more vivid.

3. **Capacity and Frequency Checks**  
   - Use the T‑100 segment data (already mentioned in the manifest) to examine whether the number of weekly departures changes persistently after dissolution. A simple regression of log‑departures on the same treatment dummies will complement the carrier‑count results.  
   - If capacity declines are observed, discuss whether the fare persistence could be due to reduced supply rather than purely pricing norms.

4. **Heterogeneity Analyses**  
   - **By airport:** Split the sample into JFK/LGA versus BOS/EWR to see whether the effect is driven by the most congested hub (JFK).  
   - **By route volume:** Define “high‑volume’’ routes (top 25 % of passenger‑weighted fare observations) and estimate treatment effects separately; the paper already hints at this concentration but a formal interaction term would be useful.  
   - **By competition intensity pre‑alliance:** Use pre‑NEA HHI quartiles to test whether the ratchet is stronger where markets were originally more competitive (i.e., where the alliance created a larger shock).

5. **Dynamic Reversion Test**  
   - Extend the post‑dissolution window beyond Q4 2024 if data are available (e.g., include 2025 Q1–Q2). A longer horizon will reveal whether the ratchet gradually fades or remains stable.  
   - Plot the cumulative treatment effect over time to illustrate any “recovery’’ trajectory.

6. **Placebo Tests Using Other Alliances**  
   - As a falsification, apply the same two‑shock DiD to a different airline partnership that was not dissolved (e.g., the Southwest‑Alaska “code‑share” that persisted). The expectation is a null effect, which will bolster confidence that the observed ratchet is specific to the NEA dissolution.

7. **Discussion of Antitrust Policy Implications**  
   - The conclusion could be enriched by quantifying the *consumer surplus* loss using standard demand elasticity estimates from the airline literature (e.g., price elasticity ≈ ‑0.6). Translate the 4.4 % persistent premium into a dollar amount for the affected passenger base.  
   - Mention how the ratchet finding informs the design of consent decrees (e.g., requiring divestiture of slots or mandated entry of new carriers) rather than merely terminating coordination.

8. **Technical Presentation**  
   - Move the event‑study figure from a table to a plotted line graph with confidence bands; visual inspection is far easier for readers.  
   - Clarify the notation in Equation (1): define the “Formation’’ and “Dissolution’’ indicators explicitly (binary vs. continuous).  
   - Include a brief paragraph on why two‑way clustering (route × quarter) is appropriate, perhaps referencing recent Monte‑Carlo evidence on multiway clustering in DiD settings.

9. **Data and Code Transparency**  
   - Provide a reproducible code repository (e.g., a GitHub link) that includes data‑cleaning scripts and Stata/R/Python code to generate all tables and figures. This will help reviewers and future researchers replicate the analysis.

10. **Minor Corrections**  
    - Table 1: the “HHI’’ column should be labeled “HHI × 10 000’’ for consistency with the notes.  
    - In the abstract, replace “passenger‑weighted fares rose 7.4 % … and remained 4.4 % elevated after dissolution’’ with a more precise phrasing: “the passenger‑weighted average fare increased by 7.4 % during the alliance and fell by only 3.0 % after dissolution, leaving a residual 4.4 % premium relative to the pre‑alliance level.”  
    - Ensure that all citations have full bibliographic entries in the reference list (e.g., Ciliberto & Williams 2014, Porter 1999, Askern & Mullin 2010).

Implementing these suggestions will tighten the identification, broaden the evidence base for the proposed mechanism, and make the policy relevance of the “alliance ratchet’’ clearer to both academia and antitrust practitioners.
