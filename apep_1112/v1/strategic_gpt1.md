## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple, important antitrust question: when courts break up a coordination agreement, does competition actually come back? Using the formation and court-ordered dissolution of the JetBlue-American Northeast Alliance, the paper argues that fares rose when coordination began and did not fully fall after the alliance was dissolved, because the alliance changed market structure in ways that persisted.

A busy economist should care because the paper is not really about one airline alliance; it is about the reversibility of antitrust remedies. If dissolving an anticompetitive arrangement does not restore pre-conduct competition, then a great deal of retrospective antitrust thinking is too optimistic.

**Does the paper itself articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Mostly yes, and better than many submissions. The opening gets to the punchline quickly and the question is intelligible. But it still slips too fast into institutional detail (“airline alliances occupy an unusual space...”) before fully crystallizing the broader economic question. The paper’s opening is stronger than average, but it can be sharper and more general-interest.

**What the first two paragraphs should say instead:**

> Antitrust policy often assumes that stopping coordination restores competition. But that need not be true if coordination changes market structure, pricing norms, or rivals’ participation in ways that outlast the agreement itself. This paper studies that question using a rare natural experiment: the formation and court-ordered dissolution of the JetBlue-American Northeast Alliance.
>
> I show that fares on affected routes rose when the alliance began and fell only partially after the court forced it to end. The key implication is broader than airlines: antitrust remedies that merely prohibit conduct may leave much of the consumer harm in place if the conduct has already reshaped the market. The paper calls this dynamic the “alliance ratchet.”

That version leads with the world question, not the case study.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Antitrust enforcement often acts as if stopping bad conduct restores the market to its prior competitive state. In airlines, alliances can mimic mergers through revenue pooling, scheduling coordination, and slot arrangements.

### Tension
But markets are dynamic. If coordination causes rivals to exit, frequencies to shift, or competitive interactions to soften, then ending the agreement may not restore the old equilibrium. The puzzle is whether antitrust dissolution actually reverses harm or merely stops further deterioration.

### Resolution
The paper finds that fares rose during the NEA and fell only partially after dissolution; the residual premium is concentrated on high-volume routes, and the market appears to remain structurally less competitive.

### Implications
Behavioral or conduct remedies may systematically overpromise. By the time coordination is unwound, some consumer harm may already be baked into the market. That points toward stronger ex ante enforcement or structural remedies.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
It has one, but it is not fully disciplined. The paper does have setup-tension-resolution-implications, which already puts it ahead of many empirics papers. But there is some slippage into being a **collection of alliance facts** rather than a tightly organized argument about reversibility.

The biggest source of narrative drift is the coexistence of several possible stories:

1. airline alliance fare effects,
2. passenger-weighted vs unweighted divergence,
3. persistent market structure,
4. tacit collusion,
5. antitrust remedy design.

That is too many stories competing for center stage.

### What story should it be telling?
One story only:

> **Coordination is easier to start than to unwind.**  
> The NEA provides a rare chance to observe both onset and forced termination of coordination. The central finding is incomplete reversal. The reason matters: market structure changed. Therefore, antitrust remedies that only end conduct may recover far less consumer welfare than policymakers assume.

The weighted/unweighted divergence should support that story, not become a separate mini-paper. Tacit collusion should be mentioned as a possible channel but not overplayed if the paper’s strongest mechanism evidence is structural persistence.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?
“Its main result is that when a court forced JetBlue and American to dissolve their alliance, most of the fare increase did not go away.”

Better still:  
**“Antitrust unwinding recovered less than half the fare increase, and the remaining premium was concentrated on the routes most passengers actually use.”**

That is the hook.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
They would lean in—if presented as an antitrust-remedy paper. They would reach for their phones if it is presented as “a DB1B paper on the NEA.” The issue is not the evidence; it is the packaging.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Probably one of these:

- “Why didn’t fares revert—was it tacit collusion or entry/exit?”
- “Is this specific to slot-constrained airports?”
- “Should we think of this as a general lesson for antitrust remedies or just for airlines?”
- “How much should antitrust authorities discount expected benefits of behavioral remedies?”

Those are good questions. The introduction should be written to invite exactly them.

### If the findings are modest: is the modesty interesting?
Yes, but the paper needs to say so more explicitly. This is not a giant headline treatment effect paper; it is a **partial reversal** paper. Partial reversal is substantively interesting because the null benchmark is not “zero effect,” but “full restoration.” Learning that competition comes back only partway is valuable.

The author should emphasize that the policy counterfactual is not “did the court help?” but “did the court restore the status quo ante?” On the paper’s own telling, the answer is no. That is the economically interesting result.
