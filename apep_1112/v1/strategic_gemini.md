## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a fundamental question for competition policy: when the government wins an antitrust case and dissolves an anticompetitive alliance, does competition actually return? Using the complete lifecycle of the JetBlue-American Northeast Alliance (NEA), the paper finds that while fares rose 7.4% during coordination, they fell by only 3% after court-ordered dissolution, leaving a persistent 4.4% "ratchet" effect—a finding of major consequence for the design of antitrust remedies.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this very well. The first two paragraphs of the introduction are punchy and establish the "natural experiment" and the high-stakes outcome immediately. The existing pitch is strong.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**Contribution:** The paper identifies an "alliance ratchet" where coordination-induced fare increases persist after legal dissolution due to irreversible changes in market structure (competitor exit).

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It is well-differentiated. While Agrawal & Ni (2024) look at the *formation* of the NEA, this is the first to exploit the *dissolution* to test reversibility.
*   **Framing:** It is framed as a question about the **WORLD** (does antitrust enforcement actually work?) rather than just a gap in literature. This is a significant strength for the AER.
*   **Clarity:** A smart economist would immediately understand the "ratchet" concept. It moves beyond "another DiD paper" by linking the pricing result to a specific, persistent structural mechanism (carrier counts).
*   **Making it bigger:** To make this truly "top-5" massive, the author should lean harder into the **General Equilibrium of Antitrust**. If behavioral remedies (just saying "stop coordinating") don't work because the market structure has already been "broken," then the paper isn't just about airlines; it’s a critique of the entire DOJ/FTC playbook for non-merger conduct cases.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

*   **Closest Neighbors:** Borenstein (1990) on airport dominance; Kim and Singal (1993) on merger price effects; Ciliberto and Williams (2014) on tacit collusion; and Wollmann (2019) on "stealth" consolidation.
*   **Strategy:** The paper currently builds on these. It should arguably be more aggressive in "attacking" the implicit assumption in the merger retrospective literature (e.g., Ashenfelter et al.) that the harm of a deal is simply the delta during its existence. This paper suggests the harm is a "stock," not a "flow."
*   **Narrow vs. Broad:** The audience is correctly broad. It speaks to IO, Law and Economics, and Applied Micro.
*   **Missing Conversations:** The paper could benefit from connecting to the **"Hysteresis"** literature in macro/trade. The idea that a temporary shock (the alliance) has permanent effects (the ratchet) is a classic hysteresis problem.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

*   **Setup:** Antitrust authorities assume that stopping bad behavior restores the status quo.
*   **Tension:** The NEA was a massive, court-dissolved experiment. If the assumption holds, fares should revert. If not, the "remedy" is an illusion.
*   **Resolution:** Fares don't revert. Specifically, they don't revert on the high-volume routes because the alliance killed off the "competitive fringe" (other carriers) who didn't come back just because a judge signed an order.
*   **Implications:** Structural remedies (divestitures) are not optional; they are necessary because pricing is "sticky" once the competitive environment is degraded.

**Evaluation:** The arc is excellent. It is a "story" paper, not a "results" paper.

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

*   **The Fact:** "The DOJ won the case, but the passengers still paid 60% of the overcharge."
*   **Reaction:** Lean in. This is a "common sense" result that economists suspect but rarely see identified so cleanly.
*   **Follow-up:** "Why didn't the other airlines come back?" The paper answers this (Table 4, carrier counts), which is the most important part of the dinner conversation.

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

*   **Front-loading:** The paper is well-structured. However, the divergence between "unweighted" and "passenger-weighted" results (Table 2) is so central to the "welfare" story that it needs even more prominent placement.
*   **Appendix:** The standardized effect size table (Table 6) is a bit "report-like." I would integrate the core of those magnitudes into the main text discussion to emphasize the economic (not just statistical) significance.
*   **The "Ratchet" Definition:** Be careful with the term "Ratchet" vs "Tacit Collusion." The paper does a good job in the Discussion, but the distinction should be airtight. Is it a ratchet because of *costs/norms* or because of *entry barriers*?

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The gap is **Ambition regarding the "Mechanism of Re-entry."**

The paper shows carrier counts stay low. An AER-level paper would ideally dig deeper into **why** they don't re-enter. Is it because JetBlue/American kept the slots? Is it because the "threat" of future coordination looms? If the author can provide even descriptive evidence on *which* specific competitors left and *why* they stayed away (e.g., slot constraints vs. gate access), the paper moves from a "very good airline paper" to a "definitive antitrust paper."

**Single most impactful advice:** Quantify the "Remedy Gap" in dollar terms of consumer surplus and explicitly link the lack of fare reversion to **specific entry barriers** (like slot control at JFK/LGA) to prove the "Ratchet" is structural.
