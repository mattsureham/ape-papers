## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple, important antitrust question: when courts unwind an anticompetitive agreement, does competition actually come back? Using the formation and court-ordered dissolution of the JetBlue–American Northeast Alliance, the paper argues that fares on the most important routes rose under coordination and did not fully return to baseline after the alliance ended, suggesting that antitrust remedies may be less reversible than policymakers assume.

A busy economist should care because the paper is really about the effectiveness of antitrust remedies, not just about one airline alliance. If coordination leaves durable changes in market structure or pricing, then “unwinding” conduct may not restore the pre-violation world.

**Does the paper itself articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Mostly yes, but not optimally. The opening is stronger than average, and the core idea appears quickly. But the paper slightly undersells the broader question and overspecializes too fast into airlines. The first two paragraphs should more explicitly frame the paper as a general test of remedy reversibility in antitrust, with the NEA as a rare empirical setting.

**The pitch the paper should have:**

> Antitrust policy often assumes that stopping unlawful coordination restores competition. But that assumption is rarely tested, because economists seldom observe both the onset of coordination and its forced unwinding in the same market. This paper uses the formation and court-ordered dissolution of the JetBlue–American Northeast Alliance to ask whether antitrust remedies actually reverse harm or whether coordination leaves persistent effects on prices and market structure.
>
> The answer, in the paper’s preferred traveler-weighted estimates, is that fares on affected high-traffic routes rose during the alliance and fell only partially after dissolution. The broader implication is that conduct remedies may be much less effective than standard policy analysis assumes: once coordination reshapes the competitive landscape, ending the agreement may not be enough to restore the status quo ante.

That is the paper’s AER-relevant pitch. Right now the introduction is close, but it still reads a bit like “an airline paper with an antitrust implication” rather than “an antitrust paper with an airline setting.”

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Antitrust enforcement often assumes that dissolving an anticompetitive arrangement restores competition. Airline alliances are a good place to test this because they can approximate merger-like coordination without a formal merger, and the NEA was both formed and then judicially dismantled.

### Tension
We have lots of work on whether coordination raises prices, but much less on whether unwinding coordination reverses those effects. If prices remain elevated after dissolution, then the usual remedy logic is incomplete.

### Resolution
The paper claims that in passenger-weighted terms fares rose during the alliance and only partially fell after dissolution, leaving a meaningful residual premium, especially on high-volume routes.

### Implications
If coordination changes market structure in durable ways, then conduct remedies may restore less competition than antitrust agencies and courts expect. That pushes policy toward earlier intervention or more structural remedies.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
**Serviceable, but not fully disciplined.**

The bones are there, but the story is currently split between two different papers:

- **Paper A:** Antitrust remedies do not fully reverse harm.
- **Paper B:** The average route effect is small, but high-volume routes saw persistent price increases.

Those are related, but the paper has not fully integrated them. In fact, one of the most striking features is that the unweighted route-level estimates look weak or even opposite-signed relative to the weighted ones. That is not fatal, but it means the narrative cannot simply be “the NEA raised fares and dissolution didn’t fix it.” It has to be:

> The alliance’s harms were highly concentrated on the routes that matter most for traveler welfare, and those harms persisted after dissolution.

That is a sharper and more honest story.

### If it is a collection of results looking for a story, what story should it be telling?
It should tell this story:

> Temporary coordination can have permanent-looking effects where market stakes are largest, because the relevant object is not the average market but the high-volume market where competitive structure matters most. The NEA offers a rare test of whether antitrust can truly put competition back together after coordination has already reshaped the market.

That story can absorb the weighted/unweighted contrast instead of being embarrassed by it.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?
I would lead with:

> “When the court killed the JetBlue–American alliance, most of the fare increase on the busiest affected routes did not disappear.”

That is the most interesting version. Not “310 treated routes,” not “two-shock DiD,” not even “alliance ratchet.” Just the fact.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
**They would lean in, if presented as an antitrust-remedies fact.**  
They might reach for their phones if presented as a niche airline pricing exercise.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Almost certainly:

> “Why didn’t prices revert?”

And then:

> “Is this because the alliance changed market structure, because firms learned to coordinate, or because something else about these routes changed?”

That is good news: the natural follow-up is exactly the paper’s intended mechanism.

### If the findings are modest or mixed, is that okay?
Yes, but only if the paper embraces the mixed pattern rather than smoothing it over. The unweighted estimates are not consistent with a broad, average-route fare ratchet. The interesting result is **concentrated persistence**. That is still quite publishable as a fact, but the paper should not oversell it as a universal route-level phenomenon.

The null-ish unweighted result is not a failed experiment; it is evidence that the welfare-relevant action is concentrated. But the paper needs to make that case much more deliberately.
