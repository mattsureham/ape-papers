## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether a sharp price cap in a homebuyer subsidy becomes a de facto price ceiling for sellers. Using the universe of Irish housing transactions, it shows that new-build sales bunch just below the €500,000 Help to Buy eligibility threshold, suggesting that developers adjust prices to preserve subsidy eligibility and therefore capture part of the transfer intended for first-time buyers.

Why should a busy economist care? Because this is a clean illustration of a general equilibrium design problem in means-tested subsidies: when eligibility is tied to a price notch, market participants may reorganize transactions around the rule, altering incidence and distorting product-market outcomes.

**Does the paper articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Reasonably well, but not as sharply as it could. The current opening is competent and intuitive, but it still reads like a well-executed bunching application in a specific setting. For AER purposes, the paper needs to announce a bigger question immediately: **when do policy thresholds become market prices?** The first paragraphs should make clear that this is not mainly “an Ireland housing paper,” but a paper about **the incidence and design of capped subsidies in markets with strategic sellers**.

### The pitch the paper should have

Governments often cap subsidies at a maximum eligible price in order to target benefits to “affordable” goods. But when sellers can price strategically, such caps may not just ration eligibility—they may become focal prices that reshape the market and shift subsidy incidence to producers. This paper studies that mechanism in Ireland’s Help to Buy program and shows that the €500,000 eligibility threshold generates sharp bunching of new-build transactions just below the cap, implying that developers actively price to preserve eligibility and capture part of the subsidy.

That framing says “this is about subsidy design and incidence in regulated markets,” not merely “there is bunching in Ireland.”

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Governments often use price caps in homebuyer subsidies to target support toward affordable housing and limit fiscal cost. In principle, such caps determine who is eligible.

### Tension
But when sellers can influence the transaction price, the cap may become a strategic focal point. Instead of merely selecting beneficiaries, the rule may reshape prices and alter incidence. The puzzle is whether the cap is just an administrative threshold or actually a market-making force.

### Resolution
In Ireland, new-build transactions pile up just below the €500,000 threshold, while second-hand homes and placebo thresholds do not, indicating that the cap materially distorts pricing behavior. The evidence suggests that developers compress prices downward to keep units subsidy-eligible.

### Implications
Sharp eligibility thresholds can undermine the intended targeting of subsidies by transferring part of the benefit to sellers and distorting product design or quality. Policy design should therefore pay more attention to market responses to discrete caps.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
It has a **serviceable** arc, but it is still too close to “setup → methodology → result → robustness.” The story exists, but the paper does not yet fully dramatize the tension. The tension should be sharper:

- Policymakers think they are drawing a line around “affordable homes.”
- Markets interpret that line as a **focal price**.
- As a result, the policy both redistributes and reshapes the goods being sold.

That is a strong narrative. Right now the paper mostly says: “there is bunching, and that matters.” It should say: **the targeting rule itself becomes an object of strategic pricing.**

This would also help the discussion section, which is currently sensible but somewhat after-the-fact. The “incidence/design distortion” story should not first appear in Section 6; it should organize the whole paper from page 1.
