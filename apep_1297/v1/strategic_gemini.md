**SUBJECT:** Strategic Assessment of "The Developer’s Ceiling: Price Bunching at Ireland’s Help to Buy Cap"

---

### 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
The paper examines how a large, notched housing subsidy in Ireland (Help to Buy) distorts the price distribution of new homes. By analyzing nearly 800,000 transactions, it finds that the €500,000 eligibility cap creates a massive "developer's ceiling," forcing prices downward and causing a significant portion of the subsidy to be captured by the supply side or lost to quality reductions.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this clearly, particularly in identifying the "developer's ceiling" as the central motif. However, the first two paragraphs are somewhat technical. They should lead more aggressively with the **fiscal efficiency** angle: governments spend billions on "affordability" only to inadvertently create price floors and ceilings that distort the very market they aim to help.

**Revised Pitch for Paragraph 1:**
"When governments use price-capped subsidies to aid first-time buyers, they intend to target the poor; instead, they often create 'notches' that dictate market prices. This paper shows that in Ireland, the €500,000 subsidy cap has become a universal anchor for the housing market, where the 'market price' is no longer a reflection of value, but a strategic response to tax policy."

---

### 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**Contribution:** The paper identifies and quantifies a massive price distortion (approx. €11,700 per transaction) caused by a subsidy notch, using a clean "new vs. second-hand" placebo design that isolates policy-driven bunching from psychological round-number bias.

- **Differentiation:** It stands apart from Best & Kleven (2018) by looking at *subsidies* (positive incentives) rather than *taxes* (negative incentives), and by having a perfect control group (second-hand homes) in the same geographic market.
- **Framing:** It is currently framed as "filling a gap" in the bunching/housing literature. It needs to be framed as a question about **Policy Incidence and Market Failure**: *Who actually gets the check when the government subsidizes a house?*
- **The "So What?":** A smart economist would say, "It's a very clean bunching paper." To make it "AER big," it needs to address the **quality margin**. If prices are stuck at €499k, are developers just building smaller houses? Without that, we don't know if this is a transfer of surplus or a deadweight loss of square footage.

---

### 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
The paper sits at the intersection of Public Finance (Kleven, Saez) and Urban Economics (Hilber, Turner).

- **Closest Neighbors:** Best & Kleven (2018) on UK stamp duty; Kopczuk & Munroe (2015) on mansion taxes; and the broader incidence literature (Chetty et al.).
- **Strategy:** It should **synthesize**. It currently "adds to" these papers. It should instead position itself as the "cleanest evidence to date of supply-side capture in housing."
- **Missing Conversations:** The paper should speak to the **Macro/Inflation** literature. The finding that nominal rigidity in policy caps (fixed at €500k since 2017) causes *increasing* distortions as inflation rises is a very "AER" point that is currently under-emphasized.

---

### 4. NARRATIVE ARC
- **Setup:** Governments want to help buyers with cash grants.
- **Tension:** To save money, they cap the grants. This creates a "cliff" (notch).
- **Resolution:** Developers "price to the notch," creating a massive spike in transactions just below the limit.
- **Implications:** Subsidies don't just lower prices; they *freeze* them, potentially degrading the quality of the national housing stock.

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong but the "Implications" section is the weakest. It needs to move from "here is a bunching ratio" to "here is how much taxpayer money was wasted."

---

### 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
At a dinner party, I’d lead with: *"In Ireland, the government tried to help people buy homes by capping the price at €500k, and now almost every new house costs exactly €499,000, even if it's worth much more or much less."*

- **Reaction:** People lean in.
- **Follow-up:** "Does that mean the houses are getting smaller?" (The paper currently can't answer this—this is the "AER gap").

---

### 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
- **Front-load:** Move the Dublin vs. Non-Dublin results earlier. It’s the best evidence that the "at-risk" density drives the distortion.
- **Appendix:** Move Table 4 (Polynomial orders) to the appendix. We believe the bunching is real; we don't need five versions of it in the main text.
- **Expansion:** The "Incidence" section in the Discussion (Section 6) should be its own section with at least a *proxy* for quality (e.g., matching the PPR data to planning permissions or floor area data if available).

---

### 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The gap is **Ambition**. Right now, it is a perfectly executed technical exercise. To be an AER paper, it needs to solve the "Price vs. Quality" mystery.

**Single Biggest Advice:** Find a way to proxy for "house size" or "amenities" (perhaps through scraping property listings or matching to building energy ratings/BER data). If you can show that €499k buys a smaller house in 2024 than in 2017 because of this cap, you have a top-5 paper.
