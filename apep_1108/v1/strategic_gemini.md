---

### 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
The paper investigates whether the largest industrial policy in modern U.S. history—the $52.7 billion CHIPS Act—has triggered the "reshoring rent" crisis feared by advocates and the media. By exploiting the staggered announcement of multi-billion dollar grants across 21 counties, the author finds a precisely estimated null effect on home values and a slight decrease in rents. It suggests that industrial policy, when targeted at supply-elastic regions (like the Sun Belt), can expand production without displacing local residents.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this well in the first two paragraphs, though it leans slightly too heavily on "media reports" as the foil. The pitch the paper *should* have is more focused on the theoretical tension of place-based policies: do they generate local welfare via jobs, or destroy it via capitalization into housing?

### 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
The paper provides a precisely estimated null effect of massive localized capital injections on housing markets, suggesting that supply elasticity in "Sun Belt" industrial hubs mitigates the standard displacement concerns of place-based policy.

*   **Differentiation:** It differentiates itself from **Greenstone et al. (2010)** (Million Dollar Plants) by showing that the "winner’s curse" of rising home values is not a law of nature.
*   **Framing:** It is framed as answering a question about the **WORLD** (the costs of industrial policy), though the abstract drifts back into "gap-filling" language.
*   **Clarity:** A smart economist would say: "It’s a CHIPS Act paper that finds zero housing displacement, likely because the factories are going to the Sun Belt where they actually build houses."
*   **Bigger Contribution:** To make this "AER big," the paper needs to move beyond the null and prove the *mechanism*. It currently speculates that supply elasticity is the reason. If the author could show that the null *only* exists in high-elasticity counties (using Saiz or Wharton indices) while finding a positive effect in inelastic ones, the contribution moves from a "local null" to a "general theory of industrial policy placement."

### 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
The paper sits at the intersection of **Place-Based Policies** (Bartik, 2019; Slattery & Zidar, 2020) and **Urban Supply Elasticity** (Glaeser et al., 2005; Saiz, 2010).

*   **Neighbors:** Greenstone, Hornbeck, and Moretti (2010) on plant openings; Diamond (2016) on local welfare; and the nascent CHIPS literature (Erten et al., 2025).
*   **Strategy:** The paper should **attack** the assumption that industrial policy necessarily leads to "reshoring rents." It currently "contrasts" with them—it should be more aggressive in defining the conditions under which the "Million Dollar Plant" effect fails.
*   **Conversation:** The paper is having the right conversation but could benefit from connecting to the **Internal Migration** literature. If prices didn't move, did people? Or are these jobs being filled by the existing local unemployed?

### 4. NARRATIVE ARC
*   **Setup:** The U.S. is spending $50B+ to bring back manufacturing, concentrated in a few hubs.
*   **Tension:** Economic theory suggests these "shocks" should be capitalized into land values, potentially hurting the very people (local workers) the policy aims to help.
*   **Resolution:** It didn't happen. Prices didn't budge.
*   **Implications:** Industrial policy is "safe" for renters if you pick the right (elastic) spots.

**Evaluation:** The arc is clean. However, the "Resolution" is currently a bit of a "shrug." The narrative would be stronger if the "Resolution" explained *why* (e.g., a "Supply-Side Industrial Policy" success story).

### 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
*   **The Lead Fact:** "The government dropped $8 billion on a single county in Ohio, and Zillow didn't even notice."
*   **Reaction:** Lean in. Every economist is currently obsessed with the "Supply Side" and "Industrial Policy."
*   **Follow-up:** "Is it because the houses aren't built yet, or because they're building them so fast in the Sun Belt?" (This is the critical question the author must answer to survive).

### 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
*   **Front-load the Heterogeneity:** The split between "High Elasticity" vs. "Low Elasticity" counties shouldn't be a discussion point (Section 6); it should be a core table in Section 5.
*   **Appendix:** The "Leave-one-out" is standard robustness; it's fine as a table but doesn't need much prose.
*   **The "Muted Rent Effect":** The negative rent result is the "smoking gun" for a supply-side response. If the author can show that CHIPS announcements triggered *housing permits*, that should be a main result, not a footnote.

### 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The paper is currently a "very good" empirical note. To be an AER paper, it needs **Ambitious Mechanisms.** 

A null result is only interesting if you can explain why it's a null. Is it because the market is efficient and elastic? Or is it because the "shock" is small relative to the denominator? The author admits that 6,000 jobs in Maricopa County is only 0.3% of the workforce. If the answer is "the shock was actually small," it’s not an AER paper. If the answer is "the shock was huge, but supply responded perfectly," that is a landmark paper.

**Single most impactful advice:** Prove the supply response. Use building permit data (census) at the county level to show that CHIPS announcements led to an anticipation of housing demand that was met by new supply, thereby keeping prices flat.
