# Review: The Floor That Didn't Bite: Geneva's Record Minimum Wage and Cross-Border Labor Flows

## 1. Idea Fidelity
The paper adheres closely to the original idea manifest. It utilizes the specified data source (Swiss Federal Statistical Office Grenzgängerstatistik), covers the mandated time period (2002–2025), and focuses on the precise policy shock identified (Geneva's November 2020 minimum wage). The identification strategy follows the proposed triple-difference (DDD) design, exploiting sector-level variation in minimum wage bite. While the manifest mentioned using all 27 cantons and 86 sectors, the paper justifiably narrows the sample to five border cantons and 15 high/low-bite sectors to sharpen identification and reduce noise. This is a standard and appropriate refinement for causal inference rather than a deviation from the core idea. The outcome variable (quarterly cross-border worker counts) matches the manifest exactly.

## 2. Summary
This paper estimates the impact of Geneva's world-record minimum wage (CHF 23.27/hr) on the flow of cross-border workers from France. Using administrative data and a triple-difference design comparing high-bite versus low-bite sectors across border cantons, the author finds a precisely estimated null effect on worker counts. The result challenges standard competitive labor market predictions in a setting with highly elastic labor supply, suggesting either monopsony dynamics, wage compression above the floor, or formalization effects offsetting displacement.

## 3. Essential Points
The paper is well-executed but faces three critical econometric hurdles that must be addressed to sustain the causal claim.

1.  **Control Group Contamination (Vaud):** The paper lists Vaud as a primary control canton, asserting it did not have a minimum wage during the period. However, Vaud approved a cantonal minimum wage in September 2020, effective January 2021. Since Geneva's treatment began November 2020, the post-periods overlap significantly. Including Vaud in the control group biases the difference-in-differences estimator toward zero (attenuation bias), as the "control" group was also treated shortly after. You must either exclude Vaud from the control group or explicitly model Vaud's treatment effect in a multi-treatment DDD framework.
2.  **Inference and Clustering Levels:** The treatment variation is at the canton level (only one treated canton: Geneva), yet standard errors are clustered at the canton-sector level (75 clusters). While 75 clusters exceeds the common rule of thumb (>50), the lack of variation in the treatment dimension (only Geneva) makes inference fragile to canton-specific serial correlation. If shocks to Geneva's labor market are correlated across sectors (e.g., a tourism recovery affecting both hospitality and retail), canton-sector clustering will underestimate standard errors. You should report wild cluster bootstrap confidence intervals at the canton level or acknowledge the limitation of having only one treated unit.
3.  **Hours vs. Heads Margin:** The outcome is worker counts, not hours worked. A null effect on heads is consistent with the competitive model if employers adjusted along the intensive margin (reducing hours per worker) rather than the extensive margin. Given the high wage floor, hour reduction is a plausible substitution effect. The paper currently relegates this to the discussion section; it should be elevated to a primary limitation. Without wage or hours data, the "null result" on labor demand is incomplete.

## 4. Suggestions
The following recommendations are intended to strengthen the robustness and interpretability of the findings. While not strictly fatal if unaddressed, implementing them would significantly elevate the paper's contribution to the literature.

**Refine the Control Group Strategy**
Beyond the Vaud contamination issue, consider the economic similarity of the remaining control cantons. Neuchâtel and Jura are smaller and economically distinct from Geneva (less finance/international orgs). Basel-Stadt is a better economic comparator but geographically distant from France (cross-border workers come from Germany/France). I suggest running a specification that excludes Vaud entirely to see if the standard errors widen significantly. If the null result holds with only Basel-Stadt, Neuchâtel, and Jura, the finding is more robust. Additionally, consider a synthetic control method at the canton level to construct a weighted combination of control cantons that better matches Geneva's pre-treatment sectoral composition. This would mitigate concerns that the simple average of control cantons does not represent the counterfactual Geneva trend.

**Strengthen Inference with Permutation Tests**
Given the single treated canton, conventional asymptotic inference is risky. I recommend implementing a permutation test (placebo-in-space) where you iterate through each control canton, pretending it was the treated unit, and estimate the DDD coefficient. This generates an empirical distribution of coefficients under the null hypothesis. If Geneva's coefficient lies well within this distribution, the null result is robust. If Geneva's coefficient is an outlier (even if statistically insignificant in the main table), it suggests unique dynamics. Report the p-value from this permutation distribution alongside the conventional clustered SEs. This is standard practice in single-treated-unit literature (e.g., *Abadie et al.*) and would bolster confidence in the "precisely estimated null" claim.

**Investigate Anticipation Effects**
Geneva's minimum wage was debated for nearly a decade before implementation (2011 initiative, 2020 referendum). Firms often adjust hiring freezes or wage structures in anticipation of such policies. The event study shows flat pre-trends, but this may mask anticipation if firms adjusted slowly starting in 2018 or 2019. I suggest interacting the treatment variable with a "high anticipation" indicator (e.g., sectors with higher union density or previous wage violations) to see if adjustment happened earlier. Alternatively, shift the treatment date back to January 2020 (pre-COVID) in a robustness check to see if the coefficient absorbs any pre-implementation adjustment. If the result is sensitive to the exact treatment month, the interpretation shifts from "no effect" to "effect occurred earlier."

**Address Zero-Inflation and Transformation**
The paper uses $\log(\text{CBW} + 1)$ to handle zeros. However, Table 1 shows median counts as low as 207 for control sectors, implying some sector-canton-quarter cells may indeed be zero or very small. The inverse hyperbolic sine (IHS) transformation is used in robustness, but the coefficient interpretation differs from logs. I suggest reporting the Poisson QMLE results (Column 5) more prominently if the data contains many zeros, as this models the count process directly without transformation bias. Additionally, check the distribution of residuals; if the data is highly skewed (many small sectors, few large ones), the mean-based OLS estimates might be driven by large sectors (e.g., Employment Activities). A weighted least squares specification, weighting by sector size, might provide a more representative estimate of the average worker's experience.

**Deepen the Ticino Replication**
The paper mentions a Ticino replication (CHF 19 minimum wage, Italian workers) yielding a null. This is a valuable external validity check but is currently under-utilized. Ticino's economy is distinct (more tourism/construction, less finance). I recommend pooling Geneva and Ticino in a stacked difference-in-differences design. This increases the number of treated units from one to two, improving inference. You can interact the treatment with a "Canton" dummy to test if the effect differs by the height of the minimum wage (CHF 23 vs. CHF 19). If both show nulls, the evidence for "no disemployment" across different wage floors and border contexts becomes much stronger.

**Clarify the "Bite" Measurement**
The bite classification relies on the 2018 Wage Structure Survey (LSE). However, wage distributions shift over time. A sector with 35% bite in 2018 might have only 10% bite in 2020 due to general wage growth. I suggest updating the bite measure using the 2020 or 2021 LSE data if available, or at least discussing how general wage inflation in Switzerland (which was positive during this period) might have eroded the real value of the CHF 23 floor. If the bite was smaller at implementation than estimated in 2018, the null result is less surprising. A sensitivity analysis varying the bite threshold (e.g., >10% vs. >20%) would show whether the result is driven by the specific classification cutoff.

**Expand on Policy Implications**
The discussion touches on the EU Adequate Minimum Wages Directive. I suggest expanding this to discuss the specific mechanism of *enforcement*. Cross-border workers are harder to inspect than domestic workers. If enforcement is weak in high-bite sectors (e.g., cleaning, hospitality), the statutory wage floor may not be the effective floor. Adding a paragraph on tripartite commission inspection rates in Geneva versus control cantons would help rule out non-compliance as an alternative explanation for the null. If enforcement was strict and the null still holds, the economic interpretation (monopsony/formalization) is much more credible.

**Visual Enhancements**
Finally, improve the event-study figure. Ensure it includes both point estimates and confidence intervals for each lead and lag. Explicitly mark the pre-trend test p-value (e.g., testing joint significance of all pre-leads). Visual clarity is crucial for convincing readers of the parallel trends assumption, especially when the main result is a null. Consider adding a histogram of the sector-level estimates to show the distribution of effects across the 15 sectors; this helps readers see if the null is an average of offsetting positive and negative effects.

By addressing the control group contamination and strengthening the inference strategy, this paper has the potential to be a definitive reference on minimum wages in transnational labor markets. The data is unique and the question is timely; rigorous econometric handling will ensure the findings withstand scrutiny.
