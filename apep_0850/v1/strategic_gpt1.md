## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple and potentially important question: when a place with unusually open borders adopts an extremely high minimum wage, does mobile low-wage labor get pushed out? Using Geneva’s 2020 introduction of the world’s highest statutory minimum wage and administrative data on French cross-border commuters, the paper argues that even in a setting where labor should be highly mobile, there is no detectable decline in cross-border employment in the sectors where the minimum wage should have bitten most.

Why should a busy economist care? Because Geneva is close to a “stress test” for the standard employment-displacement story: if a very high wage floor is going to price out marginal workers anywhere, one might expect to see it among cross-border commuters in low-wage sectors. A null result in that setting, if persuasive, would matter for how we think about minimum wages, labor mobility, and monopsony.

### Does the paper articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Reasonably well, but not optimally. The introduction currently does many things right: it gives scale, institutional context, and the core prediction. But it gets into design details too quickly, and it undersells the big conceptual hook. The paper’s most attractive feature is not “a triple-difference using Swiss administrative data.” It is: **the paper studies the most extreme plausible case for minimum-wage-induced labor reallocation and finds little evidence of it.**

The first two paragraphs should lean harder into that point.

### The pitch the paper should have

> Minimum wage debates often turn on whether higher wage floors reduce employment, but in most settings that prediction is hard to test cleanly because labor supply is not very mobile. Geneva offers an unusually sharp test. In 2020 it introduced the world’s highest statutory minimum wage while relying heavily on tens of thousands of cross-border commuters from France who can move across jobs and jurisdictions with relatively low frictions.
>
> If minimum wages price out low-wage labor, this is a place we should see it: in low-wage Geneva sectors that employ many cross-border workers. Using administrative data on cross-border employment by sector and canton, I show that these workers did not exit Geneva’s high-bite sectors after the policy. The result suggests that even an extreme wage floor need not reduce employment on one of the most mobile labor margins in Europe.

That is the story. The paper should not start from “I estimate a triple-difference.” It should start from “this is a hard test of a foundational prediction.”

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper’s contribution is to show that Geneva’s extraordinarily high minimum wage did not measurably reduce cross-border employment in the low-wage sectors where the policy should have bound most, despite unusually high worker mobility.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?

Partly, but not sharply enough. The introduction cites Card-Krueger / Dube / Cengiz and a monopsony literature, but the paper’s true differentiator is not “another minimum wage employment paper.” It is:

1. **Cross-border workers as the margin of adjustment**
2. **An unusually extreme policy level**
3. **A very high-mobility setting**
4. **A direct test of whether minimum wages operate as barriers to labor mobility**

That is distinct from the standard domestic minimum wage literature, but the paper risks being read as a small extension of “employment effects are small.” That is too generic and too crowded a lane.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a gap in a literature?

Mixed. The strongest parts are world-facing: what happens to mobile cross-border labor when a border region imposes a very high wage floor? But some of the introduction lapses into “this contributes to three literatures” language and “first credible causal estimate of minimum wages on the composition of cross-border labor flows.” That is literature-gap framing. It is weaker.

AER papers usually lead with a real-world question or a conceptual tension, not a missing cell in a literature matrix.

### Could a smart economist explain to a colleague what’s new here?

Right now, maybe, but only if they are charitable. The likely paraphrase is: “It’s a DiD/DDD paper on Geneva’s minimum wage and cross-border workers; basically they find no effect.”

That is not enough. The desired paraphrase is: “They study perhaps the cleanest extreme case where a minimum wage should have pushed out mobile low-wage labor, and it still doesn’t.”

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Several possibilities:

- **Reframe the outcome more fundamentally.** Right now the paper focuses on “cross-border worker counts by sector.” That sounds narrow. The broader question is whether minimum wages **reallocate mobile labor across jurisdictions**. The paper should foreground jurisdictional labor allocation, not just counts.
- **Show whether the result is about composition across worker origins.** If Geneva did not lose total cross-border workers, did it substitute across French residents, domestic Swiss workers, or other migrants? Even descriptive evidence here would enlarge the stakes.
- **Lean harder into the “barrier to mobility” question.** The paper could be framed as evidence on whether labor standards impede international labor-market integration in border regions.
- **Develop one mechanism more convincingly at the narrative level.** Right now the discussion throws out three possible explanations. That feels diffuse. Pick one central interpretation—e.g., the result is more consistent with employer labor-market power / incidence on firms / limited effective bite than with the competitive textbook model—and structure the paper around that.
- **Clarify whether Geneva is a “most likely case” for displacement.** This is the strongest claim available. If even here there is no cross-border reallocation, that is more generalizable than “Geneva is weird.”

The biggest way to enlarge the contribution is not another table. It is to make the paper about **whether labor standards disrupt mobile labor markets**, not about one more minimum wage estimate.

4. **Manning (2003)** and newer monopsony work such as **Azar et al.**

Potentially also adjacent:

- Spatial equilibrium / local labor market adjustment literature
- Papers on labor standards and informality/formalization, especially if the paper wants to invoke formalization

### How should the paper position itself relative to those neighbors?

- **Build on** the minimum wage literature, not attack it. The result is consistent with the broad modern minimum wage literature finding modest employment effects.
- **Differentiate from** that literature by stressing the margin: this is not standard local employment; it is cross-border mobile labor.
- **Build on and extend** the cross-border labor literature by asking how labor-market integration interacts with labor regulation.
- **Be cautious** about overselling monopsony. Right now the paper leans toward “this null challenges the competitive model and is consistent with monopsony.” That is okay as a suggestive interpretation, but as positioning it may invite skepticism because the paper is not directly testing monopsony.

### Is the paper currently positioned too narrowly or too broadly?

Somehow both.

- **Too narrowly** because the empirical object—cross-border worker counts in Geneva sectors—can sound niche.
- **Too broadly** because the paper claims contribution to three literatures without a single dominant conversation.

The right audience is not “everyone interested in labor.” It is economists interested in **minimum wages under high labor mobility** and **whether integrated labor markets weaken or strengthen employment effects**. That is a precise and potentially important conversation.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?

A few things feel underdeveloped:

- **Spatial labor market adjustment / local labor markets / commuting margins**
- **European border-region labor integration** and cross-border commuting
- **Labor standards and market integration**—how regulation interacts with integrated product/labor markets
- Possibly **migration incidence and destination choice** work, if the paper wants to frame this as reallocation across jurisdictions

The paper cites migration papers, but the connection is not yet organic. It cites them because it feels obliged to, not because it is actively participating in that conversation.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?

Not yet. The current conversation is “minimum wages and employment effects.” That is a crowded conversation in which this paper risks seeming incremental. The better conversation is:

> What happens when labor standards are imposed in highly integrated cross-border labor markets? Do they redirect mobile workers, or not?

That is fresher, more international, and more directly tied to the institutional setting.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Geneva is highly integrated with neighboring France and depends heavily on cross-border commuters. In 2020 it adopts the world’s highest statutory minimum wage. Standard intuition suggests that if labor can move easily and low-wage labor becomes more expensive, firms should cut back on precisely those mobile workers in low-wage sectors.

### Tension

The tension is excellent in principle: **this is a setting where the standard displacement prediction should be especially visible.** If minimum wages reduce employment via substitution away from marginal workers, cross-border commuters in hospitality, retail, and related sectors look like the natural margin. So what happened?

### Resolution

The paper finds no detectable decline in cross-border worker counts in high-bite sectors relative to relevant controls. If anything, the point estimates are weakly positive.

### Implications

The paper implies that minimum wages may not function as de facto barriers to labor mobility even in border regions with highly mobile labor supply. More broadly, it weakens the claim that competitive labor-demand effects should be strongest when labor is very mobile across jurisdictions.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the ingredients, but the arc is only partly realized. The paper currently reads a bit like: interesting setting, straightforward empirical design, null result, some robustness, then discussion. It is coherent, but not fully narrativized.

The biggest issue is that the paper’s central dramatic tension—“this should have been the place where disemployment showed up”—is not sustained strongly enough throughout. The null is presented competently, but the paper doesn’t fully persuade the reader that this null is surprising in a theoretically meaningful way.

### If it is a collection of results looking for a story, what story should it be telling?

It should be telling this story:

1. **Geneva is a hardest test for the displacement view.**
2. **Cross-border workers are the margin most likely to adjust if that view is right.**
3. **The adjustment does not show up where it should.**
4. **Therefore, economists should update not just on Geneva, but on the idea that labor mobility necessarily magnifies minimum wage job loss.**

That is a real story. The current draft is close, but too often slips back into method-first exposition.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?

I would lead with:

> Geneva imposed the world’s highest minimum wage on a labor market that includes roughly 90,000 daily cross-border commuters from France, and the paper finds no detectable decline in cross-border employment in the sectors where the wage floor should have bound most.

That is the one-liner.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

They would lean in initially. The setting is inherently interesting. “World’s highest minimum wage” plus “freely mobile foreign commuters” is a strong hook.

But they might reach for their phones if the presentation quickly turns into “we run a DDD on sector-level counts and get a null.” The paper’s job is to preserve the initial interest by making clear why this null is genuinely informative rather than just underpowered administrative bookkeeping.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Almost certainly:

- “Okay, but did firms cut hours rather than headcount?”
- Or: “Did the policy affect wages, composition, or substitution toward other worker types?”
- Or: “Why should I interpret this as informative about theory rather than just low power?”

Those are exactly the questions the current framing needs to anticipate. Since this memo is not about empirical validity, I won’t evaluate whether the paper answers them well. But strategically, the paper should explicitly foreground that its contribution is about **the absence of large reallocation on the extensive margin**. That is narrower than “no employment effect,” but still meaningful.

### Is the null itself interesting?

Yes, but only if framed correctly.

The null is interesting because the setting is a “most likely case” for seeing displacement of mobile low-wage labor. In ordinary settings, a null can feel uninformative. Here, a null can be informative because the policy is extreme and the adjustment margin is unusually salient.

The paper partially makes this case, but it needs to make it much more directly and repeatedly. Otherwise the result risks reading like a failed attempt to detect an effect in a noisy small-sample design.

Mostly **ambition**. The novelty is there in the setting. The problem is that the paper does not extract enough conceptual value from that setting.

**Reframe the paper around a broader question—whether labor standards impede labor-market integration in highly mobile border regions—and present Geneva as a hardest test of that proposition, rather than as another minimum wage employment design.**

If the author changes only one thing, it should be that.
