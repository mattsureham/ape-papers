## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper exploits the introduction of the world’s highest minimum wage (Geneva, 2020) to test the competitive model of labor markets in an extreme setting: a region where labor supply is arguably perfectly elastic due to EU free-movement agreements. Despite a massive wage floor (CHF 23.27) and a highly mobile workforce that could easily shift across the border, the author finds no detectable reduction in cross-border worker flows, challenging the standard prediction that high-bite wage floors necessarily lead to disemployment in open labor markets.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this pitch quite well. The first two paragraphs clearly state the setting (world’s highest minimum wage), the theoretical tension (highly elastic supply vs. competitive model predictions), and the empirical strategy. 

*The pitch the paper should have (minor refinement):*
"In 2020, Geneva implemented the world's highest minimum wage in a labor market where 25% of workers are daily cross-border commuters with near-zero switching costs. If the competitive model holds, this extreme wage floor should have triggered immediate, large-scale displacement of mobile workers toward neighboring regions. Instead, using administrative universe data and a triple-difference design, I find a precise null effect, suggesting that even in the most open labor markets, the 'disemployment' channel is surprisingly weak."

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**State the contribution:** The paper provides a high-stakes "stress test" of the minimum wage disemployment hypothesis by showing that even the world’s highest wage floor fails to reduce employment in a setting with nearly perfectly elastic labor supply.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It differentiates itself from the domestic US literature (Cengiz et al. 2019) by focusing on a "worst-case scenario" for the competitive model (international borders/mobile workers).
*   **Question vs. Literature:** It is framed as answering a question about the **world** (specifically, the integration of border labor markets). This is a strength.
*   **Clarity:** A smart economist would understand it as the "open economy" version of Card & Krueger.
*   **Making it bigger:** To be a "slam dunk" AER, the paper needs to move beyond the null on *counts*. The author admits they lack data on hours or skill-upgrading. Finding a way to proxy for **labor-labor substitution** (e.g., did firms hire more Swiss residents at the expense of French ones?) would make the contribution much more significant.

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

*   **Closest Neighbors:** Card & Krueger (1994), Beerli et al. (2021) on Swiss free movement, Manning (2003) on Monopsony, and Harasztosi & Lindner (2019) on "Who pays?".
*   **Positioning:** It currently "builds on" and "extends" the literature. It should more aggressively **attack** the notion that "minimum wages are especially dangerous for mobile/immigrant populations."
*   **Missing Conversations:** The paper should speak more to the **International Trade** literature (specifically the Heckscher-Ohlin/Factor Price Equalization logic), as border-crossing workers are a form of factor mobility that usually disciplines local policy.

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

*   **Setup:** Geneva depends on 90,000 French commuters who can quit and work elsewhere at no cost.
*   **Tension:** Geneva passes a record-breaking minimum wage. Economics 101 says these commuters should be the first to lose their jobs.
*   **Resolution:** Five years of data show they didn't. The "floor didn't bite" the workers; it might have bitten the firms' profits or changed intensive-margin hours instead.
*   **Implications:** High minimum wages are politically and economically viable even in hyper-integrated regional economies.

**Evaluation:** The narrative arc is strong and clean. It follows a "Surprising Fact" structure which is ideal for the AER.

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

*   **The Fact:** "Geneva set a $26 minimum wage and the 90,000 French people who commute there every day didn't lose their jobs."
*   **Reaction:** Lean in. It's a counter-intuitive finding for such a high wage.
*   **Follow-up:** "Did they cut hours?" "Did the price of a coffee in Geneva go to 15 francs?"
*   **Null Result Value:** This is a "Goldilocks" null. It is interesting because the treatment was so large (the highest in the world) that a null is actually a rejection of a very strong prior.

---

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

*   **Front-loading:** The paper is well front-loaded.
*   **Appendix:** The "Ticino replication" is great but could be moved to a dedicated section to give it more weight as an independent confirmation rather than just a "robustness check."
*   **Buried Treasure:** The discussion on "formalization of informal work" (Section 6) is a very compelling mechanism. It deserves more than a mention—if the author can find any data on "reported" vs "unreported" workers, that would be an AER-level breakthrough.

---

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The gap is **Mechanisms**. A null result on employment counts is a great "teaser," but the AER board usually wants to know *why* the competitive model failed. 
*   Is it because firms have massive monopsony power even over commuters? 
*   Is it because of price pass-through to Geneva's wealthy consumers? 
*   Is it because of labor-labor substitution?

**Single most impactful advice:** Find a way to measure **intensive margin (hours)** or **price pass-through** in Geneva's service sector. If you can show that prices rose but employment stayed flat, you have a complete story of who paid for the world's highest minimum wage.
