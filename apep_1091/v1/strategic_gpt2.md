## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper studies a striking historical episode: Japanese immigrant men in the United States could briefly reunify with wives through the “picture bride” system, while Chinese men remained largely shut out of family formation. The paper asks whether that family reunification shock improved immigrant men's economic outcomes, and argues that the gains showed up not in occupational upgrading but in land acquisition—especially where the law allowed property ownership.

A busy economist should care because the paper is really about a broader question: when does family-based immigration generate economic returns, through what channels, and what standard measures of immigrant mobility miss. That is potentially important well beyond this historical setting.

**Does the paper articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Not quite. The opening is vivid and historically rich, but the pitch arrives as “did picture brides affect Japanese men's outcomes?”—which sounds narrower and more antiquarian than the paper wants to be. The real pitch is about **family reunification as an economic institution**, **property rights as a complement**, and **measurement failure in occupational-status metrics**. Those ideas should appear immediately.

**The first two paragraphs should say something more like this:**

> Family reunification is one of the most important and controversial channels in immigration policy, yet economists still know surprisingly little about how access to a spouse changes immigrants’ economic trajectories. Does family formation raise earnings by stabilizing workers and promoting occupational mobility, or does it matter mainly by enabling household enterprise, asset accumulation, and settlement?
>
> This paper studies a rare historical policy shock that sharply changed immigrants’ ability to form families: the 1908–1920 “picture bride” system, which allowed Japanese men in the United States to bring wives from Japan, while similarly excluded Chinese men had no comparable channel. I show that this family-reunification shock substantially increased marriage and farm ownership among Japanese men, but not occupational income scores. The central lesson is that the economic return to family reunification ran through property and household production—and depended on legal access to ownership.

That is the story. Right now the paper opens as an interesting history paper with economic analysis. It needs to open as an economics paper using an extraordinary historical episode.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:**  
The paper argues that family reunification for immigrants generated economic gains primarily through household-based asset acquisition—especially land ownership—rather than occupational upgrading, and that these gains depended on property-rights institutions.

That is a plausible contribution. But it is not yet cleanly differentiated.

1. another historical immigration DiD,
2. another paper showing family structure matters,
3. another paper criticizing OCCSCORE in historical settings.

The author needs to say more sharply what is new relative to each:
- not just “first causal evidence on picture brides,” which is too topic-specific;
- not just “family matters,” which is already well understood;

The distinctive claim is the **triangular link**:

That is the novelty. The paper should build around that triangle.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a literature gap?
At its best, it is framed as a question about the world: **how family-based immigration affects economic mobility**. That is good. But the draft slips too often into “first paper to estimate X in this historical setting.” That is weaker and AER-unfriendly.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the intro?
At present, they might say: “It’s a historical DiD on picture brides showing no effect on occupational scores but some effect on farm ownership.” That is not enough. Several possibilities:

- **Make the outcome space more central and more coherent.** If property is the real channel, then farm ownership alone may feel thin. ## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Immigration policy often separates labor migration from family reunification. Economists usually think about immigrants’ economic assimilation through jobs, wages, and occupational status. In early-20th-century America, Japanese men briefly had access to family reunification through picture brides, while Chinese men largely did not.

### Tension
If family formation improves economic outcomes, through what channel should that appear? The standard expectation would be occupational upgrading. But in an economy where immigrant advancement may depend on household labor and farming, the relevant gains may instead show up in property acquisition. Further, those gains may depend on legal access to ownership.

### Resolution
The paper finds a sharp increase in spouse presence, no increase in occupational income scores, and an increase in farm ownership. It interprets this as evidence that family reunification paid off through land and household production, not through occupational ladder-climbing.

### Implications
The paper asks readers to revise two beliefs:
1. family reunification can have economically meaningful returns even if wages/occupations do not improve;
2. standard historical mobility measures may miss those returns when mobility occurs within occupations through ownership.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
**Serviceable, but not fully coherent.** The ingredients are there, but the paper still reads somewhat like:
- a cool historical episode,
- a DiD with a strong first stage,
- a null main outcome,
- then a more interesting secondary outcome,
- then a broad claim about measurement and institutions.

That is backwards. The paper should not read as “we expected occupational gains, found a null, then found land.” It should read as:

> Family reunification could create gains through household enterprise rather than labor-market advancement. This setting lets us test that. The occupational null is therefore not a disappointment; it is a clue. The economically relevant action is in ownership.

That would transform the null from a dead end into part of the story’s tension and resolution.

There is also a serious narrative issue around the heterogeneity section: the text says the property channel depends on legal rights, but the table/exposition as written is not fully aligned and is confusing. Even aside from identification, the narrative logic there is shaky. If property rights are central, that section has to be crystalline.
