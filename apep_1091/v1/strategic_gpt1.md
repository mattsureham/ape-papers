## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper studies a historically unusual immigration episode: Japanese “picture bride” migration temporarily allowed male immigrants in the U.S. to reunify with spouses, while Chinese men in similar labor markets remained largely barred from family formation. The central claim is that family reunification did not raise occupational standing, but instead increased access to farm ownership—and that this economic payoff depended on whether immigrants had legal rights to hold land.

Why should a busy economist care? Because the paper is trying to say something bigger than Japanese American history: family-based immigration may generate economic gains through household enterprise and asset ownership rather than through wages or occupational upgrading, and standard labor-market measures may miss that.

**Does the paper articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Not quite. The current introduction is competent, but it leads with the historical episode and then quickly slides into a somewhat diffuse set of claims: immigration economics, occupational mobility, land, property rights, and a critique of OCCSCORE. The paper has multiple possible stories and has not fully chosen one.

**What the first two paragraphs should say instead:**  
The paper should open with the world question, not the archive. Something like:

> Family reunification is one of the largest channels of immigration, but economists still know surprisingly little about how it changes immigrants’ economic trajectories. Does bringing a spouse improve labor-market outcomes directly, or does it matter mainly by enabling a different form of economic organization—household production, entrepreneurship, and asset accumulation?
>
> This paper studies that question using the early-twentieth-century Japanese “picture bride” system, which sharply expanded spouse access for Japanese men in the United States while comparable Chinese men remained excluded from family reunification. I show that the economic return to reunification did not appear in occupational income measures; instead, it appeared in land acquisition, especially where law allowed immigrants to hold property. The broader lesson is that family-based immigration can reshape economic mobility through assets and household enterprise, and conventional labor-market proxies can miss those gains.

That is the pitch this paper wants but does not yet fully deliver.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Before this paper, the broad view is that immigrant advancement is often measured through occupations and earnings, while family reunification is politically salient but economically understudied. In this historical setting, Japanese immigrants briefly had access to spouse migration, unlike Chinese immigrants.

### Tension
If family reunification matters economically, through what channel does it matter? Standard intuition says more stable households should improve labor-market outcomes. But in an agricultural immigrant economy, the relevant gains may come through family labor, household enterprise, and property acquisition rather than better occupations. And those gains may be blocked by discriminatory property law.

### Resolution
The paper finds a large increase in co-resident spouses, no effect on occupational income measures, and a positive effect on farm ownership, with the returns shaped by Alien Land Laws.

### Implications
Economists may be looking in the wrong place when they evaluate the returns to family-based immigration. Asset-based and household-enterprise channels matter, and institutional constraints determine whether family formation can be converted into economic advancement.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the ingredients, but not yet the discipline. Right now it reads somewhat like:
- interesting historical setup,
- first-stage result,
- null on OCCSCORE,
- positive farm ownership result,
- heterogeneity,
- critique of measurement.

That is more a collection of findings than a fully locked narrative.

### What story should it be telling?

The paper should tell a single story:

> Family reunification changed the economic technology available to immigrant households. For Japanese immigrants, a spouse did not translate into a better wage job; it translated into the ability to run a family farm and accumulate property. Whether that happened depended on the legal ability to hold land.

Everything else should be organized around that story. The OCCSCORE result is not the headline; it is the contrast that sharpens the headline.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?

I would say:

> “A major family-reunification shock for Japanese immigrants didn’t raise occupational status, but it did increase farm ownership substantially—and those gains disappeared where the law blocked land rights.”

That is a real hook.

### Would people lean in?

Some would. Economic historians, labor economists, and immigration economists would lean in. But the current presentation risks losing them because the lead fact is not yet distilled cleanly enough. “Picture brides” is memorable; “OCCSCORE null plus farm ownership increase” is interesting; “and property rights mediate the effect” is what upgrades it from curiosity to economics.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Probably:  
“Is this really about marriage, or about broader community formation and the transition from migrant labor to settled household production?”  

That is actually the right question, and the paper should embrace it. The design limitations are already acknowledged. Strategically, the paper should not oversell precision; it should instead sell the importance of the economic pattern it uncovers.

### Are the findings modest or null?

The occupational result is null; the farm ownership result is not. The paper does a decent job arguing the null is informative because OCCSCORE misses within-occupation gains. That is smart. But if the paper leads too heavily with the null, it will sound like a failed attempt to find wage effects. The positive asset result must come first in the framing.

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

Without rewriting the paper, several structural changes would improve readability and strategic force.

### 1. Front-load the main result sooner
The introduction currently takes a bit too long to reveal the paper’s real point. By paragraph two, the reader should already know:
- family reunification shock,
- no occupational effect,
- yes property/land effect,
- dependence on legal rights.

### 2. Reduce methodological throat-clearing in the introduction
The paragraph beginning “To be clear about the estimand...” comes too early and is too defensive for an introduction. It belongs later, or in a shorter form. Editors and readers need the big idea first, caveats second.

### 3. Reorder the contributions paragraph
The strongest contribution is not “first causal evidence on picture brides.” That should be third, not first. Lead with the general economics contribution:
- family reunification works through assets/household production,
- property rights condition the return,
- standard occupational measures can miss this.

### 4. Tighten historical background
The history is useful but somewhat long relative to the size of the paper’s conceptual contribution. Keep what establishes the institutional shocks and comparison group, but trim narrative detail.

### 5. Elevate the property-rights interaction
The heterogeneity table is strategically important and currently underexploited. If this is the institutional punchline, it should appear earlier in the results narrative and be framed as a core test, not a side exercise.

### 6. Be careful with the discussion of measurement
The critique of OCCSCORE is potentially important, but right now it risks feeling tacked on. It should be presented as a direct implication of the main finding, not as a separate mini-paper.

### 7. Conclusion should do more than summarize
The current conclusion is punchy, but it mostly restates findings. It should end with the larger implication:
- economists may underestimate returns to family-based immigration when mobility occurs through assets and household enterprise;
- institutions determine whether those gains are realized.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

At present, the gap is mostly **framing and ambition**, with some **scope** concerns.

### Is it a framing problem?
Yes, substantially. The paper has a potentially strong idea but is still dressed as a historical episode paper. AER wants the episode to illuminate a broader economic mechanism.

### Is it a scope problem?
Also yes. The story rests heavily on one positive outcome—farm ownership. That may be enough for a good field journal if the design is persuasive, but for AER the mechanism needs to feel richer and the payoff broader. Even without changing identification, the paper needs a more expansive conceptual interpretation of what farm ownership represents: household production, proprietorship, asset accumulation, settlement, intergenerational foothold.

### Is it a novelty problem?
Somewhat. The general insight that family structure matters for economic behavior is not new. Nor is the idea that discriminatory institutions mediate minority advancement. What is novel is the specific combination:
- family reunification shock,
- immigrant economic mobility,
- asset rather than occupational channel,
- property-rights mediation.
The paper needs to state that combination more sharply.

### Is it an ambition problem?
Yes. The paper is intellectually more interesting than it presents itself as. It currently sounds careful and competent, but a bit safe. It needs to make a stronger claim about what economists have been missing.

### Single most impactful advice
**Reframe the paper around one big claim: family reunification changes immigrants’ economic trajectories by enabling asset-based household enterprise, and whether that return materializes depends on property rights.**  

That framing would turn the picture bride episode from a historical curiosity into a test case for a broader economic mechanism.
