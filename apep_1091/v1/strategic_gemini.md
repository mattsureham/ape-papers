## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
The paper examines the economic impact of a unique "natural experiment" in family reunification: the 1908–1920 "picture bride" loophole that allowed Japanese immigrant men to marry and bring wives to the U.S. while Chinese men remained legally barred from doing so. Using this cross-race variation, the author finds that while family formation did not raise occupational income (wages), it significantly increased farm ownership—but only where legal property rights (Alien Land Laws) did not block the path.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this pitch very clearly in the first two paragraphs. It successfully identifies the policy shock, the control group (Chinese immigrants), and the primary tension (wages vs. property). It does not need a rewrite of the pitch; it already speaks the language of the AER.

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
The contribution is showing that the economic returns to family reunification are realized through household-based capital accumulation (property) rather than individual labor market upgrading (wages), and that these returns are contingent on the legal institutional environment.

- **Differentiation:** It differentiates itself from Abramitzky et al. (2012, 2014) by challenging the "occupational upgrading" metric as the sole measure of assimilation.
- **World vs. Literature:** It is framed as a question about the world—specifically, how families build wealth in hostile environments.
- **Novelty:** A smart economist would say: "It's a paper about how marriage is an economic firm that enables land acquisition for immigrants, provided they have property rights."
- **Bigger Contribution:** To make it even bigger, the paper could more explicitly link to the **"economics of the household as a firm."** Is the farm ownership result driven by pooled labor or pooled savings? Opening that black box would move it from "historical episode" to "general theory of household formation."

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
The paper sits at the intersection of Economic History (Assimilation), Labor Economics (Marriage Premiums), and Law & Economics (Property Rights).

- **Neighbors:** Abramitzky, Boustan, & Eriksson (2014) on assimilation; Arai & Kawaguchi (2020) on Alien Land Laws; Ward (2020) on measurement in history.
- **Strategy:** It builds on Abramitzky et al. but acts as a "constructive critic" regarding their use of OCCSCORE. It synthesizes the immigration literature with the legal history of racial exclusion.
- **Missing Conversations:** The paper could engage more with the **Development Economics** literature on "The Family as a Credit/Labor Constraint." The picture bride is essentially a solution to a labor/capital constraint for Japanese farmers.

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC
- **Setup:** A "bachelor society" of Japanese immigrants in the early 1900s.
- **Tension:** A sudden policy opening allows families to form, but they face a wall of racial discrimination and land laws. 
- **Resolution:** Marriage doesn't get you a better-paying job in the city; it gets you a farm in the country.
- **Implications:** Standard measures of "success" (wages) miss the real story of immigrant wealth building (property), and immigration policy cannot be viewed in isolation from property law.

**Evaluation:** The narrative arc is exceptionally strong. It reads like a cohesive story where the null result (on wages) is actually a vital part of the discovery.

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
- **Lead Fact:** "When Japanese immigrants were finally allowed to bring wives, they didn't get better jobs—they just bought the farm." 
- **Response:** People would lean in. The "marriage premium" is a classic topic, but finding that it operates entirely through a "property rights channel" is a fresh twist.
- **Follow-up:** "If they couldn't own land in California, what did the wives do? Did they just move to states where they could?" (This leads to the heterogeneity result, which is the strongest part of the paper).

---

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
- **OCCSCORE critique:** The section explaining why OCCSCORE fails for farmers (page 3 and 10) is excellent. This should be highlighted even more—it’s a methodological contribution that will interest all historical labor economists.
- **Front-loading:** The paper is well front-loaded.
- **Appendix:** The "Standardized Effect Sizes" (Table 6) are useful but could stay in the appendix to keep the main narrative focused on the raw, intuitive numbers (40% increase in farm ownership).

---

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The gap is **Mechanisms.** Currently, we see the "Before" (bachelors) and "After" (farm owners). To be a slam-dahk AER, we need to know *why* the wife led to the farm. 
1. **Labor Channel:** Did the wife provide the necessary labor to make a small farm viable? 
2. **Savings Channel:** Did the household save more? 
3. **Legal Circumvention:** Did they use the wife (or later, American-born children) to hold title?

**Single Biggest Piece of Advice:** Use the census data to look at household composition and "unpaid family labor" indicators to prove that the "Premium" was specifically a **labor-pooling effect** that made proprietorship possible.
