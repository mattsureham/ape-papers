**Summary.**  
This paper estimates how the 2023 Supreme Court ban on race-conscious admissions (SFFA v. Harvard) shifted institutional racial enrollment shares using a panel of 1,835 IPEDS institutions (2017–2024). A continuous difference‑in‑differences design interacts pre‑SFFA admission selectivity with a post‑2024 indicator to show modest URM share declines but large increases in Asian shares (and concomitant declines in White shares) at more selective schools. Placebo tests for prior affirmative‑action ban states and HBCUs support the interpretation that the effects operate where the Court’s decision introduced a novel constraint.

**Essential Points.**  
1. **Parallel–trends/identification clarity.** The identification hinges on the assumption that, absent SFFA, the interaction between selectivity and time would not affect racial shares. The manuscript notes significant pre‑trends at the four‑year horizon but does not show event‑study estimates nor formally test for differential trends across intensity levels. Given the single policy date and only one post year, demonstrating pre‑trends (or lack thereof) is critical. Please present an event study (or at least the lead coefficients) with confidence intervals and discuss whether the apparent t–4 significance undermines identification. If pre-trends remain, consider controlling flexibly for intensity‑specific trends (e.g., interact intensity with linear time or use synthetic control-type matching) or rerun the main specification on a restricted window where pre-trends are demonstrably flat.

2. **Treatment intensity interpretation and omitted confounders.** The key “treatment” is 1 minus the pre‑SFFA admit rate. But selectivity correlates with many institutional characteristics (endowment shocks, enrollment demand swings, pandemic recovery speed) that could independently drive race composition, especially in a single post year. The paper should more fully justify why the admit rate proxies only for race-conscious admissions and not for other concurrent shocks. Adding controls interacted with post (e.g., institutional size changes, test-optional adoption, state demographic shifts), showing that results are robust to alternative intensity measures (e.g., indicator for admit<25% or using pre‑SFFA share of applicants by SAT score), and/or demonstrating that intensity has no predictive power for pre‑SFFA trends in other margins would bolster credibility.

3. **Outcome definition and economic significance.** The use of 12‑month total undergraduate enrollment mixes cohorts and may dilute the immediate impact on entering classes. Given the policy directly affects admissions decisions, the paper should clarify how much of the observed shift reflects changes in freshmen versus upperclassmen, and whether the substantial Asian gain persists when focusing on first‑time freshmen (if available) or by adjusting for cohort aging. Additionally, translating the reported percentage point changes into absolute headcounts for selective institutions—perhaps weighted by enrollment—would help readers assess the policy’s magnitude beyond percentage shares.

**Suggestions.**  
- **Depict the event study.** A plot of year leads/lags interacted with intensity (with 2018–2023 shown) would allow readers to assess pre–trend dynamics visually and provide context for the t–4 significance. If power is limited for some leads, aggregate adjacent years (e.g., 2017–2018) or report the full vector in an online appendix.

- **Reconcile sample scope with the manifest.** Explain why the analysis is limited to 1,835 institutions and whether the excluded schools differ systematically (for instance, community colleges without admit data). Discuss whether differential post‑policy dynamics in the excluded sample could bias the key comparisons—particularly since the manifest emphasized the universe of 6,000+ institutions.

- **Address compositional implications.** The manuscript highlights Asian gains as the “dominant reallocation,” but it would be useful to show how these share changes translate into absolute numbers (e.g., by applying the estimated share changes to selective institutions’ enrollment totals) and to assess whether the shifts persist after adjusting for the baseline share of Asians and Whites. A table showing predicted post‑SFFA shares (or counts) for a prototypical selective school would concretize the economic story.

- **Explore alternative intensity proxies or instrumentalization.** Admission rates may be endogenous to unobserved institutional trends. Consider constructing an instrument for intensity using pre‑existing measures of race-conscious admissions (e.g., historical use of affirmative action disclosed in university statements or lawsuits) or combining admit rate with independent rankings of selectivity. Alternatively, perform sensitivity analyses where the treatment is discretized (e.g., high‑intensity quartile vs. others) to show that the results are not driven by the tails of the admit rate distribution.

- **Cascade analysis enhancement.** The tiered regressions are informative but could be sharpened by modeling the “reallocation” explicitly: for example, regress URM shares in each tier on both the post indicator and an aggregate intensity variable, or estimate a system of equations where top tier URM declines are allowed to influence subsequent tiers. This would better quantify whether the positive coefficient at the selective tier offsets losses at the highest tier.

- **Discuss robustness to alternative outcome definitions.** Since 12-month counts include retention and transfer effects, the observed compositional shifts may partly reflect differential persistence or graduation rather than admissions. Include robustness checks using fall headcount (if available) or control for changes in retention (e.g., by including year fixed effects interacted with selectivity to capture broader trends in persistence). At minimum, acknowledge this limitation and argue why admissions dynamics likely dominate the observed shift.

- **Clarify clustering and inference.** State‑level clustering assumes 50 clusters, but the primary variation arises from cross‑institution differences in intensity. Consider reporting wild-cluster bootstrap p‑values or using higher-level clusters (e.g., Census regions) as a robustness check, especially since the main coefficient is near conventional significance thresholds.

- **Expand the discussion of mechanisms.** The paper posits race-neutral alternatives and cascade effects as explanations for the small URM decline, but these remain speculative. Where possible, incorporate direct evidence—for example, mention whether selective institutions adopted test-optional policies or outreach initiatives in 2023–2024 (drawing on press releases or Common App data) and, if feasible, control for these in the regression. This would strengthen the causal story about how institutions mitigated the ban’s impact.

- **Address external validity for future cohorts.** The limitation of one post‑treatment year is important; a brief simulation or scenario discussion about how subsequent cycles might unfold (e.g., whether the Asian gain is likely to persist or is a short-term response) would help readers understand the temporal scope of the findings. Outline plans for updating the analysis as more data become available.

Overall, the paper tackles a timely and important question with a novel dataset and plausible design. Addressing the issues above—especially reinforcing identification and clarifying interpretation—will substantially increase confidence in the core findings.
