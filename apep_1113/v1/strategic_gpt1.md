## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a timely, high-salience question: after *SFFA v. Harvard* ended race-conscious admissions, how did the racial composition of college enrollment actually change across U.S. higher education? Its central claim is that the biggest immediate compositional shift was not the widely anticipated drop in Black and Hispanic enrollment, but a reallocation from White to Asian students at more selective institutions.

A busy economist should care because this is one of the first broad-based attempts to say what the post-*SFFA* world actually looks like, using national institutional data rather than a handful of elite campuses. If true, the paper challenges the dominant public and academic narrative about who affirmative action was really affecting at the margin.

### Does the paper articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Mostly yes, and better than many papers. The opening is punchy, and “The predictions were wrong—or rather, they were looking at the wrong margin” is exactly the right instinct. The problem is that the introduction oversells the sharpness of the revisionist claim before the reader has confidence in what is being measured. It also introduces the provocative “admissions illusion” framing too early and too confidently, before establishing the distinction between campus-level flow outcomes and aggregate stock enrollment.

The first two paragraphs should be a bit more disciplined and world-question-first. Right now the paper sounds like it wants to win a media argument. AER papers usually need to sound like they are changing how economists think about an economically important mechanism.

### The pitch the paper should have

> The end of affirmative action was expected to sharply reduce Black and Hispanic representation at selective colleges. This paper asks a broader question: when race-conscious admissions ends, which margins of enrollment adjustment matter most across the higher-education system?
>
> Using national IPEDS data on institutions spanning the selectivity distribution, I show that the first post-*SFFA* enrollment changes are not dominated by large declines in underrepresented minority enrollment. Instead, the largest immediate compositional shift at more selective institutions is an increase in Asian enrollment offset by a decline in White enrollment. The paper’s contribution is to show that removing race-conscious admissions changed who enrolls, but along a different margin than the policy debate emphasized.

That is the paper’s strongest version: less slogan, more question, more economically legible.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper’s contribution is to show, using national institution-level enrollment data, that the first broad post-*SFFA* enrollment adjustment at selective colleges appears to have occurred more along the Asian–White margin than along the URM–non-URM margin.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3-4 papers?

Partially, but not yet sharply enough.

The paper distinguishes itself on **data scope**—IPEDS universe-level institutional coverage rather than single-campus or applicant-level proprietary data. That is real. But “I use broader data” is not, by itself, a top-journal contribution. The author needs to be much clearer that the broader data allow a different substantive question: **system-wide incidence and margin of adjustment**, not just what happened at Harvard, MIT, or the Common App pool.

Right now the introduction says “first universe-level analysis” and mentions a continuous-treatment design, cascade analysis, and placebo tests. That is too design-centric. The differentiator should be:

- other papers study **admissions or enrollment at elite institutions / selected applicant pools**;
- this paper studies **how the national higher-education system adjusted across the selectivity distribution**;
- and therefore can speak to **incidence, substitution, and redistribution across groups and tiers**, not just whether one elite campus became less diverse.

That is a better differentiator.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or filling a gap in a LITERATURE?

It is mixed, and it should be more decisively about the world.

Its strongest world question is: **When affirmative action ends, who actually loses seats, who gains seats, and where in the system does adjustment happen?** That is strong. But the paper keeps slipping back into literature-gap language (“first universe-level analysis,” “continuous treatment intensity design”). Those are support points, not the reason to publish in AER.

### Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what’s new?

At present, maybe—but the explanation would likely be: “It’s a national DiD on the *SFFA* ruling using IPEDS, and it says the biggest shift was Asian up, White down, not a huge URM collapse.”

That is not bad. But it still risks sounding like “another policy-evaluation paper with a provocative headline.” The paper needs to sharpen the conceptual novelty so the colleague instead says:

> “It changes the incidence story. We thought the first-order margin was URM versus everyone else; this paper says the main immediate adjustment was between Asian and White students at selective institutions, and that matters for how we think about the equilibrium effects of affirmative action.”

That is more memorable and more AER-like.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Several possibilities:

1. **Move from stock enrollment to entering-cohort outcomes.**  
   The current outcome is total undergraduate enrollment share. For this particular question, freshman composition is the natural object. The paper itself admits the stock measure mechanically dilutes treatment effects. If the author could bring in freshman-level outcomes, the paper becomes much more consequential.

2. **Show system-wide reallocation explicitly.**  
   The paper hints at “cascade effects” but does not own that as a central result. If it could document where displaced students go across selectivity tiers, that would make the contribution much bigger: from “composition changed” to “the system re-sorted students in this way.”

3. **Speak more directly to incidence.**  
   The paper should frame itself as identifying the incidence of race-conscious admissions across groups, not as correcting a journalistic misconception. “Who bears the incidence of admissions preferences?” is a larger and more durable question.

4. **Connect enrollment changes to institutional behavior.**  
   If the paper could more directly show that institutions used race-neutral substitutes and that those substitutes muted URM declines, the story becomes richer and more structural in interpretation.

If the author can only enlarge one dimension, it should be the first: **freshman outcomes or a clearer flow-based system-reallocation story**.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

### Closest neighbors

Based on the introduction, the closest neighbors are likely:

- **Bleemer (2024)** on post-*SFFA* effects, likely using selective-campus or system evidence
- **Arcidiacono et al. (2024)** on admissions/enrollment responses, probably from elite or applicant-level data
- **Dynarski et al. (2024)** on Common App or early post-ban evidence
- **Bleemer (2022)** on California Proposition 209 / affirmative action bans
- **Hinrichs (2012)** on affirmative action bans and college enrollment
- Possibly **Backes (2012)** on state affirmative action bans and redistribution effects

There is also a broader literature the paper should engage more deliberately:

- affirmative action bans and mismatch/reallocation/system effects;
- economics of discrimination and constrained optimization in admissions;
- college admissions and matching/sorting;
- perhaps even political economy / law-and-econ on judicial shocks affecting institutional behavior.

### How should the paper position itself relative to those neighbors?

**Build on and reframe**, not attack.

This is not a “previous papers got it wrong” situation. The better position is:

- campus-level papers are valuable for measuring direct effects at elite institutions;
- applicant-level papers are valuable for understanding admissions processes and applicant behavior;
- this paper complements them by asking a different question: **what happened at the national system level, and which compositional margins moved most?**

That is much stronger than trying to imply that prior papers missed the truth.

### Is the paper currently positioned too narrowly or too broadly?

Oddly, both.

- It is **too narrow** in the sense that it spends a lot of time on a very specific public controversy—whether commentators overstated URM declines.
- It is **too broad** in the sense that it gestures toward “the broader economics of discrimination in competitive markets” in a way that feels generic and unearned.

The sweet spot is narrower and deeper:  
**This is a paper on the incidence and general-equilibrium adjustment of affirmative action in higher education.**

That is a real economics conversation.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of? What fields should it be speaking to?

The paper underplays at least three conversations:

1. **Matching and sorting in higher education.**  
   If the paper wants to talk about cascade effects across tiers, it should engage the literature on student-college matching and stratification.

2. **Incidence of affirmative action / who bears the burden.**  
   The paper should frame the Asian–White result as evidence on incidence, not just as a compositional curiosity.

3. **Organizational adaptation to legal shocks.**  
   The paper repeatedly suggests universities rapidly substituted toward race-neutral tools. That connects to institutional adaptation and policy substitution, which could broaden the audience.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?

Not quite. It is currently having a conversation with the media narrative plus a narrow set of post-*SFFA* papers. The more impactful conversation is:

> What is the incidence of race-conscious admissions, and how does a legal ban reallocate students across groups and tiers in the college market?

That framing gives the paper more staying power and makes it less vulnerable to seeming like a one-news-cycle paper.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Before this paper, the dominant expectation was that ending affirmative action would sharply reduce Black and Hispanic representation at selective colleges. Existing evidence came from prior state bans and from a few highly visible elite campuses.

### Tension

But there are two uncertainties: first, *SFFA* was a national shock hitting a heterogeneous system, not a single state or single campus; second, when a constraint is removed, the main adjustment may occur on margins different from those emphasized in the public debate.

### Resolution

Using national institution-level data, the paper argues that the first post-*SFFA* compositional changes at more selective institutions were modest for URM shares overall and larger on the Asian–White margin.

### Implications

The implications are that economists should rethink the incidence of affirmative action, and policymakers should not evaluate the ban solely through headline changes in Black and Hispanic enrollment at a few elite campuses.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the bones of one, yes. But it is still somewhat a collection of results looking for the most headline-friendly story.

The current story is: “Everyone thought X; actually Y.” That gets attention, but it is a bit thin for AER. The better story is:

- affirmative action is a multi-margin allocation mechanism;
- eliminating it changes the incidence of admissions competition in ways that are not obvious from the public debate;
- national data reveal not just direct effects on intended beneficiaries but substitution across non-target groups and across institutional tiers.

That story would integrate the Asian result, the prior-ban placebo, and the cascade discussion into one coherent economics narrative.

Right now the “cascade” result feels bolted on. The paper needs to decide whether it is centrally about:
1. the wrong group margin, or
2. the system-wide re-sorting across groups and tiers.

The latter is more ambitious and more AER-worthy. The former is more op-ed-like.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?

I would say:

> “In the first national enrollment data after *SFFA*, the biggest composition change at selective colleges wasn’t a large drop in Black and Hispanic enrollment—it was an increase in Asian enrollment and a decline in White enrollment.”

That is the paper’s hook.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

They would lean in—initially. The topic is hot, and the claim cuts against a widely shared prior. But the very next thing they would want to know is whether this is a measurement artifact of looking at total enrollment rather than freshman classes, and whether the result is really about elite schools or about the whole selectivity distribution.

That means the paper has a strong hook but not yet a fully satisfying “second sentence.”

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Almost certainly:

- “Is this true for entering freshmen, or only diluted stock enrollment?”
- “Is the White-to-Asian shift happening at the very top, or in a broader set of selective institutions?”
- “Are URM students displaced downward in the quality distribution, even if aggregate shares don’t move much?”

These are exactly the questions the introduction should anticipate and organize around.

### If findings are modest, is the modesty itself interesting?

Yes—conditionally.

A finding that the end of affirmative action did **not** produce a dramatic immediate collapse in URM enrollment is interesting if the paper frames it as evidence of institutional adaptation, substitution, and redistribution rather than as “nothing happened.” The paper partly does this, but it still risks reading like a failed version of the expected story plus a more provocative side result.

To avoid that, the author should say clearly:

> The important fact is not that the average URM effect is modest; it is that the incidence and locus of adjustment differed from what both proponents and opponents of affirmative action implicitly assumed.

That makes the modesty informative rather than disappointing.

   - draw out what this means for how economists think about incidence and substitution in affirmative action; or

Reasonably, yes. The hook is early. But too much of the really interesting interpretation is still dispersed. The reader gets the headline quickly, but not the framework that makes the headline feel important.

### Are there results buried that should be in the main results?

The **prior-ban-state placebo** is conceptually central and belongs very near the main result, not as a later robustness/check-the-box exercise. It is part of the story: the effect appears where the policy shock plausibly bites.

### Is the conclusion adding value?

Not enough. It is mostly a rhetorical summary. It should become a short substantive takeaway section about incidence, substitution, and system-level reallocation.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

At the moment, the main gap is **not primarily technical**. It is a combination of **framing** and **scope/ambition**.

### What is the gap?

#### 1. Framing problem
Yes. The science may be serviceable, but the current title and rhetoric (“admissions illusion”) push the paper toward contrarian think-piece territory. AER will want a paper that changes how economists think, not one that sounds like it is dunking on the media.

#### 2. Scope problem
Also yes. For a question this important, total undergraduate stock composition is not quite the ideal outcome. The paper itself knows that. To be an AER paper, it likely needs either:
- fresher/more targeted outcome data on entering cohorts, or
- a much more developed system-reallocation analysis that turns the current limitation into a substantive advantage.

#### 3. Novelty problem
Somewhat. The question is obviously important and newly relevant, so there is topical novelty. But the paper must show that it is doing more than “another reduced-form estimate of a major court decision.” The novelty has to be the **incidence story** and the **system-level perspective**.

#### 4. Ambition problem
Yes. The current paper is competent but still a bit safe in its intellectual ambition. It shows effects on shares by race and gestures at mechanisms. The AER version would more fully answer:
- who gains and loses;
- where students re-sort;
- what this implies about how affirmative action operates in equilibrium.

### Single most impactful piece of advice

**Reframe the paper around the incidence and system-wide reallocation effects of ending affirmative action—and, if at all possible, replace or supplement total enrollment shares with entering-cohort evidence so the core claim is about the margin where admissions actually operates.**

That is the one change that would most increase its AER plausibility.
