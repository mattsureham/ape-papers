## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks: after the Supreme Court banned race-conscious admissions in **SFFA v. Harvard**, how did the racial composition of college enrollment change across U.S. institutions? Using national IPEDS data and exploiting the fact that the ruling mattered much more for selective colleges than for open-access ones, the paper argues that the first-order compositional shift was not a dramatic collapse in Black/Hispanic enrollment, but a reallocation from White to Asian students at selective schools.

A busy economist should care because this is one of the first attempts to measure the national incidence of the affirmative-action ban rather than narrate a few elite campuses. The interesting claim is not just “affirmative action mattered,” but **which margins adjusted when it disappeared**.

### Does the paper articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Mostly yes, but in a way that is too clever and slightly too polemical. “The predictions were wrong—or rather, they were looking at the wrong margin” is punchy, but the paper is trying to do two jobs at once: intervene in a political/media narrative and make an economics contribution. For AER, the introduction should lead with the economics question, not the op-ed line.

It also overstates the outcome. The paper studies **institution-level enrollment composition in the first post-SFFA cycle**, not “who goes to college” in the broad welfare or access sense. That distinction matters for positioning.

### The pitch the paper should have

> The end of race-conscious admissions was widely expected to sharply reduce underrepresented minority representation at selective colleges. This paper provides the first national evidence on how enrollment composition changed across the U.S. higher-education system after **SFFA v. Harvard**, using administrative data on nearly all degree-granting institutions and the fact that the ruling bound much more tightly for selective institutions than for open-access colleges.  
>   
> I find that the immediate national effect is not a large collapse in Black and Hispanic representation in overall undergraduate enrollment, but a compositional reallocation at selective institutions that is largest on the Asian-White margin. The paper’s contribution is to show that the incidence of the affirmative-action ban differs from the way the policy debate was framed: the biggest short-run adjustment occurred not only through losses for underrepresented minorities, but through substitution across non-URM groups and across the selectivity hierarchy.

That is the story. Less slogan, more economics.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### Contribution in one sentence

This paper provides early national evidence that the first post-**SFFA** enrollment response at selective U.S. colleges was a modest decline in URM shares but a larger reallocation from White to Asian students.

### Is it clearly differentiated from the closest papers?

Not yet clearly enough. The draft says it is the “first universe-level analysis” using IPEDS, which is a valid data contribution, but data coverage alone is not an AER contribution. Relative to the closest papers, the paper needs to be much sharper about **what only a national panel can reveal**:

1. **Incidence across the whole institutional hierarchy**, not a handful of elites.
2. **Cross-group substitution patterns**, especially Asian vs. White.
3. **Potential cascade/re-sorting across tiers**, not just effects at one campus.
4. **External validity** beyond selected institutions or application platforms.

Right now, a smart reader may still summarize it as “another DiD paper on affirmative action with a nationwide dataset.” The paper needs to make it impossible to describe it that way.

### Is the contribution framed as a question about the world or a gap in the literature?

It starts with a world question, which is good: what actually changed after SFFA? But it then slips into a literature-gap framing: “this paper complements contemporaneous NBER papers by using the full universe.” That is secondary. The stronger framing is:

- **World question:** When a salient group-targeted admissions policy is removed, where in the system do the seats actually reallocate?
- **Economic question:** What margins absorb the shock in a stratified market with constrained admissions and endogenous institutional adjustment?

That is much stronger than “we use bigger data.”

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new?

At present: only somewhat. They would say, “It’s a national panel paper on SFFA showing smaller-than-expected URM declines in stock enrollment and bigger Asian gains.” That is better than “another DiD paper,” but still not yet memorable enough.

The memorable version is: **the ban’s short-run incidence appears to fall more on the Asian-White margin than on the URM-non-URM margin, at least in national enrollment stocks.** That is a conversation-starting claim.

### What would make the contribution bigger?

Most importantly: **make the paper about incidence and system-wide reallocation, not just about whether URM enrollment fell.**

Concretely, the contribution would become much bigger if the paper did one of these better:

- **Different outcome framing:** Emphasize institutional composition and seat reallocation rather than “who goes to college.” As written, the title and conclusion oversell what the data can speak to.
- **Different mechanism/comparison:** Lean hard into **cross-group substitution** and **tier-to-tier cascade**. That is the genuinely interesting part.
- **Different framing:** Position the paper as evidence on how removing a constrained preference changes allocation in a vertically differentiated market.
- **Different scope:** Show, if possible, where displaced seats go in the hierarchy—not just “Asian up, White down,” but “at which tiers and sectors.”

The paper gets bigger if it answers: **What equilibrium pattern did SFFA generate nationally?** Not just: **Did selective schools become less diverse?**

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Before this paper, the common expectation was that banning affirmative action would sharply reduce URM representation at selective colleges. Existing evidence came from state-level bans, individual institutions, and highly publicized freshman-class snapshots.

### Tension

The national policy shock was large and salient, but it was not obvious **where** the resulting reallocation would appear in the broader higher-education system. The public debate focused on URM losses at elite schools; the system-wide incidence could have been very different.

### Resolution

The paper finds that in the first post-SFFA enrollment cycle, selective institutions saw only a modest decline in URM shares in total undergraduate enrollment, while the larger observed compositional shift was increased Asian representation and decreased White representation.

### Implications

The first-order short-run consequence of eliminating race-conscious admissions may not be well captured by the standard “URM versus everyone else” frame. Economists should think about affirmative-action bans as shocks that generate **multi-margin reallocation** in a hierarchical market, not just one-for-one losses for intended beneficiaries.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has one, but it is not fully disciplined. The paper oscillates among three stories:

1. **The media got the story wrong.**
2. **National evidence differs from campus anecdotes.**
3. **Removing a binding constraint shifts allocation on unexpected margins.**

The third is the most AER-ish; the second is useful; the first is the least valuable. Right now the paper leans too much on (1).

It is not just a random collection of results, but the story is still under-edited. The paper should tell this story:

> SFFA was understood as a policy about URM access at elite schools. But in a national, stratified market, the immediate incidence of removing race-conscious admissions shows up through broader compositional reallocation, especially on the Asian-White margin and potentially across tiers. The point is not that URM effects are zero; it is that the equilibrium response is more complex than the public debate assumed.

That is the coherent narrative.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?

“I expected the first national evidence on SFFA to show the biggest short-run effect on Black and Hispanic representation. Instead, in national enrollment data, the biggest compositional shift at selective schools is an increase in Asian share and a decline in White share.”

That is the fact.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

They would lean in. It is surprising, policy-relevant, and cuts across prior assumptions. But they will immediately ask whether the paper is measuring the right margin.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Almost certainly: **“Is this because you’re looking at total undergraduate enrollment rather than entering freshmen?”**

And second: **“Does this reflect re-sorting across tiers rather than true resilience in URM access?”**

Those are not referee-style identification questions; they are exactly the strategic questions the paper must anticipate in its framing. The current title and rhetoric invite overinterpretation. The paper needs to preempt the obvious objection by being upfront that this is an early, stock-based, system-wide read on incidence.

### If the findings are modest: is the modesty itself interesting?

Yes, but only if framed correctly. A modest URM effect is interesting **if the paper makes clear that what looked like a massive policy shock had surprisingly muted short-run effects in aggregate enrollment stocks because institutions adjusted and because the system re-sorted.** That is informative.

If instead it is framed as “the predictions were wrong,” it will sound glib and vulnerable. The contribution is not that nothing happened; it is that **what happened is not where most observers were looking.**
