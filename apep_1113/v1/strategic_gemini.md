### 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper evaluates the first year of the national ban on race-conscious admissions (*SFFA v. Harvard*) using the universe of US colleges. It finds that while the policy debate focused on a potential collapse in Black and Hispanic enrollment, the actual dominant effect was a reallocation of seats from White students to Asian students at selective institutions, with underrepresented minority (URM) enrollment proving surprisingly resilient.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this pitch very well. The "Admissions Illusion" branding is catchy and effective. The second paragraph clearly defines the empirical strategy (IPEDS universe + continuous selectivity intensity). The first two paragraphs effectively set up the "conventional wisdom" vs. "surprising reality" tension that the AER prizes.

### 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:** The paper provides the first comprehensive, national evidence that the end of affirmative action primarily reshuffled the "non-minority" pool—shifting enrollment from White to Asian students—rather than causing the predicted systemic decline in Black and Hispanic enrollment.

*   **Differentiation:** It differentiates itself from the "Big 3" contemporaneous NBER papers (Bleemer; Arcidiacono; Dynarski) by moving from proprietary/subset data to the full IPEDS universe. This allows for a "cascade" analysis and a stronger national claim.
*   **Framing:** It is framed as answering a question about the **WORLD** (the actual impact of a landmark SCOTUS ruling), though it uses the "filling a gap" (universe-level data) to justify its existence alongside the NBER papers.
*   **Clarity:** A smart economist would immediately understand the "three-player game" versus "two-player game" distinction.
*   **Expansion:** To make the contribution "bigger," the paper needs to more deeply explore the *why* of the White-to-Asian shift. Is it a pure "meritocratic" correction, or are legacy/recruited athlete preferences (which lean White) the next binding constraint being challenged?

### 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

*   **Neighbors:** Bleemer (2022/2024) on California/Prop 209; Arcidiacono et al. on Harvard/UNC; Fryer & Loury (2005) on the mythology of affirmative action.
*   **Strategy:** The paper currently builds on these but should more aggressively "synthesize" them to explain why 2024 looks different from 1996 (California). The key is the "race-neutral alternatives" (socioeconomic preferences) that didn't exist in the 90s.
*   **Missing Conversations:** The paper is light on the **Political Economy** of university behavior. Universities are not passive actors; they are strategic maximizers of prestige and diversity. Connecting this to the literature on "Prestige Maximization" would elevate the narrative.

### 4. NARRATIVE ARC

*   **Setup:** A world expecting a "diversity cliff" following the SCOTUS decision.
*   **Tension:** If universities value diversity, will they find workarounds? And who actually "wins" when the explicit race constraint is removed?
*   **Resolution:** URM numbers held steady-ish (the "illusion"), while the real zero-sum conflict was between Asian and White applicants.
*   **Implications:** Affirmative action acted as a "ceiling" for Asian applicants more than a "floor" for URM applicants.

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong. It moves from a high-stakes legal shock to a counter-intuitive empirical finding.

### 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

**The Dinner Party Fact:** "After the Supreme Court banned affirmative action, the biggest losers weren't Black students—they were White students, who lost seats to Asian applicants at elite schools."

**Reaction:** People lean in. The follow-up is: "Why? Did White students' qualifications drop, or were they being protected by the old system?" This indicates a high-impact finding.

### 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

*   **The "Stock vs. Flow" Issue:** As the author admits in Section 6, using 12-month enrollment (stock) for a shock that only affects freshmen (flow) dilutes the results by 4x. **Action:** The author *must* move heaven and earth to get the IPEDS Fall Enrollment (first-time freshmen) data. Using the "stock" measure makes the results look "modest" in a way that might be a measurement artifact.
*   **Robustness:** Move the "Public vs. Private" distinction (Table 5) earlier. It’s a key mechanism: private schools had more "discretion" to lose.

### 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

**The Gap:** Currently, the paper is a very high-quality "Policy Evaluation" (it tells us what happened). To be AER, it needs more "Economic Mechanism." 

**Single Biggest Improvement:** The paper needs to test *why* the URM share stayed resilient. Is it because of "Test Optional" policies? If the author can correlate the URM resilience with specific institutional policy changes (e.g., dropping the SAT), it moves from a "fact" paper to a "mechanism" paper.
