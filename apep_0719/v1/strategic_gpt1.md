## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper studies what happened when several U.S. states banned Japanese immigrants from owning or leasing agricultural land in the early 1920s. Using linked census data, it argues that these laws pushed Japanese farmers out of agriculture and into higher-status nonfarm occupations, with effects that persisted for decades. A busy economist should care because the paper speaks to a broad question: when discrimination closes off one sector, does it simply impoverish the targeted group, or does it reallocate talent in ways that reshape long-run economic trajectories?

Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs? **Only partly.** The current intro is vivid and readable, but it takes a while to get to the real intellectual stake. It opens with an anecdote and then says the paper asks about “human capital consequences,” which is weaker and narrower than the actual contribution. The paper’s real question is not “did one man reinvent himself?” but “how do exclusionary institutions reallocate labor and alter immigrant assimilation paths?” That broader question should be explicit immediately.

### The pitch the paper should have

A stronger opening would say something like:

> Economic discrimination is usually studied as a tax on opportunity. But when exclusion blocks one occupation rather than all occupations, it may also reallocate workers across sectors. This paper asks whether the Alien Land Laws of the 1920s, which barred Japanese immigrants from owning or leasing agricultural land in several U.S. states, merely displaced Japanese farmers or instead redirected them into different and potentially better nonfarm careers.
>
> Using linked census panels, I show that Japanese farmers in states newly adopting these laws were substantially more likely to leave agriculture and moved into higher-status nonfarm occupations, with effects that persisted to 1940. The paper’s core contribution is to show that discriminatory institutions can have an important general-equilibrium-like sorting effect on the targeted group’s occupational trajectory, even while restricting rights and likely harming wealth accumulation.

That is cleaner, bigger, and more world-facing.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:**  
The paper argues that anti-Japanese land restrictions in the 1920s did not just exclude Japanese immigrants from farming; they durably redirected them into higher-status nonfarm occupations.

Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest papers? **Not yet enough.** The paper mentions Botticini and Eckstein and the immigrant assimilation literature, but the differentiation is still a little generic. Right now the contribution can be paraphrased too easily as: “another historical policy shock paper showing discrimination changed occupational outcomes.” The paper needs to say more sharply what is distinctive:

1. It studies **sector-specific exclusion**, not broad discrimination.
2. It studies **targeted occupational displacement** among an immigrant minority with preexisting sectoral specialization.
3. It finds a **paradoxical reallocation effect**: exclusion from one sector increased occupational status elsewhere.
4. It can potentially distinguish **occupational prestige mobility from wealth mobility**, which is intellectually important even if the current paper only partially does so.

Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world or filling a gap in a literature? **Mostly literature-gap framed, with some world-facing language.** It should lean harder into the world question: *What do exclusionary laws do to the economic paths of minority groups?* That is stronger than “this provides a quasi-experimental test of Botticini-Eckstein.”

Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what’s new? **Somewhat, but not crisply enough.** Right now they would say: “It’s a historical DiD-style paper on Alien Land Laws and Japanese occupational mobility.” That is competent, but not memorable. The memorable version is: “It shows that targeted exclusion from farming can accelerate nonfarm occupational upgrading among immigrants, so discrimination may reshape the composition of economic activity even when it suppresses rights.”

What would make this contribution bigger?

- **A better outcome frame:** right now the headline outcome is Duncan occupational score. That is publishable but not yet AER-big. If the paper could connect the reallocation to earnings proxies, business ownership, urban transition, children’s outcomes, or wealth accumulation, the contribution becomes much more consequential.
- **A sharper mechanism:** retail, services, and crafts are mentioned, but the paper does not yet fully exploit the opportunity to say *why* these occupations. Was this movement into ethnic entrepreneurship? Urban enclaves? Family businesses? Human-capital transfer? Community networks? This is where the story could become much richer.
- **A more balanced welfare frame:** the current “paradoxically generated upward occupational mobility” line is interesting, but somewhat dangerous. Without a stronger treatment of wealth, land equity, or welfare, readers may resist the premise. The paper itself notes this, but too late. It should lead with “occupational upgrading despite exclusion,” not imply “discrimination helped.”
- **A broader comparative framing:** the paper could be bigger if framed as evidence on how minorities respond to sectoral exclusion more generally, not only as a Japanese-American historical episode.

2. **Abramitzky, Boustan, Eriksson and related immigrant assimilation papers** — long-run occupational mobility and immigrant adaptation.
3. Historical work on **Japanese immigrants and Alien Land Laws** — likely legal-historical and economic history pieces such as Ferguson, Iwata, Chuman.
4. Literature on **discrimination and occupational sorting**, including work on Black migration, exclusion from occupations, and minority entrepreneurship.
5. Potentially literature on **forced reallocation after policy shocks**, including displacement from sectors due to regulation or conflict.

How should the paper position itself relative to those neighbors? **Build on and bridge them.** It should not “attack” the existing literature. The right posture is:

- Build on Botticini-Eckstein by providing a modern historical micro test of forced occupational reallocation.
- Connect to immigrant assimilation work by showing that policy-induced sectoral displacement, not just voluntary convergence, can shape mobility.
- Speak to discrimination literature by emphasizing that exclusion can alter *where* discrimination bites and *how* targeted groups adapt.

Is the paper positioned too narrowly or too broadly? **Currently a bit too narrowly in data and too broadly in claim.** It is narrowly executed as a historical census panel paper about one legal episode, but the rhetoric occasionally reaches toward very broad claims about discrimination and policy today. The fix is not to narrow the claim further; it is to position the paper around a more precise broad question: **sector-specific exclusion and occupational reallocation**.

What literature does the paper seem unaware of, or at least under-engaged with?

- **Occupational licensing / barriers to entry / exclusion from sectors** more generally.
- **Minority entrepreneurship and enclave economies.**
- **Historical discrimination and economic adaptation** beyond immigration per se.

Is the paper having the right conversation? **Almost, but not quite.** The current conversation is “discrimination + immigrants + historical episode.” A more impactful conversation is “how targeted exclusion reshapes the allocation of minority talent across sectors.” That would resonate beyond economic history.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Japanese immigrants had become disproportionately successful in intensive agriculture in some states. White backlash produced Alien Land Laws designed to exclude them from owning and leasing agricultural land.

### Tension
What happens when a discriminatory law cuts off a minority group’s main economic niche? Standard intuition says exclusion should reduce opportunity. But another possibility is that workers reallocate into other occupations, perhaps revealing hidden margins of adaptation and upward mobility.

### Resolution
Japanese farmers in states adopting these laws were more likely to leave farming and moved into higher-status nonfarm occupations, with persistent effects.

### Implications
Discrimination may do more than depress outcomes contemporaneously: it can redirect occupational trajectories in durable ways. That matters for how economists think about adaptation, assimilation, and the unintended consequences of exclusionary policy.

Does the paper have a clear narrative arc? **Serviceable, but not fully disciplined.** The paper does have a story, but it oscillates between three possible stories:

1. discrimination harmed Japanese farmers;
2. discrimination paradoxically helped them;
3. discrimination reallocated them across sectors.

The third is the strongest. The first is obvious but underdeveloped. The second is provocative but risky and, as written, somewhat overstated relative to the outcomes measured. Right now the paper sometimes sounds like it wants the “paradox of prejudice” hook, but it does not yet have enough evidence on welfare to carry that framing cleanly.

So the story it **should** be telling is:

> Alien Land Laws were designed to expel Japanese immigrants from farming. They succeeded in doing so. But exclusion from agriculture did not simply produce occupational downgrading or exit; instead it redirected many Japanese farmers into higher-status nonfarm work, with durable consequences for the group’s occupational distribution. The paper thus shows that discriminatory institutions can simultaneously restrict rights and induce upward occupational reallocation.

That is coherent and defensible.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

What fact would I lead with at a dinner party of economists?

> “When states banned Japanese immigrants from owning or leasing farmland in the 1920s, Japanese farmers left agriculture in large numbers and ended up in higher-status nonfarm occupations for decades.”

Would people lean in or reach for their phones? **Lean in, initially.** The result is genuinely counterintuitive. The combination of blatant discrimination and upward occupational mobility is interesting.

What follow-up question would they ask?

Almost certainly one of these:

- “Higher-status by what measure — prestige, income, or wealth?”
- “Did they become shopkeepers and small business owners, or just leave farming for marginal urban jobs?”
- “Is this a story about adaptation or about selection/survival?”
- “Did their children benefit?”
- “So was discrimination actually beneficial?”  

That last question is exactly why the current framing needs care. The paper must get ahead of it. If the answer is “occupational upgrading but likely not higher overall welfare,” that has to be stated early and repeatedly.

Are the findings modest or null? No. The findings are not null. But the paper’s **headline variable is modest in ambition**. Occupational score is one step removed from welfare. The paper has to convince the reader that occupational sorting itself is a first-order object, not just a convenient measure.

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

Without rewriting the paper, here is what would make it read better strategically.

### 1. Rebuild the introduction around the big question
The first two paragraphs should move quickly from episode to question: sectoral exclusion, minority adaptation, and long-run occupational reallocation. The anecdote is fine, but it currently occupies too much prime real estate relative to the conceptual stakes.

### 2. Stop foregrounding identification rhetoric in the introduction
The intro spends a lot of valuable space on “three features of the design strengthen causal identification.” That is seminar-defense language, not editorially optimal framing. In an AER-caliber paper, the introduction should foreground the **finding and why it matters**, not a mini-methods section. The design can be summarized in one sentence early and unpacked later.

### 3. Move some “robustness” results into the main narrative
Two results are conceptually central, not robustness:

- non-farm Japanese workers show no change;
- interstate mobility does not increase.

These are mechanism-defining facts. They belong in the main results or main discussion, not as secondary checks.

### 4. Expand the discussion of where people went
If the paper can show the destination occupations more concretely, that belongs in the main text, probably as a simple table or figure. “Retail, services, and skilled crafts” is too vague for such an important mechanism.

### 5. Shorten the threats-to-validity subsection in the main text
This section is overlong relative to the strategic needs of the paper. Referees can handle limits later. Right now it slows the narrative.

### 6. The conclusion should do more than summarize
The current conclusion is competent but generic. It should end with the sharper takeaway: discriminatory policy can reallocate targeted populations across sectors in ways that persist long after the original law, and economists need to distinguish occupational mobility from welfare.

### 7. Remove distracting nonstandard elements for a top-journal submission
The autonomous-generation acknowledgment is a major distraction and, candidly, a likely nonstarter in current top-journal practice. Even in a private memo: this will shape how editors and referees read the manuscript, and not in a good way. If the goal is AER placement, presentation conventions matter.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

The main gap is **not primarily identification** for purposes of this memo. The gap is a combination of **framing and scope**.

### Framing problem
Yes, definitely. The paper’s strongest idea is larger than the way it currently sells itself. It should be a paper about **how exclusion from one sector reshapes minority economic trajectories**, not just “did Alien Land Laws affect occupational score?”

### Scope problem
Also yes. The current outcome set is a bit thin for the ambition of the claims. To excite the top people in this field, the paper likely needs one of:
- clearer destination occupations/mechanisms,
- stronger welfare-relevant margins,
- intergenerational consequences,
- productivity or entrepreneurial outcomes,
- stronger comparative context.

### Novelty problem
Moderate. The episode is interesting and the result is counterintuitive, but a skeptical reader may still ask whether this is just another historical policy-shock paper with a prestige-score outcome. The novelty is there conceptually, but it needs to be made unmistakable.

### Ambition problem
Yes. The paper is clever and promising, but currently feels a bit safe. It settles for “occupational score rose” when the bigger, more ambitious question is “how did targeted exclusion reshape the economic organization of a minority community?” That is the AER-level version.

### Single most impactful piece of advice
**Reframe the paper around sectoral exclusion and occupational reallocation, and then show concretely where displaced Japanese farmers went and why that margin matters economically.**

If they only change one thing, it should be that. Not a cosmetic intro edit, but a deeper repositioning: from a neat historical DiD to a broader statement about how discriminatory barriers redirect talent.
