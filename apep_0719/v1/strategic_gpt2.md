## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks what happens when a discriminatory policy shuts an immigrant minority out of a key sector of the economy. Studying state Alien Land Laws in the 1920s, it argues that Japanese farmers were pushed out of agriculture and reallocated into higher-status nonfarm occupations, with effects that persisted into 1940. A busy economist should care because the paper speaks to a broad question: whether exclusionary institutions merely depress minorities economically, or instead redirect their specialization in durable ways.

The paper does **not** yet articulate this pitch as clearly as it should in the first two paragraphs. Right now the opening is vivid, but the framing is too local and too quickly slides into “human capital consequences,” which undersells the bigger question. The real hook is not one man in Washington; it is that a legally targeted, race-based exclusion from land can reshape an entire group’s occupational trajectory.

### The pitch the paper should have in its first two paragraphs

> Discriminatory policies often aim to exclude minority groups from particular markets, but their economic effects need not stop at simple loss. When a targeted group is blocked from one sector, does exclusion trap it in worse jobs, or does it force a reallocation into other occupations and alter the group’s long-run economic specialization? This is a first-order question for the economics of discrimination, immigration, and structural change.  
>  
> This paper studies that question using the Alien Land Laws enacted by U.S. states in the early 1920s, which were explicitly designed to remove Japanese immigrants from agriculture. Linking individuals across the 1920, 1930, and 1940 censuses, I show that these laws pushed Japanese farmers out of farming and into higher-status nonfarm occupations, with persistent effects over two decades. The broader implication is that discriminatory exclusion can reallocate human capital across sectors in ways policymakers intend politically but may not anticipate economically.

That is the AER version of the story.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### Contribution in one sentence

The paper’s contribution is to show that a discriminatory policy designed to exclude Japanese immigrants from landholding did not just reduce their access to agriculture, but durably redirected them into higher-status nonfarm occupations.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest papers?

Only partly. The paper names broad literatures, but the differentiation is still too thin. At present, the contribution reads as a competent historical policy paper with linked-census data, not yet as a paper that decisively changes how we think about discrimination and occupational sorting.

The key differentiation should be:

1. **Relative to historical work on Alien Land Laws:** most of that literature is legal, historical, or descriptive; this paper claims individual-level evidence on occupational reallocation.
2. **Relative to immigration assimilation papers:** those study mobility trajectories of immigrants; this paper studies a race-targeted legal shock that forcibly changes sectoral allocation.
3. **Relative to discrimination papers:** most emphasize losses in earnings, exclusion, or depressed outcomes; this paper emphasizes **reallocation** and **occupational specialization**, not welfare gains per se.
4. **Relative to Botticini-Eckstein style arguments:** that work offers a long-run theory of exclusion-induced specialization; this paper offers a modern, policy-driven historical episode with micro data.

That comparison is available in the draft, but it is not yet crisp enough.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or filling a gap in a LITERATURE?

It is mixed, but too much of the current introduction slips into literature-gap mode. The stronger version is clearly a **world question**:

- What do exclusionary policies do to the economic allocation of a targeted minority?
- Do they impoverish through exclusion alone, or also induce occupational re-sorting?

That is much better than “there is little quasi-experimental evidence on forced sorting.”

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?

Right now they could probably say: “It’s a linked-census historical DiD/DDD paper showing Alien Land Laws pushed Japanese farmers into somewhat higher-status jobs.”

That is not nothing, but it is still too close to “another DiD paper about discriminatory policy.” The introduction needs to tell them why this is a broader conceptual result about **how discrimination changes sectoral specialization**.

### What would make the contribution bigger?

Three concrete ways:

1. **Make the outcome more economically meaningful.**  
   “Occupational score” is useful, but it is too abstract to carry a top-journal story by itself. The paper needs to foreground what these people became: merchants? proprietors? craftsmen? urban self-employed? If the core finding is really about movement into entrepreneurship, commerce, or family business, that is much bigger and more intuitive than a 2-point Duncan-score increase.

2. **Clarify mechanism as sectoral reallocation, not “discrimination helped them.”**  
   The provocative framing is currently a little sloppy. The bigger contribution is not that prejudice produced upward mobility in a welfare sense; it is that exclusion from land changed the sectoral use of immigrant human capital. That distinction is crucial and should anchor the paper.

3. **Connect more directly to contemporary policy debates about targeted economic exclusion.**  
   The current China/foreign land-ownership sentence feels tacked on. If the authors want modern relevance, they should frame the paper as evidence on what happens when states use property restrictions to target politically disfavored groups. That broadens the audience beyond economic history.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

### Closest neighbors

The closest neighbors seem to be:

1. **Botticini and Eckstein, *The Chosen Few*** — on exclusion from land and occupational specialization among Jews.
2. **Abramitzky, Boustan, and Eriksson** on immigrant assimilation and occupational mobility in the U.S.
3. Historical work on **Japanese immigrants and Alien Land Laws** — e.g., legal/historical treatments like Chuman, Iwata, and perhaps more recent historical political economy work if available.
4. Broader papers on **discrimination and economic adjustment**, including work on race-targeted institutions and minority occupational sorting.
5. Potentially papers on **forced structural change / labor reallocation after shocks**, even if from other contexts.

### How should it position itself relative to those neighbors?

It should mostly **build on and synthesize**, not attack.

- Build on **Botticini-Eckstein** by saying: here is a modern micro-historical setting where state policy explicitly excludes a minority from land, allowing us to observe the reallocation process directly.
- Build on **immigrant mobility** work by emphasizing that this is not ordinary assimilation; it is coerced occupational change caused by targeted legal exclusion.
- Build on **discrimination** literatures by saying that sector-specific exclusion can have equilibrium effects on occupational allocation that standard earnings-focused approaches miss.

It does **not** need a combative “the literature has overlooked” posture. The better move is: “These literatures have mostly studied exclusion, mobility, and specialization separately; this paper links them.”

### Is the paper positioned too narrowly or too broadly?

Right now it is oddly both:

- **Too narrowly** in the body: very focused on Japanese farmers in 7 states, which risks sounding niche.
- **Too broadly** in the claims: “discriminatory land restrictions paradoxically generated upward occupational mobility” sounds like a sweeping theorem from one episode.

The right balance is: narrow empirical setting, broad conceptual question.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?

A few areas it should probably speak to more directly:

1. **Structural change / sectoral reallocation** literatures.  
   This is not just an ethnicity/discrimination paper; it is a paper about forced movement across sectors.

2. **Economic history of exclusionary institutions and minority adaptation.**  
   There is a broader conversation here than the current intro signals.

3. **Entrepreneurship / ethnic enclave / self-employment** literatures, if the destination occupations support it.  
   If these displaced farmers moved into retail trade and small business, that is a much richer and more established conversation than generic “occupational status.”

4. **Political economy of targeted property-rights restrictions.**  
   The paper could speak to how states use property law as a discriminatory tool.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?

Not quite yet. It is currently having a conversation with:
- discrimination,
- immigration,
- and a bit of economic history.

The higher-impact conversation is:
- **targeted exclusionary institutions as engines of reallocation and minority specialization**.

That is a stronger and somewhat less obvious frame.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Japanese immigrants had become disproportionately successful in certain segments of agriculture, especially intensive farming. States then enacted Alien Land Laws specifically to remove them from that sector.

### Tension

We know such policies were designed to harm and exclude. But what do they do economically beyond immediate exclusion? Do they simply depress outcomes, or do they redirect the group into different occupations, possibly durably?

### Resolution

The paper finds that Japanese farmers in states adopting these laws were more likely to leave farming and moved into higher-status nonfarm occupations, with persistence to 1940.

### Implications

Discrimination can reshape occupational allocation, not just lower access. Sector-specific exclusion can induce minority groups to redeploy skills into other sectors, meaning the effects of discriminatory institutions may be more complex than simple contemporaneous loss.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the bones of one, but not yet a fully controlled arc. Right now the paper oscillates between three stories:

1. a paper about discriminatory policy;
2. a paper about occupational mobility;
3. a paper about a paradoxical “benefit” of exclusion.

That third story is dangerous. It is catchy, but it invites skepticism and moral confusion, and it is not actually the strongest version of the paper. The paper should not be telling a “prejudice helped its victims” story. It should be telling a “targeted exclusion caused durable sectoral reallocation” story.

That would give it a cleaner arc:
- exclusion from land,
- forced exit from agriculture,
- transition into commerce/services/skilled work,
- persistence,
- implications for how we think about discrimination and economic adaptation.

At present, some results read like they were assembled after the fact to support the word “paradox.” The paper will be stronger if it abandons the search for a provocative moral twist and leans into the economics.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?

“A set of race-targeted land laws in the 1920s appears to have pushed Japanese immigrants out of farming and durably into higher-status nonfarm occupations.”

That is the best dinner-party version.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

Some would lean in, but the reaction would depend entirely on the second sentence. If the presenter says “discrimination increased occupational prestige,” people will immediately become suspicious: prestige relative to what? income? wealth? selection? If the presenter says “the laws reallocated a minority group out of agriculture and into retail and skilled urban occupations,” that is much more compelling.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Almost certainly:
- “Into what occupations exactly?”
- Followed by: “Does higher occupational score mean they were actually better off?”

The paper anticipates the second question with the caveat that prestige is not welfare, but that caveat is currently buried in the discussion and is actually central. It should be near the front. If the paper cannot show income or wealth, it needs to own that limitation strategically rather than sounding as if it has discovered that discrimination was beneficial.

### If the findings are modest or null, is that okay?

The results are not null, but they are modest in substantive interpretation because the marquee variable is occupational score. The result is still interesting, but only if the paper persuades the reader that this is evidence of meaningful reallocation rather than a scale artifact. Without concrete destination occupations, the paper risks feeling smaller than the coefficients suggest.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

At the moment, the gap is mainly a combination of **framing problem** and **scope problem**, with a smaller **novelty risk**.

### Framing problem

This is the biggest issue. The paper is currently framed as a paradox: discriminatory laws caused upward occupational mobility. That is catchy, but not quite the right claim and not the most persuasive one. The AER version is: discriminatory exclusion from a major sector changed the long-run occupational allocation of a targeted minority. That is bigger, cleaner, and more credible.

### Scope problem

The paper’s main outcome is too narrow and too stylized. Occupational prestige is not enough by itself to make the paper feel field-defining. The paper needs to show what “up the ladder” actually means:
- what occupations,
- what sector,
- self-employment vs wage work,
- urban commerce vs manual labor,
- perhaps family business formation if possible.

Even without new identification machinery, adding that substance would make the paper feel much more consequential.

### Novelty problem

There is some novelty, but the paper risks being perceived as “another historical anti-Asian policy paper using linked census data” unless it sharpens the conceptual advance. The underlying episode is interesting; the current presentation does not yet extract the maximum novelty from it.

### Ambition problem

A bit. The paper is competent and tidy, but safe in what it documents and slightly over-bold in what it claims. AER papers usually either:
- answer a very big question cleanly, or
- uncover a truly striking fact with broad implications.

This paper can get closer if it stops treating the result as a quirky paradox and starts treating it as evidence on how exclusion reallocates minority talent across sectors.

### Single most impactful advice

**Reframe the paper around sectoral reallocation under discriminatory exclusion, and show concretely where displaced farmers went—not just that their Duncan scores rose.**

That one change would simultaneously improve the pitch, the contribution, the literature positioning, and the “so what.”
