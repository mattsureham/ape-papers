**Idea Fidelity**

The paper closely tracks the original idea manifest. It uses the QWI race/ethnicity panel (2000–2022), focuses on state EITC supplements, treats NAICS 56 as the low-wage “treated” industry, and implements the proposed triple-difference (state EITC × NAICS 56 × Hispanic) with the recommended fixed effects structure. The author also estimates Callaway–Sant’Anna group-time ATT/event study and conducts the stated robustness checks (control sectors, finance placebo, continuous generosity). One omission: the manifest anticipated comparisons with broader industry × ethnicity heterogeneity (e.g., other sectors). While the paper hints at broader gains in the discussion, it never shows sector-by-sector results beyond NAICS 56, so the potential for a more comprehensive picture of “which sectors respond for which workers,” as mentioned in the manifest, remains unexplored.

**Summary**

The paper documents a “sorting dividend” from state EITC supplements: Hispanic employment in NAICS 56 (administrative support) declines relative to non-Hispanics and higher-wage control sectors in treated states. The triple-difference coefficient (-8.5 log points) is robust to Callaway–Sant’Anna estimation, alternative specifications, and placebo tests. The author interprets the result as Hispanics reallocating out of precarious temp-sector employment because EITC subsidies make other sectors more attractive.

**Essential Points**

1. **Causal interpretation hinges on “clean” triple difference.** The triple-difference implicitly assumes that the differential Hispanic vs. non-Hispanic trend in NAICS 56 would have been the same in treated and untreated states absent the EITC. The paper shows nice pre-trends for the Hispanic×NAICS 56 cell, but does not document analogous pre-trends for the two control comparisons (Hispanic in other sectors; non-Hispanic in NAICS 56). Without this, it is unclear whether some third factor (e.g., state-level immigration enforcement, sectoral demand shifts) differentially affects Hispanics in NAICS 56 in the treated states but also asymmetric non-Hispanic or non-NAICS 56 trends. Please present pre-period event studies or other graphical evidence for each of the three parallel-trend comparisons to demonstrate the triple difference is valid.

2. **Mechanism remains speculative without evidence on worker destinations.** The core interpretation is that Hispanic workers move from NAICS 56 to “better” jobs elsewhere. Yet the data do not track individual flows, and the paper never shows that Hispanic employment rises in other sectors or that non-Hispanic employment in NAICS 56 is unaffected (or less affected). The narrative would be much stronger if the author showed—within the same framework—that Hispanic employment grows (or falls less) in the control sectors in treated states after adoption, ideally with event studies. Alternatively, exploring employment changes in industries known to attract displaced administrative-support workers (e.g., healthcare, education) would help validate the reallocation story versus other explanations (e.g., selective out-migration of Hispanics from treated states after the EITC or compositional shifts within NAICS 56).

3. **Possible confound from concurrent low-income policies or economic cycles.** The paper argues that other state labor policies are unlikely to target Hispanic workers in NAICS 56. However, several of the same states that adopt generous EITCs also implement (or expand) minimum wages, paid family leave, or workforce development programs contemporaneously. These policies could plausibly affect Hispanic employment in NAICS 56 disproportionately if Hispanics are over-represented in low-wage jobs. Because identification ultimately comes from within-state, within-year variation, state×year controls do not remove the concern if co-occurring policies interact with ethnicity×industry composition differently across states. Please either control for major policy expansions (minimum wage, workforce development, immigrant integration programs) or show that treatment timing is uncorrelated with those policies’ changes. At minimum, provide robustness checks that include these state-level policy trends or synthetic controls to ensure the EITC is not proxying for a broader low-wage policy bundle.

**Suggestions**

- **Provide richer event-study evidence for all legs of the triple difference.** The presented event study focuses on the Hispanic×NAICS 56 cell. To give readers confidence in the triple-difference assumption, plot the analogous pre- and post-treatment estimates for: (i) non-Hispanic workers in NAICS 56 (holding industry fixed), and (ii) Hispanic workers in the control sectors. If those legs also exhibit flat pre-trends and flat post-trends (or move differently), it clarifies that the triple difference truly isolates Hispanic-specific, sector-specific effects of the EITC.

- **Illustrate destination sectors or aggregate Hispanic employment shifts.** Since the reallocation story is central, try to show where the workers go. Two feasible approaches: (a) Estimate a related triple difference where the “treated” industry is each control sector (one at a time) to test whether Hispanic employment increases there after EITC adoption—this would show a redistribution from NAICS 56 to other sectors. (b) Use the QWI totals to compare Hispanic employment growth across treated and control states in the higher-wage sectors to show an offset. Even if the reallocations are noisy, demonstrating that NAICS 56 declines are not offset by broad Hispanic outflows strengthens the interpretation.

- **Explore heterogeneous effects by worker characteristics and EITC generosity.** The manifest noted the variation in state generosity and phased expansions. Consider interacting the treatment with the generosity share (beyond the continuous coefficient) or with the state’s share of single-parent Hispanic households, to test whether the effect is larger where the EITC bite is strongest. Likewise, separate results for early vs. late adopters, or for high vs. low pre-treatment Hispanic concentration, could reveal whether the sorting dividend depends on the pre-existing low-wage Hispanic population.

- **Address potential measurement concerns in the QWI.** Administrative data can suffer from state-level changes in UI system coverage, especially around NAICS 56 (temp agencies can misreport). Include a discussion (or appendix table) showing that NAICS 56 data quality does not change around treatment (e.g., no spike in missing values, consistent share of total employment). If possible, replicate the main result using alternative measures (e.g., total employment vs. stable employment) to show robustness to different QWI constructs.

- **Clarify the economic magnitude with a back-of-the-envelope calculation.** The discussion cites “approximately 128,000 workers reallocated.” Provide the steps: from the 8.5% log-point decline and Hispanic employment levels to the implied headcount. This would help readers gauge the importance of the sorting dividend.

- **Discuss policy implications more cautiously.** The conclusion that the EITC improves job quality rests on the assumption that other sectors offer better jobs. Without data on wages or benefits, clarify that the inference is tentative and highlight avenues for future research (e.g., linking to LEHD or using CPS to track wages). This modesty will strengthen credibility and show awareness of data limits.

- **Expand on the continuous treatment analysis.** Column (2) of Table 4 is underpowered. Consider instrumenting generosity (e.g., using political alignment or lagged generosity) or pooling the binary treatment with an intensity measure to better estimate dose-response. Alternatively, show that the binary adoption effect increases monotonically with generosity by estimating separate coefficients for high- vs. low-generosity states (some of this appears in Appendix Table A1 but expand on it in the main text).

- **Highlight how the “sorting dividend” differs from displacement.** Critics may worry that the administrative-support decline simply reflects displacement (workers shifting to unemployment or out of the labor force). Provide evidence (perhaps from unemployment claims or labor force participation trends, if available) that overall Hispanic employment does not decline in treated states. At minimum, emphasize that the hiring decline, paired with separations falling, is consistent with reduced inflows rather than layoffs, but explicitly rule out net labor-force withdrawal.

By addressing these points, the paper will better convince readers that the observed triple-difference effect is indeed an EITC-driven reallocation rather than an artifact of other trends, and will make the “sorting dividend” story substantially stronger.
