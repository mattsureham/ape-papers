### 2. Summary
This paper exploits staggered state EITC supplements in a triple-difference framework using QWI ethnicity data to estimate causal effects on Hispanic employment in administrative support (NAICS 56, a low-wage, high-Hispanic, precarious sector) relative to non-Hispanics and higher-wage controls. It finds a statistically robust 8.5% decline in stable employment (peaking at 10.9% after 8 years), driven by reduced hiring inflows, with Callaway-Sant'Anna and event-study evidence supporting parallel pre-trends and a gradual post-treatment dynamic. The results reveal a novel "sorting dividend," where EITCs appear to reallocate Hispanic workers from temp-like roles to stabler sectors, extending EITC literature beyond aggregate participation to sectoral composition.

### 3. Essential Points
1. **Theoretical coherence of negative effect**: The manifest (and canonical EITC theory, e.g., Eissa-Liebman) anticipates *increases* in EITC-eligible employment for marginal groups like low-wage Hispanics; the paper's negative finding in NAICS 56 requires stronger justification for "sorting out" rather than overall employment growth offset by reallocation. Explicitly model/estimate the positive double-difference (EITC × Hispanic across *all* sectors, as hinted) to confirm net employment rise with compositional shift—without this, the "dividend" claim risks overinterpretation of a potentially spurious triple-diff.

2. **Event-study visualization and full reporting**: Pre/post coefficients are described but not tabulated/figure-ized (e.g., no \cref{fig:event} or dynamic table), undermining the "most compelling evidence" claim. Provide a full event-study graph (with 95% CIs) for TWFE/CS estimators across all outcomes, explicitly testing pre-trend joint significance (e.g., via Walsh test). The described gradual phase-in is plausible but must be shown to rule out anticipation or anticipation/reversal.

3. **Control sector validity and treatment coding**: Restricting controls to 6 higher-wage sectors (vs. manifest's "all other industries") risks bias if NAICS 56 trends differ systematically from this non-representative bundle (e.g., retail/food service may contaminate low-wage parallels). Report a specification using all 20 NAICS 2-digit sectors; clarify exact 29-state list/adoption dates (appendix table) and why 2 states/DC dropped (e.g., data gaps?). Continuous generosity is noisy ($p=0.10$); weight by credit size in binary coding.

These are fixable but foundational; addressing them is required for revise-and-resubmit.

### 4. Suggestions

Compute implied reallocation flows: e.g., back-of-envelope using national Hispanic NAICS 56 totals (~1.5M) and ATT to quantify workers shifted (~128k, as noted). Contrast with prior EITC studies (e.g., Nichols-Schaefer 2015 AER on intensive margins) by estimating payroll effects (mentioned in data but not tabled).
