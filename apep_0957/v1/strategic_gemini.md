**FROM:** Editor, American Economic Review
**RE:** Strategic Positioning of "The Sorting Dividend: State EITC Supplements and Hispanic Worker Reallocation"

---

### 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks if work subsidies do more than just increase labor supply—do they improve the *quality* of jobs workers hold? Using state EITC expansions, it finds that while total Hispanic employment rises, employment in the precarious "temp" sector (NAICS 56) actually falls. This suggests a "sorting dividend" where subsidies allow low-wage workers to bypass the most unstable jobs in favor of more stable, higher-value employment.

**Evaluation:** The paper does a decent job in the first two paragraphs, but it is currently too focused on the "how" (DDD, NAICS codes) and not enough on the "so what."

**The pitch it should have:** 
"Thirty years of EITC research has focused on whether the credit moves people from home to work. This paper argues we have missed the most important welfare margin: *which* work they choose. By increasing the effective after-tax return to stable employment, the EITC allows vulnerable workers to escape the 'temp-agency trap' of the administrative support sector, creating a sorting dividend that improves job quality even without direct wage mandates."

---

### 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**The Contribution:** The paper identifies a sectoral reallocation margin of the EITC, showing that work subsidies can reduce employment in precarious sectors while increasing it in stable ones.

*   **Differentiation:** It moves beyond the binary "working vs. not" of Eissa/Liebman or Meyer/Rosenbaum. It differs from Chetty et al. (2013) by looking at sectoral composition rather than just intensive/extensive margins of earnings.
*   **Framing:** Currently framed as filling a gap in the EITC literature. It would be stronger if framed as a question about **Labor Market Dualism**: Does the tax system act as a bridge from the secondary (precarious) labor market to the primary (stable) labor market?
*   **Clarity:** A smart economist would get the "Sorting Dividend" concept immediately.
*   **Bigger Contribution:** To truly hit AER levels, the paper needs to prove *where* they go. The author admits the QWI doesn't track individuals. Without showing the "destination" (e.g., healthcare, retail) is actually "better" in a welfare sense, it's just a paper about a sector-specific decline.

---

### 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

*   **Closest Neighbors:** Eissa & Liebman (1996); Rothstein (2010); Hotz & Scholz (2006); and the recent DiD literature (Callaway & Sant’Anna).
*   **Positioning:** It should "Build on" the extensive margin literature and "Synthesize" with the literature on **Frictional Labor Markets**. The EITC isn't just a subsidy; it's a search-capital injection that lets people wait for a better firm match.
*   **Missing Conversations:** The paper is silent on the **"Search and Matching"** literature. If the EITC makes people more selective, it’s a search model story. It also ignores the **"Firm-Side"** literature—do temp agencies lower their wages when the EITC rises, or do they just lose their labor supply to "better" firms?

---

### 4. NARRATIVE ARC

*   **Setup:** EITC is the primary US tool for boosting low-wage work.
*   **Tension:** If EITC boosts labor supply, why would it ever cause employment to *fall* in a specific low-wage sector?
*   **Resolution:** Because it boosts the *relative* value of stable jobs more than precarious ones, causing workers to "sort up."
*   **Implications:** Policy evaluations that only look at aggregate employment numbers understate the welfare gains of the EITC.

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong. It has a "counter-intuitive" result (a subsidy reducing employment) that is resolved by a logical economic mechanism.

---

### 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

*   **The Lead Fact:** "The EITC actually makes people *quit* temp agencies."
*   **Reaction:** Lean in. Economists love when a subsidy leads to a decline in a specific quantity because it implies a change in the *shadow price* of stability.
*   **Follow-up:** "Where do they go, and do they stay there?" If the author can't answer this with QWI, the "So What" starts to fizzle by the end of the presentation.

---

### 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

*   **Front-load the Mechanism:** Move the "Mechanism" section from Institutional Background (Page 4) into the Introduction. The comparison between a $24k temp job and a $26k healthcare job is the "hook."
*   **Appendix:** The Callaway-Sant'Anna discussion is standard now; keep it brief in the text.
*   **The "Hiring vs. Separations" result (Page 10):** This is a goldmine. It suggests the EITC affects *entry* choices. This deserves a dedicated figure, not just a row in Table 2.

---

### 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

**The Gap:** Currently, this is a very high-quality *Applied Economics* or *JHR* paper. To be *AER*, it needs to move from "This happens" to "This is why it matters for our understanding of labor market equilibrium."

**The Ambition Problem:** The paper is currently a "Case Study of NAICS 56." It needs to be a "Theory of Subsidized Sorting." 

**Single Biggest Improvement:**
The author must attempt to quantify the **Welfare Gain of the Reallocation**. Use the employment shifts to estimate a structural parameter of how workers value "job stability" or "benefits" relative to cash. If you can put a dollar value on the "Sorting Dividend," you have an AER paper.
