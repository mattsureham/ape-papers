## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether the EITC changes not just whether low-income people work, but where they work. Using staggered adoption of state EITC supplements, it argues that Hispanic employment falls in administrative support services—especially temp/contract-labor-heavy employment—suggesting that work subsidies may reallocate workers away from relatively precarious jobs and toward better alternatives.

Why should a busy economist care? Because if true, this reframes the EITC from a participation policy into a policy that also changes the composition and possibly quality of low-wage employment. That is a potentially important idea. But in the current draft, the paper’s evidence is much narrower than the rhetoric: it shows a relative decline in one sector for one group, not yet a broad result about job quality or “sorting dividends.”

### Does the paper articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Partly. The introduction is better than average at stating the question quickly. But the paper’s current first paragraphs are still too “inside baseball” and too sector-specific too soon. They ask the right question, but they don’t persuade the reader that this is a first-order economic question rather than a clever application to NAICS 56. The big idea should come first: **work subsidies may reshape the allocation of labor across jobs of different quality.** Then the administrative-support sector should appear as the empirical test case, not as the whole point of the paper.

### The pitch the paper should have

> The Earned Income Tax Credit is usually studied as a policy that increases labor force participation and earnings. But work subsidies may also change the allocation of workers across jobs: by raising the payoff to formal employment, they may pull low-income workers away from unstable, contingent work and toward more durable employment relationships.  
>   
> This paper studies that reallocation margin using staggered adoption of state EITC supplements and administrative employment data by industry and ethnicity. I show that Hispanic employment declines in administrative support services—the sector that includes temporary-help and labor-contracting firms—relative to other workers and sectors after state EITC adoption. The broader implication is that tax credits may affect the composition, not just the level, of low-wage employment.

That is the version a top journal reader can remember.

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper claims that state EITC supplements reallocate low-wage Hispanic workers away from administrative support/temp-sector employment, revealing a sectoral-composition margin of work-subsidy effects that standard employment analyses miss.

### Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest papers?

Not enough. The paper says “the literature has been silent on sectoral composition,” which is directionally plausible, but it does not sharply map the novelty relative to the main EITC papers and adjacent literatures on job quality, contingent work, and worker sorting. Right now the contribution reads as:

- EITC paper + Hispanic subsample + sectoral outcome + triple-diff.

That is not yet distinctive enough for AER. The paper needs to say much more crisply: **existing EITC work asks whether people work more; this paper asks whether subsidies move workers across margins of job quality and employment stability.** And then it needs to show that this is truly different from prior work on earnings/hours/incidence.

### Is the contribution framed as a question about the world, or a gap in a literature?

Mixed, but too often as a literature gap. The stronger version is clearly a world question:

- **World question:** When government subsidizes low-wage work, does it pull workers out of contingent labor-market arrangements and into more stable jobs?
- **Weaker literature-gap version:** No one has used QWI race/ethnicity data to study sectoral composition under state EITCs.

The latter is not an AER contribution. The former might be.

### Could a smart economist explain what’s new after reading the introduction?

Not cleanly. Right now they would probably say: “It’s a triple-diff on state EITCs showing Hispanic employment falls in NAICS 56, maybe because workers move to better jobs.” That is too hedged and too design-labeled. They would not yet say: “This paper changes how we think about the EITC.”

### What would make the contribution bigger?

Most importantly: **show where the workers go, or show that the jobs they move toward are observably better.** Without that, “sorting dividend” is branding, not contribution.

Specific ways to make it bigger:

1. **Different outcome variable:**  
   Move beyond employment in one sector to outcomes that more directly capture job quality:
   - employment in high-stability sectors,
   - average earnings per worker,
   - job-to-job transitions if available,
   - within-state changes in shares employed by sector quality tier,
   - separation/hiring rates aggregated into a “stability” metric.

2. **Different comparison:**  
   Rather than one treated sector versus ad hoc controls, construct a broader industry ranking by wages/turnover/benefits exposure and test whether EITCs shift employment toward better jobs along that gradient.

3. **Different mechanism:**  
   The paper’s key claim is about worker sorting, but current evidence cannot separate worker reallocation from firm demand or compositional changes. A stronger mechanism section would test whether effects are largest in sectors/places where EITC eligibility is highest, where temp penetration is highest, or where alternative stable jobs are more available.

4. **Different framing:**  
   Stop centering “Hispanic labor market outcomes” as a standalone contribution. The real contribution, if any, is to **tax policy and labor-market allocation/job quality**. Hispanic workers are the empirical lens because they are disproportionately exposed in this sector, not the ultimate audience.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

### Closest neighbors

The closest neighbors are likely:

1. **Eissa and Liebman (1996)** / **Meyer and Rosenbaum (2001)** — canonical EITC labor supply/participation.
2. **Chetty, Friedman, and Saez (2013)** — EITC, earnings responses, salience/take-up/admin tax data angle.
3. **Rothstein (2010)** — EITC incidence and labor-market equilibrium.
4. **Hotz and Scholz (or Hotz/Mullin/Scholz-style surveys and empirical work)** — standard EITC framing.
5. On contingent work/temp-help:
   - **Autor and Houseman** on temporary-help/intermediation and low-wage labor-market outcomes.
   - Possibly papers on job ladders, outsourcing, and fissured work arrangements.

I would also think about links to:
- job quality / precarious work,
- sectoral sorting,
- labor-market polarization and contingent employment,
- incidence of wage subsidies.

### How should the paper position itself relative to those neighbors?

**Build on** the canonical EITC literature, not attack it. The right pitch is: prior work established effects on labor-force attachment; this paper asks whether those effects also operate through reallocation across types of jobs.

It should **synthesize** EITC and job-quality/contingent-work literatures. That is where a more surprising conversation lies. Right now it is too trapped between “another EITC paper” and “another ethnic-employment paper.”

### Is the paper positioned too narrowly or too broadly?

Paradoxically, both.

- **Too narrowly** in the empirical object: one ethnicity, one industry, one policy margin.
- **Too broadly** in the claim: “sorting dividend” and “better employment” are much bigger than what is shown.

This mismatch is the central strategic problem.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of?

It under-engages with:
- contingent work / temp agencies / fissured workplace,
- job quality and labor-market segmentation,
- EITC incidence and equilibrium effects,
- papers on occupational or sectoral mobility among low-wage workers,
- perhaps the literature on immigrant/Hispanic sorting across sectors and local labor markets.

The “Hispanic labor market outcomes” literature is not the right second pillar unless the paper truly has something to say about Hispanic workers specifically. Right now Hispanic status looks more like a targeting proxy and exposure channel than the central economic object.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?

Not quite. The better conversation is:

**How do work subsidies affect the composition and quality of low-wage employment?**

That is more interesting than:

**How do state EITCs affect Hispanic employment in administrative support?**

The current framing puts the paper in a smaller room than it deserves—while also making claims too grand for the evidence in that room.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

The EITC literature says work subsidies increase employment and earnings among low-income workers. The policy is generally understood as acting on participation and perhaps hours.

### Tension

But low-wage work is heterogeneous. A worker can respond not only by entering work but by choosing among jobs and sectors with very different stability and quality. We know little about whether subsidies shift workers out of contingent, high-turnover employment into more stable jobs.

### Resolution

The paper finds that after state EITC adoption, Hispanic employment in administrative support services declines relative to non-Hispanic workers and higher-wage sectors, with the decline concentrated in hires and building gradually over time.

### Implications

If interpreted as worker reallocation, the EITC changes the composition of low-wage employment and may improve job quality beyond what aggregate employment counts reveal.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the skeleton of one, yes. But it is not fully earned. The paper currently has a better **hook** than **resolution**. The resolution overreaches relative to what is directly observed.

The draft wants the story to be:
- EITC raises after-tax return,
- workers leave precarious temp-sector jobs,
- they sort into better employment,
- this is a welfare gain.

But the evidence only gets you to:
- relative Hispanic employment declines in NAICS 56 after state EITC adoption.

That is one step short of the story being told. So the narrative arc is serviceable but not fully coherent; parts of the “resolution” and “implications” are asserted rather than demonstrated.

### If it is a collection of results looking for a story, what story should it be telling?

The cleanest story is:

> Work subsidies may alter the composition of low-wage employment. I provide evidence consistent with this reallocation channel by showing that state EITCs reduce relative employment in a canonical contingent-work sector.

That is narrower, more defensible, and actually stronger editorially because it does not promise what the data cannot show.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?

“I think state EITC supplements may move low-wage workers out of temp-agency-style employment, not just into work overall.”

That is the intriguing version. Not: “Hispanic employment in NAICS 56 falls by 8.5 log points.”

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

They would lean in for about 20 seconds—because the idea is genuinely interesting. Then they would ask the obvious question:

**“Do you actually know they moved into better jobs?”**

And right now the answer is no, not directly.

That is the paper’s dinner-party problem. The first sentence is good. The second sentence triggers the fatal third question.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

- Where did the workers go?
- Is this really worker sorting, or something about firms in this sector?
- Why Hispanic workers specifically?
- Why should I think administrative support is the right proxy for precarious work?
- Is this about low wages, immigration, outsourcing, or tax credits?

If the author cannot answer the first two convincingly in the paper itself, the paper stays in the “interesting but incomplete” category.

### If the findings are modest, is the result still interesting?

Yes, potentially. A well-established policy having an overlooked margin of impact is inherently interesting. Even a modest sectoral response could matter conceptually if it changes our understanding of the EITC. But the paper has to make the case that learning “the EITC does not raise all forms of low-wage employment equally” matters for theory and welfare. At present it half-makes that case.

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

### What would make it read better?

1. **Shorten the econometric exposition in the introduction.**  
   The introduction currently gets too quickly into fixed effects and estimator names. For AER-level positioning, the intro should spend more time on the question and the economic significance, less on the plumbing.

2. **Move some method detail out of the introduction.**  
   The entire paragraph beginning “I test this hypothesis using a triple-difference design...” is too long and too mechanical for its current placement. Keep one sentence; save the rest.

3. **Bring the key limitation forward.**  
   The paper’s main interpretive limitation—that it does not observe destinations and therefore cannot directly establish “better jobs”—should appear earlier, maybe at the end of the introduction. Better to seem disciplined than overconfident.

4. **Reorganize around the claim hierarchy.**  
   Main text should distinguish:
   - what is shown directly,
   - what is inferred,
   - what is conjectured.  
   Right now these bleed together.

5. **Trim rhetorical overstatement.**  
   Phrases like “reveals a sorting dividend” and “improve the quality of Hispanic employment” are too strong for the evidence. Replace with “consistent with” until the paper can directly verify destinations/job quality.

6. **Reconsider the title.**  
   “The Sorting Dividend” is catchy, but it presumes the conclusion. A title more like  
   **“Do Work Subsidies Reallocate Workers Across Sectors? Evidence from State EITCs and Administrative Support Employment”**  
   would be less flashy but more credible.

7. **The discussion section should do more than restate.**  
   It currently repeats the main finding and then lists limitations. It should instead situate the result in broader economic terms: implications for incidence, job ladders, contingent work, and how tax policy interacts with labor-market segmentation.

8. **Appendix some lower-value material.**  
   The standardized effect size appendix does not help the strategic case. It feels generated rather than economically motivated. I would rather see an appendix table mapping sectors by wage/turnover and explaining why the chosen controls are meaningful.

### Is the paper front-loaded with the good stuff?

Reasonably. Better than many submissions. But it front-loads too many coefficients before fully earning why the reader should care about NAICS 56.

### Are there results buried in robustness that should be in the main results?

Potentially yes: any evidence on broader sectoral patterns or destination sectors—if it exists—belongs in the main text, not robustness. The current “alternative controls” and placebo are standard and fine, but they do not enlarge the paper.

### Is the conclusion adding value?

Only modestly. It mostly summarizes. It should either broaden the stakes or stop sooner.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

Be honest: the gap is substantial.

This is not mainly a standard “framing problem,” though framing is part of it. The bigger issues are **scope** and **ambition**.

### What is the gap?

#### 1. Scope problem
The paper is built around one sector and one group. That is too narrow for the claims being made. AER would want this to speak more broadly about labor-market composition, job quality, or the equilibrium effects of work subsidies.

#### 2. Ambition problem
The paper finds a relative decline in one sector and leaps to “sorting dividend.” That is an ambitious interpretation grafted onto a relatively modest empirical object. The fix is not to talk bigger; it is to show more.

#### 3. Novelty problem
The idea is novel enough to be interesting, but the evidence package is not yet rich enough to make it feel definitive. Right now it risks reading as a competent applied paper that found an interesting corner result.

#### 4. Framing problem
The current emphasis on Hispanic workers as a contribution category weakens the paper unless there is a sharper reason the result is specifically about Hispanic workers. Otherwise it looks like sample selection disguised as substantive framing.

### Single most impactful piece of advice

**If the author can only change one thing: show where the workers go—or, failing that, reframe the paper much more modestly as evidence consistent with a reallocation margin rather than proof of a “sorting dividend.”**

That is the whole ballgame. If the paper can document that the employment decline in NAICS 56 is offset by gains in observably more stable or higher-quality sectors, the paper becomes much more interesting and much more credible. If it cannot, it must stop claiming welfare-improving sorting and instead sell a narrower but still useful point: EITCs alter the sectoral composition of low-wage employment in ways aggregate employment measures miss.
