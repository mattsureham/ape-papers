## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
The paper examines how a common but "hidden" feature of subsidy design—applying a single average rate to total capacity rather than marginal rates to graduated bands—creates massive distortions in green technology adoption. Using the UK solar market, it shows that these "tariff cliffs" led to the most extreme bunching ever recorded in the literature (a ratio of 2,230:1), effectively capping renewable capacity for thousands of households.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this very clearly. The first two paragraphs of the current draft are excellent; they define the "hidden notch" and provide a vivid, dollar-denominated example of the incentive mismatch. It doesn't need a rewrite, but it could lean harder into the "hidden" nature—that the policy looks like a standard graduated schedule but functions like a cliff.

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**The Contribution:** This paper identifies and documents the "average-rate notch," a design flaw in regulatory schedules that produces behavioral distortions orders of magnitude larger than standard tax kinks or explicit notches.

**Evaluation:**
*   **Differentiation:** It differentiates itself well from the "explicit notch" literature (like Kleven/Waseem) by showing that the UK solar case is a "hidden" notch. It also distinguishes itself from Klimsa et al. (2024) by showing the UK distortion is significantly more extreme due to the average-rate structure.
*   **Question vs. Literature:** It is currently a mix. It frames the question as "Do these bands create notches?" but spends much time on "this extends the bunching lens." To be AER-ready, it should frame itself as a warning about **optimal policy design in the energy transition.**
*   **Clarity:** A smart economist would immediately understand the "cliff" vs. "marginal" distinction.
*   **What would make it bigger?** A welfare analysis or a counterfactual simulation of "lost carbon abatement." If the author can translate 19MW of foregone solar into a social cost of carbon (SCC) figure, the contribution moves from "cool bunching fact" to "first-order policy failure."

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
The paper sits at the intersection of **Public Economics (Bunching)** and **Environmental Economics.**

*   **Closest Neighbors:** Kleven & Waseem (2013) on notches; Klimsa et al. (2024) on German solar; Best & Kleven (2018) on UK stamps.
*   **Positioning:** It builds on these by offering a "cleaner" identification (using the 2016 merger) and a more extreme coefficient. It should "attack" (gently) the standard assumption that regulatory bands are usually kinks.
*   **Conversation:** It is currently speaking to the bunching specialists. It *should* be speaking to the **designers of green subsidies.** The most impactful framing is: "We are spending billions on subsidies, but bad math in the schedule is causing us to leave 12% of potential capacity on the table."

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC
*   **Setup:** Governments use feed-in tariffs to spur solar adoption, using bands to account for scale economies.
*   **Tension:** If you apply the rate to the *whole* system, you create a "hidden" cliff where building a slightly larger system actually reduces your total revenue.
*   **Resolution:** The UK solar market bunching was astronomical (2,230:1). When the cliff was removed in 2016, the "missing tail" of larger systems immediately returned.
*   **Implications:** Policy designers must use marginal rates (like income tax) or risk massive sizing inefficiencies.

**Evaluation:** The arc is strong. It is not just a collection of results; it’s a forensic investigation of a policy error.

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
*   **The Lead Fact:** "In the UK, only 0.6% of people built a solar system slightly over 4kW, because doing so would cost them £2,800 in lost subsidies for the sake of 0.02kW of extra power."
*   **Reaction:** People will lean in. The "2,230:1" ratio is a "pre-registration-level" eye-popper.
*   **Follow-up:** "How many other subsidies are designed this poorly?" (This is the question the paper should answer in the discussion).

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
*   **Front-loading:** The paper is well-structured.
*   **Appendix:** The 10kW analysis is a good control, but it feels a bit "secondary." I would move the technical robustness of the Kleven-Waseem estimator at 10kW to the appendix to keep the focus on the dramatic 4kW collapse.
*   **Mechanism:** Section 6.1 (Inverter limiting) is fascinating. It shows that installers aren't just removing panels; they are "gaming" the electronic capacity. This should be highlighted more as evidence of "professional gaming."

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The paper is currently a "very high-quality technical note." To make it an AER paper, it needs **Ambition and Scope.**

**The Gap:** It feels a bit narrow—just one threshold in one country.
**The Advice:** Connect this "average-rate notch" to other domains. Are there similar cliffs in EV subsidies? In carbon credits? In small business tax breaks? If the author can show this isn't just a "UK Solar Quirk" but a recurring "Tax Design Pathology," it becomes a general-interest paper.

**Single most impactful change:** Add a section (even if speculative) on the **Aggregated Carbon Loss.** Quantify the cost of this "bad design" in terms of missed climate targets.
