## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
This paper examines the housing market consequences of the first federal regulation of "forever chemicals" (PFAS) in drinking water. It asks whether a national mandate for cleaner water increases home values via remediation or decreases them via the "stigma" of disclosed contamination. The findings suggest a wash: the negative informational shock roughly offsets the positive cleanup signal, except in states where the information was already known, where a "remediation premium" emerges.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this well, but it buries the "why should I care" under technical specs. It reads like an EPA evaluation rather than a top-tier econ paper.
*   **The Pitch it should have:** "Economists have long debated whether environmental regulations act as a tax on local assets or a value-adding investment in public goods. By exploiting the first federal standard for PFAS, this paper identifies a 'marginal information' effect: regulation only moves markets when it reveals new secrets. In transparent markets, regulation is a pure asset-value booster; in opaque ones, the fear of the newly revealed poison cancels out the value of the cure."

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
**Contribution:** The paper identifies that the capitalization of environmental regulation is conditional on the pre-existing information set of the market.

*   **Differentiation:** It differentiates itself from Greenstone & Gallagher (2008) by focusing on a *diffuse* and *active* pollutant (drinking water) rather than localized legacy sites (Superfund), and from the "stigma" literature (Pope 2008) by showing the interaction between disclosure and remediation.
*   **Framing:** It is currently framed as "answering a gap in the PFAS literature." To hit the AER, it must be framed as "a fundamental test of the Informational Efficiency of Hedonic Markets."
*   **The "So What":** A smart economist would say, "It’s a nice DiD that shows why we sometimes get null results in environmental headers—the 'bad news' and 'good regulation' effects cancel out."
*   **Bigger Contribution:** To make this "AER big," the author needs to explore the **sorting mechanism**. Who moves out when the news hits? Does the "stigma" effect drive out high-income households, while the "remediation" keeps the property value flat?

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
The paper sits at the intersection of Environmental Economics and Urban Economics.

*   **Closest Neighbors:** Greenstone and Gallagher (2008, QJE); Chay and Greenstone (2005, JPE); Banzhaf and Walsh (2008, AER); and Linden and Rockoff (2008, AER).
*   **Strategy:** The paper should **synthesize** these. It shouldn't just say "PFAS is new"; it should say "previous studies found conflicting results because they couldn't observe the counterfactual of *information without remediation* or vice versa. I can."
*   **Missing Conversations:** The paper is silent on the **Political Economy** of regulation. Why did 7 states regulate early? Is the "cleanup premium" in those states actually a "well-governed state" premium? It needs to speak to the literature on state vs. federal regulatory overlap.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC
*   **Setup:** PFAS is everywhere, and people are scared.
*   **Tension:** The EPA finally steps in. Theory says prices could go up (cleaner water) or down (revealed contamination).
*   **Resolution:** Locally, they do neither. But when you split the sample by prior knowledge, the gears of the market become visible.
*   **Implications:** Federal "one-size-fits-all" environmental mandates have wildly different wealth effects depending on state-level transparency.

**Evaluation:** The arc is "Serviceable." It feels a bit like a technical report. It needs to lean harder into the "Tension"—the paradox of the null result in the baseline DiD.

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
*   **The Fact:** "When the EPA told people their water was toxic, their house prices didn't budge—but only because they were already expecting a cleanup."
*   **Response:** People would lean in, then ask: "Wait, so if I live in a state that didn't disclose, is my house a ticking time bomb?"
*   **The Null:** The null here is the most interesting part, but only because of the triple-difference. A pure null would be a "desk reject." The "offsetting forces" story is what saves it.

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
*   **Front-load:** The "Prior State Standards" section (page 4) is the soul of the paper. It should be moved up or integrated into the intro.
*   **Appendix:** The Rambachan-Roth sensitivity is good for the "referee" version but should be a single paragraph in the text.
*   **Expansion:** The paper is currently quite short (11 pages of text). To be an AER paper, it needs a section on **heterogeneity by income or demographics**. Do poor ZIP codes get the "stigma" but not the "cleanup premium"?

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The gap is **Ambition**. It currently looks like a very high-quality "Journal of Environmental Economics and Management" (JEEM) paper. 

**The single most impactful piece of advice:** Move beyond the "average effect" and show me the **distributional consequences and household sorting**. If the "Forever Chemical Discount" is zero on average, but -$20k for families with children and +$20k for speculators betting on the cleanup, you have a landmark paper on how regulation reshapes American cities.
