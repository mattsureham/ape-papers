### 2. Summary
This paper estimates the housing price capitalization of the EPA's April 2024 PFAS drinking water MCL using a DiD design comparing ZIPs served by above- vs. below-MCL water systems, finding a statistically insignificant null effect (0.077 log points). A novel DDD exploiting pre-existing state PFAS standards reveals that any response operates through the regulation's marginal information content: positive in prior-MCL states (0.290 log points, p=0.049) but offset by a negative differential (-0.319 log points, p=0.054) in other states. The analysis credibly identifies a short-run informational channel while bounding potential parallel trends violations, filling a gap in the environmental capitalization literature.

### 3. Essential Points
**1. Insufficient post-treatment variation undermines causal identification.** The analysis relies on a single post-period (2024), capturing only an announcement-year effect amid noisy annual FHFA indices. Housing markets often respond with lags to environmental news (e.g., 2–3 years in Superfund studies like Greenstone & Gallagher 2008), and remediation is years away (2029–2031). Without 2025+ data (now feasible via updated FHFA/UCMR5), claims about "short-run capitalization" or the DDD's informational channel are premature; extend the panel or reject outright if unresolvable.

**2. Parallel trends assumption is questionable and inadequately addressed.** The event study shows persistently negative pre-trends for treated ZIPs (-0.06 to -0.19 log points, approaching significance in 2019), suggesting selection on slower-growing (often exurban/industrial) areas rather than regulation shocks. State×year FE attenuate but do not eliminate this (DiD shrinks to 0.021, p=0.70), and Rambachan-Roth bounds generously include zero only under lax M>0 assumptions. Provide joint pre-trend tests (e.g., Sun & Abraham 2021) or synthetic controls; current evidence weakens DDD credibility.

**3. Treatment definition ignores multi-system service and detection limits.** ZIP treatment is assigned if *any* serving system exceeds MCL, but 20%+ of ZIPs have multiple PWSIDs (per manifest crosswalk); this contaminates controls if partial exposure matters and overstates treatment intensity. Non-detects coded as zero ignore left-censoring (typical LOQ ~2 ppt), biasing controls downward. Disclose multi-system prevalence, compute exposure-weighted treatment (e.g., population- or flow-weighted max PFAS), and sensitivity to LOQ imputation (e.g., LOQ/√2).

### 4. Suggestions
The paper is well-structured for AER:Insights, with crisp writing, timely novelty, and strong institutional detail. The DDD is a clever quasi-placebo that credibly isolates information effects, complementing Marcus & Mueller (2024), and sensitivity bounds add rigor. To elevate to "accept with minor revisions," prioritize feasibility and polish below.

**Data and Sample Enhancements.** Update to 2025 FHFA data (available quarterly) for 1–2 post-periods, enabling dynamic event studies with leads/lags. Supplement the balanced panel with unbalanced data (~19,000 ZIPs) via inverse probability weighting to reduce selection (e.g., ZIPs with sparse pre-2014 HPI). Validate ZIP-PWS linkages empirically: tabulate % ZIPs with multiple systems by treatment status and test if results hold restricting to single-system ZIPs. For PFAS, report system-level summaries (e.g., % exceeding via PFOA only vs. PFOS) and plot dose-response kernel densities pre/post. Link ACS/ZTRAX (manifest secondary outcomes) for covariate balance (e.g., income, demographics by treatment×state-MCL) and heterogeneity (e.g., renter vs. owner shares).

**Empirical Refinements.** Replace pooled Post with rolling release dates (e.g., state-quarter dummies for UCMR5 drops) to exploit "temporal information variation" from manifest. In DDD, clarify interpretation: estimate fully saturated (\(\beta_1\) prior-MCL treated, \(\beta_2\) no-prior treated, etc.) with state-specific trends. Add within-state matched controls: propensity-score match above/below-MCL ZIPs on pre-2014 HPI trends, baseline PFAS (UCMR3/4 if available), and geography (e.g., distance to military sites). For dose-response, use MCL-exceedance hazard (e.g., max PFAS/4) or MCL gap (max-4 ppt) to better proxy remediation costs. Report power calculations: with SD(log HPI)=0.85, N=5k ZIPs, state clusters=56, minimum detectable effect is ~0.15 log points (30% of SD)—contextualize null accordingly.

**Robustness Expansion.** Beyond current checks, include: (i) exclude COVID years (2020–2022) as pre-trends may confound; (ii) border-pair DiD (above/below-MCL ZIP pairs <10km apart); (iii) alternative indices (Zillow ZHVI for broader transactions); (iv) falsification on non-PFAS outcomes (e.g., ACS employment). For clustering, justify state-level (treatment correlates intra-state) but report wild-bootstrap p-values (Roodman et al. 2019) given 56 clusters. Figures are absent—add: (a) map treated ZIPs by state-MCL; (b) event-study plot with 95% CIs and trend fit; (c) binned scatter of Δlog HPI vs. log PFAS.

**Broader Contributions and Framing.** Quantify economic stakes: e.g., 0.077 log point on $300k median home = $230/home; scale to $X billion nationally vs. EPA's $12B benefits. Discuss mechanisms via appendices: media coverage (GDELT hits on "PFAS [state] water"), Google Trends, or lawsuits pre/post-MCL. Heterogeneity by ZIP traits (e.g., % public water served, poverty) for EJ angle. Tone down "null is informative" without stronger bounds—frame as "no economically large effect." References are solid; add Bayer et al. (2024 JUE) on multi-channel capitalization.
