## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a simple and important question: when a commodity boom raises rural household income in a poor, smallholder economy, does it pull children out of school and into work, or does it finance more human capital? Using Ghana’s cocoa boom in the 2000s, the paper argues that higher cocoa incomes reduced adult labor supply and likely improved children’s literacy, suggesting that when commodity rents reach poor farming households, booms can support human capital rather than undermine it.

Why should a busy economist care? Because this is really a paper about the sign of income versus substitution effects in development, and about whether resource booms are necessarily bad for long-run development. Ghanaian cocoa is a useful case because the rents accrued to many smallholders rather than a government or enclave sector.

### Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Not quite. The current introduction is competent, but it undersells the paper by making it sound like a fairly standard “commodity shock and schooling” application. The first paragraphs move too quickly to theory and child labor, before clearly establishing the broader economic question: **what happens when a large, tradable income shock hits poor smallholder households?**

The introduction should lead less with “there is theoretical ambiguity” and more with the substantive world question and the surprising empirical fact. Right now, the paper’s strongest finding is actually adult labor exit, with education effects more suggestive. But the intro treats schooling as the main event and labor reallocation as secondary, even though the results say the reverse.

### The pitch the paper should have

A stronger opening would say something like:

> Commodity booms are usually studied as sources of distortion: they raise opportunity costs of schooling, pull labor into the booming sector, and may leave countries worse off. But that prediction is less obvious when the boom accrues to poor smallholder households rather than firms or governments. In that case, higher commodity prices may relax the very credit constraints that keep children in work and adults trapped in low-productivity agriculture.
>
> We study this question using Ghana’s cocoa boom of the 2000s. Combining three population censuses with regional cocoa exposure, we show that high-exposure regions experienced a substantial decline in adult employment and suggestive gains in children’s literacy during the boom. The broader message is that the developmental consequences of commodity windfalls depend critically on who receives the rents and on the production structure of the commodity itself.

That is a cleaner AER-facing pitch: not “another paper on child labor,” but “the incidence of commodity rents changes the development consequences of booms.”

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### One-sentence contribution

The paper’s contribution is to show that in a poor smallholder setting, a commodity price boom that passes through to households can reduce labor supply and plausibly increase human capital, implying that commodity windfalls need not operate through the usual substitution-effect or resource-curse channels.

### Is this clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?

Only partially. The paper does identify the main comparison set—Kruger on coffee, Edmonds on rice, Beegle et al. on shocks and child labor, Black et al. on coal—but the differentiation is still too “case-study plus sign flip.” The paper says, in effect: “They found substitution effects elsewhere; we find income effects here.” That is true, but not yet enough.

To feel like more than another treatment-effect paper, the paper needs to sharpen exactly why Ghana cocoa is theoretically different:
- **smallholder incidence of the shock**
- **state-mediated but substantial pass-through**
- **tree-crop technology limiting short-run labor demand expansion**
- **high baseline credit constraints / school cost barriers**

Those are the ingredients of a more general contribution. The paper gestures at them, but does not crystallize them into a portable conceptual claim.

### Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the world, or filling a gap in a literature?

It is mostly framed as a world question, which is good. But it occasionally slips into literature-gap prose: “This paper contributes to the literature on commodity shocks and human capital...” That is much weaker than the world-facing version. The stronger claim is not “the literature has not studied Ghana cocoa,” but “the effects of booms depend on who gets the rents and whether the production technology raises labor demand.”

### Could a smart economist explain to a colleague what’s new after reading the introduction?

Right now they could say something like: “It’s a DiD/Bartik paper showing Ghana’s cocoa boom maybe improved kids’ literacy and reduced adult work.” That is not enough. The risk is exactly what you flag: it can sound like “another DiD paper about commodity prices and schooling.”

What they should be able to say is: “It shows that a commodity boom can reduce labor supply and improve human capital when it hits poor smallholders directly—so the incidence and production structure of the boom determine whether income or substitution effects dominate.” That is much stronger.

### What would make this contribution bigger?

Several possibilities:

1. **Make adult labor reallocation the centerpiece, not an auxiliary result.**  
   The most credible and surprising fact in the paper is not education—it is that adult employment falls in exposed regions. That is a bigger statement about household labor supply, nonfarm transition, and structural change.

2. **Show where labor goes, not just that employment falls.**  
   If the paper could distinguish between reduced labor force participation, more schooling, migration, movement into nonfarm self-employment, or household production, the contribution would become much larger. Right now “employment fell” is provocative but incomplete.

3. **Tie the education result to a more durable outcome.**  
   Literacy is fine, but for top-journal positioning, completed schooling, secondary transition, or young-adult attainment would be more compelling. The paper has a young-adult attainment section, but it feels underdeveloped and underconfident. If that is the long-run margin, it should be elevated.

4. **Generalize the claim beyond Ghana.**  
   The introduction should state an explicit hypothesis about when booms help versus hurt human capital: e.g., booms are more likely to improve human capital when (i) rents go to constrained households, (ii) child labor is not highly productive at the margin, and (iii) schooling costs bind. That would make the paper useful beyond cocoa.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

### Closest neighbors

The nearest papers seem to be:

- **Kruger (2007)** on coffee booms and school attendance in Brazil
- **Edmonds and Pavcnik (2005)** on rice prices and child labor/schooling in Vietnam
- **Beegle, Dehejia, and Gatti (2006)** on transitory income shocks and child labor in Tanzania
- **Cogneau and Jedwab (2012)** on cocoa price collapse and schooling in Côte d’Ivoire
- **Black, McKinnish, and Sanders (2005)** on coal booms and educational attainment

A second ring of neighbors includes:
- **Atkin (2016)** on manufacturing jobs and schooling
- **Charles, Hurst, and Notowidigdo (2018)** on housing booms and college-going
- papers on **resource booms / local labor markets / structural transformation**, e.g. **Allcott and Keniston (2018)**, **Aragón and Rud / Caselli-type local windfall papers**, **McMillan and Rodrik / McMillan et al.** on structural change.

### How should the paper position itself relative to those neighbors?

Mostly **build on and reinterpret**, not attack. The paper should say: prior work has established that booms often raise the opportunity cost of schooling; our setting shows when the opposite happens. That is not a refutation of Kruger or Black; it is a boundary-condition paper. The right move is to propose a framework for sorting settings:
- wage-labor/enclave booms → substitution effects likely stronger
- smallholder household booms with credit constraints → income effects likely stronger

That is a synthesis contribution, and it is more interesting than “Ghana differs from Brazil.”

### Is the paper positioned too narrowly or too broadly?

Currently, oddly, both.

- **Too narrowly** in the details: lots of Ghana-specific institutional detail, a long methods discussion, and a somewhat case-study tone.
- **Too broadly** in the claims: occasional gestures to the resource curse debate feel overextended relative to the evidence.

The paper’s natural audience is the intersection of development, labor, and political/resource economics. It should not try to become a grand resource-curse paper unless it can say more about long-run outcomes or sectoral reallocation. Right now the most credible broad framing is: **household incidence of commodity shocks and human capital/labor supply responses**.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of, or under-engaged with?

A few areas need stronger connection:

1. **Agricultural household models / labor supply under income shocks.**  
   The paper hints at this, but it would benefit from speaking more directly to household labor allocation in farm economies, not just child labor.

2. **Structural transformation and rural nonfarm transition.**  
   Since the strongest result is adult employment exit, this literature should be central, not decorative.

3. **Resource incidence / distribution of rents.**  
   The paper repeatedly says “who gets the rents matters,” but does not anchor that proposition in a clear literature on pass-through, local windfalls, or household incidence.

4. **Cash transfers / schooling income effects.**  
   If the point is that relaxing household constraints raises human capital, the paper should speak more explicitly to transfer-like literatures. That gives the result broader meaning.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?

Not fully. It thinks it is in the “commodity shocks and child labor” conversation. That is part of it, but the more consequential conversation is: **what happens when poor households receive a large positive tradable-income shock?** That conversation connects development, labor supply, human capital, and structural change. That is the more interesting room in the house.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Before this paper, the standard instinct is that commodity booms can be bad for human capital because they raise the return to work and pull children or young adults out of school. More broadly, resource booms are associated with labor reallocation toward the boom sector and worries about Dutch disease or the resource curse.

### Tension

But that logic may break down in low-income smallholder settings where the boom is an income windfall to constrained households rather than a labor-demand shock generated by firms. Then the same price increase could ease school-cost constraints and reduce the need for household labor. We do not know ex ante which force dominates in Ghana cocoa.

### Resolution

The paper finds a robust decline in adult employment in more exposed regions and suggestive evidence of improved child literacy and educational attainment. The implied story is that the cocoa boom relaxed constraints and financed labor withdrawal rather than labor intensification.

### Implications

The consequences of commodity booms depend on incidence and production structure: when rents reach poor smallholders, booms may support human capital and household reallocation rather than harm development. This matters for how economists think about resource booms, rural household behavior, and commodity-sector policy.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the ingredients, but the arc is not fully disciplined. At present it reads somewhat like:
- setup: cocoa prices rose
- theory: income vs substitution
- results: some schooling effects, employment effects, heterogeneity, robustness, resource curse discussion

That is serviceable, but not elegant. The paper currently feels a bit like a collection of estimates looking for the highest-status framing. The schooling story and the adult employment story are not fully integrated.

### What story should it be telling?

The story should be:

> A commodity boom hit poor smallholder households. In such settings, the first-order margin may not be labor demand but household income. In Ghana, the windfall appears to have let adults work less and children acquire more human capital. The broader lesson is that the development effects of booms depend on whether they act like labor-demand shocks or household-income shocks.

That unifies the paper. Adult labor exit is the clearest revealed-preference sign that this was an income effect. Education then becomes a complementary implication, not a separate paper stapled on.

## 5. THE “SO WHAT?” TEST

### What fact would I lead with?

I would lead with: **in Ghana’s cocoa regions, the 2000s cocoa boom reduced adult employment rather than increasing it.** That is the surprising fact. Then I would add: **and children’s literacy appears to have improved rather than worsened.**

If you lead with “literacy rises by 5.5 percentage points under conventional inference but not under randomization inference,” the room cools instantly. That is not dinner-party material. If you lead with “a farm commodity boom made adults work less,” people lean in.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

They would lean in if the paper presents itself as overturning the presumption that commodity booms necessarily increase labor supply and hurt schooling. They would reach for their phones if it is pitched as a cautious Ghana case study with one significant education coefficient and a lot of inferential caveats.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Probably: **Where did the labor go?**  
Second: **Is the education effect real and durable?**  
Third: **What features of cocoa/Ghana make this happen, and would it generalize?**

Those are exactly the questions the paper should anticipate and frame around.

### If findings are modest or null, is the null interesting?

For education, the results are modest and somewhat fragile. The paper is admirably candid on that. But it has not fully adjusted its rhetoric to that reality. It still wants the schooling result to carry the paper, even though the better editorial strategy is to say:

- the strong finding is labor exit;
- the education evidence points in the same direction but is more suggestive;
- together they support an income-effect interpretation.

That is honest and stronger than overselling literacy.

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS

### What would make it read better?

1. **Front-load the main fact earlier.**  
   The intro should reveal immediately that the strongest finding is adult employment decline. Right now that comes only after several paragraphs and competes with too many technical details.

2. **Move much of the methods and inference prose out of the introduction.**  
   The opening pages spend too much energy on Bartik, doubly robust DiD, permutation inference, cluster counts, etc. That is not what gets a paper into AER. The intro should sell the question, the setting, the main facts, and the general lesson.

3. **Shorten the related literature section.**  
   It is too long and reads like a seminar-prep document. For a top-field journal, the literature review should be more selective and argumentative: who found substitution effects, who found income effects, and why this setting adjudicates between them.

4. **Trim the conceptual framework unless it generates sharper predictions.**  
   The current framework is generic. It does not really buy much beyond the obvious income/substitution decomposition. Either make it more distinctive—especially around tree crops, household incidence, and labor demand—or compress it sharply.

5. **Promote the young-adult attainment material if it is meaningful.**  
   If there is any credible long-run attainment result, even imprecise, it belongs more centrally because it speaks directly to lasting human capital. If it is too weak, then cut or downplay it rather than leaving it as an afterthought.

6. **Demote some robustness detail.**  
   The paper is over-eager to narrate every inferential nuance in the main text. Some of that belongs in footnotes, tables, or appendix discussion. The private rule: every paragraph in the main text should either advance the story or directly answer the obvious skeptical question.

7. **Rewrite the conclusion to do more than summarize.**  
   The current conclusion is decent, but it still reads like a recap. It should end with the more general lesson: economists should think of commodity booms not just by sector, but by the distribution of rents and the production technology.

### Is the paper front-loaded with the good stuff?

Not enough. The good stuff is there, but buried under method and exposition. The reader should know by page 2:
- what the big question is,
- why Ghana is a crucial test case,
- what the surprising fact is,
- what broader lesson follows.

### Are results buried in robustness that should be in the main results?

Yes: the fact that the education effect is fragile under randomization inference is not “robustness”; it is central to how the paper should position its contribution. Likewise, if adult employment is the robust headline, the exact-inference result for employment should be elevated as a main result, not treated as methodological housekeeping.

### Is the conclusion adding value?

Some, but not enough. It summarizes findings and offers policy remarks, but the highest-value concluding move would be to articulate a more general taxonomy of booms:
- booms that raise labor demand,
- booms that raise household income,
- booms where rents accrue to the state,
- booms where rents accrue to households.

That would leave the reader with a framework, not just a Ghana result.

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?

At the moment, the gap is mostly a combination of **framing problem** and **ambition problem**, with a bit of **scope problem**.

- **Framing problem:** The paper undersells its broadest idea and overemphasizes estimation architecture.

If I ask what would excite the top 10 people in this field, it is not “another country case where prices affected schooling.” It is:

> a paper showing that the consequences of commodity booms hinge on whether the boom is fundamentally a labor-demand shock or a household-income shock, and that smallholder commodity sectors can generate the latter.

That is potentially AER-worthy. But the paper must commit to that bigger idea.

### Single most impactful advice

**Recenter the paper around the robust finding that the cocoa boom functioned as a household income shock that reduced adult labor supply, and use the education results as supporting evidence for a broader claim about how the incidence of commodity rents shapes development outcomes.**

That one change would improve the title, introduction, literature positioning, narrative arc, and takeaway simultaneously.
