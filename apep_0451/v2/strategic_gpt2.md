## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks a classic development question in a high-stakes setting: when a commodity boom raises farm incomes, does it pull children out of school and into work, or does it relax poverty constraints and increase human capital investment? Using Ghana’s cocoa boom in the 2000s, the paper argues that higher cocoa incomes reduced adult labor supply and likely improved children’s literacy, suggesting that when commodity rents accrue to smallholder households rather than states or firms, booms can foster human capital accumulation rather than undermine it.

A busy economist should care because this is not really a paper about cocoa. It is a paper about what commodity windfalls do to poor households, whether resource booms help or hurt long-run development, and when income effects dominate substitution effects in schooling decisions.

### Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?

Partly, but not well enough. The current opening is competent and serious, but it is still written like a cautious applied paper rather than a top-journal paper with a sharp claim. It opens with a price fact, then moves immediately to theory and child labor. That is fine, but the introduction buries the bigger conceptual payoff: this is potentially a paper about **who receives commodity rents** and therefore whether booms generate labor intensification or human capital investment.

Right now the first two paragraphs pitch:
- cocoa prices rose,
- theory is ambiguous,
- Ghana has child labor,
- we study the effect.

That is narrower and more incremental than the paper should sound.

### The pitch the paper should have

A stronger opening would say something like:

> Commodity booms are usually feared for what they do to development: they raise the return to work today and can pull labor away from school, manufacturing, and other long-run investments. But that logic depends on who receives the windfall. When commodity rents flow directly to poor smallholder households, a boom may relax the very income constraints that keep children out of school.
>
> We study this question in Ghana’s cocoa boom of the 2000s, when world cocoa prices tripled in a country where hundreds of thousands of smallholder households depend on the crop. Using population census microdata, we show that cocoa-exposed regions saw a robust decline in adult employment and suggestive gains in children’s literacy. The central message is that commodity booms can finance human capital accumulation when the beneficiaries are poor households rather than governments or large firms.

That framing is better because it elevates the paper from “another education-and-child-labor paper” to “a paper about the developmental consequences of commodity rents under different ownership structures.”

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

### Contribution in one sentence

The paper’s contribution is to show that Ghana’s cocoa boom appears to have reduced adult labor supply and likely improved children’s human capital, implying that commodity booms can be development-enhancing when income gains accrue to smallholder households.

Not enough. The introduction cites a long list of adjacent papers, but the differentiation is still muddy. The paper wants to distinguish itself from:
- commodity-boom papers showing substitution effects dominate,
- negative-income-shock papers showing schooling falls,
- resource-curse papers focused on macro outcomes.

But it does not yet crisply state what is new relative to each.

What the paper should say more explicitly is:

1. **Relative to Kruger/Black/Atkin/Charles:** those papers emphasize rises in the opportunity cost of schooling; this paper asks when the opposite happens because the windfall relaxes household constraints.
2. **Relative to Edmonds/Beegle/Cogneau:** those papers show income matters for schooling; this paper adds the distinctive case of a tradable commodity boom with direct pass-through to smallholders.
3. **Relative to the resource-curse literature:** that literature studies state- or firm-centered resource rents; this paper studies household-centered resource rents and a human-capital channel.

That is a much cleaner three-part positioning.

It is mixed. The paper often lapses into literature-gap language (“we contribute to four literatures,” “we speak to…”). The stronger parts are about the world: when do commodity booms increase schooling rather than child labor? That world-question should dominate.

This should be framed as:
- **World question:** What do commodity windfalls do to poor households’ labor allocation and human capital investment?

- **Literature question:** There is little evidence on cocoa booms in Ghana.

The latter is publishable somewhere; the former is AER-relevant.

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING

### Closest neighbors

The most relevant neighbors appear to be:

- **Kruger (2007)** on coffee booms and schooling/child labor in Brazil.
- **Edmonds and Pavcnik (2005)** on rice prices and child labor/schooling in Vietnam.
- **Beegle, Dehejia, and Gatti (2006)** on income shocks and child labor in Tanzania.
- **Cogneau and Jedwab (2012)** on the cocoa shock in Côte d’Ivoire.
- Possibly also **Black, McKinnish, and Sanders (2005)** on coal booms and schooling, and **Atkin (2016)** on manufacturing and schooling.

A second ring of conversation:
- **Sachs and Warner / van der Ploeg** resource-curse literature.
- **McMillan and Rodrik / Gollin et al.** on structural transformation.

### How should the paper position itself relative to those neighbors?

**Build on and reframe, not attack.**

The paper should not present itself as disproving Kruger or the substitution-effect literature. Instead, it should say those findings are real in settings where booms raise labor demand or opportunity costs more than they relax constraints. Ghana is informative because its institutional and production structure flips that balance.

So the posture should be:
- Kruger/Black/Atkin identify one margin: booms can pull people into work.
- Edmonds/Beegle identify another: income growth can reduce child labor.
- This paper shows that in a smallholder commodity sector with direct rent pass-through, the second margin can dominate.

That is a synthesis with an organizing principle.

### Is the paper currently positioned too narrowly or too broadly?

Oddly, both.

- **Too narrowly** in the sense that much of the introduction reads like a Ghana cocoa paper for development/agriculture audiences.
- **Too broadly** in the sense that it claims to sit in four literatures, which diffuses the message rather than sharpening it.

The right audience is narrower but more ambitious: development economists interested in household responses to shocks, labor allocation, human capital, and the microfoundations of resource booms.

### What literature does the paper seem unaware of, or under-engaged with?

Two underdeveloped conversations:

1. **Household labor supply / non-labor-income effects.**  
   The adult employment result is central, but the paper treats it almost as a side effect. It should connect more explicitly to labor supply responses to income shocks, household enterprise behavior, and structural transformation at the micro level.

2. **Sectoral organization and incidence of commodity rents.**  
   The paper is gesturing toward “smallholder versus corporate/state rents,” but this is the real conceptual contribution and it is not developed enough. It should speak more to political economy and incidence, not just child labor.

### Is the paper having the right conversation?

Not quite. It is having the safe conversation: “commodity prices and schooling/child labor.” The more impactful conversation is:  
**“Why do commodity booms have such different development consequences across settings?”**

That opens the door to a broader economics audience.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup

Commodity booms are often thought to distort development by raising the return to work and discouraging schooling or diversification. Yet for poor rural households, the same boom may provide badly needed income that finances school and reduces reliance on family labor.

### Tension

The empirical literature points in both directions, and existing evidence does not make clear when commodity windfalls worsen human capital and when they improve it. Ghana’s cocoa sector is a useful test case because it combines a large boom, smallholder production, and meaningful transmission of world prices to farm households.

### Resolution

The paper finds a robust decline in adult employment in cocoa-exposed regions and suggestive improvements in children’s literacy and schooling-related outcomes. The interpretation is that the cocoa boom operated more as an income windfall than as a labor-demand shock.

### Implications

Commodity booms need not be “curses”: when rents reach poor households directly, they may relax constraints, reduce labor supply, and increase human capital investment. More broadly, the developmental consequences of commodity shocks depend on sector structure and rent incidence.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?

It has the ingredients, but not the discipline. The current paper often feels like:
- intro,
- methods defense,
- several outcome tables,
- then a discussion section trying to infer the story.

In other words, it is closer to **a collection of results looking for a story** than a fully controlled narrative.

The main problem is that the paper has **two competing stories**:

1. child labor / schooling,
2. structural transformation / labor exit.

The stronger story is clearly the second, with the first as an important extension. But the paper is written as if the schooling result is the main event and the employment result is a supporting fact. Strategically, that is the wrong choice.

### What story should it be telling?

The story should be:

- Commodity booms can either increase work effort or relax poverty.
- Which effect dominates depends on how production is organized and who receives rents.
- Ghana’s cocoa boom is a case where rents accrued to smallholders.
- In that setting, the first-order response was reduced adult labor supply and likely improved human capital.
- Therefore, the right general lesson is not “commodity booms are good” but “commodity booms are developmentally heterogeneous, and rent incidence explains why.”

That is a paper with a spine.

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

### What fact would I lead with?

I would lead with:  
**“When cocoa prices tripled in Ghana, adults in cocoa-growing regions worked less, not more.”**

That is the most surprising and conversation-starting fact. Then I would add:  
**“And children’s literacy seems to improve rather than deteriorate.”**

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?

If presented that way, they lean in.  
If presented as “we estimate the effect of a cocoa price shock on literacy using census microdata and a Bartik-style design,” they reach for their phones.

The surprise is the adult labor supply response. That is the hook.

### What follow-up question would they ask?

Probably one of these:
- Why did employment fall—income effect, measurement, migration, mechanization, or occupational shifting?
- Did people leave agriculture for better jobs, or just work less?
- Is this really about schooling, or mainly about reduced labor supply?
- What is special about cocoa relative to coffee, mining, or oil?

Those are exactly the questions the framing should anticipate.

### If the findings are null or modest, is the null interesting?

The education results are modest and somewhat fragile in the paper’s own telling. As currently framed, that makes the paper vulnerable: it sounds like a paper whose “main” human-capital finding is not fully secure.

That is why the paper should not market itself primarily as “cocoa booms increase literacy.” It should market itself as:
- a robust labor-supply paper,
- with suggestive evidence of improved human capital.

The null on enrollment is fine and even informative if the paper says baseline enrollment was already high and the margins of adjustment were quality/literacy and adult labor supply rather than school attendance. But that argument needs to be cleaner and less defensive.
