## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether firms strategically redesign ownership stakes to sit exactly at the UK’s 25% beneficial-ownership disclosure threshold, thereby preserving opacity while formally complying with transparency rules. Using Companies House PSC data, it documents a striking concentration of four-owner firms in the configuration where all four owners are reported in the 25–50% band, and argues that this pattern reveals a structural loophole in beneficial-ownership regulation with implications far beyond the UK because the same threshold is used internationally.

Why should a busy economist care? Because the paper is not really about one quirky registry fact; it is about whether disclosure rules with bright-line thresholds are inherently gameable, and whether a globally harmonized anti-money-laundering design feature creates predictable avoidance behavior.

**Does the paper itself articulate this pitch clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Mostly, but not quite. The opening is competent and readable, but it still sounds like a niche compliance paper until paragraph 2. The paper’s best asset is the vivid empirical fact; it should surface that fact immediately and make the broad-design point faster. Right now the introduction overinvests in institutional background before landing the punchline.

**What the first two paragraphs should say instead:**

> Beneficial ownership registries are meant to reveal who really controls companies. But when disclosure is triggered only above a bright-line ownership threshold, firms may be able to restructure around the rule rather than reveal themselves. This paper asks whether the UK’s 25% disclosure threshold—now the global standard in AML policy—induces precisely that kind of strategic ownership engineering.
>
> Using the universe-scale UK PSC register, I document a stark pattern: among firms with exactly four individual beneficial owners, an extraordinarily large share place all four in the lowest reportable ownership band, the configuration most consistent with splitting control into four near-25% stakes. The result suggests that the central design parameter of modern beneficial-ownership regulation may itself generate systematic evasion, turning a transparency rule into a focal point for avoidance.

That is the AER pitch: thresholds create behavior, and here the behavior undermines the policy’s core purpose.

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### What is the setup?
Governments increasingly rely on beneficial ownership registries to expose the humans behind companies, and the canonical design uses a 25% threshold to determine who must be disclosed.

### What is the tension?
A threshold intended to reveal control may instead invite firms to atomize formal ownership just enough to avoid meaningful transparency. The policy’s central design feature may undermine its purpose.

### What is the resolution?
The paper finds a highly unusual concentration of firms with exactly four individual PSCs all in the lowest reportable band, especially among foreign-linked firms, consistent with strategic ownership splitting around the threshold.

### What are the implications?
Disclosure thresholds may be intrinsically leaky; regulators may need lower thresholds, tiered thresholds, or different definitions of control to make beneficial ownership regimes effective.

### Does the paper have a clear narrative arc?
**Serviceable, but not fully convincing.** The ingredients are there, but the paper still feels somewhat like a collection of descriptive patterns orbiting a central anomaly. The “equal-split puzzle” is memorable, but it is not yet a full narrative by itself.

The current narrative is:
1. Here is a weird pattern.
2. Here is an independence benchmark.
3. Here are some correlates.
4. Here are some policy implications.

That is not quite enough for AER because the story remains narrower than the stakes the paper wants to claim.

### What story should it be telling instead?
The paper should tell this story:

- **Setup:** Modern transparency regimes use simple ownership thresholds because they are administrable.
- **Tension:** Those same thresholds create a manipulable map from real control to formal disclosure.
- **Resolution:** Firms appear to exploit that map by engineering ownership configurations exactly where the rule is easiest to game.
- **Implications:** The policy problem is not just noncompliance or weak enforcement; it is that the formal architecture of disclosure induces substitution into legally compliant opacity.

That is a real economics story. It is about incentives and institutional design, not just an anomaly in a register.

Those are not referee comments here; they are strategic comments. The key point is that the paper’s dinner-party fact is good, but the next question exposes the current limitation of the framing.
