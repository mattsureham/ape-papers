## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH

This paper asks whether the UK’s 25% beneficial-ownership disclosure rule creates a focal point for how firms formally divide equity stakes. Using Companies House PSC data, it documents that firms with exactly four listed individual PSCs are disproportionately likely to place all four in the 25–50% reporting band, suggesting that bright-line transparency rules can induce “minimum-compliance” ownership architectures.

Why should a busy economist care? Because this is a clean, memorable example of how regulatory thresholds shape organizational form, and because the same 25% cutoff sits at the center of AML and beneficial-ownership regimes well beyond the UK.

**Does the paper articulate this clearly in the first two paragraphs?**  
Almost, but not quite. The current introduction is punchy, but it overstates what the evidence shows. The paper more clearly shows **threshold-induced formal structuring** than a demonstrated “leak” in transparency. As written, it sometimes implies hidden ownership or evasion of disclosure, but the headline configuration it documents still consists of four *disclosed* PSCs. That is a strategic-design result, but not yet direct proof of opacity, money laundering, or concealment.

**The pitch the paper should have:**

> Beneficial-ownership regimes rely on bright-line thresholds, but we know little about whether those thresholds reshape how firms formally organize ownership. Using UK PSC data, this paper shows that the 25% disclosure cutoff generates a striking concentration of firms with exactly four listed individual PSCs, all placed in the lowest reportable ownership band. The result suggests that transparency rules do not merely reveal ownership; they also create a focal architecture for minimum-compliance firm design, with implications for the global use of 25% thresholds in AML policy.

That is a stronger and more defensible AER-style pitch than “I found a leak in the AML regime.”

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY

**One-sentence contribution:**  
The paper documents that a widely used beneficial-ownership disclosure threshold appears to induce a highly specific ownership configuration—four reported owners each just above the minimum reportable band—showing that transparency rules can distort formal ownership structure.

### Evaluation

**Is this contribution clearly differentiated from the closest 3–4 papers?**  
Only partially. The paper differentiates itself from classic bunching papers by saying this is not standard continuous-variable bunching, but a “configuration anomaly” in an organizational choice. That is potentially novel. But the introduction still reads too much like “apply bunching logic to a new setting.” It needs to say more sharply:

- existing bunching papers study earnings, taxes, or compliance on continuous margins;
- existing beneficial-ownership papers study concealment, shell companies, or enforcement gaps;
- **this paper is about how a disclosure threshold changes the combinatorics of ownership architecture itself.**

That is the real novelty.

**Is the contribution framed as answering a question about the WORLD, or filling a gap in a LITERATURE?**  
Mostly the world, which is good. But the paper backslides into literature-gap language in the contribution paragraph. The stronger framing is: *How do bright-line transparency rules change firm organization?* That is a world question.

**Could a smart economist who reads the introduction explain to a colleague what's new?**  
Right now, maybe not cleanly enough. They might say: “It’s a bunching-ish paper about beneficial ownership thresholds.” That is not enough. You want them to say: “It shows disclosure rules can create specific formal ownership architectures—especially the four-way split just above 25%—which is a new type of regulatory distortion.”

**What would make this contribution bigger? Be specific.**  
A few possibilities:

1. **Shift the framing from AML-compliance leak to organizational response to regulation.**  
   That would make the contribution more general and economically interesting.

2. **Show whether the phenomenon sharpens around policy changes.**  
   The paper gestures at 2016 and 2023 but does not build the narrative around temporal change. Even without getting into identification details, the paper would feel much bigger if it could say: this structure became more prevalent when the threshold became salient.

3. **Connect the formal pattern to real economic implications.**  
   For example: are these firms different in age, sector, related-party ties, cross-border exposure, or subsequent corporate activity? The paper currently gives a stylized fact plus correlates. For AER, it needs the stylized fact plus a broader implication.

4. **Clarify the mechanism.**  
   Is this about minimizing disclosure burden, avoiding control attribution, managing internal governance, or obscuring a latent beneficial owner? Right now the paper wants the strongest mechanism—opacity—but has evidence most directly for the milder mechanism—minimum-compliance design.

The paper would become substantially bigger if it could show not just “there is bunching in ownership configurations,” but “this threshold changes who gets revealed, how control is allocated, or how much tracing power the registry really provides.”

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC

### Setup
Governments increasingly require disclosure of beneficial owners, typically using a 25% threshold. These regimes are meant to reveal who controls companies and reduce opacity.

### Tension
A bright-line threshold may not just reveal ownership; it may also induce firms to redesign formal ownership in response. But we have little evidence on whether disclosure thresholds create systematic, manipulable ownership architectures.

### Resolution
In UK PSC data, firms with exactly four listed individual PSCs are disproportionately likely to place all four in the lowest reportable ownership band. This pattern is too concentrated and too specific to seem accidental, and it is stronger in firms with foreign PSCs.

### Implications
Transparency rules can be endogenous to the structures they observe. A registry built around a single threshold may create a focal “minimum-compliance” ownership form, which complicates the interpretation and effectiveness of beneficial-ownership disclosure systems.

### Evaluation
There is the skeleton of a strong narrative arc here, but the paper currently oscillates between two stories:

1. **A credible story:** disclosure thresholds shape formal ownership architecture.
2. **A more ambitious but less supported story:** actors are evading transparency and creating a major AML leak.

The paper keeps trying to tell story 2 with evidence that mainly supports story 1. That weakens the narrative. It makes the paper feel slightly like a collection of provocative patterns looking for the largest possible policy claim.

**What story should it be telling?**  
This one:

> Bright-line disclosure rules change what firms look like on paper. The 25% threshold appears to induce a specific minimum-compliance ownership structure. That matters because regulators and researchers often treat registry data as a passive reflection of underlying control, when in fact the reporting rule may itself shape the observed ownership graph.

That is a real story.

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST

### What fact would I lead with?
“Among UK firms with exactly four listed individual PSCs, about a third put all four in the lowest reportable ownership band—far more than you would expect if ownership shares were not being engineered around the 25% cutoff.”

That is a good dinner-party fact. It is memorable and intuitive.

### Would people lean in or reach for their phones?
They would lean in—initially. The fact is crisp and surprising.

### What follow-up question would they ask?
Immediately: **“But does that actually hide anything?”**

And that is exactly the paper’s strategic problem. The current draft does not fully satisfy that question. Four people at 25.01% are all still disclosed. So the paper needs to answer a subtler version:

- does this structure reduce the informational content of the registry?
- does it make tracing control harder?
- does it substitute for reporting a dominant owner?
- does it correlate with other opacity-enhancing structures?

If the answer remains indirect, the paper should stop pretending it has directly shown concealment. It has shown behavioral adaptation to a threshold, which is still interesting.

### If the findings are modest, is the modesty itself interesting?
Yes. Even if the paper cannot prove criminal evasion or large welfare losses, it still offers an interesting design lesson: **registries do not merely disclose organizational reality; they may partially manufacture it.** That is not a failed experiment. But the paper has to embrace that more modest and more believable lesson.
